 **Summary:**
The paper explores the iterative pruning of strong lottery tickets (SLT) in neural networks, focusing on the issue of "dying edges" in the Edge-Popup (EP) algorithm. The authors propose a Soft Iterative Edge-Popup (Soft iEP) method to mitigate this problem by gradually increasing the pruning rate without entirely disabling bottom edges, thus allowing for a more comprehensive search space exploration. This method is supported by empirical evidence showing that Soft iEP outperforms traditional EP and other pruning methods in terms of accuracy and network efficiency. The paper also discusses the theoretical implications of the EP algorithm and the impact of its modifications on the performance of SLT.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research.
- The authors have identified a significant issue with the EP algorithm, specifically the problem of "dying edges," and proposed a novel solution to address this.
- The proposed method, Soft iEP, shows significant improvements in performance over traditional EP and other pruning methods, as evidenced by extensive empirical results.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the EP algorithm, including the dying edge problem and its impact on performance, and offers a theoretical explanation for the observed phenomena.
- The methodology of Soft iEP is simple yet effective, and the paper presents a comprehensive comparison with other pruning methods, including iterative pruning and rewinding.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different network architectures and the scalability of the results to larger models.
- The empirical results do not clearly demonstrate the superiority of Soft iEP over other methods like IteRand, and the paper does not sufficiently address the issue of dying edges in the context of SLT.
- The paper does not provide a detailed theoretical justification for why Soft iEP works better than traditional EP, which could strengthen the argument for its effectiveness.
- There is a lack of clarity on the definition and role of "dying edges" in the context of SLT, and the paper does not adequately distinguish between different types of edges (e.g., top, bottom, dying) which could affect the interpretation of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous theoretical analysis and a broader range of experiments to validate the claims and enhance the understanding of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the Soft iEP method specifically addresses the issue of dying edges in the context of SLT?
- How does the performance of Soft iEP compare to other pruning methods, especially in terms of accuracy and network efficiency, when applied to larger models?
- What are the specific advantages of Soft iEP over traditional EP, and how does it improve the performance of SLT?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and roles of different types of edges (e.g., top, bottom, dying) in the context of the EP algorithm and their impact on the results?
- How does the Soft iEP method perform in scenarios where the pruning rate is set to 100%, and what are the implications of this for the overall performance of the model?
