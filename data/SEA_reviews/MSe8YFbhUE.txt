 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DrM, a novel method for balancing exploration and exploitation in visual reinforcement learning (RL) by minimizing the dormant ratio of neurons in the policy network. DrM employs a weight perturbation mechanism, an exploration scheduler, and an exploitation mechanism to manage the exploration-exploitation trade-off effectively. The method is evaluated across three continuous control benchmark environments and shows promising results, particularly in reducing the number of samples needed to achieve peak performance. Despite these strengths, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses, including insufficient experimental validation, lack of clarity in methodological descriptions, and an inadequate comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and its application, making it accessible and understandable.
- The proposed method, DrM, is innovative and provides a novel approach to balancing exploration and exploitation in visual RL, which is crucial for advancing the field.
- The empirical results demonstrate that DrM achieves significant improvements in sample efficiency and asymptotic performance compared to existing methods, showing its practical utility.
- The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies that support the effectiveness of the proposed method across various tasks, providing a robust evaluation of its components.
- The use of the dormant ratio as a metric to assess the activity level of an agent's policy network is a novel and insightful approach, offering a new perspective on how to evaluate the performance of RL agents.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks sufficient experimental validation to fully support the claims made about the method's effectiveness, particularly in terms of its generalizability across different environments and its robustness to different initializations.
- The methodology is not clearly described, with crucial details missing that are necessary for reproducibility and understanding of the experimental setup.
- The comparison with existing methods is insufficient, and the paper does not adequately discuss how DrM compares to these methods in terms of performance, efficiency, and other relevant metrics.
- The paper's claims about the method's effectiveness are sometimes overstated, particularly in the abstract and introduction, which could mislead readers about the actual contributions and limitations of the work.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that detract from the professional quality of the paper.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the methodology used in the experiments, particularly how the dormant ratio is calculated and how it is used to guide exploration and exploitation?
- How does the proposed method perform in discrete action tasks, and what modifications would be necessary to adapt it to such environments?
- In terms of the experimental results, can you provide more details on how the dormant ratio varies across different environments and how it relates to the performance of the RL agent?
- How does the method handle the trade-off between exploration and exploitation, especially in environments with sparse rewards or where the optimal policy is not well-defined?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of baselines and how DrM compares to these methods in terms of sample efficiency, asymptotic performance, and other relevant metrics?
