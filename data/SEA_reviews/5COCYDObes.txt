 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel bilevel framework named Bilevel-LLM, aimed at enhancing decision-making in complex environments using Large Language Models (LLMs). The framework comprises three components: a prompt generation policy, a chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning policy, and an action policy. The prompt generation policy selects appropriate prompts based on historical observations, which are then used to generate CoT reasoning. This reasoning guides the action policy to perform specific actions in the environment. The framework is evaluated on several benchmarks, showing improvements over existing methods. However, concerns about the clarity of the paper, the need for more detailed comparisons with existing methods, and the generalizability of the results are noted.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel bilevel framework that integrates a prompt generation policy, a chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning policy, and an action policy, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The framework is evaluated on several benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness in complex decision-making tasks.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the methodology and results, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The use of Large Language Models (LLMs) for decision-making tasks is a promising approach, and the paper presents a well-structured framework that effectively leverages LLMs.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a logical flow of content, which enhances its readability and comprehensibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of the effectiveness of the prompt generation policy and the generalizability of the results across different environments.
- The clarity of the paper could be improved, especially in the introduction and methodology sections, where the definitions and roles of various components are not clearly explained.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the framework, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for training and the generalizability of the results to more complex or varied environments.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed ablation studies to better understand the individual contributions of each component of the framework.
- The use of LLMs for prompt generation and CoT reasoning is not novel, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and effectiveness of the prompt generation policy in the bilevel framework? How does it compare to other methods in terms of performance and generalizability?
- How do the authors ensure the generalizability of the framework across different environments and tasks?
- Could you provide more details on the computational resources required for training the framework and the scalability of the results to more complex or varied environments?
- In the ablation studies, how do the different components of the framework (e.g., prompt policy, CoT reasoning, and action policy) contribute to the overall performance?
- How does the framework handle the generation of prompts for tasks where no prompt candidates are available?
- Can you discuss the limitations of the proposed framework and how they might affect its applicability and performance in real-world scenarios?
