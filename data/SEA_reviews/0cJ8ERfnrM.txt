 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new benchmark, "Antibody DomainBed," for domain generalization in antibody design, focusing on the out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization problem. It employs a lab-in-the-loop approach, where experimental results are used to update models, simulating real-world scenarios. The benchmark utilizes a dataset derived from the Structural Antibody Database (SAbDab) and graphinity, and applies domain generalization algorithms from the DomainBed framework. The study evaluates various domain generalization methods and identifies ensembling as the most effective approach. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited scope, lack of novelty, and insufficient experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel application of domain generalization to the antibody design problem, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed benchmark, Antibody DomainBed, is designed to emulate real-world scenarios, providing a valuable tool for evaluating domain generalization models in practical settings.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The inclusion of a new dataset and the adaptation of the DomainBed framework to the antibody domain provide a solid foundation for future research in this area.
- The paper addresses an important problem in drug discovery and provides a valuable resource for the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing benchmarks, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed methods.
- The evaluation of domain generalization methods is limited to a few environments, which might not sufficiently demonstrate the robustness of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational challenges associated with domain generalization in the context of large-scale protein sequences, which could limit the practical applicability of the proposed methods.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it primarily adapts existing domain generalization methods without significant innovation.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of scalability and generalizability to other types of protein structures.
- The paper's reliance on a single dataset and a limited set of baseline models may not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods over existing approaches.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing benchmarks to demonstrate the advantages of the proposed benchmark?
- How do the authors plan to address the computational challenges associated with domain generalization in the context of large-scale protein sequences?
- Could the authors expand the evaluation of domain generalization methods to include a broader range of environments to better demonstrate the robustness of the proposed methods?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of scalability and generalizability to other types of protein structures?
- Could the authors clarify the specific contributions of the proposed methods beyond the adaptation of existing domain generalization techniques?
