 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a unified algorithmic framework, Multi-Agent Maximize-to-Explore (MAMEX), for multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) in general-sum Markov Games (MGs) with function approximation. The framework is designed to ensure sample efficiency in learning various equilibrium notions, including Nash Equilibrium, Coarse Correlated Equilibrium, and Correlated Equilibrium. The authors propose a novel complexity measure, the Multi-Agent Decoupling Coefficient (MADC), to quantify the exploration-exploitation tradeoff in MARL. This measure is used to prove that MAMEX achieves sublinear regret for learning these equilibria in classes with small MADCs. The paper also discusses the practical implementation of the framework and its theoretical contributions, including the use of an equilibrium-solving oracle and an oracle that solves regularized supervised learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a unified algorithmic framework, MAMEX, for MARL in general-sum MGs, which is the first to ensure sample efficiency in learning Nash Equilibrium, Coarse Correlated Equilibrium, and Correlated Equilibrium for both model-based and model-free MARL problems with low MADC.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed proofs in the appendix, and the algorithm is easy to follow.
- The introduction of the Multi-Agent Decoupling Coefficient (MADC) is a significant contribution to the field, as it captures the exploration-exploitation tradeoff in MARL and can be used to prove the sample efficiency of the MAMEX algorithm.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the MAMEX algorithm, including regret and sample complexity bounds, and demonstrates the practical implementation of the framework with an equilibrium-solving oracle and an oracle that solves regularized supervised learning.
- The paper is significant in its theoretical contributions, as it provides a unified framework for MARL that covers both model-based and model-free approaches, and the theoretical results are robust and sound.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions and theorems are not clearly stated, which could lead to confusion and misunderstanding. Specifically, the assumptions and theorems are relegated to the appendix, which is not ideal for clarity and accessibility.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- There is a lack of empirical evaluation, which is essential for validating the theoretical claims and demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the MAMEX algorithm.
- The paper's contribution in terms of novelty and originality is questionable, as the framework and the MADC measure seem to be a straightforward extension of existing methods, such as the MEX framework.
- The paper's theoretical results are limited to the Nash Equilibrium, which may not be the most relevant or practical equilibrium concept for all applications.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the assumptions and theorems mentioned in the paper, particularly the role and implications of the realizability and completeness assumption?
- How does the MAMEX algorithm perform in scenarios where the MADC is not small, and what are the implications of this for the sample efficiency and regret bounds?
- Can you provide more details on the practical implementation of the MAMEX algorithm, including the computational complexity and the scalability to larger games?
- How does the MAMEX algorithm compare to existing methods in terms of sample efficiency and regret bounds, especially in scenarios where the MADC is not small?
- Can you elaborate on the novelty and originality of the MAMEX framework and the MADC measure, particularly in relation to existing methods in MARL?
- Could you provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the practical implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis?
