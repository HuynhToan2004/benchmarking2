 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach using differentiable decision trees (DDTs) to learn interpretable reward functions from pairwise preferences, aiming to enhance the interpretability of reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms. The methodology involves training DDTs with different internal node architectures, including simple and sophisticated nodes, and evaluates their performance against a neural network baseline in various environments such as CartPole, MNIST gridworld, and Atari games. The results indicate that DDTs can achieve competitive performance while providing interpretability, though the interpretability is more evident in simpler environments. The paper also discusses the potential for using DDTs as a debugging tool for misaligned reward functions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for learning interpretable reward functions using differentiable decision trees (DDTs), which is a significant advancement in the field of reinforcement learning.
- The method is well-motivated and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method across various domains, including CartPole, MNIST gridworld, and Atari games, demonstrating its effectiveness and versatility.
- The use of DDTs for learning interpretable reward functions is novel and interesting, particularly in the context of visual domains where interpretability is crucial.
- The paper includes a comprehensive experimental setup and analysis, which helps in understanding the method's strengths and limitations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the trade-offs between interpretability and performance, particularly in complex environments like Atari.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodology for obtaining pairwise preference labels from human feedback, which is crucial for the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method to more complex environments, such as those involving high-dimensional image observations.
- The evaluation metrics used are not standardized, and the comparison with other interpretable RL methods is lacking.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its scalability and the potential for overfitting to training data.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with other interpretable RL methods and a clearer discussion on the scalability and generalizability of the method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the pairwise preference labels are obtained from human feedback and discuss the implications of this process on the method's performance?
- How does the method perform in more complex environments, such as those involving high-dimensional image observations?
- What are the specific advantages of using DDTs over other interpretable RL methods, and how does the method handle the trade-off between interpretability and performance?
- How does the method ensure that the interpretability is not compromised by the complexity of the environments or the depth of the decision trees?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for overfitting to the training data and how this might affect the generalizability of the method?
- Would it be possible to include a more rigorous comparison with other interpretable RL methods to better understand the strengths and limitations of the proposed method?
