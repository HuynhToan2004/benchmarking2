 **Summary:**
The paper introduces JudgeLM, a method for evaluating large language models (LLMs) in open-ended tasks using a dataset generated by fine-tuning LLMs on GPT-4 judgments. The dataset is used to train a new LLM, JudgeLM, which is claimed to outperform existing methods like PandaLM and GPT-3.5 on various metrics. The paper also discusses the biases introduced by fine-tuning LLMs and proposes methods to mitigate these biases, such as swap augmentation, reference support, and reference drop. Despite its strengths in dataset size and experimental setup, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in definitions and methodologies, particularly in the comparison to baseline methods and the presentation of results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new dataset for evaluating LLMs in open-ended tasks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method, JudgeLM, achieves state-of-the-art results on two benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper addresses biases in fine-tuning LLMs as judges and proposes methods to mitigate these biases, which is a novel approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive evaluations and ablation studies.
- The introduction of a dataset for evaluating LLMs in open-ended tasks is a valuable resource for the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining the task and the role of the LLM judge, which could confuse readers about the evaluation process.
- The comparison to baseline methods is not fair due to differences in dataset sizes and model sizes, which could skew the results.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the potential biases introduced by using GPT-4 as a teacher model and does not provide a detailed analysis of the biases in the dataset.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the method to larger datasets or the impact of different reference answers on the evaluation process.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the limitations of the method and the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition of the task and the role of the LLM judge in the evaluation process?
- How does the paper address the potential biases introduced by using GPT-4 as a teacher model, and what steps were taken to mitigate these biases?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the method to larger datasets and how the evaluation process might be affected by different reference answers?
- How does the proposed method compare to other approaches like ZenoBuild, and what are the specific advantages of using JudgeLM over these methods?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed approach and how these might be mitigated?
