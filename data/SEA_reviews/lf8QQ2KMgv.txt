 **Summary:**
The paper critically examines the study by Feldman and Zhang (2020) which claimed that memorization is necessary for generalization in deep learning models. The authors identify several methodological errors in the original study, including lack of model convergence, data leakage, and sub-population shifts, which led to an overestimation of memorization's impact on test accuracy. By addressing these issues, the paper shows that memorization has a minimal impact on test accuracy, challenging the original findings. The paper also proposes improvements in methodology to better discern memorized points from sub-populations, encouraging future research to refine definitions and techniques.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- It addresses a critical issue in the field of deep learning by questioning the necessity of memorization for generalization, which is a topic of significant interest and relevance.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the methodological errors in a previous study, which is crucial for ensuring the reliability and accuracy of research findings.
- The authors have made substantial efforts to reproduce the experiments and address the identified issues, which demonstrates a commitment to scientific rigor and transparency.
- The paper encourages future research to refine definitions and techniques for identifying memorized points, which could lead to more robust and reliable findings in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear conclusion and does not provide a new methodology or definition for memorization, which could limit its impact and utility for the community.
- The experimental results, while interesting, are somewhat limited in scope and depth, focusing mainly on the CIFAR-100 dataset and not exploring broader implications or generalizability to other datasets or models.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the broader implications of its findings, such as how the results might apply to different datasets or models, or how they could be used to guide practical applications in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's claims about the impact of memorization on test accuracy are not convincingly supported by the data presented, which could limit the paper's credibility and influence.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific methodology used to identify memorized points and how this methodology differs from or improves upon previous approaches?
- How do the authors justify the claim that memorization is not necessary for generalization, and what evidence supports this claim beyond the experimental results presented?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies demonstrating the practical applications of their findings, particularly in real-world scenarios?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of their methodology, such as its applicability to different datasets or models, and what steps are being taken to extend the research beyond the current scope?
