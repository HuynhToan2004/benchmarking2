 **Summary:**
The paper introduces RuLES, a benchmark designed to evaluate the rule-following abilities of large language models (LLMs) in various scenarios. The benchmark consists of 15 scenarios, each with specific rules that the LLMs are expected to adhere to. The authors have conducted extensive testing using both manual and automated methods, involving over 800 test cases. The results indicate that most models fail to follow the rules, with GPT-4 showing the best performance. The benchmark aims to provide a framework for evaluating the robustness of LLMs against adversarial inputs and to stimulate research in this area. However, the paper's contribution is seen as limited due to the simplicity of the rules and the lack of novelty in its approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear motivation for the research, focusing on the importance of rule-following in large language models (LLMs).
- The authors have conducted extensive testing on various LLMs, including GPT-4, GPT-3.5, Claude Instant, Claude, PaLM, Vicuna, Llama 2, and Mistral, which demonstrates a thorough evaluation of the models.
- The introduction of RuLES, a benchmark for evaluating rule-following behavior in LLMs, is a significant contribution to the field, as it provides a standardized framework for testing the robustness of LLMs against adversarial inputs.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of test cases, which are crucial for evaluating the models' performance and identifying areas for improvement.
- The inclusion of a systematic test suite that includes six attack strategies and a manual test suite adds depth to the evaluation process.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the results and findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the potential limitations of the RuLES benchmark, which could affect its applicability and reliability in real-world scenarios.
- The rules used in the benchmark are considered too simple and may not adequately challenge the models, as they are similar to existing instruction-following tasks.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the impact of different prompt formats on the models' performance, which could be crucial for optimizing the evaluation process.
- The paper's contribution is seen as limited due to the simplicity of the rules and the lack of novelty in the approach, as similar evaluations have been conducted in previous studies.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential biases in the test cases and the evaluation process, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The paper does not provide enough details on how the models were tested, particularly in terms of the specific prompts used and the conditions under which the tests were conducted.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the rules in the RuLES benchmark were designed and what considerations were taken into account during their development?
- How do the authors plan to address the limitations of the benchmark, particularly in terms of its applicability and reliability in real-world scenarios?
- Can you discuss the potential impact of different prompt formats on the models' performance and how this was addressed in the evaluation process?
- How do the authors ensure that the test cases are not biased towards specific models or types of models?
- Could you provide more details on the evaluation process, particularly how the models were tested and what conditions were used?
- What steps were taken to ensure the reliability and validity of the results, especially in terms of the potential biases in the test cases and the evaluation process?
