 **Summary:**
The paper presents a unified framework for analyzing robust loss functions, aiming to address the underfitting issue in training with noisy labels. It introduces a standard form for loss functions, which includes a shared implicit loss function and different implicit design choices such as sample-weighting functions and output regularizers. This framework is used to derive a curriculum view of loss functions, providing insights into the training dynamics and the impact of different design choices on noise robustness and underfitting. The paper also explores how regularizers affect training dynamics with different loss functions and provides empirical evidence to support its claims. However, the paper's novelty and the practical utility of its theoretical contributions are questioned, as it primarily re-derives existing results with minimal new insights.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a unified framework for analyzing robust loss functions, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- It presents a clear and well-structured analysis of the training dynamics of robust loss functions, which is both theoretically sound and empirically validated.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of robust training, which is the underfitting issue, and provides a novel approach to understanding this phenomenon.
- The empirical evidence provided is comprehensive and convincing, with detailed results and analyses that support the claims made in the paper.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and detailed derivations that make the content accessible and understandable.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily re-derives existing results from previous works, such as Wang et al. (2019), without significantly advancing the understanding of robust loss functions.
- The practical utility of the theoretical contributions is questionable, as the paper does not clearly demonstrate how these contributions can be applied to improve robust loss functions in practical scenarios.
- The paper's focus on specific types of loss functions (e.g., those with closed-form expressions) may limit its applicability to other types of loss functions, such as those with complex or non-differentiable components.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implications of its findings, particularly how the insights gained from the analysis of robust loss functions can be applied to improve the design of loss functions in real-world applications.
- There are some concerns about the clarity and organization of the paper, particularly in the presentation of figures and the explanation of certain terms and concepts.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the practical utility of the theoretical contributions made in the paper? How can these contributions be applied to improve the design of robust loss functions in practical scenarios?
- How do the authors justify the choice of focusing on loss functions with closed-form expressions, and what are the implications of this choice for the applicability of the framework to other types of loss functions?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples of how the insights gained from the analysis of robust loss functions can be applied to improve the design of loss functions in real-world applications?
- In the context of the underfitting issue, how do the authors explain the observed underfitting in some cases, such as with TCE on CIFAR100?
- How does the paper's framework handle the issue of label noise, particularly in terms of the impact of different types and levels of noise on the training dynamics and the effectiveness of the proposed solutions?
- Can the authors clarify the terms and concepts used in the paper, particularly those that are not well-defined or explained, such as the "implicit loss function" and the "memorization effect"?
