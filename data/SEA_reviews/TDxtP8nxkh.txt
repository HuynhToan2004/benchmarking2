 **Summary:**
The paper introduces NAP2, a novel hyperparameter optimization (HPO) method for deep neural networks (DNNs) that leverages changes in weights and gradients to predict final performance. NAP2 models these changes using meta-features, which are then used to train an LSTM model for performance prediction. The method is evaluated against state-of-the-art baselines on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, showing improvements in top-K precision and resource usage. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, the limited evaluation on different datasets and architectures, and the clarity of presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to hyperparameter optimization (HPO) by focusing on the changes in weights and gradients of neural networks, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The proposed method shows promising results in terms of accuracy and resource efficiency, demonstrating a potential advantage over existing methods.
- The method is well-articulated and the paper is generally easy to follow, which facilitates understanding and reproducibility.
- The inclusion of a dataset with meta-features for neural networks is a valuable resource for future research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it appears similar to existing methods that use early training dynamics to predict the performance of neural networks.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the use of different datasets and architectures.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to specific types of neural networks, such as CNNs, and its applicability to other architectures like transformers is unclear.
- The paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in the description of the method and the results, which could hinder comprehension and reproducibility.
- The evaluation is limited to two datasets, which may not sufficiently demonstrate the method's generalizability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific contributions of their method compared to existing approaches that use early training dynamics for performance prediction?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and resource usage with other state-of-the-art HPO methods?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other methods, including those that use different datasets and architectures?
- How does the method perform on larger datasets like ImageNet, and what are the implications of using different types of neural networks, such as transformers?
- Could the authors address the clarity issues in the paper, particularly in the descriptions of the method and the results, to enhance understanding and reproducibility?
