 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the memorization phenomenon in text-to-image diffusion models, focusing on the impact of text prompts on image generation. It proposes a method to detect memorization by analyzing the text-conditioned noise prediction and introduces strategies to mitigate memorization by either minimizing it during inference or filtering it during training. The method involves using a simple metric based on the difference in noise predictions between text-conditioned and unconditioned embeddings to detect memorization. The paper also provides a method to identify significant tokens in prompts that contribute to memorization, suggesting their removal to mitigate the issue. The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of these strategies in reducing memorization, although concerns were raised about the generalizability of the findings and the lack of comprehensive experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue of memorization in text-to-image diffusion models, which is crucial for ensuring the privacy and security of training data.
- The proposed detection method is simple, efficient, and effective, requiring only a single generation per prompt and not requiring multiple generations or access to the training data.
- The paper provides a novel method for detecting memorized prompts by inspecting the magnitude of text-conditional predictions, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method to identify significant tokens in prompts that contribute to memorization is innovative and could be useful for users to adjust their prompts, reducing the likelihood of memorization.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the experiments are well-conducted, providing a clear demonstration of the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive experimental validation, particularly in terms of the generalizability of the findings. The experiments are limited to specific datasets and do not cover a broader range of models or different types of prompts.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the detection method would perform on models with a large number of parameters, such as those used in larger models like Stable Diffusion v2 and SDv3.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that the proposed method can effectively detect memorization in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant omission.
- The methodology and experimental setup are not clearly explained, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the method's implementation and effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform on models with a large number of parameters, such as those used in larger models like Stable Diffusion v2 and SDv3?
- Can the authors provide more evidence or real-world examples to support the claim that the proposed method can effectively detect memorization in real-world scenarios?
- How does the detection method compare in terms of effectiveness and efficiency with other methods that might require more complex or time-consuming processes?
- Is there a possibility of false positives in the detection process, and how might this impact the reliability of the results?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of memorization in models that have been pre-trained on a large dataset, such as LAION, and then fine-tuned on a smaller dataset?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and methodology to make the paper more accessible and understandable to readers?
