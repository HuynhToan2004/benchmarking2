 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel prompting method, Hypothesis-based and Structure-based (HS) prompting, aimed at enhancing the problem-solving capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) in complex domains such as medical diagnosis and business consulting. The method leverages the Mutually Exclusive and Collectively Exhaustive (MECE) framework to guide LLMs in generating, prioritizing, and validating hypotheses through targeted questioning and data collection. The approach is validated through case studies and evaluated by domain experts, demonstrating its effectiveness in real-world scenarios. The methodology is designed to streamline the problem-solving process by structuring the problem space and encouraging active data collection, which is crucial for accurate hypothesis validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel prompting method, HS, which effectively utilizes a MECE framework to guide LLMs in generating and prioritizing hypotheses, and actively inquiring to verify these hypotheses.
- The method is supported by a comprehensive evaluation involving domain experts in both business consulting and medical diagnosis, providing a robust validation of its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the methodology and results, and includes detailed case studies that demonstrate the practical application of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in real-world scenarios, particularly in domains like healthcare and business, where many-to-one problems are prevalent, and identifies a critical need for a method that can effectively navigate these complexities.
- The authors provide a clear and detailed guide for generating examples, allowing users to create tailored examples for their specific tasks, which enhances the applicability of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other prompting methods, such as ToT and GoT, which are also designed for complex problem-solving tasks. This omission limits the understanding of the relative advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on a MECE framework and the need for a comprehensive understanding of the problem space might limit its applicability to more complex and dynamic real-world scenarios, where the MECE framework may not be fully effective.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the performance of the LLM used (GPT-4) in these tasks, which is crucial for understanding the role of the model in the results and the potential impact of different model architectures or training methods.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the HS method comprehensively, which could help in understanding its scope and potential areas for improvement.
- The evaluation primarily focuses on GPT-4, and it is unclear how the method would perform with other LLMs, especially those not fine-tuned for specific domains.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with other prompting methods, such as ToT and GoT, to highlight the unique advantages of HS?
- How does the method perform with different LLMs, especially those not fine-tuned for specific domains?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the HS method and discuss potential areas for improvement?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the MECE framework may not be fully effective, and what are the potential implications for the accuracy and completeness of the hypotheses generated?
- The paper mentions that the method encourages LLMs to ask sharply targeted questions or request specific data to validate or invalidate hypotheses. Can the authors provide more details on how this process is implemented and how it affects the performance of the method?
