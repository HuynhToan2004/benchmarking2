 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SketchEdit, a novel method for stroke-level editing of freehand sketches. It employs a diffusion model to predict stroke positions, which are then used to synthesize sketches. The method is designed to decouple strokes from the sketch sequence, enabling selective editing and replacement of strokes. The approach involves a stroke encoder to encode each stroke separately and a sequence decoder to synthesize the final manipulated sketch. The paper claims that SketchEdit achieves significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods in sketch reconstruction, although it does not directly compare its editing capabilities. The methodology is evaluated using a dataset of 17 categories and shows superior performance in terms of image quality and semantic similarity. However, the paper suffers from issues in clarity and presentation, with numerous typos and unclear figures, and lacks a comprehensive evaluation of its editing capabilities.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for editing sketches at the stroke level, which is a significant advancement in the field of sketch editing.
- The use of a diffusion model to predict stroke positions is innovative and adds a new dimension to the stroke editing process.
- The proposed method achieves significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods in sketch reconstruction, as demonstrated by quantitative and qualitative results.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive review of related work, highlighting the authors' understanding of the field.
- The proposed method is flexible and can be applied to various categories of sketches, which is a notable advantage over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the editing capabilities of the proposed method. The evaluation primarily focuses on sketch reconstruction, not directly comparing the editing capabilities of the method.
- There is a significant issue with the clarity and presentation of the paper, with numerous typos and unclear figures which hinder understanding and assessment of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly the potential over-smoothing of strokes which may not align with human drawing styles.
- The methodology section is not clearly explained, making it difficult to understand the specific contributions of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of existing methods and how they were addressed, which could enhance the credibility and impact of the research.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the meaning of "stroke" as used in the paper? Is it a sequence of points or a single point?
- How does the proposed method compare with other diffusion-based methods for sketch editing, such as those mentioned in references 1 and 2?
- What are the specific contributions of the proposed method over existing methods, particularly in terms of the use of stroke embeddings and the token mixture block?
- Could you provide more details on the training process, such as the specifics of the loss functions and the training data used?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of over-smoothing of strokes, and what measures are in place to ensure that the edited sketches maintain the natural style of human drawing?
- Could you provide more examples and detailed analysis to support the claims made about the superior performance of the proposed method over existing methods?
