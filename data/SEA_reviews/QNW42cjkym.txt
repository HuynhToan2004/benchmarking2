 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the cold start problem in biomedical image classification by proposing a three-step method: (1) pretraining an encoder using self-supervised learning, (2) sampling the most informative data points for annotation, and (3) initializing a model ensemble to overcome the lack of validation data. The methodology includes a novel application of furthest point sampling (FPS) for data selection and model soups for ensemble training. The paper evaluates the proposed method on four biomedical datasets, showing improvements over existing methods. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the method, the clarity of experimental details, and the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method addresses a significant and challenging problem in biomedical image classification, which is crucial for improving the accuracy and efficiency of diagnosis systems.
- The paper includes a comprehensive ablation study that provides insights into the effectiveness of different components of the proposed method, such as the SSL method, sampling strategy, and ensemble training.
- The methodology is well-organized, with clear explanations of the experimental setup and results, including the use of visual aids like Figure 3 to illustrate the effectiveness of the SSL method.
- The paper is reproducible, with the code and data provided, which is essential for advancing research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the hyperparameters used in the SSL methods and their impact on the performance of the model.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly concerning the hyperparameters used in the SSL methods and the computational requirements of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the proposed method to other biomedical datasets or different medical conditions, which is crucial for validating the robustness of the approach.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's performance relative to existing solutions.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough literature review and a clearer presentation of the method's contributions to the field.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the hyperparameters used in the SSL methods and their impact on the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method perform on other biomedical datasets, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
- What are the specific computational requirements of the proposed method, and how do they compare to other methods in terms of efficiency?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the validation set in the proposed method, particularly in the context of the model soups?
- How does the proposed method handle class imbalance, and what are the implications for the performance of the model in such scenarios?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including a discussion on the method's advantages and disadvantages compared to these approaches?
