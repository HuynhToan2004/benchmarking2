 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel diffusion model architecture, termed LEGO bricks, designed to enhance the efficiency and adaptability of diffusion models. This architecture integrates Local-feature Enrichment and Global-content Orchestration, enabling the model to be reconfigured at test time for variable-resolution image generation. The model's efficiency is demonstrated through reduced training and sampling times, along with improved performance in terms of FID scores. The architecture is designed to be modular and scalable, allowing for the selective skipping of bricks to reduce computational costs. The paper also explores the use of pre-trained diffusion models as LEGO bricks, enhancing the model's capability to generate high-resolution images.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed LEGO architecture is novel, well-motivated, and provides a significant improvement in efficiency over existing diffusion models.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the proposed methods, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The experimental results are compelling, showing that the proposed method can significantly reduce training and sampling times while maintaining or improving performance.
- The use of pre-trained diffusion models as LEGO bricks is a novel approach that enhances the model's capability to generate high-resolution images.
- The modular design of the LEGO bricks allows for efficient training and inference, as well as the ability to skip bricks during inference to save computational resources.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art diffusion models, particularly in terms of FLOPs and training costs.
- The use of FID as the primary evaluation metric may not be sufficient to fully assess the quality of the generated images, especially in terms of diversity and sharpness.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of generating images at variable resolutions, which is a significant claim of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The experimental results presented in the paper are not convincing enough to support the claims of superior performance over existing methods.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of FLOPs and training costs with state-of-the-art diffusion models?
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the computational efficiency of the proposed method, including the training and inference times?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of generating images at variable resolutions, especially higher resolutions?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the dataset and training parameters used?
- How does the method handle the skipping of bricks during inference, and what are the implications for the quality and diversity of the generated images?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the classifier-free guidance in the performance of the model, and how it affects the generation process?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods in terms of generating high-resolution images, and what are the limitations of the method in this regard?
