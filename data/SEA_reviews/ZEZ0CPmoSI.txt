 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to non-convex optimization using matrix-valued step sizes in compressed gradient descent (CGD) algorithms. The authors propose two algorithms, det-CGD1 and det-CGD2, which leverage matrix smoothness to improve convergence rates. These algorithms are analyzed theoretically for both single-node and distributed settings, showing that they can achieve better convergence rates than traditional methods. The paper also explores the practical implications of these algorithms in federated learning settings, where communication complexity is reduced through sketching techniques. The theoretical results are supported by empirical evidence, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and structured presentation of the theoretical results and empirical evidence.
- The introduction of matrix-valued step sizes in CGD algorithms is a novel approach that enhances the understanding of non-convex optimization.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms, including convergence guarantees and optimal step size selection.
- The practical implications of the proposed algorithms are highlighted, particularly in the context of federated learning where communication complexity is reduced.
- The empirical evidence supports the theoretical claims, showing that the proposed algorithms can achieve better convergence rates and reduced communication complexity.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implementation of the algorithms, particularly in terms of the computational efficiency and complexity of the matrix operations involved.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of convergence rates and the practical implications of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed algorithms, such as the assumptions required for optimal performance and the potential impact of sub-optimal choices of matrix D.
- The theoretical results are not sufficiently intuitive or easy to follow, and some sections of the paper could benefit from clearer explanations and more intuitive examples.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of the computational complexity and the practical applicability to large-scale problems.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed discussion on the practical implementation of the proposed algorithms, including the computational complexity and the impact on the overall optimization process?
- How does the choice of matrix D affect the performance of the proposed algorithms, and what are the implications of sub-optimal choices?
- Could you clarify the assumptions required for the optimal performance of the proposed algorithms, and discuss the practical feasibility of these assumptions in real-world applications?
- How do the proposed algorithms compare in terms of convergence rates and practical performance with existing methods, particularly in scenarios where communication complexity is a concern?
- Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the theoretical results and the assumptions used in the proofs, particularly in terms of their practical implications and the scalability of the proposed methods?
