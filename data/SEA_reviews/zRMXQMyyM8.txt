 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DISCRET, a novel framework for estimating Individual Treatment Effects (ITEs) using a self-interpretable method. DISCRET leverages a deep reinforcement learning algorithm to identify similar samples from a database and estimate ITEs based on their average treatment effects. The method is evaluated across various datasets, including tabular, natural language processing (NLP), and image data, showing competitive performance against existing methods. The framework's interpretability is highlighted, with the paper providing extensive experiments to demonstrate its effectiveness. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper, particularly in explaining the methodology and the interpretability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to estimating Individual Treatment Effects (ITEs) using a self-interpretable framework, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it accessible to readers, and the paper is structured to provide clear explanations and intuitive examples.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and settings, demonstrating the robustness and versatility of the proposed method.
- The use of reinforcement learning for rule learning is innovative and adds a new dimension to the application of this technology.
- The paper addresses a critical issue in healthcare and beyond by focusing on the interpretability of ITE estimations, which is crucial for stakeholders to understand the underlying factors influencing predictions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the methodology and the presentation of results.
- The interpretability of the results is questionable, with the paper relying on a rule-based approach that may not provide a clear understanding of the causal factors influencing the ITEs.
- There is a need for a more detailed discussion on the generalizability of the method across different types of data and settings, especially those not considered in the current study.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and interpretability.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, with figures and tables needing clearer labeling and more detailed explanations.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for larger datasets or more complex scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the methodology ensures the interpretability of the results, particularly in terms of the causal factors influencing the ITEs?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the treatment effect is not homogeneous across different subgroups of the population?
- Can you provide more details on the scalability of the method, especially in terms of the computational resources required for larger datasets or more complex scenarios?
- How does the method handle different types of data, such as text or image data, which were not evaluated in the current study?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of datasets and the rationale behind the selection of specific datasets for evaluation?
- How does the method compare to other interpretable machine learning methods and post-hoc explanations in terms of faithfulness and effectiveness?
