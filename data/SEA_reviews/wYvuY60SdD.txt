 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to node classification on graphs using a mixture of weak and strong experts, termed Mowst. The method employs a weak expert, typically a multi-layer perceptron (MLP), and a strong expert, a graph neural network (GNN), to handle different aspects of graph data. The weak expert specializes in local node features, while the strong expert leverages neighborhood information. The collaboration between these experts is facilitated by a "confidence" mechanism based on the dispersion of the weak expert's prediction logits, which determines when the strong expert is activated. Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness in handling both homophilous and heterophilous graphs, showing significant accuracy improvements over state-of-the-art baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by combining a weak expert (MLP) with a strong expert (GNN) to handle different aspects of graph data, which is both innovative and theoretically sound.
- The use of a confidence mechanism based on the dispersion of the weak expert's prediction logits is an effective way to manage collaboration between the two experts, enhancing the model's performance.
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings, making it accessible and understandable.
- The experimental results are robust, showing significant accuracy improvements over state-of-the-art baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of the proposed method, particularly the specialization and denoising modes, which contribute to a deeper understanding of the model's operation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other related methods, particularly in the context of heterophilous graphs where the proposed method is expected to perform well.
- There is a need for more detailed discussion on the choice of the confidence function and its impact on the model's performance, especially in scenarios where it may not be optimal.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale graph learning applications.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse datasets and benchmarking against a broader range of existing methods to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the term "noisy" in the context of the denoising mode and how it relates to the behavior of the model?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other methods like H2GCN, especially in scenarios where the MLP and GNN are optimized separately?
- Is there a specific reason why the paper focuses on binary classification, and how does this choice affect the generalizability of the findings?
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the graph?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the GNN expert fails to optimize effectively, and what are the potential implications for the overall performance of the model?
