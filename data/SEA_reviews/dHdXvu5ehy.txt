 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ESC-GNN, a novel method aimed at enhancing the counting power of subgraph GNNs by precomputing and encoding structural information into the graph. This method allows for more efficient substructure counting, theoretically and empirically, compared to traditional subgraph GNNs. The approach is based on the observation that the distance from rooted nodes within subgraphs is crucial for boosting the counting power of GNNs. ESC-GNN encodes this information into a structural embedding, which is then used to augment the input graph, reducing the computational cost and memory requirements. The paper provides both theoretical and empirical evidence supporting the efficiency and effectiveness of ESC-GNN, although concerns are raised about the clarity of the method's implementation and the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The proposed method is novel and interesting, providing a theoretical and empirical analysis that supports its effectiveness.
- The method is well-motivated, and the authors have demonstrated a clear understanding of the limitations and potential of their approach.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the method's performance, comparing it to various baselines and demonstrating its effectiveness in substructure counting.
- The theoretical results are presented clearly, and the paper offers a detailed analysis of the computational costs and memory requirements of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the implementation of ESC-GNN, particularly how the structural embeddings are computed and how they are used in the model.
- The method's efficiency claims are not convincingly supported by empirical evidence, and the paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other efficient methods like I2-GNN.
- The paper's focus on counting substructures may limit its applicability to other tasks, and the generalization of the results to larger or more complex graphs is not convincingly demonstrated.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its practical applicability and potential future improvements.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the theoretical results and how they translate into practical applications.

**Questions:**

- How does the method handle different types of substructures, and what are the implications of focusing on a specific set of substructures for the model's performance?
- Could you discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
