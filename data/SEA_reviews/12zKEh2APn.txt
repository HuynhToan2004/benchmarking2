 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PROSE, a transformer-based neural network designed for solving ordinary differential equations (ODEs) by learning both the solution operator and the governing equation from data. The model processes multimodal inputs, including numerical data and symbolic expressions, and outputs numerical predictions and symbolic expressions. The authors demonstrate that PROSE can handle noisy data and errors in symbolic representations, showcasing its ability to generalize to out-of-distribution data. The model's architecture includes a data encoder, symbol encoder, feature fusion, data decoder, and symbol decoder, all connected through self-attention and cross-attention mechanisms. Experiments are conducted on 15 distinct ODEs, demonstrating the model's effectiveness in predicting solutions and learning governing equations. However, the paper is criticized for its limited evaluation, lack of comparison with existing methods, and insufficient experimental validation on real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel methodology for learning differential equations from data, specifically focusing on ODEs, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The model's architecture is well-designed, with clear explanations of its components, and the methodology is well-articulated, making the paper easy to follow.
- The paper demonstrates the model's ability to handle noisy data and errors in symbolic representations, showing its robustness and adaptability.
- The use of multimodal inputs, including numerical data and symbolic expressions, enhances the model's ability to learn complex relationships and generalize to new data.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the model's performance, including accuracy metrics and comparisons with baseline models, which supports the claims of its effectiveness.
- The inclusion of a detailed appendix and supplementary materials, such as code and additional experiments, provides a comprehensive view of the model's capabilities and potential applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the model handles different types of errors, such as numerical errors and errors in symbolic expressions, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The evaluation of the model is limited, with a small number of ODEs used for testing, which may not sufficiently demonstrate the model's generalizability and robustness.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, such as traditional operator learning methods and other recent deep learning-based models for ODEs, which could have provided a clearer context for the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the model to more complex or high-dimensional ODEs, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's presentation and organization could be improved, particularly in the clarity of figures and the structure of the methodology section, which could enhance the reader's understanding of the model and its components.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the model handles different types of errors, especially numerical errors and errors in symbolic expressions?
- How does the model perform when the number of ODEs used for training and testing is varied? What are the implications for the model's generalizability and performance?
- Can you clarify the process of generating symbolic expressions and the role of the symbol decoder in this process?
- How does the model handle the fusion of different modalities, particularly in terms of weighting the importance of each modality?
- Could you provide more examples or case studies to demonstrate the model's ability to learn and generalize to more complex or real-world ODEs?
- How does the model compare in terms of performance, accuracy, and computational efficiency with other existing methods for learning ODEs from data?
