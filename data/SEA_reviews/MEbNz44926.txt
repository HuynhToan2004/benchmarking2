 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for binarized image super-resolution (SR) using a flexible residual binarization (FRB) technique. This approach involves second-order residual binarization (SRB) and distillation-guided binarization training (DBT) to enhance the representation capacity of binarized image SR networks. The FRB method is applied to both CNN and Transformer-based SR networks, demonstrating superior performance over existing methods. The paper also provides extensive experimental results, showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the method, as it builds upon existing techniques like binarized SR and distillation-guided binarization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a flexible residual binarization (FRB) technique that effectively binarizes full-precision image super-resolution (SR) networks during training.
- The proposed second-order residual binarization (SRB) and distillation-guided binarization training (DBT) are novel and effective in enhancing the representation capacity of binarized image SR networks.
- The paper provides extensive experimental results and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed components.
- The methodology is clearly explained, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on various datasets, showing significant improvements over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and failure modes of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and robustness.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and hardware compatibility.
- The paper does not provide sufficient quantitative results or visual comparisons to clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it builds upon existing techniques like binarized SR and distillation-guided binarization.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational overhead and complexity introduced by the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical deployment on resource-constrained devices.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the limitations and failure modes of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and hardware compatibility with existing methods?
- Can the authors provide more quantitative results or visual comparisons to clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of speedup compared to full-precision models?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the design of the distillation loss and the choice of the hyperparameter β?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training strategy and the choice of the input size in the ablation study?
- Can the authors provide more details on the hardware and software used for the experiments, including the GPU memory and the training time?
