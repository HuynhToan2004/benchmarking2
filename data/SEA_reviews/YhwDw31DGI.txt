 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MIPGen, a novel generative framework designed to generate isomorphic Mixed-Integer Programming (MIP) instances, which are crucial for training machine learning-based frameworks. MIPGen employs a three-stage problem generation process involving instance classification, node splitting and merging, and scalable problem construction. The methodology leverages a bipartite graph representation to capture the complex structures of MIP instances, which are then processed using a Variational Graph Auto-Encoder (VGAE) and a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) for classification and clustering. The paper presents experiments demonstrating that MIPGen can generate instances similar to original problems and enhance the performance of machine learning-based frameworks. However, the paper suffers from unclear presentation, inadequate experimental validation, and a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel generative framework for autonomous MIP instance generation, which is crucial for training machine learning-based frameworks.
- The methodology is well-articulated, providing a clear explanation of the problem and the proposed solution.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method is innovative, leveraging a bipartite graph representation to capture the complex structures of MIP instances, and utilizing a Variational Graph Auto-Encoder (VGAE) and a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) for classification and clustering.
- The paper provides empirical evidence that the generated instances are similar to the original problem instances, which enhances the performance of machine learning-based frameworks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The presentation of the paper is poor, with numerous grammatical errors and unclear figures that hinder understanding.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which is essential for evaluating the effectiveness and novelty of the proposed approach.
- The experimental validation is inadequate, with insufficient details provided about the datasets used, the training and testing processes, and the evaluation metrics.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in generating large-scale MIP instances.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the generated instances with the original problem instances, which is necessary to demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.

- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how do these affect its applicability and effectiveness?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the training and testing processes, and the evaluation metrics used?
