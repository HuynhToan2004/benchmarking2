 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel model for time series prediction under event disturbance, leveraging causal representation learning to address the challenges of out-of-distribution (OOD) problems. The model, named CRP, consists of an encoder and a decoder, with the encoder extracting causal representations from time series data and the decoder using these representations to predict the next time step. The model is designed to handle event-related disturbances by separating causal representations influenced by events from those not influenced. The paper presents experimental results demonstrating the model's superiority over existing methods in terms of prediction accuracy, robustness, and generalization. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in the explanation of the model's architecture and the mathematical formulations used.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in time series prediction under event disturbance, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The authors introduce a novel model, CRP, which effectively addresses the OOD problem by utilizing causal representation learning to extract causal representations that differentiate between those affected and unaffected by events.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear descriptions of the model architecture and the training process, which aids in understanding the methodology.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model, showing improvements in prediction accuracy, robustness, and generalization compared to existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for academic papers in this field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the model's architecture and the mathematical formulations used. This could make it difficult for readers to fully understand the methodology and its implications.
- There are several grammatical and typographical errors throughout the paper, which detract from its professionalism and readability.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed model, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential shortcomings.
- The paper's reliance on specific mathematical formulations and notations (e.g., Eq. 1 and 2) without adequate explanation could confuse readers unfamiliar with the specific mathematical framework used.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made by the model and how these assumptions affect the model's applicability and performance.

**Questions:**

- How does the model handle different types of events, and what are the implications of this on the model's performance?
- Can you clarify the mathematical formulations used, particularly Eq. 1 and 2, and their relationship to the causal representations I and C?
- How does the model ensure that the causal representations extracted are robust to different types of events, and what are the implications if the causal representations are not robust?
- Could you discuss the limitations of the proposed model and how these might affect its applicability and performance in real-world scenarios?
- How does the model handle different types of time series data, such as those with varying frequencies or different data distributions?
- Can you provide more details on the computational requirements of the model, especially in terms of training and inference times, and how these compare to other state-of-the-art methods?
