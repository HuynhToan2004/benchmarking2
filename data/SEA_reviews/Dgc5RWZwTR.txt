 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multi-task learning framework for combinatorial optimization problems (COPs) using multi-armed bandits (MAB) to manage task selection. It proposes a method for decomposing the loss function and constructing an influence matrix to guide the selection process, aiming to improve the training efficiency and generalization across different COPs. The framework is evaluated against various baselines, including single-task learning and multi-task learning methods, and the results show that it can achieve comparable or superior performance with fewer training epochs. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in the presentation and the empirical evaluation, which is seen as not sufficiently robust to support the claims made.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using multi-armed bandits for task selection in multi-task learning, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The proposed method is theoretically grounded, with a detailed analysis of the influence matrix and its implications for task selection and performance.
- The paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive evaluation against various baselines, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of multi-task learning for combinatorial optimization problems is a promising area of research, and the paper contributes to this field by proposing a novel approach that could potentially improve training efficiency and generalization.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental results, which enhances its readability and comprehensibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, particularly in the description of the proposed method and the experimental setup, which makes it difficult to understand the key contributions and the rationale behind the choices made.
- The evaluation of the proposed method is limited to a small number of tasks, which raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art approaches in multi-task learning, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The theoretical analysis of the influence matrix and its implications for task selection and performance is not sufficiently detailed or intuitive, which could limit the understanding of the proposed method.
- The paper does not address the limitations of its approach, which could have provided a more balanced view of the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the proposed method works, particularly in the context of multi-task learning and the use of multi-armed bandits for task selection?
- How does the proposed method compare with state-of-the-art approaches in multi-task learning, and what are the key advantages or disadvantages compared to these methods?
- What are the specific advantages of using the influence matrix for task selection, and how does it compare with other methods of task selection in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the tasks were selected and how the results were validated across different instances of the tasks?
- How does the proposed method handle the challenge of balancing the training budget across different tasks, and what are the implications of this balance for the overall performance of the system?
