 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a large-scale, high-quality dataset of 9,262 three-dimensional computed tomography (CT) volumes with per-voxel annotations of 25 anatomical structures and seven types of tumors, named ImageNetCT-9K. This dataset, along with pre-trained models, is aimed at enhancing the performance of segmentation tasks in medical imaging. The paper also explores the benefits of supervised pre-training over self-supervised methods and demonstrates the transferability of these models to various segmentation tasks. The dataset and models, including a suite of supervised pre-trained models (SUPreM), are made publicly available, aiming to advance research in medical imaging and potentially reduce the need for manual annotations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear figures and tables that effectively illustrate the main points.
- The authors have made significant efforts to ensure the quality of the dataset by involving expert radiologists in the annotation process, which is a crucial aspect of medical imaging research.
- The creation of a large-scale, high-quality dataset of 9,262 three-dimensional computed tomography (CT) volumes with per-voxel annotations of 25 anatomical structures and seven types of tumors is a substantial contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods, demonstrating the superiority of supervised pre-training over self-supervised methods in terms of data and computational efficiency, and transfer learning ability.
- The paper also highlights the potential for reducing annotation costs by fine-tuning models on limited annotated data, which is a significant practical application.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the dataset, such as the potential for biases due to the involvement of only three expert radiologists in the annotation process.
- There is a lack of comparison with other datasets and models, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the findings to other medical imaging modalities, such as MRI, which could be a significant limitation given the variety of imaging technologies used in clinical settings.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the potential societal impacts of the research, which is a crucial aspect of ethical considerations in medical imaging research.
- The paper does not include the code for the experiments, which could limit the reproducibility of the results.

**Questions:**
- How were the images selected for inclusion in the dataset, and what were the criteria used to ensure their quality and relevance to the research question?
- Can the authors provide more details on the annotation process, such as the inter-rater reliability and the process of resolving discrepancies between annotations?
- How does the dataset compare in terms of annotation quality and diversity to other publicly available datasets, and what steps were taken to ensure the dataset's generalizability to other medical imaging modalities?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential societal impacts of their research, particularly in terms of the ethical considerations and the implications for patient privacy and data security?
- How does the paper address the limitations of the dataset, such as the potential for biases and the generalizability of the findings to other medical imaging modalities?
