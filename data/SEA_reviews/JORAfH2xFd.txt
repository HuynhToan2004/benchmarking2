 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the stability of generative models retrained on mixed datasets containing both real and synthetic data. It provides theoretical and empirical evidence to support the stability of iterative retraining under certain conditions, such as the model being well-initialized and the proportion of real data being substantial. The authors demonstrate that when retraining involves a mixture of synthetic and real data, the model can avoid collapse and maintain stability. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the practical deployment of generative models in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex theoretical concepts accessible.
- The authors provide both theoretical and empirical evidence to support the stability of iterative retraining, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper addresses a timely and relevant topic, focusing on the impact of retraining generative models on mixed datasets, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of these models in practical applications.
- The theoretical results are robust and provide a comprehensive understanding of the conditions under which generative models can be retrained without collapse.
- The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the stability of retraining under various conditions, including different fractions of synthetic data.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the theoretical framework, which is well-supported by empirical evidence.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of how they might be applied in real-world scenarios.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the local strong convexity of the Hessian, are quite strong and not clearly justified, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the assumptions made, which could be seen as a significant drawback.
- There is a lack of discussion on the relationship between the theoretical results and existing literature, which could help to contextualize the contributions of the paper.
- The experimental results, while interesting, do not convincingly demonstrate the stability of retraining under all conditions, particularly when the fraction of synthetic data is high.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods and a more comprehensive discussion on the practical implications of the findings.

**Questions:**
- How do the authors justify the strong assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the local strong convexity of the Hessian?
- Could the authors provide more detailed practical implications of the theoretical results, perhaps through examples or case studies?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of their assumptions, and what are the implications of these limitations for the generalizability of the results?
- What are the key takeaways from the empirical results, and how do they compare with the theoretical predictions?
- How do the authors envision the application of their findings in practical scenarios, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in implementing these findings?
- Could the authors elaborate on the relationship between their results and existing literature, particularly in terms of how their contributions build on or differ from previous work?
