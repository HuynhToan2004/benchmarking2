 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the use of large language models (LLMs) as post-hoc explainers for other predictive models, specifically focusing on in-context learning (ICL) strategies. It presents four distinct prompting strategies to generate explanations, comparing LLM-generated explanations with those from state-of-the-art post-hoc explainers. The experiments are conducted on four datasets, demonstrating that LLMs can generate explanations that are competitive with traditional methods, albeit with varying effectiveness across different models and datasets. The paper also discusses the potential of LLMs as explainers, highlighting their ability to mimic the behavior of existing post-hoc explainers. However, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the findings to more complex models and datasets, and the need for a broader evaluation across different types of models and datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The study of LLMs as post-hoc explainers is a significant and under-explored area, which the paper addresses effectively.
- The paper introduces a novel framework for evaluating the effectiveness of LLMs as post-hoc explainers, providing a new perspective on explainable AI (XAI) research.
- The empirical studies presented are robust and well-executed, demonstrating the potential of LLMs in generating explanations that are on par with existing methods.
- The paper's findings are significant, indicating that LLMs can effectively mimic the behavior of post-hoc explainers, which is a promising avenue for future research in XAI.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's findings are limited to simpler models and datasets, raising concerns about the generalizability of the results to more complex models and datasets.
- The evaluation metrics used in the study are not clearly defined or explained, making it difficult to assess the faithfulness of the explanations generated by the LLMs.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the study, such as the potential for overfitting to the datasets used and the lack of evaluation on more complex models like deep neural networks.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of LLMs over traditional post-hoc explainers are not convincingly supported, and the comparisons are not sufficiently fair or comprehensive.
- The paper's focus on tabular datasets may not adequately represent the complexity and diversity of real-world data, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the evaluation metrics used in the study, particularly how faithfulness is measured and what constitutes a faithful explanation?
- How does the paper address the potential for overfitting to the datasets used in the experiments?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of datasets and models for the experiments, and discuss the implications of using simpler models and datasets for the generalizability of the findings?
- How do the LLM explanations compare to explanations generated by the models themselves, and what are the implications of using LLMs as post-hoc explainers versus explanations from the models themselves?
- Can you discuss the potential societal implications of using LLMs as post-hoc explainers, particularly in terms of trust and accountability in AI systems?
- How do the authors envision the integration of LLMs into existing explainable AI frameworks, and what are the challenges and opportunities in this area?
