 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for detecting out-of-distribution (OOD) data in Transformer models by leveraging the smoothness of intermediate layer transformations. The method, termed BLOOD, quantifies smoothness using the square of the Frobenius norm of the Jacobian matrix, providing an unbiased estimator to mitigate computational limitations. The authors demonstrate that BLOOD outperforms other state-of-the-art methods in text classification tasks, particularly in detecting background shifts. The method is white-box and does not require access to training data, making it practical for real-world applications. The paper also discusses the differences in smoothness between in-distribution and OOD data and provides empirical evidence to support these claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of the methodology and results, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is novel, leveraging the smoothness of intermediate layer transformations in Transformer models for OOD detection, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method does not require access to training data, making it practical for real-world applications where such data is not available.
- The paper provides empirical evidence that the method outperforms other state-of-the-art methods in text classification tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The authors have analyzed the differences in smoothness between in-distribution and OOD data, providing insights into how these differences are utilized for OOD detection.
- The paper is organized well, with clear sections and subsections that guide the reader through the content.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with open-box methods, which could have provided a more complete evaluation of the proposed method.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to text classification tasks, which may not fully demonstrate its applicability across different domains.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- There is a lack of discussion on the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to large-scale models or different domains.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the method, especially in relation to the assumptions made about the smoothness of transformations in ID data.
- Some typographical errors and minor issues with clarity in the presentation of results need to be addressed.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancy in performance between the proposed method and other baselines, particularly in terms of the effectiveness of the Jacobian-based measure?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of datasets, such as image datasets, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the method, particularly in terms of its scalability to larger models or different domains?
- What are the specific assumptions made about the smoothness of transformations in ID data, and how do these assumptions affect the applicability of the method in different scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the method and provide suggestions for future research to address these limitations?
- In the context of the results presented, could the authors elaborate on the differences in performance between different datasets and models, and how these differences might affect the generalizability of the findings?
