 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the issue of vanishing gradients in reinforcement fine-tuning (RFT) of language models, particularly focusing on small reward standard deviations. The authors demonstrate that when the reward standard deviation is small, the expected gradient for an input sequence vanishes, which can hinder the optimization process. They propose that an initial supervised fine-tuning (SFT) phase can help alleviate this issue by reducing the number of samples with small reward standard deviation. The paper includes theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support these findings, showing that SFT can significantly improve RFT performance. The authors also discuss the implications of these findings for the design of reinforcement learning algorithms and the practical application of RFT in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear and well-written exposition of the problem of vanishing gradients in reinforcement fine-tuning (RFT) and the role of reward standard deviation in this phenomenon.
- The theoretical analysis and empirical evidence presented are convincing, and the paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow.
- The paper introduces a novel perspective on the issue of vanishing gradients in RFT, focusing on the reward standard deviation, which is an interesting and novel approach.
- The proposed solution of using an initial supervised fine-tuning (SFT) phase is well-supported by empirical evidence and is shown to be effective in reducing the number of samples with small reward standard deviation.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of related work and positions the current study well within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its findings from previous works, particularly those that have also discussed the impact of reward standard deviation on gradient vanishing.
- The empirical evidence provided is limited to specific datasets, and the generalizability of the findings to other datasets or model architectures is not clear.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed solution, such as the additional computational costs and data requirements associated with SFT.
- The theoretical analysis and empirical evidence are not always clearly linked, and the paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on how the theoretical insights inform the empirical findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the implications of the findings for the design of reinforcement learning algorithms and the practical application of RFT in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the findings in this paper and previous works that have discussed similar issues related to reward standard deviation and gradient vanishing?
- How does the proposed solution of using SFT compare to other potential solutions, such as those based on reward shaping or alternative optimization algorithms?
- Could the authors provide more detailed discussions on how the theoretical insights inform the empirical findings, particularly in the context of the controlled experiments?
- How generalizable are the findings to other datasets or model architectures, and what are the implications of these findings for the broader application of RFT?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of their findings for the design of reinforcement learning algorithms and the practical application of RFT in real-world scenarios?
