 **Summary:**
The paper explores the efficient approximation of non-arithmetic activation functions using polynomials for private inference on Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) systems. The authors propose a layerwise optimization approach using a weighted least squares method to minimize the Mean Squared Error (MSE) and a dynamic programming algorithm to determine the optimal polynomial degrees for each layer. This method aims to reduce the inference time while maintaining accuracy by considering the characteristics of each layer. The paper also introduces a moduli-chain management technique to optimize bootstrapping time. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation to only two network architectures (ResNet-20 and ResNet-32) and a single dataset (CIFAR-10), which raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed method.
- The use of a weighted least squares method to approximate activation functions and a dynamic programming algorithm for layerwise optimization are innovative and effective in reducing inference time.
- The proposed method achieves significant reductions in inference time for ResNet-20 and ResNet-32 models, demonstrating a substantial improvement over previous methods.
- The paper provides a theoretical foundation for the proposed method, which is supported by empirical results showing improvements in inference time.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of inference time and accuracy.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to only two network architectures (ResNet-20 and ResNet-32) and a single dataset (CIFAR-10), which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the potential limitations or challenges in applying the proposed method to larger or more complex datasets, which could affect its practical applicability.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational overhead and the impact of the proposed method on the training time of pre-trained models.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the security implications of the proposed method, particularly concerning the trade-offs between security and performance.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a discussion on the trade-offs between inference time and accuracy?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger or more complex datasets, such as ImageNet?
- What is the impact of the proposed method on the training time of pre-trained models, and how does this compare to the benefits in terms of inference time?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations or challenges in applying the proposed method to different types of neural network architectures, such as transformer-based models?
- How does the proposed method affect the security of the encrypted data, and what are the potential risks or vulnerabilities introduced by the use of polynomial approximations?
