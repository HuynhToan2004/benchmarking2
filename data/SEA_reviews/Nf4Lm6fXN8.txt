 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Replay across Experiments (RaE), a method for reusing experience from previous experiments to enhance exploration and bootstrap learning in reinforcement learning (RL). RaE allows for the reuse of data from prior experiments, which can improve performance and reduce the need for extensive changes in the RL workflow. The method is evaluated across various RL algorithms and domains, including locomotion and manipulation tasks, and is shown to be effective in improving exploration and reducing training times. Despite its simplicity, the paper's contributions are seen as incremental, with the main focus on empirical demonstrations rather than significant algorithmic innovations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a straightforward methodology.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, and the experiments are comprehensive, covering a variety of domains and tasks.
- The results demonstrate that the method can improve exploration and reduce training times, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with baselines and includes an ablation study, which helps in understanding the method's effectiveness and limitations.
- The paper's presentation is clear and concise, making it accessible to a broad audience, and it includes code which enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as the method primarily reuses existing data without significant algorithmic innovation.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the method, such as its applicability to different types of RL algorithms and its impact on training efficiency.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to a few domains and tasks, which may not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness across a broader range of scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the work.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the experimental results, particularly in terms of how the method performs in different scenarios and how it compares to other methods.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of RL algorithms, such as those that do not use experience replay?
- Can the method be adapted to work with different types of data, such as video data, and how might this affect its performance?

- What are the specific advantages of using RaE over other methods that reuse data, such as those that use expert data or data from previous experiments?
- How does the method perform when applied to tasks with different reward structures, such as those with sparse rewards versus those with dense rewards?
- What are the implications of using RaE for training efficiency, and how does it compare to other methods in terms of computational resources and training time?
