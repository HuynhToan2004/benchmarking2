 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel active learning algorithm, ELSA, designed to enhance the efficiency of labeling in large datasets dominated by negative samples. ELSA leverages a combination of nearest neighbor search and random sampling to identify positive samples in the latent space of a self-supervised learning model. The algorithm's effectiveness is evaluated using the ImageNet dataset, demonstrating improvements in labeling efficiency and discovery rate compared to baseline methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper suffers from significant issues in clarity, writing style, and presentation, which hinder understanding and assessment of its contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to active learning, focusing on identifying positive samples in the latent space of a self-supervised learning model, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is clearly outlined, with a detailed description of the algorithm and its components, which aids in understanding the proposed approach.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of active learning, particularly in the context of large datasets dominated by negative samples.
- The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in terms of labeling efficiency and discovery rate, as shown in Table 2 and Figure 3.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance, including a comparison with baseline methods and a detailed discussion on the algorithm's components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from significant issues in clarity and presentation, with poor writing style and inconsistent use of terminology, which makes it difficult to follow and understand.
- The related work section lacks depth and does not adequately situate the paper within the existing literature, which could lead to misunderstandings about the novelty and contributions of the work.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with insufficient detail on the experimental conditions and a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method to larger datasets or different types of data, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- There are numerous typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which detract from the professional quality of the submission.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies in terminology and notation used throughout the paper, particularly in the definitions of sets and parameters?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of efficiency and effectiveness with other active learning methods, such as those based on contrastive learning or different self-supervised learning models?
- Can the authors provide more detailed information on the experimental setup, including the specific conditions under which the experiments were conducted and the statistical significance of the results?
- How does the proposed method scale to larger datasets or different types of data, such as text or audio data?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations and negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability and ethical considerations?
