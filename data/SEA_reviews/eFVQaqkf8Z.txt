 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to binary classification, termed "equality separation," which challenges the traditional halfspace separator by using a different indicator function. This method, supported by theoretical underpinnings such as the VC dimension, is applied to anomaly detection, demonstrating its effectiveness in both synthetic and real-world datasets. The authors propose a new activation function, "bump," which is used to approximate the equality separator, and this is integrated into neural network pipelines. The paper also discusses the concept of "closing numbers," a quantitative measure of the capacity of classifiers to form closed decision regions, which is crucial for anomaly detection. The experimental results show that the proposed method can detect both seen and unseen anomalies, although it does not consistently outperform existing methods in all scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a novel approach to binary classification that challenges traditional methods.
- The introduction of the equality separator and the concept of "closing numbers" is innovative and provides a theoretical framework for understanding the expressivity of the proposed method.
- The method is supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results, demonstrating its effectiveness in both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The paper is well-motivated, with clear explanations of the theoretical underpinnings and the experimental results.
- The proposed method is simple and has the potential to be integrated into neural network pipelines, making it practical for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the VC dimension and the theoretical analysis of the expressivity of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not consistently better than existing methods, and the paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications and limitations of the proposed method, especially in terms of its scalability and applicability in different scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the definitions and explanations of certain terms and concepts.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive experiments, including comparisons with a wider range of baseline methods and in different scenarios to better demonstrate the robustness and effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the VC dimension and the theoretical analysis of the expressivity of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data is highly imbalanced or where the anomalies are not well-represented in the training data?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and explanations of certain terms and concepts, such as "closing numbers" and the "globality" or "locality" of classifiers?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art anomaly detection methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results and analysis on the experiments, including the training and test AUPRs for all methods, and a more detailed comparison of the performance of the proposed method in different scenarios?
