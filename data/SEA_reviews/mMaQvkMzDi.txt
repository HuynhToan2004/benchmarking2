 **Summary:**
This paper evaluates various large multimodal models (LMMs) using a novel evaluation framework, focusing on five aspects: hallucinations, abstention, compositionality, explainability, and instruction following. The study reveals that while these models have strengths in areas like instruction following and explainability, they also exhibit significant flaws such as hallucinations and lack of compositional understanding. To address these issues, the paper introduces new in-context learning (ICL) techniques, including Multitask-ICL, Chain-of-Hindsight-ICL, and Self-Correcting-ICL. These methods aim to improve the models' performance in areas like hallucinations and abstention, although the paper acknowledges that they do not fully solve all the identified issues.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a thorough and systematic evaluation of large multimodal models (LMMs), focusing on their limitations and potential improvements through in-context learning (ICL).
- It introduces novel ICL techniques like Multitask-ICL, Chain-of-Hindsight-ICL, and Self-Correcting-ICL, which are promising approaches to addressing specific flaws in LMMs.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed experimental setups and results that are reproducible.
- It provides valuable insights into the current capabilities and limitations of LMMs, which are crucial for understanding and advancing the field.
- The proposed ICL variants are simple yet effective, and the paper includes comprehensive ablation studies to demonstrate their impact.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the ICL methods, particularly how they affect the performance of LMMs.
- The evaluation of LMMs is limited to a few tasks, which might not fully represent their capabilities across different modalities.
- There is a lack of comparison with other models like BLIP2, which could provide a broader perspective on the performance of LMMs.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the models it evaluates, which could be a significant concern for their deployment.
- The paper's focus on a few models (IDEFICS-9B and IDEFICS-9B) limits the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a need for more detailed analysis on how the models perform under different conditions, such as varying numbers of shots in the ICL method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the ICL methods affect the performance of LMMs, particularly in terms of potential negative impacts?
- How do the proposed ICL variants compare to other methods in terms of efficiency and effectiveness?
- Would it be possible to include a broader range of models in the evaluation to enhance the generalizability of the findings?
- How do the models perform under different conditions, such as varying numbers of shots in the ICL method?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the models handle different types of questions, especially those that are more complex or require deeper reasoning?
- What are the specific societal impacts of the models, and how are these addressed in the paper?
