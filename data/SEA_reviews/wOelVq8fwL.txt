 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Learning through Communication (LTC) framework, which enables large language models (LLMs) to adapt to new tasks and environments through iterative interactions with their environments and other agents. The LTC framework is evaluated across four datasets, showing improvements over instruction-tuning baselines in various tasks. It proposes a novel training paradigm that integrates reinforcement learning (RL) with LLMs, allowing for flexible communication patterns tailored to different tasks. Despite its innovative approach, concerns are raised about the clarity of the methodology, the choice of baselines, and the overall novelty of the approach compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to training LLMs, focusing on the exploration and updating phases, which are crucial for adapting LLMs to new tasks and environments.
- The proposed method, Learning through Communication (LTC), is evaluated on four datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness across different scenarios.
- The LTC framework is designed to be flexible, with various communication patterns tailored for single-agent and multi-agent environments, which enhances its applicability.
- The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The empirical results show that the LTC approach outperforms instruction-tuning baselines, highlighting its effectiveness in enhancing LLM agents' capabilities.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the training methodology, particularly how the LLM is trained and how the communication patterns are designed for different tasks.
- There is a significant omission of relevant baselines, such as Reflexion and DPO, which could have provided a more robust comparison.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The methodology's novelty is questionable as similar approaches have been previously explored, such as using LLMs for RLHF and iterative training for LLMs.
- The paper suffers from unclear definitions and potential errors in terminology and notation, which could confuse readers and detract from the paper's credibility.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently detailed, and the choice of baselines seems biased towards instruction-tuning methods, which may not accurately reflect the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the LLM is trained in the LTC framework, particularly in the exploration and updating phases?
- How are the communication patterns designed for different tasks, and what considerations are made to ensure their effectiveness?
- Why was GPT-4 chosen as the agent for exploring the environments, and how does this choice impact the performance and generalizability of the results?
- Can you provide more details on the computational cost of the LTC approach, especially in comparison to other methods like instruction tuning?
- How does the LTC approach compare to other methods that use LLMs for RLHF, such as Reflexion and DPO?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of baselines and why certain methods were not included, such as Reflexion and DPO?
- In the experimental results, could you include more detailed metrics, such as mean and standard deviation, to better assess the robustness of the results?
