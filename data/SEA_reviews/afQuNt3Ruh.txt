 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called "shuffle coding" for compressing unordered data, particularly focusing on graph data structures. It extends previous work on entropy coding for ordered data by integrating the concept of unordered data structures, leveraging the automorphism groups of graphs to reduce redundancy in data representation. The method is shown to achieve state-of-the-art compression rates across various datasets, including molecular and social network graphs. The paper also provides theoretical insights into the optimization of entropy coding for unordered data and presents experimental results that demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of unordered data compression, which is crucial for handling large datasets effectively.
- The proposed shuffle coding method is a novel and innovative approach that extends previous work on entropy coding for ordered data to unordered data, specifically focusing on graph data structures.
- The methodology is supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental validation, demonstrating its practical applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations that facilitate understanding of the complex concepts involved.
- The authors have released the source code and datasets used in the experiments, which will benefit the community by enabling further research and application of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on lossless compression, which may not be the most practical approach for all types of data, especially when considering the trade-offs between compression ratio and computational efficiency.
- The methodology relies heavily on the assumption of a fixed size for the unordered data, which may not hold true for all types of data, such as graphs with varying sizes.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to specific datasets, which may not be representative of a broader range of graph structures.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other recent state-of-the-art methods, which could help in understanding the relative performance of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the potential of using more sophisticated probabilistic generative models to improve compression rates, which could be a significant limitation for some applications.
- The paper does not address the limitations of the method, such as the potential for approximation errors in computing canonical orderings and the computational complexity of the method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method performs with different types of graph structures, especially those with varying sizes?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent state-of-the-art methods in terms of compression efficiency and computational complexity?
- Is there a potential for extending the method to handle other types of unordered data beyond graph structures?
- How does the proposed method handle the initial bits cost, which is a significant issue in practical applications?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the method and discuss potential future directions for research to address these limitations?
