 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel decoding framework, DAEMON, which optimizes the decoding distribution to match human texts across multiple aspects simultaneously. This is achieved by formulating the decoding problem as an optimization problem and providing an analytical solution using a sequence-level energy function. The framework, DAEMON, is shown to improve perplexity on human texts and aligns better with human evaluations across various metrics, including repetition, coherence, diversity, and information content. The method is evaluated against several baselines and demonstrates superior performance in both automatic and human evaluations. The authors also provide theoretical insights into the framework's effectiveness and discuss its implications for future research in text generation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, DAEMON, which formulates the decoding problem as an optimization problem, providing a novel approach to aligning generated texts with human texts.
- The method is supported by a theoretical analysis that demonstrates its effectiveness in improving perplexity on human texts, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- Extensive experiments are conducted, demonstrating the method's effectiveness across multiple domains and model scales.
- The proposed method is shown to outperform existing baselines in both automatic and human evaluations, indicating its practical applicability and potential impact.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of their method, which is a commendable effort in transparency and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of text generation tasks and the potential trade-offs between different evaluation metrics.
- The method's reliance on a set of metrics for evaluation raises concerns about its generalizability and applicability to other types of text generation tasks.
- There is a lack of discussion on the impact of different metrics on the final results and the potential for optimizing a single metric at the expense of others.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of its computational cost and practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of its computational efficiency and practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when optimizing for a single metric, and what are the implications for the overall quality of the generated text?
- Can the authors provide more details on the convergence behavior of the method and the sensitivity of the results to different initialization points?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of text generation tasks, such as summarization or machine translation?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of different metrics on the final results and provide more insights into the trade-offs between different aspects of text generation?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational cost and practical applicability to other existing methods, particularly in terms of its scalability and efficiency?
