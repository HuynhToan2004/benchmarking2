 **Summary:**
The paper introduces InfoBatch, a dynamic data pruning method aimed at reducing training costs by discarding less informative samples and adjusting the gradient expectations to maintain unbiasedness. The method involves randomly pruning a portion of samples based on their loss distribution and rescaling the gradients of the remaining samples to approximate the original gradient. This approach is evaluated across various datasets and architectures, showing promising results in terms of training efficiency and lossless performance. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings and practical implications of the method, including its compatibility with other acceleration techniques and its application across different tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined problem.
- The proposed method is simple, efficient, and effective, as demonstrated through extensive experiments and ablation studies.
- The method is compatible with other acceleration techniques and can be applied to different tasks, including classification, segmentation, vision pertaining, and instruction finetuning.
- The theoretical analysis provided supports the claims made about the method's effectiveness and its compatibility with other techniques.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, and includes detailed experimental settings and supplementary materials.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods in terms of training time and performance, which could affect the assessment of the method's effectiveness.
- The method's reliance on the loss distribution for pruning could limit its applicability in scenarios where the loss distribution does not accurately reflect the importance of samples.
- The method's compatibility with other techniques, such as mixed-precision training and augmentation/regularization methods, is not adequately discussed, which could affect the practical implementation of the method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method comprehensively, which could affect the understanding of its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- The paper's claims of lossless performance are not fully supported by the experimental results, which could mislead readers about the method's actual performance.
- The method's impact on the model's generalization and its applicability to different datasets and architectures are not thoroughly discussed, which could limit the understanding of its practical utility.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in terms of training time and performance across different datasets and architectures?
- How does the method perform when combined with other acceleration techniques, such as mixed-precision training and augmentation/regularization methods?
- Could the authors clarify the method's performance in scenarios where the loss distribution does not accurately reflect the importance of samples?
- How does the method handle the potential bias introduced by the pruning process, particularly in terms of its impact on the model's generalization?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and results, particularly in the appendices, to enhance the understanding and reproducibility of the results?
