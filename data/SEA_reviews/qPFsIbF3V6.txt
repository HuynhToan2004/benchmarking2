 **Summary:**
The paper introduces "Guess & Sketch," a novel neurosymbolic approach to assembly code transpilation that integrates a large language model (LLM) with a symbolic solver. This methodology leverages the LLM to generate initial translations, identify potential errors, and extract aligned subsequences, which are then refined by the symbolic solver. The approach is evaluated against a variety of baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling complex program translations. The paper also presents a new benchmark dataset for assembly code transpilation and discusses the method's potential for improving the translation of low-level code. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the paper's presentation, the complexity of the method, and the generalizability of the results beyond the specific use case.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel neurosymbolic approach to assembly code transpilation, combining the strengths of large language models (LLMs) and symbolic solvers to tackle the complexities of program translation.
- The methodology is clearly articulated, with detailed explanations of the "Guess" and "Sketch" phases, and the paper includes a comprehensive evaluation against a variety of baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a complex topic like assembly code transpilation.
- The introduction of a new benchmark dataset for assembly code transpilation is a significant contribution, providing a valuable resource for future research in this area.
- The paper presents a novel approach to assembly code transpilation, which is a challenging and important problem in software engineering.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from clearer explanations and visual aids to help readers understand the methodology and the results more easily. For example, a high-level visualization of the method's components and their interactions could enhance understanding.
- The complexity of the method, involving multiple stages and components, might make it difficult for readers to grasp fully without additional explanations or simplifications.
- The paper's scope is somewhat limited to assembly code transpilation, which might not be generalizable to other programming languages or types of code translations.
- There are concerns about the scalability of the approach, particularly in handling larger and more complex programs, which could be addressed by expanding the evaluation to include more diverse and complex programs.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the results beyond the specific use case of assembly code transpilation, which could limit the perceived impact and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to clarify how the method handles the alignment and correction of subsequences, especially in complex or ambiguous cases?
- How does the method handle the potential for instruction hallucinations in the LLM output, and what measures are in place to ensure the accuracy and correctness of the final translations?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of parameters and settings in the LLM and the symbolic solver, and how these affect the performance and outcomes of the transpilation process?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of programming languages or code translations, and what modifications or adaptations might be necessary to apply the approach to these different contexts?
- Can the authors discuss potential extensions or modifications of the method to address the scalability issues and expand the evaluation to include more diverse and complex programs?
