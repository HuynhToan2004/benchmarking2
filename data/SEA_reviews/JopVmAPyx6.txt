 **Summary:**
The paper explores the impact of in-context learning on the representation of Transformers, specifically focusing on the interplay between in-context and in-weights components. It introduces a synthetic dataset based on the Shapes3D dataset, which allows for controlled experiments on the difficulty of learning in-context components. The paper presents a theoretical framework and empirical evidence to support the notion that in-context learning is influenced by both the in-context and in-weights components, with the latter being critical for effective in-context learning. The authors propose a method to improve in-context learning by synergistically training both components, which is supported by both empirical and theoretical analyses.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel synthetic task that allows for controlled experiments on the difficulty of learning in-context components, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis to support their findings, which is a strong point in validating their claims.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making the complex concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The proposed method of synergistically training the in-weight and in-context components is innovative and could be a valuable contribution to the field.
- The study of in-context learning and its impact on representation is an interesting and under-explored area, which the paper addresses effectively.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's motivation and the necessity of studying in-context learning are not clearly articulated, which could limit the understanding and relevance of the findings.
- The theoretical analysis, particularly Proposition 5.3, is not well-explained, and the assumptions made are not clearly justified.
- The experimental setup and the choice of parameters are not adequately detailed, which could affect the reproducibility and the generalizability of the results.
- The paper's claims about the in-weights component being overlooked in traditional training methods are not substantiated with sufficient empirical evidence.
- The experimental results are not convincingly presented, and the paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method against other state-of-the-art techniques.
- The paper's writing needs improvement, particularly in terms of clarity and coherence, which could affect the readability and the comprehension of the content.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the motivation behind studying in-context learning and how this study contributes to the broader understanding of this phenomenon?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art techniques in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the theoretical assumptions and their implications for the practical application of the findings?
- How were the parameters for the probe model determined, and what is the rationale behind the choice of these parameters?
- In light of the experimental results, how do the authors justify the claims made about the in-weights component being overlooked in traditional training methods?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the Shapes3D dataset for the synthetic task and discuss the implications of this choice for the generalizability of the findings?
