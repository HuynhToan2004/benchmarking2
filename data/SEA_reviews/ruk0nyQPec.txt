 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to language model training by utilizing a two-component system: a parametric model trained on permissively licensed data and a non-parametric component that stores and retrieves data during inference. This system, termed SILHOUETTE, aims to address legal concerns by ensuring that the non-parametric component does not infringe on copyrights. The authors propose using a variety of data from different domains to train the parametric model, which is then augmented with a retrieval component to enhance performance. The paper provides empirical evidence of the model's effectiveness in improving performance on out-of-domain tasks, although it raises concerns about the scalability and practicality of the approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue of using permissively licensed data for training language models, which is crucial for the field given the ongoing debate on data ownership and usage.
- The proposed method of using a combination of parametric and non-parametric models to manage legal risks is innovative and could be a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and detailed empirical results that support the claims made.
- The inclusion of an open license corpus (OLC) is a valuable contribution to the community, providing a large and diverse dataset for further research.
- The methodology, particularly the use of nearest neighbor language models and retrieval in context, is well-motivated and provides a robust empirical evaluation of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implementation and scalability of the approach, particularly concerning the size and diversity of the data required for the non-parametric component to be effective.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the non-parametric component is updated and managed, which could be a significant challenge in practical applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential for data leakage, especially given the use of datasets that have been used in pretraining models, which could affect the fairness of the comparisons made.
- The methodology section could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples, particularly in the retrieval-in-context (RIC) method, which is not clearly explained.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the societal impact of the proposed approach, particularly concerning the potential for misuse or unintended consequences.

**Questions:**
- How is the non-parametric component updated, and what are the implications for data management and scalability?
- Could you provide more details on the data selection process for the open license corpus, particularly how the selection criteria were determined and how the data was validated?
- How do you ensure that the non-parametric component does not introduce biases or unfair advantages in downstream tasks?
- Given the potential for data leakage, how do you ensure that the comparisons made in your experiments are fair and unbiased?
- How does the proposed approach handle data from different domains, particularly those with significant differences in content or style?
- Could you discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed approach, particularly concerning the use of high-risk data and the potential for misuse or unintended consequences?
