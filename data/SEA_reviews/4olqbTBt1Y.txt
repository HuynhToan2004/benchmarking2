 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DREAM, a novel method for open-set graph domain adaptation, which addresses the challenges of identifying and classifying target graphs from new categories under domain shift and label scarcity. DREAM utilizes a dual structured exploration approach, integrating a graph-level representation learning branch and a subgraph-enhanced branch, to explore graph topological structures from both global and local perspectives. The method is evaluated on six datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness compared to state-of-the-art approaches. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental scope, the lack of detailed implementation and dataset descriptions, and the absence of a discussion on limitations and societal impact.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and challenging problem of open-set graph domain adaptation, which is a significant and less explored area in graph domain adaptation.
- The proposed DREAM method is technically sound and innovative, utilizing a dual structured exploration with mixup to enhance graph representation learning, which is particularly effective in handling graph-level and subgraph-level representations.
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation and easy-to-follow content, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results are promising, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to graph domain adaptation, which is of interest to the community and addresses a significant gap in the current literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the datasets used, including their characteristics, sizes, and specific details, which could hinder the reproducibility and understanding of the results.
- There is a notable absence of a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical implementation.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, focusing on a small number of datasets and not adequately comparing the proposed method with existing state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the claims of its superiority.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the societal impact of the proposed method, which is essential for ensuring the ethical and responsible use of the technology.
- There are several minor typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed descriptions of the datasets used, including their characteristics, sizes, and specific details?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational complexity and practical implementation?
- What are the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how do these impact its applicability and effectiveness?
- Can the authors discuss the societal impact of the proposed method and provide guidelines for its responsible and ethical use?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of semantic drift in traditional graph domain adaptation techniques?
- In the context of the open-set graph domain adaptation problem, how does the proposed method ensure the accuracy and reliability of the pseudo-labels generated from the two branches?
