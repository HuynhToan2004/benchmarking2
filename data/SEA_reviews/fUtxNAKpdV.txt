 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel model named NOUGAT, designed to convert rasterized document pages into structured text, particularly focusing on scientific research papers. This model, which is trained on a newly created dataset, utilizes a transformer-based encoder-decoder architecture and is capable of handling complex mathematical expressions and other scientific notations found in academic documents. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments that show it outperforms existing OCR tools like GROBID in certain metrics. Despite its strengths, the paper acknowledges limitations such as potential repetition issues and the need for further optimization and generalization to non-scientific documents.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to converting rasterized document pages into structured text, which is a significant advancement in the field of document processing.
- The model is capable of handling complex mathematical expressions and other scientific notations found in academic documents, which is a notable achievement.
- The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology, including the creation of a new dataset specifically for this task.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, demonstrating the model's effectiveness in various scenarios, and the results are convincing.
- The model's architecture is based on the Donut model, which is known for its efficiency, and it utilizes a transformer-based encoder-decoder architecture, enhancing its capability to handle complex document layouts and textual content.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the model's performance, including comparisons with existing tools like GROBID, which shows the model's superiority in certain metrics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of repetition in the model's output, which could lead to inaccurate results and misinterpretation of data.
- The model's performance on non-scientific documents, such as those in other languages or with different layouts, is not thoroughly tested or discussed, which could limit its practical applicability.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential societal impacts of the model, which could be significant given its potential use in various industries and applications.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the model's limitations, particularly in handling complex layouts and textual content, which could affect its generalization to other types of documents.
- The evaluation metrics used in the paper are not well-explained, which could make it difficult for readers to fully understand the model's performance and the significance of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalization of the model to other types of documents, which could limit its practical utility.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the model's performance on non-scientific documents and discuss any potential strategies for adapting the model to handle a wider range of document types?
- How does the model handle different languages and scripts beyond Latin-based languages?
- What are the specific reasons for the observed repetition issues in the model's output, and how might these be addressed in future versions of the model?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples of the model's output, particularly where it may fail or produce inaccurate results, to help clarify its limitations?
- How does the model handle different layouts and textual content beyond what is typically found in scientific documents?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the model's limitations and potential areas for improvement, particularly in terms of generalization and practical applicability?
