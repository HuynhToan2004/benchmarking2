 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for solving inverse problems in image reconstruction using a latent diffusion model. It proposes a severity encoder that predicts the degradation severity of noisy images in the latent space of an autoencoder, which is then used to adaptively adjust the sampling trajectory in the diffusion process. This approach allows for sample-adaptive inference times and potentially reduces computational costs. The method is tested on various datasets, including CelebA-HQ and FFHQ, showing competitive performance against existing diffusion-based methods. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited experimental scope, particularly in terms of dataset diversity and the generalizability of the severity encoder to different degradation types.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method that uses a severity encoder to estimate the degradation severity of noisy images in the latent space of an autoencoder, which is a significant contribution to the field of image reconstruction.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear figures and detailed experimental results, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The paper demonstrates competitive performance against existing diffusion-based methods, showing that the proposed method can achieve state-of-the-art results in some cases.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results, which enhances its readability and comprehensibility.
- The use of a severity encoder and adaptive sampling in the diffusion process is a novel approach that has the potential to improve efficiency and performance in image reconstruction tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the severity encoder, particularly in how it generalizes to different degradation types and how it performs under varying noise levels.
- The experimental setup is limited to a single dataset (CelebA-HQ), which may not adequately demonstrate the method's effectiveness across different image types and noise conditions.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other existing methods, such as CCDF, which could provide a more robust evaluation of its performance.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the methodology and the description of certain experimental setups, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as its dependence on the accuracy of the severity encoder and its performance under varying noise levels and forward model mismatches.

**Questions:**
- How does the severity encoder generalize to different degradation types, and how does it perform under varying noise levels?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and testing of the severity encoder, including the size of the dataset used and the specifics of the training procedure?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods, such as CCDF, in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more examples of the predicted severity scores and the corresponding reconstruction results to better illustrate the method's capabilities?
- How does the proposed method perform under varying noise levels and forward model mismatches, and what are the implications of these conditions on the method's effectiveness?
