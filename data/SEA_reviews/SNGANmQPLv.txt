 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Unified Multimodal Instruction Tuning (UMIT), a framework designed to enhance in-context learning capabilities for multimodal vision and language models. By integrating diverse visual instruction datasets into a unified multimodal instruction format, UMIT aims to improve the model's ability to focus on task-specific information within in-context exemplars. This framework is evaluated across various vision-language tasks, demonstrating significant improvements over existing formats. Key components include a task definition component, enhanced exemplar sampling, and a unified instruction format. The paper is commended for its clear writing and comprehensive evaluation, though concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach and the need for more detailed comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-understand language, and a clear structure that aids in comprehension.
- The proposed method is intuitive and straightforward, with a focus on unifying multimodal instruction formats, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experimental setup is comprehensive, covering a wide range of vision-language tasks, and the results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the impact of different components in multimodal instruction formats on in-context learning ability, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The proposed UMIT framework shows promising results in enhancing in-context learning capabilities, as evidenced by significant improvements over existing formats.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The novelty of the approach is limited as the idea of unifying instruction formats has been previously explored in similar works.
- There is a lack of detailed comparative analysis with existing methods, particularly in terms of the number of tasks used and the specifics of the data collection and processing methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the discrepancies between the pretraining and fine-tuning stages, which could affect the efficiency of instruction tuning.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and testing methodologies, such as the specifics of the data selection process and the impact of different training parameters on the results.
- There are some inconsistencies and typographical errors in the paper, which could detract from its professionalism and clarity.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and testing methodologies, particularly how the data is selected and how it impacts the results?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of the number of tasks used and the specifics of the data collection and processing methods?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies between the pretraining and fine-tuning stages, and how these are addressed in the proposed method?
- In the context of the task definition component, how does the paper ensure that the task descriptions are comprehensive and accurate, especially when generated by an Oracle model?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for data contamination during the data collection and processing stages, and what steps are taken to mitigate this risk?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the zero-shot performance of the proposed method, especially in comparison to existing methods?
