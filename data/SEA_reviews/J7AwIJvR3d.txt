 **Summary:**
The paper explores the discrepancies between human brain responses and language model (LM) predictions, focusing on three specific phenomena: emotional understanding, figurative language processing, and physical commonsense. The authors employ a dataset of Magnetoencephalography (MEG) responses to a written narrative, fine-tuning LMs on datasets related to these phenomena to observe improvements in alignment with human brain responses. The study demonstrates that fine-tuning LMs can enhance their performance in predicting brain activity, suggesting that the observed divergences between LMs and human brains may be due to inadequate representation of these specific types of knowledge in LMs. Despite these findings, the paper is criticized for its limited scope in terms of the number of participants and the generalizability of its results, as well as the potential for overfitting and the lack of novelty in its methodology.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and interesting question about the differences between LMs and human brains, which is an area of ongoing research in both neuroscience and natural language processing.
- The study is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical structure that makes it accessible to readers.
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of the data, including a comprehensive literature review and a detailed discussion of the results, which are supported by a robust methodology.
- The paper includes a novel approach to identifying the differences between LMs and human brains by using a dataset of MEG responses to a written narrative and fine-tuning LMs on datasets related to specific phenomena.
- The results of the study are insightful and provide a new perspective on the alignment of LMs with human brain responses, which could be useful for improving the performance of LMs in predicting brain activity.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader exploration of the differences between LMs and human brains, focusing only on three specific phenomena. This limited scope may not fully capture the complexity of the differences between these two systems.
- The dataset used in the study is relatively small, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a concern about the potential for overfitting due to the small dataset size and the use of a single dataset for fine-tuning.
- The methodology for identifying the differences between LMs and human brains is not novel, as similar approaches have been used in previous studies.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of novelty, as the findings may not be surprising given the known limitations of current LMs.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of human brain responses over LMs in certain tasks (e.g., emotion and figurative language processing) are not convincingly supported by the data.
- The study does not adequately address the potential confounding factors, such as the impact of linguistic cues on the results.

**Questions:**

- How do the results of this study compare to previous studies that have also explored the differences between LMs and human brains?
- Given the potential for overfitting due to the small dataset size, how do you ensure that the findings are not merely an artifact of the data used?
- Could you discuss the implications of the study for the design and development of future LMs, particularly in terms of how to better align them with human brain responses?
- How do you address the issue of novelty in your study, especially given that similar approaches have been used in previous research?
- Can you clarify the methodology used for identifying the differences between LMs and human brains, particularly in terms of how you control for confounding factors such as linguistic cues?
