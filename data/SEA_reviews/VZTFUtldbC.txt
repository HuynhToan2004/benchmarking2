 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MeMo, a method for learning modular controllers for robots, which involves training a master controller and separate modules for each substructure using a hierarchical architecture. The method uses an information bottleneck to ensure that the modules do not rely too heavily on the master controller, thereby improving transferability and generalization. The approach is evaluated in both locomotion and grasping environments, showing that it outperforms existing methods in terms of generalization and sample efficiency. However, the method's applicability to more complex robots and its comparison with other baselines are questioned, with concerns about the clarity of the experimental setup and the novelty of the approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The idea of modular controllers is innovative and addresses a significant challenge in robotics by enabling the transfer of control policies across different robot morphologies.
- The experimental setup is well-designed, with clear comparisons to existing methods and a detailed ablation study that supports the claims made.
- The use of an information bottleneck to encourage modules to learn independent representations is a novel approach that has been shown to be effective in improving training efficiency.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the method, including structure and task transfer experiments, which demonstrate the method's effectiveness in improving training efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the advantages of using MeMo over other methods, especially in terms of generalization and transferability.
- The method's applicability to more complex robots is not demonstrated, which limits its practical utility.
- The experimental setup and comparisons are not always fair or clearly explained, which could lead to confusion about the method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method to more complex robot morphologies or the impact of varying robot sizes on the method's performance.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the method, which could be crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the advantages of using MeMo over other methods, especially in terms of generalization and transferability?
- How does the method perform when transferred to robots with significantly different morphologies, such as robots with fewer or more modules?
- Can you provide more details on the training time and computational resources required for the proposed method?
- How does the method handle varying robot sizes, and what are the implications of this for the method's performance?
- In the experimental setup, how does the method compare to other baselines, especially in terms of training time and performance metrics?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the robot morphologies used and the training conditions for the baselines?
