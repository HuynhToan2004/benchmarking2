 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the Chinese Spelling Correction (CSC) task, focusing on the quality of training data and its impact on model performance. It compares two common data augmentation methods: random replacement and OCR/ASR-based generation, highlighting the calibration issues of the latter. The authors propose a method to refine the OCR/ASR-based corpus by filtering data based on the confidence of the CSC model trained on random replacement data. This approach aims to improve the model's calibration and reduce over-correction. The paper presents a theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method on several benchmarks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and under-explored issue in Chinese Spelling Correction (CSC), focusing on the quality of training data and its impact on model performance.
- It provides a clear and detailed theoretical analysis on how different data augmentation methods affect model confidence, which is crucial for understanding the data-model interaction.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes comprehensive empirical evaluations that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, with significant improvements in F1 scores over state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper includes a theoretical analysis of model confidence from a Bayesian inference perspective, which is a novel approach in this context.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to other languages or tasks beyond CSC.
- The methodology and its comparison to existing methods are not clearly explained, making it difficult to understand the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not provide sufficient empirical evidence to support the claims made about the calibration of models trained on different types of data.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the methodology, including the definitions and explanations of key terms and equations.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the findings to other datasets or tasks, and the experimental setup is limited to specific datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with other state-of-the-art methods and a more detailed analysis of the impact of different data augmentation strategies on model performance.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other languages or tasks beyond CSC?
- Can the authors clarify the methodology used in the theoretical analysis, particularly the definition and role of the confusion set in the data augmentation process?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of calibration and over-correction rates?
- What are the specific impacts of different data augmentation strategies on the model's performance, and how does the proposed method address these issues?
- Could the authors provide more detailed empirical evidence to support the claims made about the calibration of models trained on different types of data?
- How does the proposed method handle the generalization of the findings to other datasets or tasks, and what steps were taken to ensure the robustness of the results?
