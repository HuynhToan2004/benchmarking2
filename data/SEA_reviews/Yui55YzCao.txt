 **Summary:**
The paper introduces NewtonNet, a spectral GNN designed to learn spectral filters based on the homophily ratio of the graph. The method utilizes Newton interpolation to approximate polynomial spectral filters and incorporates shape-aware regularization to adapt to varying homophily levels. Theoretical and empirical analyses are provided to support the effectiveness of the proposed method, demonstrating its ability to handle both homophilous and heterophilous graphs. The paper claims that low-frequency components are beneficial for homophilous graphs, while high-frequency components are crucial for heterophilous graphs, which is supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear theoretical analysis of the relationship between graph frequencies and homophily ratios, which is supported by both theoretical proofs and empirical results.
- The proposed NewtonNet method shows significant improvements in performance compared to other methods, especially in scenarios with weak supervision.
- The methodology is innovative, utilizing shape-aware regularization to adapt to the homophily ratio, which is a novel approach in spectral GNNs.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and easy-to-follow proofs, making the content accessible and understandable.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in both homophilous and heterophilous graphs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other methods, particularly those that also use shape-aware regularization or similar techniques.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with existing methods, including a discussion on how the proposed method differs from and improves upon these existing approaches.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the limitations and potential drawbacks of the proposed method, including its computational complexity and scalability.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved by adding more figures and visual aids to enhance the understanding of complex concepts and results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of graphs and the potential negative societal impacts.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use shape-aware regularization or similar techniques?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform on different types of graphs, such as directed or weighted graphs?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated?
- How does the proposed method handle the problem of overfitting, especially in scenarios with limited training data?
- In the experiments, how are the hyperparameters chosen, and what impact do these choices have on the performance of the method?
