 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of meta-out-of-context learning (meta-OCL) in large language models (LLMs) and neural networks, focusing on how models internalize and utilize information from out-of-context data. This includes experiments on a synthetic dataset, showing that LLMs can learn to associate variables with entities based on definitions, even when these definitions are not contextually relevant. The study also explores the implications of meta-OCL in various settings, including synthetic and real-world data, and discusses potential mechanisms such as gradient alignment and selective retrieval. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in defining and measuring meta-OCL, and for not sufficiently differentiating it from other forms of learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and provides a thorough examination of the phenomenon of meta-OCL, which is an intriguing aspect of LLMs.
- The experiments are extensive, covering a wide range of settings and model sizes, and the results are clearly presented, making the paper easy to follow.
- The paper is significant in its exploration of how LLMs internalize and utilize information from out-of-context data, which is an important area for understanding the capabilities and limitations of these models.
- The paper includes a detailed discussion on the implications of meta-OCL for future AI systems, highlighting both potential benefits and risks.
- The experiments are conducted on both synthetic and real-world data, providing a robust validation of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining and measuring meta-OCL, making it difficult to distinguish from other forms of learning and to evaluate its effectiveness.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate between in-context and out-of-context learning, which are crucial for understanding the phenomenon.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly demonstrated to be generalizable to real-world scenarios, as the experiments are primarily conducted on synthetic data.
- The paper does not adequately address how the findings relate to other forms of learning, such as in-context learning, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the phenomenon.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of meta-OCL, which is an important consideration for the ethical implications of the research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how meta-OCL is differentiated from other forms of learning, especially in-context learning?
- How does the paper address the potential negative societal impacts of meta-OCL, particularly in terms of model bias and misinformation?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or experimental setups that demonstrate the generalizability of the findings to real-world scenarios?
- How does the paper address the potential for LLMs to internalize and utilize information from out-of-context data in a way that could be harmful or misleading?
- Could the authors provide more formal definitions and metrics for measuring meta-OCL, to better evaluate the effectiveness and generalizability of the phenomenon?
