 **Summary:**
The paper explores the reasoning process in large language models (LLMs) by employing counterfactual examples (CEs) to identify key attention heads that contribute to the reasoning ability of LLMs. It introduces a methodology called Word-of-Interest (WoI) normalization to filter out irrelevant words and focus on those related to specific reasoning tasks. The authors perform path patching experiments using the Qwen-7B and LLaMA2-7B models to understand the impact of these attention heads on the reasoning performance. The paper also discusses the limitations of the methodology, such as the generalizability of the findings and the reliance on a specific model architecture.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using counterfactual examples (CEs) to identify key attention heads that contribute to the reasoning ability of large language models (LLMs), which is a significant advancement in understanding the internal mechanisms of LLMs.
- The use of path patching to explore the reasoning mechanism of LLMs is both innovative and effective, providing insights into the components of LLMs that are crucial for reasoning tasks.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible and understandable to a broader audience.
- The authors conduct comprehensive experiments on various datasets, demonstrating the robustness of their findings across different scenarios.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of large language models, focusing on the interpretability of LLMs, which is a critical area for further research and development.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's reliance on a specific model architecture, such as the decoder-only transformer, may limit the generalizability of the findings to other model architectures like encoder-decoder models.
- The methodology used in the paper, particularly the path patching and WoI normalization, is not entirely novel and has been previously explored in other works.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential limitations of the methodology, such as the generalizability of the findings to other models or tasks, and the potential biases introduced by the use of specific datasets or prompt templates.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the ethical implications of the research, particularly in the context of large language models and their potential societal impacts.
- There are concerns about the reproducibility of the results due to the complexity of the methodology and the lack of detailed descriptions and justifications for certain experimental setups and choices.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition and role of "behavior" as used in the paper, particularly in relation to the input-output pairs and specific tasks or behaviors mentioned?
- How do you address the potential biases introduced by the use of specific datasets or prompt templates, and what steps are taken to ensure the generalizability of the findings to other models or tasks?
- Can you provide more details on the selection process for the reference examples (REs) and counterfactual examples (CEs), and how these choices might affect the results of the experiments?
- How do you ensure the validity and reliability of the results when using the WoI normalization method, particularly in scenarios where the output of the model is not entirely controlled by the attention heads under investigation?
- Could you discuss the potential ethical implications of the research, particularly in the context of large language models and their societal impacts?
- How do you plan to make the code and data available to the research community to promote reproducibility and further exploration of the findings?
