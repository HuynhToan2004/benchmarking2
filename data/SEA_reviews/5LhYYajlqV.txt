 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called In-Context Unlearning (ICUL) for removing training instances from large language models (LLMs) without retraining the model. ICUL works by providing the instance alongside a different label and additional correctly labelled instances as inputs to the LLM at inference time. The method is evaluated on various text classification tasks and is shown to effectively remove specific information from the training set while maintaining performance levels. The paper also discusses the challenges of unlearning in LLMs, including the computational infeasibility of exact unlearning and the need for a new paradigm. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its limited scope to LLMs and the lack of a comprehensive evaluation across diverse tasks and model sizes.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel unlearning paradigm for LLMs, specifically focusing on in-context unlearning, which is a first in the field.
- The method proposed, ICUL, does not require access to model parameters and is effective in removing the influence of training instances, as demonstrated by the results.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method is effective in removing the influence of training instances, as demonstrated by a decrease in adversary's likelihood of classifying forget points as part of the training set.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including an ablation study that shows the impact of different factors on the unlearning process.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's evaluation is limited to text classification tasks and does not include other types of tasks such as summarization or generation, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's effectiveness.
- The paper lacks experiments on larger models, which could demonstrate the scalability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other unlearning methods, such as those that use gradient ascent or those that remove concepts, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the method's effectiveness.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a few datasets and does not include a broader range of datasets, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper lacks a discussion on how the method could be extended to handle multiple instances, which is a common scenario in unlearning applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform on other types of tasks such as summarization or generation?
- How does the method perform on larger models, and what are the potential challenges in scaling the method to these models?
- How does the method compare with other unlearning methods, such as those that use gradient ascent or those that remove concepts?
- Can the method be extended to handle multiple instances, and what are the potential challenges in doing so?
- How does the method handle instances that are not correctly labeled, and what are the potential implications of this on the unlearning process?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of data, such as tabular or image data?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the method could be adapted to handle different types of data, and what are the potential challenges in doing so?
