 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for training video-based robot policies using RGB video demonstrations without explicit action annotations. This approach leverages a text-conditioned video generation model to synthesize video demonstrations, which are then used to infer actions through dense correspondences and optical flow estimation. The method is evaluated on both simulated and real-world robotics tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in various scenarios. The paper also provides an open-source framework for efficient video modeling, which is crucial for training high-fidelity policy models. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its reliance on synthetic data, which may limit its generalization to real-world scenarios, and the clarity of its methodological descriptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to training video-based robot policies using RGB video demonstrations, which is a significant advancement in the field of robot learning.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear figures and detailed descriptions, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The paper includes extensive experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various tasks, including table-top manipulation and navigation.
- The availability of an open-source framework for efficient video modeling is a valuable contribution to the community, facilitating the training of high-fidelity policy models.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, which is crucial for a broad audience in the robotics community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's reliance on synthetic data for training raises concerns about the generalization of the method to real-world scenarios, where data may not be as controlled or predictable.
- There is a lack of clarity in some methodological descriptions, particularly in Section 3.2, which could benefit from additional explanations or examples.
- The paper's approach to learning from video demonstrations is not entirely novel, as similar methods have been previously explored, such as learning from video demonstrations and using optical flow for action inference.
- The paper's evaluation primarily focuses on simulated environments, which may not fully represent the complexity and variability of real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address how it compares to other recent works in the field, such as those mentioned in the related works section, which could provide a more robust context for the evaluation.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its method, particularly in terms of generalization to real-world scenarios and the potential impact of different camera angles and lighting conditions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the methodology for learning from video demonstrations, particularly how it compares to existing methods and how it addresses the challenges of generalization to real-world scenarios?
- How does the paper address the limitations discussed in the weaknesses section, particularly the potential impact of different camera angles and lighting conditions on the method's effectiveness?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and inference efficiency of their method, especially in comparison to other methods like UniPi?
- How does the paper ensure that the learned policies can be effectively deployed on various robots and environments, and what steps are taken to address the potential variability in camera angles and lighting conditions?
- In the context of the replanning strategy, could the authors elaborate on how the decision to replan is made and how it impacts the overall performance of the method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the handling of occlusions and the impact of different object poses on the method's effectiveness?
