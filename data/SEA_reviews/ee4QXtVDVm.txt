 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Subword Embedding from Bytes (SEB), a novel approach to subword embedding that addresses privacy concerns in federated learning by mapping subwords to byte sequences, reducing the number of subwords required to represent each subword. SEB employs a byte-level embedding matrix to aggregate byte representations into subword embeddings, which are then used to maintain the semantic meaning of subwords. The method is evaluated on various NLP tasks including machine translation, sentiment analysis, and language modeling, showing comparable or superior performance to traditional subword embeddings. The paper also explores the trade-offs between privacy and performance, suggesting that SEB could potentially enhance privacy without significantly impacting performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to subword embedding that addresses privacy concerns.
- The introduction of Subword Embedding from Bytes (SEB) is innovative and provides a new perspective on how to handle subword embeddings in federated learning, potentially enhancing privacy and reducing the number of subwords required.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of SEB in various NLP tasks, showing comparable or superior performance to traditional subword embeddings, and highlighting the trade-offs between privacy and performance.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including a comparison with existing methods and a thorough analysis of the impact of hyperparameters on model performance.
- The method is well-motivated and addresses an important issue in privacy-preserving federated learning, which is crucial for the development of secure and efficient language models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly the trade-offs between privacy and performance, and the generalization ability of SEB across different tasks and datasets.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods and a broader range of datasets to better understand the scalability and effectiveness of SEB.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis on the computational overhead and memory requirements of SEB compared to traditional subword embeddings, which could be critical for practical deployment in federated learning scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation of the privacy properties of SEB, particularly in terms of its resistance to different types of attacks and its ability to maintain privacy in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could be improved by including a more detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of privacy and security.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the notation used in the paper, particularly in the complex mathematical expressions, to improve readability and understanding?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of privacy and performance with other existing methods, particularly those that use byte-level embeddings or different tokenization strategies?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead and memory requirements of SEB compared to traditional subword embeddings, and discuss how these trade-offs impact the practical deployment of the method?
- How does the proposed method perform in federated learning scenarios with large-scale data, and what are the implications for privacy and performance in such settings?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of privacy and security, and propose mitigation strategies to address these concerns?
