 **Summary:**
The paper explores the inverse approximation theorem for nonlinear recurrent neural networks (RNNs), extending the "curse of memory" phenomenon from linear to nonlinear settings. It introduces a Bernstein-type theorem to characterize the regularity of target functions that can be stably approximated by RNNs, demonstrating that nonlinear functionals with long-term dependencies must have exponentially decaying memory. The paper also proposes a reparameterization method to address the limitations of RNNs in modeling long-term dependencies. The theoretical analysis is supported by numerical experiments, although the paper's assumptions and the practical implications of the results are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear problem statement and well-defined contributions.
- The paper establishes a Bernstein-type approximation theorem for nonlinear functional sequences through nonlinear RNNs, which characterizes the essential limits of nonlinear RNNs in learning long-term relationship.
- The analysis provides insights into the limitations of RNNs in modeling long-term dependencies, suggesting that an appropriate reparameterization method could alleviate these limitations.
- The paper is technically sound and provides a detailed proof of the inverse approximation theorem for nonlinear recurrent neural networks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The results are clearly presented and supported by numerical experiments, enhancing the paper's credibility and practical relevance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions, such as the boundedness and continuity of the target function, are strong and may limit the general applicability of the results.
- The paper does not provide a clear discussion on the limitations of the proposed reparameterization method, which is a crucial aspect for practical applications.
- The experimental section is weak, with limited scope and lacks detailed comparisons with other methods, which could undermine the paper's claims.
- The paper's contributions are seen as incremental, with the main theorem being a direct extension of previous work, and the reparameterization method not being sufficiently novel or practical.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the results, particularly how the theoretical findings translate into practical improvements in RNN architectures.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify whether the results hold for discrete time RNNs and whether the assumptions about the target function are necessary and reasonable?
- How does the proposed reparameterization method compare to other existing methods in terms of effectiveness and practicality?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons and discussions on the limitations and practical implications of their proposed reparameterization method?
- Is there a possibility to extend the results to other types of RNNs, such as GRUs and LSTMs, and what would be the implications for these models?
- How do the authors address the concerns regarding the incremental nature of their contributions and the practical relevance of their findings?
