 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Merlin, a novel goal-conditioned reinforcement learning (GCRL) method inspired by diffusion modeling. Merlin constructs trajectories that "diffuse away" from potential goals and trains a policy to reverse them, analogous to the score function in diffusion models. This approach allows for the learning of goal-conditioned policies without requiring a value function, which is a significant departure from traditional methods. The methodology includes a novel trajectory stitching technique based on nearest-neighbor search, which enhances the generalization of policies across different goals. The paper validates Merlin on offline goal-reaching control tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness compared to state-of-the-art methods. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the paper, particularly in the explanation of the trajectory stitching operation and the choice of metrics, which could affect the reproducibility and applicability of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations that make it accessible to readers.
- The idea of using diffusion models to solve goal-conditioned reinforcement learning (GCRL) is innovative and interesting, providing a novel perspective on GCRL.
- The paper presents a new trajectory stitching technique based on nearest-neighbor search, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is supported by theoretical justification, and the experiments are comprehensive, showing that Merlin outperforms previous methods.
- The paper provides a novel approach to learning goal-conditioned policies without requiring a value function, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the trajectory stitching operation and the choice of metrics, which could affect the reproducibility and applicability of the method.
- The experiments are limited to simple tasks, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the method to more complex or diverse environments.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the architecture of the policy and the hyperparameters used, which could limit the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the related work, particularly in the area of diffusion-based reinforcement learning, to better contextualize the contributions of the study.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the trajectory stitching operation, particularly how it is performed in practice and how the nearest-neighbor search is implemented?
- How do you choose the distance threshold for the nearest-neighbor search, and what are the implications of this choice on the performance of the method?
- In the experiments, how do you ensure that the dataset is balanced in terms of the number of trajectories for different goals?
- How does the method handle the potential for data contamination due to the use of hindsight relabeling?
- Could you provide a more detailed discussion on the related work, particularly in the area of diffusion-based reinforcement learning, to better contextualize the contributions of the study?
- How does the method perform in environments with continuous state and action spaces, and how does it compare to methods that use learned value functions?
