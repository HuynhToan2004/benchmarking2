 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel learning method called Hard View Selection (HVS) for self-supervised learning (SSL), aimed at improving model performance by selecting harder image views for training. HVS operates by sampling multiple views for each image and selecting the pair with the highest loss during forward passes, which are then used for backpropagation. This method is designed to enhance the robustness of models by exposing them to more challenging samples, thereby potentially improving performance on various downstream tasks. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of HVS through experiments on ImageNet and other datasets, showing improvements over baseline methods. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the approach, as it bears similarities to existing methods like MADAug and AutoAugment.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a simple yet effective approach to improving the performance of self-supervised learning (SSL) methods by selecting harder image views, which is a novel concept.
- The methodology is straightforward and does not require significant changes to existing SSL pipelines, making it easy to implement.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method consistently improves performance across various baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed descriptions of the method and the experiments.
- The empirical analysis provided in the paper is thorough, including a study on the impact of the number of sampled views and the effect of different types of augmentations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as similar methods like MADAug and AutoAugment have been previously proposed, which selects the hardest image pairs for training.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with these existing methods, which could help in understanding the unique contributions of the proposed method.
- The selection of harder image pairs is based on a heuristic approach, which might not be the most effective or efficient method for selecting hard samples.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational overhead introduced by the HVS method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods like MADAug and AutoAugment to highlight the unique contributions of their proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness with these existing methods, especially in large-scale applications?
- Is there a possibility to adapt the HVS method to other SSL methods, such as those that use negative samples or different loss functions?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical application?
- How does the HVS method handle the selection of harder samples in the context of different SSL methods, and what are the implications of this on the overall performance and efficiency of the training process?
