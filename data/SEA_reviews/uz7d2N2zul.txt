 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to federated learning (FL) by integrating Bayesian coreset optimization to improve the efficiency of training on client devices. This methodology allows for the selection of a representative subset of data from each client, reducing the need for extensive data transfer. Theoretical analysis is provided, demonstrating the optimality of the proposed method, and empirical results show improvements over existing methods such as FedAvg and PFedBayes. However, the paper suffers from several issues, including poor writing quality, unclear presentation of methodology, and insufficient theoretical depth. The experimental results are also criticized for being insufficiently robust, with some results showing no improvement over baseline methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an interesting problem by proposing a novel approach to coreset optimization in federated learning (FL), which is a significant area of research.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, offering insights into the optimality of the proposed method.
- The empirical results show improvements over existing methods such as FedAvg and PFedBayes, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-motivated, clearly stating the problem and its potential impact, which is crucial for understanding the relevance and significance of the research.
- The methodology is straightforward and easy to understand, which is beneficial for replication and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from poor writing quality, with numerous grammatical errors and typos that detract from its professionalism and readability.
- The presentation of the methodology is unclear and lacks sufficient detail, particularly in the explanation of key components such as the objective functions and their roles in the optimization process.
- The experimental results are insufficiently robust, with some results showing no improvement over baseline methods, and others being inconclusive due to a lack of statistical significance.
- The paper lacks a clear definition and discussion of the main contributions, which makes it difficult to grasp the novelty and significance of the proposed approach.
- There is a notable absence of comparisons with relevant baselines, such as those that use different coreset selection algorithms or different federated learning algorithms, which limits the ability to assess the effectiveness of the proposed method in diverse settings.
- The paper contains several theoretical errors and unclear definitions, which undermine the credibility and rigor of the theoretical contributions.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and definition of the objective functions mentioned in the paper, particularly Eq. (5) and its relationship to the overall optimization process?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the coreset size in their experiments, and what are the implications of this choice on the performance of the model?
- In the context of the experiments, why was only the accuracy metric reported, and not other metrics such as training time or communication overhead?
- How does the proposed method compare to other federated learning algorithms that also use coreset selection, such as those mentioned in the literature?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the coreset selection was performed and how the results were validated statistically?
- Given the theoretical errors and unclear definitions in the paper, can you provide a more rigorous proof or clarification of the theoretical results, especially those related to the convergence rates and the minimax optimality of the proposed method?
