 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of sampling strategies on the zero-shot generalization (ZSG) capabilities of reinforcement learning (RL) agents, focusing on the mutual information (MI) between the agent's internal representation and the set of training levels. It introduces a novel approach called Self-Supervised Environment Design (SSED) that generates new training levels using a variational autoencoder, aiming to minimize MI while reducing distributional shift. The authors argue that this method improves ZSG by reducing instance overfitting and overgeneralization. The paper is structured around theoretical justifications, empirical validations, and comparisons with existing methods, showcasing the effectiveness of SSED in various environments. However, concerns are raised about the scalability of the method to more complex environments and the practical applicability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for improving zero-shot generalization (ZSG) in reinforcement learning (RL) by reducing the mutual information between the agent's internal representation and the identity of training levels, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The theoretical justification provided by the authors is comprehensive and well-supported by empirical evidence, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various settings.
- The paper is well-written, making complex concepts accessible and understandable, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The experimental results are robust, showing significant improvements over existing methods, which underscores the practical applicability of the proposed approach.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology, including the use of variational autoencoders for level generation, which adds an innovative dimension to the study.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the technical aspects, particularly the role of the variational autoencoder (VAE) in the level generation process and the optimization of the VAE's parameters.
- The generalization of the findings to more complex environments, such as those involving high-dimensional level parameters or different types of environments, is not thoroughly addressed.
- The paper could provide more examples or detailed descriptions of the environments used in the experiments, especially those with more complex level parameters, to enhance the reader's understanding and applicability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the findings, particularly how the proposed method could be applied in real-world scenarios where data collection is costly or time-consuming.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts, which could be crucial for understanding the broader implications of the research.

**Questions:**
- How does the VAE's parameter optimization affect the level generation process, and what are the specific parameters optimized?
- Can the authors provide more examples or detailed descriptions of the environments used in the experiments, especially those with more complex level parameters?
- How does the proposed method perform in environments with higher-dimensional level parameters, and what are the implications of this for the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their work, particularly in terms of data collection and privacy concerns?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use similar approaches, such as data augmentation or ensemble methods?
- What are the specific advantages of the SSED method over other methods that prioritize levels based on their value loss?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the variational autoencoder in the level generation process and how it contributes to the overall effectiveness of the method?
