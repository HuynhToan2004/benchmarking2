 **Summary:**
The paper explores the efficiency of synchronous and asynchronous federated learning (FL) methods through a theoretical formulation that quantifies time and resource usage. It highlights the inefficiencies of asynchronous FL due to stale model updates, which is crucial for understanding the relative performance of these methods. The formulation is evaluated on five datasets, demonstrating that neither method consistently outperforms the other, which could guide FL practitioners in choosing between synchronous and asynchronous FL based on resource constraints. The paper also includes practical improvements such as dataset contribution scaling and bias impact, which enhance the predictive accuracy of the formulation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of federated learning (FL), focusing on the efficiency of synchronous and asynchronous FL methods.
- The theoretical formulation is sound and well-articulated, providing a clear understanding of the time and resource usage in FL.
- The paper includes practical improvements that enhance the predictive accuracy of the formulation, such as dataset contribution scaling and bias impact.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, covering a variety of datasets and FL approaches, and the results are convincingly presented.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The inclusion of a comprehensive related work section helps in situating the research within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on time and resource usage in FL is commendable, but the evaluation of these aspects is limited to end-to-end training time and client-side computational resources. It would be beneficial to include other metrics such as energy consumption and client-side memory usage.
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the limitations of the proposed formulation and its assumptions, which could affect its generalizability and applicability in various FL settings.
- The paper does not adequately address the convergence rate of asynchronous FL, which is crucial for understanding its practical implications.
- The paper's experimental results are not sufficiently convincing, particularly in demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the research, which is a significant omission in the ethical considerations of FL.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed formulation and its assumptions?
- How does the proposed formulation handle the convergence rate of asynchronous FL, and what are its implications for practical FL applications?
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies noted in the experimental results, particularly the low RMSE values observed in the baseline methods?
- How does the proposed formulation perform in scenarios where the client-side computational resources vary significantly, and what are the implications for the overall FL process?
- Could the authors elaborate on the ethical considerations of their research, particularly in the context of FL applications?
