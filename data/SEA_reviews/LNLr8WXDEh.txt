 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the 3D scene understanding capabilities of the Stable Diffusion model by probing its representations through linear classifiers trained on various datasets. The model is tested against other large-scale models such as DINO, CLIP, and VQGAN, using a methodology that involves adding noise to latent representations and extracting features from different layers and time steps. The study reveals that Stable Diffusion performs well in tasks related to scene geometry, support relations, shadows, and depth, but struggles with material and occlusion. The paper's methodology is straightforward, involving a grid search for optimal feature extraction, and the results are presented in a clear and structured manner. However, the paper's contributions are seen as limited, and the methodology's novelty is questioned, as it primarily involves a grid search over model parameters without significant advancement in the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and good presentation.
- The methodology is straightforward, involving a grid search for the best feature extraction, which is applicable to other models.
- The study provides valuable insights into the capabilities of the Stable Diffusion model in understanding 3D scenes, specifically in tasks such as scene geometry, support relations, shadows, and depth.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation of the model's performance across various properties of 3D scenes, which is useful for understanding the model's capabilities and limitations.
- The experimental results are well-presented, with a clear discussion of the model's performance on different properties.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contributions are limited, primarily focusing on the performance of the Stable Diffusion model without significant advancements in the field.
- The methodology is seen as simple and not sufficiently innovative, as it primarily involves a grid search over model parameters without substantial new insights or methodologies.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the model, particularly in tasks where the model performs poorly, such as material and occlusion.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse models for comparison, such as ViT-based models, to better understand the generalization capabilities of the model.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of the work, which is a significant omission in the discussion section.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the performance of the model on the occlusion task, specifically how it handles the grouping of separated parts of an object?
- How does the model perform on more nuanced properties of 3D scenes, such as contact relations and object orientation?
- In the experimental setup, why was a grid search chosen over other methods, and what were the considerations behind this decision?
- How do the results compare when different models are used for the linear SVM classifier, and what are the implications of this choice on the model's performance?
- Could you elaborate on the potential applications of the model in real-world scenarios, and how the findings could be used to enhance existing models or develop new applications?
