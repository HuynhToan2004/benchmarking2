 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel quantization-aware training framework aimed at reducing the distribution mismatch in image super-resolution networks. This framework incorporates variance regularization to regulate the variance in features during training, which is critical for maintaining reconstruction accuracy. Additionally, selective distribution offsets are introduced to address significant mismatches in layer distributions. The methodology is validated through extensive experiments on various super-resolution architectures, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance with reduced computational overhead. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its reliance on existing techniques like variance regularization and channel-wise shifting, which have been previously explored in other contexts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of the proposed method, and includes comprehensive experiments and ablation studies.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with a straightforward and clear description of the training process.
- The paper demonstrates a significant performance gain over previous methods, particularly in the context of image super-resolution, where quantization has been a significant challenge.
- The methodology effectively reduces the distribution mismatch problem, as evidenced by the experimental results which show improved accuracy and reduced computational overhead.
- The paper is original in its approach to addressing the distribution mismatch problem in quantized networks, offering a novel perspective on how to improve the quantization process in SR networks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is limited as it primarily applies existing techniques such as variance regularization and channel-wise shifting to a new domain, which may not be seen as a significant advancement.
- The computational overhead of the proposed method, while minimal, is not negligible, and the paper does not compare the computational efficiency of different components of the method, such as variance regularization and selective distribution offsets.
- The paper's methodology is somewhat heuristic, relying on the selective application of channel-wise shifting and scaling, which may not be optimal or universally applicable across different network architectures.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could include scenarios where the method might not perform optimally or where it may not be applicable.
- There is a need for more rigorous comparisons with other quantization methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy across different network architectures.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency with other quantization methods, especially in scenarios where computational resources are limited?
- Can the proposed method be applied to other domains beyond image super-resolution, such as object detection or segmentation? If so, how would the performance compare?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the distribution mismatch is significant, particularly in layers where the mismatch is severe?
- Could the authors provide more detailed insights into the method's limitations and potential areas for improvement?
- Given the method's reliance on selective channel-wise shifting and scaling, how does the method perform when applied to networks with different architectures, such as those with different numbers of layers or different types of convolutional layers?
