 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Simple-TTS, a novel end-to-end text-to-speech (TTS) system that leverages a latent diffusion model to generate speech directly from text, bypassing the need for complex pipelines or external tools for alignment. The system is designed to be simpler and more efficient than existing methods, utilizing a byte-level language model to capture linguistic properties and a modified diffusion noise schedule to enhance text-speech alignment. The authors claim that Simple-TTS outperforms other open-source TTS systems in terms of intelligibility and similarity to prompts, as evidenced by both automated and human evaluations. The paper also discusses the adaptation of the U-ViT architecture for audio generation and the use of EnCodec for audio compression, enhancing the model's efficiency and performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodologies and results.
- The introduction of a simple yet effective end-to-end TTS system using a diffusion model is a significant contribution to the field, especially in reducing complexity compared to existing methods.
- The use of a byte-level language model, ByT5, instead of BPE models, and the modification of the diffusion noise schedule are innovative approaches that improve the model's performance.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including both automated and human evaluations, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the Simple-TTS system.
- The simplicity of the model and its design make it more accessible and easier to implement compared to other TTS systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art TTS systems, such as NaturalSpeech2 and VoiceBox, which could have provided a clearer understanding of the advantages and limitations of Simple-TTS.
- There is a lack of discussion on the generalizability of the proposed method to other languages or datasets, which is crucial for broader application.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of the technology, which could be significant given the potential for misuse.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the model's performance under different conditions, such as varying the length of the audio clips or the complexity of the text.
- There are some issues with the clarity and organization of the paper, particularly in the presentation of the methodology and the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the EnCodec encoder is used in the model, including its input and output dimensions?
- How does the model handle different accents or dialects, especially given its reliance on a byte-level language model?
- Can the authors clarify the training process for the model, especially regarding the use of clean audio for training and the handling of different speakers?
- Would it be possible to include a comparison of the model's performance with other diffusion models, such as stable diffusion, to better understand the advantages of the proposed method?
- How does the model perform when trained on different datasets, and what are the implications of using different text encoders or noise schedules?
- Can the authors provide more details on the model's generalizability to other languages or dialects, and discuss the potential for adapting the model to different contexts?
