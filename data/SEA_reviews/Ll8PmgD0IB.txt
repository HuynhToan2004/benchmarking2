 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for continual learning, focusing on orthogonal projection to reduce computational complexity. The authors propose a local model space projection (LMSP) method that approximates the low-rank structure of the global representation matrix using anchor points, thereby reducing the complexity of the Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) required for orthogonal projection. This method aims to facilitate forward and backward knowledge transfer, allowing for efficient learning across tasks. Theoretical analysis and empirical results are provided to support the efficacy of the proposed method, which is evaluated on several public datasets. Despite the promising approach, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity in presentation, potential over-reliance on prior works, and limited experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to continual learning by approximating the low-rank structure of the global representation matrix using anchor points, which is a creative and innovative approach.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear definitions and theoretical analysis that support the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The approach is theoretically justified, which adds credibility to the proposed method.
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in continual learning by reducing the computational complexity of orthogonal projection, which is a common requirement in many continual learning methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the definition of key terms and the explanation of certain methodological steps.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some results showing marginal improvements or even decreases in performance compared to existing methods.
- The paper relies heavily on prior works, particularly in the use of definitions and methodologies, which may limit the perceived novelty of the contributions.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and memory usage.
- The paper's assumptions and theoretical claims, such as the convergence rate and the effectiveness of backward knowledge transfer, are not adequately justified or supported by empirical evidence.

**Questions:**

- How does the method perform in scenarios where the number of anchor points is varied?
- Could you provide more details on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the method?
- How does the method handle the trade-off between computational complexity and performance, especially in scenarios where the number of anchor points is increased?
- Can you provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of the assumptions and theoretical claims made in your paper?
