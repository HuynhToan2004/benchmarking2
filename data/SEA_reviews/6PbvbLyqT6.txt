 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel algorithm, Dynamic Discounted CFR (DDCFR), which dynamically adjusts the discounting scheme for Counterfactual Regret Minimization (CFR) algorithms. This approach, formulated as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), enables the algorithm to learn the optimal discounting weights, thereby improving the convergence rate and generalization ability across various games. The algorithm, trained using an evolution strategy, is shown to outperform traditional CFR variants in both training and testing scenarios. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty, as the idea of learning discounting weights dynamically has been previously explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning the discounting weights of CFR algorithms, which is a significant departure from traditional fixed-weight methods.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear definitions and explanations that facilitate understanding.
- The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing CFR variants.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The generalization ability of the learned discounting weights is a notable contribution, as it allows the algorithm to perform well on unseen games.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the specific design of the MDP, which is crucial for understanding the algorithm's effectiveness.
- The novelty of the approach is limited as similar ideas have been previously explored in the context of learning discounting weights for CFR.
- There is a lack of theoretical guarantees for the convergence of the learned discounting weights, which is a significant drawback given the theoretical underpinnings of CFR algorithms.
- The experimental results, while showing improvements, do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods like DCFR.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and a broader range of experimental results to validate its effectiveness across different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the motivation behind the specific design of the MDP and how it contributes to the effectiveness of the algorithm?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing CFR variants that use learned discounting weights, such as those mentioned in related works?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the theoretical guarantees of the convergence of the learned discounting weights?
- In the context of the experimental results, can the authors explain the discrepancies between the results shown and the performance of DCFR in similar scenarios?
- How does the algorithm perform when the number of training games is varied, and what are the implications of this on the generalization ability of the learned discounting weights?
