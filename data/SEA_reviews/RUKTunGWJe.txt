 **Summary:**
The paper introduces INRSteg, a novel steganography framework that employs Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) to hide multiple data types across various modalities such as images, audio, video, and 3D shapes. INRSteg utilizes weight freezing techniques to preserve the weights of secret data while fitting additional weights to the cover data. It also incorporates a permutation encoding mechanism for security. The framework demonstrates state-of-the-art performance in cross-modal steganography and achieves high-quality stego data without altering the original INR. However, concerns about the novelty of the method and the lack of comprehensive comparison with existing methods were raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, INRSteg, which effectively hides multiple data without altering the original INR, ensuring high-quality stego data.
- INRSteg is modal-agnostic and can perform cross-modal steganography for various modalities including image, audio, video, and 3D shapes, achieving state-of-the-art performance compared to previous intra-modal steganographic methods.
- The neural representations of secret data are first concatenated to have independent paths that do not overlap, then weight freezing techniques are applied to the diagonal blocks of the weight matrices for the concatenated network to preserve the weights of secret data.
- INRSteg demonstrates a neural network stacking procedure that enables lossless hiding and revealing of multiple secret data at once.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method shows significant improvement in distortion evaluation, capacity, and security in various experiments, including intra and cross-modal steganography, compared to previous steganography methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the permutation encoding mechanism works and its impact on the security of the method.
- The method's novelty is questionable as it primarily involves concatenating INRs and fitting them to cover data, which might not be considered a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of steganography metrics and performance across different modalities.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of the number of secret data being limited by the number of hidden layers in the INR, which might not be practical for real-world applications.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the method's robustness against various attacks and its ability to handle different types of cover data.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with some results being misleading or inadequately explained.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the process of permutation encoding and its impact on the security of the method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and security with existing methods, particularly in cross-modal steganography?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup and results, particularly in the areas of robustness and practical applicability?
- How does the method handle different types of cover data, and what are the implications for its performance and security?
- Could you elaborate on the method's ability to handle different types of secret data and the impact of this on the method's effectiveness and security?
