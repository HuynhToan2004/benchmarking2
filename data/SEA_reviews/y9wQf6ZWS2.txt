 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Q-learning algorithm named RegQ, which integrates a regularization term to ensure convergence under linear function approximation. Theoretical analysis is provided using a switching system framework, and the algorithm's stability and convergence are demonstrated through experiments in simple environments. The paper claims that RegQ converges faster than existing algorithms and provides a theoretical error bound. However, the experimental scope is limited, and the novelty of the algorithm is questioned due to its resemblance to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and understandable notations and a logical flow of ideas.
- The proposed algorithm, RegQ, is a practical approach for Q-learning with linear function approximation, which is a significant area of interest in the reinforcement learning community.
- The use of a regularization term to enhance convergence is a novel approach that has been validated through both theoretical analysis and empirical results.
- The paper provides a theoretical error bound for the solution where the algorithm converges, which is a useful contribution to the field.
- The convergence analysis of the proposed algorithm is thorough, and the paper is generally easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental results are limited to simple environments, and it would be beneficial to see how the algorithm performs in more complex scenarios such as the Atari suite or other standard benchmarks.
- The novelty of the algorithm is questionable as it closely resembles existing methods, particularly those that use regularization terms to enhance convergence.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithm and does not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential issues of over-regularization, which could lead to slower convergence or suboptimal solutions.
- There are some typographical and formatting errors that need correction to improve the readability and professionalism of the paper.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to existing methods, particularly those that also use regularization terms?
- How does the choice of the regularization parameter affect the performance of the algorithm, and what are the potential risks of over-regularization?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods and a broader range of environments?
- Is there a possibility to extend the theoretical analysis to more complex scenarios, such as nonlinear function approximation?
- Can the authors address the concerns about the limited experimental scope and the potential for over-regularization in their method?
