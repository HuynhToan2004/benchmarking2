 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the reliability of feature visualizations in deep neural networks, questioning their ability to accurately represent the network's behavior on natural inputs. The authors propose two methods to manipulate feature visualizations: a fooling circuit and silent units. These methods aim to demonstrate that feature visualizations can be misleading, as they often respond differently to natural inputs and feature visualizations. The paper also introduces a sanity check to evaluate the similarity between natural and visualized inputs, suggesting that feature visualizations may not provide a reliable explanation of how neural networks process natural images. The theoretical analysis supports these findings by showing that feature visualizations are unreliable for understanding neural network behavior unless strong assumptions are made about the network's structure.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a timely and important question about the reliability of feature visualizations in deep neural networks, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of these models.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating that feature visualizations can be manipulated to show arbitrary patterns, which casts doubt on their ability to explain neural network behavior on natural inputs.
- The theoretical analysis is solid and supports the empirical findings, providing a deeper understanding of the limitations of feature visualizations.
- The paper introduces a sanity check for feature visualizations, which is a useful tool for assessing the reliability of these visualizations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the fooling circuit and silent units, particularly in terms of their applicability to different network architectures and the potential for generalization.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational costs associated with the proposed methods, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- There is a need for more detailed explanations and justifications for the experimental setups and methodologies used, particularly in the comparison of natural and visualized inputs.
- The paper does not sufficiently explore the implications of the findings for other types of interpretability methods, such as saliency maps and attribution methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the implications of the findings for the development of more reliable feature visualizations and interpretability methods in general.

**Questions:**
- How can the findings of this paper be used to develop more reliable feature visualizations or interpretability methods in general?
- Can the fooling circuit and silent units be adapted to other network architectures, and how might this affect their effectiveness?
- Is there a possibility to generalize the findings of this paper to other types of interpretability methods, such as saliency maps and attribution methods?
- How do the results of this paper relate to the broader implications of interpretability in machine learning, particularly in terms of trust and reliability in AI systems?
- Could the authors provide more detailed justifications for the experimental setups and methodologies used in the paper, particularly in the comparison of natural and visualized inputs?
