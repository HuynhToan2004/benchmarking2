 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the expressiveness of different context-based fine-tuning methods, focusing on prompting and prefix-tuning, and their capabilities in eliciting skills from pretrained models. It theoretically demonstrates that these methods, while potentially less expressive than full fine-tuning, can effectively utilize the pretrained model's skills without altering the attention patterns. The paper also discusses the limitations of these methods, such as their inability to learn new tasks from scratch and their reliance on the pretrained model's existing capabilities. The findings are supported by empirical evidence, including experiments on simple tasks and toy models, which illustrate the theoretical concepts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex theoretical concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- It provides a comprehensive review of context-based fine-tuning methods, including prompting, soft prompting, prefix-tuning, and in-context learning, and discusses their limitations and potential applications.
- The theoretical analysis is sound, and the paper offers a novel perspective on the limitations and capabilities of these methods, which could guide future research and practical applications.
- The experiments conducted are well-designed, and the results are convincing, supporting the theoretical claims made in the paper.
- The paper offers valuable insights into the practical implications of these methods, such as their potential for eliciting skills from pretrained models and their limitations in learning new tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical contributions are somewhat limited, as the findings are largely expected and do not offer significant new insights beyond what is already known.
- The empirical evidence provided is somewhat limited and does not convincingly demonstrate the generalizability of the findings across different models or tasks.
- The paper's claims about the expressiveness of different fine-tuning methods are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence, which could weaken the paper's argument.
- There are some typographical errors and inconsistencies in the paper that need to be corrected for clarity and accuracy.
- The paper's scope is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on autoregressive language models and not addressing other types of models or tasks, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the distinction between prefix-tuning and suffix-tuning, and how this affects the expressiveness and capabilities of the fine-tuning methods?
- How does the paper's theoretical analysis hold up under more complex or varied conditions, such as different model architectures or training datasets?
- Could the authors provide more detailed empirical evidence to support their claims about the expressiveness and capabilities of different fine-tuning methods?
- What are the specific implications of the findings for practical applications, such as in-context learning or prompt engineering?
- How do the authors address the potential for catastrophic forgetting or other negative effects in models that are fine-tuned using these methods?
- Could the paper benefit from a broader scope or more comprehensive empirical validation to strengthen its claims and contributions to the field?
