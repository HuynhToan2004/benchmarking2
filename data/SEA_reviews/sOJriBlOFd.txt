 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for generating high-framerate human motion using a two-stage process. The first stage involves learning a latent representation of motion using a variational INR, which is optimized for different framerates. The second stage utilizes a diffusion model to generate motion from this latent representation, enabling conditional generation of motion based on various conditions. The methodology is designed to handle mixed-framerate data and to generate motion at arbitrary framerates, which is a significant advantage over existing methods that typically require fixed framerates. The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method across various datasets and tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency compared to existing approaches.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to handle motion data with different framerates, which is a significant challenge in the field of motion synthesis.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with a clear motivation and a logical structure that makes it easy to follow.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in handling various framerates and generating high-quality motion sequences.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The use of INRs to represent motion data is innovative and provides a new perspective on handling motion data.
- The method's ability to generate motion at arbitrary framerates is a notable advantage over existing methods that typically require fixed framerates.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The comparison with existing methods is somewhat limited, particularly in terms of high-framerate motion synthesis, which is a critical aspect of the study.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the explanation of certain methodological choices and the role of specific components like the codebook-coordinate attention.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed visualizations and comparisons to better illustrate the advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of high computational costs associated with handling high-framerate motion data.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, particularly in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the proposed method handle motion data with very low framerates, which could be challenging due to the limited information available?
- Could the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the codebook-coordinate attention in the methodology?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of generating motion at arbitrary framerates, especially in scenarios where the framerate is significantly different from the training data?
- Could the authors provide more detailed visualizations and comparisons to better illustrate the advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches?
- How does the method handle the potential issues of overfitting or underfitting when optimizing the latent representation?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of the framerate on the quality and diversity of the generated motion, especially in scenarios where the framerate is very high or very low?
