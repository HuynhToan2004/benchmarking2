 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Siamese Masked Interaction Learning (SMILE) framework for Audio-Visual Speech Recognition (AVSR), leveraging masked representation modeling and mix-attention encoder layers to enhance interactions between audio and visual modalities. The framework aims to capture the complementary relationship between these modalities, facilitating single-modal and cross-modal interactions. Extensive experiments on the LRS2 and LRS3 datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing state-of-the-art performance in both low-resource and high-resource settings. Despite its strengths in performance and novelty, the paper has been critiqued for its limited technical contribution and the need for more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical structure that effectively presents the main contributions.
- The proposed method is innovative, utilizing a Siamese architecture with masked representation modeling and mix-attention encoder layers to enhance interactions between audio and visual modalities.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on the LRS2 and LRS3 datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in both low-resource and high-resource settings.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the model's performance and the impact of different components, including ablation studies that show the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-motivated, with clear explanations of the rationale behind the proposed method and its contributions to the field of audio-visual speech recognition.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of the size and complexity of the models used.
- There is a limited discussion on the technical contributions of the proposed method, which could be seen as a simple extension of existing methods like masked image modeling.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for overfitting due to the use of a large number of parameters and the need for more extensive data for training.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of the results, including a discussion on the generalizability of the findings and the impact of different model configurations on the performance.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need correction to enhance the professionalism of the paper.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of model size and complexity?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other audio-visual speech recognition datasets, such as the VoxCeleb2 dataset?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the impact of different masking strategies on the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method handle the temporal correspondence between audio and visual modalities, and what are the implications of this alignment for the overall performance of the model?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the dynamic scaling factor in the model's performance?
- What are the specific challenges and limitations of the proposed method, and how do these compare to the challenges and limitations of existing methods in the field?
