 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Bayesian optimization framework, LBN-MOBO, designed to handle large-batch, multi-objective optimization problems. This framework employs a neural network-based surrogate model and a customized acquisition function that utilizes NSGA-II for Pareto front approximation. The methodology is evaluated on real-world problems, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency compared to existing methods. The paper also includes a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential future improvements, such as the handling of constraints and noisy data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and under-explored problem in Bayesian optimization (BO), focusing on large-batch, multi-objective optimization, which is crucial for various engineering and scientific applications.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments, including comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of their proposed method in handling large datasets and achieving better results in fewer iterations.
- The paper is well-structured, providing a clear motivation and detailed explanation of the methodology, including the use of neural network surrogates and a novel acquisition function.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed method can achieve a dense Pareto front and is efficient in handling large batches of data.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in handling constraints and noisy data, which are critical aspects of real-world optimization problems.
- The choice of benchmarks and their relevance to the proposed method is questionable. The paper could benefit from using more relevant benchmarks, such as those from the literature on multi-objective Bayesian optimization.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for large-batch optimization.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability in different scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, which could help in understanding its relative performance and advantages.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed method, especially concerning its convergence properties and computational complexity.
