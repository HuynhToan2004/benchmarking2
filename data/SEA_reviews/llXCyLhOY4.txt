 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the issue of off-policy bias in multi-step goal-conditioned reinforcement learning (GCRL), specifically focusing on two types of biases: shooting and shifting. The authors propose a method that utilizes quantile regression to estimate the upper quantile of the target value distribution, which is claimed to reduce both biases. Additionally, they introduce a truncation technique to manage the shifting bias. The paper provides empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness through experiments on various tasks, demonstrating improvements over existing methods like MHER and HER. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the mathematical definitions and the experimental results, which do not consistently outperform existing methods across all tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of goal-conditioned reinforcement learning, focusing on the issue of off-policy bias in multi-step learning.
- The authors provide a clear and well-structured explanation of the problem and propose a novel method to address it, which is supported by theoretical and empirical evidence.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the problem, proposed solutions, and experimental results.
- The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method can improve the performance of multi-step goal-conditioned reinforcement learning algorithms in terms of both learning efficiency and success rate.
- The authors introduce two types of biases in multi-step GCRL, categorizing them into shooting and shifting biases, which is a novel approach to understanding and addressing biases in this context.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the mathematical definitions and notations, particularly in the equations involving the action-value function and the target value.
- The experimental results do not consistently outperform existing methods, such as MMHER, and the improvements are sometimes marginal or not statistically significant.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where it might not perform as well as other methods.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as similar approaches have been previously used in other contexts, such as multi-step RL and distributional RL.
- The paper's claims about the benefits of off-policy biases are not convincingly supported, and the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method are not sufficiently robust or generalizable.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed method compare to other baseline methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- What are the specific scenarios where the proposed method is expected to underperform, and how can these be avoided or mitigated?
- How does the paper's approach differ from similar methods in multi-step RL and distributional RL, and what are the unique contributions of this work?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the standard deviations and statistical significance of the observed improvements?
- In the context of the proposed method, how is the target value determined, and what are the implications of this determination for the overall effectiveness of the method?
