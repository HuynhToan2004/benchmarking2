 **Summary:**
The paper explores the effectiveness of postprocessing methods in achieving fairness and accuracy in machine learning models, particularly focusing on tabular data. It critiques the overemphasis on pre- and in-processing methods, arguing that these methods may not be as effective as post-processing in terms of fairness and accuracy. The authors conduct extensive experiments using various datasets and models to demonstrate the superiority of post-processing, proposing a new method called "unprocessing" to optimize group-specific thresholds. The study also introduces a new Python package for post-processing, aiming to provide a fairer and more accurate comparison among different models. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited scope to tabular data and its lack of theoretical justification for the observed phenomena.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant issue of fairness in machine learning models, a topic that has been under-explored in recent years.
- The experiments conducted are extensive and rigorous, involving thousands of model evaluations across multiple datasets, which demonstrates the robustness of the findings.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of the "unprocessing" method and the post-processing Pareto frontier is a novel contribution that could be of significant interest to the algorithmic fairness community.
- The paper provides a new perspective on the effectiveness of post-processing in achieving fairness and accuracy, which challenges the common belief that pre- and in-processing are more effective.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is limited to tabular data, which may not generalize well to other types of data such as images or text, thereby limiting its applicability to a broader range of machine learning models.
- There is a lack of theoretical justification for the observed phenomena, particularly the superiority of post-processing over other methods. This lack of theoretical backing weakens the paper's argument.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of its findings, which could include the impact of different datasets, the generalizability of the results to other types of data, and the potential societal implications of the findings.
- The scope of the paper is somewhat narrow, focusing primarily on tabular data and a limited set of datasets, which may not be representative of all real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a deeper analysis of the results, particularly in explaining why post-processing is more effective than other methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of their findings, particularly in terms of the generalizability of their results to other types of data and machine learning models?
- How does the "unprocessing" method compare to other post-processing methods, and what are the advantages of using "unprocessing" over other methods?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the societal implications of their findings, particularly in terms of the potential impact on fairness in real-world applications?
- The paper mentions that the results may not generalize well to other types of data. Could the authors discuss the potential reasons for this and suggest ways to address these limitations in future research?
- In terms of the theoretical justification for the observed phenomena, could the authors provide a more detailed discussion or additional theoretical insights that could support their findings?
