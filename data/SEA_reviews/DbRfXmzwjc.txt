 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MAGNet, a novel graph-based model designed for molecule generation that operates by first generating abstract shapes and then allocating atom and bond types. This approach aims to improve the flexibility and diversity of generated molecules by abstracting motifs into shapes, which can be freely combined to create various structures. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive experiments on multiple datasets, showing improvements in molecular diversity and the ability to generate molecules with more topologically distinct structures. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of detailed methodological explanations and insufficient comparative analysis with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to molecule generation by abstracting motifs into shapes, which allows for more flexible and diverse molecular structures.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the model's design and its implementation, making it easy to understand and replicate.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in terms of molecular diversity and the ability to generate molecules with more topologically distinct structures.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and concise presentation of the research, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.
- The use of a hierarchical generation approach, which first generates a molecule's abstract shape graph before defining its atom-level representation, is innovative and provides a new perspective on molecule generation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed explanations and justifications for some of the methodological choices, such as the selection of the number of shapes and the rationale behind the fragmentation scheme.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparative analysis with existing methods, including a discussion on the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method compared to these approaches.
- The paper's focus on the generation of shapes and their allocation to atoms and bonds is not clearly distinguished from existing methods like MiCaM, which also uses a similar approach.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of the computational resources required for generating large molecules.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential issues with the generalizability of the generated shapes across different datasets and the potential for overfitting due to the use of a large vocabulary of shapes.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the rationale behind the selection of the number of shapes and how this number was determined?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, particularly in generating large molecules?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and the ability to generate molecules with specific properties?
- Can you provide more details on how the method handles the fragmentation of molecules into shapes, and how this process affects the diversity and complexity of the generated molecules?
- How does the method ensure the generalizability of the generated shapes across different datasets, and what measures are taken to prevent overfitting due to the large vocabulary of shapes?
- Could you provide more examples or visualizations to illustrate the effectiveness of the method in generating molecules with diverse structures and properties?
