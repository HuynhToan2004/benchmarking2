 **Summary:**
The paper introduces OpenPatch, a novel method for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection in 3D point clouds, leveraging pre-trained models to extract patch representations for known classes. These representations are used to create a memory bank for reference, which aids in determining the novelty of new samples based on their patch similarity to known classes. The method is evaluated on the 3DOS benchmark, showing promising results against various baselines. However, concerns are raised about the method's novelty, the depth of experimental validation, and the clarity of its presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The method is straightforward, using a pre-trained network to extract patch representations for known classes, which is a practical approach.
- The proposed method is evaluated on the 3DOS benchmark, showing promising results against various baselines, indicating its effectiveness.
- The paper is clearly presented, making it easy to understand and apply the method in practical scenarios.
- The authors provide a clear motivation for their research, highlighting the need for OOD detection in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's novelty is questionable as it appears to be a simple application of existing techniques, such as the use of nearest neighbors for OOD detection.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and performance in various scenarios.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, with limited experiments conducted on different datasets and with different pre-trained models, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational efficiency of the method, which is crucial for real-time applications.
- There are issues with the clarity and presentation of the paper, with some sections being difficult to follow and understand.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which is necessary to establish the method's effectiveness and novelty.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including scenarios where it might not perform optimally?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the known classes are not well-represented in the training data?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods to establish the effectiveness and novelty of their approach?
- Is there a plan to extend the method to handle different types of OOD detection, such as domain shift or style shift, beyond semantic shift?
- How does the method handle the computational efficiency, particularly in real-time applications?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, especially in terms of the computational resources used and the impact of different pre-trained models on the results?
