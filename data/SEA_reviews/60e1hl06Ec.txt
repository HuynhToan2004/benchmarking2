 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the issue of simplicity bias in neural networks, focusing on the use of simpler features that are often spurious and less informative for out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization. It introduces a novel framework called CMID (Conditional Mutual Information Debiasing) which trains a complex model to minimize its conditional mutual information with a simpler model, thereby encouraging the use of more diverse features. This approach is tested on various datasets and shows promising results in improving OOD generalization, subgroup robustness, and fairness. However, the paper is criticized for its reliance on a simplistic definition of simplicity bias and for not sufficiently comparing its method with existing approaches.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of machine learning, specifically the simplicity bias in neural networks.
- The proposed method, CMID, is well-motivated and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The empirical results are strong, showing that CMID outperforms ERM in most cases and is competitive with other state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper includes both theoretical and empirical analyses, providing a comprehensive approach to the problem.
- The method is straightforward to implement and does not require extensive computational resources or complex model architectures.
- The paper includes a variety of experiments and datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across different domains.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the simplicity bias might not be effectively addressed.
- The definition of simplicity bias is too simplistic and does not adequately capture the complexity of the problem, especially in real-world scenarios where the relationship between features and labels can be more complex.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing approaches, which could provide a better understanding of its advantages and disadvantages.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the method, particularly in the definitions and notations used, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalization of the method to other domains or the scalability of the method to larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational cost of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition of simplicity bias and how it is operationalized in the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing approaches in terms of computational cost and scalability?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their method and how it might not be effective in certain scenarios?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the simplicity bias is not the primary issue, such as in datasets with complex relationships between features and labels?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods to better establish the advantages of their proposed method?
- How does the method handle the trade-off between using simple features and complex features, especially in scenarios where the simple features might be more predictive but less informative?
