 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Video Decomposition Prior (VDP) framework, a novel inference-time optimization method that decomposes videos into multiple layers, including RGB and opacity layers, without requiring external data. This framework is applied to various video editing tasks such as video object segmentation, dehazing, and relighting, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance. The VDP framework is designed to be task-agnostic, leveraging the motion and appearance of the input video, and optimizes the convolutional U-Net architecture for each task. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its lack of novelty in the use of a video decomposition prior, unclear writing, and insufficient experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is task-agnostic, demonstrating good performance on various video editing tasks such as video object segmentation, dehazing, and relighting.
- The paper is well-written, providing clear descriptions of the methodology, and the results are impressive, outperforming other methods in the experiments.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to video editing that does not require training on external datasets, which is a significant advantage in terms of data efficiency and flexibility.
- The method is simple, straightforward, and the results are impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and the figures and tables are well-designed, enhancing the understanding of the content.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty in the use of a video decomposition prior, as similar methods have been previously proposed, such as DoubleDIP.
- The writing quality is poor, with several grammatical errors and unclear descriptions, making it difficult to fully understand the methodology and results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of test-time optimization, which is crucial for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental validation, particularly in terms of the generalizability of the method across different types of videos and the robustness of the results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the potential negative societal impacts of its applications.
- The paper does not provide the code, which limits the reproducibility of the results and the ability for other researchers to build upon the work.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method addresses the challenges of test-time optimization more effectively than existing methods?
- How does the method handle different types of videos, especially those with complex motion or lighting conditions?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including quantitative comparisons and ablation studies, to better demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the method perform when applied to videos with different resolutions or frame rates?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and the negative societal impacts of its applications?
- Could the authors provide the code for their method to facilitate reproducibility and further research?
