 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ShareFormer, a novel transformer-based architecture designed for image restoration tasks. The main innovation is the shared attention mechanism (SPSA) that allows for efficient sharing of attention maps across adjacent layers, reducing computational costs and improving inference speed. This mechanism is complemented by residual connections to enhance model trainability. The method was tested on various tasks including super-resolution, denoising, and JPEG compression artifact reduction, demonstrating significant improvements in latency and performance compared to existing methods. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty in the SPSA mechanism, which is similar to existing methods like EDT, and for not sufficiently addressing the trade-off between latency and trainability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the proposed method.
- The proposed method, ShareFormer, achieves good performance in terms of latency, trainability, and performance across various image restoration tasks.
- The paper includes extensive experiments and ablation studies that support the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method's design is simple and efficient, reducing computational complexity by sharing attention maps and using residual connections.
- The paper provides a clear motivation for the research and presents a comprehensive analysis of the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison of the SPSA mechanism with similar existing methods like EDT, which could undermine the perceived novelty of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the trade-off between latency and trainability, which is a significant issue in the design of the method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its efficiency.
- The paper does not compare the method's performance with the latest state-of-the-art methods in some tasks, such as image denoising and JPEG compression artifact reduction.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential future research directions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between SPSA and EDT, particularly in terms of their computational complexity and efficiency?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between latency and trainability, especially in high-resolution image restoration tasks?
- Could the authors provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, including a comparison with existing methods?
- In terms of the experimental results, how does the proposed method compare with the latest state-of-the-art methods in tasks like image denoising and JPEG compression artifact reduction?
- How does the method perform when applied to other image restoration tasks such as image deblurring, deraining, and dehazing?
- Can the authors provide more details on the method's performance under different hardware configurations, such as different GPUs and CPUs?
