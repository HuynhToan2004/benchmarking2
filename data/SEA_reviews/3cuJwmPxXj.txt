 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the problem of intervention extrapolation, focusing on predicting the effects of interventions on an outcome variable, even when those interventions are not observed at training time. It proposes a method called Rep4Ex that leverages identifiable representations to map observed features into a subspace that facilitates nonlinear extrapolation. The method is based on a control function approach and is validated through synthetic experiments. The paper demonstrates that Rep4Ex can predict the effects of unseen interventions effectively, though it relies on strong assumptions such as linearity and full support of the residual when regressing latent features on action variables.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and accessible, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The theoretical results are well-supported, with a clear structure and a logical flow that aids in comprehension.
- The methodology is innovative, particularly the use of identifiable representations for intervention extrapolation, which is a novel approach.
- The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis and empirical validation, which supports the claims made.
- The paper is original in its approach to causal representation learning and intervention extrapolation, offering a fresh perspective on these topics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies on strong assumptions, such as the linearity of the effect of action variables on latent features and the full support of the residual when regressing latent features on action variables. These assumptions may limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The experimental results are limited to synthetic data, which may not adequately represent real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its approach, which could provide a more balanced view of the research.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the method performs in more complex scenarios, such as when the number of action variables is larger than the number of latent variables.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing approaches, which could help in understanding the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when the number of action variables is larger than the number of latent variables?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the action variables are generated and how they influence the latent features?
- How does the method compare with other existing methods for intervention extrapolation, such as those mentioned in the related work section?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their approach and how these might affect the practical applicability of the findings?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle more complex scenarios, such as those involving multiple interventions or non-linear effects?
