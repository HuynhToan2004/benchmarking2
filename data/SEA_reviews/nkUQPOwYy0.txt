 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new attention mechanism, Fast Multipole Attention (FMA), designed to address the quadratic complexity of self-attention in long sequences. FMA employs a hierarchical approach, grouping queries, keys, and values into multiple levels of resolution, which reduces the complexity to O(nlogn) or O(n). The mechanism is evaluated on autoregressive and bidirectional language modeling tasks, showing improvements in memory size and accuracy compared to other efficient transformers. The methodology is inspired by fast summation methods from n-body physics and the Fast Multipole Method, aiming to learn basis functions that compute group quantities, enhancing the model's efficiency and accuracy.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel attention mechanism, Fast Multipole Attention (FMA), which effectively reduces the complexity of self-attention in long sequences, improving memory efficiency and accuracy.
- FMA is evaluated on both autoregressive and bidirectional language modeling tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and versatility.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it easy to follow, and the paper is well-written, with clear and concise descriptions of the proposed FMA.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the approximation error of the attention matrix of FMA, which is crucial for understanding the model's performance.
- The experiments conducted show that FMA outperforms other efficient transformers in terms of memory size and accuracy, demonstrating its practical applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed FMA, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The comparison with other efficient attention variants, such as H-transformer and MRA attention, is not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of the number of levels and the number of summary vectors used.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison of the computational cost of FMA with other efficient attention variants, which could affect the understanding of its efficiency.
- The paper does not discuss the potential impact of the number of levels and summary vectors on the performance of FMA, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the memory cost and inference speed of FMA with other efficient attention variants, which could affect the evaluation of its efficiency.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed FMA and how they might affect its practical application?
- How does the number of levels and summary vectors in FMA affect its performance, and what are the optimal values for different scenarios?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost, memory cost, and inference speed of FMA with other efficient attention variants to better understand its efficiency?
- What are the specific advantages of learning basis functions in FMA compared to using fixed averaging basis functions, and how does this affect the model's performance and applicability?
- Could the authors clarify the notations used in the paper, particularly in Equations 3 and 4, to improve the clarity and understanding of the proposed method?
