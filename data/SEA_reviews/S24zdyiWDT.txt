 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the sample complexity of Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) in both offline and online settings, proposing two algorithms, Reward Learning with Pessimism (RLP) and Reward Learning with Exploration (RLE). It introduces a novel metric for measuring the distance between reward mappings and provides theoretical guarantees for the sample complexity of these algorithms. The paper also discusses the transferability of learned reward functions to different MDPs and establishes lower bounds for sample complexity in both settings. Despite its theoretical contributions, the paper is critiqued for its limited practical applicability due to the complexity of the algorithms and the strong assumptions used.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the sample complexity of inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) in both offline and online settings, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- It introduces a novel metric for measuring the distance between reward mappings, which is a novel approach that extends the existing uniform accurate V-function metric to the trajectory setting.
- The paper presents a clear and concise writing style, making it accessible and easy to understand, which is crucial for a paper of this complexity.
- Theoretical results are well-organized, and the paper includes detailed proofs and discussions, enhancing the credibility of the findings.
- The paper establishes lower bounds for sample complexity in both settings, providing a solid foundation for understanding the practical implications of the proposed algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies on strong assumptions, such as the single-policy concentrability and well-posedness assumptions, which may not hold in practical scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the results.
- The algorithms proposed, RLP and RLE, are computationally intensive and may not be practical for large-scale problems, as they require the estimation of all value functions and advantage functions for all states and actions.
- The paper lacks empirical results, which would be beneficial for validating the theoretical claims and demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed methods.
- The paper could benefit from a clearer exposition of the main ideas and a more detailed discussion of the limitations and assumptions, especially in the context of practical applications.
- The paper's scope is limited to tabular MDPs, which may not generalize well to more complex environments like those found in robotics or other real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the assumptions used in the paper, such as the single-policy concentrability and well-posedness, affect the practical applicability of the results?
- How does the paper's metric for measuring the distance between reward mappings compare to existing metrics, particularly in terms of its ability to distinguish between different reward functions?
- In the context of the transfer learning setting, how does the paper ensure that the learned reward functions remain effective when transferred to different MDPs?
- Could the authors provide more details on the practical implementation of the algorithms, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and assumptions, especially in the context of practical applications. Can the authors expand on these points to help readers better understand the scope and applicability of the results?
