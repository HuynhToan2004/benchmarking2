 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for efficient k-NN search using cross-encoder models, which are typically computationally expensive. It proposes a two-stage approach where item embeddings are pre-computed using a dual-encoder model and a sparse matrix factorization method. During query time, the test-query embedding is computed using these pre-computed embeddings and a linear system of equations, followed by adaptive retrieval of items and re-ranking based on the cross-encoder model. The method is claimed to achieve significant improvements in recall and efficiency over existing methods like CUR-based approaches and dual-encoder distillation. The paper also explores different parameterization methods for matrix factorization and adaptive k-NN search, providing empirical evidence of their effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The proposed method significantly improves the efficiency of cross-encoder similarity search by reducing the number of calls to the cross-encoder model and the amount of data processed at test time.
- The paper presents a novel approach to using cross-encoder models for k-NN search, which is an innovative application of these models in a practical setting.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and retrieval tasks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of different parameterization methods for matrix factorization and adaptive k-NN search, contributing to a better understanding of these methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other relevant works, such as pseudo-relevance feedback methods and other efficient k-NN search methods.
- The methodology relies heavily on the dual-encoder model for initializing embeddings, which may not generalize well to other types of models or datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and practical implementation of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of training and inference times.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the limitations or failure cases of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- The paper's claims of efficiency and effectiveness are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence, particularly in terms of the practical deployment of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other relevant works, such as pseudo-relevance feedback methods and other efficient k-NN search methods?
- How does the proposed method perform when the number of training queries is very small, and what are the implications of this for the method's effectiveness?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the dual-encoder model in the proposed method and discuss the implications of using different types of models for initializing embeddings?
- What are the specific computational costs and training times associated with the proposed methods, and how do these compare to other methods in terms of practical deployment?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the number of items that can be retrieved is limited by computational resources, and what are the implications of this for the method's effectiveness?
