 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel memory selection strategy, Feature-Distance Based Sample Selection (FDBS), and a new contrastive learning loss, IWL, aimed at improving the performance of memory-based online continual learning methods. The FDBS method evaluates the representability of new data by assessing the distance between new and memory set data, while IWL leverages importance weights computed during memory selection to enhance data representation. The paper demonstrates that these methods can be integrated with existing rehearsal-based CL methods to improve their performance in both balanced and imbalanced scenarios. Experiments were conducted on various datasets, including MNIST, Cifar-100, PACS, DomainNet, and mini-ImageNet using different architectures. The paper's contributions include the design of benchmarks for task-free online CL with respect to imbalanced data and the introduction of a novel replay-based online CL method, MSCL, which integrates FDBS and IWL.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel memory selection strategy, Feature-Distance Based Sample Selection (FDBS), and a new contrastive learning loss, IWL, which can be easily integrated with existing rehearsal-based CL methods to improve their performance.
- The proposed method can be applied to both balanced and imbalanced scenarios, demonstrating its versatility.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a thorough discussion of related work.
- The experiments are conducted on various datasets and architectures, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper introduces a novel replay-based online CL method, MSCL, which integrates FDBS and IWL, showing significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art memory-based CL methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of memory and time complexity.
- The paper does not include experiments with larger datasets like ImageNet, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods.
- There is a lack of clarity in the mathematical notation and the explanation of certain equations, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which is crucial for understanding their applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not include a comparison with the latest state-of-the-art methods, which could affect the evaluation of the proposed methods' effectiveness.
- The paper does not provide the code, which is necessary for reproducibility and further validation of the results.
