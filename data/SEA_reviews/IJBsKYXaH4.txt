 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SDDiff, a novel method for molecular conformation generation that utilizes a diffusion model to shift the distribution of inter-atomic distances from Gaussian to Maxwell-Boltzmann. This approach is inspired by physical atom motion phenomena, aiming to reverse the disintegration process of molecular structures. The method is evaluated against existing baselines on two datasets, showing improvements in coverage and matching metrics. However, the paper lacks a thorough discussion on the physical plausibility of the proposed model and the rationale behind certain design choices, such as the use of Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution and the method's performance on planar structures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the methodology and the use of a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution, which is well-explained with figures.
- The proposed method, SDDiff, shows improvements in coverage and matching metrics over existing baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper includes a novel approach to molecular conformation generation by modeling the distribution of inter-atomic distances, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is physically grounded, with the authors providing a convincing explanation of the physics behind their model, which is a notable strength.
- The paper includes a thorough analysis of the inter-atomic distance distribution, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the physical plausibility of the proposed model, particularly the use of a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution which is not typically observed in molecular systems.
- The paper does not adequately address the method's performance on planar structures, which could limit its applicability to certain types of molecules.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could include issues with the model's assumptions and the potential for inaccuracies in the generated conformations.
- The paper does not include a comparison with more recent diffusion models, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the method's scalability and its applicability to larger or more complex molecular structures.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the physical plausibility of the proposed model, particularly the use of a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution in molecular systems?
- How does the method perform on planar structures, and what are the implications of this for the method's applicability to certain types of molecules?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method, including any assumptions made by the model that could limit its applicability or accuracy?
- How does the method compare to other recent diffusion models, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the method's effectiveness and applicability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the method's scalability and its ability to handle larger or more complex molecular structures?
- What is the rationale behind the method's design choices, particularly the decision to use a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution and the method's approach to handling planar structures?
