 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training-free diffusion model that utilizes a smoothed closed-form score function to generate samples without training a neural network. This model, termed a smoothed CFDM, generates samples by flowing Gaussian noise through a velocity field derived from the smoothed score function, which is shown to converge towards the barycenters of tuples of training points. The authors demonstrate the model's ability to generalize without neural score approximations and provide theoretical and empirical evidence to support their claims. The model is evaluated on various datasets, including a subset of the Smithsonian Butterflies dataset and CIFAR-10, showing comparable sample quality to a DDPM trained for a significant amount of time. However, the model's performance in pixel space is noted as being less detailed than that of a DDPM trained for the same duration.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology, making it accessible to readers not deeply familiar with the field.
- The proposed method is novel, innovative, and provides a significant contribution to the field of diffusion models by introducing a training-free approach that generalizes without neural score approximations.
- The theoretical results are well-supported by empirical evidence, and the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through experiments that show the model's ability to generalize and converge towards barycenters of tuples of training points.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear figures and a clear structure that aids in understanding the complex concepts presented.
- The methodology is simple, intuitive, and easy to understand, which is a notable strength in a field that can be complex and difficult to grasp.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claims of novelty are somewhat overstated, as similar methods have been previously discussed in the literature.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and sample quality.
- The experimental results are limited to small-scale datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the scalability and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method's dependence on the training set and its inability to generate high-quality images directly in pixel space are significant limitations.
- The paper's focus on image generation in latent space rather than pixel space is a concern, as it may not fully address the practical applications of the model.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to more complex datasets and its scalability to larger models and datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancy between the claims of novelty and the prior work discussed in the literature?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and sample quality with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of training and inference times?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other methods, particularly in terms of the scalability and effectiveness of their method?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle more complex datasets or to generate high-quality images directly in pixel space?
- How does the method handle the issue of training set dependence, and what are the implications of this dependence on the model's performance and generalization?
- Could the authors provide more detailed insights into the method's performance on larger, more complex datasets, and discuss potential strategies for improving the method's scalability and applicability?
