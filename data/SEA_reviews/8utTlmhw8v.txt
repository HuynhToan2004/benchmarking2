 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the problem of learning Nash equilibria (NE) in rank-1 games, focusing on a novel algorithm that converges to an epsilon-approximate NE in O(1/epsilon^2 log(1/epsilon)) iterations. The algorithm is based on optimistic mirror descent applied to a parametrized bilinear function with a regularization term. It is shown that the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium by leveraging the structural results of Adsul et al. (2021). The paper also discusses the limitations of the algorithm when applied to rank-0 games, where it may not converge to a Nash equilibrium. Despite its theoretical contributions, the paper is criticized for its limited practical applicability and the lack of experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel algorithm for learning an epsilon-approximate Nash equilibrium in rank-1 games, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a structured presentation that aids in understanding the complex theoretical concepts.
- The theoretical results are supported by rigorous proofs and the paper effectively utilizes existing results to advance the understanding of rank-1 games.
- The paper provides a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithm, particularly in the context of rank-0 games, which is commendable for its transparency.
- The connection between the Minty condition and the convergence of the algorithm is highlighted, adding depth to the theoretical analysis.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's practical applicability is limited as the algorithm requires access to the true parameter lambda, which is not always available in real-world scenarios.
- The algorithm's complexity and the number of iterations required to converge are not compared with existing methods, which could undermine the perceived efficiency of the proposed approach.
- The paper lacks experimental validation, which is crucial for demonstrating the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed algorithm in real-world scenarios.
- There are concerns about the novelty of the algorithm, as it appears to be a straightforward application of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper's reliance on existing results, such as those from Adsul et al. (2021), may limit its perceived originality and the depth of its theoretical contributions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the algorithm's complexity and the number of iterations required with existing methods to clarify its efficiency?
- How does the algorithm perform in scenarios where the true parameter lambda is not known, and what are the implications for its practical applicability?
- Is there a possibility to extend the algorithm to handle rank-2 games, and if so, what are the challenges and potential solutions?
- Can the authors elaborate on the specific challenges in proving a lower bound for the algorithm's convergence, and what are the implications if such a bound cannot be established?
- How does the paper's approach compare to other recent works in the field, particularly those that also address the convergence to approximate Nash equilibria in games?
