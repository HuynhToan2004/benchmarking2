 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Diffused Value Function (DVF), which employs diffusion models to estimate value functions from state sequences without the need for reward or action labels. The approach factors the value function into three components: occupancy measure, reward model, and policy representation, which are trained separately using different datasets. The method is evaluated across various benchmarks, including Maze2D and Franka Kitchen, demonstrating its potential in offline reinforcement learning. Despite its innovative approach, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper's presentation, the complexity of the method, and the limited experimental validation, particularly in more complex and realistic environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to estimating the value function using a diffusion model, which is a significant advancement in the field of reinforcement learning.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the challenges and solutions, and the paper is generally well-written, making it accessible to readers.
- The experimental results are promising, showing that the proposed method can perform well on various tasks, including offline reinforcement learning.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity and precision in its presentation, with several typos, grammatical errors, and unclear figures that hinder understanding.
- The experimental validation is limited, with results mainly focused on simple environments like Maze2D, and the method's performance in more complex and realistic environments like D4RL is not adequately demonstrated.
- The method's complexity and the need for specific policy representations might limit its applicability in practical settings.
- There are concerns about the method's generalizability, especially in environments with significant domain gaps between training and testing phases.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance metrics.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and uses of various symbols and equations throughout the paper, especially in the preliminaries section?
- How does the method perform in more complex and realistic environments, such as those in D4RL, and what are the implications of these results for the method's practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the policy representations used and their implications for the method's performance and generalizability?
- How does the method compare in terms of computational efficiency and training time with other diffusion-based methods, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the practical deployment of the method?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods in the field of offline reinforcement learning, particularly in terms of performance and generalizability?
