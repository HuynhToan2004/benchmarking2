 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, PEEP, for predicting enzyme functionality using a metric learning approach. It integrates biology priors such as protein functionality, substrate-scope information, and active site residues to enhance the model's performance. The method employs a contrastive loss to learn the representation of protein sequences, leveraging pre-trained feature extractors like ESM-1b and ESM-2. The paper demonstrates that PEEP outperforms existing methods on benchmark datasets, showing improvements in F-1 scores. However, the paper's novelty is questioned, with concerns about the lack of significant differentiation from existing methods and the need for more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in computational biology, specifically the prediction of enzyme functionality, which is crucial for the development of biocatalysts for industrial or pharmaceutical applications.
- The methodology integrates biology priors into the learning process, enhancing the model's understanding of protein functionality, substrate-scope information, and active site residues.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a comprehensive evaluation, including ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of each component of the proposed method.
- The use of pre-trained feature extractors like ESM-1b and ESM-2 allows for the learning of protein representations without the need for extensive training on specific datasets.
- The paper demonstrates the method's ability to generalize to unseen protein sequences with unseen functionalities, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art models, particularly those that use advanced techniques like graph neural networks or transformers, which could provide a clearer picture of the method's performance and novelty.
- There is a significant overlap in the methodology with existing works, such as CLEAN, which raises questions about the novelty of the proposed method.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with marginal improvements reported.
- The paper's claims regarding the generalization of the method to unseen protein sequences with unseen functionalities are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art models, particularly those that use advanced techniques like graph neural networks or transformers?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements to other existing methods?
- Could the authors clarify the method's performance on larger datasets or in more diverse scenarios to better understand its scalability and generalizability?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential biases in the data, particularly those related to sequence similarity and substrate-scope information?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to demonstrate the practical applicability of the method in real-world scenarios?
