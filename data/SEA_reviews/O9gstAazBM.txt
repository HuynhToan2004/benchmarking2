 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to constructing equivariant neural networks that handle multiple groups acting on multiple inputs, extending the concept of equitune to larger groups. The authors propose a model-agnostic method for designing equivariant layers that can be used with any pre-trained model, demonstrating its effectiveness through experiments on datasets like Caltech-101, 15-Scene, and SCAN. The method is designed to be computationally efficient, particularly useful for large product groups, and is evaluated on various tasks including multi-image classification and compositional generalization in language. Despite its theoretical and experimental contributions, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and potential lack of rigorous proofs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to constructing equivariant neural networks that handle multiple groups acting on multiple inputs, extending the concept of equitune to larger groups.
- The method is designed to be model-agnostic, which means it can be applied to any pre-trained model, enhancing its applicability and versatility.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is shown to be computationally efficient, which is crucial for large product groups, making it a practical solution for real-world applications.
- The experiments conducted on various datasets such as Caltech-101, 15-Scene, and SCAN demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the proposed method, enhancing its credibility and reliability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion of related works, particularly those that address similar problems such as group-equivariant models for multiple groups and larger groups.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it heavily relies on existing techniques like equitune and probabilistic symmetrization.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its efficiency.
- The proofs provided in the paper are not rigorous, with some theorems and lemmas relying on assumptions that are not well-defined or justified.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its results with other relevant works, which could help in understanding the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach, which could hinder its practical application in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the computational complexity of the proposed method and provide a detailed comparison with other methods?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in large-scale experiments?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the proofs, particularly those in Theorem 1 and Lemma 1, to ensure their rigor and clarity?
- How does the proposed method handle the limitations discussed in the paper, and what are the potential solutions to these limitations?
- Can the authors provide a more comprehensive discussion of the related works, especially those that address similar problems and methodologies?
