 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a task-agnostic communication strategy for multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) environments, leveraging a pre-trained set autoencoder to encode global observations into a fixed-size latent state. This approach allows for the sharing of communication strategies across tasks within an environment, enhancing sample efficiency and scalability. The methodology is supported by theoretical guarantees under mild assumptions, and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach in various MARL benchmarks. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as it heavily relies on existing techniques like the PISA architecture and lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability and generalization of the communication strategies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and presents a novel approach to task-agnostic communication strategies in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The use of a pre-trained set autoencoder to encode global observations into a fixed-size latent state is a novel and effective method for sharing communication strategies across tasks, enhancing sample efficiency and scalability.
- Theoretical guarantees are provided under mild assumptions, which are crucial for ensuring the reliability and applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper includes empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach in various MARL benchmarks, showing that the task-agnostic communication strategy outperforms task-specific strategies in novel tasks.
- The method supports variable numbers of agents, which is a significant advantage in dynamic environments where the number of agents can change.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's reliance on the PISA architecture, which is not new, limits its novelty, and the main technical contribution is seen as the use of a set autoencoder.
- The scalability of the communication strategies is a concern, as the paper does not discuss how the communication strategies can be scaled to handle an increasing number of tasks or agents.
- The generalization of the communication strategies to new environments is also a concern, as the paper does not discuss how the strategies can be adapted to new environments that are not part of the training set.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as the need for full connectivity between agents and the potential inefficiencies in communication when the number of agents exceeds the number used during pre-training.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other relevant works, such as those that use differentiable communication strategies or self-supervised learning, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Can you elaborate on how the communication strategies can be scaled to handle an increasing number of tasks or agents?
- How does the generalization of the communication strategies to new environments work? Can you provide examples or experimental results to support this?
- What are the specific advantages of using the PISA architecture over other set autoencoders?
- How does the proposed method compare with other communication strategies that use differentiable communication or self-supervised learning?
- Can you discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the need for full connectivity between agents and the potential inefficiencies in communication when the number of agents exceeds the number used during pre-training?
