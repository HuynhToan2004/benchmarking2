 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Gaussian Process (GP) model for learning the curl-free and divergence-free components of a dynamical system using the Helmholtz-Hodge decomposition. This model leverages symmetry-preserving GPs to enhance interpretability and predictive performance, particularly in noisy and sparse data environments. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness through experiments on several benchmark dynamical systems, showing improved predictive accuracy and energy evolution estimation compared to baseline models. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the model and its application to real-world scenarios, though it lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and broader implications of the approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel application of the Helmholtz-Hodge decomposition to dynamical systems, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The use of symmetry-preserving extensions of the model enhances the predictive performance and interpretability of the results, making the model more robust to noisy and sparse data.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a detailed appendix that provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed model.
- The model's ability to learn physically meaningful representations of dynamical systems from sparse, noisy observations is a notable strength, particularly in the context of learning dynamical systems from data.
- The paper includes a variety of experiments that demonstrate the model's ability to accurately predict the dynamics and energy evolution of dynamical systems, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed model, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and scope.
- The experimental results are limited to a small number of data points, which might not adequately represent the model's performance in real-world scenarios with larger datasets.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant baselines, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the model's performance.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the model, which is a significant limitation given the potential for high computational costs associated with the Helmholtz-Hodge decomposition.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the model to higher-dimensional systems, which could limit its applicability in more complex scenarios.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the broader implications of the model, particularly in terms of its applicability to other types of dynamical systems and its potential impact on the broader machine learning community.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed model and discuss its applicability to other types of dynamical systems?
- How does the model perform with larger datasets, and what are the implications for its scalability and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors discuss the computational complexity of the model and explore ways to reduce it, such as through sparse variational inference or low-rank approximation?
- How does the model compare to more recent and relevant baselines, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the model's performance and applicability?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the broader implications of the model, particularly in terms of its potential impact on the broader machine learning community?
