 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel benchmarking strategy, Contrast Instructions, to measure the consistency of reward models (RMs) in the context of reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF). The authors identify that existing RMs, when trained with standard ranking objectives, fail to recognize semantic changes in prompts, leading to inconsistencies in reward assignments. To address this, two techniques, ConvexDA and RewardFusion, are proposed to enhance reward consistency through extrapolation during RM training and inference stages, respectively. The paper also explores how these techniques can improve the performance of RLHF models by generating more useful responses. The benchmark is constructed using datasets with human preference, and the paper evaluates the effectiveness of these methods through experiments and human evaluations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmarking strategy, Contrast Instructions, which can be applied to any instruction-tuning or human preference dataset, enhancing the scalability of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the inconsistency problem in existing Reward Models (RMs) and proposes two techniques, ConvexDA and RewardFusion, to address this issue.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the paper includes both automatic and human evaluations, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the experiments and the datasets used, which is crucial for reproducibility and transparency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the "trickle-down effect" of reward inconsistency on the downstream RLHF process, which is mentioned in the abstract and conclusion but not adequately explored.
- The benchmarking strategy, while innovative, does not provide a comprehensive solution to the inconsistency problem as it only measures reward consistency in a specific context.
- The paper's methodology, particularly the use of ConvexDA and RewardFusion, is not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to understand the full implementation and effectiveness of these techniques.
- The paper does not compare its methods with other existing RM training methods, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed techniques.
- The paper's evaluation primarily focuses on one RM (LLaMA 7B), which might not generalize well to other RMs or different model sizes.
- The paper's conclusions are somewhat limited by the scope of the experiments, which only use one dataset (StackExchange) for the RLHF experiments.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the "trickle-down effect" of reward inconsistency on the downstream RLHF process? How does this effect manifest, and what are its implications for the overall performance of the RLHF model?
- How does the proposed benchmarking strategy, Contrast Instructions, compare to other existing RM training methods? What are the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed strategy compared to these methods?
- Could the authors clarify the use of the ConvexDA and RewardFusion techniques? How do these techniques specifically address the inconsistency problem in RMs, and what are their limitations?
- How does the paper ensure that the RMs trained with the proposed techniques do not overfit to the training data, especially when using techniques like ConvexDA and RewardFusion?
- Could the authors provide more details on the selection of the threshold for RewardFusion and its impact on the performance of the RM?
- How does the paper address the potential for overfitting to the training data when using the Contrast Instructions benchmark?
