 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the HyperCombinator (HC), a novel neural architecture for reinforcement learning (RL) policies, which utilizes a piecewise-linear function with a small number of sub-policies. This architecture aims to balance performance and interpretability by allowing each sub-policy to be linear with respect to interpretable features. The HC is tested across various control and navigation tasks, showing competitive performance compared to neural baseline methods. The interpretability of the HC is demonstrated through visualizations, which show the policy's decision-making process and its response to different sub-policy choices. The paper also discusses the challenges of balancing performance and interpretability and proposes a method to control the number of sub-policies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach by combining piecewise-linear policies with interpretability, which is a significant advancement in the field of reinforcement learning.
- The proposed method is supported by extensive empirical evaluations and visualizations, demonstrating the interpretability of the HyperCombinator (HC) architecture.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its implementation.
- The experimental results are compelling, showing that the HC can match or exceed the performance of neural baseline methods while maintaining interpretability.
- The authors provide a detailed discussion on the trade-off between performance and interpretability, which is crucial for understanding the limitations and potential of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and justification for the concept of "interpretability," which is a central theme of the study.
- There is an inconsistency in the terminology used for interpretability, which could confuse readers and detract from the paper's clarity.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing interpretable reinforcement learning methods, which could help contextualize the contributions of the study.
- The experimental results, while promising, are somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on simple environments. It would be beneficial to see how the HC performs in more complex, high-dimensional environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the challenges in achieving a balance between performance and interpretability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of "interpretability" in the context of their study?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing interpretable reinforcement learning methods, especially in terms of performance and interpretability?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the HC architecture is implemented in complex, high-dimensional environments?
- How do the authors address the issue of interpretability in the context of the HC architecture, particularly in terms of feature importance and causal explanations?
- Can the authors elaborate on the challenges they encountered in balancing performance and interpretability, and how these challenges were addressed?
