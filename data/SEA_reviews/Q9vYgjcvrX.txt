 **Summary:**
The paper explores the generation of instruction data for instruction-tuned models using open-source models, focusing on both self-training and instruction generation strategies. The authors propose an instruction filtering strategy to improve the alignment between generated instructions and responses, and an extract-then-generate strategy to enhance the diversity of generated instructions. The experiments are conducted on the LongForm benchmark, comparing the proposed method against various baselines and demonstrating that the instruction-generation strategy outperforms self-training. However, concerns are raised regarding the novelty of the method and the clarity of the experimental setup, particularly the definition and role of "unsupervised" responses.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods.
- The proposed instruction filtering strategy is innovative and shows effectiveness in improving the quality of generated instructions.
- The extract-then-generate strategy is simple yet effective, enhancing the diversity of the generated instruction data.
- The method does not rely on closed-source models, which is a significant advantage in terms of accessibility and transparency.
- The paper provides a comprehensive comparison of the proposed method with various baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of the instruction-generation strategy.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited, as similar approaches have been previously explored, such as using LLMs to generate instruction data.
- The experimental setup is not clearly defined, particularly the role and definition of "unsupervised" responses.
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the limitations and failure cases of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the instruction generation model and the training parameters.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of the instruction-generation strategy over self-training are not convincingly supported by the data, particularly in the results shown in Figure 1.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential issues of hallucination and the alignment between generated instructions and responses.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the definition and role of "unsupervised" responses in your experimental setup?
- How does the instruction filtering strategy compare to other methods, such as the use of different evaluation metrics like PPL?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the specifics of the instruction generation model and the training parameters?
- How do you address the potential issues of hallucination and the alignment between generated instructions and responses?
- Can you provide examples or additional data to support the claims made in the paper, particularly the superiority of the instruction-generation strategy over self-training?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for model-specific biases in the generated instructions?
