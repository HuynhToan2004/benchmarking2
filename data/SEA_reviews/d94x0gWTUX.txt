 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Themis, a tool-augmented reward modeling framework aimed at enhancing the interpretability and accuracy of LLM-based reward models. Themis integrates tool invocation and reasoning processes to improve the performance of LLMs in various tasks. The framework is evaluated using a novel dataset, TARA, which includes interactions with seven external tools. Themis is shown to outperform vanilla RMs across multiple tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in practical scenarios. However, concerns about the novelty and the need for more comprehensive evaluations against existing baselines persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methodology and its implementation.
- The integration of tool invocation and reasoning processes into the reward modeling framework is innovative and addresses the limitations of traditional RMs.
- The methodology is supported by a novel dataset, TARA, which includes interactions with seven external tools, enhancing the robustness and applicability of the study.
- The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation, including ablation studies and human evaluations, which provide a thorough analysis of the proposed method.
- The proposed method, Themis, is shown to outperform vanilla RMs on multiple tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in practical scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the complexity of tool invocations and the potential for increased computational costs.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing baselines, particularly in terms of the use of external tools and the incorporation of reasoning processes, which limits the assessment of the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The evaluation primarily focuses on the accuracy of positive/negative answer classification, which may not fully capture the benefits of the proposed method in more complex tasks.
- The dataset used in the study is not comprehensive enough, and the selection criteria for the tools used are not clearly defined, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential biases introduced by the use of external tools in the reward modeling process, which could affect the fairness and reliability of the model's predictions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the complexity of tool invocations and the potential for increased computational costs?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing baselines in terms of the use of external tools and the incorporation of reasoning processes?
- What are the specific criteria used for selecting the tools in the TARA dataset, and how were the negative answers generated in the dataset?
- How does the proposed method handle biases introduced by the use of external tools, and what steps have been taken to mitigate these biases?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples and case studies to illustrate the effectiveness of the tool invocation and reasoning processes in the proposed method?
