 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SHINE, a novel method designed to protect deep reinforcement learning (DRL) agents from backdoor attacks. SHINE employs a two-stage explanation method to identify backdoor triggers and then retrains the DRL agent using a policy retraining algorithm to mitigate the impact of these triggers. The method is theoretically grounded and has been extensively tested across various benchmark RL environments, demonstrating its effectiveness against different types of backdoor attacks. SHINE's approach is innovative, combining a feature-level explanation method with a step-level explanation method to pinpoint the critical time steps and features that trigger backdoor attacks. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation scope and the need for more comprehensive comparisons with other existing defenses.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear and concise presentation of the proposed method.
- The methodology is novel and innovative, combining a feature-level explanation method with a step-level explanation method to effectively identify backdoor triggers.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear introduction of the method and its components, and a clear presentation of the experimental results.
- The theoretical analysis provided is comprehensive, and the paper offers a thorough analysis of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper is technically sound, with a clear presentation of the problem, method, and results, and a well-structured experimental evaluation.
- The methodology is supported by extensive experiments that demonstrate its effectiveness in various RL environments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other existing defenses for DRL, particularly in terms of performance metrics and practical applications.
- The evaluation scope is limited, focusing only on specific types of backdoor attacks and not considering other types of attacks or defenses.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method in detail, which could affect the credibility of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical analysis of the proposed method's effectiveness against adaptive attacks or in more complex environments.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the generalizability of the method across different types of backdoor attacks and environments.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly regarding the knowledge of the attacker's strategy and the trigger location?
- How does the proposed method perform against adaptive attacks, and what are the potential limitations in such scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method and discuss how this might affect its practical application?
- How does the method perform in more complex environments, such as those with high-dimensional state spaces or continuous action spaces?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the method in more detail, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of backdoor attacks and environments?
- How does the method compare with other existing defenses for DRL in terms of performance metrics and practical applications?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and the results reported in the paper, particularly in terms of the statistical significance of the results?
