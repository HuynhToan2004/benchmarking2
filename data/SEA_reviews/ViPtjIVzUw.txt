 **Summary:**
The paper introduces T-MARS, a data filtering approach aimed at enhancing the performance of image-text models by removing text-heavy images that could potentially bias the models towards learning text features over visual ones. This method involves masking out text in images and evaluating the similarity between the masked image and the original caption, thus improving the quality of the training data by focusing on visual features. The approach was tested on various datasets, including LAION and DataComp, showing significant improvements in zero-shot classification accuracy. The paper also includes a pilot study and ablation experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The idea of using text masking to filter out text-dominated images is novel and straightforward, showing potential for improving the performance of image-text models.
- The proposed method is scalable and can be applied to large-scale datasets, which is a significant advantage in the field of image-text model training.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the dataset, including a pilot study that helps in understanding the dataset's characteristics and motivates the proposed method.
- The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through a series of ablation experiments, showing that it can outperform existing methods in data filtering.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation on various datasets, demonstrating the method's applicability and effectiveness across different scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational resources and dataset sizes used.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential biases introduced by the text masking approach, which could affect the generalization ability of the models.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that text masking can improve model performance, especially in scenarios where the text is not the primary focus of the image.
- The method's reliance on CLIP score for filtering could limit its effectiveness in scenarios where the text is not the primary focus of the image.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method, particularly in scenarios where the text is not the primary focus of the image, which could affect the method's applicability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational resources required for the proposed method, which could be a significant drawback for practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including the computational resources and dataset sizes used?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the text is not the primary focus of the image?
- Could the authors discuss the potential biases introduced by the text masking approach and how these might affect the generalization ability of the models?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how might these limitations impact its practical application?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the text is not the primary focus of the image, and what are the implications of this for the model's performance?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational resources required for the proposed method, including the time and memory costs associated with text masking and re-scoring?
