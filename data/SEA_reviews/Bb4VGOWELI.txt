 **Summary:**
The paper introduces OPRO, a method that leverages large language models (LLMs) to optimize solutions by generating new prompts based on a meta-prompt that includes previously generated prompts and their scores. The method is evaluated on various tasks such as mathematical optimization and prompt optimization, showing promising results in improving test set accuracy on natural language tasks. The authors demonstrate that OPRO can outperform human-designed prompts and can be applied to different LLMs, including instruction-tuned and pre-trained models. Despite its novel approach, the paper faces criticism for its lack of theoretical analysis, insufficient comparisons with existing methods, and limited evaluation on mathematical optimization tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear explanations of the experimental setup and results.
- The proposed method, OPRO, is novel and innovative, utilizing LLMs for optimization, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper demonstrates that OPRO can effectively optimize prompts for natural language tasks, improving test set accuracy and outperforming human-designed prompts.
- The method's applicability is shown across various LLMs, including instruction-tuned and pre-trained models, indicating its broad applicability.
- The paper includes a thorough evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of OPRO through detailed experiments and comparisons with baseline methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks theoretical analysis, particularly in the area of mathematical optimization, which could provide deeper insights into the effectiveness and limitations of the proposed method.
- The evaluation on mathematical optimization tasks is insufficient, with only a few examples and a lack of comparison with other optimization methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the proposed method, such as its dependence on the quality of the initial prompt and the risk of overfitting to the training set.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the explanation of the meta-prompt and the optimization process, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare OPRO with existing methods, such as those mentioned in the related work section, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the mathematical optimization tasks, including the specific problems used and the results achieved?
- How does the proposed method compare with other optimization methods, particularly those mentioned in the related work section?
- Could the authors clarify the role and function of the meta-prompt in the optimization process, especially in relation to the optimization trajectory?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting to the training set, and what measures have been taken to prevent this?
- In the prompt optimization experiments, how were the starting points for the optimization chosen, and what impact does this choice have on the results?
- Could the authors elaborate on the optimization process, particularly in the context of the mathematical optimization tasks, to provide a clearer understanding of how the LLM generates new solutions?
