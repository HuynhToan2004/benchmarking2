 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for adversarial training (AT) by assigning perturbation radii based on the vulnerability of individual data points, which is estimated using a logit margin. This approach aims to improve robustness against adversarial attacks by adjusting the perturbation radius dynamically, depending on the natural sample's vulnerability. The methodology is supported by empirical results across various datasets and adversarial attacks, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper's theoretical justification is questioned, particularly regarding the perturbation radius and its impact on adversarial loss. Additionally, the experimental setup and the clarity of the paper's presentation are noted as areas for improvement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the problem addressed is significant and well-motivated.
- The proposed method is simple, computationally cheap, and can be applied to any adversarial training variant.
- The method is supported by empirical results that demonstrate its effectiveness, showing improvements over existing methods across various datasets and adversarial attacks.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the robustness of adversarial training against adversarial attacks, and the experiments are extensive, covering multiple datasets and attack types.
- The use of a margin-based reweighting function for perturbation budgets is novel and innovative, addressing the challenge of uniform perturbation in adversarial training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation on why using a larger perturbation radius leads to an increase in adversarial loss, and the theoretical analysis is not robust, particularly in Theorem 1 and 2.
- The experimental setup is not clearly described, and there is a lack of detail on the adversarial training process and the evaluation of robustness.
- The paper does not adequately address the impact of the hyperparameter α on the results, and the choice of this parameter is not justified.
- There is a lack of comparison with other adaptive perturbation radius methods and other margin-based methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper suffers from some inconsistencies and potential errors in the text, such as incorrect citations and missing references.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a clearer explanation on why using a larger perturbation radius leads to an increase in adversarial loss?
- How does the proposed method compare to other adaptive perturbation radius methods, and what are the advantages of the proposed method over these?
- How is the robustness of the trained model evaluated, and what are the specifics of the adversarial training process used?
- How does the choice of the hyperparameter α affect the results, and can the authors provide more details on its selection and impact?
- Can the authors clarify the inconsistencies and potential errors noted in the text, such as the incorrect citations and missing references?
