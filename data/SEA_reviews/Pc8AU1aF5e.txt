 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Synapse, a novel method for computer control that leverages large language models (LLMs) to perform tasks in complex environments such as web browsing. Synapse employs a trajectory-as-exemplar prompting technique, which allows LLMs to process trajectories of abstracted states and actions, thereby improving decision-making in multi-step scenarios. This method also includes an exemplar memory component that stores and retrieves relevant trajectories for generalization to new tasks. The paper evaluates Synapse on benchmarks like MiniWoB++ and Mind2Web, demonstrating improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the incremental nature of the contributions and the lack of detailed experimental results, which could affect the reproducibility and thoroughness of the study.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and the contributions of the proposed method, Synapse.
- Synapse introduces a novel prompting technique, trajectory-as-exemplar, which effectively utilizes LLMs to process trajectories of abstracted states and actions, enhancing multi-step decision-making.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation of Synapse on two benchmarks, MiniWoB++ and Mind2Web, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods.
- The methodology of Synapse is general and applicable to a wide range of tasks, showing significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the methodology, including the state abstraction process and the exemplar memory, which are crucial components of the Synapse system.
- Synapse is the first in-context learning method to solve the book-flight task in MiniWoB++ and achieves a 56% relative improvement in average step success rate over the previous state-of-the-art prompting scheme in Mind2Web.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed experimental results, particularly concerning the number of exemplars used, which could affect the reproducibility and thoroughness of the study.
- The contributions of Synapse, such as state abstraction and exemplar memory, are seen as incremental and not sufficiently novel.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, including the high inference latency and the dependence on the quality of exemplars.
- The paper's reliance on LLMs for decision-making could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where LLMs might not be available or effective.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare Synapse with existing methods, particularly in terms of the use of LLMs and the performance on different benchmarks.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the failure cases and a broader evaluation across different domains and tasks to demonstrate the generalizability of the method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, particularly concerning the number of exemplars used and the specifics of the exemplar memory retrieval process?
- How does Synapse compare to other LLM-based methods in terms of performance and efficiency, especially in scenarios where LLMs might not be optimal?
- What are the specific challenges in applying Synapse to different domains and tasks, and how does it compare to other methods in these scenarios?
- How does Synapse handle failure cases, and what are the primary reasons for these failures?
- Can the authors provide more details on the exemplar memory retrieval process, including the similarity metrics used and the specifics of the retrieval algorithm?
- How does Synapse perform in scenarios where the LLM might not be the most effective solution, such as in real-time or resource-constrained environments?
