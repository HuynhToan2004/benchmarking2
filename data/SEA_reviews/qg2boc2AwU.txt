 **Summary:**
The paper introduces EBMDock, a novel method for protein-protein docking using an energy-based learning framework. This approach employs a learnable energy function based on statistical potential derived from distance likelihood, which is optimized using Langevin dynamics. The method is evaluated on two datasets, showing competitive performance against traditional and deep learning-based methods. The authors claim that EBMDock can serve as a plug-in to enhance existing methods and is faster than traditional methods. However, concerns are raised about the lack of comparisons with other relevant methods, such as AlphaFold-Multimer and EquiDock, and the limited scope of the experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative energy-based learning framework for protein-protein docking, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The method is well-motivated and effectively utilizes a statistical potential based on distance likelihood, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method demonstrates competitive performance against both traditional and deep learning-based methods, showcasing its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, which is crucial for a complex technical topic.
- The methodology is straightforward and effective, with the authors providing clear explanations and detailed methodologies, which enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other relevant methods, such as AlphaFold-Multimer and EquiDock, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The experiments are limited to a small subset of the DIPS-Het dataset, which might not fully demonstrate the method's capabilities.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational resources required for training and inference, which is essential for assessing the scalability and efficiency of the method.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the method's novelty and its contribution to the field, particularly in comparison to existing methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant oversight.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other relevant methods, such as AlphaFold-Multimer and EquiDock, to better evaluate the proposed method's performance?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational resources and efficiency with other methods, particularly in large-scale applications?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might impact its practical application?
- How does the proposed method handle the variability in protein structures, especially in cases where the structures are not well-defined or contain errors?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the EquiformerPP in the overall performance of the method, particularly in terms of its contribution to the energy model's effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other deep learning-based methods in terms of accuracy and efficiency, especially in large-scale applications?
