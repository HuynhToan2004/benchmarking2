 **Summary:**
The paper explores the convergence and generalization properties of Federated Averaging (FedAvg) in federated learning (FL) using a two-layer convolutional neural network (CNN) model. It introduces a novel theoretical framework based on feature learning theory, which allows for the analysis of the dynamics of signal learning and noise memorization. The paper demonstrates that FedAvg can achieve near-zero test error by effectively increasing the signal-to-noise ratio in feature learning, while local training without communication achieves a large constant test error. It also shows that a weighted FedAvg method, based on the similarity of input features across clients, can effectively address data heterogeneity issues in FL. The paper is supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results on synthetic and real-world datasets, highlighting the effectiveness of the weighted FedAvg approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is the first to provide a theoretical analysis of the convergence and generalization properties of FedAvg in federated learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical results are supported by both synthetic and real-world datasets, enhancing the credibility of the findings.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the weighted FedAvg approach.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to analyzing the convergence and generalization properties of federated learning using feature learning theory, which is a unique perspective not commonly seen in previous studies.
- The theoretical results are presented in a clear and concise manner, making it easier for readers to understand and apply the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to two-layer CNN models and does not generalize to more complex models like transformers, which could limit the applicability of the findings.
- The experimental results are limited to specific datasets and do not include a broader range of datasets, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the orthogonality of signal vectors and the large sample size, may not hold in practical federated learning scenarios, which could affect the validity of the theoretical results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its work, which could help readers better understand the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper's theoretical analysis relies heavily on the feature learning theory, which might not be easily accessible to readers without prior knowledge of the theory.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the orthogonality of signal vectors and the large sample size, may not hold in practical federated learning scenarios, which could affect the validity of the theoretical results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the orthogonality of signal vectors and the large sample size, and discuss how these assumptions affect the validity of the results in practical federated learning scenarios?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their work and provide suggestions for future research directions?
- How does the proposed approach compare to other federated learning methods, such as FedBN and FedPer, in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the hyperparameters used and the number of clients involved, to enhance the reproducibility of the results?
- How does the proposed approach perform when applied to other types of neural networks, such as transformers, and what are the implications of this for the broader applicability of the findings?
