 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to protein design by integrating dynamical properties into the diffusion probabilistic model, specifically focusing on protein backbone generation. It leverages Normal Mode Analysis (NMA) to capture the dynamics of protein structures and formulates a new conditional score to guide the sampling process. This method allows for the generation of proteins that not only maintain the desired dynamical properties but also adhere to predefined structural constraints. The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis and demonstrates the effectiveness of the method through empirical results. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental scope, lack of comprehensive validation against state-of-the-art methods, and insufficient discussion on the novelty and applicability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive theoretical analysis that supports the proposed method.
- The introduction of the conditional score to guide the sampling process is novel and could significantly enhance the functionality of protein design.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the method through empirical results, showing that it can generate proteins with desired dynamical and structural properties.
- The method is flexible and can be transferred to other models, potentially opening new avenues for protein design.
- The paper is significant in its attempt to incorporate dynamical behavior into protein design, which is a promising direction for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive validation against state-of-the-art methods, which limits the ability to assess the superiority of the proposed method.
- The experimental scope is limited, focusing primarily on the CATH dataset, which may not generalize well to other datasets or real-world applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the novelty of the proposed method compared to existing approaches, such as those using eigenvector calculations.
- The applicability of the method is limited to specific types of protein motions, which may not be applicable to all protein design scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and its practical implications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other methods, especially those using eigenvector calculations, to highlight the advantages of the proposed method?
- How does the method perform when applied to other datasets or real-world scenarios?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the method and potential scenarios where it may not be effective?
- How does the method compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability to existing methods?
- Could the authors provide more examples or case studies to demonstrate the versatility and effectiveness of the method in different protein design scenarios?
