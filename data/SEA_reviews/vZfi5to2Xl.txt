 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Structured Activation Sparsification (SAS), a novel method that projects activation maps into a higher dimensional space to achieve structured sparsity in activations. This approach is designed to enhance the expressiveness of neural networks by allowing the activation map to be sparse while maintaining a constant number of multiplications, which is crucial for efficient computation on GPUs. The paper also proposes a modified version of Adam called ERAdam to address the stability issues during training with SAS. The experiments conducted demonstrate that SAS can achieve higher accuracy than structured weight sparsity (SWS) and unstructured weight sparsity, especially in scenarios with small network widths. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as it primarily extends the concept of SWS to activations without significant additional theoretical or empirical insights.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive discussion on the proposed method and its implementation.
- The idea of sparsifying activation is novel and interesting, with potential for significant impact in the field of neural network efficiency.
- The proposed method, SAS, is compatible with NVIDIA GPUs, which could be beneficial for practical applications.
- The paper includes a comprehensive analysis of the proposed method, including its capacity analysis and the development of a cuSAS library for efficient implementation.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate that SAS can achieve higher accuracy than SWS and unstructured weight sparsity, especially with small network widths.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the relationship between SAS and SWS, which are similar in concept but differ in application.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it primarily extends the concept of SWS to activations without significant additional theoretical or empirical insights.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its compatibility with low-bit weights and its impact on model capacity and training efficiency.
- The experimental setup and results could be improved, particularly in terms of including more diverse network architectures and training configurations to better demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other related works, such as those using attention mechanisms for dynamic kernel selection.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between SAS and SWS, and discuss the key differences between the two methods?
- How does the proposed method perform when combined with low-bit weights, and what are the implications for model capacity and training efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training efficiency of SAS, particularly how it compares to other methods in terms of the number of epochs required to reach a certain accuracy?
- How does the proposed method perform with different network architectures, such as ResNet-101, and what are the implications for the expressiveness and efficiency of the model?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other methods using attention mechanisms for dynamic kernel selection, and discuss the potential advantages or disadvantages of these approaches?
