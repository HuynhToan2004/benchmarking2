 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Knowledge Tracing (KT) model, ReKT, which employs a lightweight Forget-Response-Update (FRU) framework. This model is designed to trace students' knowledge states from three distinct perspectives: questions, concepts, and domains, using a combination of linear regression units. The FRU architecture, which is based on Bruner's cognitive development model, is claimed to be both simple and effective, outperforming many state-of-the-art KT models across multiple datasets. The paper also includes an ablation study that explores the impact of different knowledge states on performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear descriptions of the proposed method and experiments, making it easy to understand.
- The model's simplicity and effectiveness are highlighted, particularly in its ability to outperform more complex models on multiple datasets.
- The paper includes a comprehensive ablation study that explores the impact of different knowledge states on performance.
- The FRU architecture, which is based on Bruner's cognitive development model, is a novel approach to modeling student knowledge states.
- The paper provides a thorough comparison with 22 state-of-the-art KT models, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed explanations of how the knowledge states are constructed and how they contribute to the model's performance.
- The FRU architecture, while claimed to be simple, is not clearly distinguished from other models like LSTM or GRU, which could lead to confusion about its uniqueness and effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the model, particularly in terms of handling large datasets or diverse student populations.
- The novelty of the FRU architecture is questionable, as it appears to be a simplified version of existing models like LSTM or GRU, which might not offer significant advantages over these models.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the model, such as its applicability to different types of educational data or its performance in real-world educational settings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the time and space complexity of the FRU architecture, which could be crucial for understanding its practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- How does the FRU architecture differ from other models like LSTM or GRU in terms of its design and effectiveness?
- Can the FRU architecture be adapted to handle different types of educational data, such as those from different domains or at different levels of education?
- How does the model perform in real-world educational settings, particularly in terms of its ability to generalize to new students or educational contexts?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the model and how these might affect its practical application?
- How does the model handle the scalability issues, particularly in terms of its ability to handle large datasets or diverse student populations?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the time and space complexity of the FRU architecture, and how these compare to other models like LSTM or GRU?
