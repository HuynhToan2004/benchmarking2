 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel intrinsic motivation method for reinforcement learning (RL), termed Constrained Intrinsic Motivation (CIM), which aims to address the limitations of existing methods such as coverage-based and mutual information (MI) maximization. CIM is designed to optimize both the conditional state entropy and mutual information, leveraging a constrained optimization approach to balance these objectives. This method is evaluated in both reward-free pre-training (RFPT) and exploration with intrinsic motivation (EIM) settings, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods in several MuJoCo robotics environments. The authors also propose an adaptive temperature schedule for EIM tasks to reduce the bias introduced by intrinsic rewards. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is critiqued for its lack of theoretical justification, insufficient empirical evidence, and unclear presentation in some sections.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of intrinsic motivation for reinforcement learning, proposing a novel method that integrates the benefits of both coverage-based and mutual information (MI) maximization.
- The authors provide a comprehensive review of existing intrinsic motivation methods, highlighting their limitations and motivating the need for a new approach.
- The proposed method, Constrained Intrinsic Motivation (CIM), is well-articulated and supported by a detailed mathematical derivation and experimental validation.
- The paper includes a thorough comparison with existing methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of CIM in terms of sample efficiency, skill diversity, and state coverage.
- The method is adaptable to different intrinsic motivation methods, making it a versatile tool for various RL applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear theoretical justification for the proposed method, particularly in explaining why the proposed lower bound is effective and how it differs from existing methods.
- The empirical evidence provided is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of CIM over existing methods, as the experiments are limited to a few tasks and do not include a comprehensive comparison across different environments.
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the introduction and the explanation of the CIM method. This includes unclear definitions and inconsistencies in the notation and mathematical formulations.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and scalability of the CIM method, which is crucial for its practical application in more complex environments.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced perspective and guide future research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed theoretical justification for the proposed lower bound and its effectiveness in maximizing the intrinsic motivation?
- How does the CIM method handle the trade-off between exploration and exploitation in different RL scenarios, particularly in the context of reward-free pre-training and exploration with intrinsic motivation?
- Can the authors clarify the mathematical formulations and definitions, particularly in Section 3.1.1 and the use of the Frank-Wolfe algorithm?
- How does the computational complexity of the CIM method scale with the size and complexity of the environment, and what are the practical implications of this complexity in terms of training time and resource requirements?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a broader range of environments and tasks to better demonstrate the generalizability and effectiveness of the CIM method?
- How does the CIM method perform in different types of environments, such as those with discrete or continuous actions, and how does it compare to other methods in terms of sample efficiency and skill diversity?
