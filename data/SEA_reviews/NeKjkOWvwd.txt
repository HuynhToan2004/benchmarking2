 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization of deep neural networks (DNNs) by analyzing the influence of different frequency components in image classification. It introduces a novel method called Class-wise Frequency Augmentation (CFA) that leverages Shapley values to quantify the impact of frequency components on model performance. This approach allows for the enhancement of model performance by augmenting favorable frequency components and suppressing unfavorable ones. The paper presents extensive experiments across various OOD benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of CFA in improving the generalization ability of DNNs. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in methodological descriptions and the need for more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to analyzing the out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization problem using Shapley values, which is both innovative and well-explained.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across multiple benchmarks.
- The use of Shapley values to quantify the influence of frequency components is a novel and interesting approach that could lead to new insights and methods for improving model generalization.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which enhances its readability and comprehensibility.
- The methodology is simple and can be applied to any model architecture, making it broadly applicable.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the description of the proposed method, particularly in the algorithm section, which could be improved with additional illustrations or clearer explanations.
- The method's generalizability and applicability beyond image classification tasks are not clearly established, which limits its broader impact.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including a discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method.
- The theoretical underpinnings of the method, particularly the use of Shapley values, are not thoroughly explored, which could limit the depth of understanding and the method's perceived robustness.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights into its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed illustrations or additional explanations in the algorithm section to clarify the method's operation?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational efficiency and scalability compared to other methods?
- Can the method be applied to other types of data beyond images, such as time-series data?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how do these impact its practical applicability?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of the method, particularly the use of Shapley values, to provide a deeper understanding of the method's theoretical foundations?
