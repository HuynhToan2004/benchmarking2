 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel adversarial training method for robust reinforcement learning (RL) called Robust Reinforcement Learning with Structured Adversarial Ensemble (ROSE). ROSE addresses the challenges of over-optimism and over-pessimism in adversarial training by employing a group of adversaries instead of a single adversary, thereby improving the accuracy of approximating the inner optimization problem. The method also introduces a new objective function that averages the performance of the worst-k adversaries to prevent the agent from becoming over-conservative. Extensive experiments are conducted on MuJoCo environments, demonstrating the effectiveness of ROSE in enhancing the robustness of RL agents.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of a structured adversarial ensemble to address the over-optimism and over-pessimism in adversarial training is innovative and well-motivated.
- The theoretical analysis provided is solid, and the paper is complemented by extensive empirical studies that show the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of robust RL, which is of high relevance and practical importance.
- The proposed method, ROSE, is simple to implement and does not require extensive computational resources, making it accessible to a wide range of researchers and practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the convergence of the RL agents under adversarial training, which is a critical aspect of robust RL.
- The experiments are limited to MuJoCo environments, and it would be beneficial to see how ROSE performs in more diverse and complex environments.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost associated with the introduction of an adversarial ensemble, which could be a significant limitation for practical implementation.
- The theoretical results are not sufficiently strong, particularly in terms of their applicability and the assumptions made, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art robust RL methods to establish the superiority of ROSE.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the convergence of the RL agents under adversarial training, especially in the context of ROSE?
- How does ROSE perform in more diverse and complex environments beyond MuJoCo?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and discuss how these assumptions affect the applicability of the results?
- How does ROSE compare with other state-of-the-art robust RL methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors elaborate on the computational cost associated with the introduction of an adversarial ensemble and discuss potential strategies for mitigating this cost?
