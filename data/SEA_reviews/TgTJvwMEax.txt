 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to solving inverse problems by embedding the solution into a higher-dimensional space, thereby enhancing the regularization process. This methodology, which is applicable to both linear and nonlinear inverse problems, employs a learnable embedding matrix and a regularization function to optimize the solution. The proposed method is evaluated on several inverse problems, including the duathlon problem, image deblurring, and magnetics, demonstrating its effectiveness in recovering original signals from noisy measurements. The authors claim that their approach improves the convergence of optimization algorithms by making the problem more convex, thereby reducing the reliance on heuristic priors. However, the paper has been criticized for its lack of clarity in presentation, insufficient comparison with existing methods, and potential over-reliance on the choice of regularization functions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach to solving inverse problems by embedding the solution into a higher-dimensional space, which is a novel method not commonly used in recent literature.
- The use of a learnable regularization function is highlighted as a significant advancement over traditional methods, which can be less effective in certain scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and logical structure that effectively communicates the research's objectives and results.
- The methodology is supported by theoretical analysis and practical demonstrations, showing its effectiveness in solving inverse problems, particularly in cases where traditional methods fail.
- The paper is original in its application of embedding techniques to inverse problems, which could potentially lead to new insights and improvements in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, particularly in the mathematical descriptions and the definitions of key terms and variables, which could hinder understanding for readers unfamiliar with the subject matter.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the choice of regularization functions.
- There is a notable absence of quantitative data, such as running times and computational costs, which are crucial for evaluating the practical feasibility and efficiency of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, especially in terms of the embedding dimension and the computational resources required.
- The experimental results and their interpretations are not sufficiently detailed or convincing, with some results showing marginal improvements over existing methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the choice of regularization functions and the potential for overfitting with high-dimensional embeddings.
