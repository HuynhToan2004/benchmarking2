 **Summary:**
The paper explores the task of decontextualization, focusing on the rejection option in classification with a fixed predictor. It introduces a new problem formulation and a surrogate loss function to optimize the rejectionor function, which is crucial for scenarios where multiple correct labels are possible. Theoretical analysis is provided to justify the use of this loss function, and empirical results show that it outperforms existing methods, such as MaxProb and Cross-Entropy loss. The paper also discusses the challenges of annotation and presents a new dataset of 2,000 manually-labeled examples for this task. However, the scope of the study is limited to the decontextualization task, and the theoretical assumptions and practical implications of the proposed method need further exploration.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem of learning to reject with a fixed predictor, which is particularly relevant for scenarios where there are multiple correct labels.
- It introduces a novel formulation of the problem, a new surrogate loss function, and a new dataset for the decontextualization task, which are significant contributions to the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in terms of coverage and precision.
- The paper is significant in the context of decontextualization, a less explored area in natural language processing, and could inspire further research in this direction.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to the decontextualization task, which may not be generalizable to other tasks or domains without modification.
- The theoretical assumptions made, such as the existence of a convex function relating the calibration gaps, are not well-justified, and the practical implications of these assumptions are not explored.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the annotation process and the choice of parameters, which could affect the reproducibility and applicability of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing methods for learning to reject, such as those by Cortes et al. (2016) and Charoenphakdee et al. (2021), which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed approach.
- The experimental results are limited to a single task and a single dataset, which may not convincingly demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How do the authors justify the theoretical assumptions made, particularly the existence of a convex function relating the calibration gaps?
- Could the authors provide more details on the annotation process, including the criteria used to determine the correctness of edits and the choice of parameters?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods for learning to reject, such as those by Cortes et al. (2016) and Charoenphakdee et al. (2021)?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to clarify the definition of the surrogate loss function and its relationship with the rejection loss?
- What are the implications of the results in terms of practical applications, and how do the authors envision the deployment of their method in real-world scenarios?
