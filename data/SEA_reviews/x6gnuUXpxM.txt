 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to constructing sparse neural networks using Ramanujan graphs, focusing on both fully connected and convolutional layers. This method, termed "Pruning at Initialization (PaI)," is aimed at reducing network density while maintaining accuracy. The authors claim that their approach, which is deterministic and does not rely on randomness, offers advantages such as path-connectedness and high symmetry, which are not typically achieved by previous methods. The paper includes experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method on various datasets like CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, comparing it to existing methods. However, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity in defining and demonstrating the advantages of the Ramanujan graph-based pruning, and for the limited scope of its experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions and notations, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed method is novel, using Ramanujan graphs to construct a sparse neural network, which is a new approach in the field of neural architecture search.
- The paper provides a comprehensive literature review, effectively positioning the work within the existing body of knowledge.
- The methodology is clearly explained, with detailed explanations of the construction of Ramanujan graphs.
- The paper includes a thorough experimental section, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method on various datasets like CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing methods, highlighting the advantages of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and demonstration of the advantages of using Ramanujan graphs in the context of sparse neural networks.
- The experimental results are limited to only CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, which may not sufficiently demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity in the paper regarding the specific advantages of using Ramanujan graphs over other graph-based methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with the latest methods in the field, which could help in understanding its competitive advantage.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the Ramanujan graph-based pruning method offers advantages over other graph-based methods in terms of accuracy and efficiency?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity with other graph-based methods, especially in larger and more complex neural network architectures?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including scenarios where it may not be as effective?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of sparsity in the network, especially in terms of maintaining the performance of the network when a large number of weights are pruned?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with the latest methods in the field, including a discussion on how it compares in terms of accuracy, efficiency, and other relevant metrics?
