 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel formulation for the Optimal Multiple Transportation (OMT) problem, which extends the traditional Optimal Transport (OT) framework to handle multiple probability measures. The authors propose an iterative Sinkhorn algorithm to solve this problem, demonstrating its application in visual point set matching and model fusion. They also explore the application of OMT in the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP), proposing a new formulation for TSP-OMT. The paper provides empirical evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness through experiments on datasets like Pascal VOC and Willow Object Class, comparing it with existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty and practical applicability of the proposed methods, particularly the TSP-OMT formulation and the generalization of the OMT algorithm to more than three measures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel formulation for the Optimal Multiple Transportation (OMT) problem, which extends the traditional Optimal Transport (OT) framework to handle multiple probability measures.
- The proposed algorithm, ROMT-Sinkhorn, is shown to be effective in solving the OMT problem, with empirical evidence provided through experiments on the Pascal VOC and Willow Object Class datasets.
- The paper provides a comprehensive literature review, effectively situating the proposed work within the existing body of research.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the methodology and experimental results, making it accessible to readers.
- The authors demonstrate the application of their method to the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP), showing the potential of their approach in solving complex optimization problems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the novelty and advantages of the proposed OMT formulation compared to existing methods, particularly the Multi-marginal Optimal Transport (MMOT) formulation.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and applicability to more complex scenarios.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with marginal improvements over existing methods and a lack of comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially in scenarios involving large datasets or more complex optimization problems.
- There is a lack of discussion on the societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of the research.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous theoretical analysis and a deeper exploration of the practical implications of the OMT problem.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the OMT formulation with existing methods, particularly the MMOT formulation, highlighting the advantages and novelty of their approach?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the number of measures exceeds three? What are the potential challenges and limitations in such scenarios?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup and results, including a comparison with state-of-the-art methods and a discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of their approach?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational complexity and scalability issues, particularly in large-scale datasets or more complex optimization problems?
- Can the authors provide a deeper theoretical analysis of the OMT problem and its implications, especially in terms of its practical applications and potential limitations?
- How does the proposed method address the societal impacts of its application, particularly in areas such as privacy and security?
