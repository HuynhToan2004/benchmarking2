 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for achieving causal fairness when the true causal graph is only partially known, represented by a partially directed acyclic graph (PDAG). This framework, which models fair prediction using a PDAG and formulates a constrained optimization problem to balance fairness and accuracy, is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The approach aims to address the limitations of existing methods that assume a fully known causal graph, by leveraging the concept of interventions and modeling fair predictions as the effect of all observational variables. The paper also discusses the limitations of the work, including the assumption of no selection bias or latent confounders.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and concise definitions of key concepts.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and well-motivated, with a focus on the identification of interventional fairness criteria in MPDAGs.
- The paper provides a clear identification condition for interventional fairness measures on MPDAGs and develops a framework for achieving interventional fairness on partially known causal graphs.
- The methodology is supported by both synthetic and real-world experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness in achieving fairness and accuracy.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to causal fairness that does not require a fully known causal graph, which is a significant departure from existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the causal graph is not fully known.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, focusing only on accuracy-fairness trade-offs and not exploring other fairness notions.
- The paper does not provide a clear definition or explanation of the "admissible attribute" used in the interventional fairness criterion, which could confuse readers.
- There is a lack of comparison with other existing methods for causal fairness, which could help to establish the relative performance of the proposed method.
- The paper's assumption of no selection bias or latent confounders may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the proposed method can be applied to other types of causal fairness notions, such as path-specific fairness and counterfactual fairness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of the "admissible attribute" in the interventional fairness criterion?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the causal graph is not fully known?
- Can the authors provide a clearer explanation of the "background knowledge" mentioned in the paper?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for causal fairness, particularly in terms of performance and applicability?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the method can be applied to other types of causal fairness notions, such as path-specific fairness and counterfactual fairness?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the causal graph is not a tree?
- Can the authors discuss the implications of their assumptions about selection bias and latent confounders on the applicability and effectiveness of their method?
