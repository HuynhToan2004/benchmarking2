 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel zero-shot image editing technique that leverages optical flow networks to guide the diffusion sampling process, enabling precise manipulation of image structure without the need for text inputs or architecture constraints. This method, termed "motion guidance," allows for the specification of dense, complex motion fields, which can handle a wide range of complex motions and produce high-quality edits of both real and generated images. The approach does not require training and can be applied to various types of images, including paintings and sketches. The paper also discusses the limitations of the method, such as its susceptibility to noise and the potential for motion inconsistencies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel zero-shot image editing technique that allows for precise manipulation of image structure without relying on text inputs or specific diffusion architecture.
- The method can handle a wide range of complex motions and produces high-quality edits of both real and generated images.
- The approach does not require any training and can be applied to various types of images, including paintings and sketches.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and understandable figures and descriptions of the method.
- The authors have provided a comprehensive discussion of the limitations of their approach, which adds transparency and credibility to the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a quantitative evaluation, which is crucial for validating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- There is a need for more detailed implementation details, especially regarding the handling of occlusions and the construction of target flow fields.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the context of existing methods that use optical flow for image editing.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the handling of noisy images and the stability of the optical flow estimator under different conditions.
- The method's sensitivity to the quality of the optical flow estimator and the potential for motion inconsistencies due to the optimization of different models are not adequately addressed.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a quantitative evaluation to validate the effectiveness of their method?
- How do the authors handle the stability of the optical flow estimator under different conditions, and what measures are in place to ensure consistent motion estimation?
- Can the authors clarify how they handle noisy images and the impact of this on the quality of the edited images?
- How does the method handle motion inconsistencies that might arise from the optimization of different models?
- Can the authors provide more details on the construction of target flow fields and how they handle occlusions in their method?
- How does the method compare to other existing methods that use optical flow for image editing, particularly in terms of accuracy and efficiency?
