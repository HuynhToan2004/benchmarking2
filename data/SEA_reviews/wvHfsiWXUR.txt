 **Summary:**
The paper investigates how declarative knowledge in training data influences the generalization of large language models (LLMs) during domain shifts. It specifically examines the impact of declarative statements on LLM predictions, demonstrating that these statements can alter model behavior even when there is no overlap between the declarative descriptions and the test-time generations. The study employs a toy dataset of weather reports to test this hypothesis and shows that declarative statements can influence model predictions, although the effect is subtle and not consistently demonstrated across different model sizes. The paper also discusses the limitations of the study, including the need for more comprehensive testing across different models and scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and tables that effectively convey the results.
- The research question is interesting and relevant, focusing on the impact of declarative knowledge in training data on the generalization of large language models.
- The study includes a thorough analysis of the results, including ablations and additional experiments to address potential confounders.
- The experiments are designed to be convincing, with the use of a toy dataset that effectively demonstrates the impact of declarative statements on model behavior.
- The paper provides a valuable contribution to the understanding of how declarative knowledge in training data influences the generalization of large language models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear demonstration of the effect of declarative statements on model predictions, with some results showing no significant difference between models trained with and without such statements.
- The experiments are limited to a single dataset and model size, which may not generalize to other scenarios or models.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential confounding effects of token matching, which could be a significant factor in the observed results.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the findings across different model sizes, which is a critical aspect of the research.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive testing across different models and scenarios to validate the generalizability of the findings.
- The use of the term "internalization" may be misleading as it implies a deeper understanding or memory of the declarative statements, which may not be the case.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancy between the results shown in Figure 3 and those in Table 2, particularly the significant difference in DAE values between models trained with and without declarative statements?
- How do the authors explain the absence of a significant effect in the results shown in Table 2, given the large standard deviations reported?
- Could the authors provide more details on the generation process for the demonstrations and descriptions, including the prompts used and the specific models employed?
- How do the authors address the potential confounding effects of token matching in their experiments, and what steps were taken to ensure that the results are not simply due to such effects?
- Could the authors elaborate on the scalability of the findings across different model sizes, and what implications this might have for the generalizability of the results?
- In light of the concerns about the use of the term "internalization," could the authors clarify how they define and measure this concept in their study?
