 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for generating counterfactual explanations using a deep learning approach, focusing on backtracking counterfactuals in deep structural causal models. This method is designed to preserve causal relationships by altering latent variables rather than intervening directly on observed variables. The approach involves a constrained optimization problem that minimizes the distance between latent variables while ensuring adherence to causal laws. The paper provides empirical evidence using datasets like Morpho-MNIST and CelebA, comparing the proposed method with existing approaches. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of theoretical underpinning, limited experimental scope, and potential overclaiming of the method's capabilities.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of counterfactual explanations, focusing on the generation of counterfactuals that are causally compliant and do not require retraining the model.
- The approach is well-motivated and provides a clear and logical explanation of the methodology, which is supported by detailed experimental results.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with a clear distinction between the methodology and the experimental setup.
- The proposed method is computationally efficient, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to backtracking counterfactuals, which is a less explored area in counterfactual explanations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive review of related work, particularly in the area of backtracking counterfactuals and causally compliant counterfactuals.
- The experimental setup is limited, focusing only on two datasets and not comparing the proposed method with a broader range of existing approaches.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical guarantees or proofs to support the claims made about the method's capabilities and its adherence to causal laws.
- The method's reliance on the invertibility of the generative model and the assumption of a known causal graph may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper overclaims the method's capabilities, particularly in terms of its ability to handle complex graph structures and its applicability in general settings.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the invertibility of the generative model and the knowledge of the causal graph, are not clearly stated or justified.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that use different approaches to generating counterfactuals?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the causal graph is not known or when the generative model is not invertible?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the method and provide more detailed justification for these assumptions?
- How does the method ensure that the counterfactuals generated are causally compliant, and what theoretical guarantees can be provided to support this claim?
- Could the authors elaborate on the experimental setup and results, particularly in terms of the metrics used and the choice of datasets?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability with other existing methods?
