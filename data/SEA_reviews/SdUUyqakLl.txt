 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Byzantine attack called STRIKE, which exploits the gradient skew phenomenon in federated learning (FL) to hide malicious gradients within the skewed honest gradients. The authors demonstrate that under certain conditions, the average of honest gradients can deviate significantly from the optimal gradient, making it easier for attackers to manipulate the aggregation process. Theoretical analysis and empirical evidence from experiments on three datasets (CIFAR-10, ImageNet-12, and FEMNIST) support the proposed attack's effectiveness. The paper also discusses the implications of this attack on the robustness of existing Byzantine defenses and proposes potential mitigation strategies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel attack called STRIKE that exploits the gradient skew phenomenon, which is a significant contribution to the field of Byzantine robustness in federated learning.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, and the empirical evidence supports the effectiveness of the proposed attack.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the results are well-presented, enhancing the paper's readability and comprehensibility.
- The authors have proposed a two-stage attack that effectively hides Byzantine gradients within the skewed honest gradients, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The paper discusses potential mitigation strategies that could be employed to counteract the STRIKE attack, which is a valuable contribution to the ongoing research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing Byzantine attacks, which could provide a better understanding of the STRIKE attack's uniqueness and effectiveness.
- The paper's theoretical analysis and empirical results are not sufficiently robust, particularly in demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed attack across a broader range of datasets and settings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed attack, such as its effectiveness in different data distributions and the potential for countermeasures.
- The paper's reliance on certain assumptions, such as the unbiased nature of stochastic gradients, may not hold in all practical scenarios, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's discussion on the impact of the STRIKE attack on the overall system and the potential countermeasures is insufficiently detailed, which could limit the practical applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the datasets used in the experiments, particularly the data distribution and the number of clients involved?
- How does the STRIKE attack perform in different data distributions, and what are the implications for the effectiveness of the attack?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the hyperparameter ν in the STRIKE attack and how it affects the attack's performance?
- What are the potential countermeasures against the STRIKE attack, and how could these be implemented to mitigate the attack's impact?
- How does the STRIKE attack perform under different attack scenarios, such as varying the number of Byzantine clients or the level of data non-IIDness?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing Byzantine attacks to better understand the STRIKE attack's uniqueness and effectiveness?
