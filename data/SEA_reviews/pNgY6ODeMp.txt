 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for interpretable image classification using a Concept Decomposition Vector (CDV) model. This model decomposes class-related visual concepts into a set of vectors, which are then used to predict concept scores and class labels. The method is designed to improve interpretability by reducing the reliance on predefined text concepts and instead using visual concepts learned from training images. The authors claim that this approach enhances the model's ability to explain its decisions in a cross-modality manner, which is demonstrated through experiments on several datasets. However, the paper has been criticized for its complexity, lack of clarity in presentation, and limited evaluation on larger, more diverse datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for learning concept decomposition vectors (CDVs) from visual language models (VLMs) that can be used to predict concept scores and class labels, enhancing interpretability.
- The method is supported by a comprehensive set of experiments across various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, with clear figures and a logical structure that aids in comprehension.
- The concept of using CDVs to decompose class-related visual concepts and reduce bias in image classification is innovative and contributes to the field of interpretable machine learning.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the proposed method, including the learning process and cross-modality interpretation, which is supported by extensive experiments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is complex, with numerous hyperparameters and components that make it difficult to understand and reproduce.
- The method's reliance on well-trained VLMs like CLIP raises concerns about its applicability to other VLMs and its generalizability across different datasets and domains.
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the explanation of key concepts and methodologies, which could hinder comprehension.
- The evaluation of the method is limited, with insufficient comparisons to other interpretable methods and a lack of experiments on larger, more diverse datasets.
- The paper's claims of superior interpretability are not convincingly supported by the results, particularly in terms of accuracy and interpretability metrics.
- The method's dependence on a large number of CDVs might lead to overfitting, and the training process for these vectors is not clearly described.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the number of CDVs is determined and how it affects the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of interpretability and accuracy to other interpretable methods, especially those that do not rely on VLMs?
- What are the specific advantages of using CDVs over other interpretable features like activation maps or attention maps?
- Can the authors clarify the training process for the CDVs and explain how they are initialized and trained?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting due to the large number of CDVs used?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the datasets used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
