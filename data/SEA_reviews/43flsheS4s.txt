 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for adversarial distillation named retrospective online adversarial distillation (ROAD), which leverages the robust model's predictions from the last epoch and a natural model trained on clean data to guide the student model's learning. This approach aims to address the trade-off between robustness and natural accuracy by using the student model itself as a teacher, which is a departure from traditional adversarial distillation methods that rely on pre-trained robust models. The paper presents extensive experiments on various architectures and datasets, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in improving robustness and natural accuracy while reducing training time and computational costs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to adversarial distillation by utilizing the student model's predictions from the last epoch as supervision, which is a departure from traditional methods that rely on pre-trained robust models.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and effective, with a well-structured paper that is easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The method demonstrates significant improvements in both robustness and natural accuracy, as evidenced by extensive experiments across various architectures and datasets.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed method, including theoretical insights and detailed empirical evaluations, which support the claims made.
- The method's ability to reduce training time and computational costs makes it particularly appealing for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with other methods, particularly in terms of the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches.
- The method's reliance on the robust model's predictions from the last epoch might not fully address the trade-off between robustness and natural accuracy, especially in scenarios where the model's predictions are overly confident.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the method, particularly the training time and memory usage, which could be a significant concern for resource-constrained environments.
- The paper's claims about the method's effectiveness in reducing overconfidence in adversarial examples are not convincingly supported by empirical evidence.
- There is a lack of discussion on the method's limitations and potential negative societal impacts, which could be critical for understanding the method's applicability and ethical implications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a clearer comparison of the proposed method with other existing methods, particularly in terms of the specific advantages of the proposed approach?
- How does the proposed method address the trade-off between robustness and natural accuracy, especially in scenarios where the model's predictions are overly confident?
- Could the authors provide more detailed empirical evidence to support the claims made about the method's ability to reduce overconfidence in adversarial examples?
- How does the method perform in terms of computational complexity, particularly in terms of training time and memory usage, compared to other methods?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and its potential negative societal impacts, and how these might be addressed or mitigated?
