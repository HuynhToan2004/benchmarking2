 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of blueprint models in generating text with citations, focusing on enhancing the attribution capabilities of LLMs in long-form question-answering tasks. The authors propose two models: an abstractive model that generates questions from scratch and an extractive model that copies questions from the input. These models are evaluated using the AQuAMuSe dataset, demonstrating improvements in summary quality and citation accuracy compared to LLM-based pipelines. The paper also discusses the potential of blueprint models in transferring skills across different tasks and domains. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited scope, lack of comparative analysis with state-of-the-art models, and unclear presentation in some sections.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue of improving the attribution capabilities of LLMs in long-form question-answering tasks, which is a significant area of research.
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of the effects of different blueprint models, including abstractive and extractive models, and have demonstrated the potential of these models in generating more informative, factual, and faithful responses.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed blueprint model is innovative and has shown promising results, particularly in improving the quality of summaries and the accuracy of citations.
- The evaluation is comprehensive, covering various aspects of the model's performance, including the transferability of skills across different tasks and domains.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on the AQuAMuSe dataset and not exploring other benchmarks such as those used in the ALCE paper, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
- There is a lack of comparative analysis with state-of-the-art models, which could help to establish the relative effectiveness of the proposed models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the blueprint model is generated, particularly the process of selecting and ranking questions, which is currently described as ad-hoc and not well-explained.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with some sections appearing unclear or inadequately explained, such as the generation of blueprint questions and the evaluation metrics used.
- The paper's claims regarding the transferability of skills across different tasks and domains are not well-supported, and the evidence provided is insufficient to substantiate these claims.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the blueprint model is generated, particularly the process of selecting and ranking questions?
- How does the model handle cases where the input passages do not provide sufficient information to answer the generated questions?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the effectiveness of the blueprint model in generating more informative, factual, and faithful responses?
- How does the model ensure that the generated questions are not too specific or generic, and how does this affect the quality of the responses?
- Could the authors explain the rationale behind using the T5-11B model for entailment classification and how this model was trained?
- How does the model handle the trade-off between the number of questions generated and the quality of the responses?
- In the evaluation, how were the metrics chosen, and how do they ensure that the model is not simply memorizing the training data?
