 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ComFormer, a novel transformer-based architecture designed for crystalline materials, focusing on geometric completeness and preserving crystal symmetries. ComFormer employs two variants, iComFormer and eComFormer, which utilize invariant and equivariant vector representations, respectively, to capture the geometric information of crystals effectively. The method is evaluated on three benchmarks and demonstrates superior predictive accuracy compared to existing methods. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty in methodology, lack of comprehensive experimental validation, and insufficient discussion on the scalability and generalization of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to constructing graph representations for crystalline materials, which is geometrically complete and maintains crystal symmetries.
- The methodology is supported by theoretical proofs and extensive experimental evaluations, demonstrating its effectiveness in various crystal benchmarks.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive discussion on the construction of crystal graphs and the design of transformers.
- The proposed ComFormer achieves state-of-the-art performance on three benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness in crystal property prediction.
- The paper provides a thorough discussion on the challenges of geometric completeness for crystal graphs and proposes a novel method to achieve it, which is both geometrically complete and preserves crystal symmetries.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential failure cases of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and reliability.
- There is insufficient discussion on the scalability and generalization of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the complexity of crystal structures and the variety of crystal properties that can be predicted.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing methods, such as those that use matrix forms, which could provide a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impact of the proposed method, which is an essential consideration for ethical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of its applicability to large-scale crystal structures.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform on amorphous materials, which are not crystalline?
- Can the proposed method be applied to predict higher order crystal properties, such as band structures or electronic properties?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods that use matrix forms for crystal property prediction?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how can these be mitigated?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability to large-scale crystal structures?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability and generalization issues, especially in predicting properties for crystals with varying sizes and complexities?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the proposed method to other types of materials, such as polymers or biological macromolecules?
