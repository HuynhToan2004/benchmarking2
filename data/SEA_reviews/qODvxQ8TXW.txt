 **Summary:**
The paper explores the advantages of Learning Rate Rewinding (LRR) over Iterative Magnitude Pruning (IMP) in sparse neural network training, specifically focusing on the impact of parameter sign flips and the overparameterization of neural networks. The authors demonstrate that LRR is more likely to converge to a ground truth target and is more robust to sign perturbations due to its ability to switch signs effectively. Theoretical insights are provided through a simplified model of a single hidden neuron network, supported by empirical evidence from CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and Tiny ImageNet datasets. The paper also introduces a novel approach to rewinding Batch Normalization parameters to enhance LRR's performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the advantages of LRR over IMP, supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments on CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and Tiny ImageNet datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of their proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear explanations of the theoretical insights and empirical results.
- The analysis of the sign flipping behavior of LRR and its impact on sparsification is novel and interesting, providing new insights into the effectiveness of LRR.
- The paper's findings are significant and could potentially lead to the development of more efficient sparse training algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to a single hidden neuron network, which may not generalize well to more complex networks.
- The empirical results are not consistently strong across all datasets and conditions, particularly in cases where LRR's performance is not superior to IMP.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how it compares to other state-of-the-art methods.
- The theoretical insights provided in the paper are not always directly applicable to more complex networks, and the empirical results are sometimes contradictory to the theoretical expectations.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their proposed method and how it compares to other state-of-the-art methods?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex architectures, such as Vision Transformers?
- Could the authors clarify the contradiction between the theoretical expectations and the empirical results, particularly in the cases where LRR does not outperform IMP?
- Is there a possibility to extend the theoretical analysis to more complex networks, and if so, what are the main challenges?
- How do the authors justify the use of a single hidden neuron network for theoretical analysis, and what are the implications of this choice for the generalizability of the findings?
