 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Out-of-Distribution (OOD) detection method inspired by the Neural Collapse phenomenon. It leverages the observation that in-distribution (ID) features tend to cluster around weight vectors, while out-of-distribution (OOD) features are more dispersed. The method proposes a new score, the NC score, which measures the proximity of features to weight vectors and incorporates an L1 norm to filter out OOD samples. The authors claim that this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on various benchmarks, including CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as the core ideas are derived from existing works, and the method's performance on certain datasets is not as strong as expected.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their motivations.
- The proposed method is simple and intuitive, using a novel score (pScore) to detect OOD samples based on feature proximity to weight vectors, which is computationally efficient and rapid in inference speed.
- The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art OOD detection performance across a wide range of OOD benchmarks, improving generalizability of existing work.
- The method is applicable to any neural network, making it versatile and applicable in various scenarios.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, including ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the proposed method, which is supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as the core ideas are derived from existing works, particularly the Neural Collapse paper, which may limit its contribution to the field.
- The performance of the method is not consistently strong across all datasets, particularly on some datasets like CIFAR-100 and ImageNet, where the performance is not as good as on others.
- The method's reliance on weight vectors might limit its applicability in scenarios where weight vectors are not available or are not well-defined.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the potential for overfitting to specific datasets or the impact of different training conditions.
- There are concerns about the scalability of the method to larger datasets or more complex models, which could affect its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the experiments, particularly the experimental setup and conditions under which the results were obtained?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of OOD datasets, such as those with different distributions or characteristics?
- What are the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how might these affect its practical application?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, especially the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the results?
- How does the method handle the absence of weight vectors, and what are the implications of this for the method's effectiveness?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the method's performance on larger or more complex models, and how it might be adapted to such scenarios?
