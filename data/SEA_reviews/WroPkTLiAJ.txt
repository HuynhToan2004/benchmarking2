 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to one-shot federated learning (FL) named FedLPA, which leverages layer-wise posterior aggregation to address the challenges of non-IID data distribution. FedLPA employs a Bayesian probabilistic framework to approximate local posterior distributions using empirical Fisher information matrices, which are then aggregated to train the global model parameters. The methodology is supported by theoretical analysis and extensive experiments across various datasets, demonstrating significant performance improvements over existing methods. However, concerns were raised about the clarity and organization of the paper, particularly the lack of detailed explanations and justifications for certain methodological choices and experimental setups.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in federated learning by focusing on personalized one-shot federated learning, a topic of high relevance and interest in the field.
- The proposed method, FedLPA, is innovative and well-motivated, providing a novel approach to aggregating local models in federated learning.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive experimental setup that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The theoretical analysis provided is sound, and the experiments conducted are extensive, supporting the claims made in the paper.
- The methodology is well-justified, with clear explanations of the computational and communication overheads associated with the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the background and related works sections, which could benefit from more detailed explanations and a clearer organization.
- There is a lack of discussion on the convergence rate and generalization error bounds, which are crucial for understanding the performance of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the scalability of the method with the number of clients, which is a significant limitation given the focus on federated learning.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims of privacy protection and computational efficiency.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which detract from its professional quality.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that address similar issues in federated learning, which could undermine the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed explanation of the computational and communication overheads associated with the proposed method?
- How does the method perform when the number of clients increases, and what are the scalability implications?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and the hyperparameter settings, to ensure a fair comparison with existing methods?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and privacy protection?
- How does the method handle label skew, and what are the implications of this on the performance of the model?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the convergence rate and generalization error bounds of the proposed method?
- How does the method compare to existing methods in terms of scalability and computational efficiency, particularly in large-scale federated learning scenarios?
