 **Summary:**
The paper introduces V-JEPA, a self-supervised learning method for video representation that predicts masked spatio-temporal regions in a learned representation space. V-JEPA leverages a joint embedding predictive architecture to learn representations from video, which are evaluated on various downstream tasks such as action recognition, spatio-temporal action detection, and image recognition. The methodology involves predicting masked regions in latent space, which is claimed to enhance the model's ability to handle motion understanding tasks. Extensive experiments are conducted to validate the model's effectiveness, showing improvements over existing methods in frozen evaluation settings. However, the paper's claims of novelty and the necessity of video-specific pretraining are questioned, as similar approaches have been previously explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive literature review that effectively positions the work within the current research landscape.
- The proposed method, V-JEPA, shows promising results in frozen evaluation settings, outperforming other self-supervised learning methods in various downstream tasks.
- The use of the joint embedding predictive architecture in video learning is novel and innovative, potentially leading to better performance on video understanding tasks compared to image-based models.
- The experiments are comprehensive and well-designed, including comparisons with a wide range of models and datasets, and the inclusion of ablation studies that provide insights into the model's performance and limitations.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other self-supervised learning methods, such as MAE and VMAE, which are crucial for establishing the superiority of the proposed method.
- The claim that video-specific pretraining is necessary for motion understanding tasks is not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which show that image-based models can perform comparably or even better in certain tasks.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of information leakage in the pretraining dataset, which could affect the fairness and validity of the results.
- There is a lack of novelty in the approach, as similar methods have been previously explored, such as using masked autoencoding for video understanding.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational costs and training times of the proposed method, which are crucial for practical application and scalability.
- The method's effectiveness in more complex video understanding tasks, such as video object segmentation and video captioning, is not explored, which limits the understanding of the model's general applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational costs and training times of V-JEPA compared to other methods?
- How does the performance of V-JEPA compare when using different video datasets, such as those used in VideoMAE?
- In the ablation study regarding the pretraining dataset scale, could the authors clarify the specific datasets used and the criteria for selecting the datasets?
- Why was the decision made to predict masked regions in the latent space, and how does this approach compare to predicting masked regions in pixel space?
- How does the model handle the issue of information leakage, especially in the pretraining dataset?
- Can the authors discuss the potential for extending the model to more complex video understanding tasks, such as video object segmentation and video captioning?
