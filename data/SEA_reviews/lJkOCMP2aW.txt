 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multi-scale Transformer architecture, Pathformer, designed for time series forecasting. Pathformer utilizes adaptive pathways to dynamically select and aggregate scale-specific characteristics based on varying temporal dynamics in input time series, enhancing prediction accuracy and generalization. The model integrates both temporal resolution and temporal distance for multi-scale modeling, and dual attention is performed over these patches to capture global correlations and local details as temporal dependencies. The paper claims state-of-the-art performance across multiple real-world datasets and demonstrates strong generalization abilities in transfer scenarios. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the experimental setup and the fairness of comparisons, particularly in the context of different input lengths and model configurations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel multi-scale transformer architecture with adaptive pathways, which is a significant advancement in the field of time series forecasting.
- The proposed method integrates both temporal resolution and temporal distance for multi-scale modeling, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The adaptive pathways within multi-scale Transformers are a novel contribution, enabling the model to adaptively extract and aggregate multi-scale characteristics based on the temporal dynamics of input data.
- The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art prediction accuracy across multiple real-world datasets, showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper conducts transfer learning experiments across datasets, validating the strong generalization of the model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- There is a need for more comprehensive ablation studies to understand the individual contributions of different components of the model.
- The experimental setup is not clearly described, particularly the input lengths for different models, which could affect the fairness of comparisons.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of computational efficiency and model size with other state-of-the-art models, which is essential for understanding the practical implications of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed visualizations and explanations of the adaptive pathways, which are a key contribution of the work.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the hyperparameters used in the experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the input lengths for different models, to ensure a fair comparison?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and model size with other state-of-the-art models?
- Can the authors provide more detailed visualizations and explanations of the adaptive pathways to better understand their impact on the model's performance?
- What are the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments, and how do they affect the model's performance and generalizability?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and provide suggestions for future research directions?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential issue of overfitting, particularly in scenarios with limited training data?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other models in terms of model architecture and experimental setup to better understand the contributions and implications of the proposed method?
