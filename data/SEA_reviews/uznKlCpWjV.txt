 **Summary:**
The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the PPO-Clip algorithm, a variant of the widely used Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) algorithm in reinforcement learning. The authors establish the convergence of the PPO-Clip algorithm to a stationary point, addressing the challenges posed by the non-smoothness of the clip operator and the unbounded nature of the score function. The paper introduces a new tool that might be of independent interest for analyzing other optimization algorithms. Despite the theoretical contributions, the paper has been critiqued for its limited scope in terms of practical applicability and the absence of a discussion on the optimality of the stationary points reached by the algorithm.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the PPO-Clip algorithm, which is known for its practical success in reinforcement learning tasks.
- The authors introduce a new tool that might be of independent interest for analyzing other optimization algorithms, showcasing the potential for broader applications in the field.
- The analysis addresses the challenges posed by the non-smoothness of the clip operator and the unbounded nature of the score function, which are significant issues in the context of PPO-Clip.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the theoretical results are sound, providing a solid foundation for further research.
- The paper tackles a challenging problem by analyzing the convergence of PPO-Clip, which has been a topic of interest for a long time in the reinforcement learning community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the optimality of the stationary points reached by the algorithm, which is a significant gap in understanding the practical implications of the theoretical results.
- The results presented are mainly theoretical, with limited practical implications, which may limit the paper's appeal to the broader reinforcement learning community.
- The assumptions made in the paper are somewhat restrictive, particularly concerning the policy parametrization and the learning rate, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the relationship between the theoretical results and practical implementations of PPO-Clip, especially in terms of the convergence rate and the practical implications of the assumptions made.
- The paper does not include numerical experiments to support the theoretical claims, which could have strengthened the paper's empirical evidence.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of the optimality of the stationary points reached by the PPO-Clip algorithm?
- How do the assumptions made in the paper affect the generalizability of the results, and could these be relaxed to make the results more applicable to a broader range of policy gradient algorithms?
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between the theoretical results and practical implementations of PPO-Clip, especially in terms of the convergence rate and the assumptions made about the learning rate?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the learning rate in their analysis, and could they provide more details on how this choice impacts the practical applicability of the results?
- Could the authors include numerical experiments to support the theoretical claims, and if so, how would these experiments be designed to validate the theoretical results?
