 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LLMs as Tool Makers (LATM), a framework that allows large language models (LLMs) to create and use their own tools for various tasks. This involves a two-stage process: tool-making, where a powerful LLM generates a Python function for a specific task, and tool-using, where a smaller LLM applies this tool to solve instances of the task. The framework is designed to optimize computational resources by leveraging a functional cache, which stores and reuses tools for similar tasks. The paper evaluates the LATM framework on several datasets, demonstrating its potential for reducing computational costs while maintaining performance. However, concerns are raised about the scalability and generalizability of the approach, particularly in real-world applications and diverse task domains.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative concept of LLMs as Tool Makers (LATM), which is both intuitive and practical.
- The proposed framework, LATM, is well-articulated and clearly presented, with a focus on reducing computational costs through the use of a functional cache.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of LATM in reducing computational costs while maintaining performance, and the paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across various complex reasoning tasks.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of LLMs, specifically focusing on reducing computational costs and improving efficiency.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the LATM framework, particularly in terms of its scalability and generalizability across different domains and tasks.
- There is a need for a more detailed discussion on how the LATM framework performs in real-world scenarios, especially in handling tasks that are not part of the training set.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence or analysis to support the claim that the LATM framework can be effectively applied to a wide range of tasks, especially those that require more complex reasoning abilities.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the challenges and complexities involved in tool-making, such as the need for a high degree of capability and the potential for errors in tool generation.
- The evaluation of the LATM framework is limited to a few datasets, which may not fully demonstrate its effectiveness or applicability in diverse real-world scenarios.

- Can you discuss the potential limitations or challenges of the LATM framework, and how these might impact its scalability and applicability in different domains or tasks?
- Can you provide more details on the evaluation process, particularly how the performance of the LATM framework was measured and what metrics were used?
