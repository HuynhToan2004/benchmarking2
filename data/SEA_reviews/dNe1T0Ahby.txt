 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a dual-encoder model for extreme multi-label classification (XMC), proposing a novel loss function and a training method that outperforms existing methods on several benchmark datasets. The authors argue that their method, which includes a differentiable top-k error-based loss function and a decoupled softmax loss, can improve performance on XMC tasks. The methodology involves a memory-efficient distributed training approach and a focus on optimizing recall metrics. Despite the paper's strengths in empirical results and methodological innovation, concerns were raised about the novelty of the proposed methods and the clarity of the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) results on several benchmark datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The introduction of a differentiable top-k error-based loss function and a decoupled softmax loss is innovative and addresses the limitations of existing loss functions in XMC tasks.
- The paper provides a memory-efficient distributed training approach, which is crucial for handling large datasets with limited computational resources.
- The experiments are well-designed, including ablation studies that support the proposed methods, and the results are supported by extensive empirical evidence.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and scope.
- The novelty of the proposed loss functions is questionable, as similar methods have been previously proposed, which could limit the perceived impact of the contribution.
- The experimental setup is not clearly explained, particularly the choice of negative samples and the impact of different loss functions on performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing methods, including a broader range of datasets and a more detailed analysis of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method to very large datasets, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the choice of negative samples and their impact on the performance of the proposed loss functions?
- How does the proposed method perform on datasets with a very large number of labels, and what are the implications for computational efficiency and scalability?
- Could you clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of negative samples and the impact of different loss functions on performance?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed loss functions over existing methods, and how do they contribute to the overall performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially when dealing with very large datasets?
