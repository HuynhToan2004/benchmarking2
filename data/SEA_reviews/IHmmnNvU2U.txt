 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel optimization problem, Weighted Risk Invariance (WRI), aimed at addressing covariate shift in invariant features. It proposes a method that learns the density of invariant features and model parameters simultaneously, demonstrating superior performance over existing invariant learning methods. The WRI approach is validated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing that it outperforms state-of-the-art methods in scenarios with covariate shift. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the WRI problem and its practical implications, including the use of learned invariant feature densities for out-of-distribution detection.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel optimization problem, Weighted Risk Invariance (WRI), which is well-motivated and addresses the challenge of covariate shift in invariant features.
- The proposed method is technically sound, with a clear and well-structured presentation that aids in understanding the theoretical framework and practical implementation.
- The paper includes extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing superior performance over existing methods.
- The use of learned invariant feature densities for out-of-distribution detection is a novel and practical application of the proposed method.
- The theoretical analysis provided in the paper is robust, including a proof of Theorem 2 which shows that a linear regression model with weighted risk invariance satisfies w_{spu}=0.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the method might not perform optimally or where it may not be applicable.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently detailed or comprehensive, with some results (such as those on the DomainBed dataset) not convincingly demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other methods, including a discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method.
- The paper's assumptions and the conditions under which the proposed method is applicable are not clearly stated, which could limit the understanding of its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- There is a need for more clarity in the presentation of the paper, particularly in the introduction and the explanation of certain terms and concepts.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the support of invariant features in the test environment is not a subset of the union of the supports in the training environments?
- Can the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and other methods such as IRM and VREx, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- What are the specific conditions under which the proposed method is applicable, and how does it perform in scenarios where these conditions are not met?
- How does the proposed method handle the estimation of invariant feature densities, particularly in scenarios where the densities are not known?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and results, including the specific conditions under which the experiments were conducted and the statistical significance of the observed results?
