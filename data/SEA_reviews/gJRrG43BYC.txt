 **Summary:**
The paper introduces State-driven Implicit Modeling (SIM), a novel training method for implicit models that aims to reduce the complexity and cost of training by matching the states and outputs of a baseline model. This method is designed to be efficient and scalable, allowing for parallel training and the application of custom objective functions for parameter reduction and robustness. The approach is validated through experiments on datasets like CIFAR-100 and 20NewsGroup, demonstrating its effectiveness in sparsification and robustness training. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental validation and the lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the strength of its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel training method, State-driven Implicit Modeling (SIM), which is a significant contribution to the field of implicit modeling.
- The method is well-articulated, with a clear and structured presentation that makes it accessible to readers.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical framework and detailed algorithmic descriptions, enhancing its reproducibility and applicability.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the SIM method in sparsification and robustness training.
- The paper is original in its approach, combining sparsity and robustness training in a novel way, which could be of interest to the NeurIPS community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for its practical application.
- The experimental validation is limited, focusing on a few datasets and not comparing the method with other state-of-the-art techniques, which could weaken the strength of the claims.
- There is a noticeable absence of a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review, particularly in the area of implicit models and training methods, to better situate the research within the existing body of knowledge.
- The paper's focus on specific datasets (CIFAR-100 and 20NewsGroup) might limit the generalizability of the findings to other datasets or applications.
- The paper could be improved by including more visual aids, such as figures and graphs, to better illustrate the concepts and results.

**Questions:**
- How does the SIM method perform on other datasets beyond those used in the experiments?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the scalability of the SIM method, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger and more complex datasets?
- How does the SIM method compare with other state-of-the-art techniques in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential scenarios where it might not be the most effective solution?
- What are the potential applications of the SIM method beyond sparsification and robustness training?
- How does the SIM method address the issue of overfitting, which is a common challenge in training implicit models?
