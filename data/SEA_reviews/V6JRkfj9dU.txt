 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the generalization error of ReLU neural networks, focusing on the scaling of the error with the number of training samples. It provides a theoretical analysis using Fano's inequality and empirical results to demonstrate that the error scales as 1/sqrt(n), which is independent of network depth and width. The authors also explore the relationship between the neural network's complexity and generalization error, showing that the error decreases at a rate that is slower than the number of parameters but faster than the number of training samples. The paper's contributions include a novel theoretical analysis and empirical validation, which could help in understanding the statistical properties of neural networks and improving their generalization performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis and empirical validation of the generalization error of ReLU neural networks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors effectively use information theory, particularly Fano's inequality, to establish a lower bound on the generalization error, which is independent of network depth and width, and scales as 1/sqrt(n).
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions and notations, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The theoretical analysis is supported by empirical results, which are clearly presented and demonstrate the practical implications of the theoretical findings.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to understanding the generalization error of neural networks, which could lead to new insights and methods for improving neural network performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions, such as the Gaussian distribution of input data and the sparsity of parameters, may limit the generalizability of the results to other types of data and networks.
- The empirical studies are limited to small-scale datasets, which may not adequately represent the behavior of neural networks on larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not adequately compare its results with existing literature, particularly in terms of the generalization error bounds for different network architectures and datasets.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is complex and may be difficult for non-experts to understand fully, which could limit its accessibility.
- The paper's conclusions are somewhat limited by the specific model and dataset used, which may not generalize to other types of neural networks or datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly regarding the Gaussian distribution of input data and the sparsity of parameters?
- How does the paper's theoretical analysis apply to other types of neural networks, such as those with different activation functions or different parameter distributions?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing literature to better contextualize their findings within the broader field of neural network research?
- How do the empirical results compare with theoretical predictions, and what implications do these comparisons have for the practical application of the findings?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their research, particularly in terms of improving the generalization performance of neural networks?
