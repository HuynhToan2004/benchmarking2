 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for solving the Unbalanced Optimal Transport (UOT) problem by focusing on the computation of marginal probability distributions using a transform coefficient method. This method avoids directly calculating the coupling matrix, instead using the KKT conditions of the UOT problem to derive the coefficients. The authors also extend this method to the Partial Unbalanced Optimal Transport (PUOT) problem and demonstrate its application in a real-world unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) task. The paper claims that this approach offers computational efficiency and theoretical insights into the UOT problem. However, concerns were raised regarding the lack of novelty in the proposed method, the limited scope of the experiments, and the clarity of the theoretical contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to solving the Unbalanced Optimal Transport (UOT) problem by focusing on the computation of marginal probability distributions, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method proposed is computationally efficient and avoids the need to directly calculate the coupling matrix, which is a common approach in solving UOT problems.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive literature review that positions the work well within the current research landscape.
- The authors provide a detailed derivation of the method, including a clear explanation of the theoretical underpinnings and the practical implementation of the method.
- The paper includes several examples and experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, including a real-world application in unsupervised domain adaptation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the proposed method, particularly in explaining why focusing on marginal probability distributions is beneficial for solving the UOT problem.
- There is a lack of novelty in the proposed method, as it closely resembles existing methods and does not offer significant theoretical or computational advancements.
- The paper's theoretical contributions, such as the use of transform coefficients and the derivation of the CROT method, are not sufficiently explained or justified, leaving the reader with unclear understandings of their significance and applicability.
- The experiments conducted are limited in scope and do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority or novelty of the proposed method over existing approaches.
- The paper suffers from several typographical and formatting errors, which detract from its professional presentation and readability.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in large-scale applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the motivation behind focusing on marginal probability distributions for solving the UOT problem? What specific advantages does this approach offer over traditional methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or demonstrations of how the transform coefficients and CROT method are derived and applied in practical scenarios?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches, particularly in terms of scalability and computational complexity?
- How does the method handle the scalability issues that are common in UOT problems, especially in large-scale applications?
- Can the authors explain the rationale behind the choice of parameters such as τ in the experiments, and how these choices affect the results?
- The paper claims that the proposed method offers new insights into the UOT problem. Could the authors elaborate on these insights and how they contribute to a better understanding of the problem?
