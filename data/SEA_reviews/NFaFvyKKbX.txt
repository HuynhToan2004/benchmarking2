 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel metric termed the affinity score, designed to quantify the non-linearity of a given transformation in deep neural networks (DNNs). The metric is based on optimal transport theory and is applied to various architectures including convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and vision transformers (ViTs). The authors demonstrate the utility of the affinity score through extensive experiments, showing its potential in understanding the non-linearity propagation in DNNs and its predictive power for model performance. Despite its theoretical grounding and experimental validation, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and the need for a broader range of experiments to substantiate its claims fully.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, with a clear presentation of the proposed affinity score.
- The affinity score is novel and theoretically sound, offering a new perspective on the non-linearity of activation functions in deep neural networks.
- Extensive experiments are conducted, demonstrating the practical utility of the affinity score and providing insights into the non-linearity propagation in different architectures.
- The paper addresses an important problem in understanding the non-linearity of activation functions, which is crucial for improving the performance of deep learning models.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to support the proposed affinity score, which enhances the credibility of the metric.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive literature review, particularly in the area of non-linearity in deep learning, which could have enriched the discussion and provided a clearer context for the proposed work.
- The novelty of the proposed affinity score is limited as it appears to be a simple combination of existing metrics like the Wasserstein distance and the affinity score.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the affinity score, especially in terms of its applicability to different types of neural networks and its generalizability across various architectures and datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the affinity score with other existing metrics for quantifying non-linearity, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed metric.
- The experiments presented are limited to specific architectures and datasets, and could be extended to include a broader range of models to strengthen the claims made by the authors.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a detailed comparison of the affinity score with other existing metrics for quantifying non-linearity in deep learning models?
- How does the affinity score perform when applied to other types of neural networks beyond those tested in the paper?
- Can you discuss the limitations of the affinity score and how they might affect its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios?
- How does the affinity score relate to other measures of non-linearity, such as the Jacobian of the neural network with respect to its input?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of metrics used in the experiments, particularly in Figure 8, and how they were selected for comparison with the affinity score?
- How does the affinity score perform on different datasets, and could you provide results on other datasets to validate its generalizability?
