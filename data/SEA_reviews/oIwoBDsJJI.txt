 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph similarity measure named Graph Foster Distance (GFD), which is based on the Foster distribution derived from the resistance distance between nodes in a graph. GFD utilizes a linear programming approach to optimize the cost function, which is a key feature that distinguishes it from other graph similarity measures. The authors claim that this approach offers computational efficiency and effectiveness in graph classification tasks, demonstrated through experiments on various datasets. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as the GFD appears to be a straightforward combination of existing measures, and for not adequately comparing its computational efficiency with other methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear structure and logical flow.
- The proposed GFD is simple, effective, and computationally efficient, making it suitable for large-scale graphs.
- The paper introduces a novel approach using Foster distributions to quantify graph similarity, which is a unique contribution to the field.
- The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in computational efficiency and graph classification accuracy.
- The paper provides a clear definition and explanation of the GFD, which is a significant contribution to the field of graph similarity measures.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison of computational efficiency with other methods, which is crucial for establishing the superiority of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the GFD is questioned as it appears to be a straightforward combination of existing measures, with minimal new theoretical insights.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of detailed explanation on how the GFD handles node features, particularly in the context of directed graphs, which are common in many applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the GFD, especially in terms of its applicability to large-scale graphs, and the computational cost implications of using different graph representations.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the theoretical foundations of the GFD, particularly in relation to existing graph similarity measures and their computational complexities.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational efficiency of the GFD with other methods, including the specific computational costs and runtime comparisons?
- How does the GFD handle node features, particularly in the context of directed graphs? Is there a specific methodology or approach used to incorporate node features?
- Can you clarify the definition and role of the resistance distance in the context of the GFD? How does it contribute to the overall measure of graph similarity?
- How does the GFD perform in terms of scalability, particularly in handling large-scale graphs? What are the computational costs associated with using different graph representations, such as adjacency matrices versus edge lists?
- Could you discuss the theoretical underpinnings of the GFD in more detail, particularly in comparison to existing graph similarity measures? What are the specific advantages or disadvantages of the GFD compared to these measures?
- How does the GFD handle the incorporation of node features, especially in the context of graph classification? Is there a specific methodology or approach used to integrate node features into the GFD?
