 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel watermarking framework, WASA, aimed at enabling source attribution and data provenance in large language models (LLMs). The framework embeds watermarks into LLM training data, which are then regenerated during the generation phase, allowing for accurate source attribution. The WASA framework is designed to be scalable, robust against various attacks, and adaptable to different LLMs. The paper presents a detailed evaluation of the WASA framework, demonstrating its effectiveness in source attribution and data provenance, while maintaining the performance of the LLM. The methodology involves the use of Unicode characters as watermarks, which are invisible to humans but detectable by LLMs, ensuring data integrity and origin traceability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue of data provenance in large language models (LLMs), which is crucial for ensuring data integrity and traceability.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, with clear and detailed explanations of the methodology, including the design of watermarks and the embedding process.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and easy to follow, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the proposed method.
- The methodology is robust, with thorough evaluations demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various scenarios, including against adversarial attacks.
- The paper is innovative in its approach, using a novel method of watermarking LLMs to ensure data provenance, which is not commonly explored in the field.
- The paper is comprehensive, covering various aspects of the proposed method, including its scalability, adaptability, and performance preservation, which are crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and ethical implications of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the potential negative consequences of such a system.
- There is a need for more detailed analysis and discussion on the potential impact of the proposed method on the performance of LLMs, particularly in terms of accuracy and efficiency.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the method to a large number of data providers, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The method's effectiveness in scenarios where data from different providers may be imbalanced or similar is not thoroughly discussed, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing methods, which could help in understanding the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on the assumption that data from different providers is sufficiently dissimilar may limit its applicability in scenarios where this assumption does not hold.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be adapted to scenarios where the data from different providers is imbalanced or similar?
- How does the proposed method perform under more sophisticated adversarial attacks, and what are the potential countermeasures against such attacks?
- What are the specific limitations of the proposed method, and how do these limitations impact its practical applicability?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the data providers are not truthful, and what are the potential countermeasures against such scenarios?
- Can the method be adapted to scenarios where the data providers are not known, and how does this affect the accuracy and effectiveness of the method?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the data from different providers is highly similar, and what are the potential impacts on the accuracy and effectiveness of the method?
