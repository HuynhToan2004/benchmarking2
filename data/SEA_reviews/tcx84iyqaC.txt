 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the phenomenon of reward collapse in reward models trained on human preference rankings, where the reward distribution for different prompts converges to a single distribution. This phenomenon, termed "reward collapse," is a critical issue in large language model (LLM) training, particularly in the context of RLHF, where LLMs are fine-tuned using reinforcement learning from human feedback. The paper proposes a prompt-aware utility function to mitigate this issue, demonstrating that this approach can lead to a more varied reward distribution. Theoretical insights are provided to explain the phenomenon and its implications, supported by empirical evidence from experiments using GPT-Neo-1.3B and DeBERTa V3.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel phenomenon called "reward collapse" and provides a theoretical analysis of this phenomenon, which is an important contribution to the field of large language models (LLMs).
- The proposed prompt-aware utility function is a significant contribution that addresses the issue of reward collapse in LLMs, and the paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The experiments conducted are robust and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed prompt-aware utility function in mitigating reward collapse.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the theoretical results and empirical findings, which are crucial for understanding the implications of the reward collapse phenomenon.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical analysis is based on a large number of completions, which might not be practical or applicable in real-world scenarios where the number of completions is typically small.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with the proposed prompt-aware utility function showing only marginal improvements over the fixed loss function.
- The paper's focus on reward collapse might not adequately address other critical issues in RLHF, such as calibration and mode collapse.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the practical implications of the reward collapse phenomenon and how it affects the performance of LLMs in real-world applications.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods for addressing reward collapse, which could have strengthened the paper's argument.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
