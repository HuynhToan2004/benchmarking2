 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called State Chrono Representation (SCR) aimed at enhancing generalization in reinforcement learning by incorporating long-term information into state representations. SCR utilizes a metric-based approach to learn state distances and measurements within a temporal framework, integrating future dynamics and accumulated rewards across current and long-term future states. The method is evaluated in DeepMind Control environments, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance in demanding generalization tasks and scenarios characterized by sparse rewards. Despite its innovative approach, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper, the need for more extensive evaluations, and the potential for overfitting due to the complexity of the loss function.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and detailed explanation of the proposed method.
- The introduction of the chronological embedding and the temporal measurement is innovative, providing a novel approach to representation learning in reinforcement learning.
- The paper includes comprehensive experiments in DeepMind Control environments, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in generalization tasks and scenarios with sparse rewards.
- The ablation study is well-designed, providing insights into the effectiveness of the proposed components.
- The method is well-motivated and provides a novel approach to learning representations that capture long-term information, which is crucial for generalization in reinforcement learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern given the complexity of the loss function.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with the method performing worse than existing baselines in some cases, and the paper does not adequately explain these discrepancies.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the paper, particularly in the definitions and notations used, which could make it difficult for readers to understand the proposed method and its implications.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, which are crucial factors in practical applications.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the hyperparameters used and the specifics of the replay buffer, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the computational complexity of the proposed method and discuss potential strategies for reducing computational costs?
- In the ablation study, why was the choice made to remove the chronological embedding and the temporal measurement, and how does this affect the generalization ability of the method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the replay buffer and the hyperparameters used in the experiments?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting, especially in scenarios with sparse rewards or high-dimensional observations?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential strategies for addressing these limitations?
- In the context of the experimental results, why does the proposed method perform worse than existing baselines in some cases, and how does this affect the interpretation of the results?
