 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for estimating epistemic uncertainty in ensembles using pairwise distance estimators (PaiDEs), which avoids the need for Monte Carlo sampling. The method, which is applicable to ensembles with probabilistic outputs, such as those using normalizing flows, is shown to be computationally efficient and capable of handling high-dimensional data. The authors provide theoretical justifications and empirical evidence to support their claims, comparing the proposed method to Monte Carlo estimators. However, the paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the bias introduced by the PaiDEs and the computational costs associated with the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for estimating epistemic uncertainty in ensembles, specifically using pairwise distance estimators (PaiDEs), which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is computationally efficient, making it suitable for handling high-dimensional data, which is a common challenge in many real-world applications.
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the methodology and results, with a logical flow that aids in understanding the complex mathematical concepts.
- The paper provides a theoretical justification for the proposed method, which enhances its credibility and applicability.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the method across various benchmarks, which supports the claims made in the paper.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the bias introduced by the PaiDEs, which could affect the reliability of the uncertainty estimates, especially in high-dimensional settings.
- There is a lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art methods for estimating epistemic uncertainty, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method's performance.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational costs associated with the PaiDEs, which could be a significant limitation for practical applications.
- The paper's focus on normalizing flows as the only ensemble method for probabilistic outputs is narrow, and it would be beneficial to explore other ensemble methods that could benefit from the PaiDEs.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to replicate the results or fully understand the method's performance.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the bias introduced by the PaiDEs affects the reliability of the uncertainty estimates, especially in high-dimensional settings?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods for estimating epistemic uncertainty, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- What are the specific computational costs associated with the PaiDEs, and how do these costs scale with the number of ensemble members and data dimensions?
- How does the method perform when applied to ensembles other than those based on normalizing flows?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the size and characteristics of the training and test datasets, and the specifics of the ensemble models used?
- How does the method handle different types of uncertainty, such as aleatoric and epistemic uncertainties, and what are the implications of this for the overall performance and reliability of the estimates?
