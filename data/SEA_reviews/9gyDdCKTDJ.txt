 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Gaitor, a system designed to enable quadruped robots to perform continuous transitions between different gaits (trot, crawl, and pace) based on terrain conditions. The system utilizes a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) to learn a latent space that encodes gait information and terrain features, which are then used to generate gait transitions. The VAE is trained on expert demonstrations and can predict gait transitions and adjust swing heights based on terrain features. The system's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on the ANYmal C robot, showing successful transitions between gaits and adaptation to different terrain conditions. However, the paper suffers from unclear writing, lack of detailed experimental results, and insufficient comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to generating continuous gait transitions in quadruped robots, which is a significant advancement over traditional methods that rely on discrete gaits.
- The use of a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) to learn a latent space for gait representation is innovative and allows for the encoding of gait information and terrain features, which can be used to adjust swing heights based on terrain conditions.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the inclusion of figures and tables enhances understanding of the proposed methods and results.
- The methodology is robust, with a focus on using expert demonstrations to train the VAE, which ensures the system learns effective gait transitions.
- The paper presents a novel application of VAEs to quadruped locomotion, which is a promising direction for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, which limits the understanding of how Gaitor's approach differs or improves upon existing solutions.
- There is a lack of quantitative data to support the claims made about the effectiveness of the system, which could include metrics like energy consumption or performance on different types of terrain.
- The writing quality of the paper is poor, with some sections being unclear and difficult to understand, which could hinder the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the reliance on expert demonstrations for training, the need for high-quality terrain data, and the computational demands of the VAE.
- The paper does not discuss the scalability of the method to other types of robots or terrains, which could limit its applicability.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the training process, including the size and diversity of the training dataset, which could affect the generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the system handle transitions between gaits, particularly in scenarios where the transition might not be smooth or natural?
- Can the system be adapted to handle other types of terrain or robots, and what modifications would be required for such adaptations?
- How does the system handle the computational demands of the VAE, particularly in real-time applications?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process, including the size and diversity of the training dataset, and the criteria used for selecting expert demonstrations?
- How does the system handle variations in terrain features, such as slippery or uneven surfaces, and what are the potential impacts on the system's performance?
- Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the VAE, including the architecture and training parameters, to help reproducibility and understanding of the results?
