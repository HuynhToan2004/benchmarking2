 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel loss function, termed "tube loss," designed to estimate prediction intervals (PIs) in regression models. This loss function, which is a continuous variant of the zero-one loss, allows for the simultaneous estimation of lower and upper bounds for the PI, thereby facilitating the optimization of both the width and coverage probability. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the tube loss function through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in presentation, particularly in the mathematical formulations and experimental setups. The experimental results are also seen as insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel continuous loss function, the "tube loss," which is a significant advancement over traditional methods for estimating prediction intervals (PIs).
- The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed method through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing improvements over existing methods.
- The paper provides theoretical guarantees, including Proposition 1, which supports the convergence of the loss function to a specific value as the sample size increases.
- The method is applicable to a variety of machine learning models, including neural networks and LSTMs, which enhances its practical utility.
- The use of a continuous loss function allows for the simultaneous estimation of lower and upper bounds for the PI, which is a significant advantage over previous methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, particularly in the mathematical formulations and experimental setups. This makes it difficult for readers to fully understand the methodology and results.
- The experimental results are insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods, particularly in terms of coverage probability and prediction interval width.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the quality of the estimated PIs.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its practical applicability and potential shortcomings.
- The experimental design is not robust enough to convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the significance of the results and the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the mathematical formulations and experimental setups to enhance the clarity and comprehensibility of the paper?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and the quality of the estimated PIs?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data is highly skewed or non-normal?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including standard deviations and significance testing, to support the claims made about the superiority of the proposed method?
