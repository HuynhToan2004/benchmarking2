 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Time-equivariant Contrastive Learning (TC) method aimed at capturing disease progression in longitudinal medical imaging. TC leverages a temporal equivariance module to predict patient-specific disease progression, using a contrastive learning approach that enhances the model's sensitivity to temporal ordering. The method was tested on two datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to state-of-the-art equivariant contrastive learning methods. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental validation, lack of comparison with a broader range of baseline models, and potential issues with the clarity and depth of its methodological descriptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning equivariant representations for longitudinal medical imaging, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, with a clear description of the proposed Time-equivariant Contrastive Learning (TC) method and its application to medical imaging.
- The paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive review of related work.
- The experimental results are promising, demonstrating the effectiveness of the TC method in capturing disease progression in longitudinal medical imaging.
- The paper includes an ablation study, which helps in understanding the impact of different components of the model.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with a broader range of baseline models, which limits the understanding of the method's relative performance.
- The experimental validation is limited to two datasets, which might not be sufficient to demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential challenges.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed description of the methodology, particularly the equivariant transformation module, which is crucial for understanding the model's operation.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and implementation of the model, which could hinder reproducibility and further research.
- The paper's claims regarding the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which could be improved by including more detailed comparisons and evaluations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and implementation of the model, particularly the equivariant transformation module?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of medical imaging, such as CT or MRI scans?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential strategies for addressing these limitations?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent approaches in the field of equivariant learning, particularly those that do not rely on contrastive learning?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the number of patients and the specifics of the datasets used?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of diseases, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the findings?
