 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel iterative reflexion framework for clinical note summarization, utilizing a large language model (LLM) such as GPT-4 and a smaller content scorer model to enhance the quality of generated notes. This framework incorporates Error2Correct demonstrations to guide the LLM in refining generated content, focusing on improving the accuracy and adherence to medical guidelines. The methodology is tested on both Chinese and English datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance. However, concerns about the novelty of the approach, the fairness of comparisons, and the clarity of presentation were raised, suggesting that the paper might not meet the conference's high standards.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is innovative, combining large and small models to enhance clinical note summarization, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The use of Error2Correct demonstrations to guide the LLM in refining generated content is a novel approach that could potentially improve the accuracy and quality of clinical notes.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on both Chinese and English datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness across different linguistic and cultural contexts.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the framework's components, including the effectiveness of small-model supervision and Error2Correct demonstrations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as similar ideas have been explored in previous works, such as Reflexion, SummIt, and LLM-AUGMENTER.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The comparison with existing methods is not fair, as the proposed method uses a smaller LLM (GPT-4) for initial generation and a separate content scorer model, while the baselines primarily rely on larger LLMs.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of using LLMs for clinical note summarization, which could include issues related to data privacy and bias.
- There are concerns about the fairness of the evaluation, particularly regarding the use of GPT-4 for initial generation, which might not be comparable to the baselines that use larger LLMs.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential negative impacts of using LLMs for clinical note summarization, such as the risk of introducing bias or inaccuracies.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the proposed method differs from similar approaches mentioned in Reflexion, SummIt, and LLM-AUGMENTER?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for bias in the Error2Correct demonstrations, especially if they are sourced from biased datasets?
- In the iterative scheduler, how is the final result selected, and what criteria are used to determine if the model is stuck in an infinite loop?
- How does the proposed method ensure that the generated clinical notes adhere to medical guidelines and standards, especially in cases where these guidelines may vary across different regions or specialties?
- Can you provide more details on the data used for training the small model, and how it was selected to ensure its relevance and quality?
- How does the proposed method handle cases where the generated clinical notes conflict with the original doctor-patient dialogue, and what measures are in place to ensure the accuracy and reliability of the generated notes?
