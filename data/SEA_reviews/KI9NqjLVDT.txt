 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ReMasker, a novel method for imputing missing values in tabular data using a masked autoencoder framework. ReMasker extends existing methods by incorporating a re-masking strategy, where additional values are masked to enhance the model's ability to learn missingness-invariant representations. The method is evaluated on 12 datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in various missingness scenarios. The paper also includes a theoretical analysis to support the method's efficacy, although it lacks comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and does not explore the impact of different masking strategies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, as demonstrated through extensive experiments on 12 datasets, showing its superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper includes a theoretical analysis that supports the effectiveness of the method, and the methodology is flexible and can be adapted to various settings.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art methods, and the method is evaluated on a variety of datasets with different missingness rates and sizes.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of ablation studies that analyze the impact of different hyperparameters, model configurations, and reconstruction losses on the performance of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the impact of different masking strategies.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include its applicability to datasets with different characteristics or its performance under different missingness mechanisms.
- The paper does not explore the impact of the masking ratio on the performance of the method, which could be a significant factor in practical applications.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the scalability of the method with large datasets or its performance in high-dimensional data.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the method and its potential negative societal impacts.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental evidence to support some of the claims made, such as the superiority of the Transformer architecture over other architectures like CNNs.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including computational efficiency and the impact of different masking strategies?
- How does the proposed method perform under different missingness mechanisms, such as MCAR, MAR, and MNAR?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific datasets used and the methodology for generating the missingness patterns?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of missingness, such as missingness due to measurement errors versus missingness due to non-response?
- Could the authors provide more evidence to support the claim that the Transformer architecture is superior to other architectures like CNNs in this context?
- How does the method perform when applied to datasets with different characteristics, such as different missingness rates or different data distributions?
