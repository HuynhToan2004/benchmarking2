 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel out-of-distribution (OOD) detection method using tree-based ensemble learning, specifically focusing on computing pairwise hamming distances of tree embeddings derived from training samples. This method, named TOOD, aims to improve the interpretability and robustness of OOD detection compared to neural network-based methods. The approach is validated on various datasets, including tabular, image, and text data, showing promising results in terms of accuracy and efficiency. However, concerns are raised regarding the novelty and depth of the theoretical analysis, the clarity of the methodological presentation, and the robustness of the results against different types of OOD data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the method is simple and easy to implement, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The use of tree-based ensemble learning for OOD detection is innovative and offers a novel approach to the problem, which is supported by thorough experimental evaluations.
- The method is robust to adversarial attacks, which is a significant advantage over some neural network-based methods.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the proposed method, including theoretical analysis and empirical results that demonstrate the method's effectiveness.
- The method's interpretability and efficiency are highlighted, which are critical for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning its applicability to different types of OOD data and its robustness against various attacks.
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as similar approaches using tree embeddings have been previously explored, and the theoretical analysis provided is not sufficiently novel or insightful.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with some results showing only marginal improvements or even inferior performance in certain scenarios.
- The paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in the presentation of the methodology and the experimental results, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant OOD detection methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in Theorem 2 and how they affect the practical applicability of the method?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of OOD data, such as those from different domains or with different feature distributions?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other tree-based OOD detection methods to better position their work within the existing literature?
- How does the method handle OOD data that is not linearly separable, and what are the implications of this for the method's effectiveness?
- Can the authors elaborate on the method's robustness against different types of attacks, such as adversarial attacks or outlier attacks?
- How does the method compare in terms of computational efficiency and training time with other OOD detection methods, particularly those based on neural networks?
