 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MixDiff, a novel out-of-distribution (OOD) detection method that operates in constrained access environments where only model inputs and outputs are accessible. MixDiff leverages the perturb-and-compare approach, comparing the relative differences in model outputs when a target sample is perturbed with a similar in-distribution (ID) sample. This method does not require model parameters or activations, making it model-agnostic and applicable across various datasets in vision and text domains. The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical evidence demonstrating the effectiveness of MixDiff in enhancing OOD detection performance, particularly in scenarios where existing methods struggle.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to OOD detection in constrained access environments, which is a significant and relevant problem in real-world applications.
- The proposed method is model-agnostic and can be applied to various datasets in both vision and text domains, demonstrating its versatility.
- Theoretical analysis and empirical evidence are provided to support the effectiveness of the proposed method, which enhances the credibility of the approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The methodology is simple yet effective, and the experimental results show consistent improvements over baseline methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the method may not perform optimally or where it could be misused.
- There is a need for more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art OOD detection methods, including those that use richer information beyond model inputs and outputs.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method in larger or more complex models, which could impact its practical applicability.
- The theoretical analysis provided is considered heuristic and lacks rigorous mathematical proof, which could undermine the credibility of the method.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the practical implementation of the method, particularly in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential ethical implications of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in certain applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method in larger or more complex models?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the model's predictions are already highly confident, potentially masking OOD samples?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implementation of the method, particularly in real-world scenarios where model access is restricted?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art OOD detection methods, particularly those that use richer information beyond model inputs and outputs?
- Can the authors provide a more rigorous mathematical proof or a deeper theoretical analysis to support the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method address potential ethical concerns, particularly in applications where misuse could lead to significant harm?
