 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Incremental Randomized Smoothing (IRS) for certifying the robustness of DNNs against adversarial perturbations. IRS reuses the certification guarantees from the original smoothed model to certify an approximated model with significantly fewer samples, reducing computational costs. This method is particularly useful when the DNN is modified, such as through quantization or pruning, as it allows for efficient re-certification without recomputing from scratch. The paper provides theoretical insights and empirical evaluations demonstrating the effectiveness of IRS, showing up to 4.1x certification speedup over traditional methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a critical issue in the field of adversarial machine learning by focusing on the computational efficiency of randomized smoothing-based certification methods, which are crucial for ensuring robustness in deep neural networks.
- The proposed method, IRS, is innovative and provides a significant speedup in certification processes, making it more efficient and practical for real-world applications.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The experimental results are robust and demonstrate the effectiveness of the IRS method in reducing computational costs while maintaining robustness guarantees.
- The paper presents a novel approach to incremental randomized smoothing certification, which is a significant advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other incremental certification methods, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the advantages and limitations of the proposed method.
- There is a need for more detailed empirical evaluations, especially in terms of the sensitivity of IRS to different hyperparameters and the scalability of the method across different network sizes and types of perturbations.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on a cache for storing information from the original model's certification could introduce additional complexity and potential memory overhead, which might not be practical for all applications.
- The paper does not discuss the potential impact of different quantization methods on the effectiveness of IRS, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the IRS method performs with different quantization methods, especially those that might not be as effective in reducing the zeta value?
- How does the IRS method handle different types of perturbations beyond Gaussian noise? Could it be extended to other types of perturbations, such as salt-and-pepper noise or impulse noise?
- In the experimental results, how does the performance of IRS compare to other incremental certification methods, such as those discussed in the literature?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, especially in terms of its application in real-world systems?
- How sensitive is IRS to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the cache size and the number of samples used for certification?
- Can the authors provide more details on the memory overhead introduced by the cache mechanism, and how this might impact the practicality of using IRS in large-scale applications?
