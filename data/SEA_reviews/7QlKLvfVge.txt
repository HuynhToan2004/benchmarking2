 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Directional Rank Reduction (DRR) for defending against backdoor attacks in neural networks. This method focuses on identifying and eliminating the toxic direction, which maximizes the mean difference between benign and poisoned features, thus reducing the attack success rate while maintaining comparable accuracy. The approach is theoretically justified and empirically validated through extensive experiments on the CIFAR-10 dataset, showing superior performance compared to existing pruning-based defenses. However, concerns are raised about the method's scalability and applicability to more complex datasets and models, as well as its effectiveness against more advanced backdoor attacks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Directional Rank Reduction (DRR), for defending against backdoor attacks, which is both theoretically justified and empirically validated through extensive experiments.
- The approach is well-motivated, clearly explained, and the theoretical analysis is sound, providing a robust foundation for the method.
- The method is simple to implement and is shown to outperform existing pruning-based defenses, indicating its practical utility.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, which is crucial for reproducibility and dissemination.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's scalability is a concern, particularly in terms of computational complexity and memory requirements, which could limit its practical applicability to larger, more complex datasets and models.
- The effectiveness of the method against more advanced backdoor attacks, such as those using dynamic triggers or multiple triggers, is not thoroughly evaluated, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The method's reliance on the availability of a clean dataset and the accuracy of the direction estimation from poisoned data could introduce biases or errors in the defense mechanism.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the method, such as the potential for overfitting or the practical challenges in obtaining clean data in real-world scenarios.
- The method's performance is only evaluated on the CIFAR-10 dataset, which is relatively simple compared to other benchmarks like ImageNet, raising questions about its effectiveness on more complex datasets.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform on more complex datasets, such as ImageNet, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in applying the method to such datasets?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and memory requirements of the method, especially in relation to the size of the dataset and the model architecture?
- How does the method handle the potential biases or errors introduced by the direction estimation from poisoned data, and what measures can be taken to mitigate these issues?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical challenges in obtaining clean data in real-world scenarios and discuss potential strategies for addressing these challenges?
- Given the method's reliance on the Separation Dominance Assumption, how sensitive is the method to the quality of the poisoned dataset, and what are the implications if this assumption does not hold?
