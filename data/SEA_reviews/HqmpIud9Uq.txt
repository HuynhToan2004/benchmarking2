 **Summary:**
The paper introduces COREP, a novel method for addressing non-stationarity in reinforcement learning (RL) by leveraging causal relationships in state transitions. COREP utilizes a dual Graph Attention Network (GAT) structure, consisting of a core-GAT for learning essential graph representations and a general-GAT for compensating for potential information loss. This method is supported by a theoretical analysis and empirical evidence showing that COREP outperforms existing methods in handling non-stationarity across various environments. Despite its innovative approach and theoretical backing, the paper faces criticism for its clarity, scalability, and the practical applicability of its assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for addressing non-stationarity in reinforcement learning (RL) by leveraging causal relationships in state transitions, which is a novel approach that has not been explored in depth before.
- The proposed method, COREP, is well-supported by a theoretical analysis that provides both inspiration and theoretical support for the algorithm.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm in handling non-stationarity, showing significant improvements over existing methods in various environments.
- The paper is well-written and provides a clear and detailed explanation of the method, making it accessible to readers.
- The use of a dual Graph Attention Network (GAT) structure, consisting of a core-GAT and a general-GAT, is innovative and provides a robust framework for addressing non-stationarity in RL.
- The paper includes a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is commendable for its transparency and honesty.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity and readability, particularly in the introduction and some sections of the methodology. The notation and definitions are not consistently explained, making it difficult for readers to follow the arguments and understand the contributions of the paper.
- The scalability of the proposed method is a concern, especially in high-dimensional state spaces, where the graph-based representation could be computationally intensive.
- The paper assumes that the non-stationarity can be represented by the change of binary masks in the transition function, which may not hold true in all real-world scenarios.
- The practical applicability of the assumptions made in the paper, such as the assumption of invariance within each environment, is questionable and may limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of scalability and the practical implications of its assumptions.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with some figures and results being difficult to interpret or understand.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the meaning and implications of the "masks" mentioned in the paper, particularly in the context of non-stationarity and the transition function?
- How does the proposed method handle high-dimensional state spaces, and what measures are in place to ensure scalability?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the assumption of invariance within each environment, and discuss their practical applicability?
- In the experimental setup, how are the coefficients for non-stationarity sampled, and what impact does this have on the results?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including scalability and the practical implications of its assumptions?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, particularly in high-dimensional state spaces?
