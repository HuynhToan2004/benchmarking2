 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of orthogonal initialization in deep neural networks (DNNs) to mitigate the issues of gradient explosion and rank collapse, which are commonly associated with batch normalization. The authors propose a theoretical framework that leverages orthogonal weight matrices and a new activation shaping method to ensure stable gradient propagation and orthogonalization of representations. This approach is tested across various datasets, including CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, and is compared against traditional batch normalization methods. The paper also discusses the practical implications of these findings, suggesting that orthogonal initialization could be a viable alternative to traditional batch normalization in deep learning architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem in the field of deep learning, namely the issues of gradient explosion and rank collapse in deep neural networks, which are crucial for practical applications.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust and well-supported, with clear and concise proofs that are easy to follow.
- The paper is well-written, making complex theoretical concepts accessible and understandable, which is a notable strength given the complexity of the subject matter.
- The empirical results presented are convincing and show that the proposed methods can effectively mitigate the issues of gradient explosion and rank collapse, as evidenced by experimental results.
- The paper is original in its approach, combining orthogonal weight initialization with batch normalization, which is a novel combination that could lead to new insights and methodologies in deep learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on orthogonal weight initialization might be seen as a limitation, as it does not consider other types of initialization, such as Gaussian or Xavier initialization, which are commonly used in practice.
- The experimental results are limited to small-scale networks, which might not generalize well to larger, more complex networks, such as those used in large-scale applications like image classification.
- The paper's theoretical claims are somewhat limited by the assumption of orthogonal weights, which might not be practical or realistic in many deep learning scenarios.
- The paper's claims about the impact of orthogonal initialization on gradient explosion and rank collapse are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which are based on simple datasets and do not adequately demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods in more complex or realistic settings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the practical implications and limitations of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of their applicability to real-world deep learning problems and their scalability to larger, more complex networks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors elaborate on why orthogonal weight initialization was chosen over other types of initialization, such as Gaussian or Xavier initialization?
- How do the results of the paper translate to larger, more complex networks, such as those used in large-scale image classification tasks?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the training curves and performance metrics for different network sizes and depths?
- Is there a possibility to extend the theoretical analysis to include more general types of weight initialization, such as Gaussian or Xavier initialization?
- How does the paper's theoretical analysis compare to existing works on the implicit bias towards orthogonality in optimization, particularly in the context of deep learning?
- Can the authors discuss the practical implications and limitations of the proposed methods, including their scalability and applicability to real-world deep learning problems?
