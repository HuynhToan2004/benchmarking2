 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for single image super-resolution (SISR) using multi-modal prompts to enhance the learning of discriminative features. The proposed Dynamic Correlation Module (DCM) integrates a Spatially Multi-Modal Attention Module and a Prompt-Guided Dynamic Convolution Module to leverage multi-modal information effectively. This method is designed to be a plug-and-play module that can be integrated into existing SR networks to improve performance. The DCM is evaluated on various benchmarks, showing significant improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the methodological description, and the need for a more rigorous experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for using multi-modal prompts to guide the SR process.
- The proposed method is technically sound and effective, with significant improvements over existing methods.
- The integration of the Dynamic Correlation Module (DCM) into existing SR networks is innovative, allowing for the utilization of multi-modal information to enhance SR performance.
- The paper includes comprehensive experimental results and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The idea of using multi-modal information to enhance SR performance is novel and interesting, with potential for significant impact on the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it seems to be a simple combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of quantitative results and visual comparisons.
- The methodology section is not clearly described, particularly the integration of the DCM into existing SR networks and the training process for the prompt-guided dynamic convolution module.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for overfitting due to the use of large-scale datasets for training.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of the impact of different prompts on SR performance and a discussion on the generalizability of the proposed method across different datasets and SR tasks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process for the prompt-guided dynamic convolution module, particularly how the weights are learned and how they affect the SR performance?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of prompts, such as textual versus visual? What are the implications of using different types of prompts on the SR performance?
- The paper mentions the use of a large-scale vision-language model for extracting prompt embeddings. Could the authors clarify which specific model was used and how it was trained?
- The paper claims to be the first to introduce multi-modal prompts into convolutional kernel estimation. Can the authors provide a detailed explanation of how this innovation contributes to the effectiveness of the DCM?
- The paper mentions that the proposed method can be applied to various SR frameworks. Could the authors provide more details on how the DCM can be integrated into different SR frameworks and what are the implications for the SR performance?
- The paper does not discuss the potential for overfitting due to the use of large-scale datasets for training. Can the authors provide more insights into how they addressed this issue and what steps were taken to ensure the robustness of the trained model?
