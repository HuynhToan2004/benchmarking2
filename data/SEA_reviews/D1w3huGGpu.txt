 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel modular architecture for embodied agents that enables compositional generalization across different perceptual and action spaces. It employs a shared controller connected to differentiable modules for each space, facilitating end-to-end learning and generalization to unseen combinations. The authors validate this approach in a custom environment, demonstrating that their method outperforms individual agents trained on specific combinations. The paper also explores the benefits of the modular architecture in scenarios where new perceptual spaces are introduced, showing that the controller and action modules can be reused without retraining. However, the evaluation is limited to a single environment, which raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel modular architecture for embodied agents that enables compositional generalization, a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, with clear explanations of its benefits and a well-structured presentation that aids in understanding.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, showing that the modular architecture outperforms individual agents trained on specific combinations, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and descriptions that aid in understanding the proposed method and its implementation.
- The use of a shared controller connected to differentiable modules for each space is a novel approach that allows for end-to-end learning and generalization to unseen combinations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a single environment, which raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other methods, such as those that use differentiable interfaces or other types of modular architectures.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the specific contributions of the method, particularly in how it compares to existing methods and in the technical details of its implementation.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed descriptions of the environments and the tasks, as well as a more rigorous evaluation of the generalization capabilities of the method.
- The method's reliance on a shared controller and action modules may limit its adaptability to different types of tasks, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the method handles different types of tasks.
