 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SCOTCH, a novel method for learning graphical models from time series data using stochastic differential equations (SDEs). SCOTCH employs a variational inference approach to infer the posterior distribution over graph structures, which is structurally identifiable and consistent under certain conditions. Theoretical analyses are provided to support the method's theoretical soundness, including proofs of structural identifiability and consistency under infinite data limits. Empirical evaluations on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the method's superiority over existing methods. However, the paper has been critiqued for its presentation, particularly in the clarity of assumptions and the organization of content, and for its limited novelty in using SDEs for structure learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for learning graphical models from time series data using stochastic differential equations (SDEs), which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method, SCOTCH, is shown to be structurally identifiable and consistent under certain conditions, providing a theoretical foundation for its application.
- Extensive empirical evaluations on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods, supporting its practical utility.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, which enhances its accessibility and readability.
- Theoretical analysis provided in the paper supports the proposed method's theoretical soundness, including proofs of structural identifiability and consistency under infinite data limits.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in the clarity of assumptions and the organization of content. Some sections and equations are difficult to understand without additional context or explanation.
- The novelty of the method is limited as it primarily uses SDEs for structure learning, which is a well-known approach. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of its novel contributions compared to existing methods.
- The paper's empirical evaluation could be strengthened by including more baseline methods and real-world datasets to better demonstrate the method's effectiveness in diverse scenarios.
- There are some typographical errors and inconsistencies in the notation and assumptions used in the paper, which could confuse readers and detract from the paper's credibility.
- The paper's reliance on variational inference and the use of neural networks for parameterization might limit its applicability to scenarios where these methods are not feasible or appropriate.

- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency and scalability of the SCOTCH algorithm, especially in terms of its practical applicability to large-scale datasets?
