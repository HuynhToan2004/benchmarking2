 **Summary:**
The paper explores the problem of estimating the total variation (TV) distance between two discrete distributions using a conditional sampling oracle. It introduces a novel algorithm that makes only polynomial queries to this oracle, significantly reducing the number of queries required compared to previous methods. The algorithm is based on a reduction to the problem of estimating the probability of a single event, which is then used to approximate the TV distance. The paper also includes experimental results that demonstrate the practical applicability of the algorithm, showing that it can handle high-dimensional distributions with a small number of samples. However, the paper's experimental evaluation is limited, and it lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and interesting problem in the context of distribution testing, which is of both theoretical and practical relevance.
- The authors introduce a novel algorithm for estimating the total variation distance between two distributions using a subcube conditioning oracle, which is a notable contribution to the field.
- The algorithm is well-explained, with clear explanations of the intuition behind the approach and the technical details of the algorithm.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the proofs are detailed, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the results.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the practical applicability of the algorithm in high-dimensional settings, which is a significant advantage over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of query complexity and computational efficiency.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, focusing only on a few benchmarks and not comparing the proposed algorithm to other methods in a broader range of scenarios.
- There is a noticeable gap between the theoretical results and the practical implementation, particularly in the number of queries required to achieve a desired level of accuracy.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithm, including scenarios where it may not perform optimally or where it may not be applicable.
- The paper could be improved by providing a more detailed discussion on the implications of the results, particularly in terms of their practical utility and potential applications.
