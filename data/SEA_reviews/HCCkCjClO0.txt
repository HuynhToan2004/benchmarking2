 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an Online Weight Approximation (OWA) method for continual learning, which approximates the weight trajectory of a neural network using a finite-dimensional basis. This approach allows for the efficient storage of weights across multiple tasks, potentially reducing memory requirements. The method is tested across various continual learning benchmarks, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods like replay. However, the paper suffers from several issues, including unclear presentation, lack of comprehensive experimental validation, and insufficient discussion on the novelty and comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive introduction to the online approximation of functions.
- The proposed method is novel and shows potential, particularly in its ability to reduce memory usage by approximating weight trajectories.
- The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the method's effectiveness, showing improvements over vanilla and replay methods in certain scenarios.
- The application of online approximation of functions to continual learning is innovative and could be a significant advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The presentation of the paper is not clear, with some sections, such as the explanation of the OWA method and the experimental setup, being difficult to understand.
- The paper lacks comprehensive experimental validation, particularly in terms of testing the method across a wide range of continual learning settings and using larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing continual learning methods, nor does it discuss the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the method.
- The method's performance is not consistently superior to existing methods, as shown in some experiments where it underperforms or shows only marginal improvements.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, which is a critical aspect of continual learning.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify how the OWA method is applied in practice, particularly how the weights are approximated and how the task ID is retrieved?
- How does the method perform when the task ID is not available, and how does it handle the absence of clear task boundaries?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific continual learning benchmarks used and the reasons for choosing these datasets?
- How does the proposed method compare with other continual learning methods, particularly those that address catastrophic forgetting?
- Can you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the method and how these might be mitigated?
- Why is the method not consistently superior to existing methods, and what are the specific advantages of the OWA approach over other continual learning methods?
