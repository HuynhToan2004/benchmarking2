 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FROSTER, a novel framework for open-vocabulary action recognition, leveraging the CLIP model's robust generalization capabilities. The method employs a feature distillation approach, where the CLIP model acts as a teacher to guide the fine-tuned model in maintaining generalizability. The framework also includes a residual feature distillation method to balance generalizability and video-specific learning. Extensive experiments across various datasets demonstrate FROSTER's effectiveness, showcasing state-of-the-art performance. However, the paper's novelty and the clarity of its contributions are questioned, as the methodology appears to be a straightforward application of existing techniques.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and presents a clear methodology.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of a frozen CLIP model as a teacher to guide feature learning is innovative and well-explained.
- The proposed residual feature distillation method is simple yet effective, balancing generalizability and video-specific learning.
- The paper is original in its approach to open-vocabulary action recognition, utilizing a frozen CLIP model as a teacher, and demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on various datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it primarily combines existing techniques like feature distillation and CLIP models without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The performance gains on some datasets, such as SSV2, are minimal, which raises questions about the method's effectiveness across different datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant methods, such as Open-VCILP and other state-of-the-art models.
- The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed components, such as the residual feature distillation method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its performance in different scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets, especially those with different characteristics from those used in the experiments?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other recent and relevant methods, such as Open-VCILP, to better understand the relative performance of their approach?
- What are the specific advantages of using a residual feature distillation method over other distillation techniques, and how does it contribute to the overall effectiveness of the proposed method?
