 **Summary:**
The paper introduces EVEREST, a novel method for video representation learning that addresses the computational inefficiencies of masked video autoencoders (MVAEs) by selectively masking uninformative spatiotemporal tokens. EVEREST utilizes a distance metric to identify and mask redundant tokens, thereby reducing computational and memory requirements. This methodology is demonstrated to be effective across various datasets, including K400, UCF101, and HMDB51, achieving competitive results while significantly reducing computational overhead. The paper also includes an ablation study and comparisons with existing methods, highlighting the method's efficiency and effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and effectively communicates the problem of redundancy in masked video autoencoders (MVAEs) and the proposed solution.
- The proposed method, EVEREST, is simple yet effective, as demonstrated by its ability to reduce computational and memory requirements while maintaining or improving performance compared to existing MVAEs.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments, including ablation studies, that validate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and tasks.
- EVEREST's efficiency is highlighted by its ability to reduce the number of training epochs needed to achieve comparable or better performance than other methods, such as VideoMAE.
- The paper addresses a significant issue in MVAEs, specifically the redundancy in masked tokens, and provides a novel approach to selectively mask these tokens based on their informative value.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how EVEREST performs on more challenging datasets like Ego4D, where the redundancy-robust token selection might not be as effective due to the complexity of the dataset.
- There is a need for a more detailed comparison of EVEREST with other methods that use motion-based token selection, such as K-centered patch sampling, to better understand the relative advantages of EVEREST.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the redundancy-robust token selection process, particularly how it is implemented and how it affects the model's performance.
- The paper does not provide sufficient implementation details, such as the specifics of the distance metric used and the computational requirements of the method.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the proposed method compares to other recent works that address similar issues in video representation learning, such as MGM, MME, and MVD.
- The paper does not include a detailed analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters on the model's performance, which could provide valuable insights into the method's sensitivity and robustness.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how EVEREST performs on more challenging datasets like Ego4D and discuss the potential limitations of the redundancy-robust token selection in such contexts?
- How does the proposed method compare to other motion-based token selection methods, such as K-centered patch sampling, in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the redundancy-robust token selection process and discuss how it affects the model's performance in different scenarios?
- How does the choice of distance metric (L2 distance) impact the model's performance, and have other metrics been considered?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational requirements and efficiency of EVEREST with other methods, particularly in terms of training time and memory usage?
- How does the proposed method handle the selection of informative frames, and what are the implications of this selection process on the model's performance?
