 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SMORe, a novel method for offline goal-conditioned reinforcement learning (GCRL) that addresses the challenges of occupancy matching and distribution mismatch in offline datasets. SMORe employs a discriminator-free approach by matching mixture distributions, which is a departure from traditional methods that require learning discriminators. The method is evaluated against several benchmarks and shows significant improvements over existing methods, particularly in the robustness to decreasing expert coverage and stochastic environments. The paper also presents a theoretical framework that supports the method's effectiveness and offers a novel perspective on GCRL by focusing on mixture-distribution matching.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the proposed method, SMORe, is well-motivated and supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence.
- SMORe is a discriminator-free method, which is a significant advantage over traditional methods that rely on discriminator learning, and it is applicable to a variety of f-divergences.
- The method is evaluated on a variety of offline GCRL tasks and shows significant improvements over existing methods, particularly in robustness to decreasing expert coverage and stochastic environments.
- The paper provides a novel perspective on GCRL by focusing on mixture-distribution matching, which is a departure from traditional methods and offers a new approach to the problem.
- The theoretical framework presented supports the effectiveness of the method, and the paper is well-organized, making it easy to understand the contributions and the methodology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications for certain methodological choices, such as the use of Expectile regression and the choice of f-divergence.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of SMORe, which could provide a more balanced view of the method's strengths and weaknesses.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse environments and a broader range of baselines to better compare the method's performance against existing approaches.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the results, particularly in terms of the statistical significance of the improvements and the impact of different hyperparameters on the method's performance.
- There are some minor typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the choice of Expectile regression and how it affects the performance of SMORe?
- How does the choice of f-divergence impact the performance of SMORe, and could the authors discuss the implications of using different f-divergences?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of SMORe and how these might affect its practical application?
- How does the method perform when the goal distribution is not uniform, and what are the implications of this for the method's effectiveness?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of different hyperparameters on the method's performance, and provide guidelines for selecting these parameters in practical applications?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of baselines and the environments used, and discuss how these choices affect the comparability and generalizability of the results?
