 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel model-free algorithm named Memory-Efficient Nash Q-Learning (ME-Nash-QL) for two-player zero-sum Markov games (TZMG). ME-Nash-QL is designed to achieve a space complexity of O(SABH), a significant improvement over previous methods. The algorithm's sample complexity is also optimized, with a bound of O(H4SAH2/ε2), which is competitive with existing methods. The authors claim that ME-Nash-QL outputs both a single Markov policy and a Nash policy, which is a departure from previous algorithms that either output nested mixture policies or non-Markov policies. The paper also provides theoretical guarantees and proofs of the algorithm's effectiveness, including a discussion on the computational complexity and memory efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel model-free algorithm, ME-Nash-QL, for two-player zero-sum Markov games, which achieves significant improvements in space complexity (O(SABH)) compared to previous methods.
- The algorithm's sample complexity is also optimized, with a bound of O(H4SAH2/ε2), which is competitive with existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The authors have provided a detailed analysis of the algorithm's performance, including a discussion on the computational complexity and memory efficiency.
- The paper includes a discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithm and suggestions for future work.
- The algorithm is designed to output both a single Markov policy and a Nash policy, which is a departure from previous algorithms that either output nested mixture policies or non-Markov policies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed algorithm, which could help in understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- The algorithm's computational complexity, while improved, is still relatively high compared to some existing methods.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by including more detailed explanations of the algorithms and theoretical results, as well as clearer comparisons with existing methods.
- There are some minor typographical errors and inconsistencies in the notation and definitions used in the paper.
- The paper's contributions are somewhat limited by the specific setting of two-player zero-sum Markov games, which may not generalize well to other multi-agent settings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the proposed algorithm and the FTRL algorithm, particularly in terms of the algorithm's reliance on the assumption of a finite horizon?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare in terms of sample complexity and computational efficiency with other model-based and model-free algorithms for multi-agent reinforcement learning?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the algorithms and theoretical results, possibly including a high-level overview in the main text and more detailed proofs in the appendix?
- How does the algorithm handle scenarios where the state space is large or infinite, and what are the implications for its practical application in such settings?
- Can the authors elaborate on the advantages of the ME-Nash-QL algorithm over existing methods, particularly in terms of its sample complexity and computational efficiency?
