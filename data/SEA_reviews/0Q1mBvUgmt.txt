 **Summary:**
The paper introduces VIPER, a novel framework designed to address the challenges of time series forecasting by decomposing input sequences into trend and seasonal components. The trend component is processed through a moving average kernel, while the seasonal component is transformed using Fourier Transform and processed through a Temporal Aggregation Block. This block employs a Global Self-Attention mechanism to capture both intraperiod-variation and interperiod-variation, enhancing the model's ability to capture complex temporal dynamics. The VIPER framework is evaluated against several benchmark datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods. However, the paper's contribution is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods, particularly TimesNet, and the lack of comprehensive ablation studies to substantiate the effectiveness of the proposed components.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the experimental results are convincing.
- The proposed method is simple, yet effective, and the idea of decomposing the time series into trend and seasonal components is intuitive and reasonable.
- The experiments are conducted on a variety of datasets, and the results are promising, showing that the proposed method can outperform existing models.
- The paper addresses an important problem in time series forecasting, which is the input length bottleneck problem, and provides a novel solution to this issue.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study, particularly in Section 4.6.1, which makes it difficult to understand the individual contributions of the proposed components.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it closely resembles TimesNet, with only minor differences in the Temporal Aggregation Block.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of parameter redundancy, which is a significant concern in transformer-based models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and a comparison with more recent state-of-the-art models.
- The experimental setup and results could be improved, particularly in terms of fairness and the clarity of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the limitations of the proposed method and how these limitations affect its applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can you clarify the experimental setup, particularly in the comparison with other models, to ensure fairness and accuracy in the results?
- How does the Temporal Aggregation Block contribute to the overall performance of the model, and could you provide more detailed ablation studies to support this claim?
- Could you elaborate on the novelty of the proposed method and how it differs significantly from existing methods like TimesNet?
