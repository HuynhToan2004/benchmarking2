 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Retroformer, a novel framework aimed at enhancing the performance of large language models (LLMs) in task-oriented environments. Retroformer leverages a retrospective model that refines the prompts given to LLMs based on environmental feedback, employing a policy gradient approach. This method allows for iterative refinement of the retrospective model without accessing or modifying the parameters of the actor LLM. The framework is evaluated across various environments, including HotPotQA, AlfWorld, and WebShop, showing significant improvements over baseline methods. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty, as it builds on existing methods like Reflexion and ReAct, and for its marginal performance gains over strong baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed framework and methodology.
- The idea of using a retrospective model to refine prompts for LLMs is novel and well-motivated, offering a promising approach to improving the performance of LLMs in task-oriented environments.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it outperforms existing baselines such as ReAct and Reflexion.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the performance of the retrospective model and its ability to generate actionable insights, which is crucial for improving the performance of LLMs.
- The use of a retrospective model for refining prompts is a significant advancement in the field, as it addresses the challenge of credit assignment and provides a more effective way to learn from environment feedback.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other methods such as Reflexion, particularly in terms of performance and the effectiveness of the retrospective model in different scenarios.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods like Reflexion, with minimal modifications to the retrospective model.
- The performance gains of the proposed method are marginal, and the method does not significantly outperform strong baselines like ReAct and Reflexion.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational costs associated with the use of the retrospective model, which could be a significant concern in real-world applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as the reliance on a strong retrospective model for effective performance and the potential issues with credit assignment and root cause analysis.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with Reflexion, particularly in terms of performance and the effectiveness of the retrospective model in different scenarios?
- How does the retrospective model perform in scenarios where the credit assignment is challenging, and how does it handle cases where the root cause of failure is not clear?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the computational costs associated with the use of the retrospective model, and discuss potential strategies for reducing these costs in practical applications?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of LLMs, such as those with different parameter sizes or architectures?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending the method to include other types of feedback or reward signals, and how this might impact the performance of the retrospective model?
