 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel meta-inverse constrained reinforcement learning (M-ICRL) framework aimed at learning reward and constraint functions from expert demonstrations across multiple tasks. It formulates a bi-level optimization problem where the upper level learns a meta-prior over reward functions and the lower level learns a meta-prior over constraints. The M-ICRL algorithm is proposed, along with theoretical guarantees for convergence and generalization error quantification. The paper also provides empirical validation of the algorithm's effectiveness in adapting to new tasks with limited data. However, the paper has been critiqued for its presentation, lack of clarity in some theoretical claims, and the need for more robust experimental validations, particularly in more complex and realistic environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel meta-learning framework for inverse constrained reinforcement learning (ICRL), which extends ICRL to multiple tasks and leverages meta-learning to learn reward and constraint functions from expert demonstrations.
- The formulation of the bi-level optimization problem is novel, and the algorithm proposed is well-explained, making it easy to follow.
- The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the convergence of the algorithm and quantifies the generalization error to an arbitrary new task, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm in adapting to new tasks from limited data, showing promising performance in both simulated and real-world environments.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the challenges in computing hyper-gradients and the solutions to these challenges, which are critical for the application of the proposed algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some theoretical claims, particularly in the proofs and assumptions used, which could benefit from more detailed explanations or examples to aid understanding.
- The experimental setup is limited to simple environments, and the results are not compared against a broader range of baselines, which could undermine the perceived effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, which is crucial for practical implementation in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the algorithm performs in more complex and realistic environments, such as partially observable MDPs.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions and proofs used in the theoretical claims, particularly in Lemma 1 and Theorem 1, to enhance the understanding of the theoretical contributions?
- How does the proposed algorithm perform in more complex and realistic environments, such as partially observable MDPs?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm against existing methods to better understand its practical implications?
- In the experimental setup, could the authors consider using a broader range of baselines and more complex environments to validate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm more robustly?
