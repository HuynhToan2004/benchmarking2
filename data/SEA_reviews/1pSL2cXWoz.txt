 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection using a Bregman divergence-based framework. It proposes a new method that models the ID class-conditional distribution as a member of the exponential family, where the Bregman divergence is the KL divergence. The method employs an importance sampling technique to estimate the partition function, which is crucial for density estimation. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms existing baselines on several benchmarks, including CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, and shows potential for further improvement in long-tailed OOD detection scenarios. The paper is well-written, with clear theoretical justifications and empirical evidence supporting the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear motivation and a good explanation of the proposed method.
- The theoretical analysis is well-founded, with a clear connection between Bregman divergence and the exponential family of distributions, which is a novel approach in the context of OOD detection.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on various OOD detection benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper provides a thorough comparison with existing methods, highlighting the advantages of the proposed method.
- The method is theoretically grounded, with a focus on density estimation which is often overlooked in previous methods.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments, including ablation studies, which help in understanding the impact of different components of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could be a significant drawback for readers looking for a comprehensive understanding of the method's applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The methodology section lacks clarity on the practical implementation of the proposed method, particularly how the optimal value of p is determined for each dataset and the computational cost of the importance sampling approach.
- The paper does not include experiments on larger datasets such as ImageNet, which could help in validating the scalability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the method handles the class-imbalanced data scenario, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the method, which could include the assumptions made about the ID data distribution and the impact of different choices of Bregman divergence on the method's performance.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples in the methodology section to clarify the practical implementation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the optimal value of p is determined for each dataset and discuss the computational cost of the importance sampling approach?
- How does the proposed method handle the class-imbalanced data scenario, and what are the potential impacts on the method's performance?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the method performs on larger datasets such as ImageNet?
- What are the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how do these impact the method's applicability and performance?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of different choices of Bregman divergence on the method's performance and the implications of these choices on the method's applicability?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the ID data distribution is not uniform, and what are the potential implications of this on the method's performance?
