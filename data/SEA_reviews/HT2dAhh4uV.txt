 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to object-centric learning by incorporating a composition path into the training process, which encourages the encoder to learn compositional representations by maximizing the likelihood of composite images. This method is distinct from traditional auto-encoding objectives, as it explicitly optimizes for compositionality. The paper demonstrates that this approach can enhance the robustness of object-centric learning by reducing sensitivity to hyper-parameter choices and architectural biases. The method is evaluated on four datasets, showing consistent improvements over existing methods. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of theoretical justification and clarity in certain methodological details, such as the definition of "composability" and the rationale behind specific methodological choices.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to object-centric learning by explicitly optimizing for compositionality, which is a significant departure from traditional auto-encoding objectives.
- The methodology is well-articulated and clearly presented, making it accessible to readers and facilitating reproducibility.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through robust empirical evaluations, showing consistent improvements over existing methods.
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in object-centric learning, namely the sensitivity of current approaches to hyper-parameter choices and architectural biases, and proposes a method that reduces these sensitivities.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in terms of segmentation metrics and robustness to various hyper-parameters.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive theoretical justification for the proposed method, particularly in explaining why the proposed method is effective and how it improves over existing methods.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definition of "composability" and its relation to the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the implementation of the method, particularly in the mixing strategy for composing slot representations.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for overfitting and the computational cost of the method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the experimental setup, particularly in the description of the datasets and the evaluation metrics used.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work and a more detailed comparison with existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the mixing strategy for composing slot representations and the rationale behind the specific choices made?
- How does the proposed method compare with other approaches that also use a generative prior, such as those mentioned in related work?
- Could the authors clarify the definition of "composability" and its relation to the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of overfitting, particularly in complex scenes with many objects?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the datasets used, the evaluation metrics, and the experimental conditions under which the results were obtained?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the number of slots is unknown or varies between images?
- Could the authors provide more qualitative results to support the claims made in the paper, particularly in terms of the quality of the generated images and the effectiveness of the object-centric representations?
