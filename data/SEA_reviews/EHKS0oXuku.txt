 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of Jensen-Shannon (JS) divergence as an alternative to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence for variational inference (VI) in Bayesian neural networks (BNNs). It introduces two novel loss functions based on JS divergence, specifically JS-G and JS-A, which are bounded and symmetric, addressing the unbounded nature of KL divergence. The authors claim that these loss functions can improve optimization stability and regularization, as demonstrated through experiments on the Cifar-10 dataset with added noise and a histopathology dataset. However, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental validation, lack of comparison with other divergences, and insufficient theoretical justification for the choice of JS divergence over other metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using JS divergence to address the limitations of KL divergence in BNNs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed JS-A divergence is shown to be bounded, which is a key property for ensuring stability in optimization.
- The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including proofs of theorems, which supports the claims made about the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms the KL divergence-based loss function, showing a practical advantage.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other divergences, such as the reverse KL divergence, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the advantages of JS divergence.
- The experiments are limited to a small number of datasets, which may not sufficiently validate the claims made about the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of using JS divergence, which could be a significant drawback in practical applications.
- The theoretical justification for the choice of JS divergence over other metrics is insufficient, and the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the advantages of JS divergence in the context of BNNs.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the hyperparameter optimization and its impact on the results.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as the intractability of the JS divergence-based loss function through variational inference.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison of the computational complexity of using JS divergence versus KL divergence in practical applications?
- How does the choice of JS divergence over other metrics like KL divergence enhance the performance of BNNs, particularly in terms of regularization?
- Can the authors clarify the choice of datasets for the experiments and consider including a broader range of datasets to validate the claims more thoroughly?
- What are the specific advantages of using JS divergence over other metrics, such as KL divergence, in the context of BNNs?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method handle the intractability of the JS divergence-based loss function through variational inference, and what are the implications of this for the practical implementation of the method?
