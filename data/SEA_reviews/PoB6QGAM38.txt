 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TRACER, a novel method for explaining the decision-making processes of deep neural networks (DNNs) by employing causal inference theory. TRACER employs Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) to identify causal groups within neural network layers, followed by counterfactual generation using a GAN-based model. This method allows for the identification of causal links between layers and the generation of counterfactuals to explain misclassifications. The approach is tested on various datasets including MNIST and CIFAR10, demonstrating its effectiveness in understanding model decisions and improving model robustness. However, concerns were raised regarding the scalability of the method to larger and more complex datasets, the clarity of the methodology, and the novelty of the approach compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces TRACER, a novel method that utilizes causal inference theory to explain the decision-making processes of deep neural networks (DNNs), which is a significant advancement in the field of explainable AI (XAI).
- The methodology is clearly presented, making it accessible to readers and facilitating understanding of the proposed approach.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of TRACER across various datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR10, and ImageNet, which demonstrates the versatility and applicability of the method.
- The use of counterfactual generation and causal discovery is innovative and addresses a critical need in the field of XAI, particularly in understanding the causal dynamics of DNNs.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with existing methods such as LIME, SHAP, Grad-CAM, and LRP, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential biases in the causal discovery process, which is crucial for understanding the reliability and generalizability of the findings.
- The methodology, particularly the use of CKA for causal discovery and the GAN-based counterfactual generation, is not sufficiently novel or well-explained, raising questions about the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to small-scale datasets, which may not adequately represent the performance of TRACER on larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and efficiency of the method, which is essential for understanding its scalability and practical applicability.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and coherence, especially in the methodology section.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the baseline value in the intervention process?
- How does the method handle the scalability and efficiency issues when applied to larger and more complex datasets?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and efficiency of the method?
- How does TRACER compare to other existing methods in terms of accuracy and efficiency, especially when applied to larger datasets?
- Could the authors elaborate on the methodology and the rationale behind the use of CKA for causal discovery and GAN-based counterfactual generation?
- How does TRACER address the issue of model bias, particularly in scenarios where the model may be trained on biased datasets?
