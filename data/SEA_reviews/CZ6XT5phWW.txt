 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called PROMPTp, aimed at enhancing the zero-shot performance of Large Language Models (LLMs) by rewriting task prompts. This approach is designed to make prompts more specific, unambiguous, and complete, thereby improving the model's ability to solve tasks correctly. The methodology involves using a rewriting LLM to generate high-quality prompts, which are then used to guide the model's performance on various tasks. The paper demonstrates that these rewritten prompts can lead to significant improvements in performance across multiple datasets, showcasing the potential of this method in practical applications. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty, as it primarily involves prompt rewriting without significant technical innovation. Additionally, concerns about the generalizability of the method to different LLMs and its dependence on the quality of the rewriting LLM are highlighted.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined research question.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and can be applied to various LLMs, including GPT-4, demonstrating significant improvements in performance.
- The method's effectiveness is supported by extensive experiments on eight datasets, covering tasks such as arithmetic, logical reasoning, and code generation.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the methodology, including the process of rewriting prompts, which is supported by a comprehensive ablation study and human evaluation.
- The method's ability to rewrite prompts for different tasks and its generalizability to unseen tasks are highlighted, demonstrating its potential applicability in various scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited as it primarily involves prompt rewriting without significant technical innovation.
- The method's effectiveness is highly dependent on the quality of the rewriting LLM, which might limit its applicability to different LLMs.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods such as Chain-of-Thought (CoT) and the use of additional in-context examples, which could affect the perceived improvement in performance.
- The method's dependence on the quality of the rewriting LLM raises concerns about its generalizability and applicability to different LLMs.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of hallucinations in the rewritten prompts, which could potentially mislead the model's performance.
- The method's dependence on the rewriting LLM's ability to understand the original prompt and rewrite it correctly is not adequately addressed, which could limit its effectiveness in scenarios where the LLM struggles to understand the original prompt.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the quality of the rewriting LLM and how it affects the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods that use additional in-context examples or prompt templates, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the process of rewriting prompts and how it affects the model's performance, especially in scenarios where the LLM struggles to understand the original prompt?
- How does the method handle the issue of hallucinations in the rewritten prompts, and what measures are in place to prevent or mitigate their impact on the model's performance?
- Could the authors provide more details on the method's generalizability to different LLMs and how it performs on weaker LLMs?
- How does the method's performance compare with other methods that use different LLMs or different training strategies, and what are the implications of these differences for the method's effectiveness and applicability?
