 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GDL-DS, a benchmark designed to evaluate the performance of Geometric Deep Learning (GDL) models in scenarios with distribution shifts. It covers a diverse range of scientific domains and distribution shifts, including conditional, covariate, and concept shifts, and evaluates 3 GDL backbones and 11 learning algorithms across 30 different experiment settings. The benchmark is notable for its thorough analysis of evaluation results, which aims to guide researchers and practitioners in selecting appropriate methods for handling distribution shifts in GDL applications. However, concerns about the novelty of the proposed benchmark and the limited discussion on the benchmark's contributions compared to existing works were raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear and detailed description of the benchmark, which covers a wide range of scientific domains and distribution shifts.
- The analysis of the evaluation results is comprehensive and insightful, providing valuable insights for researchers and practitioners in the field of Geometric Deep Learning (GDL).
- The paper includes a thorough comparison with existing benchmarks, highlighting the novelty of the proposed benchmark in terms of application domain, distribution shift, and available OOD info.
- The benchmark is designed to evaluate the performance of GDL models in scenarios with distribution shifts, which is an important and relevant topic in the field of GDL.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the novelty of the proposed benchmark compared to existing works, which might lead to questions about its originality.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the benchmark, which could help readers better understand its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the benchmark's contributions, which could enhance the understanding of its significance and impact.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the impact of data augmentation and the choice of baselines, as well as a more comprehensive evaluation of the benchmark's performance across different scenarios.
- The paper's writing and presentation could be improved for better clarity and readability, especially in sections where the content is dense or complex.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the novelty of the proposed benchmark compared to existing works?
- How does the benchmark handle different types of distribution shifts, and what are the implications of this for the models evaluated?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of baselines and the impact of data augmentation on the results?
- In light of the paper's claims about the benchmark's contributions, could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on these contributions and their impact on the field?
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the benchmark and how these might affect its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios. Can the authors address these limitations?
