 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ASPeeD, a novel diffusion model for sequence prediction tasks, which leverages a deterministic diffusion process to predict future states based on historical data. ASPeeD utilizes a deterministic diffusion model to deblend a sequence of states, with the source distribution being a crucial component that can significantly impact the performance of the model. The paper explores the use of various source distributions, including a perturbed history and a DDIM-based source, to enhance the model's ability to predict future states. The methodology is demonstrated through experiments on video prediction and robot control tasks, showing promising results in terms of prediction quality and inference speed. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and lack of comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to sequence prediction using a deterministic diffusion model, which is a significant departure from traditional autoregressive models.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, and the experiments conducted on video prediction and robot control tasks demonstrate its practical utility and potential for real-world applications.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results are compelling, showing the method's ability to achieve competitive performance with fewer inference steps compared to other models.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the source distribution and its impact on the performance of the model, which is a novel contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of inference speed and computational efficiency.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it primarily adapts the IADB framework to sequence prediction, which has been previously explored in similar works.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive experiments on larger and more diverse datasets to better demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- There is a need for more detailed ablation studies to better understand the impact of different components of the model, such as the source distribution and the number of DDPM steps.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as the increased training time and the potential computational overhead of using the DDIM-based source.
- The paper could benefit from more visual results and a clearer discussion on the choice of source distributions and their impact on the model's performance.
