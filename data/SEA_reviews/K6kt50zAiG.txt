 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CAMBranch, a novel method for imitation learning in Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) by leveraging a data augmentation technique called Variable Shifting. This technique generates Augmented MILPs (AMILPs) from original MILPs, allowing the generation of expert samples for imitation learning. The method is evaluated on four combinatorial optimization problems and demonstrates superior performance compared to existing methods. CAMBranch utilizes contrastive learning to enhance the model's ability to capture MILP features, potentially improving branching decisions. However, the paper's novelty and the depth of its experimental validation are questioned, with concerns about the generalizability of the results to larger MILP instances and the practical implications of the data augmentation technique.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear problem statement and methodology.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and well-motivated, with a clear connection to the original method and a novel application of contrastive learning in the context of MILP.
- The method demonstrates significant improvements in performance over existing methods, particularly in terms of solving time and the number of branch-and-bound nodes.
- The paper provides a theoretical justification for the method's effectiveness, including the proof of identical variable selection decisions between MILP and AMILP.
- The experiments are well-designed, with a focus on demonstrating the method's effectiveness in data-scarce scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited, as the data augmentation technique is considered straightforward and similar to existing methods.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods in MILP solving, particularly in terms of performance on larger instances.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include its applicability to other types of MILP instances or its generalizability to different types of branching rules.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with insufficient detail on the data collection process and potential issues with the statistical significance of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational cost of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the rationale behind using contrastive learning specifically in this context, and how it enhances the model's ability to capture MILP features?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger MILP instances, and what are the computational costs associated with its implementation?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the data collection process, including the time taken and the number of samples collected?
- Is there a possibility of applying the proposed method to other types of branching rules, such as those based on greedy heuristics?
- How does the method handle the issue of data scarcity in real-world applications, and what are the potential implications for its practical utility?
