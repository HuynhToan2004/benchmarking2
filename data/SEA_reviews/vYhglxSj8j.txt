 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CodeChain, a novel framework that leverages chain-of-thought prompting and self-revisions to generate modularized code from LLMs. This approach involves iteratively extracting and clustering sub-modules from generated code, selecting representative sub-modules, and integrating them into a new prompt for LLMs to generate improved solutions. The framework is evaluated on the APPS and CodeContests benchmarks, demonstrating significant improvements in modularity and correctness of generated solutions. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty, as it builds upon existing techniques like chain-of-thought prompting and self-revision, and lacks detailed analysis of the reusability of generated code.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research.
- The proposed framework is innovative, combining chain-of-thought prompting with self-revision, which is a novel approach to modularized code generation.
- The experiments are comprehensive and detailed, providing a thorough evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper is original in its approach to using clustering for sub-module selection, which is a novel contribution to the field.
- The framework shows significant improvements in both modularity and correctness of generated solutions, as demonstrated by experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it builds upon existing techniques like chain-of-thought prompting and self-revision, which have been previously explored.
- The paper lacks detailed analysis on the reusability of generated code, which is a critical aspect of the proposed framework.
- The framework is only tested on a limited set of benchmarks, which may not fully demonstrate its effectiveness across different domains or problem types.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the framework, such as the specifics of the clustering algorithm and the selection of representative sub-modules.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the framework handles the selection of sub-modules for revision and the impact of different sub-module selection strategies on the final performance.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with the latest state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a better understanding of its relative performance.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed framework handle the selection of sub-modules for revision? Is there a specific strategy for selecting sub-modules, or is it random?
- Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the clustering algorithm and the selection of representative sub-modules?
- How does the framework perform when applied to different types of problems or domains beyond those tested in the experiments?
- What are the implications of the framework's performance when using different LLMs or when varying the number of revisions?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the reusability of generated code and how it impacts the overall performance of the framework?
- How does the framework compare with the latest state-of-the-art methods, and what are the specific advantages or disadvantages of the proposed method compared to these approaches?
