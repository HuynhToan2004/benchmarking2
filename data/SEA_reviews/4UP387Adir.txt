 **Summary:**
The paper introduces WSNet, a novel method for weakly supervised graph contrastive learning, which integrates signals from weak labels and graph communities to enhance node embeddings. It explores the robustness of graph contrastive learning (GCL) methods under noisy conditions and compares WSNet with existing GCL and noisy-label learning methods. The paper evaluates WSNet on five benchmark graph datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness, particularly in high noise settings. However, the method's reliance on the quality of community detection algorithms and its limited evaluation on larger, more complex datasets are noted as significant drawbacks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the problem of weakly supervised node classification, which is an interesting and challenging research direction.
- It proposes a novel method, WSNet, which integrates signals from weak labels and graph communities to learn node representations, which is a novel approach.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the methodology and experimental setup.
- The experimental results show that WSNet outperforms existing GCL and noisy-label learning methods, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper provides a thorough review of related works and positions its research within the existing literature, highlighting its contributions and distinctions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the performance of WSNet on larger and more complex datasets, which limits the understanding of its scalability and applicability in diverse scenarios.
- The method's reliance on the quality of community detection algorithms is not adequately addressed, which could impact the reliability and robustness of the results.
- The paper does not include a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in the field, which could have provided a more robust validation of WSNet's performance.
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the presentation of the paper, such as missing references and unclear descriptions of experimental setups.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of its method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform on larger and more complex datasets?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the performance of WSNet on different types of graphs, such as homophilous and heterophilous graphs?
- How sensitive is the method to the quality of community detection algorithms, and what steps are taken to mitigate potential issues?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in the field, including a discussion of the methodological and experimental differences?
- Could the authors clarify the inconsistencies and potential errors in the presentation of the paper, such as missing references and unclear descriptions of experimental setups?
- How does the method handle the issue of noisy labels, and what measures are taken to ensure the accuracy and reliability of the results?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their method and how these might affect its practical applicability and impact?
