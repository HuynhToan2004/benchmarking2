 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new evaluation framework for financial time series forecasting, focusing on the construction of a large-scale dataset and the use of transformer-based models for forecasting. It critiques the use of traditional metrics like RMSE and MAPE, proposing a new set of metrics based on portfolio evaluation. The authors argue that these metrics provide a more realistic assessment of model performance, especially in the context of financial markets. The paper also discusses the challenges of using small datasets and proposes a new baseline for financial forecasting. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental scope and lack of comparative analysis with other models and datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a large-scale dataset for financial time series forecasting, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors propose a new evaluation framework that is more realistic and relevant for financial markets, focusing on portfolio evaluation metrics.
- The paper critiques the use of traditional metrics like RMSE and MAPE, arguing that they may not adequately capture the performance of models in financial forecasting.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the limitations of current models and propose new metrics that could potentially improve the evaluation of financial forecasting models.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear and concise sections that effectively communicate the authors' ideas.
- The inclusion of a large-scale dataset and the use of transformer-based models demonstrate a commitment to advancing the state-of-the-art in financial forecasting.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparative analysis with other models and datasets, which limits the ability to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed metrics against existing approaches.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the challenges of using small datasets in financial forecasting, which is a significant limitation given the scarcity of data in this domain.
- The experimental setup is limited, with only a few models tested, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed framework, particularly the challenges of using transformer-based models in financial forecasting.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the design of the new metrics, including their rationale, implementation, and evaluation.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the ethical implications of using financial data, which could be a significant concern for some readers.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed evaluation framework perform on other datasets, particularly those outside the financial domain?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed metrics against existing approaches, including a discussion of the advantages and disadvantages of each approach?
- How does the paper address the challenges of using small datasets in financial forecasting, and what strategies could be employed to mitigate these challenges?
- Could the authors elaborate on the design of the new metrics, including their rationale, implementation, and evaluation?
- How does the paper address the ethical implications of using financial data, particularly in the context of data privacy and security?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of their findings on the broader field of time series forecasting, and how their work could be adapted to other domains?
