 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for solving partial differential equations (PDEs) using a neural mesh adapter, named Data-free Mesh Mover (DMM), which generates adaptive moving meshes without requiring labeled data. This approach, which includes a moving mesh-based neural PDE solver (MM-PDE), is designed to address the challenges of mesh adaptation in traditional methods by dynamically adjusting mesh nodes rather than adding or deleting them. The theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate that the DMM minimizes interpolation error, and the method is shown to be effective in various PDE systems. However, concerns were raised regarding the complexity of the method, the clarity of the paper, and the practicality of the moving mesh approach in more complex scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to adaptive mesh generation, focusing on moving mesh nodes rather than adding or deleting them, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The proposed method, including the Data-free Mesh Mover (DMM) and the moving mesh-based neural PDE solver (MM-PDE), is supported by a robust theoretical analysis, particularly concerning the choice of the monitor function and its impact on interpolation error.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and the theoretical underpinnings, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- Extensive experiments and ablation studies are conducted to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method, providing a strong foundation for its practical application.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of PDEs by proposing a method that can adapt to the needs of the solution space, potentially improving the accuracy and efficiency of numerical simulations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is complex and requires significant computational resources, which might limit its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The method's reliance on the DMM's ability to generate optimal monitor functions raises concerns about its robustness and generalizability, especially in scenarios where the solution space is not well-defined or complex.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, which might make it difficult for readers to follow the methodology and results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its broader implications.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing approaches, such as LAMP, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness and efficiency.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the DMM is trained and the specifics of the training process, including the computational resources required and the time taken for training?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the solution space is not well-defined or complex, and what are the potential failure modes or limitations of the method in such cases?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the monitor function and its impact on the interpolation error, particularly in terms of its generalizability and robustness?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements, especially in scenarios where the mesh adaptation is more complex or dynamic?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results and comparisons, particularly in terms of the accuracy and efficiency of the proposed method compared to other state-of-the-art methods, especially in more complex scenarios such as 3D simulations?
