 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Unified Uncertainty Calibration (U2C) to address the challenges of combining aleatoric and epistemic uncertainties in classifiers. It proposes a framework that integrates these uncertainties to enhance the reliability of predictions, particularly in out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios. The U2C method is evaluated against the traditional "reject-or-classify" (RC) approach and other state-of-the-art methods on various benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing classification errors and improving calibration. However, the paper's novelty and the depth of theoretical analysis are questioned, and the empirical results, while showing some improvements, are not consistently superior across all benchmarks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-structured presentation.
- The proposed method, Unified Uncertainty Calibration (U2C), is novel and addresses a significant gap in the literature by integrating aleatoric and epistemic uncertainties in a unified manner.
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis and empirical results that support the proposed method, showing improvements over the traditional "reject-or-classify" (RC) approach in most cases.
- The experiments are extensive, covering various benchmarks and different types of OOD detection, which demonstrates the robustness and applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the method, including its theoretical underpinnings and practical implications, which are well-supported by the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the method might not perform optimally or where it could lead to incorrect predictions.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be a straightforward combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The theoretical analysis provided is limited, and the paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical models.
- The empirical results are not consistently superior across all benchmarks, and the improvements over the RC approach are marginal in some cases.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of unknown-unknowns, which is a critical aspect of uncertainty estimation.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer distinction from related works, particularly those that address similar issues in uncertainty estimation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between the U2C method and the RC method, particularly in terms of how they handle uncertainty and the theoretical underpinnings of their approaches?
- How does the U2C method perform in scenarios where the epistemic uncertainty threshold is set incorrectly? What are the implications of this on the overall performance of the method?
- Could the authors provide more detailed insights into the practical implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical models, particularly in terms of their applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the U2C method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the U2C method and provide suggestions for future research directions to address these limitations?
