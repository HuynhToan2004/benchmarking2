 **Summary:**
The paper introduces momentum variants for FedAvg and SCAFFOLD to address challenges in federated learning (FL) such as unreliable network connections, slow communication, and substantial data heterogeneity across clients. It demonstrates that incorporating momentum allows FedAvg to converge without relying on the assumption of bounded data heterogeneity even with constant local learning rates. For SCAFFOLD, it achieves faster convergence without additional assumptions. The paper also presents new variance-reduced extensions of FedAvg and SCAFFOLD, which exhibit state-of-the-art convergence rates. Theoretical analysis and experimental results support the effectiveness of these methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their theoretical contributions.
- The paper introduces a novel approach by incorporating momentum into FedAvg and SCAFFOLD, which enhances convergence rates and provides new insights into the handling of data heterogeneity in federated learning.
- The theoretical results are robust, showing that the proposed methods can converge under unbounded data heterogeneity, which is a significant advancement over previous works.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of related literature, highlighting the importance of the problem and the significance of the proposed solutions.
- The experimental results support the theoretical findings, demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of communication and computational costs.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed methods, which could affect their practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art federated learning methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The experimental results are limited to specific datasets and network settings, which may not fully represent the performance of the proposed methods in more diverse or complex scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of communication and computational costs?
- How do the proposed methods perform when compared to other state-of-the-art federated learning methods, and what are the specific advantages or disadvantages of each approach?
- Could the authors provide more experimental results or simulations to demonstrate the performance of the proposed methods in more diverse or complex scenarios?
- How do the authors address the potential negative societal impacts of their proposed methods, and what steps have been taken to mitigate these risks?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies or inconsistencies noted in the experimental results and the theoretical assumptions, particularly regarding the impact of data heterogeneity on convergence rates?
