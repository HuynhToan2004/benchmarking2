 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel meta-learning approach for latent force models (LFMs), focusing on nonlinear differential equations (ODEs and PDEs). This method, termed DKLFM, leverages deep kernels and neural network representations to learn latent forces from observed functions, enabling the model to generalize across tasks. The approach is tested on various datasets, including synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling complex nonlinear dynamics. However, concerns about the clarity of the paper's presentation and the limited scope of the experiments were raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to meta-learning latent force models, which is a significant advancement in the field of dynamical systems modeling.
- The methodology is well-articulated, and the paper is generally easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their applications.
- The experiments are well-designed and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in handling complex nonlinear dynamics, especially in real-world applications.
- The paper addresses the scalability of latent force models, which is a crucial issue in dynamical systems modeling, and proposes a method that avoids the need for variational approximations or ODE solvers.
- The integration of deep kernels and neural network representations for learning latent forces is a novel approach that enhances the model's capacity to handle complex dynamics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from poor writing and lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the presentation of figures and the explanation of certain methodological aspects.
- The experimental scope is limited, focusing primarily on small-scale datasets, which raises questions about the scalability and applicability of the proposed method to larger, more complex datasets.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical implications and potential areas for improvement.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing approaches, such as deep GPs or other state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a better understanding of its advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and significance of the task representation (r_n) in the model, especially in relation to the input mesh (x_n)?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing approaches, such as deep GPs, in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other methods, including quantitative results and a discussion on the computational complexity of their method?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the latent forces are not directly observable, and what are the implications of this in practical applications?
- Could the authors elaborate on the training process for the task encoder and the neural network representations, particularly how they are initialized and trained?
- What are the specific advantages of using the Fourier neural operator over other encoders, and how does the proposed method perform in terms of scalability when applied to larger, more complex datasets?
