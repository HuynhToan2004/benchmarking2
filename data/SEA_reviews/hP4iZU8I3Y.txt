 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel task called Logical Session Complex Query Answering (LS-CQA), which involves understanding complex user intentions across multiple sessions. It proposes a new method, the Logical Session Graph Transformer (LSGT), to address this challenge by transforming items, sessions, and logical structures into tokens for encoding. The LSGT model is evaluated against several state-of-the-art methods, showing significant improvements in performance. The paper also provides theoretical analysis and empirical results to support the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a novel and interesting problem of session understanding, which is crucial for enhancing product recommendations, navigation suggestions, and query reformulations.
- The proposed method, the Logical Session Graph Transformer (LSGT), is a well-motivated and innovative approach to capturing interactions among items across different sessions and their logical connections using a transformer structure.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- Theoretical analysis of the proposed method is provided, including the expressiveness of the LSGT and the permutation invariance of inputs for logical operators.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities and potential shortcomings.
- The construction of the hypergraph and the choice of hyperedges are not adequately explained, which could confuse the reader about the actual representation of the data.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the societal impact of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for many applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, especially in the area of session-based recommendation, which is closely related to the topic.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as it seems to be an extension of existing methods without significant new insights or contributions.
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the construction of the hypergraph and the choice of hyperedges, which could affect the understanding and reproducibility of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the hypergraph is constructed and the choice of hyperedges?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting, especially considering the complexity of the model?
- Can you clarify the meaning and usage of "relation" in the context of your model?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Is there a potential for the method to be extended to handle more complex logical operations beyond those currently supported?
- How does the method handle the potential for data sparsity in real-world applications, and what strategies could be employed to mitigate this issue?
