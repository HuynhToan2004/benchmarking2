 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Kosmos-G, a novel multimodal large language model (MLLM) designed for subject-driven image generation. It leverages the advanced multimodal perception capabilities of MLLMs to align the output space of MLLM with CLIP, enabling zero-shot subject-driven generation with interleaved multi-image and text input. The model is trained using a "align before instruct" strategy, which involves a three-stage training process: multimodal language modeling, image decoder aligning, and instruction tuning. The authors claim that Kosmos-G outperforms existing methods in zero-shot subject-driven image generation and can be seamlessly integrated with various U-Net techniques. However, concerns are raised about the lack of comparisons with some relevant works and the limited novelty of the methodology.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to subject-driven image generation using multimodal large language models (MLLMs), which is a promising direction for the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, with a clear presentation of the three-stage training process and the use of a multimodal language model (MLM) to learn the distribution of images.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through experiments and ablation studies, showing that the model can generate high-quality images and outperform other models in certain tasks.
- The use of a multimodal language model (MLM) to learn the distribution of images is a novel approach that has not been extensively explored in previous works.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes detailed technical descriptions and visual results that demonstrate the model's capabilities.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with relevant works, particularly with models like BLIP-Diffusion and other subject-driven image generation models.
- There is a noticeable absence of quantitative results for the generated images, which is crucial for evaluating the model's performance.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training data and the data processing steps, which could affect the reproducibility and understanding of the results.
- The novelty of the methodology is limited, as it mainly involves the integration of existing techniques such as AlignerNet and score distillation, which have been previously used in other works.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the model's performance and applicability in various scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the training data and the data processing steps, particularly how entities are extracted from captions and how segmentation masks are obtained?
- How does the model handle the order of images in the input sequence, and what is the impact of this order on the generated images?
- What are the specific contributions of the proposed method compared to existing works, particularly in terms of novelty and effectiveness?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies in the evaluation metrics, such as the FID score, and provide more detailed results to support the claims made in the paper?
- How does the model perform when the number of entities in the input sequence varies, and what are the implications of this for the model's performance and applicability?
