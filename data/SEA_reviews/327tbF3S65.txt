 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a domain-agnostic latent diffusion model for implicit neural representations (INRs) that generates adaptive positional embeddings, enhancing the expressive power of INRs by shifting the focus from neural network weights to positional embeddings. The model, named DDMI, is composed of two stages: a Variational Auto-Encoder (VAE) and a diffusion model, which learns the shared latent space. The VAE generates basis fields, and the diffusion model operates on these latents, allowing for the generation of high-quality INRs across various signal domains such as images, 3D shapes, Neural Radiance Fields, and videos. The paper claims that this approach outperforms existing INR generative models, although the novelty and the depth of the contributions are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to INR generation by focusing on positional embeddings, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that rely on generating weights for INR functions.
- The proposed method demonstrates versatility and effectiveness across various signal domains, including 2D images, 3D shapes, Neural Radiance Fields, and videos, showing superior performance compared to existing INR generative models.
- The use of discrete-to-continuous space Variational AutoEncoder (D2C-VAE) to generate adaptive positional embeddings is innovative and addresses the limitations of fixed positional embeddings.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method, with results demonstrating the versatility and superior performance of the DDMI model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques such as D2C-VAE and the diffusion model, which have been previously used in other contexts.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially in comparison to existing methods.
- The motivation behind the use of positional embeddings instead of neural network weights is not convincingly argued, and the paper does not provide a clear explanation of how positional embeddings enhance the expressive power of INRs.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the training and inference procedures, particularly how the VAE is trained and how the diffusion model is integrated into the framework.
- The comparison with existing methods, such as the Functa model, is not sufficiently robust, and the paper does not adequately address the potential advantages of the Functa model in terms of flexibility and domain-specific applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method compared to existing methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to the Functa model in terms of flexibility and domain-specific applications?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of positional embeddings over neural network weights, and how does this choice enhance the expressive power of INRs?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the training and inference procedures, particularly how the VAE is trained and how the diffusion model is integrated into the framework?
- How does the proposed method handle the limitations and potential negative societal impacts, and what steps are being taken to mitigate these issues?
