 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) called Meta-Value Learning (MeVa), which aims to address the challenges of learning with learning awareness in multi-agent systems. The method is inspired by LOLA, but extends it by focusing on the gradient of the meta-value function, which is naturally consistent and accounts for longer-term and higher-order interactions. The approach avoids the need for explicit policy gradient computations and instead uses a state-value function V to approximate the Q-function, allowing for the avoidance of continuous meta-action spaces. The paper includes experiments on a toy game and repeated matrix games, comparing MeVa to prior work and demonstrating its effectiveness. However, it also highlights the method's limitations, such as scalability issues and the assumption of visibility of opponent parameters.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, Meta-Value Learning (MeVa), which is a significant advancement in the field of multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL).
- The method is clearly explained and well-motivated, with a clear connection to existing works such as LOLA and COLA.
- The experimental results are promising, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method in a toy game and repeated matrix games.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with a good balance of theoretical insights and practical demonstrations.
- The method avoids the need for explicit policy gradient computations, which is a significant improvement over traditional methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of scalability and generalization to more complex scenarios.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support the claims made about the method's performance, such as the claim that it achieves cooperation only where self-interest warrants it.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability issues associated with the method, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for training and the complexity of the policy representations.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the discussion of the experimental results and the method's theoretical underpinnings.
- The paper does not provide a clear justification for the use of the meta-value function, and the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of scalability and generalization to more complex scenarios?
- How does the method perform in more complex scenarios, such as those involving multiple agents or more complex policy representations?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion of the experimental results, particularly in terms of the method's effectiveness in achieving cooperation and its performance in different scenarios?
- How does the method address the scalability issues associated with the use of large neural network models for policy representations?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of the method, particularly in terms of its consistency and the assumptions it makes about the learning processes of different agents?
