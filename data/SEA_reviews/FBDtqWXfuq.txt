 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel setting called Modality-Collaborated Federated Learning (MCFL), where clients with different data modalities collaborate to improve the performance of their respective modalities. It proposes a framework called FedCola, which leverages a modality-agnostic transformer to facilitate parameter sharing across modalities. The framework is evaluated against existing solutions and shows promising results in enhancing performance across modalities. The authors address the challenges of model heterogeneity and modality gaps through innovative strategies such as modality compensation and warm-up techniques. The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel setting, Modality-Collaborated Federated Learning (MCFL), which is a significant contribution to the field of federated learning.
- The proposed framework, FedCola, is well-structured and provides a clear methodology for modality-agnostic transformers to address model heterogeneity and modality gaps.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, with a clear motivation and good presentation of the problem.
- The experiments are comprehensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, showing improvements over existing solutions.
- The paper addresses the challenges of model heterogeneity and modality gaps in MCFL, which are critical issues in federated learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the privacy implications of sharing model parameters across clients, which could be a significant concern in federated learning.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed methods, especially in scenarios with a large number of clients or modalities.
- The comparison with existing methods like CreamFL is not fair due to different model architectures used, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, which could provide valuable insights into the practical applicability and scope of the research.
- The evaluation is limited to a small number of clients and modalities, which may not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the proposed methods, which is a critical factor in federated learning.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed framework handle the privacy concerns related to sharing model parameters across clients?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed methods, especially in scenarios with a large number of clients or modalities?
- How do the authors justify the choice of model architectures in the comparisons, particularly in light of the potential impact on the results?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed methods and how these might affect the practical applicability of the research?
- How do the authors plan to address the computational cost of the proposed methods in future work?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the modality compensation and warm-up techniques on the performance of the model?
