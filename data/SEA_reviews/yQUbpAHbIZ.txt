 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for bivariate causal discovery using a post-nonlinear (PNL) model, which incorporates maximal correlation to enhance the accuracy and efficiency of causal relationship learning. The authors propose a new method called MC-PNL, which optimizes the nonlinear transformation using a benign objective function and employs block coordinate descent for efficient optimization. The method is evaluated on both synthetic and real datasets, showing improvements in time and accuracy compared to existing methods. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its lack of theoretical guarantees and the need for further empirical validation on larger datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a fundamental and challenging problem in causal discovery, focusing on bivariate causal discovery, which is crucial for understanding causal relationships between two variables.
- The authors have conducted a comprehensive review of existing methods, comparing and contrasting them with the proposed method, providing a thorough understanding of the current state of research in this area.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, with a clear presentation of the problem and its solution, which is supported by a well-designed algorithm.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of randomized dependence coefficient (RDC) instead of HSIC for independence testing is highlighted as a significant improvement, and the method's efficiency is demonstrated through comparisons with existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its identifiability and convergence properties, which are crucial for understanding its reliability and robustness.
- The empirical results presented in the paper are limited, and the method's performance on larger datasets or more complex scenarios is not adequately demonstrated.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the method's performance.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting in the context of the proposed method, which is a significant concern given the method's reliance on a soft-HGR approach.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to replicate the results and understand the method's performance in different scenarios.
