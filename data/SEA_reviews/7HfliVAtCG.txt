 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DE-ViT, a novel few-shot object detector that operates without the need for fine-tuning, using a frozen DINOv2 model for feature extraction. DE-ViT employs a binary classifier for each class, trained without fine-tuning, and a novel propagation-based localization mechanism that does not require finetuning. The method is evaluated on various datasets, including COCO and LVIS, and demonstrates state-of-the-art performance in both few-shot and one-shot settings. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as it builds on existing methods such as DINOv2 and Meta RCNN, and for not sufficiently addressing the scalability and efficiency of its binary classification and region propagation mechanisms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel propagation-based localization mechanism, which is a significant contribution to the field of few-shot object detection.
- DE-ViT achieves state-of-the-art performance in both few-shot and one-shot settings, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The methodology is straightforward and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods.
- The paper includes comprehensive experimental evaluations, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of frozen DINOv2 features in the DE-ViT method allows for the detection of novel classes without the need for fine-tuning, which is a significant advantage.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks sufficient experimental evidence to support the claim that the proposed method outperforms open-vocabulary detectors, particularly in terms of inference speed and computational efficiency.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it primarily builds on existing methods such as DINOv2 and Meta RCNN, with minimal new technical contributions.
- The scalability and efficiency of the binary classification and region propagation mechanisms are not adequately addressed, raising concerns about their practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including its potential negative societal impacts and ethical considerations.
- There are issues with clarity and organization in the paper, particularly in the presentation of figures and the explanation of certain methodological details.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the prototype is built using DINOv2, specifically how the DINOv2 features are used to build the prototype?
- Why does the performance of DE-ViT on COCO 2017 differ significantly from that on COCO 2014, as shown in Table 1?
- How does the performance of DE-ViT compare with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of inference speed and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the binary classification and region propagation mechanisms on the overall performance and efficiency of the DE-ViT method?
- How does the DE-ViT method handle the scalability issues, especially in scenarios with a large number of classes?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and inference processes, particularly how the binary classifier is trained and how the region propagation mechanism is implemented?
