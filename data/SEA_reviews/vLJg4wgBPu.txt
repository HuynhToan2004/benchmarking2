 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of iterative reasoning in large language models (LLMs) through a method called IRSA (Iterative Reasoning with Self-Attention). IRSA involves prompting LLMs to execute iterative processes, such as loops, by providing detailed prompts that guide the model through each step of the algorithm. The methodology involves the use of a predefined template for prompts, which includes state descriptions and transition rules, to execute algorithms like bubble sort and logical deduction. The paper demonstrates that this approach can improve the accuracy of LLMs in handling iterative tasks and argues that it could be useful in educational settings and for software engineering. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope, lack of rigorous experimental validation, and unclear presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The concept of using LLMs to execute iterative algorithms is novel and potentially impactful for educational and programming applications.
- The methodology is straightforward and could be easily adopted by others, making it accessible and practical.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods.
- The paper addresses a timely and important topic, focusing on the potential of LLMs to execute algorithms, which is a significant advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough experimental validation, with insufficient comparisons to other methods and a limited dataset.
- The presentation of the paper is unclear and could benefit from better organization and more detailed explanations.
- The methodology is somewhat limited in its applicability, as it requires specific prompts for each algorithm, which might not generalize well to other algorithms or domains.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalization capabilities of the IRSA method, which is a critical aspect for practical applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the IRSA method, which could lead to overclaiming or misunderstanding of its capabilities.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence or analysis to support the claims made about the limitations of LLMs in handling iterative tasks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the "swap flag" in the IRSA method, especially in terms of its generalizability to other algorithms?
- How does the IRSA method handle different types of algorithms, particularly those that do not follow a simple loop structure?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the effectiveness and limitations of the IRSA method in real-world applications?
- How does the IRSA method compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and practicality, especially in educational or programming contexts?
- Can the authors provide a more rigorous experimental validation, including comparisons to other methods and a broader dataset?
- How does the IRSA method address the issue of overfitting to specific prompts, which could limit its generalizability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed evidence or analysis to support the claims made about the limitations of LLMs in handling iterative tasks?
