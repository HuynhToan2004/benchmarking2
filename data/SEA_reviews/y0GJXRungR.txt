 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the effectiveness of self-repair mechanisms in code generation, focusing on the use of large language models (LLMs) to generate and repair code. It examines various aspects of self-repair, including the cost-effectiveness, the role of feedback models, and the impact of human intervention. The study involves a series of experiments using datasets like HumanEval and APPS, comparing different LLMs such as GPT-3.5 and GPT-4. The results suggest that self-repair, while not always superior to i.i.d. sampling, can be beneficial when the repair cost is taken into account. The paper also explores the role of feedback models and human intervention in enhancing the repair process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed appendices that provide additional insights and methodological details.
- The topic of self-repair in code generation is both timely and relevant, as it addresses the need for more robust and efficient methods in software development.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of different LLMs and their capabilities in code generation and repair, using a diverse set of tasks and models.
- The analysis of the impact of feedback models and human intervention on the repair process is insightful, offering a deeper understanding of the role of feedback in the self-repair mechanism.
- The paper is structured around a clear research question and presents a systematic evaluation of different factors such as the number of samples and feedback models, which are crucial for understanding the effectiveness of self-repair.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with other methods and models, particularly in terms of computational cost and performance metrics like pass@k.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the models used, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's focus on Python programming puzzles might limit the applicability of the results to other programming languages or real-world software development scenarios.
- The experimental setup could be improved to include more diverse tasks and to better simulate real-world conditions, such as varying the difficulty of tasks and using more realistic test suites.
- The paper's conclusions might be overstated in some areas, particularly in the generalizability of the findings beyond the specific models and datasets used.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the ethical implications of using LLMs for code repair, including potential biases and limitations in the models' capabilities.

**Questions:**
- Can you elaborate on the computational cost of self-repair compared to i.i.d. sampling, especially in terms of wall-clock time and resource usage?
- How does the paper's definition of pass@k compare to other definitions in the literature, and what are the implications of using a different definition?
- What are the implications of using different models for feedback generation versus code generation, and how might this affect the overall performance of the self-repair mechanism?
- How do the findings of this study generalize to other programming languages or real-world software development scenarios?
- Can you provide more details on the human study, including the selection criteria for the participants and the methodology used to collect and analyze the feedback data?
- How does the paper address the potential biases or limitations in the LLMs used for code repair, and what steps were taken to mitigate these issues?
