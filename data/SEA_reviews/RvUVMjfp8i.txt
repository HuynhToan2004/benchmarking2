 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a Robustness Analysis Curve (RAC) for evaluating the robustness of semi-supervised learning (SSL) algorithms in open environments where labeled and unlabeled data distributions differ. The authors propose a benchmark that assesses SSL algorithms across various datasets and domains, including image, text, and tabular data. The RAC is used to evaluate the performance of SSL algorithms under three types of inconsistencies: inconsistent data distributions, inconsistent label spaces, and inconsistent feature spaces. The paper provides a theoretical framework and extensive experiments to analyze the robustness of SSL algorithms, aiming to fill a gap in the literature by providing a comprehensive evaluation metric for SSL in open environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new evaluation metric, the Robustness Analysis Curve (RAC), which provides a comprehensive assessment of the robustness of SSL algorithms across different types of inconsistencies.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- Theoretical analyses and experiments are conducted to support the proposed evaluation metric, providing a robust foundation for the claims made.
- The paper covers a broad range of SSL algorithms and datasets, ensuring the results are generalizable and applicable to various scenarios.
- The paper effectively distinguishes between expected robustness and worst-case robustness, offering valuable insights into the robustness of SSL algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the design of the RAC, particularly how it is constructed and how it relates to the specific challenges of SSL in open environments.
- The motivation behind the proposed evaluation metric is not clearly articulated, making it difficult to understand the rationale behind its design.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed evaluation metric, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential biases.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods for evaluating SSL algorithms, which could help in positioning the proposed method within the current landscape of SSL evaluation.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with other robust SSL algorithms, which could help in understanding the relative advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for any technological advancement.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and construction of the RAC, particularly how it is designed to address the specific challenges of SSL in open environments?
- How does the RAC compare to other existing evaluation metrics for SSL, and what are the key differences in terms of evaluation criteria and methodology?
- What are the potential limitations or biases of the RAC, and how might these affect the evaluation of SSL algorithms?
- How do the authors address the potential for data leakage in the evaluation process, particularly in scenarios where labeled and unlabeled data are sourced from different distributions?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons of the proposed method with other robust SSL algorithms, and discuss the relative advantages and disadvantages of these approaches?
- What are the implications of the RAC for the design of SSL algorithms, and how might it influence the development of future SSL methodologies?
