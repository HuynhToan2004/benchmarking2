 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to solving Wasserstein gradient flows using the Sinkhorn divergence, termed the Neural Sinkhorn Gradient Flow (NSGF). This method approximates the velocity field of the Wasserstein gradient flow using a neural network trained to minimize the difference between the neural network's velocity field and the true velocity field. Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach, showing that it can outperform existing methods in certain scenarios. The paper also discusses the practical implications of using Sinkhorn divergence over other metrics like the Wasserstein distance, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the handling of different supports in distributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by using Sinkhorn divergence for Wasserstein gradient flow, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- Theoretical analysis is robust, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The method is shown to be effective in generating high-quality samples, outperforming existing methods in certain scenarios.
- The use of Sinkhorn divergence instead of the Wasserstein distance is highlighted as a significant advantage, particularly in handling different supports in distributions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its strengths and weaknesses.
- The empirical results are not consistently superior across all metrics, raising questions about the general applicability of the method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the method, which is crucial for understanding its practical implications.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and comparisons with existing methods to better contextualize its contributions.
- The paper's empirical results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the method's performance in high-dimensional spaces is not adequately demonstrated.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the training of the neural network, which is crucial for understanding the practical implementation of the method.

**Questions:**

- How does the method perform in high-dimensional spaces, and what are the practical implications of this limitation?
- Can you clarify the computational complexity of the method and how it compares to other existing methods?
- How does the method handle different supports in distributions, and what are the implications of this in practical applications?
- Could you provide more details on the choice of Sinkhorn divergence over other metrics, and how this choice impacts the performance of the method?
- How does the method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and sample quality?
