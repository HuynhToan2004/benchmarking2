 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for pruning attention heads in transformer models, focusing on a technique called almost-sure sparsity. This method aims to ensure that a specified fraction of heads are always closed during training, improving the sparsity and efficiency of the model. The authors propose a framework, PASS, which optimizes the gate distribution to achieve this sparsity, and introduce a new term, R_CONC, to encourage concentration of closed heads across layers. The method is evaluated on various datasets, including IWSLT14 and GLUE benchmarks, showing improvements over existing pruning methods. The paper also introduces a clipping and reopening strategy to enhance the efficiency of gate training.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel notion of almost-sure sparsity, which is well-explained and effectively implemented.
- The proposed framework, PASS, is well-motivated and theoretically sound, with a clear mathematical formulation that is easy to follow.
- The experiments conducted on IWSLT and GLUE benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing pruning methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the theoretical analysis is comprehensive and convincing.
- The use of the Hard Concrete distribution for approximating the posterior distribution is a significant contribution, providing a more effective way of achieving sparsity.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the relationship between the proposed method and other existing methods, such as pruning based on importance scores.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definition and usage of certain terms and notations, such as z_c and the distinction between z and z_c.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential negative impact on model performance due to the clipping and reopening strategy.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to only two datasets and models, which might not fully demonstrate its generalizability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other pruning methods, including those that use different pruning strategies like sparsity in expectation or Lagrangian multipliers.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the model.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the relationship between the proposed method and other existing methods, particularly in terms of the approach to pruning based on importance scores?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and scalability with other pruning methods, especially as the size of the model increases?
- Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between sparsity and model performance, particularly in scenarios where the model's performance drops significantly due to pruning?
- How does the proposed method handle the sparsity of attention weights versus the sparsity of attention heads? Is there a possibility of achieving higher sparsity levels by focusing on the sparsity of attention weights?
- Could you provide more details on the training process, such as how the clipping and reopening strategy are implemented and how they affect the training dynamics?
- Is there a plan to extend the evaluation of the method to include more datasets and models to better demonstrate its generalizability and effectiveness?
