 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FoldFlow, a family of generative models for protein backbone generation, which utilizes continuous normalizing flows (CNFs) to learn SE(3)-invariant distributions. The models, including FoldFlow-Base, FoldFlow-OT, and FoldFlow-SFM, are designed to handle deterministic and stochastic dynamics, leveraging the flow matching framework. FoldFlow-Base uses a closed-form expression for the ground truth conditional vector field, while FoldFlow-OT incorporates Riemannian optimal transport for accelerated training. FoldFlow-SFM combines Riemannian OT with simulation-free training to learn stochastic continuous-time dynamics. The models are evaluated on protein backbone generation, demonstrating competitive performance against existing methods like FrameDiff and RFDiffusion. However, concerns about the experimental setup, including the use of auxiliary losses and the comparative performance metrics, have been raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to protein backbone generation using a flow matching framework, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The manuscript is well-written, with clear explanations of the mathematical concepts involved, and the experiments are well-designed and comprehensive.
- The introduction of FoldFlow models, including FoldFlow-Base, FoldFlow-OT, and FoldFlow-SFM, demonstrates innovative applications of generative models in protein design, particularly in equilibrium conformation generation.
- The manuscript provides a detailed background on Riemannian manifolds and Lie groups, which aids in understanding the mathematical foundations of the work.
- The paper is original in its application of flow matching on SE(3) and its ability to learn stochastic dynamics over SE(3) for protein backbone generation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the experimental setup, including the use of auxiliary losses and their impact on the results.
- The choice of metrics, such as the Frac. designability score, is questionable, and the paper does not adequately explain why these metrics were chosen.
- The comparison with existing methods, such as FrameDiff, is not fair due to the use of additional information in the loss function.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other methods, such as FrameDiff, and a more comprehensive discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed models.
- The manuscript could be improved by providing a clearer explanation of the mathematical concepts, particularly in the abstract and introduction sections, and by including a more detailed comparison with existing methods.

**Questions:**
- How does the performance of FoldFlow compare to FrameDiff when using only the loss function from Eq. (10) in the training of FrameDiff?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the auxiliary losses and their impact on the results?
- How does the computational efficiency of FoldFlow compare to other methods, particularly in terms of training and inference times?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of metrics, such as the Frac. designability score, and provide a more detailed discussion on their relevance and utility in evaluating the performance of the models?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of generating novel and diverse protein structures, and what are the implications of this for drug discovery and other applications in biology?
