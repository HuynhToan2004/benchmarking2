 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Entity Interaction Transformer (EIT) for multi-object manipulation tasks, leveraging a pre-trained object-centric representation (OCR) to handle complex interactions between objects. The EIT is designed to generalize well across varying numbers of objects and is tested against a state-of-the-art method, SMORL, in a simulated environment. The approach involves a transformer architecture that processes object-centric features and integrates these with goal-conditioned reinforcement learning. Despite the innovative approach, the paper is critiqued for its limited experimental scope, focusing on a single task and a simplistic environment. The generalization capabilities of the model are also questioned, particularly in more complex and realistic scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear and intuitive explanation of the proposed methods.
- The proposed EIT architecture is innovative, utilizing a transformer-based architecture that integrates goal-conditioning and permutation invariance, enhancing the model's ability to handle complex interactions between objects.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments and ablations, providing a robust evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The use of DLP for object-centric representation is reasonable and well-justified, contributing to the overall effectiveness of the model.
- The paper demonstrates the potential for generalization to a larger number of objects, showing that the model can handle tasks with up to 10 objects.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and generalization capabilities.
- The experimental setup is limited to a single task, which may not adequately demonstrate the model's versatility or applicability in more complex or varied scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity on the role and impact of the action entity in the model, particularly in terms of how it influences the model's performance and generalization capabilities.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the model to more complex or realistic environments, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper suffers from some typographical errors and lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the action entity in the model, particularly in terms of how it influences the model's generalization capabilities?
- How does the model perform in more complex or varied environments, such as those with more complex object interactions or different types of objects?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and generalization capabilities?
- How does the model handle tasks where the optimal policy does not involve a similar procedure for each object, as assumed in Theorem 1?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their proposed methods and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
