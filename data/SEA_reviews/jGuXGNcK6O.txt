 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the challenge of least-privilege learning, where clients share only representations of their data with a server, aiming to prevent unintended information leakage. It introduces a formalization of the least-privilege principle and demonstrates a fundamental trade-off between the utility of representations and the leakage of information beyond the intended task. The authors show that under realistic assumptions on the data distribution, there is an inherent trade-off between the utility of representations and the leakage of information. The paper includes empirical experiments to validate these theoretical findings, showing that any data representation that has good utility for a given prediction task also leaks more information about the original data than the task label itself.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a formalization of the least-privilege principle for machine learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The empirical experiments are well-conducted and demonstrate the trade-off between utility and leakage of information, supporting the theoretical findings.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise sections that are easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the problem and provide a solid theoretical framework, which is supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue in machine learning, specifically the potential leakage of sensitive information during model training and inference.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how to minimize the leakage of information beyond the intended task, which is a critical aspect of the research.
- The experimental setup is limited, focusing only on a single model architecture and dataset, which may not generalize well to other settings.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the experiments were conducted, which could limit the reproducibility of the results.
- The formalization of the least-privilege principle in terms of mutual information between the representation and the task label is not well-explained, making it difficult for readers to grasp the implications of the results.
- The paper's contribution is somewhat limited by the existing literature on the generalized conditional entropy bottleneck problem, which is not adequately distinguished from the novelty of the proposed work.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the findings, particularly how the results can be applied in real-world scenarios to mitigate the risks of data misuse.
