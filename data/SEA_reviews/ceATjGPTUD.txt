 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for Generative Error Correction (GER) in Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) by integrating noise embeddings into language models, leveraging the robustness of LLMs to noisy conditions. The authors propose a method that extracts noise embeddings from the N-best hypotheses list to represent different noise types, which are then used to enhance the noise-robustness of GER. This approach is evaluated on the RobustHyPoradise dataset, showing significant improvements over existing methods. The paper also introduces a knowledge distillation method using mutual information to enhance the noise representation ability of the extracted embeddings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the critical issue of noise-robustness in Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) by integrating noise embeddings into language models, leveraging the robustness of LLMs to noisy conditions.
- The proposed method of extracting noise embeddings from the N-best hypotheses list to represent different noise types is novel and innovative.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments conducted on the RobustHyPoradise dataset demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
- The introduction of a knowledge distillation method using mutual information to enhance the noise representation ability of the extracted embeddings is a valuable addition to the field.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including visualizations of noise embeddings and data efficiency, which helps in understanding the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other noise-robust ASR methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed approach.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to other types of noises and its effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could be improved by providing more detailed descriptions of the experimental setup, including the specifics of the LLMs used and the data used for training.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of computational complexity, which could limit its scalability in real-world applications.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical deployments.
