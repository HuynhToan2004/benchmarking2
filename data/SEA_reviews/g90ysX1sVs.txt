 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to reinforcement learning (RL) by focusing on the adaptive nature of activation functions, specifically rational activation functions. These functions are capable of dynamically adjusting their parameters, providing a level of plasticity that is crucial for RL agents in constantly changing environments. The authors propose a methodology that integrates rational activation functions with residual networks, demonstrating that this combination can lead to improved performance in various Atari games compared to traditional methods like DQN and Rainbow. The paper also discusses the potential for rational activation functions to reduce overestimation and improve generalization capabilities. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty and depth of the theoretical contributions, the empirical validation, and the clarity of the presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a clear problem statement.
- The idea of using rational activation functions is innovative and interesting, offering a novel approach to reinforcement learning.
- The paper provides extensive empirical evidence, including results on Atari games, which are convincing and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.
- The theoretical results on the rational activation function are significant and well-presented, contributing to the understanding of the activation function's role in deep learning.
- The paper discusses the importance of rational activation functions in reducing overestimation and improving generalization capabilities, which are crucial for RL agents in dynamic environments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of scalability and applicability to more complex environments.
- The theoretical contributions are limited, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing works on rational activation functions.
- The empirical evidence provided is not convincing enough, particularly in terms of the statistical significance of the results and the choice of baselines.
- The paper does not adequately address the societal impact of its findings, which is a significant omission in a field that is increasingly concerned with ethical considerations.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, which could enhance the reader's understanding and engagement with the content.

**Questions:**
- How do the rational activation functions perform in environments that are not dynamic, such as those with stationary distributions?
- What are the specific advantages of using rational activation functions over other learnable activation functions, such as those based on rational functions?
- Can the authors clarify the choice of baselines and the statistical significance of the results, especially in comparison to more recent and relevant RL algorithms?
- How do the authors address the issue of overestimation in their model, particularly in dynamic environments where the policy is improving?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the architecture and hyperparameters used, to facilitate reproducibility and further validation of the results?
- How do the authors envision the application of rational activation functions in more complex environments, such as those involving continuous states and actions?
