 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Ditto, a novel framework for secure inference of Transformer models using quantization-aware MPC techniques. It proposes MPC-friendly quantization methods and distillation processes to maintain model utility, alongside efficient MPC primitives for type conversions essential in quantization. The framework aims to reduce computation and communication overhead, improving overall efficiency. The paper includes empirical evaluations on BERT and GPT2 models, demonstrating significant improvements in efficiency compared to existing methods like MPCFormer and PUMA. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the limited scope of model evaluation, and the lack of comparison with more recent and efficient MPC methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and detailed methodology.
- The proposed MPC-friendly quantization and distillation are innovative and contribute to the field of secure inference of Transformer models.
- The paper includes extensive experiments that demonstrate the efficiency and effectiveness of the proposed methods, supported by a detailed analysis of the results.
- The introduction of MPC-friendly quantization-aware distillation is novel and effective, with potential for significant impact on the field.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods, including both model utility and efficiency, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed techniques.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as the use of quantization and distillation for secure inference has been previously explored.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with more recent and efficient MPC methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the performance and efficiency of the proposed methods.
- The scope of model evaluation is limited to BERT and GPT2 models, which might not fully demonstrate the general applicability of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation and deployment of the proposed framework, which could affect the reproducibility and scalability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of the research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other MPC methods, including the most recent and efficient ones, to better understand the performance and efficiency of the proposed methods?
- How does the proposed framework perform when applied to larger and more complex models, such as those used in large language models?
- Can the authors provide more details on the implementation and deployment of the proposed framework, including any specific requirements or challenges that need to be addressed?
- How does the proposed framework handle the trade-off between model utility and efficiency, especially in scenarios where the model utility might be compromised to achieve better efficiency?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their work and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
