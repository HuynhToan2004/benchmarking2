 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LLMatic, a novel Neural Architecture Search (NAS) algorithm that leverages Large Language Models (LLMs) to generate diverse and high-performing neural network architectures. By integrating the code-generating abilities of LLMs with the diversity and robustness of QD solutions, LLMatic demonstrates competitive performance on benchmarks like CIFAR-10 and NAS-bench-201, despite evaluating only 2,000 candidates. The methodology involves using LLMs to generate neural network architectures based on prompts, which are then evaluated using QD algorithms for diversity and robustness. Despite the promising results, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as it heavily relies on existing NAS techniques and LLM-based methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by combining the code-generating abilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) with the diversity and robustness of QD solutions to create a new NAS algorithm, LLMatic.
- LLMatic is shown to produce competitive networks while evaluating just 2,000 candidates, even without prior knowledge of the benchmark domain or exposure to top-performing models for the benchmark.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components.
- The use of LLMs to generate neural network architectures is innovative and holds significant potential for the field of NAS.
- The method is open-sourced, which facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive review of related work, particularly in the area of LLM-based NAS methods, which are crucial for contextualizing the current research.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it heavily relies on existing NAS techniques and LLM-based methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of the number of candidates evaluated and the complexity of the search space.
- The paper does not compare LLMatic with other state-of-the-art NAS methods, which limits the evaluation of its effectiveness and efficiency.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the diversity of generated architectures and a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper's writing needs improvement, particularly in the clarity and coherence of the text, and the presentation of figures and tables.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific role and impact of the prompt archive in the performance of LLMatic?
- How does LLMatic handle the scalability issue, especially in terms of the number of candidates evaluated and the complexity of the search space?
- Can the authors provide more details on the diversity of the generated architectures and discuss any observed trade-offs between diversity and performance?
- Why was the decision made to limit the evaluation to only 2,000 candidates, and how does this compare to the number of candidates evaluated in other state-of-the-art NAS methods?
- How does LLMatic compare to other LLM-based NAS methods, and what are the key differences in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational resources required by LLMatic, including both wall-clock time and hardware requirements?
- How does LLMatic ensure that the generated architectures are both diverse and of high quality, and what measures are in place to prevent overfitting or other potential issues?
