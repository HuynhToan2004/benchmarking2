 **Summary:**
The paper introduces UniMedI, a unified medical image pre-training framework aimed at learning common representations across different modalities of medical images. This framework leverages diagnostic reports as a shared semantic space to create unified representations for various medical image types, including 2D and 3D images. The methodology involves training on a combination of 2D and 3D data, with a focus on enhancing the consistency and applicability of visual representations across different imaging modalities. The paper presents experimental results demonstrating the framework's effectiveness in downstream tasks such as classification and segmentation. However, concerns about the novelty and the clarity of the methodological contributions were raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of medical imaging by proposing a unified framework for learning common representations across different modalities of medical images.
- The methodology is clearly described, and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed method demonstrates good performance in downstream tasks, as evidenced by the experimental results.
- The use of diagnostic reports as a shared semantic space is innovative and helps in creating unified representations for diverse modalities of medical images.
- The paper is well-organized and provides a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology, including the use of visual aids that aid in understanding the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the specific challenges associated with learning common representations for medical images, which could limit its applicability to other domains.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as similar methods have been previously explored in other domains, and the paper does not adequately distinguish its contributions from existing literature.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodological contributions, particularly the role of different components such as self-distillation and the masking and recovery task.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training settings, such as the number of training epochs and the learning rate scheduler, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, especially in terms of performance and efficiency.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not provide a clear explanation of the computational complexity or the scalability of the method to larger datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the specific challenges associated with learning common representations for medical images and how their method addresses these challenges?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and efficiency with existing methods, especially those that use similar approaches?
- Could the authors clarify the methodological contributions, particularly the role of self-distillation and the masking and recovery task, and how these contribute to the overall effectiveness of the method?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other medical imaging tasks beyond classification and segmentation?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how do these impact the practical applicability of the method in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training settings, such as the number of training epochs and the learning rate scheduler, to enhance the reproducibility and comparability of the results?
