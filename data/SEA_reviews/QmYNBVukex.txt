 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel data selection method for pre-fine-tuning language models, focusing on the optimal transport (OT) distance between the pre-training and target distributions. This approach aims to minimize the need for costly domain-specific data by selecting samples that nudge the pre-training distribution closer to the target distribution. The method is validated across various tasks, showing significant performance improvements over existing selection methods. It is also noted for its efficiency, as it scales to millions of samples within a single GPU hour. The paper also discusses the challenges and assumptions of the method, such as the need for a candidate dataset that closely matches the pre-training data and the assumption of a small selection budget.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and principled method for data selection in pre-fine-tuning language models, which is supported by theoretical analysis and extensive empirical evaluations.
- The method is computationally efficient, making it scalable to large datasets and practical for real-world applications.
- The empirical results demonstrate significant performance improvements over existing selection methods, and the method is shown to be effective in various settings, including toxicity reduction and domain adaptation.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and intuitive explanations, and the experimental results are convincing, supporting the claims made.
- The methodology is sound, and the paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes a small selection budget, which may not be realistic in many practical scenarios where larger datasets are available.
- The effectiveness of the method depends on the assumption that the pre-training data and the candidate dataset are closely aligned, which may not always be the case.
- The method's reliance on the optimal transport (OT) distance between distributions may not fully capture the complexity of the data, as it does not consider the content of individual samples.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with a broader range of baselines, particularly those that use different metrics for data selection.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations and assumptions of the method, which could affect the generalizability and applicability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and the scalability of the method to larger datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the method and how it scales to larger datasets?
- How does the method perform when the pre-training data and the candidate dataset are not closely aligned?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of the OT distance over other metrics for data selection and discuss the potential benefits of using different metrics?
- In the context of the model detoxification task, how does the proposed method compare to other methods that use different toxicity detection tools, such as the OpenAI Modern API?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, such as the performance of the model when using different selection sizes or different pre-training datasets?
- How does the method perform when the selection budget is larger, and what are the implications for the performance and efficiency of the method?
