 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach called Masked Multimodal Learning (M3L) for robotic manipulation tasks, leveraging both visual and tactile sensory modalities. M3L employs a masked autoencoder to jointly learn visual-tactile representations, which are then used to train a policy using a reinforcement learning algorithm. The methodology demonstrates improved sample efficiency and generalization capabilities compared to baseline methods, showing benefits in zero-shot generalization and vision-only policy deployment. The paper is well-written, clear, and provides a comprehensive literature review. However, it has been critiqued for its limited novelty, incremental nature, and lack of comprehensive evaluation on more complex tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, Masked Multimodal Learning (M3L), which effectively combines visual and tactile sensory modalities for robotic manipulation tasks.
- The methodology demonstrates improved sample efficiency and generalization capabilities compared to baseline methods, showing benefits in zero-shot generalization and vision-only policy deployment.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and provides a comprehensive literature review, making it accessible and informative.
- The use of high-resolution force maps in the MuJoCo environment is highlighted, which can potentially enhance the realism and applicability of the simulations.
- The paper's experimental results showcase the benefits of training a multimodal policy, particularly in tasks where visual information is sufficient but tactile information enhances performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited, as it primarily applies existing techniques (masked autoencoding and reinforcement learning) to a new problem without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the method on more complex tasks, such as those involving multiple objects or more complex environments, which could better demonstrate the generalization capabilities of the approach.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the approach, such as the computational complexity and the need for additional training data and hardware.
- The method's incremental nature and the lack of significant technical advancements over existing methods like Guzey et al. (2023) and Kerr et al. (2022) are noted.
- The paper's reliance on a single baseline (PPO) for comparison may not sufficiently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over other existing approaches.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between M3L and the baseline methods, especially in terms of the methodology and the results?
- How does the method perform in more complex tasks involving multiple objects or more varied environments?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the computational complexity and resource requirements of the proposed method?
- How does the method handle the integration of tactile information, especially in scenarios where tactile feedback might not be as informative as visual cues?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential applications of the method in real-world scenarios, particularly in terms of its practical implications and scalability?
