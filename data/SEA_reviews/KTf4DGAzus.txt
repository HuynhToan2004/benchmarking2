 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the M^3 framework for model selection in multi-modal multi-step reasoning, focusing on subtask dependencies in LLM-based autonomous agents. It creates a new dataset, MS-GQA, and proposes a method for model selection that considers subtask dependencies and user inputs. The framework is evaluated on the MS-GQA dataset and shows improvements over existing methods. The paper also discusses the challenges of model selection in multi-modal reasoning scenarios and proposes a novel approach to address these challenges. Despite its novelty and the creation of a new dataset, the paper has been critiqued for its limited scope, lack of clarity in certain sections, and potential issues with the experimental setup and methodology.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel problem setting and formulates the model selection problem in multi-modal reasoning contexts, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The creation of the MS-GQA dataset is a valuable resource for the community, providing a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating model selection methods in multi-modal reasoning scenarios.
- The paper proposes a plug-in framework with negligible runtime overhead at test-time, which is a practical and efficient solution for model selection in multi-modal agents.
- The authors provide a clear and concise presentation of the proposed method, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.
- The paper addresses a timely and important topic, with the potential to significantly impact the field of multi-modal reasoning and multi-agent systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on the MS-GQA dataset and not exploring the applicability of the proposed methods to other datasets or real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity and detail in certain sections of the paper, particularly in the methodology and experimental setup, which could hinder reproducibility and understanding.
- The paper's contribution to the field might be considered marginal, as it primarily adapts existing methods to a new problem setting without significant novelty.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to the MS-GQA dataset, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed methods to other datasets or real-world scenarios.
- There are concerns about the fairness of the evaluation, as the proposed methods are compared against baselines that do not have access to the same dataset, potentially skewing the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specifics of the model selection process, particularly how the optimal model is chosen and how this affects the overall reasoning process?
- How does the MS-GQA dataset differ from other existing datasets in terms of its suitability for evaluating model selection methods in multi-modal reasoning scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specific models used and the methodology for data collection and preprocessing?
- How does the proposed method compare against other existing methods in terms of runtime efficiency and effectiveness in real-world scenarios?
- Can the authors elaborate on the potential societal impacts of their work and how they are addressing these concerns in their research?
