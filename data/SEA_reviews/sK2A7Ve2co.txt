 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel sampling framework called Adaptive Proposal Sampling (APS), specifically the approximate Gaussian Proposal Sampler (a-GPS), which aims to approximate the score function of the posterior distribution in deep learning. The methodology is designed to adapt to the score function of the posterior, allowing for the exploration of multi-modal landscapes in deep learning, which is typically challenging with traditional methods. The framework is tested against various baselines and shows promising results in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness. However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in its presentation, with some sections being incomplete or confusing. Furthermore, the empirical results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the theoretical underpinnings of the method are not adequately explained.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel sampling framework, Adaptive Proposal Sampling (APS), which is model-agnostic and can be applied to large models, making it suitable for various applications.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear equations and a comprehensive algorithm, which aids in understanding the proposed method.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure that facilitates reading and comprehension.
- The method demonstrates a high level of efficiency, as shown in both simulated and real-world experiments, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with existing methods, which helps in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the intuition behind the proposed method, particularly how it manages to approximate the score function of the posterior without the need for a Metropolis-Hastings correction.
- There is a significant gap in the literature review, with several related works missing, which could provide a richer context for the research.
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the methodology and results sections, which could be improved with better organization and clearer presentation.
- The empirical results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to non-Gaussian distributions.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the method, particularly how it manages to approximate the score function of the posterior.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the method, which are crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
