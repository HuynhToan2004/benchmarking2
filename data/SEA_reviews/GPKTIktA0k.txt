 **Summary:**
The paper explores a phenomenon referred to as the "Reversal Curse," which suggests that large language models (LLMs) trained on statements like "A is B" do not automatically generalize to statements of the form "B is A." This issue is demonstrated through experiments involving GPT-3 and Llama-1 models, which were fine-tuned on synthetic data and tested on real-world examples. The results indicate that these models struggle with reversing the order of statements, even when the statements are logically equivalent. The paper also discusses potential explanations for this phenomenon, including the ordering of data in training and the limitations of LLMs in handling reversed statements.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental setup is well-designed, with clear methodology and detailed results, including standard deviation and statistical tests.
- The paper raises an interesting and significant problem, the "Reversal Curse," which is not well-known and has implications for the practical use of LLMs.
- The experiments are extensive and include a variety of models and tasks, demonstrating the robustness of the findings.
- The paper is novel in its approach to understanding the behavior of LLMs and provides a detailed analysis of the reversal curse, which is a crucial aspect of LLM training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive explanation for the observed phenomena, particularly the "Reversal Curse," and does not sufficiently differentiate it from other known issues in LLMs.
- The experiments are limited to a small number of examples (30), which may not be statistically significant and could lead to overfitting or biased results.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of its experiments, such as the generalizability of the findings to different types of statements or the impact of training data size.
- The paper does not sufficiently explore the potential causes of the "Reversal Curse," such as the influence of training data order or the role of language modeling in LLM training.
- The paper does not provide a clear definition or explanation of the "Reversal Curse," which could lead to confusion about the scope and implications of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the training data used and the selection criteria for the examples?
- How do the authors define the "Reversal Curse," and what specific phenomena does it encompass?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of using GPT-4 to generate training data, and how does this affect the generalizability of the findings?
- How do the authors explain the observed differences in performance between different model sizes and configurations, particularly the lack of significant improvement in larger models?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential causes of the "Reversal Curse," such as the influence of training data order or the role of language modeling in LLM training?
- How does the "Reversal Curse" relate to other known issues in LLMs, such as the "forward-backward" problem, and what are the implications of these findings for the practical use of LLMs?
