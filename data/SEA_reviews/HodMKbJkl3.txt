 **Summary:**
The paper explores the relationship between batch size and convergence speed in training wide neural networks using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). It extends previous results on batch size saturation to the non-convex setting, focusing on the interpolation regime where the neural network perfectly fits the training data. The authors provide theoretical results and empirical evidence to support their claims, demonstrating that the iteration complexity of SGD scales linearly with batch size up to a critical point, after which further increases lead to negligible improvements. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for practical applications, suggesting that larger batch sizes may not always lead to faster convergence, especially when the neural network is sufficiently wide.

**Strengths:**
- The paper extends the analysis of batch size saturation to the non-convex setting, providing a significant contribution to the understanding of batch size selection in deep learning.
- The theoretical results are supported by empirical evidence, which helps validate the theoretical claims and enhances the credibility of the findings.
- The paper is well-written and clearly presents the main results, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The authors provide a thorough discussion on the limitations of their work, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the results.
- The paper addresses a highly relevant problem in deep learning, which has practical implications for optimizing training processes in deep learning applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper heavily relies on assumptions that are not always met in practical scenarios, such as the assumption that the neural network is sufficiently wide and that the gradient of each function is Lipschitz continuous with a small constant.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with some figures (e.g., Figure 1) being difficult to interpret and the experimental conditions not being clearly described.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the impact of batch size on the quality of solutions produced by SGD, which is a significant aspect of practical deep learning applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the results and the potential negative societal impacts of the findings.

**Questions:**
- How does the batch size affect the quality of solutions produced by SGD, and what are the implications of using a batch size that is too large or too small?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the assumptions made in the paper and discuss how these assumptions affect the applicability of the results in practical scenarios?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed method compare to other methods for optimizing batch size in deep learning applications?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly in figures where the results are not clearly presented?
- How do the results of this paper compare to similar works that have been published, and what are the key differences in terms of contributions and findings?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the findings presented in this paper, and how do the authors address these concerns?
