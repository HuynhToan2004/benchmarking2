 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two novel methods, Peer Rank (PR) and Peer Discussion (PD), aimed at evaluating large language models (LLMs) by utilizing multiple LLMs as evaluators. PR involves a tournament-style evaluation where LLMs evaluate each other's answers to a question, and the winner is determined based on the majority vote. PD, on the other hand, involves a discussion between two LLMs to reach a consensus on the preferred answer, simulating a peer review process. These methods aim to address biases in LLM-based evaluations, such as self-enhancement and position bias, by utilizing multiple LLMs to evaluate each other's responses. The paper presents experimental results on two benchmark datasets, showing that these methods can align more closely with human judgments than traditional LLM-based evaluations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces two novel methods, PR and PD, that utilize multiple LLMs to evaluate each other's responses, addressing biases in LLM-based evaluations.
- The PR method is particularly effective in inducing a self-ranking of models under an anonymous setting, where model names are unrevealed.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results are promising, showing that the proposed methods can align more closely with human judgments than traditional LLM-based evaluations.
- The paper provides Python codes to ensure replicability, enhancing the paper's contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, such as those mentioned in the related work section, which could have strengthened the paper's argument.
- There is a need for more detailed analysis of the limitations and potential biases introduced by the proposed methods, especially in terms of the accuracy and fairness of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed methods, particularly concerning the number of LLMs used and the computational resources required.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the proposed methods with other existing LLM-based evaluation methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of their relative strengths and weaknesses.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the potential impact of the proposed methods on the fairness and reliability of LLM-based evaluations, particularly in scenarios where the LLMs used for evaluation are not of comparable quality.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more detailed analysis of the limitations and potential biases introduced by the proposed methods, especially in terms of the accuracy and fairness of the results?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational resources and scalability with other existing LLM-based evaluation methods?
- Can you provide more examples or case studies to illustrate the practical application and effectiveness of the PR and PD methods in real-world scenarios?
- How do the authors address the potential for biases in the LLMs used for evaluation, particularly in scenarios where the LLMs are not of comparable quality?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the "discussion ordering effect" and "opinion holding" phenomena on the results of the proposed methods?
