 **Summary:**
The paper investigates whether a language model (LM) trained on a synthetic corpus of programs can learn semantics, focusing on the causal semantics of program states. The authors use a causal framework to probe the model's hidden states, demonstrating that the model can learn semantics that are not directly observable in the training data. They propose a novel method for probing model states to extract semantic information, showing a strong correlation between the model's ability to generate correct programs and the semantic content of its states. The paper also introduces a formal model of semantics acquisition in language modeling and presents experimental results that support the emergence of semantically meaningful representations in LMs trained for next token prediction.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a formal model of semantics acquisition in language modeling, which is both innovative and well-explained.
- The experimental results support the emergence of semantically meaningful representations in LMs trained for next token prediction, showing that the model's hidden states contain encodings of abstract semantics.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental setup is sound, with a strong correlation between the model's ability to generate correct programs and the semantic content of its states.
- The use of a causal framework to analyze the effects of probing is a significant contribution, allowing for precise attribution of probe accuracy to the semantics latent in the training data.
- The paper's methodology includes a novel intervention study that enables the separation of contributions from the LM and the probe, providing a robust empirical validation of the hypotheses.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's main hypothesis, that semantics cannot be learned from form, is not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which primarily focus on the ability of the model to generate correct programs rather than on the semantics of the programs themselves.
- The choice of program synthesis as the task for evaluating semantics acquisition is questioned, as it may not adequately represent the complexity and diversity of natural language semantics.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the findings to more complex or natural language tasks, and the experimental results are limited to a specific subset of the Karel programming language.
- The paper's claims about the model's ability to learn semantics are somewhat overstated, and the methodology used for probing the model's states is not sufficiently novel or rigorous.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methodology and does not adequately address potential confounding factors in the experimental setup.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the model's ability to generate correct programs and its ability to learn the semantics of those programs?
- How does the proposed methodology compare to existing methods for probing semantic information in language models, particularly in terms of novelty and effectiveness?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the semantic information extracted by the probing method?
- How does the proposed methodology address the potential confounding factors in the experimental setup, such as the influence of the probe on the model's output?
- Can the authors elaborate on the choice of program synthesis as the task for evaluating semantics acquisition, and discuss the potential implications of this choice for the generalizability of the findings?
