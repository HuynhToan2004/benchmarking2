 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MINT, a benchmark designed to evaluate Large Language Models (LLMs) in multi-turn interactions, utilizing tools and natural language feedback. The benchmark includes a diverse set of tasks from existing datasets, adapted for multi-turn interactions, and leverages GPT-4 to simulate user feedback. The evaluation covers both open- and closed-source models, demonstrating that tool usage and feedback can enhance performance, but also revealing discrepancies between models in their ability to leverage these aids. The paper also discusses the limitations of current evaluation methods, suggesting that MINT could provide a more comprehensive assessment of LLMs in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic of evaluating multi-turn interactions, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of LLMs in real-world scenarios.
- The experimental setup is well-designed, covering a variety of tasks and models, and the results are robust, providing valuable insights into the performance of different models under varying conditions.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The use of GPT-4 to simulate user feedback is a novel approach that could potentially save costs and streamline the evaluation process.
- The analysis of the results, particularly the discrepancies between open- and closed-source models, is insightful and could lead to further research on improving the evaluation of LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the selection of the GPT-4 model for simulating user feedback and the potential biases or limitations this might introduce.
- There is a noticeable absence of human evaluations, which could have provided a more comprehensive understanding of the effectiveness of the feedback and the performance of the models.
- The methodology of using GPT-4 to simulate user feedback is not clearly justified, and the paper does not explore the potential impact of different feedback styles or sources on the model's performance.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the benchmark, particularly in terms of how it could be adapted to include larger or more diverse models.
- The selection of models for evaluation is somewhat limited, which might not fully represent the diversity of models currently available or under development.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the selection process for using GPT-4 to simulate user feedback and the potential biases or limitations introduced by this approach?
- How do you envision the MINT benchmark evolving to include more diverse models and larger models, such as those based on the 13B and 70B architectures?
- In the context of the feedback provided by GPT-4, how does the model handle the complexity and nuance of real-world user feedback, and what are the implications for the model's performance in such scenarios?
- Could you elaborate on the decision to limit the feedback to binary responses, and how this might affect the model's ability to learn from more detailed or varied feedback?
- Given the potential for GPT-4 to influence the model's performance through its feedback, how do you ensure that the results are not biased by the specific characteristics of this model?
