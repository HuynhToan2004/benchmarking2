 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph neural network (GNN) architecture called Core-Fringe Graph Neural Network (CFGNN), which aims to address the scalability and expressivity challenges in GNNs. CFGNN leverages a core-fringe structure inspired by graph indexing techniques, such as Hub Labeling, to enable selective propagation of relevant embeddings. Theoretical and empirical analyses demonstrate the expressivity and scalability of CFGNN, showing its superiority over existing GNNs in various tasks, including classification and regression, particularly for large, long-range graphs. The paper also includes ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of the core-fringe based GNN, highlighting improved expressivity and scalability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel graph neural network learning framework based on the core-fringe structure, which is shown to achieve a complete receptive field, ensuring full information coverage for each node in the graph.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to show the expressivity and scalability of the proposed method, and empirical results demonstrate its effectiveness on diverse datasets.
- The paper presents complexity analysis and conducts experiments to showcase the significant improvement in scalability, achieving better or comparable results on a consumer-grade GPU, outperforming state-of-the-art methods that require multiple high-end GPUs.
- The core-fringe structure is a novel approach that partitions the graph into a core and a fringe, which allows for more efficient information propagation and reduces the number of iterations required for message passing-based methods.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive review of the current state-of-the-art in GNNs, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to graphs with low diameter, where the core-fringe structure may not be effective.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definition and usage of certain terms and concepts, such as the "receptive field" and "information coverage," which are crucial for understanding the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other existing methods, particularly in terms of scalability and expressivity, which could undermine the claims of its superiority.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the size and characteristics of the datasets used, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the related work, particularly in the area of graph pooling, which could have provided a richer context for the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform on graphs with low diameter, where the core-fringe structure may not be effective?
- Can the authors clarify the definition and usage of the "receptive field" and "information coverage" in the context of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of scalability and expressivity with other existing methods, particularly in large-scale graph learning scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the size and characteristics of the datasets used, to enhance the reproducibility and generalizability of the results?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of over-squashing in GNNs, as mentioned in the theoretical analysis?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other existing methods, particularly in terms of the complexity and efficiency of the core-fringe structure?
