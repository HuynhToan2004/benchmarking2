 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training scheme for GANs, termed the Slicing Adversarial Network (SAN), aimed at improving the generator's ability to approximate the target distribution by ensuring the discriminator's metrizability. This is achieved by adding a linear layer to the discriminator, which is constrained to a hypersphere, and adjusting the objective function to maximize the distance between the discriminator's output and the target distribution. The authors argue that this modification enhances the discriminator's ability to serve as a metric between the generator and target distributions, potentially improving GAN training stability and performance. Theoretical analysis and empirical results are provided to support these claims, including experiments on synthetic and real image datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions, notations, and explanations, making it accessible and easy to understand.
- The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed method, including proofs and detailed mathematical derivations, which are supported by empirical results.
- The proposed method, SAN, is novel and straightforward, requiring only minor modifications to the GAN training process, making it potentially widely applicable.
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the conditions under which the discriminator can serve as a metric, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of SAN in improving the performance of GANs, particularly in terms of FID scores.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and assumptions of the proposed method, which could affect its applicability and robustness.
- The empirical results, while showing some improvements, are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons and ablation studies.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational cost of implementing the proposed modifications, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the societal impact of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of the research.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other recent methods that address similar issues in GAN training, such as those using Wasserstein distances or other distance metrics.
