 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the expressive power of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) from a novel perspective, focusing on their ability to reconstruct graph structures from node embeddings. It introduces the concept of graph reconstructability, which is crucial for understanding the information preservation in GNNs. The authors propose a new model, GRNN, which utilizes Nearly Orthogonal Random Features (NORF) to enhance graph reconstructability and improve computational efficiency. Theoretical analysis and experiments are conducted to demonstrate the model's effectiveness in tasks like link prediction and community detection. However, the paper's experimental evaluation is limited, and the scalability and practical applicability of the proposed methods are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel perspective on analyzing the expressive power of GNNs, focusing on their ability to reconstruct graph structures, which is a less-explored aspect in existing literature.
- The theoretical analysis provided is rigorous, offering insights into the expressive power of GNNs and the conditions under which they can reconstruct graphs.
- The paper proposes a new model, GRNN, which addresses the scalability issues of existing models like GCN and GIN by using Nearly Orthogonal Random Features (NORF) to improve computational efficiency.
- The proposed model GRNN is evaluated on various tasks such as link prediction and community detection, showing promising results.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental evaluation is limited, focusing only on a few datasets and not comparing the proposed methods with state-of-the-art GNNs like Graphormer, GCNII, and GINII.
- The scalability of the proposed methods is a concern, as the experiments were conducted on small-scale graphs, which might not reflect their performance on larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and the practical applicability of the theoretical findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the relation of the proposed methods to existing methods like Graphormer and GINII, which might provide insights into the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results and the scalability of the proposed methods to larger, more complex datasets.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with state-of-the-art GNNs in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed methods to larger, more complex datasets?
- How do the authors address the scalability issues, especially considering the computational demands of the proposed methods?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of the theoretical findings and discuss how the proposed methods can be applied in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of graph isomorphism, which is closely related to graph reconstructability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the datasets used and the parameters of the models tested?
