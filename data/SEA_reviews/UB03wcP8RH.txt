 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multi-task contrastive learning method, MTCon, which aims to improve the generalization capabilities of learned embeddings by integrating supervision from multiple similarity metrics. The methodology involves training a network with multiple projection heads, each learning to represent examples based on different metrics of similarity from different tasks. The paper also proposes a weighting scheme that learns to down-weight uncertain tasks during training, which is shown to enhance out-of-domain generalization to novel tasks. Extensive experiments across three multi-task datasets demonstrate that networks trained with MTCon outperform those trained with weighted multi-task cross-entropy and previous contrastive methods. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the presentation, the depth of the theoretical analysis, and the novelty of the approach, which is seen as a simple extension of existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow content, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method, MTCon, is innovative and well-motivated, with a focus on improving the generalization capabilities of learned embeddings.
- The paper is structured around a theoretical framework that provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the MTCon method across multiple datasets.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of multi-task learning, focusing on the integration of multi-task and contrastive learning, which is a significant and relevant research direction.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have strengthened the results and provided a clearer picture of the method's performance.
- The theoretical analysis is not sufficiently deep, and the assumptions made are not clearly justified or explained.
- The paper's experimental setup is limited to specific datasets, which might not generalize well to other scenarios or datasets.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications for the choices made in the experimental setup and the methodology.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, which would make it more accessible to a broader audience.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods without significant new insights or contributions.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and impact of the projection heads in the MTCon method? Specifically, what happens to these heads during the testing phase?
- How does the method handle different types of tasks, particularly those that might not be naturally suited for contrastive learning?
- Could you provide more details on the training parameters, such as the number of epochs and the learning rate, and how these affect the performance of the method?
- How does the method perform when applied to larger, more complex datasets, and what are the implications for its scalability and applicability?
- Can you elaborate on the theoretical assumptions made in the paper and how they relate to the practical implementation of the MTCon method?
- How does the MTCon method compare to other existing methods, particularly those that also aim to improve the generalization capabilities of learned embeddings?
