 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL) by incorporating an auxiliary task that predicts the action distance between states, leveraging the learned policy to improve the reward model's embedding space. This approach aims to enhance the learning process by providing additional information about the environment's dynamics and reachability of states. The methodology is evaluated across various tasks in the Meta-World benchmark, showing improvements over existing PbRL algorithms. However, the paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other methods and does not include human studies, which are crucial for validating the effectiveness in real-world scenarios. The experimental setup and the clarity of presentation also require refinement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear illustrations and figures that aid in understanding the proposed method.
- The introduction of an auxiliary task for reward learning based on action distances is a novel and interesting approach, which could potentially improve the learning process in preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL).
- The methodology is technically sound, and the experiments are well-designed, showing promising results in terms of performance and sample efficiency.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in PbRL by leveraging the policy being learned to improve the reward learning process, which is a novel direction for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other methods, particularly in terms of the baseline algorithms used and the fairness of the comparisons.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including a broader range of tasks and environments to better evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the methodology, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key concepts such as action distances and the role of the reward model.
- The paper does not include human studies, which are crucial for validating the effectiveness of the proposed method in real-world scenarios.
- The method's applicability and effectiveness in different types of environments, such as stochastic or deterministic, are not adequately explored.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights into its practical implications and areas for future research.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods, particularly those that also use auxiliary tasks for reward learning?
- Can the authors clarify the role and definition of the reward model in the context of the proposed method?
- How does the method perform in different types of environments, such as stochastic or deterministic environments?
- Could the authors provide more details on the human studies, including the methodology and results, to better understand the effectiveness of the proposed method in real-world scenarios?
- How does the method handle the issue of noisy feedback from humans, which is a common challenge in PbRL?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical application?
