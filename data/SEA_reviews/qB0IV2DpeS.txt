 **Summary:**
The paper discusses a Byzantine-tolerant distributed optimization algorithm, named Byz-VR-MARINA-PP, which is designed to handle partial participation of clients and Byzantine workers. This algorithm extends the existing Byz-VR-MARINA method by integrating gradient clipping and compression techniques to ensure robustness against Byzantine failures. The authors prove convergence rates under various assumptions, including the Polyak-Lojasiewicz (PL) condition and smoothness assumptions, and demonstrate the algorithm's ability to converge even when the number of clients is significantly reduced. However, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty, as it primarily builds upon existing methods like Byz-VR-MARINA, and for not sufficiently addressing the computational complexity and communication efficiency, particularly in scenarios with a large number of clients.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, with clear problem definition, notation, and algorithm presentation.
- The convergence analysis is thorough and includes detailed proofs and intuitions, which are valuable for understanding the theoretical underpinnings of the algorithm.
- The proposed method is novel in its ability to handle partial client participation and Byzantine failures simultaneously, which is an important practical consideration in distributed learning systems.
- The algorithm is designed to be simple and easy to implement, which could facilitate its adoption in real-world applications.
- The paper provides a comprehensive literature review, which helps in understanding the context and contributions of the proposed method within the existing body of research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining the key quantity $\lambda_{k+1}$ and the role of the clipping operator in the algorithm. This could confuse readers about the practical implementation and theoretical analysis of the method.
- The novelty of the algorithm is limited as it primarily builds upon existing methods like Byz-VR-MARINA, with only minor modifications such as gradient clipping and compression.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity and communication efficiency, especially in scenarios with a large number of clients, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The assumptions used in the analysis, such as the bounded compressor and the PL condition, are not clearly justified or explained, which could affect the credibility of the results.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the impact of different sampling strategies on the performance of the algorithm, which is a critical factor in distributed learning systems.
- The paper does not provide experimental results, which could have validated the theoretical claims and demonstrated the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of $\lambda_{k+1}$ in the algorithm, and explain how the clipping operator is implemented in practice?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and communication efficiency with existing methods, especially in scenarios with a large number of clients?
- Can the authors provide more detailed justification for the assumptions used in the analysis, particularly the bounded compressor and the PL condition?
- How does the algorithm perform under different sampling strategies, and what are the implications of these strategies on the convergence and robustness of the method?
- Could the authors provide experimental results or simulations to demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the algorithm handle scenarios where the number of Byzantine workers is significantly larger than the number of good workers, and what are the implications of this imbalance on the convergence and robustness of the method?
