 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TAFS, a novel method for activating function search in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), which addresses the challenges of inefficient search algorithms and non-differentiable objectives. TAFS frames the activation function search as a bi-level optimization problem, employing a stochastic relaxation technique to optimize the search space, which is characterized by smoothness constraints. The method is evaluated across various datasets and tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving performance. The paper also discusses the potential of TAFS to enhance the design of activation functions tailored to specific tasks, thereby potentially improving the performance of GNNs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach for activating function search in GNNs, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with a clear formulation of the problem and a well-designed search space.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The proposed method is evaluated across various datasets and tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and potential for improving the performance of GNNs.
- The paper is the first to address the design of activation functions for GNNs, which is an important and overlooked aspect of GNN design.
- The proposed method is evaluated against state-of-the-art methods, providing a robust comparison and validation of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other activation function search methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper could benefit from more detailed analysis and discussion of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- There are some typographical errors and unclear figures which detract from the professional presentation of the paper.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of computational resources and training time.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the Lipschitz constraint and the choice of activation functions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the search space, such as the number of activation functions and the range of values for each function?
- How does the proposed method compare to other activation function search methods, and what are the specific advantages of TAFS over these methods?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the experimental results, particularly in terms of the impact of different activation functions on performance?
- How does the proposed method handle non-differentiable objectives, and what are the implications of this approach for the practical application of GNNs?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of activation functions and the role of the Lipschitz constraint in the proposed method?
- How does the computational cost of the proposed method compare to other methods, particularly in terms of training time and resource requirements?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications of the proposed method?
