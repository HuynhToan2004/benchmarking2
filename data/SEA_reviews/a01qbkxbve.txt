 **Summary:**
The paper introduces O3D, a novel framework for enhancing the performance of Large Language Models (LLMs) in sequential decision-making tasks, particularly in environments like ALFWorld and WebShop. O3D leverages offline data to improve the in-context learning capabilities of LLMs, enabling them to discover reusable skills and distill generalizable knowledge across multiple tasks. The framework consists of three stages: skill discovery, policy improvement, and policy construction, each utilizing different techniques to optimize the LLM's decision-making capabilities. Extensive experiments demonstrate that O3D outperforms existing baselines, highlighting its practical utility and potential for real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and effectively communicates the contributions and results of the research.
- The proposed framework is novel and innovative, integrating skill discovery, policy improvement, and policy construction stages, which are critical for enhancing the in-context learning capabilities of LLMs.
- The methodology is simple, effective, and extensively tested across different LLMs and environments, demonstrating its robustness and applicability.
- The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including an ablation study that demonstrates the effectiveness of the different components of the framework.
- The framework is capable of improving the performance of both text-based and code-based policies, showcasing its versatility and utility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the novelty and contribution of the work, particularly in terms of how it advances the field beyond existing methods like Demo2Code.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently robust, with some results showing marginal improvements and others being inconsistent across different models and environments.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed framework, such as the potential for overfitting to specific domains or the generalizability of the findings to other types of environments.
- There are issues with the clarity and presentation of some figures and tables, which could be improved to enhance the readability and comprehensibility of the data.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its methodology with other relevant baselines, such as those using different types of LLMs or different approaches to policy improvement.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the main contributions of this work and how it advances the field beyond existing methods like Demo2Code?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting to specific domains, and what steps have been taken to ensure the generalizability of the findings?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the offline data was collected and how it affects the performance of the LLM?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods using different types of LLMs, such as those based on different architectures or trained on different datasets?
- Can you elaborate on the choice of environments and the rationale behind the specific domains used in the experiments?
- How does the framework perform when scaled up to larger or more complex environments, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in such scenarios?
