 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the challenges of performance degradation and intellectual property (IP) leakage in Vertical Federated Learning (VFL) when a participant quits unexpectedly. It introduces PlugVFL, a method that minimizes the mutual information between the representations of the passive party and the true labels, thereby preventing IP leakage. The method also includes an alternative training method to reduce the co-adaptation of feature extractors across parties, which helps maintain model performance. The paper evaluates PlugVFL on various datasets and shows that it effectively maintains model performance and disguises label information, mitigating IP leakage. However, the paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods and does not address the scalability and computational efficiency of the proposed method in large-scale VFL scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant issue in Vertical Federated Learning (VFL) by focusing on the vulnerabilities of passive parties quitting during the deployment phase, which can lead to severe performance degradation and IP leakage.
- The proposed method, PlugVFL, is innovative and effective in mitigating these issues by minimizing the mutual information between the representations of the passive party and the true labels, thereby preventing IP leakage.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes detailed explanations of the methodology and experimental results.
- The experiments conducted on multiple datasets demonstrate that PlugVFL can improve the accuracy of the model after the passive party quits, and it can prevent the passive party from fine-tuning a classifier that outperforms random guess levels even with limited labeled data.
- The paper is the first to address the label IP leakage problem in VFL, which is a novel and significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which makes it difficult to evaluate the effectiveness and efficiency of PlugVFL against other existing methods.
- The scalability and computational efficiency of PlugVFL in large-scale VFL scenarios are not discussed, which is a critical concern for practical applications.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scalability and applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper primarily focuses on two-party VFL scenarios, which may limit its applicability to more complex and realistic scenarios involving multiple parties.
- The paper does not adequately address the privacy concerns related to the use of mutual information minimization for IP protection, which could potentially introduce new privacy vulnerabilities.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential future directions for research.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Can the proposed method be scaled to large-scale VFL scenarios, and what are the computational and communication costs associated with this scaling?
- How does the mutual information minimization approach used for IP protection affect the privacy of the data?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method and discuss its scalability in more complex scenarios?
- Is there a potential for the proposed method to be adapted to other types of federated learning scenarios beyond VFL?
- How does the proposed method address the privacy concerns related to the use of mutual information minimization for IP protection?
