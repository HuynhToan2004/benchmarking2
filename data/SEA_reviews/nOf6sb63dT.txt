 **Summary:**
The paper explores the potential of using latent fingerprints within deep learning models to verify data ownership, focusing on re-generation techniques to enhance these fingerprints. The authors propose an iterative re-generation method to amplify these fingerprints and introduce a verification protocol to validate data ownership. The approach relies on the re-generation of data and the evaluation of the distance between the original sample and the re-generated content, demonstrating the effectiveness of this method through experiments on both text and image generative models. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of this method, including the use of fixed-point theory and the convergence of self-edit distances.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible.
- The proposed method of re-generation is innovative and does not require additional models or post-processing, making it a practical approach for data ownership verification.
- The method is theoretically grounded, with proofs provided for the convergence of self-edit distances.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the method through experiments on both text and image generative models, showing promising results.
- The paper is original in its approach, focusing on the use of latent fingerprints within deep learning models for data ownership verification, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on related works, particularly in the area of watermarking and data verification, which could have enriched the context and comparison of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, with a focus on specific datasets and models, which may not generalize well to other scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of data contamination, which could significantly impact the results and the credibility of the method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method, which could provide a more balanced view of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The method's effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated, as the experiments are limited to a few models and do not include a broader range of datasets or comparisons with other methods.
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the mathematical proofs and the descriptions of the experimental setups, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role of the number of re-generation steps in the method's effectiveness and discuss the potential impact of different numbers of steps on the results?
- How does the method perform when applied to different models, particularly those not used in the experiments? Could the authors provide results on other models to validate the method's generalizability?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the method, including its applicability to different types of data and its effectiveness in scenarios where data contamination is present?
- How does the method compare to other watermarking or data verification methods in terms of effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the re-generation process is executed and the choice of parameters such as the number of iterations and the initial input?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of data, such as audio or video, and what are the potential challenges or opportunities in these domains?
