 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multi-level training framework aimed at reducing the training time and computational costs of large transformer-based models, such as BERT, GPT, and DeiT. The framework utilizes a V-cycle training process, which includes operators like coalescing, de-coalescing, and interpolation, to accelerate training by leveraging the similarities in feature maps and attention across different layers and models. This approach is supported by extensive experiments demonstrating significant reductions in computational costs (up to 51.6%) while maintaining performance. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, insufficient comparison with existing methods, and potential overclaiming of its contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear and easy-to-follow structure that makes it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is simple, practical, and does not require extensive changes to existing training frameworks, making it adaptable to various transformer-based models.
- The paper provides extensive experimental results, including comparisons with multiple baseline methods and ablation studies, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The authors have released the code, which facilitates reproducibility and further research by the community.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to accelerating training by leveraging the similarities in feature maps and attention across different layers and models, which is a significant advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claim of reducing computational costs by up to 51.6% is not adequately supported by detailed computational cost analysis, making it difficult to assess the actual efficiency gains.
- The methodology section is poorly written, with notations and equations that are not well-defined or explained, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that also aim to accelerate training, such as LiGO and Net2Net.
- The paper's experimental results are not comprehensive enough, particularly in demonstrating the generalizability of the proposed method across different model sizes and architectures.
- There are concerns about the novelty of the proposed method, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant new insights or methodologies.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed computational cost analysis to substantiate the claimed efficiency gains?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art training acceleration techniques, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and training performance?
- Could the authors clarify the notations and equations used in the methodology section to improve readability and understanding?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing techniques, particularly in terms of novelty and practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other large-scale models, such as those with over 100B parameters?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, including LiGO and Net2Net, to establish the relative effectiveness of their approach?
