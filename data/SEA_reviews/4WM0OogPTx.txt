 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Conservative Density Estimation (CDE), a novel method designed to address the challenges of out-of-distribution (OOD) extrapolation errors in offline reinforcement learning (RL). CDE leverages a new constraint on the density of the stationary distribution induced by OOD state-action pairs, which is a departure from traditional methods like DICE. This approach aims to prevent large importance ratios that can lead to instability in training and poor performance. The method is evaluated against various baselines and demonstrates superior performance in sparse reward scenarios and data-limited settings. The paper also includes theoretical analysis and empirical results to support its claims, highlighting the importance of the proposed method in real-world applications where data can be scarce or costly.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The methodology is novel, introducing a new constraint on the density of the stationary distribution induced by OOD state-action pairs, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the importance ratio and performance gap, which is crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness.
- The empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness in sparse reward scenarios and data-limited settings, showing superior performance over existing methods.
- The method is compatible with other OOD sampling distribution, which adds flexibility to its application.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the equations and assumptions, particularly in the derivation of the objective function and the assumptions made.
- The method's sensitivity to the choice of hyperparameters such as ε and ζ, and the potential impact of these choices on the performance of the method, is not thoroughly discussed.
- The experimental setup and results could be improved by including more detailed comparisons with existing methods and a broader range of datasets to better demonstrate the method's effectiveness across different scenarios.
- The paper's claims about the method's effectiveness in sparse reward settings and data-limited scenarios are not sufficiently supported by the experimental results, which could be strengthened by including more detailed comparisons and a broader range of datasets.
- The method's reliance on a uniform distribution for out-of-distribution (OOD) actions and the potential impact of this choice on the method's performance in more complex or realistic scenarios is not adequately addressed.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the equations and assumptions, particularly in the derivation of the objective function and the assumptions made?
- How does the method perform when the initial state distribution in the offline data and online environments is inconsistent?
- How sensitive is the method to the choice of hyperparameters such as ε and ζ, and what are the potential impacts of these choices on the performance of the method?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, perhaps by including more detailed comparisons with existing methods and a broader range of datasets to better demonstrate the method's effectiveness across different scenarios?
- How does the method handle the potential for overly conservative policies, and what measures are in place to prevent this?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending the method to more complex or realistic scenarios, such as those involving non-uniform OOD action distributions?
