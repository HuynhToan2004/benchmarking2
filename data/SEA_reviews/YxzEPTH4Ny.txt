 **Summary:**
The paper investigates how a language model (LM) trained to predict the next token can perform arithmetic computations, focusing on binary addition and multiplication. The authors use a simple LM architecture with a small training set to explore the model's internal representations and their correlation with arithmetic operations. They propose that the LM operates as an Encoding-Regression-Decoding (ERD) machine, where computation occurs in the value space after encoding the input token representation. The study includes experiments on binary arithmetic tasks and evaluates the model's performance on different validation sets, aiming to understand the model's generalization capabilities. However, the paper's scope is limited to binary arithmetic operations, and the results are not convincingly generalizable to larger or more complex tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors explore the internal representations of the model and their correlation with arithmetic operations, providing insights into how the model processes numerical information.
- The study includes experiments on binary arithmetic tasks, which are simple and controlled, allowing for a focused investigation of the model's capabilities.
- The paper proposes a hypothesis about the model's internal mechanisms, specifically the Encoding-Regression-Decoding (ERD) mechanism, which is supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper includes an ablation study that simplifies the model's architecture to understand its essential components, which is a useful contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to binary arithmetic operations, which may not generalize well to larger or more complex tasks, such as decimal arithmetic.
- The results presented in the paper are not convincingly generalizable, as the model's performance on validation sets varies significantly, and the model's behavior on out-of-distribution data is not explored.
- The paper's claims about the model's internal mechanisms, particularly the ERD mechanism, are not convincingly supported by the evidence provided, and the correlation between token and value representations is not adequately explained.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of its study, such as the model's inability to perform arithmetic operations on larger numbers or to generalize to more complex tasks.
- The paper's conclusions are somewhat speculative and not convincingly supported by the evidence presented, particularly regarding the model's capabilities in handling arithmetic operations beyond those tested.

**Questions:**
- How does the model perform on decimal arithmetic operations, and what are the implications of using a binary encoding for arithmetic tasks?
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies in the results presented in the paper, particularly the differences between the validation sets and the model's performance on out-of-distribution data?
- How do the authors explain the low correlation between token and value representations, and what does this suggest about the model's internal mechanisms?
- What are the implications of the model's inability to generalize to larger or more complex arithmetic tasks, and how might this affect the practical applicability of the findings?
- Could the authors provide more detailed evidence or analysis to support their claims about the model's internal mechanisms, particularly the ERD hypothesis?
