 **Summary:**
The paper explores distributed temporal difference (TD) learning in networked multi-agent Markov decision processes (MAMDPs) using a primal-dual approach. It introduces a new algorithm that does not require the assumption of a doubly stochastic matrix for communication network structures, which is a significant departure from traditional methods. The algorithm is analyzed for its convergence properties, both theoretically and experimentally, under different observation models and step-size schedules. The paper claims to provide improved convergence rates and a more general applicability compared to existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the algorithm's implementation, the theoretical assumptions, and the practical implications of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to distributed TD learning by combining primal-dual gradient dynamics with null-space constraints, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, with rigorous proofs and a clear presentation of the main results, which are supported by comprehensive experiments.
- The paper addresses the practical challenges of networked MAMDPs by proposing a method that does not require the assumption of a doubly stochastic matrix, which is a common requirement in many existing algorithms.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the theoretical concepts and a good balance between theoretical analysis and practical applications.
- The results presented are novel and interesting, providing a new perspective on distributed TD learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of convergence rates and theoretical contributions. The comparison with other distributed TD-learning algorithms based on distributed optimization algorithms is insufficiently detailed.
- The algorithm's implementation and its practical implications are not clearly explained, which could limit the understanding of its effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the full-column rank of the feature matrix and the symmetry of the graph Laplacian, are not well-justified or discussed in detail, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The experimental setup and the choice of parameters are not sufficiently explained, which could limit the reproducibility and the applicability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its method, which could affect the understanding of its practical applicability and the scope of its theoretical contributions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the practical implications of the theoretical results, especially in terms of how the algorithm's performance translates into real-world scenarios?
- How does the algorithm's performance compare to other existing methods in terms of convergence rates and theoretical contributions?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other distributed TD-learning algorithms based on distributed optimization algorithms, particularly in terms of convergence rates and theoretical contributions?
- Why is the assumption of a doubly stochastic matrix not required in the proposed algorithm, and how does this affect the algorithm's performance in practical scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the practical implications of the assumptions made in the paper, such as the full-column rank of the feature matrix and the symmetry of the graph Laplacian?
- How do the experimental results compare to existing methods, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the practical applicability of the proposed algorithm?
