 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for unsupervised crowd counting using a diffusion model to generate synthetic images with varying crowd densities. The approach involves two phases: pre-training on a synthetic dataset and fine-tuning on a dataset with noisy ground truths. The method is designed to mitigate the annotation burden and achieve state-of-the-art performance on multiple benchmarks. Despite the innovative approach, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty, potential overfitting, and lack of comprehensive experimental validation. The method's reliance on synthetic data and its generalization to real-world scenarios are also questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive review of related work.
- The proposed method is innovative, utilizing a diffusion model to generate synthetic images with varying crowd densities, which is a novel approach for unsupervised crowd counting.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on multiple benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper addresses the significant issue of annotation burden in crowd counting, which is a critical challenge in the field.
- The use of synthetic data to train a network is a promising direction for unsupervised object counting, reducing the need for manual annotations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited as it primarily uses existing models (e.g., stable diffusion, CLIP) without significant modification, and the methodology is similar to previous works.
- The paper lacks comprehensive experimental validation, particularly in terms of the generalization of the method to real-world scenarios and its performance under different conditions (e.g., varying crowd densities, different datasets).
- The method's reliance on synthetic data raises concerns about its generalization to real-world scenarios and the potential for overfitting due to the use of noisy synthetic counting data.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of noisy synthetic counting data, which could affect the accuracy and reliability of the method.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with the most recent state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a better understanding of its relative performance.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the size of the dataset used and the specifics of the data generation process, which could affect the reproducibility and validation of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform on different datasets, especially those with varying crowd densities?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the size of the dataset used and the specifics of the data generation process?
- How does the method compare to the most recent state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance and generalization to real-world scenarios?
- What are the specifics of the data generation process, including the number of images used and the parameters of the stable diffusion model?
- How does the method handle the issue of noisy synthetic counting data, and what measures are taken to mitigate its impact on the final results?
- Could the authors provide more qualitative results, such as visual comparisons of the generated images and the results of the method on different datasets?
- What are the limitations of the method, and how do these affect its practical application in real-world scenarios?
