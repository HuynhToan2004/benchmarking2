 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Image-Goal Network (IG-Net), a novel method for offline visual navigation in large-scale game maps, utilizing a transformer-based architecture that integrates image and goal-conditioned behavior. IG-Net is trained end-to-end and uses auxiliary tasks such as relative and absolute position prediction, path prediction, and distance prediction to enhance representation learning. The model is evaluated against the Visual-Graph Memory (VGM) approach in a first-person shooter game, showing improved performance in terms of success rate, success-weighted path length, and distance decrement rate. The paper also discusses the limitations of VGM in handling large-scale maps and the potential of IG-Net to address these challenges.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the proposed method is simple and effective.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in handling large-scale maps which is a significant challenge.
- The method introduces auxiliary tasks to enhance representation learning, which is a novel approach.
- The paper includes an ablation study to demonstrate the impact of different auxiliary tasks, providing insight into their effectiveness.
- The method's ability to handle large-scale maps without the need for explicit map construction is a notable strength.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some parts, particularly in the description of the training dataset and the auxiliary tasks, which could benefit from more detailed explanations.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as it appears similar to existing methods such as VGM, with minor modifications.
- The evaluation is limited to a single environment and baseline method, which does not provide a comprehensive understanding of the method's performance in varied conditions.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to other types of environments or the impact of different types of auxiliary tasks.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the scalability of the method to other environments or the generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the training dataset and the specifics of the auxiliary tasks used in the method?
- How does the method handle different types of environments beyond the first-person shooter game?
- Could the authors provide more details on the evaluation metrics and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- Is there a possibility to include more diverse baseline methods in future evaluations to better assess the method's performance?
- How does the method perform when trained with a different pre-trained MAE model, and what are the implications of this on the results?
