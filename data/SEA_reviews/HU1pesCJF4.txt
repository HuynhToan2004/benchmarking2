 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel adversarial training framework named PART, which selectively reduces the perturbation budget for less critical pixels to enhance model robustness and accuracy. This approach is supported by the use of class activation mapping (CAM) to identify significant pixel regions. The methodology involves a pixel-wise reweighting of adversarial perturbations, aiming to improve the robustness of neural networks by focusing on critical pixels. The paper presents experimental results across various datasets, showing improved robustness and accuracy compared to existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the experimental setup, and the robustness of the results against different adversarial attacks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with a straightforward implementation that includes a burn-in period and a method to select epsilon_low.
- The experimental results are robust and show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in terms of natural accuracy and robustness.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed method, including a detailed discussion on the importance of pixel regions and the impact of adversarial training on these regions.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results, and includes a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the work.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as similar ideas have been previously explored in other works, such as those focusing on instance-based reweighting and pixel-based reweighting.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other reweighted adversarial training methods, which could help clarify the unique contributions of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincing, with concerns about the fairness of comparisons and the robustness of the results against different adversarial attacks.
- The paper's methodology, particularly the use of CAM and the selection of epsilon_low, is not sufficiently justified or explained, raising questions about the method's effectiveness and reliability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of the approach and how it differs from existing methods, particularly in terms of pixel-based reweighting?
- How does the proposed method compare to other reweighted adversarial training methods, and what are the specific advantages of the proposed method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the adversarial attacks used and the training conditions?
- How does the proposed method perform against different types of adversarial attacks, and what are the implications of these results for the practical application of the method?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the methodology, particularly the selection of epsilon_low and the role of CAM in the training process?
