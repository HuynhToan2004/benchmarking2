 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to contrastive learning by assigning learnable random weights to data pairs, allowing for automatic inference of the degree of noisiness for each pair. This method is aimed at enhancing the robustness of contrastive learning in the presence of noisy data pairs, which is a common issue in many datasets. The authors propose a Bayesian framework for optimizing these weights and demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through experiments on several benchmarks, showing improvements over standard contrastive learning. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as the approach is seen as an extension of previous works, and for not sufficiently addressing the scalability and practical implementation challenges in large-scale datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in contrastive learning by incorporating learnable random weights to handle noisy data pairs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method is well-motivated and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, making it easy to follow.
- The paper is well-written and structured, with clear presentation of the method and results.
- The introduction of a Bayesian framework for optimizing the weights is innovative and could be beneficial for future research in this area.
- The experiments are conducted on several benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of computational and memory requirements, which could limit its applicability to larger datasets.
- There is a notable absence of comparison with other noise-robust contrastive learning methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implementation challenges of the method, especially in terms of hyperparameter tuning and the potential for overfitting due to the stochastic nature of the weights.
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it builds upon existing works such as Chen et al. (2022), and the methodological advancements over these prior studies are not clearly articulated.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed experimental results and a broader range of benchmarks to validate the robustness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of computational and memory requirements, and discuss potential strategies for scaling the method to larger datasets?
- How does the proposed method compare to other noise-robust contrastive learning methods, and what are the specific advantages of the proposed method over these approaches?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the hyperparameters introduced in the method, particularly in terms of their sensitivity and the potential for overfitting?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting due to the stochastic nature of the weights, and what strategies have been considered to mitigate this risk?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the method's performance under different conditions, such as varying levels of noise in the data pairs?
