 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Correlation Mode Decomposition (CMD) to analyze the dynamics of neural network training, focusing on the correlation between different weight trajectories during training. The CMD method clusters weight trajectories into several modes, each represented by a single trajectory, and approximates the dynamics of each weight by linear combinations of these modes. The authors claim that this method can effectively reduce the dimensionality of weight space and enhance the efficiency of training by approximating the dynamics of weights. The paper presents several variants of CMD, including post-hoc, online, and embedded CMD, and demonstrates their effectiveness through experiments on various datasets and neural network architectures. However, the paper has been criticized for its clarity, particularly in the presentation of results and the explanation of certain methodological choices.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Correlation Mode Decomposition (CMD), which is a significant contribution to the field of deep learning, particularly in understanding and improving the dynamics of neural network training.
- The method is simple and effective, with the post-hoc CMD variant showing good results in terms of accuracy and efficiency.
- The paper provides extensive experimental results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods on various tasks and architectures, including federated learning.
- The CMD method is original and innovative, offering a new perspective on how neural network training dynamics can be analyzed and improved.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making the complex methodologies and results accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the presentation of results and the explanation of certain methodological choices.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis and explanation for the choice of parameters such as the number of modes used and the warm-up phase.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing methods like DMD, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed approach.
- The paper's claims about the effectiveness of CMD in improving test set generalization are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer explanation of the differences between CMD and other related methods.
- There are concerns about the scalability and practical applicability of the CMD method, particularly in large-scale or complex neural networks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations for the choice of parameters such as the number of modes and the warm-up phase?
- How does the CMD method compare with existing methods like DMD, and what are the specific advantages of using CMD over these methods?
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies in the results between different methods and datasets, particularly in the federated learning experiments?
- How does the CMD method perform in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially in large-scale or complex neural networks?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including error bars and standard deviations, to support the claims of improved test set generalization?
- Is there a possibility of extending the CMD method to other types of neural network architectures or tasks, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in doing so?
