 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Davidsonian Scene Graph (DSG) framework for evaluating text-to-image (T2I) models, focusing on question generation and answering (QG/A) to assess the alignment between text prompts and generated images. DSG utilizes a directed acyclic graph (DAG) to structure questions and their dependencies, aiming to ensure atomicity, uniqueness, and valid question dependencies. The framework is validated against human annotations and existing QG/A methods, showing potential improvements in correlation with human ratings. The paper also introduces DSG-1k, a dataset of 1,060 prompts designed to evaluate T2I models. Despite its innovative approach and the potential benefits it offers, the paper has been criticized for its limited evaluation scope and the potential for biases in its methodology.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the critical issue of evaluating text-to-image (T2I) models, which is crucial for advancing the field.
- The introduction of the Davidsonian Scene Graph (DSG) framework, which includes the DSG-1k dataset, is a significant contribution to the community, providing a new benchmark for evaluating T2I models.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, which is essential for a broad audience.
- The experimental results show that the DSG questions have a higher correlation with human annotations compared to other QG methods, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed framework.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the DSG framework, including the generation of semantic tuples and the process of converting these into natural language questions.
- The inclusion of the DSG-1k dataset, which is made publicly available, enhances the reproducibility and accessibility of the research, allowing other researchers to build upon this work.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on evaluating the T2I alignment using the QG/A method, which may not comprehensively address all aspects of T2I evaluation, particularly those related to the visual quality and diversity of the generated images.
- The use of only three VQA models for validation might not be sufficient to establish the reliability and robustness of the DSG framework.
- The paper's reliance on human annotations raises concerns about potential biases and inconsistencies, which could affect the accuracy and reliability of the evaluation results.
- The DSG framework's effectiveness in handling complex or ambiguous questions is not thoroughly explored, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential biases that might be introduced during the selection of prompts from various datasets, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The methodology of the paper is somewhat confusing, with a lack of clarity in the definition and use of terms such as "atomicity" and "uniqueness," which are crucial for understanding the proposed framework.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the selection of prompts from different datasets affects the results and the generalizability of the findings?
- How do the authors ensure the consistency and accuracy of the human annotations used in the evaluation process?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples of how the DSG framework handles complex or ambiguous questions?
- How do the authors address the potential biases that might be introduced during the selection and annotation processes?
- Could the authors elaborate on the methodology used to define and measure "atomicity" and "uniqueness" in the context of the DSG framework?
- What steps are taken to ensure the reliability and robustness of the DSG framework, particularly in scenarios where human annotations might be inconsistent or biased?
