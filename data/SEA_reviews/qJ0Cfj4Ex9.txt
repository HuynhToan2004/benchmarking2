 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for learning hierarchical action abstractions using large language models (LLMs) in the context of planning from natural language instructions. The approach involves a two-level planning process where high-level actions are defined by LLMs and refined into executable plans using a hierarchical planning system. The method is evaluated on two domains: Mini Minecraft and ALFRED, demonstrating significant improvements over baseline methods. The paper's primary contribution is the integration of LLMs with hierarchical planning, allowing for the automatic learning of high-level actions and their decomposition into lower-level actions. However, the method's generalization to more complex tasks and real-world environments is limited by its reliance on specific predicates and its evaluation in simplified domains.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The integration of large language models (LLMs) with hierarchical planning is a novel and significant contribution to the field, addressing the challenge of learning useful action abstractions.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear figures and a well-structured experimental setup that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation on two domains, showing that the proposed method outperforms baseline methods, including those that use LLMs for planning.
- The approach is innovative in its use of LLMs to propose action abstractions, which are then refined using hierarchical planning to create executable plans.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's evaluation is limited to simplified domains (Mini Minecraft and ALFRED), which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method to more complex or realistic tasks.
- The method's reliance on specific predicates and the need for a fixed mapping from environment states to abstract states could limit its applicability in more dynamic or complex environments.
- The paper's approach to learning action abstractions is limited to symbolic abstractions, which may not be as expressive or generalizable as other types of abstractions.
- The method's evaluation is limited to a few tasks, which may not provide a comprehensive understanding of its performance across a broader range of tasks.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its approach in detail, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
