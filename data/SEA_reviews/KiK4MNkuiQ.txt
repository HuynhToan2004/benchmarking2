 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new clustering method for DBSCAN, focusing on a novel measure of cluster quality called geometric modularity. This method aims to optimize the clustering process by maximizing the geometric modularity score, which is shown to be highly correlated with the supervised adjusted mutual information (AMI) score. The paper also proposes a local search heuristic to further enhance the clustering results. Theoretical analysis is provided for well-separated data, showing that the proposed method can recover the ground truth. Experiments are conducted on various datasets, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in terms of AMI and other metrics. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and insufficient theoretical backing beyond well-separated cases.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a simple yet effective methodology.
- The proposed method is novel and shows good performance, as evidenced by strong theoretical results and extensive experimental validation.
- The paper provides a clear and intuitive explanation of the geometric modularity score, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors have provided a thorough theoretical analysis of the geometric modularity score, demonstrating its effectiveness in recovering ground-truth clusters in well-separated data.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing the superiority of the proposed method over existing techniques like OPTICS and DPC.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on related work, particularly concerning the use of modularity in clustering and the optimization of DBSCAN hyperparameters.
- The method's performance is limited to low-dimensional datasets, which may not generalize well to high-dimensional datasets.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with state-of-the-art clustering algorithms, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to well-separated data, and it is unclear how the method performs in more realistic, non-ideal scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous experimental setup, including a broader range of datasets and more detailed comparisons with existing methods.
- There are several minor issues with the paper, such as typographical errors and inconsistencies in the use of symbols and terminology.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the choice of parameters, particularly the resolution parameter ρ, in the geometric modularity score?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other state-of-the-art clustering algorithms, such as those mentioned in the related work section?
- Can you clarify the definition and role of the "resolution parameter" in the geometric modularity score?
- How does the method handle the selection of the hyperparameter ε, especially in the context of DBSCAN's sensitivity to this parameter?
- Could you discuss the potential of extending the theoretical analysis to more realistic, non-ideal scenarios?
- What are the implications of using a different distance metric in the geometric modularity score, and how does this affect the clustering results?
