 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for learning visual representations for robotic manipulation tasks by integrating human-oriented perceptual skills into the learning process. It proposes a multi-task learning approach that leverages tasks from the Ego4D dataset to guide the representation learning towards encoding meaningful structures for manipulation tasks. The methodology involves fine-tuning pre-trained visual encoders such as R3M, MVP, and EgoVLP using a task fusion decoder, which aims to enhance the performance of downstream manipulation policy-learning. The paper provides experimental results across various simulated and real-world manipulation tasks, demonstrating improvements over baseline methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty and depth of the experimental validation, particularly in the real-world tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for learning visual representations for robotic manipulation tasks, which integrates human-oriented perceptual skills into the learning process.
- The proposed method is evaluated on a variety of tasks and demonstrates improvements over baseline methods, showing the effectiveness of the approach.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The method is general and can be applied to different backbone models, enhancing its applicability.
- The authors provide code and datasets, which facilitates reproducibility and further research.
- The paper includes real-world robot experiments, which are a valuable addition to the evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and the specific advantages of the proposed method over these existing approaches.
- The experiments are limited to a few tasks, which may not sufficiently demonstrate the generalizability of the method across different manipulation tasks.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it primarily involves fine-tuning pre-trained visual encoders on a specific dataset, which might not constitute a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The real-world robot experiments are limited in scope, with only a few tasks demonstrated, which may not convincingly show the practical applicability of the method.
- There are inconsistencies in the presentation and explanation of the method, which could confuse readers and undermine the credibility of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and the specific advantages of their approach?
- How does the method perform on a broader set of manipulation tasks, and what are the implications of these results for the generalizability of the method?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the proposed method in the context of other state-of-the-art methods, such as those discussed in related works?
- How does the method handle the potential for spurious correlations in the learned representations, and what measures are in place to mitigate these issues?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results and analysis for the real-world robot experiments, including success rates and failure modes, to better understand the practical applicability of the method?
