 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the grokking phenomenon in neural networks, focusing on the transition from a kernel predictor to a min-norm/max-margin predictor during training. It provides a theoretical framework for understanding this phenomenon, particularly in the context of large initialization and small weight decay. The authors demonstrate how this transition can lead to either optimal solutions or misgrokking, depending on the training dynamics. The paper also introduces the concept of misgrokking, where a model initially achieves perfect training and test accuracies but later experiences a significant drop in test accuracy. Theoretical insights are supported by empirical validations, including experiments on modular arithmetic and matrix completion.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new concept, "misgrokking," which is a significant contribution to the understanding of neural network training dynamics.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides a novel perspective on the grokking phenomenon, which is well-supported by empirical results.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The results are original and provide a new perspective on the grokking phenomenon, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is structured well, with a clear explanation of the theoretical results and their implications, which are supported by empirical evidence.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions and limitations are not clearly stated, which could lead to confusion about the generalizability and applicability of the results.
- The paper's focus on specific training dynamics (large initialization and small weight decay) may limit the generalizability of the findings to other training regimes.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work, particularly in the context of similar phenomena and previous theoretical analyses.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the findings could be applied in practical settings to improve neural network training, which could limit the paper's impact.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its assumptions, such as the assumption of $L$-homogeneity, which might not hold for common activation functions like ReLU.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions and limitations of their theoretical analysis, particularly in relation to the $L$-homogeneity assumption?
- How does the paper's analysis extend to other training regimes, such as those using different initialization scales or weight decay rates?
- Could the authors provide more details on the empirical validation of the misgrokking phenomenon, particularly in terms of the experimental setup and the conditions under which it was observed?
- How do the theoretical results compare to existing analyses of the grokking phenomenon, particularly in terms of the sharpness of the transition and the implications for neural network training?
- Can the authors discuss the potential practical applications of their findings, particularly in terms of improving neural network training dynamics?
