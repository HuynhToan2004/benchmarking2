 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedHyper, a novel learning rate scheduler for federated learning that dynamically adjusts both global and local learning rates using hypergradient descent. The scheduler is designed to optimize learning rates based on the training progress, enhancing convergence speed and final accuracy. Theoretical analysis and extensive experiments across vision and language benchmark datasets demonstrate that FedHyper outperforms existing methods like FedAvg and other baselines, showing a 1.1-3x faster convergence rate and up to a 15% increase in final accuracy. The paper also addresses the challenges of hyperparameter optimization in federated learning, particularly the scheduling of learning rates, and proposes a universal and robust solution that is compatible with existing optimization algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces FedHyper, a novel learning rate scheduler that can adapt both global and local learning rates, enhancing the robustness against random initial learning rates and significantly improving convergence speed and final accuracy.
- Theoretical analysis of FedHyper's convergence rate is comprehensive, and the experimental results are convincing, demonstrating a significant improvement over existing methods like FedAvg and other baselines.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the presentation is clear and logical, with a clear description of the experimental setup and results.
- The introduction of a client-side local scheduler that adjusts the local learning rate between local epochs on the clients is a significant contribution, as it can prevent certain clients from adopting excessively high learning rates, which could potentially impact convergence.
- The paper provides a novel theoretical framework for federated learning by considering the dynamic local and global learning rates, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational cost of FedHyper, particularly the additional computational overhead introduced by the client-side local scheduler.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other hyperparameter optimization methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and practical application in federated learning environments.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the initialization and adjustment of the hypergradient in the proposed FedHyper, which could affect the reproducibility and practical implementation of the method.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of FedHyper, such as its applicability to different types of federated learning scenarios, its compatibility with other optimization algorithms, and its scalability in large-scale or non-iid settings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other learning rate schedulers like FedExp, which could help in understanding the relative advantages and disadvantages of FedHyper.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of the dataset used and the specifics of the experiments, which could limit the generalizability and reproducibility of the results.

**Questions:**

- How does FedHyper compare with other hyperparameter optimization methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and practical application in federated learning environments?
- Could you clarify the initialization and adjustment of the hypergradient in the proposed FedHyper, and discuss how this affects the reproducibility and practical implementation of the method?

- Could you discuss the limitations of FedHyper, particularly its compatibility with other optimization algorithms and its scalability in large-scale or non-iid settings?
