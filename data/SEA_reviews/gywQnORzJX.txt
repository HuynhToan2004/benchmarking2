 **Summary:**
The paper introduces NPEFF, a novel method for interpreting neural networks by factorizing the per-example Fisher information matrix into non-negative components. This approach allows for the identification of sub-computations used by the model, which can be used to understand how the model processes different examples. The method is applied to both language and vision models, demonstrating its applicability and potential for uncovering interpretable features. The paper also includes experiments that show the method's effectiveness in identifying relevant sub-computations and its ability to guide perturbations to enhance model performance. However, concerns are raised about the method's scalability, the depth of interpretation provided, and the clarity of presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, NPEFF, which decomposes the per-example Fisher information matrix into non-negative components, providing a new perspective on interpretability.
- The method is applicable to any end-to-end differentiable model, making it a versatile tool for understanding various models.
- The paper includes extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of NPEFF in identifying relevant sub-computations and guiding perturbations to enhance model performance.
- The proposed method is shown to be effective in uncovering interpretable features in both language and vision models, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed appendix that provides additional information and experiments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation and explanation for the need to factorize the per-example Fisher information matrix, which could be a barrier for understanding the method's utility.
- The scalability of the method is questionable, particularly for large models, as the computational cost and memory requirements increase significantly with the number of parameters.
- The depth of interpretation provided by the method is limited, as it only identifies sub-computations but does not provide a detailed understanding of how these computations contribute to the model's predictions.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other interpretability methods, making it difficult to assess the method's relative effectiveness.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, especially in the results section where the interpretation of the results is not straightforward.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential applications of the method, including its applicability to different types of models and its scalability.
