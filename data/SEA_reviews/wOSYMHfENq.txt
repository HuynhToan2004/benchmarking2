 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the expressiveness of normalization layers in neural networks, specifically focusing on batch normalization (BN). It demonstrates that by training only the BN parameters while keeping the weights and biases of the network fixed, a neural network can represent any convolutional layer. This is achieved by constructing a two-layer source network that can approximate any target convolutional layer. The paper also discusses the implications of this finding, including the potential for universal function approximation and the role of normalization layers in deep learning. Theoretical insights are supported by empirical evidence, though the practical applicability and the novelty of the results are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the expressiveness of normalization layers in neural networks, specifically focusing on batch normalization (BN).
- The authors demonstrate that training only the BN parameters while keeping the weights and biases of the network fixed can achieve competitive performance on standard image classification benchmark data.
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the theoretical framework and empirical results, with a clear exposition of the proof of the main theorem.
- The study extends the understanding of the role of normalization layers in deep learning, particularly in the context of universal function approximation.
- The paper is original in its approach, providing a novel perspective on the expressiveness of normalization layers, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the practical implications of the findings, particularly how the results might be applied in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing works, particularly those by Frankle et al. (2021) and Giannou et al. (2023), which have explored similar concepts.
- The paper's theoretical results are limited to specific types of networks and do not generalize well to more complex or diverse architectures.
- The empirical results are not convincing, with the performance of the networks not significantly surpassing those trained with standard methods.
- The paper's assumptions about the network architecture and the training process are not realistic, which undermines the practical applicability of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the study, particularly the assumptions made and the generalizability of the results.
