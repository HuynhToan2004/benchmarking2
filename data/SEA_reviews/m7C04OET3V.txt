 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel pretext task for self-supervised learning (SSL) in tabular data, focusing on binning methods to reconstruct bin indices rather than the original values. This approach aims to enhance the performance of SSL models by addressing the challenges of heterogeneous features and irregular functions in tabular datasets. The paper evaluates the proposed method on 25 public tabular datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving SSL performance for various downstream tasks. The method is compatible with any modifications and provides a robust initial weight for fine-tuning. However, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty, insufficient ablation studies, and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel pretext task based on the classical binning method, which is compatible with any modifications and provides a robust initial weight for fine-tuning.
- The method is straightforward and effective, with clear presentation and well-organized content that enhances understanding and reproducibility.
- The paper provides extensive experiments on 25 public tabular datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving SSL performance for various downstream tasks.
- The authors have conducted ablation studies to investigate the effects of different components of the binning method, which provides insights into the method's effectiveness.
- The method is adaptable to various input transformation methods and SSL objectives, making it a versatile approach for tabular data representation learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily focuses on the binning method, which has been previously explored in other contexts.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could have provided a more balanced view of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper's experimental results are not convincing, with the proposed method performing poorly in some datasets and not consistently outperforming other methods in all cases.
- The paper lacks detailed ablation studies on the number of bins and their impact on performance, which is a critical parameter for the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method to larger datasets, which is a significant limitation for its practical application.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more detailed ablation studies on the number of bins and their impact on performance?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets, and what are the computational requirements for training and inference on such datasets?
- Could the authors provide more insights into the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential future directions for research?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of feature heterogeneity in tabular data, and what are the implications of this for the generalizability of the results?
