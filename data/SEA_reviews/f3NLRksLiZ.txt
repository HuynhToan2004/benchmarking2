 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Reservoir Transformer (RT) architecture designed for long-term time-series prediction, which integrates reservoir computing with Transformer models. The RT architecture uses a non-linear readout mechanism and multiple reservoirs to handle historical data more effectively than traditional Transformers, which typically struggle with long input sequences. The model is evaluated on various datasets, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods. The paper also explores the use of entropy and LIME explanations to interpret the model's behavior. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned, as similar techniques have been previously used in other contexts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the challenge of long-term time-series prediction, which is a significant and relevant problem in machine learning.
- The integration of reservoir computing with Transformer models is innovative and well-explained, providing a clear methodology for handling long input sequences.
- The use of entropy and LIME explanations to interpret the model's behavior is a valuable addition to the paper, offering insights into the model's decision-making process.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes detailed experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The approach is scalable, allowing for the handling of large and complex time-series datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing models like ESN and other reservoir-based models, which could have provided a clearer benchmark for the proposed method.
- The novelty of the approach is limited as similar techniques have been used in other contexts, such as in the context of RNNs and Transformers.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational cost and training time of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous analysis of the model's performance under different conditions, such as varying input lengths and data complexity.
- The use of non-linear readout and group reservoirs is not sufficiently justified, and the paper does not provide a clear comparison of these components with existing methods.
- The paper's claims about the model's ability to handle chaos and its performance on chaotic time-series datasets are not convincingly supported by the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the proposed method performs on datasets with different levels of chaos and complexity?
- How does the model's performance vary with different input lengths, and what are the implications for the model's scalability?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost and training time of the proposed method with existing models?
- How does the model handle data with varying frequencies and complexity, and what are the implications for its performance?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed justification for the use of non-linear readout and group reservoirs, and compare these components with existing methods?
- How does the model handle the butterfly effect in chaotic time-series data, and what are the implications for its predictive accuracy?
