 **Summary:**
The paper explores the integration of multiple modalities (audio and visual) in continual learning, proposing a new benchmark called MMCL based on the VGG-Gound dataset. The authors examine the stability-plasticity trade-off and task recency bias, demonstrating that multimodal learning enhances performance by integrating complementary information from different modalities. They propose a method called SAMM, which utilizes a unified multimodal architecture to achieve dynamic multimodal inference. The paper also addresses the challenges of catastrophic forgetting and the need for models to adapt to new data while retaining previously learned knowledge. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its lack of novelty in methodology and the limited scope of its experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new benchmark for multimodal continual learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors demonstrate that multimodal continual learning can effectively mitigate forgetting by integrating complementary information from multiple modalities, enhancing the model's ability to learn robust representations.
- The paper presents a comprehensive analysis of the stability-plasticity trade-off and task recency bias in multimodal continual learning, providing valuable insights into the effectiveness of multimodal learning in addressing these issues.
- The proposed method, SAMM, is well-explained and achieves good results in the experiments, showing the potential of multimodal continual learning.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the proposed method is simple yet effective, contributing to the advancement of multimodal continual learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty in its methodology, as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The experimental validation is limited, with insufficient comparisons to other continual learning methods and a lack of ablation studies on the proposed components.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of multimodal learning over unimodal learning are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, particularly in scenarios where multimodal learning does not significantly outperform unimodal learning.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definitions and distinctions between different continual learning settings, which could lead to confusion about the actual contributions of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, which is a critical aspect of continual learning, and the proposed method does not seem to provide a significant improvement in this area.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definitions and distinctions between different continual learning settings, especially the differences between Domain-IL and GCIL?
- How does the proposed method, SAMM, specifically address the challenges of catastrophic forgetting in continual learning?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to support the claims about the superiority of multimodal learning over unimodal learning?
- Why was the decision made to use the VGGSound dataset, and how does it compare to other datasets in terms of its suitability for the proposed benchmark?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the hyperparameter settings and the selection of baselines for comparison?
- How does the proposed method handle the integration of different modalities, particularly in terms of ensuring that the features from different modalities are effectively aligned and integrated?
