 **Summary:**
The paper introduces NASGraph, a novel training-free Neural Architecture Search (NAS) method that converts neural architectures into directed acyclic graphs (DAGs). This approach allows for the computation of graph metrics, such as average degree, to predict the performance of architectures without training. The method is claimed to be efficient and effective, with the ability to search for architectures in a data-agnostic manner, making it suitable for various NAS benchmarks. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty, lack of clarity in presentation, and insufficient experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-understand content, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method, NASGraph, is a training-free method that does not require GPU resources, which is a significant advantage in terms of computational efficiency.
- The method is simple and easy to implement, with a straightforward graph conversion method that can be applied to various neural architectures.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments, comparing the proposed method with existing baselines and demonstrating its effectiveness through various benchmarks.
- The combination of graph metrics with other training-free metrics shows promising results, indicating potential improvements in the accuracy of NAS.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as the conversion of neural architectures into graphs and the use of graph metrics for NAS are not entirely new concepts.
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- The clarity of the paper could be improved, particularly in the explanation of the graph conversion method and the role of different graph blocks in the model.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with the method showing only marginal improvements over existing baselines in some cases, and significant gaps in others.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant training-free NAS methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous validation of the graph metrics used, as well as a more thorough discussion on the implications of these metrics for NAS performance.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role of different graph blocks in the model, particularly how they contribute to the overall performance of the NASGraph method?
- How does the NASGraph method handle the conversion of neural architectures into graphs, and what are the specific steps involved in this process?
- In the experimental results, why does the NASGraph method show better performance on some benchmarks but not on others? Could the authors provide more insights into the factors influencing the performance of the method?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of graph metrics and how they are used in the NASGraph method? Is there a specific reason for focusing on the average degree metric, and how does it compare to other metrics in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- The paper mentions the use of a surrogate model in the NASGraph method. Could the authors provide more details on how this model is trained and how it affects the performance of the NASGraph method?
- Given the limitations of the NASGraph method, what are the main challenges and potential solutions for improving its performance and applicability in different NAS scenarios?
