 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for detecting AI-generated text using a human-in-the-loop approach. It leverages grammatical patterns extracted from parts-of-speech (POS) tags to classify text as either human-written or AI-generated. The method involves a logistic regression classifier trained on these features, which are then presented to human annotators for validation. The paper reports promising results in terms of accuracy and interpretability, with a focus on the generalizability of the method across different language models and text domains. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned, and the robustness of the method against various types of adversarial attacks remains untested.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The idea of using grammatical patterns as features for AI text detection is novel and innovative, providing a new perspective on the problem.
- The methodology is simple and effective, with a focus on interpretability and generalizability, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including robustness against adversarial attacks, and demonstrates the effectiveness of the method in a human trial.
- The approach is well-motivated and addresses a significant problem in the field of AI-generated text detection, which is of high relevance and interest to the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it primarily relies on existing methods such as POS tagging and logistic regression, which have been previously used in similar contexts.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the method, including its robustness against different types of adversarial attacks and the generalizability of the results across various language models and text domains.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly robust, with some results, such as those in Figure 2, showing significant variability and potential overfitting.
- The paper does not adequately address the societal implications of the proposed method, particularly in terms of potential biases or misuse.
- There is a lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the regularization parameter alpha in the feature selection process?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of adversarial attacks, such as paraphrasing or other forms of text manipulation?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate how the method performs in real-world scenarios, particularly in domains where the quality of human-written text may vary?
- How does the method handle cases where the text is generated by a human using a language model, such as in a chatbot scenario?
- Could the authors discuss the potential biases or limitations of the human annotators in the study, and how these were addressed or mitigated?
- It would be beneficial to see a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods to better understand the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.
