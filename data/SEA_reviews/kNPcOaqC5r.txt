 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to learning proximal operators using neural networks, specifically focusing on the gradient of an input-convex neural network (ICNN) as a proximal operator. This method allows for the recovery of the log-prior of the data distribution, which is crucial in inverse problems. The paper proposes a new training strategy called "proximal matching" to ensure the learned regularizer recovers the log-prior, thus providing a general tool for unsupervised restoration approaches. The approach is tested on various datasets, including MNIST, CelebA, and Mayo-CT, demonstrating state-of-the-art results. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited comparison with existing methods and lack of detailed discussion on the computational complexity and generalization of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the proposed method.
- The method is novel and innovative, providing a new perspective on learning proximal operators using neural networks.
- The paper is well-motivated, with a clear introduction and a logical structure that makes it easy to understand.
- The theoretical results are sound, and the paper provides a rigorous proof of the theoretical claims.
- The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and tasks.
- The paper is original in its approach to learning proximal operators using neural networks, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and training time of the proposed method compared to other existing methods.
- The generalization of the proposed method to more complex and realistic datasets is not thoroughly discussed, which could limit the practical applicability of the findings.
- The comparison with existing methods is limited, particularly in terms of inverse problems, which could have been more comprehensive to better establish the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its strengths and weaknesses.
- There are some minor typographical errors and inconsistencies in the manuscript, such as incorrect references and formatting issues.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be extended to more complex and realistic datasets, such as high-resolution images or more challenging inverse problems?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and training time with other existing methods, especially when applied to more complex datasets?
- Could the authors provide more details on the generalization of the learned proximal operator to different datasets and inverse problems?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of quantitative metrics such as PSNR and SSIM?
- Is there a possibility to use the proposed method in other applications beyond inverse problems, such as in other types of optimization problems?
