 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for achieving multi-dimensional permutation equivariance, which is crucial for handling high-dimensional permutation tasks in fields like wireless communication and matrix computations. The framework, termed SMPE, utilizes a serial composition of 1D equivariant operations to build 2D and 3D equivariant functions, enhancing network depth and expressivity. This framework is validated through experiments on various tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving performance. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as it primarily extends existing 1D equivariant operations to higher dimensions, and for its lack of theoretical depth and clarity in presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework for multi-dimensional permutation equivariance, which is crucial for high-dimensional permutation tasks.
- The proposed SMPE framework is simple, effective, and achieves good performance in the experiments.
- The paper provides a clear definition of multi-dimensional permutation equivariance and demonstrates the construction of multi-dimensional permutation equivariant networks.
- The experiments conducted on tasks like pseudo inverse computation, transmission design for MIMO system, and transmission design for cell-free MIMO system demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions of multi-dimensional permutation equivariance and a clear presentation of the proposed SMPE framework.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily extends 1D equivariant operations to higher dimensions, which may not be considered groundbreaking.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and performance metrics.
- The paper's theoretical depth is not satisfactory, with insufficient theoretical analysis to support the claims made.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for overfitting in deep networks and the computational complexity of the proposed framework.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in the clarity of figures and the organization of the content.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method to higher dimensions, which could limit its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the computational complexity of the proposed method and compare it with existing methods?
- How does the proposed method perform when scaled to higher dimensions, and what are the potential limitations?
- In the experimental results, why does the performance of the proposed method vary significantly across different settings?
- Could you provide more details on the design choices for the 1D equivariant mapping and the pooling operation, and how these affect the performance of the framework?
- How does the proposed method handle different set sizes in practical applications, and what strategies could be employed to enhance its performance in such scenarios?
- In the context of the MIMO system, how does the proposed method handle the varying number of objects, and what are the implications for performance and generalization?
