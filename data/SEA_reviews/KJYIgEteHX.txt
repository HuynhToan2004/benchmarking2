 **Summary:**
The paper explores the robustness of deep learning models for MRI reconstruction across different data distributions, focusing on the benefits of training on diverse datasets. The authors demonstrate that models trained on a combination of various data distributions exhibit superior robustness compared to those trained on a single distribution. This robustness is maintained without compromising in-distribution performance. The study also highlights the potential for training a model on a variety of distributions to yield a more effective and robust model. Despite the empirical nature of the findings, the paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive literature review.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive literature review.
- The authors present a novel approach to training deep learning models for MRI reconstruction, focusing on the benefits of diverse training data.
- The findings are significant as they demonstrate that training on a diverse set of data distributions can yield a more effective and robust model.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the robustness of deep learning models for MRI reconstruction across different data distributions.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments to demonstrate the benefits of training on diverse data distributions, which are supported by both qualitative and quantitative results.
- The paper is well-organized and provides a clear motivation for the study, which is crucial for understanding the impact of training data on model performance and robustness in accelerated MRI.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and concise definition of the problem it aims to solve. It is unclear whether the focus is on training a single model that can handle various data distributions or on training multiple models tailored to specific distributions.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the computational costs associated with training on diverse data distributions versus training on a single distribution.
- The experiments conducted are limited to specific datasets, which may not generalize well to other datasets or real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the potential limitations or challenges associated with training on diverse data distributions, such as the need for extensive data collection or the potential for increased computational complexity.
- The paper does not explore the impact of different data augmentation techniques on the robustness of the models.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the generalizability of the findings to other medical imaging modalities or to other types of data distributions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify whether the focus is on training a single model that can handle various data distributions or on training multiple models tailored to specific distributions?
- How do the authors address the potential for overfitting when training on diverse data distributions?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational costs associated with training on diverse data distributions versus training on a single distribution?
- How do the findings of this study generalize to other medical imaging modalities or to other types of data distributions?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of different data augmentation techniques on the robustness of the models?
- How do the authors ensure that the models trained on diverse data distributions do not compromise in-distribution performance?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the data augmentation techniques used and the training parameters?
