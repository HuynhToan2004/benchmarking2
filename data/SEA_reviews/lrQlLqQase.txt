 **Summary:**
The paper explores causal reasoning in dynamical systems, proposing a novel approach to identify causal relationships using reinforcement learning (RL) frameworks. It introduces two new metrics, GRIT and REACH, which quantify the causal impact of events based on the probability of their occurrence and the maximum likelihood of their occurrence given a set of actions. The authors leverage these metrics to formulate causal inference as an RL problem, providing a theoretical foundation and experimental validations using datasets like Atari and a diabetes simulator. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for causal reasoning in dynamic environments, though it lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and assumptions of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the problem and its solution, with a clear and logical flow of ideas.
- The approach is novel, providing a new way of thinking about causal reasoning in dynamic systems, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical foundation of the paper is solid, with a clear discussion of the assumptions and limitations of the proposed methods.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods in practical scenarios.
- The paper provides a novel perspective on causal reasoning by connecting it to reinforcement learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work, particularly in the area of dynamic Bayesian networks and causal discovery in time series data.
- The assumptions made by the proposed methods, such as the knowledge of the dynamics and the availability of the reward function, are not clearly stated or discussed.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous proofreading to correct minor typos and inconsistencies in notation and terminology.
- The paper does not adequately address how the proposed methods handle complex scenarios involving multiple variables or non-linear dynamics.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, with only two datasets used, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed methods.

- Can you provide more details on how the proposed methods could be adapted to handle scenarios where the dynamics are not fully known or where the reward function is not available?
