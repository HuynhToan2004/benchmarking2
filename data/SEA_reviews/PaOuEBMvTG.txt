 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for unsupervised representation learning in multi-object images, termed Multiple Object Stitching (MOS). This method involves stitching single object images to create artificial multi-object images, which are then used to construct object-level correspondences without requiring human annotations. The MOS strategy facilitates object-level contrastive learning, enhancing the representation of multiple objects in images. The method is evaluated across various datasets, including ImageNet, CIFAR, and COCO, demonstrating improvements in object detection and instance segmentation tasks. Despite the innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its clarity in explaining the motivation behind the method and the necessity of stitching single-object images. Additionally, concerns are raised about the method's scalability and its performance on larger, more complex datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for unsupervised representation learning of multi-object images, specifically using stitching of single-object images to create multi-object images, which is a simple yet effective strategy.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on both single-object centric images and multi-object images, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes detailed experimental results, which are supported by ablation studies.
- The method's simplicity and the use of a simple baseline for comparison, such as random crop, help in understanding the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the motivation behind the method, particularly why stitching single-object images is necessary for multi-object representation learning.
- There is a concern about the scalability of the method, especially when dealing with a large number of objects in an image, which might require a large number of single-object images.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential issues of overfitting, especially when using a large number of single-object images for stitching.
- The performance of the method on larger datasets like COCO is not impressive, and the method's effectiveness is questioned when compared to other methods like iBOT.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for domain gaps between synthesized and natural images, which could affect the multi-object representation learning.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more clarity on the motivation behind the stitching of single-object images for multi-object representation learning?
- How does the proposed method perform when dealing with a large number of objects in an image? Is it scalable?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for overfitting when using a large number of single-object images for stitching?
- Why is the performance of the method on COCO not impressive, and how does it compare to other methods like iBOT?
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly the potential domain gaps between synthesized and natural images?
