 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the convergence properties of various algorithms in the context of matrix games, focusing on the last-iterate convergence of Regret Matching+ (RM+) and its variants. It explores the behavior of RM+ and its derivatives, including ExRM+ and SPRM+, and introduces restarted variants to achieve linear convergence rates. The paper also discusses the challenges in proving convergence under specific conditions and provides empirical evidence to support the theoretical claims. Despite the paper's technical strength and novelty in studying the last-iterate convergence of RM+, it lacks a broader comparison with other state-of-the-art methods and does not address the convergence rates of non-restarted algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem by studying the convergence properties of RM+, a widely used algorithm in practice, which is not well understood from a theoretical perspective.
- The introduction of new variants of RM+, such as ExRM+ and SPRM+, and the restarted versions that achieve linear convergence rates, is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides a detailed analysis of the convergence properties of these algorithms, which is crucial for understanding their practical implications.
- The authors have demonstrated that the last iterate of the algorithm converges to a Nash equilibrium under certain conditions, which is a novel result.
- The empirical results provided in the paper are convincing and support the theoretical findings, which is essential for validating the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could help in understanding the relative performance of the proposed algorithms.
- The convergence rates of non-restarted ExRM+ and SPRM+ are not discussed, which is a significant gap in the theoretical analysis.
- The paper primarily focuses on matrix games, which limits its applicability to more complex game settings such as extensive-form games.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of why RM+ and its variants fail to converge in some cases, which is crucial for understanding the limitations of these algorithms.
- There is a lack of discussion on the implications of the results for practical applications, which could be a critical factor in evaluating the usefulness of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and how these assumptions affect the practical applicability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide a comparison of the proposed algorithms with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of convergence rates and practical performance?
- How do the results of this paper compare with those of similar studies, such as those by Lee et al. (2021) and Farina et al. (2023)?
- Could you clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and discuss how these assumptions affect the practical applicability of the results?
- What are the implications of the last-iterate convergence results for practical applications, and how do these results compare with the ergodic convergence results in terms of practical performance?
- Can you provide more details on the convergence rates of non-restarted ExRM+ and SPRM+, and discuss why these algorithms fail to converge in some cases?
- How do the results of this paper contribute to the understanding of the practical limitations of RM+ and its variants, and what are the potential solutions to address these limitations?
