 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CompWoB, a new benchmark for evaluating language model agents (LMAs) in compositional web automation tasks. It demonstrates that existing LMAs, such as GPT-3.5-turbo and GPT-4, struggle with compositional tasks, achieving only 24.9% success on average. The authors also propose a new data mixture strategy, HTML-T5++, to address these challenges, showing that it can achieve human-level performance on MiniWoB and the best zero-shot performance on CompWoB. The paper highlights the importance of compositional generalization in web automation and provides insights into the challenges posed by complex instructions and task compositions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed benchmark, CompWoB, is novel and relevant, focusing on compositional generalization in web automation, which is a significant and understudied area.
- The experiments are comprehensive, including a variety of language model agents (LMAs) and a range of tasks, which demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of the findings.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the performance of different LMAs on compositional tasks, including the impact of instruction length and the order of instructions.
- The introduction of HTML-T5++, a new model trained using a data mixture strategy, represents a significant contribution to the field and shows potential for improving LMA performance in compositional tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the design choices of CompWoB, particularly the criteria for task selection and the rationale behind the specific tasks chosen.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections, such as the explanation of the data rebalancing process and the role of the "and then" connector in task composition.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential negative societal impacts of the work, which could include misuse of the benchmark for malicious purposes.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its findings with prior works, particularly those that have studied compositional generalization in similar contexts.
- The paper's conclusions are somewhat limited by the scope of the experiments, which primarily focus on web automation and do not extend to other domains where LMAs might be applied.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the design choices of CompWoB, particularly the criteria for task selection and the rationale behind the specific tasks chosen?
- How does the "and then" connector affect the performance of LMAs in compositional tasks?
- What are the specific criteria used to balance data distribution across tasks in the data rebalancing process?
- How does the performance of HTML-T5++ compare to other LMAs in tasks beyond those included in CompWoB?
- Can the authors elaborate on the potential negative societal impacts of the work and how these might be mitigated?
- How do the findings of this study compare to prior works that have explored compositional generalization in similar contexts?
