 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for long-term time-series forecasting, focusing on the capture of long-term variations that extend beyond the window size. It proposes an autocorrelation-based contrastive loss to facilitate the learning of long-term representations, alongside a decomposition architecture to enhance forecasting performance. The method is tested on various benchmarks, demonstrating significant improvements over existing models. The authors argue that their approach addresses the limitations of traditional sliding window methods by integrating long-term dependencies across windows. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the autocorrelation-based contrastive loss and the clarity of the experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the proposed method, making it easy to follow.
- The introduction of the autocorrelation-based contrastive loss is innovative and addresses the limitation of traditional sliding window methods by capturing long-term variations.
- The proposed decomposition architecture is effective in enhancing forecasting performance, and the method's clarity is evident through the use of clear figures and detailed explanations.
- The experiments are comprehensive, involving multiple datasets and various baseline models, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a novel direction for long-term forecasting and offers a more reliable method for effectively integrating long-term variations into time-series representation learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the autocorrelation-based contrastive loss and its reliance on a single window for training.
- The novelty of the autocorrelation-based contrastive loss is questioned as similar approaches have been previously used in other contexts.
- There is a need for more detailed explanations and justifications for the design choices, such as the selection of the autocorrelation function and the decomposition architecture.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, particularly in terms of the significance of the improvements and the clarity of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational efficiency of the proposed method compared to other models, which could be a significant drawback in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the selection of the autocorrelation function and its impact on the results?
- How does the proposed method perform in multivariate forecasting scenarios, and what are the implications for the selection of the autocorrelation function in such cases?
- Can the authors clarify the computational efficiency of the proposed method and compare it to other models in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of long-term variations, such as those that are not periodic or cyclical?
- In the context of the decomposition architecture, how is the separation of short-term and long-term branches determined, and what are the implications for the model's performance and interpretability?
- Could the authors provide more detailed justifications for the design choices, particularly in the context of the decomposition architecture and the selection of the autocorrelation function?
