 **Summary:**
The paper investigates non-stationary multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) in games with bandit feedback, focusing on general-sum games, potential games, and Markov games. It proposes a black-box approach that can adapt to different types of equilibria, such as Nash equilibria, correlated equilibria, and coarse correlated equilibria. The paper also introduces a black-box reduction to single-agent learning, which simplifies the testing for various types of equilibria. The approach includes a restart-based algorithm when the non-stationarity budget is known and a multi-scale testing algorithm when it is unknown. Theoretical analysis and numerical results are provided, although the paper's novelty and the clarity of its contributions are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and provides a comprehensive review of related work, making it easy to understand the context and the paper's position within the existing literature.
- The proposed algorithm is versatile and applicable to a wide range of multi-agent settings, including zero-sum, potential, and general-sum games.
- The paper introduces a novel black-box reduction to single-agent learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors provide a clear explanation of the challenges associated with non-stationary MARL and how their approach addresses these challenges.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the algorithms and their theoretical underpinnings.
- The paper includes numerical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms in various multi-agent settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the novelty of the proposed algorithms compared to existing works, particularly in terms of technical contributions.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is not sufficiently clear, with some key concepts and definitions missing or not adequately explained.
- The paper's reliance on black-box oracles for learning and testing equilibria in stationary environments may limit its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's contribution to the field of multi-agent reinforcement learning is questioned, as it primarily builds on existing works without introducing significant new theoretical insights or methodologies.
- The paper's experimental setup is limited, and it does not compare its results against existing methods, which makes it difficult to assess the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
- The paper does not adequately address the societal impact of its work, which is a significant oversight for a paper in this field.
