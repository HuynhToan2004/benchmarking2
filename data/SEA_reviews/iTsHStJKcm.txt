 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for deformable object manipulation, focusing on dough manipulation tasks. It utilizes a large language model (LLM) to generate a high-level plan, which is then refined into a set of subgoals. These subgoals are used to guide a closed-loop model predictive control strategy, leveraging differentiable physics and point-to-point correspondence to optimize tool usage and deformations. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through simulations and real-world experiments, showcasing its ability to handle complex tasks without prior demonstrations. The paper also discusses the limitations of the approach and its potential for future improvements.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in the field of deformable object manipulation by proposing a method that does not require prior demonstrations, which is a significant advancement.
- The integration of a large language model (LLM) with differentiable physics and point-to-point correspondence is a novel approach that enhances the method's ability to handle complex tasks.
- The paper is well-structured, clearly explaining the methodology and the experimental results, which are supported by both simulated and real-world experiments.
- The use of LLM for high-level task decomposition and subgoal generation is a notable contribution, as it allows for the handling of complex tasks without requiring extensive training or demonstration data.
- The paper demonstrates the method's ability to generalize to new, unseen tasks, which is a significant advantage over traditional methods that rely on extensive training data.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the area of LLM-based planning for robot manipulation, which could provide a better context for the contributions of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on the LLM for generating subgoals could limit its applicability to tasks that are not easily described in text, such as those involving complex shapes or intricate manipulations.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational efficiency of the method, which could be a significant concern for real-time applications.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a few tasks, which may not fully demonstrate its generalizability or robustness across a wider range of manipulation tasks.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method in detail, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of their method with existing works, particularly those that use LLMs for task planning and manipulation?
- How does the method handle tasks that are not easily described in text, such as those involving complex shapes or intricate manipulations?
- Can the authors elaborate on the computational efficiency of the method, especially in terms of real-time applicability?
- How does the method handle tasks that are not well-defined or have multiple possible solutions?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the method in more detail, particularly in terms of its applicability to tasks beyond those tested and its scalability to more complex or varied environments?
