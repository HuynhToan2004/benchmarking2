 **Summary:**
The paper introduces BrainLM, a foundation model for analyzing brain activity dynamics using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) data. BrainLM is trained on a large corpus of fMRI recordings and demonstrates proficiency in tasks such as predicting future brain states, decoding clinical variables, and identifying functional networks. It employs self-supervised masked-prediction training to learn representations of brain activity, which can be fine-tuned for specific tasks or used in zero-shot inference mode to identify functional networks. The model's performance is evaluated against various baselines and demonstrates superior performance in many tasks. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the methodology and the lack of detailed comparisons with existing models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel foundation model for fMRI data, BrainLM, which can be fine-tuned for specific tasks or used in zero-shot inference mode to identify functional networks.
- The model is trained on a large corpus of fMRI recordings, which is a significant advantage in terms of data size and model capacity.
- The paper demonstrates that BrainLM can generalize well to new cohorts and is proficient in both fine-tuning and zero-shot inference tasks.
- The model's ability to learn robust generalizable representations of fMRI recordings is highlighted, which is crucial for clinical applications.
- The interpretability of the model is emphasized, which is crucial for understanding the model's outputs and their implications.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of BrainLM's performance, including comparisons with various baselines and different model sizes.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other foundation models for brain activity recordings, which could help establish the novelty and effectiveness of BrainLM.
- The methodology of BrainLM is not significantly different from existing models, and the paper does not adequately discuss how BrainLM improves upon or diverges from these models.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of BrainLM, which could provide a more balanced view of the model's capabilities and potential shortcomings.
- The paper does not provide the code for the model, which could limit the reproducibility and transparency of the research.
- The paper's focus on fMRI data might limit its applicability to other modalities such as EEG or MEG, which could be crucial for certain applications.
- The paper does not discuss the ethical implications of the research, which is increasingly important in the field of neuroscience and AI.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison with other foundation models for brain activity recordings, discussing the advantages and disadvantages of each model?
- How does BrainLM differ from existing models in terms of methodology and performance?
- What are the limitations of BrainLM, and how do these affect its applicability and effectiveness?
- Could the authors provide the code for the model to enhance reproducibility and transparency?
- How does BrainLM perform on other modalities such as EEG or MEG, and could it be adapted to these modalities?
- What are the ethical implications of using BrainLM for analyzing brain activity, and how are these addressed in the research?
