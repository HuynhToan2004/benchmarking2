 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ArchLock, a novel neural architecture search (NAS) algorithm designed to reduce the transferability of deep neural network (DNN) models at the architecture level. ArchLock employs zero-cost proxies and evolutionary search to design model architectures with low transferability, maintaining high performance on the source task while degrading performance on target tasks. The methodology includes a binary predictor and a meta-learning approach to simulate target task embeddings, enhancing the efficiency of architecture evaluation. Extensive experiments on NAS-Bench-201 and TransNAS-Bench-101 demonstrate that ArchLock can significantly reduce transferability by up to 30% and 50%, respectively, with minimal performance degradation on source tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to the problem of transferability reduction in DNN models.
- The proposed method is technically sound, with a focus on reducing transferability at the architecture level, which is an innovative approach compared to existing methods that primarily address transferability at the model parameter level.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering various NAS benchmarks, and demonstrate that the proposed method can effectively reduce transferability while maintaining high performance on the source task.
- The use of a binary predictor with multiple zero-cost proxies and meta-learning to simulate target task embeddings is a novel approach that enhances the efficiency of architecture evaluation.
- The paper is well-motivated, addressing the critical issue of unauthorized transfer of DNN models, which is often overlooked in existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the scalability and generalizability of the results to more complex and varied tasks.
- The experiments are primarily conducted on smaller datasets like CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, which may not fully represent the effectiveness of the method on larger, more complex datasets.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with existing NAS methods to clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method.
- The method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios, where the target task is not known, is not thoroughly discussed.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on zero-cost proxies and meta-learning for simulating target tasks may limit its applicability to tasks with different characteristics.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the work?
- How does the method perform when the target task is not known, and how does it compare to existing NAS methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other NAS methods to clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method?
- How does the method handle tasks with different characteristics, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
- Could the authors provide more experimental results on larger, more complex datasets to validate the effectiveness of the method in real-world scenarios?
