 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ColCLIP, a variant of CLIP designed for fine-grained image retrieval. It employs the MaxSim operator to interact with text queries, enabling the model to focus on specific visual elements rather than just the most salient parts of images. The model is fine-tuned on the Visual Genome dataset and evaluated on various retrieval tasks, demonstrating its ability to handle fine-grained details effectively. Despite its promising results, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and the need for more comprehensive evaluations and comparisons with other models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an interesting approach to fine-tuning CLIP for fine-grained image retrieval, which is a significant application of pre-trained models.
- The methodology, including the use of the MaxSim operator and the asymmetric training strategy, is well-explained and supported by qualitative results.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The authors have released the code and models, enhancing the reproducibility and accessibility of the research.
- The proposed method demonstrates its effectiveness in handling fine-grained details, which is crucial for image retrieval tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other models, such as FILIP, which also addresses fine-grained image retrieval. This omission makes it difficult to assess the relative strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as the use of MaxSim and asymmetric training are not significantly different from existing methods.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to the Visual Genome dataset, which may not fully represent the generalization capabilities of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on the MaxSim operator might be overemphasized, as it is already used in CLIP and FILIP. More ablation studies are needed to clarify the specific contributions of this component.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational overhead introduced by the MaxSim operator and the asymmetric training strategy.
- The method's performance on non-uniform test sets and compositional queries is not convincingly superior to existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with FILIP, including a head-to-head comparison on the same datasets?
- What are the specific advantages of the MaxSim operator over other interaction methods, and how does it compare in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method handle the zero-shot setting, and what are the implications for retrieval tasks that require broader context or more generalized queries?
- Can the authors clarify the computational overhead introduced by the MaxSim operator and the asymmetric training strategy, and discuss potential strategies to mitigate these costs?
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets, such as MSCOCO, and what are the implications for its generalization to different image domains?
