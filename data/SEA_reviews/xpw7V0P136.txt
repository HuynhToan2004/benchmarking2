 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SynTra, a method aimed at reducing hallucination in Large Language Models (LLMs) by optimizing system messages on synthetic tasks. The method involves fine-tuning the system prompt with prefix tuning, which is then transferred to downstream tasks. The approach is evaluated on three realistic tasks, showing that the method reduces hallucination in LLMs. However, the paper is criticized for its limited evaluation scope, focusing on only two models and not sufficiently comparing against other methods. It also lacks a detailed discussion on the synthetic tasks used and their impact on hallucination reduction.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-articulated methodology.
- The proposed method of optimizing the system message to reduce hallucination is innovative and effective, as demonstrated through experiments.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's components, including system messages, reference data, and the trade-off between synthetic tasks and demonstrations.
- The experiments are thorough, covering multiple tasks and models, and include a detailed analysis of the results.
- The paper addresses a significant issue in the field of AI, namely the reduction of hallucination in LLMs, which is a crucial aspect of AI safety.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation of the method is limited, focusing only on two models (Orca and Vicuna) and not comparing against other methods, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's effectiveness.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the synthetic tasks used, particularly how they are designed and their impact on reducing hallucination.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the proposed method could be applied to different types of LLMs, such as those with different sizes or architectures.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the method, such as the requirement for a synthetic task that is not always available or easy to design.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the results, particularly in terms of how the method performs across different tasks and models.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare against other methods for reducing hallucination in LLMs?
- Can the method be applied to different types of LLMs, such as those with different sizes or architectures?
- How does the method perform on different types of tasks, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the synthetic tasks used, particularly how they were designed and how they impact hallucination reduction?
- How does the method perform when optimizing for different levels of hallucination reduction, and what are the implications for the quality of the outputs?
