 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DynaVol, a novel method for unsupervised learning of object-centric representations in dynamic 3D scenes, using a 3D scene generative model that integrates geometric structures and object-centric learning in a differentiable volume rendering framework. This methodology, which includes object-centric voxelization, allows for the capture of 3D scene dynamics and enables scene editing capabilities. The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation across various datasets, including synthetic and real-world scenes, demonstrating the method's superiority over existing approaches. However, the paper has been critiqued for its complexity, which could limit its practical application, and its reliance on specific training data configurations, which might not generalize well to other scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for unsupervised learning of object-centric representations in dynamic 3D scenes, which is a significant advancement over existing methods that primarily focus on 2D image decomposition.
- The methodology, which includes object-centric voxelization, allows for the capture of 3D scene dynamics and enables scene editing capabilities, providing a more comprehensive understanding of dynamic scenes.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method demonstrates superior performance over existing techniques in unsupervised scene decomposition in both synthetic and real-world scenarios, as shown in comprehensive evaluations across various datasets.
- The methodology is innovative, combining differentiable volume rendering with object-centric voxelization, which is a novel approach in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the computational cost and scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The method's reliance on specific training data configurations, such as the use of multiple initial views, might limit its generalizability to other scenarios.
- The complexity of the method, including its three-stage training and the need for a compositional NeRF, might make it less practical for real-world applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing methods that perform dynamic scene decomposition, which could provide a clearer understanding of its advantages and limitations.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process, particularly the number of epochs and the training time required, which are crucial for reproducibility and practical implementation.
- The method's performance in real-world scenarios is not adequately demonstrated, with most results being from synthetic datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost and scalability of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods that perform dynamic scene decomposition, particularly in terms of performance and practical applicability?
- Could the authors clarify the specific training data configurations required for the proposed method and discuss how it might generalize to other scenarios?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of objects, such as rigid and non-rigid objects, in dynamic scenes?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, particularly the number of epochs and the training time required?
- How does the proposed method handle the initialization of the voxel grid, especially in scenarios where the objects are not initially visible?
- Could the authors provide more details on the slot attention mechanism and how it contributes to the overall performance of the method?
