 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Compresso, a novel method for structured pruning in large language models (LLMs) that integrates collaborative prompting and LORA with L0 regularization. This approach allows for the optimization of pruning decisions during training, aiming to maintain performance while reducing model size. The methodology is evaluated on various benchmarks, including zero-shot and few-shot tasks, showing that Compresso outperforms existing one-shot pruning baselines. The paper also explores the use of collaborative prompting to enhance the model's performance and discusses the potential of the method in real-world applications. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the lack of comprehensive comparisons with other pruning methods, and the absence of inference speed analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method, Compresso, which includes a novel collaborative prompting strategy.
- Compresso outperforms one-shot pruning baselines in experiments, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of LoRA and L0 regularization in pruning is highlighted as a significant contribution, particularly in reducing the training cost and improving the pruning efficiency.
- The paper addresses the challenge of training-based pruning for LLMs, which is resource-intensive and requires extensive GPU memory.
- The methodology of Compresso is evaluated on various benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness across different tasks and pruning ratios.
- The introduction of a collaborative pruning prompt is innovative, enabling LLMs to play an active role in the pruning process, which is a departure from traditional methods where LLMs are treated as passive entities.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other pruning methods, which is crucial for understanding the relative performance of Compresso.
- There is no inference speed analysis, which is essential for evaluating the practical implications of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the training cost of Compresso with other pruning methods, which is necessary to assess the efficiency of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as similar techniques have been used in previous works, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its methodology from these existing methods.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impact of the proposed method, which is a significant omission in the ethical evaluation of the work.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the specifics of the collaborative prompting process, which is crucial for reproducibility and further research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the training cost, inference speed, and model performance of Compresso with other pruning methods?
- How does the collaborative prompting process work in detail? Is it a manually crafted prompt or generated by the LLM?
- Could the authors clarify the rationale behind the choice of the GPT-4-Alpaca dataset for pruning and discuss its impact on the performance of the pruned models?
- In the context of the collaborative prompting, how does the LLM adapt to the pruning task, and what are the implications of this adaptation on the overall performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger models like 13B and 65B, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in scaling up the method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process, such as the specifics of the LoRA and L0 regularization methods used, and the impact of these parameters on the pruning process?
