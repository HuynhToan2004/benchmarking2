 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel image tokenizer, SEED, designed to enable multimodal large language models (LLMs) to process images and text interchangeably. SEED employs a discrete visual code that aligns with high-level semantics, allowing for seamless integration with LLMs. The tokenizer is tested on various tasks, including image captioning, question answering, and text-to-image generation, demonstrating competitive performance against state-of-the-art models. The paper also discusses the challenges of integrating image and text processing, particularly the need for causal dependencies in image tokens, and provides a detailed experimental setup and results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel image tokenizer, SEED, which aligns high-level semantics with discrete visual codes, enabling seamless integration with large language models (LLMs).
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the complex system design and experimental results.
- The paper is well-written, with detailed explanations of the methodology, experimental setup, and results, making it accessible to readers.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed tokenizer, comparing it against existing models and demonstrating its effectiveness in various multimodal tasks.
- The authors have made the code and models available, enhancing reproducibility and further research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the challenges and limitations of integrating image and text processing, particularly the unique challenges posed by image processing compared to text.
- There is a lack of clarity in the training details, particularly in how the image-text pairs are used for training and the specifics of the pre-training phase.
- The paper could provide more detailed explanations of the experimental setup and results, including the specifics of the datasets used and the metrics employed.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of incompatibility between the unidirectional attention in LLMs and the bi-directional attention in image encoders, which could affect the model's performance.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as the use of causal visual codes and the Q-former are not entirely novel, having been previously explored in other works.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, particularly how image-text pairs are utilized and the specifics of the pre-training phase?
- How does the SEED tokenizer compare to other image tokenizers in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the start and end tokens in the image processing, and explain why they are not used in video processing?
- How does the SEED tokenizer handle the alignment of visual tokens with text tokens in the LLM, especially in cases where the text is not a complete sentence?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific datasets used and the metrics employed in the evaluation?
- How does the SEED tokenizer handle the generation of images with the LLM, particularly in terms of the quality and diversity of the generated images?
