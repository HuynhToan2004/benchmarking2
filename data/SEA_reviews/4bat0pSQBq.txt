 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph neural network (GNN) model, termed ComGNN, designed for simulating water depths in flooding scenarios. The model is structured around a two-stage approach: first, it predicts water retention based on precipitation and previous water depths, and second, it propagates water through the graph, simulating the dynamics of flood propagation. The model is evaluated against several baseline models using a dataset from Harris County, Texas, and shows promising results in terms of accuracy and efficiency. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the methodology, the generalizability of the results, and the novelty of the approach compared to existing literature.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the problem, methodology, and results, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is innovative, combining a novel approach to solving the shallow water equation with a graph neural network (GNN) architecture, which is well-explained and easy to understand.
- The paper includes a thorough literature review, positioning the work within the existing body of knowledge.
- The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the superior performance of the proposed method over various baseline models.
- The results showcase the effectiveness of the proposed method in simulating water depths in flooding scenarios, highlighting its potential utility in flood modeling.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The motivation for the methodology is not well-articulated, particularly the need for a two-stage approach and the choice of specific GNN architectures.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed description of the GNN architecture, including the number of layers, input/output dimensions, and training details.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the results, as it is limited to a specific dataset and region, raising concerns about the method's applicability in other contexts.
- There is a lack of detailed comparative analysis with other existing methods, such as neural operators and other GNN models, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper suffers from inconsistencies in notation and lacks a comprehensive explanation of certain equations and terms used, which could confuse readers.
