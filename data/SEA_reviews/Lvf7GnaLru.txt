 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization capabilities of diversification methods, specifically focusing on the impact of unlabeled data distribution and the choice of learning algorithms. It reveals that diversification methods are sensitive to the distribution of unlabeled data and the choice of learning algorithms, and that the optimal choice of learning algorithm depends on the unlabeled data. The paper also explores the co-dependence between unlabeled data and learning algorithms, showing that the optimal choice for one depends on the other. It further demonstrates that diversification alone is insufficient for OOD generalization and that increasing the number of diverse hypotheses does not improve performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the key components contributing to the OOD generalization ability of diversification methods, including the distribution of unlabeled data, the choice of learning algorithms, and the co-dependence between unlabeled data and learning algorithms.
- The study is well-organized and clearly written, making it accessible to readers and facilitating understanding of the complex concepts presented.
- The paper presents several interesting insights and findings, such as the sensitivity of diversification methods to the distribution of unlabeled data and the choice of learning algorithms, and the co-dependence between unlabeled data and learning algorithms.
- The paper includes both theoretical and empirical analyses, which support the claims made and provide a robust foundation for the conclusions drawn.
- The findings of the study can guide practitioners in how to best use existing methods and can also guide researchers in developing new, better diversification methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on two specific diversification methods, DivDis and D-BAT, and does not explore other methods such as DivDis-Seq, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the study, such as the impact of the number of hypotheses on performance and the choice of datasets.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of why the diversification method is necessary, and the theoretical underpinnings of the method are not thoroughly explored.
- The paper's claims about the insufficiency of diversification alone for OOD generalization and the need for additional biases are not convincingly supported by the empirical evidence presented.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related works, particularly those that address similar issues in OOD generalization.

**Questions:**
- Can the findings of this study be generalized to other diversification methods beyond DivDis and D-BAT?
- How does the number of hypotheses impact the performance of the diversification method, and what are the implications of using a larger number of hypotheses?
- What are the specific advantages of the DivDis-Seq method over other diversification methods, and how does it compare in terms of OOD generalization ability?
- How does the paper address the potential limitations discussed, such as the choice of datasets and the theoretical underpinnings of the diversification method?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly in sections where the results are not clearly explained or where there are discrepancies between the text and the figures?
