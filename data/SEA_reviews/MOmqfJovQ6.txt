 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the challenge of reducing the complexity of reinforcement learning (RL) in high-dimensional action spaces by grouping similar actions together. This approach aims to balance the trade-off between approximation error and estimation error, offering a novel method for action space reduction. The paper proposes a model-based RL algorithm that utilizes a generative model to optimize the grouping strategy, balancing performance loss and computational complexity. Theoretical analysis and experimental results are provided to support the claims, although the practical applicability and the clarity of the experimental setup are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in reinforcement learning (RL) by proposing a method to reduce the complexity of action spaces, which is crucial for managing the curse of dimensionality.
- The method is well-explained, with a clear presentation of the linear decomposition model used for capturing the similarity of actions within the same group.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to understand the trade-off between approximation and estimation errors, which is crucial for optimizing the grouping strategy.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the authors have demonstrated the method's effectiveness through experiments.
- The paper introduces a novel method for action space reduction in RL, which could be beneficial for practical applications where computational resources are limited.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address how the grouping strategy is determined in practical scenarios, especially when the action space is large.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the experimental setup, particularly the choice of hyperparameters and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the practical implications of the method, such as how it can be applied in real-world scenarios with limited computational resources.
- The paper's theoretical contributions, particularly the proof of Theorem 1, are not sufficiently robust, with potential errors in the proof and the assumptions made.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the boundedness of rewards, may limit its applicability in more general RL scenarios.
- The paper's experimental results are not convincingly presented, with insufficient detail on the experimental setup and the choice of hyperparameters.

**Questions:**
- How is the grouping strategy determined in practical scenarios, especially when the action space is large?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of hyperparameters and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the action space is very large, and how does it compare to other methods that do not use grouping?
- Could you clarify the assumptions made in Theorem 1, particularly the boundedness of rewards and the implications of this assumption on the general applicability of the theorem?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the action space is not naturally grouped, and what are the implications of this for the performance of the RL algorithm?
- Could you provide more details on the computational complexity of the method, especially in relation to the number of groups used in the grouping strategy?
