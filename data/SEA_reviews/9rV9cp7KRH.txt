 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an incentivized collaborative learning framework, termed ICL, which aims to address the challenge of incentivizing entities to participate in collaborative learning scenarios. The framework is designed to maximize overall system profit while ensuring individual incentives are aligned. The authors propose a pricing plan that influences the selection of active participants, thereby optimizing the collaboration process. The paper presents theoretical insights and empirical evidence across various use cases, including federated learning, assisted learning, and multi-armed bandits. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in certain sections, the limited scope of experimental validation, and potential issues in the theoretical underpinnings of the incentive mechanisms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue of incentivizing collaborative learning, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The authors provide a well-structured framework and theoretical insights, which are supported by empirical results across different use cases.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The use of game theory to model the incentive mechanism in collaborative learning is a novel and interesting approach.
- The paper provides a comprehensive discussion on the related literature, which helps in contextualizing the research within the existing body of knowledge.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of key concepts such as the pricing plan and the selection plan.
- The experimental validation is limited to a small number of entities, which may not fully represent real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative social impacts of the proposed framework, such as the potential for misuse or exploitation.
- The theoretical assumptions and their implications are not thoroughly discussed, which could limit the generalizability and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the incentive mechanisms, which could affect the practical implementation and effectiveness of the proposed framework.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed framework, which could include issues related to scalability, fairness, and the complexity of the incentive mechanisms.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the pricing plan and selection plan are implemented in practice, particularly in terms of their computational complexity and practical feasibility?
- How does the proposed framework ensure that the incentives provided are fair and equitable among all participants, especially in scenarios where data or computational resources may be highly imbalanced?
- What are the specific assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, and how do these assumptions affect the applicability and generalizability of the findings?
- How does the proposed framework handle scenarios where the incentives may not be sufficient to motivate participation, such as in cases where the rewards are not significant enough?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the number and characteristics of the entities involved, and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the proposed framework ensure that the incentives do not lead to negative outcomes, such as the exploitation of the system by some entities at the expense of others?
