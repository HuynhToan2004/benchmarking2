 **Summary:**
The paper explores the relationship between spectral and spatial domains in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by introducing a novel Spatially Adaptive Filtering (SAF) framework. It establishes a theoretical connection between spectral filtering and spatial aggregation, revealing how spectral filtering can implicitly modify the original graph to enhance spatial aggregation capabilities. The framework leverages this connection to create a new graph that facilitates non-local aggregation and signed edge weights, addressing challenges such as long-range dependencies and graph heterophily. Extensive experiments across 13 node classification benchmarks demonstrate the framework's superiority over state-of-the-art models. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticisms regarding the novelty of the spectral-spatial GNN concept, the computational complexity of the proposed method, and the clarity of its presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The theoretical connection between spectral filtering and spatial aggregation is novel and provides a new perspective on the interpretation of spectral GNNs in the spatial domain.
- The proposed SAF framework is simple yet effective, and empirical results show that it outperforms state-of-the-art models on 13 node classification benchmarks.
- The paper addresses the significant challenge of capturing long-range dependencies in GNNs, which is crucial for accurately modeling real-world graph structures.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed framework in improving the performance of spectral GNNs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed spectral-spatial GNN concept is questionable as similar approaches have been previously explored.
- The computational complexity of the proposed method is high, particularly due to the spectral decomposition and non-local aggregation, which may limit its scalability to larger graphs.
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the presentation of figures and the explanation of certain mathematical formulations.
- The method's reliance on spectral decomposition and non-local aggregation may limit its applicability to large-scale graphs, as the computational cost increases significantly.
- The paper's theoretical contributions, such as Proposition 1, are not well-explained, and the implications of the non-local aggregation and signed edge weights are not thoroughly discussed.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the scalability and computational efficiency issues.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach in comparison to previous works on spectral-spatial GNNs?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational complexity, particularly in large-scale graph applications?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the mathematical formulations, especially in sections where clarity is lacking?
- How does the method handle the trade-off between expressivity and computational cost, particularly in terms of the non-negative constraint on graph filters?
- Can the authors discuss the implications of the non-local aggregation and signed edge weights in more detail, possibly with additional empirical evidence?
- How does the method perform on larger, more complex graphs, and what are the computational costs associated with this?
