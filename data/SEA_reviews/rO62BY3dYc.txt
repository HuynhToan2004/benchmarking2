 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Pruning via Ranking (PvR), a structured pruning method for neural networks that groups similar neurons/filters and ranks their importance based on misclassification probabilities. The method involves two modules: a grouping module and a ranking module, which are used to generate a dense sub-network that complies with a user-specified parameter budget. The sub-network is then re-initialized and trained from scratch, demonstrating improved accuracy and inference latency across various datasets and models. The authors claim that PvR outperforms other pruning methods by generating optimal sub-networks that are both shallow and dense. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method and the lack of comparisons with other recent structured pruning methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel neuron/filter global ranking method, PvR, which automatically generates a shallower, dense sub-network for a given dataset, pre-trained model, and parameter budget.
- The methodology is clearly described, and the experiments are well-executed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the results show that the proposed method outperforms other pruning methods across different datasets and models.
- The use of Geometric Complexity (GC) as a proxy for network performance is innovative and adds depth to the paper's methodology.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing model size and improving accuracy and inference latency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as similar approaches have been previously proposed, such as the use of misclassification probabilities for neuron/filter importance ranking.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other recent structured pruning methods, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its performance and effectiveness.
- The experiments are limited to small-scale datasets and models, which may not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness on larger, more complex datasets.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational overhead of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the size of the groups and the number of groups used in the experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how they plan to address these limitations in future work?
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent structured pruning methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational overhead of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the model and the dataset?
- What are the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments, and how do they affect the performance of the pruned models?
- How does the proposed method handle the pruning of different types of layers, such as convolutional and fully connected layers, and how does this affect the performance of the pruned models?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the size of the groups and the number of groups used in the experiments, to ensure the reproducibility and comparability of the results?
