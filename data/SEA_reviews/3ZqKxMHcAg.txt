 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel evaluation framework for assessing the agency of large language models (LLMs) through structured negotiation games. The framework, which includes both self-play and cross-play benchmarks, is designed to evaluate LLMs' capabilities in cooperative and competitive scenarios, measuring their performance in multi-issue negotiations. The authors demonstrate that current models struggle with complex negotiation scenarios, highlighting the need for further research and development in this area. The framework also addresses ethical considerations and is open-sourced to facilitate further research and collaboration.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel evaluation framework that assesses the agency of large language models (LLMs) through structured negotiation games, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The framework is comprehensive, covering both cooperative and competitive scenarios, and is designed to modulate task complexity, making it applicable to a variety of real-world negotiation problems.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed descriptions of the experimental setup, methodology, and results.
- The authors have made significant efforts to address ethical considerations and have released the code for their framework, which will be a valuable resource for the research community.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of various models, including open-source and proprietary models, which helps in understanding the current state of LLM capabilities in negotiation scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other LLMs, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the performance of the proposed framework.
- The framework's reliance on the assumption that LLMs have agent-like behavior is not convincingly justified, especially given the potential for hallucinations and other limitations of LLMs.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of data leakage, which is a significant concern in the evaluation of LLMs.
- The framework's effectiveness in evaluating LLMs in real-world scenarios is limited by the simplicity of the negotiation scenarios used, which may not accurately reflect the complexity of real-world negotiations.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the ethical implications of using LLMs in negotiations, particularly concerning the potential for misuse or unintended consequences.
- The framework's evaluation metrics and the interpretation of the results are not sufficiently detailed or explained, which could limit the understanding and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of their framework with other existing evaluation methods for LLMs?
- How does the framework address the issue of data leakage, especially in the context of evaluating LLMs against different datasets?
- Can the authors clarify the rationale behind the choice of negotiation scenarios and how they reflect the complexity and diversity of real-world negotiations?
- How do the authors justify the use of LLMs as agents in negotiations, especially given the potential for hallucinations and other limitations of LLMs?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies that demonstrate the practical application of the framework in real-world scenarios?
- How do the authors address the ethical concerns related to the use of LLMs in negotiations, particularly concerning the potential for misuse or unintended consequences?
