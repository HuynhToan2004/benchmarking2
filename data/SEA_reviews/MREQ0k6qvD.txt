 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for inferring neural interactions in the brain by integrating generalized linear models with hidden Markov models, specifically focusing on state-switching GLMs. The authors propose two variants: Gaussian HMM-GLM and one-hot HMM-GLM, which utilize different prior distributions to model neural interactions. The one-hot HMM-GLM is particularly notable for its ability to model sparse neural interactions, which is a significant advancement over existing methods. The paper presents results from both simulated and real neural datasets, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing models in terms of predictive likelihood and the interpretability of the learned interactions. However, the method's scalability and generalizability to more complex neural systems remain concerns.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the proposed method and its application, making it accessible to readers.
- The authors provide a detailed comparison with existing methods and demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed method through experiments on both simulated and real neural datasets.
- The methodology, particularly the use of a Gumbel-Softmax prior and the decomposition of the weight matrix into connection and strength components, is novel and innovative.
- The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation, including a comparison of the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods and a detailed analysis of the results.
- The method's ability to model sparse neural interactions and its potential to improve the interpretability of neural interactions is highlighted, making it a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the method, particularly concerning its applicability to more complex neural systems with larger numbers of neurons and states.
- The method's reliance on the assumption of a fixed number of states might limit its generalizability, especially in scenarios where the number of states is unknown or dynamic.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of model complexity and its impact on the results, particularly how the method performs when compared to simpler models like GLM.
- The method's assumptions, such as the independence of transition probabilities from previous observations and the fixed number of states, are not discussed in detail, which could limit the understanding of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors discuss the scalability of the proposed method and its potential limitations when applied to more complex neural systems?
- How does the method perform when the number of states is unknown or dynamic?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and its assumptions?
- How does the method compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability to other methods, especially in large-scale applications?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the method, particularly the assumption of independence of transition probabilities from previous observations?
- How does the method handle the issue of model complexity, and what are the implications of using a more complex model on the results?
