 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AGILE, a novel method for class-incremental learning, which leverages a shared task-attention module to mitigate task interference. This method employs learnable task projection vectors to transform latent representations, reducing the impact of previous tasks on current learning. AGILE is evaluated against several baselines, showing superior performance in both task-incremental and class-incremental settings. The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation, demonstrating AGILE's effectiveness in reducing forgetting and improving generalization performance. However, concerns are raised about the scalability of the method, particularly in scenarios with a large number of tasks, and the potential for overfitting due to the use of task projection vectors.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, AGILE, is novel and effective, demonstrating strong empirical performance across various datasets and experimental settings.
- AGILE is scalable, able to handle a large number of tasks without significant overhead, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The method's approach to reducing task interference and improving generalization performance is innovative and well-supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper includes a comprehensive ablation study that helps in understanding the contributions of different components of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are robust, showing that AGILE outperforms other methods in terms of forgetting and model calibration.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on related works, particularly in the area of class-incremental learning, which could have provided a richer context for the research.
- There is a concern about the scalability of AGILE, especially when the number of tasks increases significantly, as the method's performance may degrade.
- The use of task projection vectors might lead to overfitting, which could be a significant issue in scenarios with a large number of tasks.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational and memory costs associated with the use of task projection vectors.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could have provided valuable insights into its potential drawbacks and areas for future research.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these could be addressed in future research?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability with other state-of-the-art methods?
- What are the specific computational and memory costs associated with the use of task projection vectors in AGILE?
- How does the method perform when the number of tasks increases significantly? Are there any strategies to mitigate the potential for overfitting in such scenarios?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the buffer size and the number of tasks were chosen?
- How does the method handle scenarios where there is overlap between classes in different tasks?
- Is there a plan to release the code for AGILE to facilitate reproducibility and further research by the community?
