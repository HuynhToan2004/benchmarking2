 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to learning universal features from multiple graphs through self-supervision, which is a significant extension over existing methods that typically focus on learning features from a single graph. The proposed method, named U-SSL, leverages multiple graphs of the same family to enhance the quality of learnt representations, aiming to improve performance on downstream node classification tasks. The methodology involves a graph-specific module for feature homogenization followed by a universal representation learning module for generic feature extraction. The approach is evaluated on six benchmark citation networks, demonstrating improvements in efficiency and scalability compared to traditional SSL methods. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as it primarily builds on existing methods and lacks comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem by focusing on learning universal features from multiple graphs, which is a significant advancement over traditional methods that learn features from a single graph.
- The proposed method is technically sound, with a clear presentation of the methodology and a detailed explanation of the components involved.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and includes a comprehensive experimental setup that demonstrates the superiority of the proposed method over traditional SSL methods.
- The approach is scalable and adaptable, allowing for the integration of new datasets without requiring extensive retraining, which is a significant advantage in practical applications.
- The paper includes a detailed ablation study that provides insights into the impact of different components of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it primarily builds on existing methods without introducing significant new techniques or insights.
- The methodology section is unclear, particularly in explaining how the graph-specific module operates and its impact on the overall performance.
- The paper's experimental setup is limited, with only a few benchmark datasets used, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method across different types of graphs.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with state-of-the-art methods, which is crucial for establishing the superiority of the proposed method.
- The paper's claims regarding the superiority of the proposed method over traditional SSL methods are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which show only marginal improvements.
- The paper's focus on node classification as the sole downstream task limits its applicability to other tasks such as link prediction and graph classification, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the graph-specific module operates and how it impacts the overall performance of the method?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to heterogeneous graphs, which are more complex than the homogeneous graphs used in the current study?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of their method with state-of-the-art methods to better establish its superiority?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of graphs, such as molecular graphs or social networks, which have different characteristics from the citation graphs used in the study?
- Could the authors provide more details on the graph-specific encoders used in the method, particularly how they are chosen and how they impact the performance of the method?
- How does the proposed method handle the heterogeneity of node features across different graphs, and what strategies are used to ensure consistent performance across different datasets?
