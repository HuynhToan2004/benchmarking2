 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the evaluation of few-shot learning (FSL) models, focusing on the accuracy of performance estimators and the effectiveness of model selection strategies. It critiques the current methods of model evaluation and proposes new strategies, particularly emphasizing the use of 5-fold cross-validation for estimating performance and bootstrapping for model selection. The study is based on empirical experiments across various benchmarks, including miniImageNet, CIFAR-FS, Meta-Album, and CLIP Few-Shot, using a range of FSL algorithms. The paper highlights the limitations of existing evaluation methods and proposes improvements, though it faces criticism for its limited scope and lack of theoretical depth.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a critical issue in the field of Few-Shot Learning (FSL) by investigating the limitations of current evaluation methods and proposing new strategies for performance estimation and model selection.
- The research is well-structured, with clear explanations and visualizations that effectively illustrate the findings.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive evaluation of various FSL algorithms across different benchmarks.
- The authors propose a novel method using 5-fold cross-validation for performance estimation and bootstrapping for model selection, which could potentially improve the reliability and accuracy of FSL model evaluations.
- The paper is significant as it tackles a crucial issue in the FSL community, which has been largely overlooked, and provides valuable insights into the challenges of evaluating FSL models effectively.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive theoretical analysis, which could have strengthened the arguments and provided a deeper understanding of the proposed methods.
- The scope of the research is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on image classification benchmarks and not including a broader range of tasks such as text classification or regression.
- The paper's findings may not be directly applicable to more recent or advanced FSL methods, which might employ different evaluation strategies.
- The evaluation metrics used, such as Mean Absolute Error (MAE), are not well-justified, and the paper could benefit from a more rigorous theoretical grounding and a broader set of experimental validations.
- There is a noticeable lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis to support the proposed methods, particularly in terms of how they address the challenges of evaluating FSL models?
- How do the proposed methods perform when applied to more recent or advanced FSL methods that might employ different evaluation strategies?
- Could the authors expand the scope of their research to include a broader range of tasks beyond image classification, such as text classification or regression?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed methods over existing ones, and how do they address the identified limitations in current evaluation strategies?
- How do the findings of this study relate to the broader literature on model evaluation in FSL, and what are the implications for future research in this area?
