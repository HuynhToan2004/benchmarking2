 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Vec-Tok Speech, a novel speech codec that integrates speech vectorization and semantic tokenization for various speech generation tasks, including text-to-speech, voice conversion, and speech-to-speech translation. The codec uses a pre-trained WavLM model to extract speech vectors and employs a k-means clustering method to generate semantic tokens. These components are then used to train a large language model (LLM) and a vocoder, enabling the system to handle different speech generation tasks. The paper also explores the application of Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) to reduce token length and bit rate, aiming to improve the performance of the LLM. The results show that Vec-Tok Speech outperforms other state-of-the-art models in terms of naturalness, speaker similarity, and other metrics, although it has some limitations in tasks like speech denoising and speech-to-speech translation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experiments and ablations, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The idea of using semantic tokens for LLMs is innovative and helps in the zero-shot style transfer of speaking styles and speaker identities.
- The use of Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) to reduce token length and bit rate is a significant contribution, which benefits the Large Language Models (LLMs) by reducing exposure bias and improving context coverage.
- The proposed framework is versatile and can be applied to various speech generation tasks, including TTS and S2ST, and demonstrates good performance.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear methodology and comprehensive experimental results, including both subjective and objective evaluations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed discussions on the motivation and effectiveness of using semantic tokens, particularly how they differ from other approaches like WavLM.
- The selection of semantic tokens using k-means clustering might not be the most effective method, and alternative approaches like PCA or VQ-VAE could be considered.
- The paper does not adequately address the challenges associated with training the inverse K-means model, which is crucial for the effectiveness of the semantic tokens.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art models in some tasks, such as speech-to-speech translation, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the model's performance.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and experimental results, especially in the appendix, to better understand the methodology and its implications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed discussions on the motivation and effectiveness of using semantic tokens compared to other approaches like WavLM?
- How does the paper address the potential issues with the selection of semantic tokens using k-means clustering, and are there plans to explore alternative methods?
- Could the authors clarify the training process for the inverse K-means model and discuss any challenges or insights from this process?
- How does the paper ensure the effectiveness of the semantic tokens in tasks like speech-to-speech translation, especially given the limited context coverage due to exposure bias?
- Can the authors provide more details on the methodology and results of the speech-to-speech translation task, possibly including additional experiments or results in the appendix?
