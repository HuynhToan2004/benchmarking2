 **Summary:**
The paper introduces OmniProt, a novel generative modeling technique that integrates protein sequence and three-dimensional structure using a diffusion model within a geometrically structured latent space. This approach allows for conditional protein generation, addressing tasks like protein-protein docking and inverse folding. The model is designed to be invariant to rotational transformations, ensuring the generation of sequences and structures that are independent of these transformations. The method's performance is evaluated against existing models, showing competitive results in inverse folding and protein-protein docking tasks. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces OmniProt, a novel generative modeling technique that integrates protein sequence and three-dimensional structure using a diffusion model within a geometrically structured latent space.
- The proposed method is invariant to rotational transformations, ensuring the generation of sequences and structures that are independent of these transformations.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The method is versatile and can be applied to various tasks in protein design, including protein-protein docking and inverse folding, showcasing its utility and adaptability.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate that OmniProt can outperform competitors in protein docking and is competitive with state-of-the-art for protein inverse folding.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- The novelty of the work is questioned, as the use of diffusion models in protein generation and the concept of roto-translational invariance have been previously explored.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of OmniProt's performance relative to existing techniques.
- The method's reliance on a pre-trained AlphaFold2 model for structure reconstruction raises concerns about the fairness of comparisons with other methods that do not use such models.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of the proposed technology, which is an important consideration for the ethical implications of AI applications in biology.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and impact of the AlphaFold2 model in the performance of OmniProt, particularly in terms of structure reconstruction?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance metrics and computational efficiency?
- What are the specific limitations of the proposed method, and how do these affect its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios?
- Could you provide more details on the training and testing data used in the experiments, including the size and diversity of the datasets?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting, especially given the large amount of training data used?
- Can you discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed technology, particularly in the context of protein design and biotechnology?
