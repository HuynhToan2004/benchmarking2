 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the distributed experts problem, where a central coordinator interacts with multiple servers, each containing different experts. The goal is to minimize communication costs while achieving near-optimal regret. The paper introduces several algorithms, including DEWA-S, DEWA-S-P, DEWA-M, and DEWA-M-P, which are analyzed for their communication efficiency and regret performance. The algorithms are evaluated in both message-passing and broadcast models, demonstrating significant improvements in communication efficiency over existing methods like EWA and Exp3. The paper also provides theoretical analysis and empirical results to support its claims, showing that the proposed methods outperform or match the performance of traditional algorithms while reducing communication costs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the distributed setting, where communication costs are crucial.
- The proposed algorithms are novel and innovative, particularly the use of unbiased estimators and meta-experts to improve performance.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- Theoretical analysis is robust, providing both upper and lower bounds, which are supported by empirical results showing the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper is well-positioned within the literature, clearly distinguishing its contributions from existing works.
- The communication efficiency of the proposed algorithms is demonstrated through experiments, showing significant improvements over traditional methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with the most recent and relevant works in the field, such as those by Srinivas et al. (2022) and Peng and Zhang (2022), which also address the experts problem in the data stream model.
- The experimental setup and results could be more comprehensive, including comparisons with a broader range of algorithms and different communication models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and potential future directions for research.
- The assumption of a constant probability of success in the distributed setting might not be realistic or practical in all scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of its work.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the choice of using the maximum aggregation function in your model and how it compares to other functions like the sum or average?
- In the experimental setup, how were the hyperparameters for the algorithms selected, and what impact do these choices have on the results?
- Can you explain the intuition behind the use of unbiased estimators in your algorithms and how they contribute to the overall performance?
- How does the paper handle the assumption of a constant probability of success in the distributed setting, and what are the implications of this assumption in practical scenarios?
- Could you provide more details on the limitations of your proposed methods and potential future directions for research in this area?
