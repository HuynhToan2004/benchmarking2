 **Summary:**
The paper introduces RECOMP, a method for compressing retrieved documents into summaries to improve the efficiency and performance of retrieval-augmented language models (RALMs). The authors propose two types of compressors: an extractive compressor that selects relevant sentences and an abstractive compressor that generates summaries from multiple documents. The method aims to reduce computational costs and enhance the utility of retrieved documents by providing a concise summary. The approach is evaluated on various datasets including language modeling and open-domain question answering, showing that the method can achieve significant compression rates with minimal performance loss. However, the paper is criticized for its incremental nature, lack of novelty, and limited generalizability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the proposed methods and experiments.
- The proposed method, RECOMP, is simple, effective, and efficient, with a focus on reducing computational costs and improving the efficiency of retrieval-augmented language models (RALMs).
- The method is evaluated on multiple datasets and shows promising results, outperforming baselines and achieving significant compression rates with minimal performance loss.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the method's performance, including detailed experimental results and a discussion on the limitations and potential improvements.
- The methodology is well-motivated and addresses an important problem in the field, particularly relevant in the era of large language models (LLMs).

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it primarily combines existing techniques such as extractive and abstractive summarization, and the training schemes for the compressors are not significantly innovative.
- The method's generalizability is limited as it heavily relies on the quality of the retrieved documents and the similarity of the input query to the document content.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of over-compression by the abstractive compressor, which could potentially reduce the effectiveness of the method.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a few datasets, and the results on some datasets, such as HotpotQA, are less impressive compared to others.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with more advanced summarization models or discuss the potential impact of the method on the efficiency of LLMs.
- The method's performance is not consistently superior to existing methods like T5 and GPT-3.5 across all datasets, and there are concerns about the method's ability to handle complex tasks such as HotpotQA.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and existing methods, particularly in terms of the training schemes and the use of summarization models?
- How does the method perform when the retrieved documents are not highly relevant to the input query?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training data used for the abstractive compressor and the methodology for selecting the top 5 documents for retrieval?
- How does the method handle the issue of over-compression by the abstractive compressor, and what steps are taken to ensure the generated summaries are faithful and comprehensive?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of the method on the efficiency of LLMs and provide more detailed comparisons with other summarization models?
- How does the method perform on larger datasets or more complex tasks, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in scaling up the method?
