 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL) algorithm called STAR, which integrates both spatial and temporal abstractions to address the limitations of existing methods in complex environments. STAR is designed to learn online subgoal representations and policies, leveraging a three-level hierarchy: Navigator, Manager, and Controller. The Navigator selects abstract goals, the Manager selects concrete subgoals, and the Controller learns to take actions based on these subgoals. The algorithm is evaluated on complex continuous control tasks and demonstrates superior performance compared to existing methods. The paper also provides theoretical guarantees on the suboptimality of the learned policy and presents a refinement mechanism for spatial abstractions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL) algorithm, STAR, which combines spatial and temporal abstractions, addressing the limitations of existing methods in complex environments.
- The algorithm is evaluated on complex continuous control tasks, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods, and includes theoretical guarantees on the suboptimality of the learned policy.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the proposed method and a logical structure that makes it accessible to readers.
- The introduction of a three-layer HRL algorithm that introduces both spatial and temporal goal abstractions is a significant contribution, providing a theoretical study of the regret bounds of the learned policies.
- The experimental results show that the algorithm is effective in complex continuous control tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of spatial and temporal abstractions learned by the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the differences between the proposed STAR and GARA algorithms, making it difficult for readers to understand the novel contributions of STAR.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed approach, including potential failure modes and scenarios where the algorithm may not perform optimally.
- The experimental setup is limited to a few environments, which may not fully demonstrate the scalability or generalizability of the algorithm.
- The paper could provide more clarity on the training process, particularly how the refinement mechanism for spatial abstractions is trained and how the neural network is trained.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly in relation to the deterministic environment and bounded reward signals.

**Questions:**
- How does STAR differ from GARA, and what are the specific contributions of STAR over GARA?
- Can you provide more details on the failure modes of the proposed algorithm and the scenarios where it may not perform optimally?
- How does the algorithm perform in environments where the state space is not bounded or the reward signal is not bounded?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed method scale with the size of the state and action spaces, and what are the practical implications of this complexity?
- How are the neural networks trained, and what is the impact of the choice of parameters such as the number of k-step reachability relations on the performance of the algorithm?
- Can you provide more details on the training process, particularly how the refinement mechanism for spatial abstractions is trained and how the neural network is trained?
