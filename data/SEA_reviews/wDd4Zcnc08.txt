 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HP3-NS, a graph neural network (GNN) model designed to predict the bandgap of hybrid perovskite materials. It employs a nested graph representation to encode both organic and inorganic components, distinguishing between them effectively. The model's architecture includes a nested GNN for molecular features and a GNN for overall graph representations, which are integrated to predict target material properties. The model's performance is evaluated against various baselines and shows superiority in predicting bandgaps. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its reliance on existing techniques and its limited application to only bandgap prediction.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear problem statement and a well-motivated methodology.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on the task of predicting the bandgap of perovskite materials.
- The methodology is well-explained, and the paper provides a good ablation study, highlighting the effectiveness of each component of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup is robust, and the results are convincing, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper introduces a novel nested graph representation for hybrid perovskite materials, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it primarily combines existing techniques such as nested GNNs and intra-group ratios for edge design without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art models, particularly those that use more advanced techniques like GIN, GAT, or GraphTransformer.
- The application of the proposed method is limited to predicting only the bandgap of perovskite materials, which may not generalize well to other types of materials or properties.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of scalability and generalization to other types of materials.
- The dataset used is small, which could limit the reliability and generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be applied to other types of materials or properties beyond bandgap prediction?
- How does the proposed method perform on other types of materials or properties, and can it generalize well to new, unseen materials?
- Could the authors provide more details on the data used, such as its size and the number of unique molecules and atoms it contains?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art models that use more advanced techniques like GIN, GAT, or GraphTransformer?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its ability to handle larger and more complex datasets?
