 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a locally adaptive federated learning algorithm, FedSPS, which allows each client to adjust its step size based on local geometric information. This algorithm is designed to converge faster than traditional federated learning methods like FedAvg, particularly in interpolated settings. The authors prove the convergence of FedSPS under convex and strongly convex settings and provide empirical evidence of its effectiveness through experiments. However, the paper's theoretical analysis is limited to the interpolated setting, and the experimental results are not convincing in terms of statistical significance. Additionally, the paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not adequately compare its performance against other adaptive federated learning methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to federated learning by allowing clients to adapt their step sizes independently, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including the proof of convergence in convex and strongly convex settings, and empirical evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed algorithm is simple, efficient, and requires minimal tuning, making it practical for real-world applications.
- The experiments conducted show that the algorithm is robust to hyperparameter choices, which is a critical aspect for practical deployment.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to the interpolated setting, which may not be generalizable to other settings.
- The experimental results are not convincing due to the lack of statistical significance in the comparisons with other methods like FedAvg and FedAMS.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other adaptive federated learning methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of its effectiveness.
- The experimental settings are not well-described, and the results are not convincingly presented, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the parameter c in the convergence analysis, particularly in relation to the interpolation setting?
- How does the proposed method compare with other adaptive federated learning methods like FedAMS and FedADAM, especially in terms of performance and hyperparameter tuning?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental settings, such as the data splits and client sampling strategies, to enhance the reproducibility and transparency of the results?
- How does the proposed method perform in non-convex settings, and what are the implications of using a large value of c in practice?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and suggest areas for future research to address these limitations?
