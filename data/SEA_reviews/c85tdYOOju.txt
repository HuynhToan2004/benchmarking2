 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework named Weigh and Select (WAS) for integrating multiple graph pre-training tasks. This framework addresses the challenges of selecting and weighing tasks, which are critical for enhancing graph representation learning. It proposes a method using decoupled Siamese networks to adaptively learn task combinations and weights for each instance, thereby improving performance over existing methods. The approach is evaluated on 16 datasets, demonstrating comparable performance to other leading counterparts. The paper is well-structured and presents clear experiments, although it lacks in novelty and significant advancement over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides clear explanations of the proposed method and its implementation.
- The motivation behind the study is clear, focusing on the significance of selecting and weighing tasks in graph representation learning.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering a variety of datasets and tasks, which demonstrates the robustness of the proposed method.
- The idea of using a Siamese network to learn instance-wise task selection and importance is novel and innovative.
- The paper presents a clear and detailed experimental setup, including the selection of datasets and the implementation details of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation from existing works, particularly in the area of task selection and importance weighing, which have been previously explored.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant advancement.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, especially in large-scale settings.
- The performance improvements over existing methods are not substantial, and the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms simpler baselines.
- The paper does not include a detailed analysis of the computational complexity or the time taken for training and inference, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its potential negative societal impacts.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between their proposed method and existing works, particularly in terms of the novelty and effectiveness of the approach?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, especially in large-scale settings?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and training time of the proposed method compared to existing methods?
- In light of the concerns regarding the novelty and effectiveness of the method, how does the proposed method significantly advance the state-of-the-art in graph representation learning?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other recent methods, such as those mentioned in the review, to establish the superiority of their approach?
