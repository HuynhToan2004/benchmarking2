 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph transformer architecture, DiGT, designed to handle directed graphs by incorporating edge directionality. DiGT utilizes dual node encodings and directional attention mechanisms to capture the directional information of edges, which is a significant improvement over existing graph transformers that typically ignore edge direction. The model is evaluated on various directed graph datasets, showing state-of-the-art results. The paper also introduces new datasets to validate the importance of edge direction in directed graph learning. Despite its innovative approach, concerns are raised about the novelty of the directional attention mechanism and the clarity of the paper's presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel graph transformer architecture, DiGT, which effectively handles directed graphs by incorporating edge directionality, a significant improvement over existing graph transformers that typically ignore this information.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the model and its components, including the dual node encodings and directional attention mechanisms.
- The proposed directional graph transformer architecture is innovative and shows promising results, demonstrating its effectiveness in various directed graph datasets.
- The paper introduces new directed graph datasets, which are crucial for evaluating the performance of the proposed model and contribute to the advancement of research in this area.
- The model's performance is validated through experiments, showing significant improvements over existing graph transformers and graph neural networks, highlighting the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing graph transformers, particularly in terms of the use of directional attention mechanisms. There is a need for a more detailed comparison and discussion on how the proposed method differs from existing approaches.
- The paper could benefit from a clearer presentation, particularly in the introduction and the methodology sections, to make it more accessible to readers unfamiliar with the field.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed model, which is a significant limitation given the quadratic complexity of the attention mechanism.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed model, which could include the computational cost and the scalability issues associated with the quadratic complexity of the attention mechanism.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation, including a broader range of datasets and benchmarks to validate the effectiveness of the proposed model across different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing graph transformers, particularly in terms of the use of directional attention mechanisms and how the proposed method differs from existing approaches?
- How does the proposed model handle the scalability issues associated with the quadratic complexity of the attention mechanism? Are there any plans to address this in future work?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed model and how these might affect its practical application?
- How does the model handle the computational cost associated with the quadratic complexity of the attention mechanism? Are there any plans to optimize the computational efficiency of the model?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the model's components, particularly the dual node encodings and directional attention mechanisms, to help readers better understand the model's inner workings?
- How does the model handle the issue of inductive bias, particularly in terms of learning latent edges that could positively contribute to the learning problem?
- Could the authors clarify the role and significance of the dual node encodings in the model, particularly in relation to the directionality of edges?
