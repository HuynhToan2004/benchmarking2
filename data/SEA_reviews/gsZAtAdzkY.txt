 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the ARB (Advanced Reasoning Benchmark), a new dataset designed to evaluate the reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) across various domains such as mathematics, physics, biology, chemistry, and law. The dataset is composed of advanced reasoning problems and includes a rubric-based evaluation method that allows LLMs to grade their own reasoning. The paper evaluates several LLMs including GPT-4 and Claude, demonstrating that current models perform poorly on the more challenging tasks. The dataset, while comprehensive, lacks in novelty and the methodology of evaluating LLMs using rubrics is not convincingly superior to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and concise structure that effectively communicates the main ideas and findings.
- The introduction of the ARB benchmark provides a significant contribution to the field by introducing a new dataset that addresses the limitations of existing benchmarks.
- The paper evaluates the performance of LLMs on advanced reasoning problems in various domains, highlighting the need for more challenging benchmarks to evaluate the reasoning capabilities of LLMs.
- The use of rubrics for evaluating the reasoning capabilities of LLMs is a novel approach, which could potentially lead to more reliable and cost-effective testing of complex model outputs.
- The dataset is comprehensive and covers a broad range of domains, including mathematics, physics, biology, chemistry, and law, which is beneficial for evaluating the generalizability of LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The dataset is primarily composed of problems from existing datasets, which may not be sufficiently novel or challenging to effectively evaluate the reasoning capabilities of LLMs.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the methodology of evaluating LLMs using rubrics, particularly the reliability and accuracy of these evaluations compared to human evaluations.
- There is a lack of novelty in the approach, as similar ideas have been explored in previous works, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from these existing works.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the performance of LLMs on the different types of problems in the dataset, which could help in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of different models.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the potential limitations of the dataset, such as data contamination, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the rubric-based evaluation method was developed, which could affect the credibility of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the dataset ensure that the problems are not already present in the training data of the LLMs, and what measures are in place to prevent data contamination?
- Can you provide more details on how the rubric-based evaluation method was developed, particularly how the rubrics were generated and validated?
- How do the authors ensure the reliability and accuracy of the rubric-based evaluations, especially in cases where the LLMs may not be able to accurately assess their own reasoning?
- Could you provide more details on the methodology used to evaluate the performance of LLMs on the different types of problems in the dataset, such as the criteria used for manual grading and the accuracy of automated evaluations?
- How do the authors plan to address the limitations of the dataset, such as the potential for data contamination and the need for more challenging problems?
- What steps are being taken to ensure the generalizability of the findings across different LLMs and domains, and how do the authors plan to update the dataset to reflect the latest advancements in LLM technology?
