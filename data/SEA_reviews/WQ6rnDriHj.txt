 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the General Action Discretization Model (GADM), a novel framework aimed at unifying diverse action spaces in reinforcement learning (RL). GADM learns unified and compact discrete latent actions, which are adaptively discretized from raw actions to construct unified and compact latent action spaces. This framework can adapt to various action spaces, including continuous, hybrid, or constrained actions, and can be applied to both online and offline RL training. GADM predicts confidence scores of different latent actions to mitigate instability in online RL settings and serves as an implicit constraint in offline RL cases. The paper presents several experiments demonstrating that GADM can match or outperform various approaches specifically designed for different environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear and intuitive explanation of the proposed framework.
- The framework is versatile and can be applied to both online and offline RL training, making it adaptable to different settings.
- The methodology is sound, and the experiments are thorough, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed framework in various RL environments.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning latent action representations, which can be applied to a wide range of RL scenarios, including offline RL, online RL, and safe RL.
- The framework is original and innovative, with a focus on reducing action redundancy and accommodating both offline and online training regimes.
- The paper includes a diversity-aware codetable, a state-dependent action encoder and decoder, and a latent action confidence predictor, which are crucial components of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing action discretization methods, which limits the evaluation of the proposed framework's effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of action redundancy, particularly in scenarios where actions are not semantically similar.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the latent action confidence predictor and its role in the framework.
- The paper does not provide sufficient visualization results to support the effectiveness of the framework, which could enhance the understanding of its performance.
- The paper's evaluation primarily focuses on simpler tasks, and there is a lack of testing on more complex environments like Atari games, which could demonstrate the framework's scalability and robustness.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed framework, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework and the potential negative societal impacts of its application.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing action discretization methods to better evaluate the proposed framework's effectiveness?
- How does the proposed framework address the issue of action redundancy, particularly in scenarios where actions are not semantically similar?
- Can the authors provide more visualization results to support the effectiveness of the framework in different environments?
- How does the proposed framework perform in more complex environments, such as Atari games, and what are the implications of its application in such settings?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the latent action confidence predictor and its role in the framework?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed framework compare to existing methods, and what are the implications of its application in terms of computational resources?
