 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training strategy named Smooth Training (SMOT) to address the overconfidence problem in out-of-distribution (OOD) detection. This strategy involves perturbing input images by masking label-related regions and applying label smoothing to the perturbed images. The authors argue that this approach, by reducing the confidence in ID samples, enhances the model's ability to distinguish between OOD and ID samples. The paper presents extensive experimental results across various datasets, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet-200, demonstrating the effectiveness of SMOT in OOD detection tasks. The methodology is supported by theoretical analysis and ablation studies, which help in understanding the impact of different parameters on the performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to out-of-distribution (OOD) detection, specifically using a masking strategy that is both innovative and effective in reducing overconfidence in neural networks.
- The methodology is supported by thorough theoretical analysis and extensive experimental results, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach across various datasets.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed SMOT method is simple and effective, showing significant improvements in OOD detection performance across different network architectures.
- The paper provides a clear motivation for the proposed method and conducts extensive experiments to demonstrate its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The method's reliance on masking and label smoothing may not be entirely novel, as similar strategies have been explored in previous works.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in terms of additional computational requirements during training.
- The paper's claims about the novelty of the method are not convincingly supported, as similar strategies have been previously used in the literature.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with some results showing significant variability across different runs, which could undermine the reliability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods to better establish the relative effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to more complex datasets, such as those with larger image sizes or more diverse classes?
- What are the computational costs associated with the proposed method, and how do these compare to other methods in terms of training and inference times?
- How sensitive is the method to the choice of hyperparameters, and what are the recommended values for different datasets and scenarios?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of false positive rate (FPR) and area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC) when applied to different datasets?
- Could the authors provide more details on the methodology, particularly the perturbation function and the sampling function, to better understand their impact on the method's performance?
