 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FiLM, a novel bidirectional language model designed for flexible sequence generation in any desired order, which extends the masked language modeling objective by adopting varying mask probabilities sampled from the Beta distribution. FiLM is capable of generating text from any position within a sequence, which is a significant departure from traditional left-to-right language models. The model is evaluated using various datasets, including WikiText-103, One Billion Word, and ROCStories, and shows competitive performance against causal language models. The authors also propose a new method for evaluating perplexity, which is crucial for assessing the model's performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel bidirectional language model that allows for flexible sequence generation at any position, which is a significant departure from traditional left-to-right language models.
- The use of a beta distribution for masking tokens is innovative and effectively enhances the model's training.
- The proposed method for evaluating perplexity is well-thought-out and provides a clearer comparison between FiLM and CLM.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with comprehensive and insightful experimental analysis.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments, including perplexity analysis, text infilling, and story completion, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other existing models, such as XLNet, GLM, and BLM, which also employ bidirectional context in their training.
- The evaluation of the model's performance is limited to specific datasets, and there is a need for more diverse datasets to assess the model's generalizability.
- The paper does not discuss the computational efficiency of the model, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the model, especially in terms of its ability to handle larger datasets or more complex tasks.
- The novelty of the proposed model is questioned as it seems to be a simple extension of existing models like XLNet, with similar training objectives and decoding methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the model, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- How does the model perform when scaled up to larger sizes, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in scaling?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the model, especially in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the model handle different types of text, such as poetry or highly structured text, which might not benefit from a flexible generation order?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, such as the specifics of the beta distribution used and the training schedule?
- How does the model compare to other bidirectional language models, especially in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Is there a possibility to include human evaluations for the story completion task to provide more insight into the model's effectiveness?
