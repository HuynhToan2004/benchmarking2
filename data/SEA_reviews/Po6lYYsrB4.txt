 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an embodied learning framework named Action-Aware Embodied Learning for Perception (ALP), which integrates action information into representation learning through a combination of reinforcement learning and inverse dynamics prediction. The framework is designed to learn task-agnostic visual representations and collect labeled data for downstream tasks. The authors evaluate ALP across various perception tasks, including object detection, instance segmentation, and depth estimation, demonstrating its effectiveness compared to existing baselines. The methodology involves a two-stage process: first, an exploration policy is learned, followed by the collection of labeled data for fine-tuning. The paper also includes ablation studies to analyze the contributions of different components of the framework.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by combining reinforcement learning and inverse dynamics prediction for learning task-agnostic visual representations, which is a significant advancement in the field of embodied learning.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the training process and the role of different components such as the exploration policy and the inverse dynamics model.
- The paper provides extensive experimental results, including comparisons with existing baselines and ablation studies that help in understanding the contributions of different components.
- The use of action information to improve representation learning is a promising direction, and the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach through various experiments and evaluations.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the reinforcement learning algorithm used, particularly the role of the exploration policy and the inverse dynamics model in the training process.
- The choice of environments and tasks for evaluation is limited, which might not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential challenges in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity or the scalability of the method, which could be critical factors for practical deployment.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that use similar approaches but differ in their implementation or evaluation.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role of the exploration policy and the inverse dynamics model in the training process, particularly how they are integrated and how they influence each other?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex or varied environments, and what are the implications for its scalability and generalizability?
- Could you provide more details on the computational complexity and the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of its applicability to larger or more complex datasets?
- How does the method handle the domain gap between simulated and real-world environments, and what steps have been taken to ensure its effectiveness in real-world scenarios?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of environments and tasks for evaluation, and how these choices reflect the broader applicability of the proposed method?
