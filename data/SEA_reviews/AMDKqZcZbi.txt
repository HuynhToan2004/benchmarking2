 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel biologically-inspired neural network architecture for continual learning, specifically designed to address the Morris Water Maze task. The model integrates the MESH architecture with a spatially invariant convolutional neural network and an attention mechanism to facilitate rapid learning and knowledge retention across multiple environments. The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments demonstrating the model's superior performance compared to standard neural network baselines. Despite its innovative approach and the use of a novel task, the paper is critiqued for its limited scope in terms of the number of environments and the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel task, the Sequential Morris Water Maze (sWM), which requires rapid learning and knowledge retention across multiple environments, providing a fresh perspective on continual learning.
- The model is biologically plausible, integrating the MESH architecture with a spatially invariant convolutional neural network and an attention mechanism, which is a significant departure from traditional neural network architectures.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the model and its components, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The experiments are comprehensive, demonstrating the model's ability to outperform standard neural network baselines in terms of rapid learning and knowledge retention.
- The paper provides a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential directions for future research, highlighting the model's potential contributions to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is somewhat limited, focusing on a single task (the Morris Water Maze) and a relatively small number of environments (only five), which may not adequately demonstrate the model's generalizability or robustness across a broader range of tasks.
- There is a lack of clarity in the experimental setup, particularly in how the model is trained and tested across different environments, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its model against other state-of-the-art continual learning models, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the model's performance.
- The paper's claims about the model's ability to prevent catastrophic forgetting are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which show significant performance drops in certain environments.
- There are several technical issues and unclear descriptions in the paper, including the definition and role of certain components (e.g., MESH and the attention mechanism) and the training and testing procedures, which could hinder understanding and replication of the model.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and training procedure of the attention mechanism in the model? Specifically, how does it function as a key-value memory and how is it trained?
- How does the model handle changes in the goal location within the same environment? Does it require re-learning associations between observations and actions?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the model is trained and tested across different environments, to enhance reproducibility and comparability?
- How does the model perform when trained and tested on a larger number of environments, and what are the implications for its generalizability and scalability?
- Could the authors compare their model against other state-of-the-art continual learning models to better establish its relative performance and contributions to the field?
