 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel backdoor attack scenario, termed "no-label backdoor" (NLB), where the attacker can only access unlabeled data for poisoning. This is a departure from traditional settings where labels are required. The authors propose two methods: a clustering-based NLB and a contrastive NLB, both designed to select poison samples effectively. The experiments are conducted on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet-100 datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of these methods in degrading model performance. Despite the innovative approach, concerns are raised about the practicality of the attack scenario, the clarity of the threat model, and the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel setting of backdoor attack, specifically focusing on the "no-label backdoor" (NLB) scenario, which is a significant departure from traditional attack methods.
- The authors propose two effective strategies for selecting poison sets from unlabeled data, enhancing the practicality and applicability of the proposed methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a technical paper.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, covering various SSL methods and datasets, which helps in validating the proposed methods.
- The paper provides a theoretical foundation for the proposed methods, which is a strong point for its scientific rigor.

**Weaknesses:**
- The practicality of the attack scenario is questionable, as it assumes that the attacker has access to the entire unlabeled dataset, which is unrealistic in many real-world applications.
- The threat model and the attacker's knowledge and capacity are not clearly defined, which could lead to confusion about the feasibility and effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The experiments are limited to small-scale datasets like CIFAR-10 and ImageNet-100, which may not adequately represent the performance on larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and potential countermeasures, which could enhance the robustness of the findings.
- The paper does not include a comparison with other backdoor attack methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The paper's claims about the effectiveness of the proposed methods are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, particularly in scenarios where the results are not statistically significant.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the practicality of the attack scenario and the threat model, especially regarding the assumption of the attacker having access to the entire unlabeled dataset?
- How do the proposed methods perform on larger, more complex datasets, such as ImageNet-1000 or ImageNet-1k?
- Could the authors provide more details on the trigger type used in the experiments and how it affects the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare with other backdoor attack methods, particularly in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed methods and discuss potential countermeasures or improvements?
- In the context of the experiments, could the authors provide more detailed results, such as standard deviations and statistical significance tests, to strengthen the claims made about the effectiveness of the proposed methods?
