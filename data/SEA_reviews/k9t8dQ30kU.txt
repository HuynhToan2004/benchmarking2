 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of activation functions on the learned representational geometry in neural networks, focusing on binary classification tasks. It explores how different nonlinearities like Tanh and ReLU affect the alignment of representations with input and target geometries. The study employs a variety of metrics and experiments, including binary classification tasks, to demonstrate that Tanh networks tend to learn representations that reflect the structure of target outputs, while ReLU networks retain more information about the structure of raw inputs. The paper also discusses how these findings can be extended to more complex tasks and architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and logical structure.
- The study of the impact of activation functions on the learned representation is a significant and under-explored area, and the paper provides a comprehensive analysis of this impact.
- The paper includes a variety of metrics and experiments to support the claims, which are both intuitive and convincing.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the learning dynamics of networks with one hidden layer, which is a novel approach.
- The paper includes a thorough discussion of related work, which helps in understanding the context of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper focuses primarily on binary classification tasks, which may not generalize well to more complex or varied tasks.
- The experiments are limited to simple tasks and architectures, which may not fully demonstrate the applicability of the findings to more complex or realistic scenarios.
- The paper's claims about the impact of activation functions on learned representations are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence, particularly in more complex or varied tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential impact of other factors such as input noise and task difficulty on the learned representations.
- The paper's focus on binary classification tasks may limit the generalizability of the findings to more complex or varied tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the findings of this study can be generalized to more complex or varied tasks?
- How does the paper address the potential impact of other factors such as input noise and task difficulty on the learned representations?
- Could the authors provide more detailed empirical evidence to support the claims about the impact of activation functions on learned representations, particularly in more complex or varied tasks?
- How do the findings of this study relate to or differ from those of other studies on the impact of activation functions on learned representations?
