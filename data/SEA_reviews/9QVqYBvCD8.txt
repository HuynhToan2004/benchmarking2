 **Summary:**
The paper introduces "Asking Before Acting" (ABA), a novel method for embodied decision-making that enables agents to query external sources for necessary information before taking actions. This approach is inspired by human behavior, where individuals often seek clarification before acting, thereby reducing the need for extensive trial-and-error exploration. The ABA method is evaluated across various environments, including text-based household tasks, robot arm manipulation tasks, and real-world open domain image-based embodied tasks. The paper presents extensive experiments using different LLMs and models, demonstrating the method's effectiveness and efficiency in enhancing agent performance. The ABA method involves a two-step process: first, the agent identifies the context it needs to query for, and then it asks a human or language model for the relevant information. This method is shown to be particularly effective in complex and open-ended tasks where the environment's state is not fully known.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to embodied decision-making by integrating a "human-in-the-loop" component, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that rely solely on the agent's internal state.
- The method is well-motivated and clearly explained, with a focus on reducing the need for extensive trial-and-error exploration, which is a common challenge in embodied decision-making.
- The paper provides extensive empirical evidence, including experiments in various environments and with different models, demonstrating the method's effectiveness and efficiency.
- The proposed method, "Asking Before Acting" (ABA), is a simple yet effective method that can be applied to a wide range of embodied decision-making tasks, showing significant improvements in performance and efficiency over baseline LLM agents.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear descriptions of the experiments and results, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the method scales with the complexity of the environment, especially in scenarios where the state space is large and diverse.
- The method's reliance on the quality of the human model or language model raises concerns about its robustness and generalizability, especially in real-world applications where the model's responses may not be accurate or consistent.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to more complex or open-ended tasks where the environment's state is not fully known.
- There is a lack of comparison with other methods that also use human-in-the-loop interactions, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the ethical implications of involving humans in the loop, especially in terms of privacy and data security.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the method scales with the complexity of the environment, particularly in scenarios where the state space is large and diverse?
- How does the method perform in real-world applications, where the responses from the human model or language model may not be accurate or consistent?
- Can you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of involving humans in the loop, especially in terms of privacy and data security?
- How does the method compare with other methods that also use human-in-the-loop interactions, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the human model and the data used to train it?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the environment's state is not fully known, and what are the implications of this for the method's effectiveness and efficiency?
