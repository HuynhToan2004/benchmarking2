 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SubDiff, a novel subgraph latent diffusion model designed to address the challenges of generating high-quality graphs by simplifying complex dependencies between nodes and bridging the gap between unconditional and conditional generation. The model utilizes a subgraph equivariant GNN to achieve graph equivariance and a Head Alterable Sampling (HAS) strategy for unified conditional and unconditional generative learning. Theoretical analysis and extensive experiments demonstrate that SubDiff achieves superior performance in both generative schemes, showcasing its potential in molecular generation and other graph-related tasks. However, concerns were raised regarding the clarity of the paper, the complexity of the model, and the need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel subgraph latent diffusion model (SubDiff) that effectively addresses the challenges of generating high-quality graphs by simplifying complex dependencies between nodes and bridging the gap between unconditional and conditional generation.
- The proposed subgraph equivariant GNN and head alterable sampling strategy are innovative and contribute significantly to the field of graph generation.
- The paper is well-structured, providing a clear introduction to the challenges and the proposed solution, along with detailed theoretical analysis and extensive experiments that demonstrate the superior performance of SubDiff in both generative schemes.
- The use of subgraphs as the minimum unit in graph generation is a significant contribution, offering a more interpretable and potentially more effective approach compared to node-level generative models.
- The integration of subgraphs into diffusion learning schemes raises interesting questions about how to extract subgraphs and ensure subgraph-level equivariance, which are addressed by the proposed SE-GNN.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in the explanation of the subgraph extractor and the definition of key terms such as "veracity" and "sufficiency."
- The complexity of the model and the computational cost are not adequately addressed, which could limit its practical application in real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency, which is crucial for establishing the superiority of SubDiff.
- The experimental validation is limited to molecular generation, and it would be beneficial to see the application of SubDiff in other graph-related tasks such as graph classification, graph generation, or graph regression.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and sampling processes, which could hinder the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for a complete understanding of its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the subgraph extractor and the process of extracting subgraphs from a graph?
- How does the model ensure the "veracity" and "sufficiency" of the subgraphs extracted?
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of "veracity" and "sufficiency" in the context of subgraph extraction?
- How does the model handle the complexity and computational cost, especially in large-scale graph generation tasks?
- Can the authors provide more comparisons with existing methods in terms of performance and efficiency to establish the superiority of SubDiff?
- Would it be possible to extend the experimental validation to include other graph-related tasks beyond molecular generation?
- How does the model handle the potential loss of structural information when subgraphs are extracted?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential areas for future research?
