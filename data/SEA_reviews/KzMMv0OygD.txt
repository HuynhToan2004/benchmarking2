 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for automatically generating high-quality instruction-tuning data using a task generator that produces instruction, input, and output based on human-written texts. This method is designed to minimize noise and ensure coherence and alignment in the generated tasks. The authors also employ a discriminator to filter out invalid tasks, aiming to enhance the reliability and validity of the dataset. The proposed dataset, Teg-Instruct, contains 270k instruction data and has been evaluated through manual and automated methods, showing improvements in both in-domain and out-of-domain capabilities for large language models. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as it builds on existing methods like back-translation and dual-view ICL, and for not sufficiently distinguishing itself from similar works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of language model (LM) training, focusing on the collection of high-quality instruction-tuning data, which is crucial for enhancing LM capabilities.
- The proposed method is novel and effective, utilizing a task generator that produces instruction, input, and output based on human-written texts, which is a significant advancement over existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method is effective in improving the in-domain and out-of-domain capabilities of large language models.
- The dataset introduced in the paper, Teg-Instruct, is of high quality and shows promise in enhancing the performance of LMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the differences between the proposed method and similar existing works, such as the LongForm dataset, which could lead to confusion about the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The evaluation of the dataset is limited, and the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate how the dataset can be used to evaluate the performance of LLMs, especially in different domains.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of hallucination, which is a significant concern in the field of instruction-tuning data.
- The dataset's quality and diversity are not thoroughly evaluated, and the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims of its effectiveness in real-world applications.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing methods, which could help in understanding the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method more clearly.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method differ from existing methods, particularly in terms of data quality and the effectiveness of the task generator?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the effectiveness of the method in real-world applications?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of hallucination in instruction-tuning data?
- Could the authors elaborate on the process of manual evaluation and provide more details on the criteria used for evaluating the quality of the dataset?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Is there a plan to release the dataset and the code for reproducibility and further research?
