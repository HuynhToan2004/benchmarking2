 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MetaFormer, a novel meta-learning approach designed for few-shot image classification using Vision Transformers (ViTs). MetaFormer employs two attention modules: Sample-level Attention Module (SAM) and Task-level Attention Module (TAM), which enable intra-task and inter-task interactions. SAM operates at the sample level, focusing on spatial attention and sample attention, while TAM facilitates knowledge transfer across tasks. Extensive experiments on multiple datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of MetaFormer, showing improvements over state-of-the-art methods. However, concerns are raised regarding the novelty and depth of the technical contributions, particularly in terms of the complexity and effectiveness of the attention mechanisms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the proposed methods.
- The performance of the proposed method is impressive, achieving new state-of-the-art results on multiple benchmarks.
- The paper introduces novel attention modules (SAM and TAM) that effectively leverage transformer characteristics for few-shot image classification, showing significant performance improvements.
- The design of the proposed modules is intuitive and easy to understand, with clear illustrations and detailed explanations.
- The paper provides extensive experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of each component of the proposed method.
- The autoregressive setting for few-shot learning is a novel and interesting approach, which has not been explored in previous works.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other meta-learning approaches, especially in terms of computational complexity and memory usage.
- The novelty of the proposed attention modules is questionable, as similar mechanisms have been explored in previous works.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations and failure cases of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis on the computational complexity and memory usage of the proposed modules, which could affect the scalability and practical deployment of the method.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive review of related literature, particularly in the context of meta-learning and few-shot learning.
- The technical contribution of the paper is somewhat limited, with the proposed modules (SAM and TAM) appearing to be straightforward and not significantly advancing the field.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method to larger datasets or more complex scenarios, which could limit its applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison of the computational complexity and memory usage of their proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger datasets or more complex scenarios, such as fine-grained classification or image-level recognition?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the number of tasks and the complexity of the tasks?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of query-query relationships in the autoregressive setting, and what are the implications for the overall performance?
- Could the authors provide more insights into the design of the attention modules, particularly how they compare to similar mechanisms in existing literature?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed autoregressive setting for few-shot learning, and how does it compare to other settings in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
