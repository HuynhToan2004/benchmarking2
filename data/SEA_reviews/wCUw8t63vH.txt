 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, PG-LDS-ID, for modeling the shared dynamics between two data streams with distinct statistical properties, specifically focusing on Poisson and Gaussian observations. This method involves a two-stage process where the first stage identifies shared dynamics and the second stage models residual dynamics. The approach is validated using both simulated and real data, demonstrating its effectiveness in predicting one data stream from another. The method's theoretical contributions are supported by a detailed derivation and numerical simulations, although it primarily focuses on Poisson and Gaussian processes. The paper also discusses potential limitations and extensions to other generalized linear models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of dynamical modeling, focusing on the identification of shared dynamics between two different types of data streams.
- The methodology is well-derived, with clear and concise explanations that aid in understanding the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and comprehend, which is crucial for readers to engage with the content.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method in both simulated and real-world datasets.
- The paper includes a detailed derivation of the method, which is a valuable contribution to the field and could potentially be applied in other contexts.
- The authors have provided a thorough discussion on the limitations of their method, which is commendable for transparency and completeness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on Poisson and Gaussian processes, which might limit its applicability to other types of data streams.
- The methodology is described as a straightforward extension of previous works, which might not sufficiently differentiate it from existing methods.
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the novelty and distinct advantages of the proposed method over existing techniques, such as those mentioned in related works.
- There is a potential issue with the scalability of the method, especially in high-dimensional datasets, which could affect its practical applicability.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples in the appendix to enhance the understanding of the method, particularly in complex sections like the derivation of the Hankel matrix.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the method, such as the assumptions of linear dynamics and the potential for overfitting in high-dimensional data.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific advantages of their method over existing techniques, particularly in terms of scalability and applicability to different types of data streams?
- How does the proposed method handle non-linear dynamics, and what are the implications of this assumption on the validity of the results?
- In the context of the two-stage learning process, how does the method ensure that the second stage does not overfit to the data, especially in high-dimensional settings?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or simulations to illustrate the method's effectiveness in practical scenarios, especially where the data might exhibit non-stationarity or other complex dynamics?
- How does the method perform when applied to data streams that are not Poisson or Gaussian, and what modifications might be necessary for such applications?
