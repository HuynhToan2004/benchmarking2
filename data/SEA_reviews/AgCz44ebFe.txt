 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to continual learning with noisy labels, focusing on the forgetting problem in incremental training. The method, Alternate Experience Replay (AER), leverages the phenomenon of forgetting to maintain a clear distinction between clean, complex, and noisy samples in the memory buffer. AER is enhanced by Asymmetric Balanced Sampling (ABS), which prioritizes sample selection based on current task purity and past sample complexity. The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across various datasets and noise rates, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the fairness of the comparison due to the different training settings used for the proposed method and baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant issue of forgetting in incremental training, a crucial challenge in continual learning.
- The proposed method, Alternate Experience Replay (AER), is novel and well-explained, with a clear motivation and theoretical backing.
- The use of asymmetric sampling and asymmetric balanced sampling is innovative and effectively demonstrated through experiments.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and comprehensive evaluations across various datasets and noise rates.
- The methodology is generalizable and can be applied to other continual learning methods, showing potential for broader application.
- The results are promising, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods in both accuracy and purity of the obtained buffer.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a direct comparison with existing methods that also use forgetting to purify the memory buffer, which could undermine the perceived novelty of the proposed method.
- The baseline methods are not optimally compared, particularly in terms of the number of epochs used for training, which could affect the fairness of the evaluation.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The methodology is not clearly described, particularly the operation of the Alternate Experience Replay (AER) and the Asymmetric Balanced Sampling (ABS), which could confuse readers.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a direct comparison with existing methods that also use forgetting to purify the memory buffer?
- Why was the decision made to use different training settings for the proposed method and baselines?
- Could the authors clarify the operation of the Alternate Experience Replay (AER) and the Asymmetric Balanced Sampling (ABS) to improve understanding?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational complexity, especially in practical implementations?
- Is there a possibility of extending the method to handle real-world label noise, which is often more complex and varied than synthetic noise?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their work and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
