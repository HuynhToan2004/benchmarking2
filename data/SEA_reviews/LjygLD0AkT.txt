 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection using variational autoencoders (VAEs), focusing on the "likelihood path" principle. This approach aims to enhance the reliability of OOD detection by leveraging the VAE's latent space to identify subtle differences between in-distribution (IID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) data. The methodology involves the use of minimal sufficient statistics to identify informative summary statistics, which are then used to calculate OOD scores. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for this method and demonstrates its effectiveness through empirical results, showing improvements over existing methods in certain scenarios. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the method due to its reliance on specific VAE architectures and the lack of comparison with more powerful models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the main ideas and theoretical contributions.
- The use of minimal sufficient statistics to identify informative summary statistics for OOD detection is a novel and interesting approach.
- The theoretical analysis is solid, providing a strong foundation for the proposed method.
- The paper introduces new theoretical tools such as nearly essential support, essential distance, and co-Lipschitzness, which are significant contributions to the field.
- The empirical results show that the proposed method can outperform existing methods in certain scenarios, demonstrating its practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the method performs in scenarios where the IID and OOD distributions overlap, which is a critical aspect of OOD detection.
- There is a significant reliance on the VAE architecture, particularly the encoder and decoder, which may limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with more powerful models, such as diffusion models, which could provide a more robust validation of the approach.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with some results (e.g., LPath-2M-3MD in Table 1) being worse than those of existing methods.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method in detail, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the method performs in scenarios where the IID and OOD distributions overlap?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples of the definitions and concepts introduced in the paper, particularly those related to the likelihood path principle and the L-Lipschitz and co-Lipschitz definitions?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to other types of generative models, such as normalizing flows or diffusion models?
- How does the method handle different types of OOD data, such as adversarial attacks or data from different domains?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the method in more detail, including any assumptions or constraints that might limit its applicability?
