 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel architecture, GRED, for graph representation learning that integrates linear recurrent networks with permutation-invariant neural networks. This approach allows for efficient handling of information from distant nodes without the need for positional encodings, potentially reducing computational complexity. The architecture is evaluated on various benchmarks, demonstrating competitive performance against state-of-the-art graph transformers. Theoretical analysis is provided to support the expressiveness of the model, showing it to be strictly more expressive than one-hop message-passing neural networks. However, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental validation on only a few datasets and the lack of a detailed comparison with other long-range GNNs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel architecture for graph representation learning, integrating linear recurrent networks with permutation-invariant neural networks, which effectively handles information from distant nodes without the need for positional encodings.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to support the expressiveness of the model, showing it to be strictly more expressive than one-hop message-passing neural networks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed analysis of the expressiveness of the proposed architecture.
- The model is evaluated on various benchmarks and shows competitive performance against state-of-the-art graph transformers, with reduced computational complexity.
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the model's expressiveness and its relationship to other GNN models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental validation is limited to only a few datasets, which may not adequately support the claims made about the model's effectiveness.
- There is a lack of detailed comparison with other long-range GNNs, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed model's performance.
- The paper does not discuss the scalability of the model with large graphs, which is a significant limitation given the potential application to large-scale graph problems.
- The model's complexity and its comparison with other models, particularly in terms of computational efficiency, are not adequately discussed.
- The paper does not address the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its contributions and challenges.

**Questions:**
- How does the model perform on larger datasets, and can it scale to handle very large graphs?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other long-range GNNs to better understand the model's performance in this context?
- How does the model's complexity compare to other models, and what are the computational costs associated with using this model?
- Are there any limitations to the proposed method, and how do these limitations impact the model's applicability and effectiveness?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the model to handle directed graphs, and how this might affect its performance and expressiveness?
