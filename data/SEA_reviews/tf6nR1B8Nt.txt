 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the geometry of neural network loss landscapes by analyzing the Restricted Secant Inequality (RSI) and Error Bound (EB) properties of stochastic gradients during training. The authors demonstrate that these properties, which are crucial for understanding the dynamics of optimization algorithms like SGD, remain stable across different training settings, including various architectures, datasets, and batch sizes. They also show that the cosine similarity between gradients and the direction to the final iterate is typically positive, indicating that the gradients are generally pointing towards the solution. The paper provides empirical evidence to support these findings, suggesting that these properties could inform the design of learning rate schedules and potentially enhance our understanding of neural network optimization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and an interesting set of experiments that support the claims.
- The experiments are extensive and well-documented, providing a detailed analysis of the Restricted Secant Inequality (RSI) and Error Bound (EB) properties across different settings, which is crucial for understanding the dynamics of optimization algorithms like SGD.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to understanding the geometry of loss landscapes by focusing on the properties of the stochastic gradients, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The findings, such as the positive cosine similarity between gradients and the direction to the final iterate, are both surprising and insightful, providing a new perspective on the optimization process.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear presentation of the results and a discussion of the implications of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive theoretical analysis, which could strengthen the claims made about the RSI and EB properties and their implications for optimization algorithms.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the findings relate to existing theoretical results, such as those related to the Polyak-Łojasiewicz (PL) condition and the local strong convexity of neural networks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the findings, such as the potential biases introduced by using the final iterate as a reference point.
- The experiments are somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on image classification tasks and not exploring a broader range of tasks or architectures, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the implications of the findings for practical applications, such as the design of learning rate schedules or the optimization of neural network training.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the final iterate is chosen in your experiments, particularly in relation to the epoch budget and the number of training steps?
- How do the RSI and EB properties vary with different learning rates, and what are their implications for the convergence of SGD?
- Is there a possibility that the gradients are pointing in the wrong direction during the early stages of training, and if so, what does this suggest about the optimization process?
- How do the findings relate to the PL condition and the local strong convexity of neural networks, and what are the implications of these relationships for the optimization of neural networks?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the specifics of the hyperparameters used and the initialization of the weights?
- How do the findings generalize to other tasks or architectures beyond those studied, and what are the potential applications of these findings in practical settings?
