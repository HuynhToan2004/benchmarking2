 **Summary:**
The paper explores inverse game theory and inverse multiagent learning, focusing on the computation of inverse Nash equilibria (NE) and Nash simulacra in various game settings, including one-shot and repeated games. It formulates these problems as min-max optimization problems, providing polynomial-time algorithms for their computation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their methods through empirical evaluations, showing that the proposed algorithms can recover true parameters of games and make accurate predictions in the Spanish electricity market. However, the paper faces criticisms regarding its clarity in defining key concepts, the novelty of its contributions, and the strength of its empirical evidence.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, including detailed proofs and clear mathematical formulations, which are crucial for understanding the complexities of inverse game theory and inverse multiagent learning.
- The authors introduce a novel approach by formulating the computation of inverse Nash equilibria as a min-max optimization problem, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible to readers with varying levels of expertise in game theory and multi-agent learning.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, showing that they can recover true parameters of games and make accurate predictions in real-world scenarios.
- The paper is original in its approach, particularly in its application to inverse Nash equilibrium in inverse games, which is a less explored area in the literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing works, which could lead to confusion about the novelty of the research.
- There are several typos and unclear notations throughout the paper, which could hinder the understanding of the content.
- The empirical evidence provided is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions made by the algorithms are not met.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the algorithms, which is a significant concern given the polynomial time complexity claimed.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the algorithms, particularly in relation to the assumptions made and the practical implications of these assumptions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the distinction between the contributions of this paper and those of existing works, particularly in terms of the novelty and depth of the theoretical contributions?
- How do the authors address the limitations of the proposed methods, and what are the implications of these limitations for the practical applicability of the findings?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the computational complexity of the algorithms and the assumptions made by the theoretical results?
- How does the paper's approach to inverse Nash equilibrium in inverse games differ from or build upon existing works, particularly in terms of the theoretical contributions and empirical validations?
- Could the authors elaborate on the computational efficiency of the algorithms, especially in comparison to other methods like ARIMA, and discuss the practical implications of the polynomial time complexity claims?
