 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of reinforcement learning (RL) in group systems, focusing on the moment kernel representation to address the curse of dimensionality associated with large-scale populations of agents. The authors propose a hierarchical algorithm that leverages moment kernels to represent the group system, allowing for the approximation of the value function and the learning of optimal policies. The algorithm is tested on a linear quadratic regulator (LQR) problem, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling large-scale systems. Despite its theoretical contributions, the paper is critiqued for its lack of practical applicability, limited scope of experimental validation, and insufficient discussion on the scalability and generalizability of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The authors introduce a novel approach to RL for group systems, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method are robust, providing a solid foundation for the work.
- The paper is original in its approach to using moment kernel representations for RL, which is a novel contribution to the field.
- The methodology is clearly explained, and the experiments are well-designed, showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the practical applications of the proposed method, making it difficult to understand its relevance and utility in real-world scenarios.
- The scope of the experiments is limited, focusing only on a specific type of problem (LQR), which does not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, which is a critical concern given the focus on large-scale systems.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and its assumptions, particularly in relation to the assumptions made in the theoretical proofs.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its results with existing methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the practical applications of the proposed method? How can it be applied in real-world scenarios, and what are its potential benefits?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues associated with large-scale systems? What are the computational and memory requirements of the algorithm, and how do they scale with the size of the system?
- The paper mentions the use of moment kernel representations for RL. Could the authors explain why this approach is effective and how it contributes to the overall methodology?
- The paper assumes that the state space is compact. How does this assumption affect the applicability of the method in more complex or less structured environments?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of their results with existing methods? How do their findings compare in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- In the experimental results, how do the authors justify the choice of parameters such as the truncation order and the number of iterations? What are the implications of these choices on the results?
