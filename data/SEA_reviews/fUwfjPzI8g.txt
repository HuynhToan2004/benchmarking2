 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for continual learning, specifically in the context of class-incremental learning, using a Gumbel-softmax network that learns task-specific sparsity in representations. This method involves replacing traditional non-linearities with task-winning-takes-all (TWTA) blocks, where only the winning unit for a given task is retained, and the rest are zeroed out. The network learns a global weight matrix that is not specific to a task, but evolves over time, and during training, it uses a categorical posterior distribution over winning block units to regulate weight training. This method is evaluated on several benchmarks and reportedly outperforms existing methods in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear figures and detailed experimental settings.
- The method proposed is novel and innovative, particularly in the use of task-winning-takes-all (TWTA) blocks and the Gumbel-softmax network, which are not commonly used in continual learning.
- The method is efficient in terms of computational resources, as it does not require additional computational overhead during inference, unlike other methods that rely on large buffers for storing examples from previous tasks.
- The paper provides extensive evaluations on various benchmarks, including CIFAR-100, Tiny-ImageNet, PMNIST, Omniglot Rotation, and 5-Datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.
- The method is simple yet effective, with a straightforward approach that is easy to understand and implement.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of why the method works, particularly in terms of how the TWTA blocks and the Gumbel-softmax network contribute to the performance improvements.
- There is a lack of comparison with the latest state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's effectiveness and novelty.
- The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies, particularly on the number of blocks and the size of the blocks, which are critical parameters for the method's performance.
- The method's effectiveness in more challenging scenarios, such as larger datasets or more complex tasks, is not explored.
- The paper's writing style and structure could be improved, with some sections appearing unclear or overly technical.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method and potential negative societal impacts.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the intuition behind the method, particularly how the TWTA blocks and the Gumbel-softmax network contribute to the performance improvements?
- How does the method perform when the number of blocks and the size of the blocks are varied? Could the authors provide more detailed ablation studies on these parameters?
- Is there a risk of overfitting with the method, especially given the limited training data per class?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their method, particularly in the context of continual learning?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with the latest state-of-the-art methods to demonstrate the method's effectiveness and novelty?
