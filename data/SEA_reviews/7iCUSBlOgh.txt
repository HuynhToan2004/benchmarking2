 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GRASS, a novel method for handling missing data in bio-medical tabular data, which is a significant challenge in real-world applications. GRASS leverages feature gradients to construct a graph structure from both feature- and sample-oriented perspectives, enabling it to serve as an effective starting point for existing graph-based imputation models. The methodology is supported by extensive experiments on real-world datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of GRASS in enhancing the performance of downstream tasks. The paper also discusses the limitations and potential improvements of GRASS, including its dependence on specific types of datasets and the need for further theoretical analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the bio-medical domain, focusing on the handling of missing data in tabular datasets, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The proposed method, GRASS, is novel and well-motivated, utilizing feature gradients to construct a graph structure from both feature- and sample-oriented perspectives, which is a unique approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a thorough experimental evaluation that demonstrates the effectiveness of GRASS in enhancing the performance of downstream tasks.
- The methodology is supported by a detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper provides a clear and concise description of the proposed method, along with a detailed description of the experiments, which are crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed method, which could provide a deeper understanding of its effectiveness and limitations.
- The dependence of the proposed method on specific types of datasets, such as those with missing values in a significant portion of the data, could limit its applicability to other types of datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential negative societal consequences and the need for further research.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could help in understanding the relative performance of GRASS.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
