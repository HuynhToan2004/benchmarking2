 **Summary:**
The paper explores the unification of various Shapley value estimation methods, including stochastic estimators and amortized estimators, under a linear transformation framework. This unification leads to the introduction of a new estimator, SimSHAP, which minimizes the L2 distance to the approximated Shapley values within the Euclidean space. The paper provides empirical evidence of SimSHAP's effectiveness and efficiency through experiments on tabular and image datasets, showing that it can outperform or match the performance of existing methods while offering faster computation times. Despite its novel approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as it primarily combines existing methods without significant innovation, and for its lack of comprehensive comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear unification of different Shapley value estimation methods.
- The proposed SimSHAP method is simple and effective, offering a straightforward amortized estimator that minimizes the L2 distance to the approximated Shapley values within the Euclidean space.
- The empirical results demonstrate that SimSHAP can outperform or match the performance of existing methods while offering faster computation times.
- The paper provides a thorough review of the literature on Shapley value estimation and effectively unifies various Shapley value estimation strategies as the linear transformation of values obtained from sampled subsets.
- The proposed SimSHAP method achieves orders of magnitude faster computation with comparable accuracy as shown in Fig. 1b.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it primarily combines existing methods without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks comprehensive comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, such as HarsanyiNet, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with some results showing only marginal improvements or even inferior performance in certain metrics.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in high-dimensional datasets, which could impact its practical applicability.
- The methodology section is confusing, particularly the definitions and explanations of key parameters and equations, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not provide a detailed comparison with other gradient-based methods, which are crucial for a fair evaluation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of the proposed method and how it advances the field beyond existing methods?
- Why were certain state-of-the-art methods not included in the comparison, and how does this affect the evaluation of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in high-dimensional datasets, and what are the computational complexities involved?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other gradient-based methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- Can the authors provide a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential avenues for future research?
