 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for training neural networks from scratch using low-rank adapters, specifically the LoRA-the-Explorer (LTE) algorithm. This method is designed to parallelize the training process across multiple compute nodes, reducing the need for frequent synchronization. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by training Vision Transformers (ViT) on ImageNet-100 and ImageNet-1K datasets, showing that it can match or exceed the performance of standard training methods with less memory and computational overhead. The method involves the parallel training of multiple low-rank heads across nodes, with periodic merging to approximate full-rank updates. The paper also discusses the challenges and limitations of using LoRA for pre-training and provides insights into how these issues can be addressed.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for training neural networks from scratch using low-rank adapters, which is a significant advancement in the field of distributed training.
- The proposed method, LTE, demonstrates competitive performance with standard training methods while reducing memory and computational requirements, making it particularly useful for large-scale pre-training tasks.
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the methodology and experimental results, with detailed ablation studies and comparisons that effectively demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the LTE method, including a detailed analysis of its performance and resource utilization, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness.
- The methodology is well-supported by theoretical and empirical evidence, which is crucial for validating the proposed approach and its practical implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency, which could undermine the perceived effectiveness of the proposed method.
- There is a noticeable absence of discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for the adoption of the technology.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of gradient noise in the context of parallel updates, which could affect the accuracy and efficiency of the training process.
- The scalability of the proposed method to larger, more complex models and datasets is not thoroughly explored, which could limit the practical applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational and communication costs associated with the proposed method, particularly in the context of distributed training.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, including both performance and efficiency metrics?
- How does the proposed method perform when scaled up to larger, more complex models and datasets?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of gradient noise in the context of parallel updates?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational and communication costs associated with the proposed method, particularly in the context of distributed training?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of resource utilization and efficiency with other methods that use low-rank adaptation for pre-training?
