 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Bidirectional-reachable Hierarchical Policy Optimization (BrHPO), a novel hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) algorithm that addresses the issue of unidirectional subgoal reachability by allowing mutual information exchange between high-level and low-level policies. This approach aims to enhance exploration efficiency and overall performance by enabling the high-level policy to generate subgoals that balance incentive and accessibility, while the low-level policy takes more effective actions that drive subtask trajectories closer to the subgoal. Theoretical analysis supports the design of the BrHPO algorithm, and empirical results demonstrate its effectiveness in various long-horizon tasks. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the theoretical analysis, the complexity of the algorithm, and the empirical evaluation, which was criticized for its lack of rigor and depth.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel mutual response mechanism that allows for bilateral information sharing and error correction, enhancing the exploration efficiency and overall performance in hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL).
- Theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the design of the BrHPO algorithm, and empirical results demonstrate its effectiveness in various long-horizon tasks.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-understand explanations, and the experiments are conducted on multiple environments, showing good performance.
- The introduction of a novel subgoal reachability metric and the derivation of a performance difference bound for hierarchical policy optimization are significant contributions to the field.
- The method is flexible and can be applied to a variety of different environments, and the experimental results are strong, showing good performance compared to state-of-the-art HRL baselines.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the computational complexity and training wall-time of the BrHPO algorithm, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The theoretical analysis, particularly Theorem 3.2, is complex and not well-explained, which makes it difficult for readers to understand the implications and assumptions of the theorem.
- The empirical evaluation is not rigorous, with the use of a limited number of seeds and environments, which may not sufficiently demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the BrHPO algorithm with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that use similar techniques such as mutual information and bidirectional communication in hierarchical policy optimization.
- The paper does not address the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the computational complexity and training wall-time of the BrHPO algorithm?
- How does the BrHPO algorithm handle scenarios where the high-level policy generates unattainable subgoals?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions and implications of Theorem 3.2, particularly in terms of its applicability and the conditions under which it holds?
- How does the BrHPO algorithm compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and empirical performance?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, such as the number of seeds used and the environments tested, to support the claims of the paper?
- Could the authors address the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential areas for future research?
