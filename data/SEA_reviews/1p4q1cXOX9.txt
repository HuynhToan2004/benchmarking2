 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Gelato, a novel link prediction method aimed at addressing the challenges posed by class imbalance in real-world graphs, particularly in sparse settings. Gelato integrates topological heuristics with graph learning to leverage both attribute and topological information, employing a ranking loss for addressing class imbalance and a negative sampling scheme for efficient training. The methodology is evaluated on various datasets, showing Gelato's superiority over GNN-based methods in terms of accuracy and scalability. The paper critiques the limitations of GNNs in handling class imbalance and proposes a new evaluation metric, focusing on the effectiveness of Gelato in handling unbiased testing scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and effectively motivates the proposed method, Gelato, through a clear and structured presentation.
- The authors provide a comprehensive review of related works, demonstrating a thorough understanding of the existing literature.
- Gelato is shown to outperform GNN-based methods in terms of accuracy and scalability, as evidenced by extensive experiments across various datasets.
- The paper introduces a new evaluation metric, focusing on the effectiveness of Gelato in handling unbiased testing scenarios, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is simple yet effective, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and its components.
- The authors provide a detailed explanation of the proposed method, including its components and the rationale behind their design, which enhances the understanding and reproducibility of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of Gelato, particularly in terms of its scalability and performance on larger graphs.
- There is a lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a more robust evaluation of Gelato's effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of existing methods, such as GNNs, and how Gelato addresses these limitations.
- The methodology section could be improved by including a more detailed explanation of the training process and the role of different components in the model's performance.
- The paper's reliance on certain assumptions, such as the effectiveness of the autocovariance similarity metric, could be questioned, especially in scenarios where other metrics might perform better.
- The paper's focus on the sparse setting might limit its applicability to denser graphs, which are also common in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Can Gelato be applied to denser graphs, and how does it perform in such settings?
- How does Gelato handle the issue of class imbalance in graphs, especially in scenarios where the class imbalance is not as extreme as in the datasets used in the experiments?
- Could you provide more details on the training process for Gelato, including the role of different components and the impact of hyperparameters on the model's performance?
- How does Gelato compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of accuracy and efficiency?
- In the context of the experiments, can you clarify the role of the partitioning-based negative sampling scheme in improving Gelato's performance?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the autocovariance similarity metric and discuss how it compares to other metrics in terms of effectiveness and applicability?
