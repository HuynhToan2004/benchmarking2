 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the in-context learning (ICL) capabilities of transformers, focusing on how they process representations and perform linear in-context learning. It introduces a synthetic data setting where the label depends on the input through a fixed representation function, which is a multi-layer MLP, and a linear function that varies per instance. Theoretical analysis is provided to demonstrate how transformers can approximate these algorithms with mild depth and size. Empirical results show that transformers achieve near-optimal ICL performance in this setting, with the lower layers transforming the dataset and the upper layers performing linear ICL. The paper also employs probing and pasting experiments to dissect the mechanisms within trained transformers, revealing several mechanisms such as concrete copying behaviors and post-ICL representation selection mechanisms in a harder mixture setting.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear figures and explanations that aid in understanding the complex mechanisms of in-context learning (ICL) in transformers.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the ICL problem with a representation function, which is an interesting and novel approach.
- The paper includes both theoretical analysis and empirical results, which are well-integrated and provide insights into the mechanisms of ICL in transformers.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and show that transformers can perform near-optimal ICL with representations, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper introduces a new setting for ICL with representations, which is more complex and realistic than previous settings, and provides a detailed analysis of how transformers process these representations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples of the settings used in the experiments, particularly in the dynamical system setting.
- The choice of the representation function and the number of layers in the MLP used to generate the representation is not well justified, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and the generalizability of the findings to more complex or realistic settings.
- The theoretical analysis and empirical results are not always well-aligned, and the paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with existing theories and empirical results.
- The paper could be more accessible to a broader audience by including more intuitive explanations and examples, especially in the theoretical sections.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the settings used in the experiments, particularly in the dynamical system setting? How do these settings affect the results, and are there any specific examples or scenarios where these settings are applicable?
- Why was the choice made to use a multi-layer MLP as the representation function, and how does this affect the results?
- How does the paper's analysis and findings compare to existing theories and empirical results on ICL in transformers? Are there any significant differences or similarities?
- In the theoretical analysis, could you provide more intuitive explanations and examples to help readers understand the concepts better?
- How does the paper's analysis and findings apply to more complex or realistic settings, such as those involving larger or more complex transformers, or different types of input data?
