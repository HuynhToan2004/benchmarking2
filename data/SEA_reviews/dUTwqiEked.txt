 **Summary:**
The paper introduces RetroDiff, a novel diffusion-based method for retrosynthesis, which decomposes the retrosynthesis procedure into two stages: first sampling external graph motifs from a dummy distribution and then generating external bonds to connect the products and generated motifs. This approach is designed to address the challenge of integrating a diffusion-based graph-to-graph framework while retaining essential chemical reaction template information. The method is evaluated on the USPTO-50K dataset, showing promising results in terms of top-k accuracy and validity, although the performance improvements over existing methods are marginal.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible.
- The application of diffusion models to retrosynthesis is novel and innovative, offering a fresh perspective on the problem.
- The methodology is clear, and the paper effectively explains the challenges and solutions related to integrating a diffusion-based graph-to-graph framework with essential chemical reaction template information.
- The approach of using a diffusion-based model for retrosynthesis is promising, as it allows for the generation of more diverse and creative retrosynthesis paths.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, demonstrating its effectiveness through experiments on the USPTO-50K dataset.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other diffusion-based methods, which could provide a clearer understanding of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as the use of diffusion models for retrosynthesis is not entirely new, and the method's performance improvements over existing methods are marginal.
- There are concerns about the scalability and applicability of the method to more complex or diverse datasets, such as those with more reaction types or larger molecular structures.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the method, which could be crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The performance of the model is not significantly better than existing methods, which could limit its practical utility.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the method's performance improves over existing methods, especially in terms of the marginal improvements noted?
- How does the proposed method compare to other diffusion-based methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to multi-step retrosynthesis scenarios, and if so, what are the potential challenges and benefits?
- How does the method handle the scalability issues, especially when applied to more complex or diverse datasets?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, such as the loss function used and the optimization techniques employed?
- How does the method ensure the validity of the generated molecules, especially in terms of chemical feasibility and reactivity?
