 **Summary:**
The paper explores the utility of discrete representations in reinforcement learning (RL), specifically focusing on the use of discrete representations in world modeling and continual RL. The authors compare different methods such as vanilla autoencoders, FTA autoencoders, and VQ-VAEs, showing that discrete representations can be advantageous in scenarios where the environment is complex and non-stationary. The paper provides empirical evidence that discrete representations can enhance the learning of world models and improve sample efficiency in RL agents. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited scope, focusing on only two environments, and for not adequately addressing the scalability and generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methods and results.
- It provides a comprehensive comparison of different types of representations (dense, sparse, discrete) in both world modeling and RL, which is beneficial for understanding the effects of representation choices on model performance.
- The experiments are well-designed, with multiple baselines and ablation studies that provide valuable insights into the role of representation in RL.
- The paper is novel in its approach to studying the impact of discrete representations in RL, which is a relatively unexplored area.
- The use of discrete representations is shown to be beneficial in continual RL, which is an important and relevant application.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is limited in scope, focusing on only two environments, which may not generalize well to other environments or domains.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage of discrete representations over other types of representations, such as continuous or sparse representations.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the scalability of the findings, which is crucial for understanding the practical applicability of the results in more complex or larger-scale environments.
- The methodology and experimental design are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to replicate or build upon the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the research.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the environments used and the training procedures?
- How does the paper address the potential negative societal impacts of the research?
- What are the specific advantages of using discrete representations over other types of representations, and how do these advantages translate into practical benefits in RL applications?
- How do the findings of this paper compare to other recent works on discrete representations in RL, such as those by Wang et al. (2022)?
- Can you clarify the discrepancies in the results, particularly the underperformance of VQ-VAE in certain scenarios compared to other methods?
- How do the findings of this paper contribute to the ongoing research in model-based RL, particularly in the context of continual learning?
