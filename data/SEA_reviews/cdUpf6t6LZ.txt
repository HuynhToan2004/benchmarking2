 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel NAS-RobBench-201 benchmark, focusing on adversarial training and robustness in neural architecture search (NAS). The benchmark evaluates the robustness of architectures against adversarial attacks using a variety of metrics and datasets, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet-16-120. The authors also provide a theoretical framework using Neural Tangent Kernels (NTK) to analyze the generalization bounds of robust neural networks. The paper aims to advance the understanding of robustness in NAS and contribute to the development of more robust models in the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new NAS-RobBench-201 benchmark, which is the first to focus on adversarial training and robustness in NAS, providing a valuable resource for the community.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the robustness of NAS models under adversarial training, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper includes a detailed theoretical analysis using Neural Tangent Kernels (NTK), which is well-integrated with empirical results and provides a solid foundation for future research.
- The authors have released the benchmark dataset, which will be beneficial for further research in the field.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making the complex theoretical concepts accessible to a broader audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to a specific model architecture (residual networks), which may not generalize well to other architectures or datasets.
- The robustness evaluation is limited to simple adversarial attacks (FGSM and PGD), which may not adequately represent more advanced or realistic attacks.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed benchmark, which could have provided a more balanced perspective.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its results with existing benchmarks or discuss the computational cost of adversarial training, which could be a significant barrier for practical implementation.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly how they can be applied to improve NAS algorithms in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the adversarial training hyperparameters were chosen and their impact on the results?
- How do the results compare when using stronger adversarial attacks, such as AutoAttack or C&W attacks?
- What are the implications of the theoretical results for the design of more robust NAS algorithms?
- How do the results compare with existing benchmarks, particularly in terms of the robustness of the architectures found?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of adversarial training hyperparameters and discuss the potential impact of different hyperparameters on the results?
- How do the authors address the issue of computational cost in adversarial training, and what strategies could be employed to make it more feasible for practical applications?
