 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to transform large language models (LLMs) into Tabular Foundation Models (TabFMs) by training them on a diverse set of tabular datasets. The proposed method, Generative Tabular Learning (GTL), is designed to enhance LLMs' capabilities in understanding and processing tabular data, particularly through a text-based representation of tabular features. This approach allows for both zero-shot and in-context learning, which is demonstrated to outperform existing methods in various benchmarks. The authors have also constructed a large-scale dataset of 115 tabular datasets for research, which could be valuable for further studies in this area.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to transform LLMs into TabFMs by training on tabular datasets, which is a significant advancement in the field of tabular learning.
- The proposed method, Generative Tabular Learning (GTL), is well-explained and supported by extensive experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness in enhancing LLMs' capabilities on tabular data.
- The construction of a large-scale dataset of 115 tabular datasets is a valuable resource for future research in this area.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in various benchmarks.
- The approach of using LLMs to understand tabular data is innovative and could potentially lead to significant advancements in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, which are crucial for practical applications.
- The methodology for constructing the GTL dataset and the prompt generation process is not thoroughly explained, which could limit the reproducibility and transparency of the research.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of tabular data and its scalability to larger datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other LLM-based methods, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its advantages and disadvantages.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of the statistical significance of the improvements and the practical implications of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the construction of the GTL dataset, particularly how the data was selected and how it was balanced to ensure a representative sample?
- How does the proposed method perform on different types of tabular data, such as those with categorical features or different data distributions?
- Could the authors elaborate on the process of prompt generation and how it impacts the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare with other LLM-based methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including statistical significance tests and practical implications of the improvements?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between generality and accuracy in tabular data understanding?
