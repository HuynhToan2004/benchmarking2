 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LightHGNN and LightHGNN+, novel methods designed to distill knowledge from Hypergraph Neural Networks (HGNNs) into Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) for faster inference. The approach involves a two-stage process: first, distilling knowledge from HGNNs to MLPs using a soft label from the teacher model, and second, employing a topology-aware distillation method to enhance the MLPs' performance. The proposed methods are evaluated on both graph and hypergraph datasets, demonstrating improved inference speed and competitive performance compared to HGNNs. The paper also addresses the issue of over-smoothing by introducing a topology-aware score to quantify feature relevance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper effectively bridges the gap between Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) and Hypergraph Neural Networks (HGNNs), achieving both performance and efficiency.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, as demonstrated by strong experimental results on both graph and hypergraph datasets.
- The introduction of a topology-aware score to measure the relevance of vertex features and the topology is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their implementation, and comprehensive experimental results.
- The paper addresses the significant issue of over-smoothing in HGNNs, which is a common challenge in deep learning models.
- The methodology is clearly explained, making it accessible to readers, and the paper provides a detailed analysis of the time complexity of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of why the proposed method works effectively, especially in terms of how the topology-aware distillation method enhances performance.
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily builds upon existing methods like GLNN and KRD, with the main innovation being the topology-aware distillation.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the differences between HGNNs and hypergraphs, as well as the advantages of using hypergraphs over graphs.
- The computational complexity of the proposed methods is not thoroughly analyzed, particularly in terms of the training time and the computational resources required.
- The paper could be improved by providing more detailed explanations of the experimental results, especially the results in Table 1, and clarifying the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance.
- There is a need for a more rigorous evaluation of the proposed methods, possibly including a broader range of datasets and benchmarks to validate the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of training time and computational resources?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and efficiency when applied to different types of datasets, such as those with varying numbers of vertices and edges?
- What are the specific advantages of using hypergraphs over graphs in your model, and how does the model handle the potential complexity introduced by the hypergraph structure?
- Can you clarify the impact of different hyperparameters, such as the temperature parameter and the noise ratio, on the performance of the model?
- In the experimental results, why do the results in Table 1 vary significantly across different datasets, and what does this indicate about the generalizability of the model?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of over-smoothing in HGNNs, and what are the implications of this for the model's performance and accuracy?
