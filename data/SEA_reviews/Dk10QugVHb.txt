 **Summary:**
The paper explores the causal relationship between various factors and the bias in the CLIP model, particularly focusing on the impact of protected and non-protected attributes. It introduces a synthetic dataset, CausalFace, which allows for systematic control over attributes like facial expression, lighting, and pose, enabling a more rigorous analysis of bias. The authors employ three datasets to examine the effects of different attributes on CLIP's embeddings, using metrics like cosine similarity and WEAT scores. The paper highlights the significant influence of non-protected attributes, such as facial expressions and lighting, on social perception, challenging the assumption that protected attributes are the primary drivers of bias. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in methodology, insufficient comparison with other models, and potential issues with the synthetic dataset's representativeness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and introduces a novel approach to studying the causal relationship between protected attributes and algorithmic bias using a synthetic dataset.
- The methodology is sound, and the use of a synthetic dataset allows for more controlled and precise analysis compared to traditional observational datasets.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the impact of non-protected attributes on social perception, challenging the assumption that protected attributes are the primary drivers of bias.
- The paper is the first to use a synthetic dataset to study bias in CLIP models, offering a unique perspective on how different attributes influence social perception.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its methodology, particularly in the description of how the synthetic dataset is used and the specifics of the metrics employed.
- There is a lack of comparison with other models or datasets, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for biases in the synthetic dataset, which could influence the results.
- The paper's focus on a single model (CLIP) limits the scope of the study, and the generalizability of the findings to other models or datasets is unclear.
- The paper does not provide a clear definition or explanation of the term "causal" used in the title, which could lead to confusion about the nature of the study.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the synthetic dataset is used in the analysis, particularly how the images are manipulated and how the cosine similarity is calculated?
- How does the paper ensure that the synthetic dataset does not introduce its own biases, and how are these biases accounted for in the analysis?
- Why was the decision made to focus on a single model (CLIP), and how do the findings generalize to other models or datasets?
- Could you provide more details on the metrics used in the analysis, particularly how the cosine similarity and WEAT scores are calculated and what they represent in the context of the study?
- How does the paper address the potential for spurious correlations in the synthetic dataset, and what steps were taken to mitigate these issues?
