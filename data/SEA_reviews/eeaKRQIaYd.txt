 **Summary:**
The paper introduces USLNet, an unsupervised sign language translation and generation network that leverages abundant single-modality data (text and video) without requiring parallel sign language data. It employs a sliding window method to align variable-length text with video sequences and utilizes cross-modality back-translation modules to reconstruct inputs in different modalities. The model is evaluated on the BBC-Oxford Sign Language datasets, demonstrating competitive results compared to supervised baseline models. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty, unclear methodological explanations, and insufficient experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear illustrations that aid in understanding the proposed methodologies.
- The use of unsupervised methods to address the scarcity of parallel sign language data is innovative and relevant, given the challenges in acquiring such data.
- The proposed USLNet is the first unsupervised model for sign language translation and generation, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper includes a comprehensive experimental setup and provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed components.
- The methodology is well-motivated, and the paper is well-organized, making it accessible and understandable.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as similar approaches have been previously explored in other fields such as unsupervised machine translation and unsupervised image-to-image translation.
- The paper lacks detailed explanations and justifications for certain methodological choices, such as the use of sliding window aligner and the choice of back-translation BLEU as the evaluation metric for sign language generation.
- There is a noticeable absence of comparisons with existing methods, which limits the ability to assess the relative performance of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of data scarcity, which is a significant challenge in the field of sign language translation.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincing, with insufficient details provided on the dataset and training parameters, and the results do not convincingly outperform existing methods.
- The paper suffers from several presentation issues, including grammatical errors and unclear figures and tables.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the novelty of the proposed method and how it differs from existing approaches in unsupervised machine translation and unsupervised image-to-image translation?
- Why was the sliding window aligner chosen over other possible methods, and what are the specific advantages of this approach?
- How does the paper address the issue of data scarcity, and what steps were taken to mitigate this challenge in the experimental setup?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the size of the training set and the specifics of the training parameters?
- Why was back-translation BLEU chosen as the evaluation metric for sign language generation, and how does this metric accurately reflect the quality of the generated sign language videos?
- Could you elaborate on the methodology behind the "text-video-text back-translation (T2V2T-BT)" and "video-text-video back-translation (V2T2V-BT)" modules, and how they contribute to the overall performance of the model?
