 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Dual-Prism (DP) augmentation method aimed at enhancing graph data augmentation by preserving essential graph properties while diversifying augmented graphs. The method leverages spectral analysis to focus on low-frequency eigenvalues, which are less susceptible to edge alterations, thereby maintaining graph properties. The proposed approach is validated through extensive experiments across various settings, including supervised, semi-supervised, unsupervised, and transfer learning, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on most datasets. Despite its effectiveness, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, incremental nature, and lack of theoretical support. The method's computational complexity and the need for more robust theoretical backing are highlighted as significant concerns.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the experiments are extensive, covering various settings including supervised, semi-supervised, unsupervised, and transfer learning.
- The proposed DP-Noise and DP-Mask methods are effective in graph data augmentation, as demonstrated by their ability to preserve graph properties and enhance the diversity of augmented graphs.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the interplay between graph properties, their augmentation, and their spectral behavior, which informs the development of the Dual-Prism (DP) augmentation method.
- The methodology is simple and effective, and the proposed augmentation method is well-motivated, with a focus on preserving graph properties.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the work is limited as the idea of focusing on low-frequency eigenvalues for graph augmentation has been previously explored.
- The computational complexity of the proposed method is not thoroughly discussed, which could affect its scalability, especially for large graphs.
- The paper lacks theoretical support for the effectiveness of the proposed augmentation methods, which could strengthen the argument for their efficacy.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with marginal improvements reported in many cases.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the methodology differs from existing works that also focus on low-frequency eigenvalues for graph augmentation?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed method compare to existing methods, and what are the practical implications for large-scale applications?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed augmentation methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to other spectral-based methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed method, and discuss potential directions for future research?
