 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CRL-Net, a novel deep learning method for accelerated MRI reconstruction, specifically designed for multi-coil MRI. CRL-Net integrates a coil-wise encoder with a coil-attention layer, leveraging the distinctive characteristics of each coil to enhance image reconstruction. The methodology is evaluated using the FastMRI benchmark dataset, demonstrating superior performance over existing methods in terms of image quality metrics such as SSIM, NMSE, and PSNR. The paper also discusses the limitations of the method, including the need for larger datasets and more rigorous validation by radiologists.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel deep learning method, CRL-Net, for accelerated MRI reconstruction, which leverages the unique characteristics of each coil to enhance image reconstruction.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the proposed method and its components, including the coil-wise encoder and coil-attention layer.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through comprehensive evaluations using the reputable FastMRI benchmark dataset, showing significant advancements over state-of-the-art methodologies.
- The method's ability to refine the accuracy and efficiency of MRI reconstructions is highlighted, which is crucial for medical imaging applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with more recent and relevant deep learning methods for MRI reconstruction, such as HUMUS-Net, which could provide a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The method's generalizability to other datasets and different types of MRI scans is not thoroughly explored, which is essential for demonstrating the method's effectiveness across diverse applications.
- The paper does not include qualitative results, such as visual comparisons of reconstructed images, which could provide more intuitive insights into the method's performance.
- The paper does not discuss the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical implementation in clinical settings.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to specific datasets and acceleration factors, which might not fully demonstrate the method's capabilities in real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the method, including its applicability to different types of MRI scans and the need for larger datasets for validation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other recent and relevant deep learning methods for MRI reconstruction, such as HUMUS-Net, to validate the proposed method's effectiveness more robustly?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of MRI scans, such as cardiac MRI, and what are the expected outcomes?
- Could the authors include qualitative results, such as visual comparisons of reconstructed images, to provide more intuitive insights into the method's performance?
- What are the computational costs of the proposed method, and how do they compare to other deep learning methods for MRI reconstruction?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the method and suggest areas for future research to address these limitations?
- How does the method perform when applied to other datasets, and what are the implications of using different evaluation metrics or datasets for validation?
