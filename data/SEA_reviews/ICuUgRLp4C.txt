 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HyBRiD, a novel method aimed at extracting maximally informative and minimally redundant (MIMR) high-order relationships from functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) data using a hypergraph structure. HyBRiD employs a Constructor to identify hyperedge structures and a Weighter to compute hyperedge weights, optimizing these through an information bottleneck framework. The method was evaluated on eight datasets from the Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development study, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods. Despite its innovative approach, concerns were raised about the clarity of the method's novelty, the rationale behind certain design choices, and the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem of extracting high-order relationships from fMRI data, which is crucial for understanding brain functions and predicting neurological outcomes.
- The proposed method, HyBRiD, is well-motivated and clearly described, with a focus on extracting MIMR high-order relationships from fMRI data.
- The methodology includes a novel information bottleneck framework, multi-head drop-bottleneck, which ensures the maximal informativeness and minimal redundancy of identified hyperedges.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes extensive experiments and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method is evaluated on eight datasets, which allows for a comprehensive evaluation of its performance across different conditions and tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity on the novelty of the proposed method, particularly in how it differs from existing hypergraph learning methods and how it addresses the challenges specific to fMRI data.
- The rationale behind certain design choices, such as the use of a masking mechanism and the selection of hypergraph parameters, is not well-explained.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could affect its practical applicability.
- The paper's focus on high-order relationships might be overstated, as it does not adequately address the importance of low-order relationships in understanding brain functions.
- There are issues with the clarity and organization of the paper, including unclear figures and a lack of detailed explanation in some sections.
- The paper's evaluation primarily focuses on the ABCD dataset, which might limit the generalizability of the results to other datasets or populations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the dataset?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of overfitting, especially when training on multiple datasets simultaneously?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the masking mechanism in the method, particularly in terms of its contribution to the identification of high-order relationships?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use hypergraphs for learning high-order relationships in neuroscience?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations for the design choices, such as the use of a masking mechanism and the selection of hypergraph parameters?
- How does the method handle the issue of different brain regions having varying numbers of voxels, and how does this affect the feature extraction process?
