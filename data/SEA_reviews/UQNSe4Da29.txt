 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two methods, Iterative Class Rectification (ICR) and Discontinuous Class Rectification (DCR), aimed at orthogonalizing class representations in multi-class classification tasks. These methods are designed to enhance the interpretability of class mean vectors by aligning them with coordinate axes. The proposed methods are evaluated on datasets like CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, showing comparable or slightly improved accuracy compared to existing methods. The paper also discusses the application of these methods in out-of-distribution (OOD) detection. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as it primarily builds upon existing methods, and for its experimental setup, which is considered too narrow and not sufficiently convincing.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of machine learning, focusing on the interpretability of class mean vectors.
- The proposed methods, Iterative Class Rectification (ICR) and Discontinuous Class Rectification (DCR), are well-motivated and provide a clear rationale for their application.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the methods and their theoretical underpinnings.
- The experimental results, although limited to specific datasets, show promising results in terms of accuracy and the ability to maintain or improve upon existing methods.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods, including comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and an analysis of the orthogonality and compactness of class representations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed methods compared to other orthogonalization techniques.
- The experimental setup is limited, focusing on a narrow range of datasets and not adequately addressing the scalability of the proposed methods.
- The novelty of the paper is questionable, as it primarily builds upon existing methods without significantly advancing the state of the art.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which could be a significant drawback in practical applications.
- There are several minor typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other orthogonalization techniques, including a discussion on the computational complexity and efficiency of their methods?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the Rocchio algorithm over other classification methods, and what are the implications of this choice for the interpretability of class representations?
- In the context of the experimental results, why do the results for CIFAR-100 differ significantly from those for CIFAR-10, and what does this indicate about the generalizability of the proposed methods?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the initial clustering of classes on the performance of the proposed methods?
- How does the paper address the potential biases that could be introduced by the class orthogonality, especially in terms of fairness and ethical considerations?
