 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedCompass, a semi-asynchronous federated learning algorithm designed to address the heterogeneity issue in cross-silo federated learning by dynamically assigning training tasks to clients based on their computing power. FedCompass utilizes a computing power-aware scheduler to ensure that locally trained models from clients arrive nearly simultaneously for aggregation, thereby reducing the staleness of local models and improving convergence speed. The algorithm is evaluated on several datasets and shows superior performance compared to other asynchronous algorithms. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the model in real-world scenarios, the limited dataset diversity, and the need for a more comprehensive evaluation against a broader range of baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and easy-to-follow presentation that effectively motivates the problem of cross-silo federated learning.
- The proposed FedCompass algorithm is intuitive, well-explained, and offers a novel approach to addressing the challenges of asynchronous federated learning, particularly in heterogeneous settings.
- The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of FedCompass, including a convergence analysis and empirical results demonstrating faster convergence and higher accuracy compared to other asynchronous algorithms.
- The use of a computing power-aware scheduler to dynamically assign training tasks to clients based on their computing power is a novel and practical approach that could be beneficial in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other existing methods, particularly in terms of convergence analysis and empirical results. This makes it difficult to fully understand the advantages of FedCompass over other methods.
- The practicality of the model is questionable, particularly the assumption that all clients have the same importance weight, which might not hold true in real-world scenarios.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, focusing on a few datasets and not including a comprehensive comparison with other baselines, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of client drift, which is a significant challenge in asynchronous federated learning.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need correction, which could detract from the professional quality of the paper.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role of the staleness factor in the FedCompass algorithm and how it affects the convergence of the algorithm?
- How does the proposed model handle the issue of client drift, particularly in asynchronous federated learning settings?
- Can you provide more details on the practical implementation of the model, especially in real-world scenarios where clients might have varying importance weights?
- How does the proposed model compare with other methods in terms of convergence speed and accuracy, especially when using different datasets and hyperparameter settings?
- Could you explain the rationale behind the choice of the datasets used in the experiments and consider including more diverse datasets to better validate the generalizability of the results?
