 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel control-theoretic framework to analyze the controllability of language models (LLMs) through prompt engineering. By formalizing LLMs as a class of discrete stochastic dynamical systems, the authors explore the reachable set of output token sequences and establish theoretical and empirical bounds on controllability. Theoretical results are derived for self-attention components, and empirical results demonstrate that short prompts can significantly alter output likelihoods. The paper also introduces new prompt optimization algorithms to verify the theoretical results, showing that even the least likely tokens can be made most likely with minimal prompting. Despite some limitations in the scope of analysis and the need for more comprehensive theoretical analysis, the paper is commended for its innovative approach and the insights it provides into the controllability of LLMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel perspective on prompt engineering by analyzing the controllability of language models (LLMs) using control theory, which is a less explored area in this context.
- The theoretical results presented in the paper are significant, particularly Theorem 1, which establishes a necessary condition for a desired output representation to be excluded from the reachable set.
- The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating that short prompts can significantly alter output likelihoods, even making the least likely tokens become the most likely ones.
- The paper is well-written, making complex concepts accessible, and includes both theoretical and empirical analysis, which are complementary and support the main claims.
- The introduction of new prompt optimization algorithms is a valuable contribution to the field, and the experimental results are well-presented and convincing.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's analysis is limited to the self-attention component of LLMs, which might not fully capture the complexity of the LLM's controllability.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to the self-attention module, and the reachable set of output token sequences is only analyzed for the self-attention component, which might not fully capture the complexity of the LLM's controllability.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by including more visual aids and explanations in the main text to aid in understanding complex concepts.
- The empirical results are based on a limited dataset, which might not generalize well to other datasets or models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the current work and potential directions for future research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of focusing on the self-attention component of LLMs for the theoretical analysis and its implications for the broader understanding of LLM controllability?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of the current work, such as the scope of analysis and the generalizability of the findings?
- In the context of the theoretical analysis, how does the paper's approach compare to other existing methods, and what are the implications of focusing on the self-attention component for the broader understanding of LLM controllability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the datasets used in the empirical analysis, particularly how they were selected and what steps were taken to ensure the generalizability of the findings?
- How do the authors envision the practical applications of their findings in the field of LLM controllability, and what are the potential implications for the development of more effective prompting strategies?
