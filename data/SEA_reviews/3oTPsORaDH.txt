 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SEGNO, a novel equivariant graph neural network (GNN) framework that integrates physical inductive biases to enhance the modeling of complex physical systems. SEGNO leverages second-order ODEs to model the dynamics of these systems, allowing for the prediction of continuous trajectories rather than discrete states. The authors demonstrate that SEGNO maintains equivariance properties and provides a theoretical guarantee of its generalization ability. The framework is tested against various baselines on datasets such as N-body systems, molecular dynamics, and motion capture, showing significant improvements in generalization performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel equivariant GNN framework, SEGNO, which effectively models the continuous dynamics of physical systems, offering a significant advancement in the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, including a proof of the uniqueness and boundedness of the trajectories inferred by SEGNO, which is crucial for its application in real-world scenarios.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating that SEGNO outperforms existing baselines in terms of generalization performance, as evidenced by extensive ablation studies.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methodologies and their implications.
- The integration of second-order inductive bias into GNNs is a novel approach that enhances the modeling capabilities of these networks, potentially opening new avenues for research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the second-order inductive bias is incorporated into the GNNs, particularly in terms of its impact on the equivariance properties.
- The choice of baselines seems limited, with some relevant works such as GNS not being included in the comparisons, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of SEGNO's performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of SEGNO, including potential challenges in scalability and generalization to more complex or varied physical systems.
- The paper's experimental setup and results could be improved by including more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, such as those mentioned in the related works section.
- The paper's reliance on specific numerical integrators (Euler integrators) for its approach raises concerns about the potential loss of equivariance, which could affect the overall effectiveness of the model.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the second-order inductive bias is integrated into the GNNs and discuss its impact on the equivariance properties?
- How does SEGNO compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of scalability and generalization to more complex or varied physical systems?
- Would it be possible to include a more detailed comparison with other methods, such as those mentioned in the related works section, to better understand SEGNO's relative performance?
- How does SEGNO handle the potential loss of equivariance due to the use of Euler integrators? Can the authors discuss potential solutions or modifications to address this issue?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the choice of baselines and the rationale behind their selection? Are there specific reasons why more recent or relevant works were not included in the comparisons?
