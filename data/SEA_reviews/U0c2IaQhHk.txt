 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel non-parametric online reinforcement learning (RL) algorithm, RKHS-RL, which leverages reproducing kernels to estimate the transition core using ridge regression. This algorithm is designed to handle both finite and infinite state and action spaces, balancing exploration and exploitation through infinite-dimensional confidence balls. Theoretical guarantees are provided, demonstrating that RKHS-RL achieves a sublinear regret bound of O(H√T), where T denotes the time step of the algorithm and H represents the horizon of the Markov Decision Process. The algorithm's performance is validated through simulations, showing that it outperforms existing methods like Q-Learning and SARSA in certain scenarios. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in explaining the motivation and assumptions behind the algorithm, and the experimental setup is limited to simple environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel non-parametric online RL algorithm, RKHS-RL, which leverages reproducing kernels to estimate the transition core using ridge regression. This approach is particularly useful for handling infinite state and action spaces, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper provides theoretical guarantees, demonstrating that RKHS-RL achieves a sublinear regret bound of O(H√T), where T denotes the time step of the algorithm and H represents the horizon of the Markov Decision Process. This is a significant improvement over previous methods.
- The algorithm's design is well-structured, with a clear and easy-to-follow presentation that makes it accessible to a broad audience.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is robust, and the results are well-supported by proofs and simulations, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a logical flow that facilitates understanding of the complex concepts presented.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation and explanation for the algorithm's design, particularly the choice of using infinite-dimensional confidence balls and the rationale behind the assumptions made.
- The experimental setup is limited to simple environments, which may not adequately demonstrate the algorithm's performance in more complex or realistic scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the algorithm, which is a critical factor in practical applications.
- The paper does not compare the proposed algorithm with other state-of-the-art methods, which is necessary to assess its relative performance and position within the current research landscape.
- The paper suffers from some notational errors and inconsistencies, which can confuse the reader and detract from the overall clarity of the presentation.
