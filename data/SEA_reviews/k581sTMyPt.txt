 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SUFO, a framework designed to enhance the interpretability of fine-tuned transformer models in clinical decision-making. The framework employs a range of analytical and visualization techniques, including supervised probing, unsupervised similarity analysis, feature dynamics, and outlier analysis. It examines the impact of pre-training data on fine-tuned features for clinical note classification, revealing the robustness of mixed-domain models under class imbalance, the acceleration of feature disambiguation through in-domain pre-training, and the sparsification of feature spaces. The framework is evaluated using five 110M-sized pre-trained transformer models and validated on the MedNLI dataset, demonstrating its utility in enhancing trust and safety in medical applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue of interpretability in deep learning, specifically focusing on fine-tuned transformers, which is crucial for clinical decision-making.
- The framework, SUFO, is well-articulated and includes a variety of techniques for feature analysis, such as supervised probing, unsupervised similarity analysis, feature dynamics, and outlier analysis.
- The authors conduct a thorough analysis of the impact of pre-training data on fine-tuned features, revealing insights into the robustness of different models under class imbalance and the acceleration of feature disambiguation through in-domain pre-training.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The use of domain experts in the evaluation process adds a valuable human perspective to the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and justification for the term "interpretability" used in the context of clinical decision-making, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the findings.
- The analysis is limited to a single dataset, which may not generalize well to other datasets or scenarios, and the conclusions drawn from this dataset might not be universally applicable.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the techniques used, such as the sensitivity of the clustering algorithm to the number of clusters chosen and the potential for overfitting in domain-specific models.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on how the results could be applied in real-world clinical settings, which could limit the practical impact of the research.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its findings with existing literature on feature analysis, which could help contextualize the contributions of the study.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify what is meant by "interpretability" in the context of clinical decision-making and how it is measured or evaluated within your framework?
- How do the findings from this study generalize to other datasets or scenarios, and what are the implications for the broader applicability of the techniques used?
- Could you elaborate on how the insights from your feature analysis could be practically applied in real-world clinical settings to improve patient care or outcomes?
- How sensitive is the clustering algorithm to the number of clusters chosen, and what are the implications of this sensitivity for the validity of the findings?
- Can you discuss the potential for overfitting in domain-specific models, particularly in the context of the findings presented in the paper?
- How do the conclusions drawn from the analysis of the MedNLI dataset align with those from the pathology report dataset, and what are the implications of these similarities or differences for the broader applicability of the findings?
