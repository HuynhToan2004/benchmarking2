 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the use of representation transfer in multitask imitation learning (MTIL) to improve sample efficiency in learning new tasks. It proposes a theoretical framework for analyzing the sample complexity of MTIL, focusing on the impact of task diversity on the effectiveness of representation transfer. The authors introduce a novel metric for measuring task diversity, which is positively correlated with the performance of the imitation task. Empirical results from five simulated environments demonstrate that this metric can predict the performance of the imitation task, thus providing a practical tool for evaluating the effectiveness of MTIL methods. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the sample complexity of MTIL, showing how it can be analyzed using Rademacher complexity.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the theoretical concepts and providing a comprehensive analysis of the sample complexity of multitask imitation learning (MTIL).
- The proposed task diversity metric is novel and provides a practical tool for evaluating the effectiveness of MTIL methods.
- The theoretical results are solid and provide a clear understanding of the benefits of representation transfer in MTIL.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear presentation of the theoretical analysis and empirical results.
- The experiments are well-designed and support the theoretical findings, demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's empirical results are limited to simple environments, which may not generalize well to more complex tasks.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to a specific model of representation learning, which may not be applicable to more general scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly how they can be applied to improve MTIL methods in real-world applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how it compares to other existing methods in the field.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of sample complexity in MTIL, which is a critical aspect of the study.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the number of target data affects the results?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in the field, particularly in terms of sample complexity and task diversity?
- Can the theoretical results be extended to more general scenarios, such as continuous action spaces?
- How does the paper's methodology address the issue of sample complexity in MTIL, and what are the implications of this for practical applications?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and discuss how these assumptions affect the applicability of the results?
