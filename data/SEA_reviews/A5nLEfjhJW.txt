 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SHARCS (SHARed Concept Space), a novel approach for explainable multimodal learning that maps interpretable concepts from different heterogeneous modalities into a unified concept-manifold. This approach aims to provide intuitive cross-modal concept projections, enhancing task predictions and improving performance in scenarios with missing modalities. The methodology involves learning local concepts from each modality and projecting them into a shared space, which can be used to generate explanations and visualize relationships between modalities. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its limited evaluation on small datasets and the need for a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, SHARCS, for explainable multimodal learning, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear and concise descriptions of the proposed method and its components.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear introduction of the problem, methodology, and experimental results, making it easy to follow.
- The use of visualizations, such as tSNE plots, to demonstrate the effectiveness of the SHARCS approach in reducing the modality gap is a strong point.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in multimodal learning, which is crucial for developing AI systems that can handle complex real-world problems effectively.
- The approach is model-agnostic, which makes it applicable to different types and numbers of modalities, enhancing its practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method. It only compares with one baseline, which is not sufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of SHARCS.
- The datasets used in the experiments are relatively small, which may not fully demonstrate the scalability and robustness of the SHARCS approach.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the SHARCS approach, which is essential for understanding its practical implications.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of missing modalities, which is a significant limitation of the approach.
- The paper's claims about the interpretability of the SHARCS approach are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- The paper's writing could be improved to make it more accessible to a broader audience, particularly in terms of clarifying the methodology and the experimental setup.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for explainable multimodal learning, particularly in terms of performance and interpretability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the SHARCS approach, including the time and memory requirements?
- How does the SHARCS approach handle scenarios where the number of concepts to be learned is not known in advance?
- Can the authors provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the SHARCS approach and how these limitations might affect its practical applicability?
- How does the SHARCS approach handle missing modalities, and what are the implications of this on the performance and interpretability of the model?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly in terms of the datasets used and the evaluation metrics employed?
- How does the SHARCS approach ensure that the concepts learned are semantically meaningful and interpretable, and what steps are taken to validate this?
