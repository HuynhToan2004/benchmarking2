 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the problem of learning to reject (L2R) in imbalanced datasets, where traditional methods such as Chow's rule may not be optimal. It proposes a novel plug-in approach that mimics the Bayes-optimal classifier and rejector for balanced error, aiming to improve trade-offs in terms of balanced and worst-group error. The paper also extends its analysis to other evaluation metrics, such as worst-group error, and provides theoretical insights and experimental validations. However, the paper's contributions are seen as incremental, and the experimental results do not convincingly outperform existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a relevant and timely problem, focusing on the practical application of learning to reject in imbalanced datasets, which is crucial for real-world scenarios.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, with a clear exposition of the results and their implications, which are supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the proposed methods and their theoretical underpinnings, making it accessible and understandable.
- The proposed plug-in approach is novel and practical, offering a simple yet effective solution that does not require extensive modifications to the base model.
- The paper extends the analysis to a broader set of evaluation metrics, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is seen as incremental, as the proposed methods are based on existing ideas and do not offer significant new insights or approaches.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some methods performing worse than baseline approaches, and the improvements over existing methods are not substantial.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the proposed method's advantages or disadvantages.
- The practical implications of the proposed methods, particularly the need for grid search over multipliers and the computational cost of this approach, are not adequately addressed.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, especially in the context of learning to reject and long-tail classification.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational cost and practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method compare to more recent and advanced methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of the proposed methods, especially in terms of their applicability and scalability in real-world scenarios?
- In the context of the experimental results, can the authors clarify the discrepancies between the proposed method and existing approaches, and provide a more detailed analysis of the observed improvements or lack thereof?
- How do the authors envision the practical implementation of their methods, particularly in scenarios where the number of groups is large or the computational resources are limited?
