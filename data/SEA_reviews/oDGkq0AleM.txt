 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for anomaly detection in tabular data, focusing on the variance stabilization of density estimation. The authors propose a variance-stabilized density estimation (VSDE) problem to maximize the likelihood of observed samples while minimizing the variance of the density around normal samples. This is achieved by training multiple auto-regressive probabilistic normalized networks (PNNs) with different feature permutations and using a spectral ensemble to combine their log-likelihoods. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive experiments on 52 datasets, showing state-of-the-art results. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its lack of theoretical justification, limited novelty, and insufficient discussion on the generalizability of the results beyond tabular data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The authors introduce a novel approach to anomaly detection using variance stabilization, which is supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence.
- The use of probabilistic normalized networks (PNNs) for anomaly detection is highlighted as a significant contribution, offering a new perspective on the application of these networks.
- The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on a large number of datasets, showing significant improvements over existing methods.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the results and the methodology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed method, particularly concerning the convergence and generalization of the variance-stabilized density estimation.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned, as the use of probabilistic normalized networks and the variance-stabilized density estimation are seen as incremental improvements rather than significant advancements.
- The paper primarily focuses on tabular data, limiting its applicability to other types of data, such as images or time series.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to understand the specific contributions of different components of the method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly concerning its generalizability and scalability to other data types and larger datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the relationship between the proposed method and existing anomaly detection methods, particularly those using different types of neural networks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed method, particularly concerning the convergence and generalization of the variance-stabilized density estimation?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent anomaly detection methods, particularly those using different types of neural networks?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their method and provide suggestions for future research directions?
- What are the specific contributions of the variance regularization and the spectral ensemble to the overall performance of the method?
- How does the method handle different types of anomalies, such as concept drift or data shift, and what are the implications for its practical application?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly concerning the choice of hyperparameters and the impact of different components of the method on the results?
