 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to learning acyclic graphs by proposing a differentiable relaxation of the acyclic orientation matrix, termed the smooth orientation matrix. This matrix is defined using a priority vector and a temperature parameter, which allows for a differentiable approximation of the discrete orientation matrix. The method aims to optimize this matrix to learn the adjacency matrix of a directed acyclic graph (DAG) without explicitly evaluating the acyclicity constraint. This approach is shown to be computationally efficient, with a quadratic time complexity, and empirically validated against existing methods. The paper also discusses the theoretical guarantees and practical implications of this method, including its scalability and the necessity of regularization terms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel differentiable relaxation of the acyclic orientation matrix, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is computationally efficient, with a quadratic time complexity, which is a significant improvement over previous methods.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in learning DAGs, showing improvements over existing methods in terms of both accuracy and computational efficiency.
- The paper includes a thorough theoretical analysis and empirical validation, which supports the claims made about the method's effectiveness and efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the necessity and choice of regularization terms, which are crucial for the method's effectiveness.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definition and role of the temperature parameter, which is a key component of the method.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing unconstrained methods, such as DAGUErreotype, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the method's performance.
- The paper's theoretical contributions, particularly the proofs and theorems, are not well-explained or intuitive, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the method's theoretical underpinnings.
- The paper's claims about the method's scalability and efficiency are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which do not clearly demonstrate the method's superiority over existing methods in terms of scalability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and necessity of the regularization terms in the method, and provide more detailed justification for their choice?
- How does the method perform in terms of scalability, especially in large-scale applications? Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results or comparisons to support their claims?
- How does the method compare with other unconstrained methods, particularly DAGUErreotype, in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation or proof of Theorem 1, which is a key theoretical contribution of the paper?
- How does the method ensure the acyclicity of the learned DAGs, especially in the absence of explicit acyclicity constraints?
