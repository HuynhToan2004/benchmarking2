 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel metric, the Transfer Score (TS), aimed at evaluating the performance of Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) models without the need for target-domain labels. The TS metric is designed to assess the uniformity of the classifier and the transferability and discriminability of deep representations, enabling unsupervised evaluation of UDA models. The metric's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive experiments on various datasets, showing that it can be used for model selection, hyperparameter tuning, and checkpoint selection. However, the paper is criticized for lacking a theoretical foundation and detailed justification for the choice of metrics, and for not providing sufficient comparisons with existing metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel metric, the Transfer Score (TS), which is applicable to UDA model evaluation without requiring target-domain labels, a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed TS metric is demonstrated to be effective in model selection, hyperparameter tuning, and checkpoint selection, as shown through extensive experiments on various datasets.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and the experiments are comprehensive, covering a wide range of UDA methods and datasets.
- The TS metric is innovative in its approach to evaluating model performance without the need for target-domain labels, which is a common challenge in real-world scenarios.
- The authors have provided a detailed analysis of the TS metric's effectiveness, including a comparison with existing metrics and an ablation study that shows the contribution of each component of the metric.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed justification for the choice of metrics used in the TS, particularly the Hopkins statistic and mutual information, which are crucial components of the metric.
- There is a lack of theoretical foundation for the proposed metric, which could limit its acceptance in the academic community.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which is a significant concern in UDA, particularly when using the TS metric for model selection.
- The experiments are limited to specific UDA methods and datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the TS metric across different scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing metrics, particularly in terms of how the TS metric performs in different scenarios and how it compares in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability.
- There are several typographical errors and inconsistencies in the paper, which could detract from its professional presentation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed justification for the choice of metrics used in the TS, particularly the Hopkins statistic and mutual information?
- How does the TS metric address the issue of overfitting in UDA, and what steps have been taken to ensure that the model selected using the TS is not overfitted?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of the TS metric, and how it compares to existing metrics in terms of its effectiveness and practical applicability?
- How does the TS metric perform in scenarios where the data distribution is highly imbalanced, and what steps have been taken to ensure that the metric remains effective in such scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing metrics, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- What are the implications of using the TS metric for model selection in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
