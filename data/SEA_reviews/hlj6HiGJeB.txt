 **Summary:**
The paper introduces NeuralMatrix, a framework that transforms the computation of deep neural networks (DNNs) into linear matrix operations, enabling their execution on a single general-purpose matrix multiplication (GEMM) accelerator. This approach aims to enhance computational efficiency by reducing the reliance on diverse hardware units for different computation types, thereby simplifying hardware design for DNNs. The framework includes a piecewise linearization method to approximate nonlinear operations and is evaluated across various DNN architectures, including CNNs, Transformers, and GNNs. The results demonstrate that NeuralMatrix can achieve a 49.58x improvement in throughput-per-watt compared to CPUs, 8.50x compared to GPUs, and 2.35x compared to SoCs. However, the paper has been criticized for its limited evaluation scope, lack of comparisons with other nonlinear approximation methods, and insufficient discussion on the limitations and scalability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, NeuralMatrix, which transforms the computation of DNNs into linear matrix operations, enabling their execution on a single GEMM accelerator, thereby reducing hardware design complexity for DNNs.
- The piecewise linearization method used in NeuralMatrix is innovative and has been validated through main-stream DNNs and their variant models, showing a 1.32% accuracy loss on average.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of NeuralMatrix, comparing it to general-purpose processors like CPUs and GPUs, as well as to application-specific accelerators, demonstrating its superior computational efficiency.
- The methodology of NeuralMatrix is well-explained, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the framework's design and implementation.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear structure that effectively communicates the framework's goals and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation of NeuralMatrix is limited in scope, focusing primarily on three widely-used DNN backbone models and their variant models, which may not fully demonstrate the framework's effectiveness across a broader range of DNN architectures.
- There is a lack of comparisons with other nonlinear approximation methods, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of NeuralMatrix's advantages and limitations.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of NeuralMatrix, which could help readers understand its applicability and potential challenges in practical scenarios.
- The scalability of NeuralMatrix is not adequately explored, particularly in relation to the size and complexity of the DNNs it can handle.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the hardware design of the GEMM accelerator used in the evaluation, which could be crucial for understanding the framework's effectiveness and applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the hardware design of the GEMM accelerator used in the evaluation, including its architecture and performance metrics?
- How does the accuracy loss of NeuralMatrix compare to other nonlinear approximation methods, and what are the implications of this comparison for the practical applicability of NeuralMatrix?
- Can the authors elaborate on the limitations of NeuralMatrix and discuss potential challenges in scaling the framework to handle larger and more complex DNNs?
- Given the focus on general-purpose matrix multiplication accelerators, how does NeuralMatrix address the potential challenges associated with the scalability and flexibility of these accelerators in handling a wide range of DNN architectures and sizes?
- Could the authors provide additional experimental results or comparisons that could help clarify the impact of NeuralMatrix on hardware design complexity and its scalability?
