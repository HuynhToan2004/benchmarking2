 **Summary:**
The paper introduces OSD-DICE, a novel offline reinforcement learning (RL) method aimed at addressing the challenge of distributional shift and value overestimation. It proposes a new approach by regularizing the density ratio directly on an optimal supported dataset, which is expected to reduce bias towards the optimization objective. This method is supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results showing improved performance on D4RL benchmarks compared to baseline methods. The paper also discusses the potential of this method in improving the performance of existing offline RL algorithms. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, the clarity of the theoretical explanations, and the practical applicability of the assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and theoretical analysis.
- The proposed method, OSD-DICE, is novel and has been theoretically analyzed, showing good empirical performance.
- The method is simple and intuitive, which could make it easier for practitioners to implement.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the proposed method, which is supported by empirical results showing improved performance on D4RL benchmarks compared to baseline methods.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of offline reinforcement learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the optimal supported dataset, which is crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it seems to be a modification of existing methods like OptiDICE, with similar theoretical guarantees.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the method, particularly concerning the assumptions made and their practical applicability.
- The theoretical analysis and proofs in the paper are not well-explained, making it difficult for readers to follow and understand the theoretical contributions.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental evidence to support the claims made, particularly concerning the effectiveness of the method in more complex or realistic settings.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the method, which are important factors for practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a clearer definition and explanation of the optimal supported dataset and how it is constructed?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods like OptiDICE in terms of performance and theoretical guarantees?
- Can you clarify the role and impact of the regularization term in the method, particularly how it affects the optimization process?
- How do the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis translate to practical scenarios, and what are the implications if these assumptions are not met?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the dataset used and the experimental conditions, to better understand the results?
- How does the method handle the computational complexity and scalability issues, particularly in more complex or large-scale settings?
- Can you discuss the limitations of the method and suggest potential directions for future research to address these limitations?
