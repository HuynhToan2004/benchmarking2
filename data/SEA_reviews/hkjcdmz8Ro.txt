 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PAIR, a novel method for generating adversarial prompts that can "jailbreak" large language models (LLMs) by exploiting their vulnerabilities to harmful or objectionable content. PAIR operates by iteratively refining prompts through a two-stage process involving an attacker LLM that generates prompts based on feedback from a target LLM. This method is designed to be efficient, requiring fewer queries than traditional methods, and can be applied to both open-source and closed-source LLMs. The paper also discusses the challenges in defining what constitutes a "jailbroken" LLM and the potential for transferability of attacks across different models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for generating adversarial prompts that can "jailbreak" large language models (LLMs) with fewer queries than previous methods.
- The methodology is well-explained, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- The use of two LLMs, an attacker and a target, to generate adversarial prompts is a novel approach that has shown promising results in transferability across different models.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including comparisons with existing methods and ablation studies.
- The potential applications of the method in red teaming and security testing are significant and could have a substantial impact on the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining what constitutes a "jailbroken" LLM, which could lead to confusion and inconsistencies in the evaluation of the attack's success.
- The reliance on the quality and diversity of the attacker LLM's responses could limit the generalizability and effectiveness of the method.
- The method's effectiveness might be limited by the specific prompts used, and the paper does not discuss how sensitive the method is to variations in these prompts.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the transferability of the attacks across different models and the factors that influence this transferability.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the specific prompts used in the experiments, which could limit the reproducibility and evaluation of the method.
- The paper does not discuss the potential ethical implications of the method, particularly in terms of its potential misuse or the societal impact of the attacks it generates.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how they define a "jailbroken" LLM and discuss the implications of this definition on the evaluation of the attack's success?
- How does the method ensure that the attacker LLM's responses are diverse enough to effectively explore the space of possible prompts?
- Could the authors discuss the sensitivity of the method to variations in the attacker LLM's prompts and how this might affect the transferability of the attacks?
- How does the method ensure that the generated prompts are interpretable and explainable, especially in the context of the adversarial examples they produce?
- Could the authors provide more details on the specific prompts used in the experiments, including how they were chosen and how they were varied across different models?
- How does the method handle the potential ethical implications of the attacks it generates, and what steps have been taken to mitigate these risks?
