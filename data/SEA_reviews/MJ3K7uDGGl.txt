 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge distillation (KD) by proposing a temperature scaling method on the teacher side only, termed Transformed Teacher Matching (TTM) and Weighted Transformed Teacher Matching (WTTM). This approach is theoretically justified by showing that it is equivalent to KD with an additional Renyi entropy regularization term. The paper also explores the use of sample-adaptive weighting coefficients to improve the student model's matching to the teacher's probability distribution. Extensive experiments across various datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, showing improvements over traditional KD methods. However, concerns were raised regarding the fairness of comparisons due to the different settings used in the experiments, and the paper's clarity in explaining certain theoretical contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge distillation by proposing a temperature scaling method on the teacher side only, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The theoretical justification provided for this approach is robust, showing that it is equivalent to KD with an additional Renyi entropy regularization term, which enhances the understanding of the distillation process.
- Extensive experiments are conducted across various datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods in improving the student model's performance.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental results are convincing, showing improvements over traditional KD methods.
- The introduction of a sample-adaptive weighting coefficient into the distillation process is a novel and effective approach that enhances the student model's capability to match the teacher's probability distribution.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the theoretical contributions and the derivation of certain equations.
- There are inconsistencies in the experimental settings, such as the use of different teacher models and student models in different experiments, which could affect the fairness of the comparisons.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper's claims about the equivalence of TTM to KD with an additional Renyi entropy regularization term are not convincingly supported, and the proofs provided are not clear or rigorous.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and significance of the Renyi entropy term in the distillation process? How does it contribute to the overall performance of the student model?
- In the experiments involving different teacher-student pairs, how were the hyperparameters (e.g., T, β) selected, and what impact do they have on the performance?
- Why was the decision made to use different teacher and student models in different experiments, and how does this affect the fairness of the comparisons?
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed methods, especially in comparison to traditional KD methods?
- How do the authors address the concerns regarding the fairness of the comparisons due to different experimental settings?
- In the proof of equation 11, there appears to be an inconsistency in the handling of entropy terms. Could you clarify this?
- The paper mentions the use of sample-adaptive weighting coefficients. Could you elaborate on how these coefficients are determined and how they affect the distillation process?
