 **Summary:**
The paper explores the emergent equivariance in deep ensembles under data augmentation, leveraging Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) theory. The authors demonstrate that in the large width limit, deep ensembles become equivariant to the orthogonal representation of the group G, without requiring the ensemble members to be equivariant. This equivariance holds for both finite and infinite width networks and is validated through experiments on datasets like FashionMNIST and the Ising model. The paper also discusses the limitations of the ensemble size and the approximation of continuous symmetry groups, suggesting that these factors may affect the equivariance of the ensemble.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear introduction to the necessary background and a logical structure that makes it accessible to readers.
- The theoretical results are sound and well-supported by experiments, which are conducted on both toy and real-world datasets, demonstrating the practical applicability of the theoretical findings.
- The concept of emergent equivariance in deep ensembles is novel and significant, particularly in the context of deep learning for symmetry-related tasks.
- The paper provides a detailed discussion on the limitations of the theoretical analysis, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples in the experimental section, particularly regarding the datasets and the computations involved.
- There is a lack of comparison with other methods, such as symmetrization or canonicalization, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the effectiveness of the ensemble approach.
- The paper primarily focuses on equivariance, which might limit its applicability to other types of symmetries or tasks that require different types of symmetry.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, especially in terms of how they can be applied to real-world problems.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the term "ensemble" as used in the paper, particularly whether it refers to a set of ensemble members or a single ensemble?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the NTK theory for analyzing deep ensembles, and what are the implications of using this approach?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computations involved in the experiments, especially how the NTK is computed and how the ensemble size affects the results?
- How does the paper address the issue of finite width corrections in the NTK theory, and what are the implications of these corrections for the practical applicability of the theoretical results?
