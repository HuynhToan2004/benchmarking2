 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Topology-Invariant Pooling (TIP), aimed at integrating persistent homology (PH) with graph pooling layers to enhance the expressiveness and interpretability of graph neural networks (GNNs). TIP aligns the filtration operation in PH with the graph pooling process, allowing message passing along persistent sub-topology. This approach is demonstrated to improve performance across various graph classification tasks, showing significant gains over existing methods. The method's theoretical underpinnings and experimental validations support its efficacy, although concerns are raised regarding its scalability and the clarity of its integration with existing GNNs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel methodology that effectively integrates persistent homology with graph pooling layers.
- The proposed method is simple, efficient, and effective, with a simple loss function that is differentiable and can be applied to any graph pooling method.
- The paper demonstrates the method's ability to preserve topological information during pooling and enhances the performance of graph classification tasks.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method can outperform existing baselines in graph classification tasks, and the code is provided for reproducibility.
- The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for the method's effectiveness and demonstrates its superiority over existing dense pooling methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The experimental validation is limited to a few datasets, and the method's scalability and applicability to larger and more complex datasets are not adequately addressed.
- The integration of persistent homology with GNNs is not clearly explained, and the paper does not provide sufficient implementation details, making it difficult to reproduce the results.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods for graph pooling, which could provide a more robust evaluation of its effectiveness.
- The paper does not address how the method handles graphs with different topological structures, which could affect its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity and the memory requirements of the proposed method, which are crucial for assessing its scalability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the computational complexity and memory requirements of the proposed method, especially in comparison to existing methods?
- How does the proposed method handle graphs with different topological structures, and what are the implications for its performance in such scenarios?
- Could the authors provide a detailed explanation of how persistent homology is integrated with GNNs, and what are the specific benefits of this integration?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods for graph pooling in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the number of training epochs and the learning rate schedule used in the experiments?
- What are the specific advantages of using the proposed method over existing methods in terms of interpretability and performance?
