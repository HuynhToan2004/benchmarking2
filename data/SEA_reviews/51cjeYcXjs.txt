 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for representing malware binaries using data dependency graphs (DDGs) and their hashes, aiming to capture semantic similarity and structural properties. This approach is used to perform search and retrieval of binary executable programs based on similarity of behavioral properties, with an adjustable level of feature resolution. The methodology involves extracting DDGs, encoding each program as a set of graph hashes, and using k-Nearest Neighbors (k-NN) to search in a metric space constructed from examples. The paper claims to have demonstrated the effectiveness of this method in recognizing patterns in novel malware with functionality not previously identified. However, the paper is critiqued for its lack of clarity in methodological details, insufficient experimental validation, and inadequate comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for representing malware binaries using data dependency graphs (DDGs) and their hashes, which is a promising approach for capturing both semantic similarity and structural properties of malware.
- The methodology is clearly explained, with a detailed description of the data collection process and feature construction, which aids in understanding the proposed method.
- The paper provides a good overview of related work, positioning the research within the current landscape of malware analysis and machine learning.
- The use of k-NN for search in a metric space constructed from examples is a good idea, and the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach in recognizing patterns in novel malware.
- The approach of using DDGs to represent malware binaries is innovative and potentially useful for malware analysis, especially in identifying functional similarities between malware samples.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the methodological details, particularly in the construction of data dependency graphs and the segmentation of basic blocks. This makes it difficult to understand the actual implementation of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup is insufficient, with a limited number of samples and a lack of validation against existing methods. The paper does not include a comprehensive comparison with other feature extraction methods or baseline approaches, which is crucial for evaluating the effectiveness and novelty of the proposed method.
- The paper suffers from several typographical errors and lacks a formal mathematical notation, which makes it difficult to follow and understand the methodology and results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims made, particularly concerning the generalizability and effectiveness of the proposed method in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of malware obfuscation and its impact on the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the construction of data dependency graphs, specifically how operands are defined and how the graph is constructed from the data movement operations?
- How does the proposed method handle the segmentation of basic blocks, and what are the criteria used for segmenting the code into basic blocks?
- Can you clarify the use of k-NN in your methodology and how it contributes to the overall effectiveness of the method?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially when dealing with large datasets or more complex malware samples?
- Could you provide more evidence or examples to support the claims made in the paper, particularly concerning the generalizability and effectiveness of the method in real-world scenarios?
- How does the method handle malware obfuscation, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in applying the method to such malware samples?
