 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for identifying adversarial attacks in a white-box setting, where adversaries have full access to model architecture and parameters. The methodology involves embedding an implicit watermark into the model during training, which can be detected by analyzing the output distribution of adversarial examples. The proposed framework uses two metrics, KL divergence and ratio metric, to determine the similarity of the output distribution between adversarial examples and the original data. Extensive experiments on various datasets and models demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in identifying adversarial examples. However, concerns are raised about the robustness of the method against adaptive attacks and the generalizability of the findings to other types of adversarial attacks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and practical, with a straightforward implementation that does not require significant modifications to existing models.
- The method can be used in both white-box and black-box settings, enhancing its applicability in various scenarios.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing promising results across different datasets and model architectures.
- The paper provides a novel approach to identifying adversarial attacks by focusing on the output distribution of adversarial examples, which is a less explored area in adversarial robustness research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its robustness against adaptive attacks and its generalizability to other types of adversarial attacks.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on untargeted attacks and not including a wide range of adversarial attacks and defenses, which could limit the understanding of the method's effectiveness in diverse scenarios.
- There is a lack of detailed explanation and justification for the choice of specific hyperparameters, such as the blended ratio and the number of fingerprint samples, which could affect the performance and robustness of the method.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with existing methods and a deeper analysis of the trade-offs between model performance and adversarial detection accuracy.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability and computational efficiency of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical deployments.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost and scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the proposed method perform against adaptive attacks, and what measures can be taken to enhance its robustness against such attacks?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of specific hyperparameters and their impact on the performance and robustness of the method?
- In the experimental setup, how does the method perform against targeted attacks, and what are the implications of this performance for the practical applicability of the method?
- The paper mentions the use of a single adversarial example for identification. How does the method handle scenarios where multiple adversarial examples are present, and what are the implications of this for the overall effectiveness of the method?
