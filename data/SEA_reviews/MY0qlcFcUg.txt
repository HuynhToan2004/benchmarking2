 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for enhancing diffusion models by incorporating multi-task learning (MTL) through a task-routing strategy. This approach, named Denoising Task Routing (DTR), selectively activates specific channels in the diffusion model, leveraging the inherent task affinity between adjacent timesteps. DTR aims to improve the performance of diffusion models by integrating prior knowledge of denoising tasks, thereby reducing negative transfer and enhancing the model's ability to handle various denoising tasks effectively. The method is tested on multiple datasets, including FFHQ, ImageNet, and MS-COCO, and the results show significant improvements over existing models. However, the paper's novelty and the clarity of its methodological contributions are questioned, with concerns about the generalizability of the method and the need for more extensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its implementation.
- The proposed method, Denoising Task Routing (DTR), is simple yet effective, with significant improvements in performance demonstrated across various datasets.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed method, including ablation studies and visualizations, which help in understanding the method's effects.
- The method is compatible with other multi-task learning loss weighting techniques, enhancing its applicability and versatility.
- The paper demonstrates that the proposed method can be integrated into existing diffusion models with minimal modifications, making it practical for real-world applications.
- The method shows improved training speed, which is a significant advantage in practical scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is questioned as similar task-routing techniques have been previously used in other contexts, such as in the training of neural networks for different tasks.
- The method's generalizability is limited to diffusion models, which might not be applicable to other types of models.
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for why the proposed method is effective, especially in terms of its impact on the negative transfer issue.
- The method's effectiveness is not clearly demonstrated in all experimental setups, particularly in scenarios where the method's performance is not significantly better than existing methods.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods to better establish the method's advantages.
- The method's computational cost and memory requirements are not discussed, which could be a concern for large-scale applications.
- The paper could provide more detailed visualizations and examples to better illustrate the method's effects and its impact on the generated images.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the novelty of the proposed method in relation to existing task-routing techniques?
- How does the proposed method address the negative transfer issue specifically, and what is the rationale behind its effectiveness?
- Can the proposed method be adapted to other types of models beyond diffusion models? If so, how might the implementation need to be modified?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of tasks beyond denoising, such as generation or classification?
- Could you provide more details on the computational cost and memory requirements of the proposed method, especially in large-scale applications?
- Can you provide more visualizations and examples to better illustrate the method's effects and its impact on the generated images?
- How does the method compare in terms of training speed and convergence rate with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in large-scale applications?
