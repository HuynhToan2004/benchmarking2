 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Disentangled Slot Attention (DISA), which aims to disentangle shape and texture information in object-centric learning. DISA builds upon Invariant Slot Attention (ISA) by introducing a Sobel filter to separate texture and shape information in the latent space. The method is tested on several synthetic datasets, demonstrating improved disentanglement and reconstruction capabilities compared to existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the method's applicability to real-world data and its potential to outperform existing state-of-the-art methods like ISA. The paper is praised for its clarity and the novelty of its approach, but criticized for its limited evaluation on only synthetic datasets and the lack of comparisons with more advanced baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for disentangling shape and texture information in object-centric learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is clearly explained, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed description of the experimental setup, enhancing reproducibility.
- The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation, including both quantitative and qualitative results, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear structure and a good balance between theoretical background and practical application, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method shows promising results in disentangling shape and texture, which is a significant challenge in object-centric learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily evaluates the method on synthetic datasets, which limits the generalizability of the findings to real-world applications.
- The method's performance is not significantly better than existing methods like ISA, and in some cases, it performs worse.
- The paper lacks comparisons with more advanced baselines, such as those using GANs or other advanced models, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on a Sobel filter to separate texture and shape information raises concerns about the robustness of the disentanglement, particularly in scenarios where the filter may not be effective.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of positional invariance, which is crucial for object-centric learning.
- The paper's experimental results are not convincing, with some results, such as the CLEVRTex dataset, showing poor performance.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the rationale behind the choice of the Sobel filter for separating texture and shape information?
- How does the method ensure that the disentanglement of shape and texture information is robust across different datasets and scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the hyperparameters used and the training details, to enhance reproducibility?
- How does the method perform on real-world datasets, and what are the implications of using a method trained on synthetic data for real-world applications?
- Could the authors explain the discrepancies in the performance of the method on different datasets, particularly the poor performance on CLEVRTex?
- What are the implications of the method's reliance on a fixed number of slots for disentanglement, and how does this affect the generalization of the method to different datasets?
