 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel federated learning (FL) method called Local Superior Soups (LSS), which leverages pre-trained models to address communication round reduction in FL. LSS utilizes model interpolation and regularization techniques to maintain model diversity and affinity, thereby improving convergence rates and reducing communication rounds. The methodology is validated through experiments on various datasets, showing improvements over existing methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticisms regarding its novelty, clarity in writing, and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach to reducing communication rounds in federated learning by leveraging model interpolation and regularization techniques, which is novel and well-motivated.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in reducing communication rounds and improving model performance.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and provides a detailed analysis of the methodology, including the use of model soups and regularization techniques.
- The proposed method shows potential for reducing communication rounds in federated learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related works, particularly those that have explored similar ideas in federated learning.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with more detailed explanations of the proposed method and clearer figures and tables.
- There is a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which makes it difficult to assess the true effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper suffers from some inconsistencies and unclear statements, which could confuse readers and detract from the credibility of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of computational resources and memory requirements.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the affinity and diversity terms in the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use pre-trained models in federated learning, such as FedFTG?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of parameters, such as the number of local steps and the learning rate, in the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of scalability, particularly in terms of computational resources and memory requirements?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods to better establish the effectiveness of their proposed method?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of client drift, and what are the implications of this in terms of model performance and convergence rates?
