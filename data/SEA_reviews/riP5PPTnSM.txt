 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LMORT, a novel approach to dense retrieval that leverages the output of a frozen large language model (LLM) to enhance retrieval performance without altering the internal states of the LLM. The methodology involves analyzing the alignment and uniformity of LLM's hidden states, which are then used to fine-tune a lightweight retriever. The authors demonstrate that this approach can effectively balance alignment and uniformity in dense retrieval tasks, showing improvements over existing methods like DPR and GTR. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the method, as it primarily fine-tunes a lightweight retriever on top of a frozen LLM, and its performance improvements over existing methods are marginal.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a good balance of theoretical analysis and practical implementation.
- The proposed method, LMORT, is innovative in its approach to dense retrieval by leveraging the output of a frozen LLM, which is a novel and effective way to balance alignment and uniformity in dense retrieval tasks.
- The method is shown to be efficient in terms of training time and parameter count, making it a practical solution for real-world applications.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method across multiple datasets and models, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.
- The authors have conducted extensive ablation studies to understand the impact of different components of the model, which helps in validating the design choices and enhancing the reproducibility of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the selection of the optimal alignment and uniformity layers, which is a critical aspect of the methodology. The rationale behind the selection and its impact on the results are not adequately explained.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it primarily involves fine-tuning a lightweight retriever on top of a frozen LLM, which has been explored in previous works.
- The performance improvements over existing methods are marginal, and the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art dense retrievers like GTR or CPT-Text.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definition and usage of terms such as "uniformity" and "alignment," which could confuse readers and undermine the credibility of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its scalability and applicability to different types of LLMs and retrieval tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the selection of the optimal alignment and uniformity layers, including the criteria used for selection and the impact of this choice on the results?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art dense retrievers like GTR and CPT-Text in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the definition and usage of terms such as "uniformity" and "alignment" to ensure that their meaning is consistent throughout the paper?
- Given the marginal performance improvements over existing methods, what are the key advantages of the proposed method that make it a compelling choice for dense retrieval tasks?
- How does the proposed method perform on more recent and larger LLMs, such as GPT-4 or Falcon, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in applying the method to these models?
