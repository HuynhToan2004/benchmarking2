 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the data contamination issue in large language models (LLMs) by proposing a method to detect contamination at both the instance and partition levels. The authors use a guided instruction prompt to generate completions from a random portion of a dataset instance and compare these with general instructions to assess contamination. This method involves using metrics like BLEURT and ROUGE-L to measure semantic similarity and statistical significance, respectively. The paper also explores the use of GPT-4 as a classifier to detect contamination, comparing its results with human annotations. The proposed method is evaluated across multiple datasets and models, including GPT-4 and GPT-3.5, and shows promising results in identifying contaminated data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue of data contamination in large language models (LLMs), which is crucial for ensuring the reliability and validity of research in the field.
- The proposed method is straightforward, easy to implement, and does not require extensive computational resources, making it accessible for practical applications.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The evaluation is extensive, covering multiple datasets and models, including GPT-4 and GPT-3.5, and comparing the proposed method with human annotations, which strengthens the credibility of the findings.
- The paper introduces an innovative approach by leveraging LLMs to detect contamination, which could be a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The methodology is limited to detecting contamination from specific datasets that the LLM has seen during pre-training, which might not generalize well to other types of contamination such as metadata or non-textual data.
- The evaluation is based on a small number of instances per dataset, which might not be representative of the entire dataset and could lead to biased results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The use of GPT-4 as a classifier raises concerns about the reliability and fairness of the results, as it might be biased towards its own training data.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other methods or baseline models, which could help in understanding the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the method performs with different LLM sizes and training data, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when the LLM has not been trained on the dataset?
- Can the method be adapted to detect other types of contamination, such as metadata or non-textual data?
- How does the method perform with different LLM sizes and training data?
- Could you provide more details on the evaluation setup, particularly how the 10 instances per dataset were selected and how the results were validated?
- How do the authors address the potential biases in using GPT-4 as a classifier, especially considering its own training data?
- Would it be possible to include a comparison with other methods or baseline models to better understand the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method?
