 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CAIAC, a data augmentation method aimed at enhancing the generalization capabilities of offline learning algorithms in robotic tasks. By leveraging causal action influence (CAI) as a local causal measure, CAIAC swaps causally unaffected parts of the state-space from different observed trajectories to generate counterfactual data. This method is designed to improve generalization and sample efficiency in high-dimensional environments by reducing the reliance on heuristics for causal discovery. The approach is evaluated in various robotic environments, including the Franka-Kitchen and Fetch-Push, demonstrating its effectiveness in out-of-distribution tasks and in scenarios with limited data availability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and understandable language, making it accessible to readers.
- The methodology is well-explained, with a clear distinction between the proposed approach and existing methods.
- The empirical results are robust, showing that the proposed method, CAIAC, significantly outperforms existing methods in certain scenarios, particularly in high-dimensional environments.
- The paper is innovative, combining causal action influence (CAI) with counterfactual data augmentation, which is a novel approach.
- The experimental setup is comprehensive, including a variety of environments and tasks, which helps to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental results are not consistently superior across all environments and tasks, which raises questions about the generalizability of the method.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- The paper does not adequately address how the method performs in scenarios where the assumption of sparse interactions between entities is not met, which could limit its applicability.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed visualizations and explanations of the causal relationships and state-action pairs used in the experiments.

**Questions:**
- How does the performance of CAIAC vary when the threshold θ is changed? What are the optimal settings for different environments?
- Can the method be extended to handle more complex environments, such as those with a larger number of entities or more intricate causal relationships?
- How does the method perform when the assumption of sparse interactions between entities is not met? Are there any scenarios where CAIAC might not be effective?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency and scalability of the method, especially in comparison to other existing methods?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the causal influence is not clearly defined, such as in environments with complex or non-linear causal relationships?
- Is there a possibility to include more detailed visualizations or explanations of the causal relationships and state-action pairs used in the experiments to enhance the reader's understanding?
