 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the robustness of reinforcement learning policies trained with and without rewards in high-dimensional state representation Markov Decision Processes (MDPs). It demonstrates that policies trained without rewards are more robust against adversarial perturbations and deviations from optimal trajectories. The study contrasts the robustness of these policies with those trained using inverse Q-learning, which learns both a policy and a reward function from expert trajectories. Theoretical insights are provided on the subspace structure of Q-functions learned by inverse Q-learning, showing how perturbations in the subspace can lead to uncorrelated reward predictions. The paper also explores the implications of these findings for the alignment of learned policies with true rewards, suggesting that policies trained without rewards may be more robust but less aligned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic of understanding the robustness of reinforcement learning (RL) policies, which is crucial for ensuring the reliability and safety of RL systems in real-world applications.
- The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and provides a thorough literature review, enhancing its readability and comprehensiveness.
- The theoretical analysis is solid and well-presented, providing a strong foundation for the empirical results.
- The empirical results are well-executed, showing a clear trend in the robustness of policies trained without rewards compared to those trained using inverse Q-learning.
- The paper raises significant questions about the robustness of RL policies and the alignment of learned policies with true rewards, which are crucial for the development of safe and effective RL systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution to the field is somewhat limited as it primarily combines existing ideas from robustness in RL and inverse RL, without significantly advancing the state of the art.
- The theoretical results, while interesting, are not particularly novel and do not significantly advance the understanding of the subspace structure of Q-functions learned by inverse Q-learning.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly robust, with some results (e.g., the correlation between predicted and true rewards) being counterintuitive or difficult to interpret.
- The paper's focus on policies trained without rewards being more robust could be misleading, as it does not consider the potential benefits of policies trained with rewards, such as better alignment with true rewards and potentially better generalization.
- The paper's claims about the robustness of policies trained without rewards are not well-supported by the empirical results, which do not convincingly demonstrate that these policies are more robust in all scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the discrepancy between the theoretical predictions and the empirical results, particularly in the correlation between predicted and true rewards?
- How do you interpret the counterintuitive results in Figure 1, where the correlation between predicted and true rewards decreases as deviations from the optimal trajectory increase?
- What are the implications of the findings for the practical deployment of RL systems, especially in terms of ensuring robustness and alignment with true rewards?
- How do you address the potential limitations of the policies trained without rewards, such as their generalization ability and alignment with true rewards?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the hyperparameters used and the environments tested, to better understand the conditions under which the robustness of policies trained without rewards is demonstrated?
