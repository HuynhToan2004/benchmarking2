 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for causal inference in continuous settings, leveraging a model that integrates both interventional and observational data to estimate causal effects. The approach involves a "twinned" system where interventional and observational data are processed together, and the model includes a regularization term to address the issue of stiffness in the ODEs. The paper also proposes a method to handle missing data and provides theoretical justification for the use of observational data. The methodology is validated through experiments on benchmarks such as Half-Cheetah and tumor growth data, demonstrating the model's ability to handle complex dynamics and confounding effects.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach for causal inference in continuous settings, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors have provided a theoretical justification for the use of observational data in continuous settings, which is a notable advancement.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is versatile and can be applied to various real-world problems, such as medical treatments, which are relevant and timely.
- The paper includes comprehensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in handling complex dynamics and confounding effects.
- The use of a regularization term to address the issue of stiffness in the ODEs is a clever solution that enhances the model's robustness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the regularization term and its impact on the model's performance, especially in terms of computational efficiency and the handling of stiff ODEs.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the ethical implications of the work, which is crucial for a field that deals with real-world applications.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the challenges of handling missing data and the assumptions made regarding the availability and quality of data.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the handling of complex dynamics.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous discussion on the assumptions made about the data, such as the availability and quality of observational and interventional data.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the regularization term and its impact on the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of stiffness in the ODEs, and what are the implications for computational efficiency?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and its applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the model handle the ethical implications of its work, particularly in terms of data privacy and patient consent?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the handling of complex dynamics?
- How does the model handle missing data, and what assumptions are made about the availability and quality of data?
