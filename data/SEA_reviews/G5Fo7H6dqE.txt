 **Summary:**
The paper introduces iDQN, a novel variant of the DQN algorithm designed to enhance learning by performing multiple Bellman iterations in parallel, thereby improving the learning process. The authors propose a method where the Q-value function is updated multiple times in each gradient step, aiming to reduce the approximation error. Theoretical analysis and empirical results on the Atari benchmark demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness. However, the paper is criticized for its incremental nature, lack of significant novelty, and potential overfitting issues. The reviewers suggest that the method's effectiveness is limited by the number of Bellman iterations performed and the complexity of the environment.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear motivation for the research.
- The proposed method is simple and intuitive, making it easy to understand.
- The theoretical analysis is solid and the empirical results are promising, showing that the method outperforms DQN on the Atari benchmark.
- The paper provides a novel perspective on iterative learning in DQN, which is well-articulated and supported by empirical evidence.
- The method is original in its approach to handling the Bellman operator, which is a significant contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not adequately compare it with other state-of-the-art methods.
- The method's effectiveness is limited by the number of Bellman iterations performed, and the empirical results suggest that the method's performance saturates quickly.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the proposed method, which could be a significant drawback in practical applications.
- The theoretical analysis is based on simplifying assumptions that may not hold in more complex environments, such as the Atari benchmark.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which is a known problem in deep reinforcement learning.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the method's scalability and generalization to more complex environments.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancy in the number of samples seen by the algorithm as depicted in Figure 9?
- How does the method perform in environments where the Bellman operator is more complex, such as those with stochastic transitions or sparse rewards?
- What is the computational cost of the proposed method compared to DQN, and how does this affect the practical applicability of the method?
- How does the method handle the issue of overfitting, and what measures have been taken to prevent this in the experiments?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the rolling step frequency and the number of Bellman iterations, and how these affect the method's performance?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle non-Markovian environments, and how might this affect the method's effectiveness?
