 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Spatially-Aware Transformers (SAT), a novel approach to modeling episodic memory in AI systems that integrates spatial information alongside temporal order. SAT employs an Adaptive Memory Allocator (AMA) to optimize memory utilization based on reinforcement learning. The model is evaluated across various tasks such as supervised prediction, image generation, and reinforcement learning, demonstrating improvements in memory utilization and task performance. The approach is further validated through experiments in environments like Room Ballet and MiniWorld, showcasing the model's ability to handle complex spatial reasoning tasks. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is critiqued for its limited evaluation on more complex tasks and its reliance on simplifying assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to modeling episodic memory in AI systems using Spatially-Aware Transformers (SAT), which effectively integrates spatial information alongside temporal order, enhancing memory utilization and task performance.
- The proposed Adaptive Memory Allocator (AMA) method is innovative and provides a flexible memory management strategy beyond traditional FIFO memory writing approaches.
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the research question, the proposed methodology, and the experimental results, making it accessible to readers.
- The evaluation of SAT across various tasks such as supervised prediction, image generation, and reinforcement learning demonstrates the model's versatility and applicability in diverse scenarios.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of SAT, including a detailed discussion of the limitations, which enhances the transparency and credibility of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's evaluation primarily focuses on simpler tasks, which may not fully demonstrate the capabilities of the SAT in more complex environments.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods that use spatial information in episodic memory, which could have provided a better context for the proposed approach.
- The paper relies on simplifying assumptions, such as the availability of spatial annotations, which may not hold in all real-world scenarios.
- The generalization of the Adaptive Memory Allocator (AMA) to more complex tasks and environments is not clearly demonstrated.
- The paper does not include a discussion on how the SAT-AMA model handles non-spatial reasoning tasks in spatial environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed approach and how these might impact the practical applicability of the model.

**Questions:**
- How does the SAT-AMA model handle non-spatial reasoning tasks in spatial environments?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the AMA method is trained and how it generalizes to more complex tasks and environments?
- How does the SAT-AMA model perform when there is no clear spatial structure in the environment?
- Would it be possible to include a discussion on how the SAT-AMA model compares to other methods that use spatial information in episodic memory?
- Can the authors clarify how the SAT-AMA model handles scenarios where the spatial annotations are not readily available or are inaccurate?
- How does the SAT-AMA model scale with an increase in the number of spatial dimensions, such as in higher dimensional spaces?
