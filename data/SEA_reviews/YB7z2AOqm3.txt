 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Byzantine attack called JUMP, designed to evaluate the robustness of distributed learning algorithms against adversarial attacks. JUMP operates by constraining the adversarial gradients to be collinear with the average of honest gradients, aiming to maximize the total training loss. The attack is tested against various robust aggregation rules and shows superior performance in terms of reducing accuracy. The paper also includes theoretical and empirical analyses, demonstrating that JUMP can effectively bypass existing defenses, particularly in settings with high data heterogeneity. The experiments are conducted on datasets like MNIST and CIFAR-10, showing that JUMP outperforms other attacks in terms of accuracy reduction.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel Byzantine attack, JUMP, which is a significant contribution to the field of distributed learning.
- The attack is well-motivated and theoretically justified, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of JUMP against various robust aggregation rules, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing accuracy.
- The theoretical analysis of JUMP's performance is well-articulated, and the empirical results are convincing, showing that JUMP outperforms other attacks in terms of accuracy reduction.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the JUMP attack, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger, more complex datasets and models.
- The theoretical analysis of JUMP's performance is limited to two-dimensional settings, which may not fully capture its behavior in higher-dimensional scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of JUMP compared to other attacks, which could be a significant drawback in practical implementations.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the robustness of JUMP against countermeasures or defenses that could potentially mitigate its effects.
- The paper does not compare JUMP with other adaptive attacks that use reinforcement learning, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of its effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the implications of JUMP's assumptions, such as the collinearity of Byzantine vectors and the segment length, on its practical applicability and performance.

**Questions:**
- How does JUMP perform in scenarios with a larger number of Byzantine workers, and what are the implications for its effectiveness?
- Can JUMP be adapted to work with pre-aggregation methods like NNM, and how might this affect its performance?
- How does the computational complexity of JUMP compare to other attacks, and what are the practical implications of this in terms of its scalability?
- Is there a theoretical or empirical basis for the claim that JUMP can achieve better asymptotic performance than other attacks?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of segment length in JUMP and its impact on the attack's performance and complexity?
- How does JUMP compare to other adaptive attacks that use reinforcement learning, and what are the key differences in their effectiveness and applicability?
