 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the performance of large language models (LLMs) in handling specification-heavy tasks using in-context learning (ICL), revealing significant gaps compared to state-of-the-art (SoTA) performance. The study identifies three primary reasons for these discrepancies: the inability to specifically understand context, misalignment in task schema comprehension, and inadequate long-text understanding ability. The authors propose that these issues stem from the limitations of ICL rather than the inherent capabilities of LLMs. They suggest that fine-tuning and instruction tuning could potentially bridge these gaps, although these methods are currently limited by computational costs and the need for extensive, human-annotated datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of in-context learning (ICL) in handling specification-heavy tasks, providing a detailed analysis of the challenges and potential solutions.
- The authors have identified critical issues such as the inability to specifically understand context, misalignment in task schema comprehension, and inadequate long-text understanding ability, which are crucial for advancing the capabilities of LLMs.
- The paper is well-structured, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments conducted are robust, involving a variety of tasks and models, which supports the conclusions drawn about the limitations of ICL in handling specification-heavy tasks.
- The findings of the paper are significant for the field of LLMs, particularly in understanding the limitations of ICL and proposing potential solutions like fine-tuning and instruction tuning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other alignment methods like RLHF, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the effectiveness of different alignment techniques.
- The analysis provided is somewhat limited, particularly in terms of the depth of error analysis and the exploration of potential solutions beyond fine-tuning and instruction tuning.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational costs associated with fine-tuning, which could be a significant barrier for practical implementation.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of the findings, such as the implications for the development and deployment of LLMs in various applications.
- There are some minor issues with clarity and presentation, such as unclear figures and a lack of detailed explanation in certain sections.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other alignment methods like RLHF to better understand the relative effectiveness of different approaches?
- How do the findings of this study relate to other works that have explored the limitations of LLMs in handling complex tasks?
- Can the authors elaborate on the potential societal impacts of their findings, particularly in terms of how these limitations might be addressed in practical applications?
- What are the specific computational costs associated with fine-tuning, and how do these costs compare to the benefits in terms of performance improvement?
- In the context of the findings on the inability to specifically understand context, can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate this phenomenon?
- How do the authors address the potential for bias in the models due to the alignment with human expectations, particularly in tasks where these expectations might not align with the true goals or outcomes?
