 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GoLLIE, a model that leverages annotation guidelines to enhance zero-shot information extraction by large language models (LLMs). GoLLIE is trained on a diverse set of IE tasks and fine-tuned to adhere to guidelines, demonstrating significant improvements over baseline models in zero-shot IE tasks. The model's approach involves representing guidelines in a structured format, such as Python code, which helps the model understand and follow the rules more effectively. The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation and ablation studies that highlight the effectiveness of the guidelines in enhancing the model's performance. However, concerns were raised about the generalizability of the model to different domains and the potential for overfitting due to the structured representation of guidelines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to utilizing annotation guidelines to improve zero-shot information extraction, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is clearly presented, making it easy to follow and understand, and the experimental setup is well-designed, providing a robust comparison with state-of-the-art models.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation and ablation studies that provide insights into the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of guidelines to improve the performance of large language models (LLMs) in information extraction (IE) tasks is innovative and addresses a significant challenge in the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, and includes a detailed analysis of the results, which is helpful for understanding the model's performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claim that the model is generalizable to unseen IE tasks is not convincingly supported, as the model's performance is not evaluated on a diverse set of domains beyond the news and biomedical domains.
- The method's reliance on structured guidelines for IE tasks might limit its generalizability to other domains, as the guidelines need to be carefully crafted for each domain.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for overfitting due to the structured representation of guidelines, which could limit the model's ability to handle unstructured or ambiguous data.
- The method's comparison with other state-of-the-art models is not fair, as it uses a different backbone LLM (Code-LLaMA) which might not be directly comparable to other models like FlanT5.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different domains and the potential for overfitting.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify whether the model's performance on unseen IE tasks is evaluated on a diverse set of domains beyond news and biomedical? If not, how do they ensure the model's generalizability to other domains?
- How does the model handle ambiguous or unstructured data, especially in domains where the guidelines are not as well-defined or structured?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, such as the specifics of the training data and the hyperparameters used?
- How does the model perform when evaluated on a broader range of IE tasks beyond NER, EE, and EAE?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for overfitting due to the structured representation of guidelines and how this might affect the model's performance on unstructured or ambiguous data?
- How does the model compare with other state-of-the-art models when using the same backbone LLM?
