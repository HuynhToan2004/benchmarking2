 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel methodology, LAMPP, which leverages large language models (LLMs) to provide prior distributions over labels, decisions, and parameters in various tasks, including semantic image segmentation, robot navigation, and video action recognition. The approach aims to improve generalization capabilities by integrating LLMs with traditional models, allowing for more accurate and efficient predictions, particularly in out-of-distribution scenarios. The methodology involves defining a generative model that integrates LLMs with task-specific models, which can be queried for prior distributions that guide decision-making processes. The paper presents several case studies demonstrating the effectiveness of LAMPP in improving performance and reducing computational costs compared to baseline models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and its applications across various tasks.
- The proposed method is innovative and well-motivated, particularly in its application of language models as probabilistic priors, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The method is computationally efficient, reducing the number of queries to LLMs, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper includes several case studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the method, particularly in scenarios where LLMs can provide useful prior knowledge.
- The methodology is flexible and can be adapted to different tasks, which is highlighted through the diversity of tasks used in the experiments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly those that use LLMs as probabilistic priors, which could have strengthened the argument for the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on LLMs for providing prior distributions could limit its applicability in scenarios where such models may not be available or suitable.
- The paper does not discuss the potential societal impacts of the method, which could be a significant concern in real-world applications.
- The method's effectiveness is not thoroughly validated across a broader range of tasks or datasets, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the method compares to existing methods that use LLMs as probabilistic priors? What specific advantages does the proposed method offer over these approaches?
- How does the method handle scenarios where LLMs may not provide accurate or relevant prior knowledge?
- Can the method be adapted to work with different types of LLMs, or is it limited to specific models?
- How does the method perform when applied to a wider range of tasks or datasets, particularly those that are not covered in the current study?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the method, particularly in terms of the number of queries required and the computational resources needed?
- How does the method handle the potential biases and errors in LLM outputs, and what steps are taken to ensure the reliability and accuracy of the prior distributions provided by the LLMs?
