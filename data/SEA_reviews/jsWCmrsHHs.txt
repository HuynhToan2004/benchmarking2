 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel deep reinforcement learning (DRL) approach for solving the Job Shop Scheduling Problem (JSSP) by utilizing a graph neural network (GNN) to represent the solution space as a disjunctive graph. The methodology involves a two-module architecture, the topological embedding module (TPM) and the context-aware embedding module (CAM), which encode the topology and node neighbors, respectively. The DRL policy outputs a pair of operations to improve the current solution, and the method is shown to outperform existing DRL-based methods and classical heuristics on benchmark instances. The paper also discusses the computational complexity of the proposed method and its comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using disjunctive graphs to represent the solution space, which is different from previous works that used a disjunctive graph to represent the problem.
- The proposed method outperforms existing DRL-based methods and classical heuristics on benchmark instances, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making the complex methodologies and experimental results accessible.
- The use of graph neural networks (GNN) to represent the solution space as a graph and the application of GNN to the scheduling problem are innovative and potentially impactful.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the proposed method, including the architecture of the policy network, and the message-passing mechanism for evaluating solutions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to other methods, which could help in understanding its efficiency.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it appears to be an extension of existing methods to the scheduling domain, and the technical contributions are not clearly articulated.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, and the paper does not compare the proposed method with a broader range of state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the results.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the need for a large amount of data for training and the scalability of the method to larger problems.
- There are some issues with the clarity and presentation of the paper, including typos and unclear explanations in certain sections.

**Questions:**

- Can you clarify the role and impact of the two embedding modules (TPM and CAM) in the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method handle the generation of an initial solution, and what are the implications of using different initial solutions?
