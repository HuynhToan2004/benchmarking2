 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to graph contrastive learning by proposing a Gaussian Mutual Information (GMI) objective, which maximizes the mutual information between node representations under a Gaussian distribution assumption. This method aims to bridge the gap between contrastive-based and decorrelation-based methods by providing a theoretical framework that connects these two approaches. The GMI objective is shown to be equivalent to decorrelation-based methods under certain conditions, and its effectiveness is validated through extensive experiments across multiple datasets. Despite its theoretical contributions, the paper faces criticisms regarding the clarity of its presentation, the novelty of its approach, and the practicality of its assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method, making it accessible and understandable.
- The proposed method of using Gaussian Mutual Information (GMI) to estimate mutual information is novel and provides a new approach to graph contrastive learning.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the proposed method, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments conducted on six widely used benchmark datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper connects decorrelation-based self-supervised methods to contrastive-based methods, providing a new perspective on the relationship between these approaches.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions, particularly the Gaussian distribution assumption, are considered strong and may not hold in practical scenarios, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental results, while showing effectiveness, do not convincingly demonstrate superiority over existing methods, especially in terms of performance metrics like ACC.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the practical implications of the assumptions made, such as the Gaussian distribution assumption, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing methods, which is necessary to establish the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper's presentation and organization could be improved to make it more accessible and easier to follow, particularly in sections where the content is dense or complex.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the Gaussian distribution assumption does not hold?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these limitations affect its applicability and effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the balancing coefficient in the proposed method?
- How does the paper address the issue of dimensional collapse, and what are the implications of this approach in practical applications?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the impact of different parameters and settings on the performance of the method?
