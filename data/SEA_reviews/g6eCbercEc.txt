 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called InfoCon, designed to automatically learn manipulation concepts from unlabeled demonstrations using a VQ-VAE architecture. This method aims to discover key states in robot manipulation tasks by learning generative and discriminative goals, which are then used to train policies that utilize these concepts. The approach is evaluated against various baselines, showing promising results in terms of task success rates and the ability to learn key states that align well with human intuition. The method is also compared to other existing methods, demonstrating its effectiveness in both seen and unseen environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.
- The method is novel, using a VQ-VAE architecture to learn manipulation concepts from unlabeled demonstrations, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the method, including the use of generative and discriminative goals, which are innovative approaches to learning key states in robot manipulation.
- The experiments are thorough, showing that the proposed method outperforms other baselines in terms of task success rates and aligns well with human intuition.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed descriptions of the method, particularly the use of positional encoding and the role of different components in the method.
- There is a lack of comparison with some relevant baselines, such as those mentioned in previous works like AWE and CoTPC, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in more complex or varied environments.
- The method's reliance on a specific training dataset raises concerns about its generalizability to other datasets or environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, including potential failure modes and the impact of different hyperparameters on the results.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and implementation of positional encoding in the method?
- How does the method handle variations in the number of key states across different tasks?
- What are the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments, and how do they impact the results?
- Can you provide more details on the training dataset and how it was collected?
- How does the method perform when trained on different datasets or in more complex environments?
- Could you provide more detailed visualizations or explanations of the key states learned by the method, especially in comparison to human-defined key states?
- How does the method compare to other approaches that use LLMs to generate key states, particularly in terms of performance and interpretability?
