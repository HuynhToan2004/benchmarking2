 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel pretraining method, In-Context Pretraining (ICLM), which reorders documents based on semantic similarity to enhance language models' understanding of context. ICLM utilizes a retrieval model to identify related documents and a graph traversal algorithm to construct coherent input contexts. This approach aims to improve the model's performance in various downstream tasks, such as in-context learning, reading comprehension, and retrieval augmentation, by providing more contextually relevant data during training. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on multiple datasets, showing improvements in perplexity, in-context learning, and downstream task performance. However, concerns about the scalability and generalization of the approach, as well as the need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, are highlighted.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to pretraining language models by reordering documents based on semantic similarity, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The use of a retrieval model to identify related documents and a graph traversal algorithm to construct coherent input contexts is a novel and interesting approach.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various tasks and datasets.
- The approach of using in-context pretraining to enhance language models' understanding of context is highly relevant and could have a significant impact on the field.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including the evolution of performance during pretraining and an ablation study on the design of in-context pretraining.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other existing methods for retrieving and grouping documents, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The scalability of the approach is questionable, particularly in terms of the computational resources required to process the CommonCrawl dataset, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant given the potential misuse of language models.
- The method's generalization to other domains or languages beyond English is unclear, and the paper does not explore the impact of different pretraining corpus sizes on the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the approach and potential areas for future research.

**Questions:**
- How does the performance of the proposed method compare with other existing methods for retrieving and grouping documents, such as those mentioned in related work?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational resources required to process the CommonCrawl dataset and the scalability of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different languages or domains beyond English?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and discuss strategies for mitigating these risks?
- What are the specific computational resources and time required to construct the document graph and perform the graph traversal algorithm?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods that use related documents for pretraining, such as those mentioned in the related work section?
