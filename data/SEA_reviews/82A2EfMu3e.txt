 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for solving evolutionary partial differential equations (PDEs) using a discrete-time variant of physics-informed neural networks (PINNs), termed TL-DPINN. This approach leverages transfer learning to update PINNs sequentially, addressing the challenge of temporal causality in continuous-time PINNs. The methodology is supported by theoretical analysis and extensive numerical experiments, demonstrating the accuracy and efficiency of the proposed method. The paper's contributions include the integration of discrete-time PINNs with transfer learning, which is shown to outperform existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the theoretical analysis, and the scope of the experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for solving evolutionary partial differential equations (PDEs) using a discrete-time variant of physics-informed neural networks (PINNs), which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis and proof of the error estimation result of the proposed TL-DPINN method, which is a rigorous approach to validate the method's effectiveness.
- The numerical results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance among various PINN frameworks in a trade-off between accuracy and efficiency.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, which is crucial for its accessibility to a broad audience.
- The methodology of using transfer learning to update PINNs sequentially is innovative and addresses the challenge of temporal causality in continuous-time PINNs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the difference between the proposed method and existing works, particularly in the context of transfer learning and discrete PINNs.
- The theoretical analysis, while present, is not convincingly robust, and the proofs provided are not convincing enough to support the claims made.
- The experimental validation is limited to a few benchmark problems, which may not sufficiently demonstrate the general applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of numerical instability in discrete PINNs, which could lead to inaccurate results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational cost and complexity of the proposed method compared to other state-of-the-art methods.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and scope.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the main differences between the proposed method and existing works, particularly in terms of transfer learning and discrete PINNs?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of numerical instability in discrete PINNs, and what measures have been taken to address this?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost and complexity of the proposed method compared to other state-of-the-art methods?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to other types of PDEs, such as elliptic or hyperbolic equations?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of accuracy and efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in more complex scenarios?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its applicability in real-world scenarios?
