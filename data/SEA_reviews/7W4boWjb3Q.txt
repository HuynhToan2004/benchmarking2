 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel algorithm called Partitioned Learned Count-Min Sketch (PL-CMS), which extends the Learned Count-Min Sketch (LCMS) algorithm by partitioning items based on predicted frequencies. This method allows for more efficient use of the learned model by assigning items with higher predicted frequencies to a separate counter, while those with lower predicted frequencies are handled by the Count-Min Sketch. The paper demonstrates that this approach can significantly reduce false positive rates compared to existing methods, particularly in datasets with heavy-tailed frequency distributions. However, the method's performance is less pronounced in datasets with non-heavy-tailed distributions, and the experimental evaluation is limited to a few datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology.
- The proposed method, PL-CMS, is simple, intuitive, and effectively reduces the false positive rate (FPR) compared to existing methods like CMS and LCMS.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the FPR upper bound, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experimental results demonstrate that PL-CMS significantly outperforms existing methods in terms of FPR reduction.
- The idea of using a partitioned CMS is novel and shows promise in improving the performance of heavy hitter identification in data streams.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental evaluation is limited to a few datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the general applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other learning-augmented CMS algorithms, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method's advantages.
- The performance of PL-CMS is less pronounced in datasets with non-heavy-tailed distributions, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not address the computational overhead of the learned model, which could be a significant factor in practical implementations.
- The methodology and its assumptions, such as the assumption of a fixed set of score thresholds, are not thoroughly discussed, which could limit the understanding of its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the computational overhead of the learned model and discuss its impact on the practical implementation of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in datasets with non-heavy-tailed distributions, and what are the implications of this performance for real-world applications?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other learning-augmented CMS algorithms to better understand the advantages of the proposed method?
- Is there a possibility to extend the PL-CMS method to other learning-augmented CMS algorithms, such as those mentioned in the review?
- How does the paper address the assumptions made about the fixed set of score thresholds, and what are the implications of these assumptions on the practical applicability of the method?
