 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to masked image modeling (MIM) by replacing traditional masking with random orthogonal projection (ROP). This method projects patches into a random subspace, which is then used to reconstruct the original image, promoting the recovery of removed information. The proposed method, termed Random Orthogonal Projection Image Modeling (ROPIM), has been tested on various datasets including ImageNet-1k, iNaturalist 2017, and ADE20K, showing improvements over existing MIM methods. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the method and provides empirical evidence of its effectiveness. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the method and its theoretical robustness, particularly in handling high masking ratios and diverse data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, ROPIM, is novel and introduces a simple yet effective approach to masked image modeling, showing significant improvements in accuracy and training efficiency.
- The methodology is supported by a robust theoretical framework, which is well-integrated with the experimental results.
- The paper presents a clear motivation for the use of random orthogonal projection (ROP) in masked image modeling, providing a compelling rationale for its effectiveness.
- The results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches, highlighting its potential impact on the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in handling high masking ratios and diverse data.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches, especially when considering the impact of different hyperparameters and data augmentations.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be a modification of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art techniques, which could have strengthened the argument for its effectiveness.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the computational complexity and the practical implications of the method, particularly in terms of training time and resource requirements.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive ablation study to better understand the impact of different components of the method on its performance.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the ROP method performs under different masking ratios and with diverse data?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements with other existing methods?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of different hyperparameters in the performance of the method?
- Why was the decision made to use a linear probing setup rather than a linear evaluation setup, and how does this affect the comparison with other methods?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential issues of overfitting or underfitting, especially in large-scale datasets like ImageNet-1k?
- Could the authors provide more details on the practical implications of the method, such as its applicability in real-world scenarios and its scalability to larger datasets?
