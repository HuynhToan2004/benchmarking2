 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework called Chain-of-Table, which integrates tabular data into the reasoning chain to enhance table-based question answering and fact verification. This approach extends the concept of Chain-of-Thought, utilizing in-context samples to guide Large Language Models (LLMs) in iteratively generating operations and updating tables. The method involves dynamic planning and table evolution, which is demonstrated to outperform existing methods on benchmarks like WikiTQ, TabFact, and FeTaQA. The paper also provides extensive experiments and ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method called Chain-of-Table, which extends the concept of Chain-of-Thought to table-based reasoning, utilizing in-context samples to guide LLMs in iteratively generating operations and updating tables.
- The method is well-motivated, intuitive, and easy to follow, with extensive experiments and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise descriptions of the proposed method and experiments, and it provides detailed prompts and results for reproducibility.
- The proposed framework achieves state-of-the-art performance on three benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency.
- The method is flexible and adaptable to various LLM choices, and it can be applied to different table-based reasoning tasks, such as table-based question answering and fact verification.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how the LLM generates arguments for the atomic operations, and the ablation study does not include the impact of removing the argument generation step.
- The performance improvement on the TabFact dataset is limited, and the paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the performance on the WikiTQ dataset.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of tables and the scalability of the method to larger or more complex tables.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods that also utilize table-based reasoning, such as Dater.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity or the efficiency of the proposed method, especially in scenarios where the tables are large or the operations are complex.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the LLM generates arguments for the atomic operations and include the impact of removing the argument generation step in the ablation study?
- How does the proposed method perform on the WikiTQ dataset, especially in terms of accuracy and performance metrics?
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the performance of the proposed method on different types of tables, such as tables with complex structures or tables with incomplete data?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods that also utilize table-based reasoning, such as Dater?
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed method, especially in scenarios where the tables are large or the operations are complex?
