 **Summary:**
The paper explores the impact of early stopping metrics on hyperparameter optimization (HPO) and neural architecture search (NAS), specifically focusing on the selection of metrics that can lead to early stopping decisions. The authors propose a set of metrics that incorporate model uncertainty, arguing that these metrics can enhance the reliability of early stopping decisions. Through empirical experiments and theoretical analysis, the paper investigates the effectiveness of various metrics, including training and validation losses, and their impact on HPO performance. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of these metrics and their implications for model selection and budget allocation in future HPO algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The topic of early stopping in hyperparameter optimization (HPO) and neural architecture search (NAS) is both relevant and interesting, as it addresses a critical aspect of machine learning model training.
- The paper provides a thorough empirical study on nine HPO tasks in two widely used HPO benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed metrics.
- The theoretical analysis provided in the paper offers valuable insights into the nature of early stopping and the factors influencing its effectiveness.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to incorporating model uncertainty into early stopping metrics, which could potentially enhance the reliability of early stopping decisions.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, covering a variety of benchmarks and metrics, and the results are well-presented and analyzed.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and discussion of the "uncertainty" metric, which is crucial for understanding the proposed methodology.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly the computational costs associated with ensemble averaging.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of metrics, especially why training loss is preferred over validation loss in certain scenarios.
- The theoretical analysis, while interesting, is somewhat disconnected from the empirical results and could be better integrated into the main text.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed methods, especially in terms of their applicability to larger, more complex models.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the proposed methods compare to existing methods, such as those that use Bayesian optimization or other uncertainty-based approaches.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the proposed methods, especially in terms of their impact on the training and validation processes.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition and role of the "uncertainty" metric in your proposed methodology?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods that use Bayesian optimization or other uncertainty-based approaches?
- What are the specific computational costs associated with the ensemble averaging method, and how do these costs scale with the size of the ensemble?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the model has not yet fully converged, and what are the implications of this for the accuracy of the early stopping decision?
- Can you provide more details on how the model uncertainty is calculated and how it is incorporated into the early stopping decision-making process?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the model's complexity is high, and what are the implications of this for the accuracy of the early stopping decision?
- Could you elaborate on the practical implications of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its impact on the training and validation processes?
