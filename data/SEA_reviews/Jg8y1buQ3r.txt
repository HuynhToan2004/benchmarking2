 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for detecting hateful memes using a multimodal framework that integrates a large language model (LLM) with a memory module. This memory module is trained using a self-rejection technique to optimize the encoding of visual and textual features, which are then used to predict the hatefulness of memes. The approach is evaluated on two datasets, showing improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the memory module and the effectiveness of the self-rejection training, which is not adequately justified or compared with other baseline methods. The paper also faces criticism for its clarity in explaining the memory module and the effectiveness of the proposed training method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method.
- The method achieves good performance on two datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The use of a memory module to capture correlations between visual and textual features is novel and interesting, contributing to the field of multimodal information processing.
- The paper includes a comprehensive experimental section that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method, with comparisons against several baseline methods.
- The authors have provided detailed explanations of the training process and the motivation behind the proposed method, which is well-supported by experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the memory module, particularly how it captures correlations between visual and textual features and how it is trained.
- The novelty of the memory module is questionable as similar methods have been previously explored, such as using memory vectors for cross-modal retrieval.
- The effectiveness of the self-rejection training is not adequately justified or compared with other baseline methods, raising concerns about its contribution to the model's performance.
- The paper's experimental setup and comparisons are limited to specific datasets and models, which may not generalize well to other datasets or models.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the explanation of the memory module and the training process, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not discuss potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant omission given the sensitive nature of the task.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the memory module captures correlations between visual and textual features? How is it trained, and what are the specific training objectives?
- How does the memory module compare to other methods for capturing multimodal correlations, such as co-attention mechanisms?
- Can the authors clarify the effectiveness of the self-rejection training and how it contributes to the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of hateful memes, and what are the specific challenges in detecting them?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated?
- Is there a plan to release the code and models for reproducibility and further research by the community?
