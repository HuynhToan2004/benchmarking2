 **Summary:**
The paper explores the generalization capabilities of Transformer models, specifically focusing on their ability to generalize to longer input sequences. It proposes a new framework, RASP-L, which aims to predict the generalization of Transformers by analyzing whether a task can be represented by a short RASP-L program. This framework is supported by experiments that demonstrate how the simplicity of a RASP-L program correlates with the generalization performance of Transformers. The paper also discusses how the RASP-L program can be modified to improve generalization, such as by adding index hints to simplify the program. The framework is tested on various tasks, including addition and parity, and the results suggest that the Transformers' generalization is influenced by the complexity of the RASP-L program.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the generalization capabilities of Transformers, which is crucial for understanding the limitations and potential of these models.
- The proposed RASP-L framework offers a novel approach to understanding and predicting the generalization performance of Transformers, which is supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors demonstrate the practical applicability of the RASP-L framework by showing how it can be used to improve the generalization performance of Transformers on tasks such as addition and parity.
- The paper provides a systematic and comprehensive analysis of the factors influencing the generalization of Transformers, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper focuses primarily on decoder-only Transformers, which might not generalize well to encoder-only or encoder-decoder architectures.
- The experimental results are limited to specific tasks and do not cover a broader range of symbolic tasks, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the RASP-L framework, such as its applicability to different types of tasks and its scalability to more complex or diverse datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related works, particularly those that have explored similar concepts such as the "min-degree-interpolator" model.
- The paper's claims about the generalization capabilities of Transformers are not convincingly supported by the empirical evidence provided, which might be due to the limited scope of the experiments.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify whether the RASP-L framework can be applied to other types of tasks beyond the symbolic tasks considered in the paper?
- How does the RASP-L framework compare to other approaches for understanding the generalization capabilities of Transformers, such as the "min-degree-interpolator" model?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific tasks used and the methodology for evaluating the generalization performance?
- How does the RASP-L framework handle tasks that do not have simple solutions, and what implications does this have for the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors elaborate on the implications of the RASP-L framework for the design of more generalizable Transformer models, and how it might be applied in practical scenarios?
