 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two variants of conservative Forward-Backward (FB) representations to address the zero-shot reinforcement learning (RL) challenge using low-quality datasets. These variants, Value-Conservative FB (VC-FB) and Measure-Conservative FB (MC-FB), employ conservative penalties to suppress the values of out-of-distribution (OOD) actions, enhancing the performance of zero-shot RL on sub-optimal datasets. The authors evaluate these methods across various domains and datasets, demonstrating that they outperform existing state-of-the-art methods like FB representations and CQL. Despite their effectiveness, the paper acknowledges that these methods require significant computational resources and may not perform as well on larger, diverse datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of zero-shot reinforcement learning (RL) using low-quality datasets, which is crucial for advancing the applicability of RL in real-world scenarios.
- The proposed methods, VC-FB and MC-FB, are well-motivated and well-explained, with a clear theoretical framework and empirical evidence supporting their effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The introduction of conservative FB algorithms represents a significant advancement in the field of zero-shot RL, potentially enhancing the performance of RL systems in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed methods with other relevant baselines such as SF-LAP, DVC-FB, and offline TD3, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The computational expense of the conservative variants is a significant concern, with the training process taking approximately 3 times longer than the vanilla FB, which might limit their practical applicability.
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the performance discrepancy between VC-FB and MC-FB, particularly in different dataset sizes, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, including their applicability in different types of environments and their sensitivity to the quality and diversity of the datasets used.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational requirements and the practical implications of using the proposed methods in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed methods with other relevant baselines such as SF-LAP, DVC-FB, and offline TD3, including a discussion on the performance of these methods in different types of environments?
- What are the specific computational requirements of the conservative variants, and how do these compare to the vanilla FB in terms of training time and resource utilization?
- How sensitive are the proposed methods to the quality and diversity of the datasets used, and what are the implications of using datasets with varying levels of quality and diversity?
- Could you elaborate on the performance discrepancy between VC-FB and MC-FB, particularly in different dataset sizes, and provide insights into the underlying reasons for these differences?
- How do the proposed methods perform in environments with different types of tasks, and what are the implications of using these methods in such environments?
