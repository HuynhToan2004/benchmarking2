 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a transformer-based architecture for predicting valence and arousal from physiological signals using the CASE dataset. The methodology involves a multivariate time series model that integrates time and frequency domain attention mechanisms, aiming to enhance the prediction of emotional states from physiological signals. The approach utilizes a multitask learning framework and a novel attention mechanism, Two-Stage Attention (TSA), to decompose signals into frequency and time domain representations. The paper claims state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on the CASE dataset but faces criticism for its limited novelty, as it heavily relies on existing methods without significant innovation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to understand and follow.
- The authors have conducted experiments on the CASE dataset, which is considered a gold standard for emotion recognition.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on the CASE dataset.
- The paper introduces a novel attention mechanism, Two-Stage Attention (TSA), which effectively captures the relationship between physiological signals and affective changes.
- The use of wavelet and Fourier attention in the frequency domain and auto-correlation attention in the time domain is innovative and enhances the understanding of physiological signals.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as it primarily replicates existing methods without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential improvements.
- The performance of the model heavily relies on the quality of the training dataset, which could limit its effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its results with other state-of-the-art models, which is necessary to validate the claims of SOTA performance.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the model's architecture and training parameters, which are essential for reproducibility and further research.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincing, particularly in the absence of comparisons with other models and the lack of detailed analysis of the model's performance across different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of their approach and how it differs from existing methods?
- How does the model perform when the training dataset is of lower quality or contains noisy data?
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of their model's performance with other state-of-the-art models, including both qualitative and quantitative comparisons?
- How were the hyperparameters of the model determined, and what impact do these parameters have on the model's performance?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results and analysis, including the impact of different hyperparameters and the performance of the model across different scenarios?
- How does the model handle the domain inconsistency between input and target signals, particularly in terms of frequency and time domain representations?
- Can the authors provide more details on the model's training and inference processes, including the computational resources and time required?
