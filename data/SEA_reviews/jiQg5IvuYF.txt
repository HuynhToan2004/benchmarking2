 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Corgi2, an extension of the CorgiPile algorithm, which aims to improve data shuffling efficiency in stochastic gradient descent (SGD) by incorporating a two-step partial data shuffling strategy. The first step is an offline iteration using CorgiPile, followed by an online iteration. This approach is designed to reduce the block variance and improve convergence rates. Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of Corgi2 in reducing the block variance and improving convergence rates, although the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, unclear presentation, and lack of detailed experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel two-step partial data shuffling strategy for SGD, which combines the strengths of both offline and online data shuffling techniques.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to demonstrate the convergence properties of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed algorithm, Corgi2, shows a significant reduction in block variance compared to the original CorgiPile algorithm.
- The paper includes a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed method, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it builds upon existing works like CorgiPile and does not introduce significant new theoretical insights.
- The paper lacks detailed experimental results, particularly in terms of the number of queries, which is crucial for evaluating the efficiency of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the paper, making it difficult to understand the main contributions and the methodology used.
- The paper does not adequately address the convergence rate of the proposed method, which is a critical aspect of the study.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other existing methods, such as those mentioned in the related work section, to establish the superiority of the proposed method.
- The theoretical analysis assumes strong convexity, which might not be applicable in practical scenarios, and the assumptions made in the proofs are not adequately justified.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the proofs, particularly in Theorem 2, and provide justifications for these assumptions?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data is not homogeneous, and what are the implications of this for the block variance and convergence rates?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the number of queries and the training time, to better evaluate the efficiency of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods, particularly those mentioned in the related work section, in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors elaborate on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of their applicability to real-world scenarios?
- In light of the concerns regarding the novelty and the clarity of the presentation, can the authors clarify the main contributions of the paper and how they build upon existing work?
