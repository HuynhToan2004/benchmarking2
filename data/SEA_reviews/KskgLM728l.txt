 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Bio-RFX, a method designed for biomedical named entity recognition (NER) and relation extraction (RE) tasks. It employs a two-stage approach, first predicting relations and then extracting entities, which is claimed to enhance performance, particularly in low-resource scenarios. The method leverages structural constraints between entities and relations to guide the model's hypothesis space, which is evaluated on several biomedical datasets. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty, limited evaluation on larger datasets, and potential issues with generalization and computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to biomedical NER and RE by predicting relations first and then extracting entities, which is a departure from traditional methods.
- The use of structural constraints between entities and relations to guide the model's hypothesis space is innovative and could be beneficial for improving extraction performance.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides clear explanations of the proposed methodology.
- The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on several biomedical datasets, showing improvements over baseline methods.
- The paper addresses the challenge of extracting structured data from unstructured texts in the biomedical domain, which is crucial for advancing research and clinical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough evaluation of the method's performance on larger datasets, which could better demonstrate its scalability and generalization capabilities.
- There is a potential for data leakage due to the use of the same datasets for both training and testing, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with state-of-the-art models, which could undermine the claim of its effectiveness.
- The method's reliance on the availability of relation categories and entity types could limit its applicability in scenarios where such data is not readily available.
- The computational cost of the method is high, which could be a significant drawback in practical applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of error propagation in the pipeline training, which could lead to discrepancies between training and testing.
- The method's novelty is questioned as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods without significant innovation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how they addressed the potential issue of data leakage in their experiments?
- How does the method perform when applied to other biomedical datasets that were not used in the initial evaluation?
- Can the authors clarify the rationale behind the choice of using SciBERT over other pre-trained models for the relation classifier?
- How does the method handle cases where the relation categories and entity types are not readily available?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost of their method against other state-of-the-art models?
- How does the method handle cases where the relation types are not explicitly mentioned in the text, but can be inferred from the context?
- Could the authors provide more details on the methodology used for the ablation studies, particularly how the "without structure" and "without number" variations were implemented?
