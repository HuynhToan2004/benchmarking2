 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method to enhance the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) by incorporating a "chat vector" derived from the parameter space of pre-trained models. This vector, obtained by subtracting the weights of a pre-trained model from those of a model fine-tuned for chat-based tasks, is used to fine-tune models in non-English languages, specifically Traditional Chinese and Korean. The method aims to improve the models' performance in chat-based tasks, such as following instructions and generating safe responses, by adjusting the model weights. The paper presents experimental results showing some effectiveness of the chat vector in improving model performance, although the results are not consistently superior to baseline models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method of using a chat vector to enhance the capabilities of LLMs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method can improve the performance of LLMs in chat-based tasks, particularly in non-English languages.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, which is crucial for academic publications.
- The methodology is straightforward and efficient, requiring only continual pre-training and the addition of a chat vector, which is computationally less expensive than traditional methods.
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue by focusing on the development of LLMs in non-English languages, which is a significant area of research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the chat vector, particularly why it is effective and how it influences model performance.
- The experimental results are not consistently superior to baseline models, raising questions about the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method to larger models, which could limit its applicability in practical scenarios.
- The evaluation metrics used in the paper, such as GPT-4 evaluation and the Perspective API, are not sufficiently robust or reliable for assessing the quality of the models' outputs.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training datasets and fine-tuning processes, which are crucial for reproducibility and validation of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the method, such as the potential for the chat vector to introduce biases or errors in model performance.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the chat vector, particularly how it influences model performance and why it is effective?
- How does the method perform with larger models, and what are the scalability limitations?
- Could you elaborate on the training datasets and fine-tuning processes used in the experiments?
- How were the hyperparameters for the continual pre-training and fine-tuning stages selected, and what impact do these choices have on the results?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how are these addressed?
- Could you clarify the experimental setup, particularly the use of different models in the baseline and proposed methods?
- How does the paper address the potential for the chat vector to introduce biases or errors in model performance?
