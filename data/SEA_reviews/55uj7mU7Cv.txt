 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the issue of unsupervised domain translation (UDT) where the content of images needs to be preserved while translating from one domain to another. The authors propose a novel method that leverages auxiliary variables to ensure identifiability of the translation function, aiming to reduce the likelihood of multiple solutions. This method is supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results, showing that it outperforms existing methods like CycleGAN in certain scenarios. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited scope in terms of dataset diversity and the practicality of the auxiliary variable assumption.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The theoretical analysis is solid, providing a rigorous foundation for the proposed method.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of unsupervised domain translation (UDT), focusing on the identifiability of translation functions.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with good experimental results that demonstrate its practical utility.
- The paper includes a theoretical analysis that shows the method's effectiveness, particularly in the context of unsupervised domain translation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental results are not convincing, with some results showing no significant improvement over baseline methods.
- The paper assumes that the translation function is bijective, which may not hold in practical scenarios where multiple "correct" translations are possible.
- The auxiliary variable assumption, while useful, may not be practical in all scenarios and could limit the applicability of the method.
- The paper's theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions that may not hold in real-world scenarios, such as the assumption of a deterministic and bijective translation function.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its practical applicability.
- The paper's experimental setup is limited, focusing on a few datasets which may not adequately demonstrate the method's effectiveness across different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and necessity of the auxiliary variable in the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where it may not be available or practical?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the translation function is not bijective, and what are the implications of this on the method's effectiveness?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or scenarios where the auxiliary variable assumption holds true, and where it may not?
- In terms of practical implementation, how does the method handle scenarios where the translation function is not deterministic or bijective?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of their method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
- How does the method compare to other existing methods that do not rely on auxiliary variables, and what are the implications of this comparison for the method's effectiveness?
