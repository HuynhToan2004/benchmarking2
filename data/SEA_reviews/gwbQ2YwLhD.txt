 **Summary:**
The paper explores the impact of variable scales on the performance of structure learning algorithms, focusing on Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) and their optimization through least square and log-likelihood losses. The authors demonstrate that under certain conditions, the MMSE (Mean Squared Error) and log-likelihood losses can favor incorrect DAGs, particularly in scenarios where variable scales are not considered. Theoretical proofs and empirical evidence are provided to support these findings, showing that the scale of variables can significantly influence the optimization outcomes. The paper also introduces a new scale-robust loss to mitigate the effects of variable scales on the learning process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the theoretical results, which are supported by empirical evidence.
- The problem of variable scales in DAG structure learning is well-motivated and the paper addresses a significant and relevant issue in the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors have demonstrated the impact of variable scales on the optimization outcomes of structure learning algorithms, which is a novel contribution to the field.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the paper provides detailed descriptions of the experimental setup, including the code for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in real-world applications.
- The experimental setup and results are somewhat limited, focusing primarily on specific types of DAGs and not adequately addressing more complex or varied structures.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the linear relationship between variables, may not hold in real-world scenarios, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed methods to larger or more complex datasets, which could be a significant limitation for practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions may not hold, such as in non-linear relationships or non-Gaussian noise.

**Questions:**
- How do the authors envision the practical implementation of the proposed scale-robust loss in real-world scenarios, especially in applications where the assumptions may not hold?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies demonstrating the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in complex or varied DAG structures?
- How do the authors address the scalability of their methods to larger or more complex datasets, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in doing so?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their assumptions, particularly the linear relationship between variables and the Gaussian noise assumption, and how these might affect the applicability of the findings in real-world scenarios?
