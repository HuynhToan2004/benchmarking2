 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the fairness trade-offs in federated learning (FL) by introducing a novel information-theoretic perspective, specifically through the lens of partial information decomposition (PID). It identifies three types of disparities—unique, redundant, and masked—which contribute to global and local fairness. The authors propose a convex optimization problem, AGLFOP, to explore the trade-offs between accuracy and fairness. Theoretical insights are supported by experiments on synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating the practical implications of these disparities. Despite the novel approach and theoretical contributions, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper, the lack of comprehensive experimental validation, and the generalizability of the findings beyond the specific datasets used.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative information-theoretic perspective on fairness trade-offs in federated learning, which is both novel and insightful.
- It provides a clear and detailed explanation of the partial information decomposition (PID) and its application to fairness in federated learning, making the paper accessible and understandable.
- The theoretical results are well-structured, with clear definitions and detailed proofs, which are supported by empirical evidence on synthetic and real datasets.
- The paper is well-written, with clear motivation and a logical flow that makes it easy to follow.
- The use of PID to identify and analyze three types of disparity is a significant contribution to the field, providing a new way to understand and quantify fairness in federated learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on related works, particularly in the area of fairness in federated learning, which could have enriched the context and comparative analysis.
- The experimental results are limited to specific datasets and do not include a broader range of datasets to validate the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definitions and relationships between different fairness metrics, such as statistical parity and equalized odds, which could lead to confusion about the actual fairness outcomes.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples for each theorem and definition, especially those that are less intuitive or not well-explained.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and the assumptions made, which could affect the applicability and robustness of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed optimization problem and the trade-offs it represents, as well as a comparison with existing methods in the field.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples for the definitions and theorems that are less intuitive or not well-explained in the paper?
- How does the paper address the limitations and assumptions made in the proposed methods, and what are the implications of these limitations for the practical applicability of the findings?
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the different fairness metrics used in the paper, especially the relationship between statistical parity and equalized odds?
- How does the paper ensure that the results are not overfitting to the specific datasets used in the experiments, and what steps were taken to validate the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors discuss the potential implications of the findings for other types of fairness notions beyond statistical parity?
- How does the paper address the practical challenges of implementing the proposed methods in real-world federated learning scenarios, particularly in terms of computational complexity and scalability?
