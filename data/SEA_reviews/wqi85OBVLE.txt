 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to reward adaptation in reinforcement learning (RL) by utilizing pre-trained Q-values from multiple source tasks to optimize a target reward function. This method, termed Q-manipulation, involves pruning actions based on the Q-values of the source tasks, which are then used to train a policy for the target reward function. The paper presents theoretical results and empirical evaluations across various domains, demonstrating that the proposed method can significantly reduce the sample complexity compared to existing methods like Q-learning and Q-decomposition. However, the paper faces criticisms for its clarity in presentation, the scope of its empirical evaluations, and the assumptions made about the availability and computational efficiency of Q-values from source tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in reinforcement learning (RL) by focusing on the reduction of sample complexity in reward adaptation, which is a critical aspect of RL.
- The theoretical results presented are sound, and the paper provides a clear and well-structured exposition of the problem and its proposed solution.
- The empirical results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various domains, including grid-world and continuous control environments.
- The paper introduces an innovative approach to pruning actions based on Q-values from source tasks, which is a novel application in the context of RL.
- The methodology is supported by a rigorous mathematical framework, including proofs that the proposed method maintains the optimality of the policy, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a clearer presentation, particularly in the introduction and related work sections, to enhance its accessibility and comprehensibility.
- The empirical evaluations are limited to specific domains, which might not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method across a broader range of environments.
- The assumptions made about the availability and computational efficiency of Q-values from source tasks are not thoroughly explored, which could impact the practical applicability of the method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scope and applicability.
- The related work section could be expanded to provide a more comprehensive overview of existing methods and their comparisons with the proposed approach.
- There are some minor errors and inconsistencies in the manuscript, such as incorrect references and typographical errors, which need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and usage of Q-min in the context of the proposed method, especially in relation to the Q-values from source tasks?
- How do the authors justify the assumption that the Q-values from source tasks are easily accessible and computationally efficient?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in relation to the size of the state and action spaces?
- In the empirical evaluations, could the authors consider using more complex environments to better demonstrate the scalability and applicability of the method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of sample complexity and computational efficiency, especially in more challenging environments?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might impact its practical applicability?
