 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the intersection of group robustness and poisoning defense in machine learning, focusing on the challenges that arise when these two methods are applied concurrently. It highlights how group robustness methods, which aim to enhance model performance on underrepresented groups, can inadvertently amplify poison samples, thereby benefiting adversaries. Similarly, poisoning defenses, designed to eliminate outliers, often eliminate minority samples, which harms group robustness. The paper provides empirical evidence and theoretical insights to support these findings, suggesting that current methods may not effectively distinguish between legitimate minority samples and poison samples. It also discusses the implications of these findings for the design of future methods that can balance these competing objectives.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic, highlighting the need for a balanced approach in machine learning that considers both model performance and fairness, particularly in the context of group robustness and poisoning attacks.
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the problem and its implications, with a logical flow that aids in understanding the complex interactions between group robustness and poisoning defense.
- The empirical results are robust, demonstrating the significant impact of group robustness methods on poison samples and the potential for these methods to harm underrepresented groups.
- The paper provides theoretical insights that complement the empirical findings, enhancing the understanding of the underlying mechanisms and their implications.
- The paper is original in its approach, providing a novel perspective on the intersection of group robustness and poisoning defense, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's findings are not surprising, as it is well-known that group robustness methods can amplify outliers, including poison samples, and that poisoning defenses can eliminate minority samples.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the implications of these findings for the design of future methods that balance group robustness and poisoning defense.
- The empirical results are limited to a few datasets, which may not generalize well to other scenarios or datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the impact of hyperparameters on the results, which could be crucial for understanding the robustness of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of its methodology, such as the potential impact of different types of poisoning attacks or the generalizability of the findings to other machine learning models or datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the broader implications of its findings, including potential solutions or mitigations for the identified issues.

**Questions:**
- How do the findings of this paper relate to the broader literature on fairness in machine learning, particularly in the context of poisoning attacks?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the impact of different hyperparameters on the results, especially in terms of how they affect the identification and amplification of poison samples?
- How might the findings of this paper be applied in practical scenarios to improve the design of machine learning systems that balance group robustness and poisoning defense?
- Could the authors expand on the theoretical insights provided in the paper, particularly in terms of how they might be used to guide the development of more effective group robustness methods that do not amplify poison samples?
- In light of the findings on the impact of poisoning defenses on group robustness, how might these findings be used to inform the design of more effective poisoning defenses that do not harm underrepresented groups?
