 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Contextualized Policy Recovery (CPR), a method for learning interpretable policies in medical decision-making scenarios. CPR models context-specific policies as linear observation-to-action mappings, leveraging historical patient information to generate interpretable decision models. This approach allows for the identification of outlier patients and the exploration of context-specific decision models, which is particularly useful in medical settings where interpretability is crucial. The method is evaluated on two medical datasets and a simulated environment, showing promising results in terms of interpretability and performance. However, concerns are raised about the method's generalizability and the practicality of the interpretability claims, as well as the lack of a comprehensive comparison with other interpretable policy learning methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The application of the proposed method to medical decision-making tasks is highly relevant and impactful, addressing the need for interpretable and personalized policies in healthcare.
- The methodology is novel, particularly in its use of contextualized policy recovery, which allows for the identification of outlier patients and the exploration of context-specific decision models.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including real-world datasets and a simulated environment, which helps in validating the effectiveness and robustness of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem in the field of interpretable policy learning, which is crucial for ensuring the safety and effectiveness of AI systems in critical domains like healthcare.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other interpretable policy learning methods, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the strengths and limitations of the proposed method.
- The interpretability of the model is questionable, as the paper does not convincingly demonstrate how the learned policy parameters directly influence the decisions made by the model.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the proposed method to other domains or scenarios beyond medical decision-making.
- The method's reliance on historical patient information raises concerns about data privacy and the potential for bias in the training data.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the method, which could provide valuable insights into its applicability and potential pitfalls.
- The paper's evaluation could be improved by including more diverse datasets and a broader range of tasks to better assess the method's performance and generalizability.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other interpretable policy learning methods, particularly in terms of performance and interpretability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples of how the interpretability of the model is ensured, especially in the context of medical decision-making?
- How does the method perform when applied to other domains or scenarios beyond medical decision-making?
- What are the specific limitations of the method, and how do these impact its applicability and effectiveness?
- How does the method handle the potential for bias in the training data, and what steps are taken to mitigate this risk?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results or analyses, such as the impact of different hyperparameters or the effects of data splits on the model's performance?
