 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel algorithm named ProUD for semi-supervised domain generalization (SSDG), focusing on scenarios where only one domain is labeled and the rest are unlabeled. The methodology includes prototype-based pseudo-labeling, uncertainty-adaptive integration of unlabeled domains, and contrastive learning for domain-invariant representations. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through experiments on three benchmark datasets, showing superior performance over existing baselines. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the method, as it heavily relies on existing techniques, and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise language that effectively communicates the ideas and results.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed method significantly outperforms existing baselines in domain generalization and semi-supervised learning.
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem of data inequality across domains, which is highly relevant in real-world scenarios.
- The use of domain-aware prototypes and uncertainty-adaptive mixing strategies is innovative and effectively leverages the potential of samples from unlabeled source domains.
- The methodology is well-motivated, with a clear problem statement and a well-structured approach to addressing the challenges of domain generalization in semi-supervised learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited as it heavily relies on existing techniques such as prototype-based pseudo-labeling, uncertainty-adaptive integration of unlabeled domains, and contrastive learning for domain-invariant representations.
- The paper lacks comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that have been specifically designed for semi-supervised domain generalization.
- The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to verify the effectiveness of each component of the proposed method.
- The method's performance is not thoroughly analyzed, especially in terms of its limitations and potential failure cases.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the ethical implications of the work, which is crucial given the focus on addressing data inequality.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not include a reproducibility statement.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed ablation studies to verify the effectiveness of each component of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more detailed analysis on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential failure cases?
- How does the method perform when the number of labeled samples in the labeled domain is small?
- Can the authors provide more detailed discussions on the ethical implications of their work, especially in the context of addressing data inequality?
- How does the method perform when the domain shift is significant, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the results?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations on the method's reliance on existing techniques and how it innovates beyond these methods?
