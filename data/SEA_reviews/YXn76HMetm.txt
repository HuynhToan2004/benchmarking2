 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to pixel-level active learning for semantic segmentation under domain shift, utilizing a hyperbolic neural network to estimate the unexplained class complexity and acquire informative pixels. The hyperbolic radius is interpreted as a metric for class complexity, and the method uses a combination of this metric and prediction uncertainty to select the most informative pixels for labeling. The proposed method, HALO, is evaluated on multiple benchmarks and demonstrates superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty and unclear justification of the hyperbolic radius's interpretation as a measure of class complexity.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel geometric interpretation of the hyperbolic radius as a proxy for the concept of unexplained class complexity, which is a significant advancement in the field of active learning for semantic segmentation.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the hyperbolic radius, including its correlation with class complexity and label scarcity, which is supported by experimental evidence.
- The proposed method, HALO, demonstrates superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods on multiple benchmarks, indicating its effectiveness in active learning scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a good balance between theoretical analysis and practical application.
- The idea of using a hyperbolic neural network for active learning is innovative and well-explained, with a clear motivation for its use in active learning for semantic segmentation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as the hyperbolic neural network and hyperbolic radius are well-studied concepts, and the main contribution seems to be the application of these concepts to active learning for semantic segmentation.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The justification for interpreting the hyperbolic radius as a measure of class complexity is not convincing, and the paper does not adequately address why this interpretation is superior to existing methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more experimental evidence to support the claim that the hyperbolic radius can accurately estimate class complexity?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of class imbalance in the data, particularly in scenarios where some classes have significantly fewer labels than others?
- Could the authors clarify the rationale behind interpreting the hyperbolic radius as a measure of class complexity, and how this interpretation differs from existing methods?
- How does the proposed method perform under different labeling budgets, and what are the implications of using different budgets on the overall performance of the method?
