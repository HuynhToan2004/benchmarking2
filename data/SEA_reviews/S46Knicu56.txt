 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for estimating the Average Dose-Response Function (ADRF) in the presence of measurement errors in treatment variables. The authors propose a variational framework that integrates neural networks to model the data-generating process, allowing for the estimation of treatment effects by treating unobserved treatments as latent variables. The framework leverages a variational autoencoder (VAE) to approximate the posterior distribution of the treatment variable, which is then used to estimate the ADRF. The methodology is validated through simulations and semi-synthetic datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency compared to existing methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited empirical evaluation and the lack of a thorough discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for estimating the Average Dose-Response Function (ADRF) in the presence of measurement errors, which is a significant and relevant problem in the field of causal inference.
- The proposed method integrates neural networks to model the data-generating process, which allows for the estimation of treatment effects by treating unobserved treatments as latent variables.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and the experimental results.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, comparing the proposed method with several state-of-the-art methods and demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency.
- The paper is original in its approach, combining ideas from causal inference with those from variational inference, which is a novel and interesting combination.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the method, particularly how the variational inference framework is used to estimate the ADRF and how the error in the treatment measurement is handled.
- The empirical evaluation of the method is limited, with only a few datasets used for validation, which raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing methods that also handle measurement error, such as those by Huang and Zhang (2023), which could provide a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- There are some minor errors and inconsistencies in the paper, such as incorrect references and unclear definitions, which need to be addressed for clarity and accuracy.
- The paper's reliance on strong assumptions, such as the independence of measurement error from covariates, treatment, and potential outcomes, may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where these assumptions are not met.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the variational inference framework is used to estimate the ADRF and how the error in the treatment measurement is handled?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods that also handle measurement error, such as those by Huang and Zhang (2023)?
- Could the authors discuss the assumptions made in the model, particularly the strong assumptions like the independence of measurement error from covariates, treatment, and potential outcomes?
- In the experiments, how were the hyperparameters for the baselines chosen, and why were certain baselines not included in the evaluation?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the measurement error in the model, particularly how it affects the estimation of the ADRF?
