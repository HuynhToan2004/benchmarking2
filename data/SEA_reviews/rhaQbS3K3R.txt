 **Summary:**
The paper explores the performance of object recognition models on crowdsourced, globally representative datasets such as DollarStreet and GeoDE, comparing it to traditional benchmarks like ImageNet. The study reveals that progress on these datasets lags significantly behind standard benchmarks, and improvements on standard benchmarks often exacerbate geographic disparities. The authors suggest that this could be due to the lack of geographic diversity in existing datasets, which could mislead about the true performance of models in real-world scenarios. The paper proposes using more curated and representative training datasets to mitigate these issues, emphasizing the need for such datasets in the research community.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear and concise presentation of the research questions and findings.
- The research addresses a significant and timely issue in the field of computer vision, specifically the underperformance of models on globally representative datasets compared to standard benchmarks.
- The paper includes a comprehensive empirical study involving nearly 100 vision models across various architectures and datasets, providing a robust evaluation of model performance.
- The paper introduces new metrics for measuring progress and disparities in model performance, which are novel and potentially valuable for future research.
- The findings of the study, such as the exacerbation of geographic disparities with improvements on standard benchmarks, are significant and could lead to new research directions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the reasons behind the observed performance disparities between models on globally representative datasets and standard benchmarks.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the models were trained and fine-tuned, which could affect the reliability and generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its approach, such as the potential biases in the datasets used and the generalizability of the findings to other types of datasets or models.
- The paper's claims about the progress gap and geographic disparities are not well-supported by the data, and the statistical significance of the results is questionable.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential solutions and interventions to address the identified issues, such as the curation of more representative datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the training and fine-tuning procedures for the models used in the study, including the specific datasets and methods employed?
- How do the authors ensure that the models used in the study are not biased towards specific geographic regions or types of images?
- What are the specific contributions of the paper beyond the empirical findings, and how do these contributions advance the field of computer vision?
- Can you provide more details on the statistical significance of the results presented in the paper, particularly the claims about the progress gap and geographic disparities?
- How do the findings of this study generalize to other types of datasets or models, and what implications do they have for the broader field of computer vision?
