 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to training neural networks using node perturbation (NP), which involves injecting noise into the activation of neurons to learn the gradient direction. This method, inspired by biological learning, aims to improve upon traditional backpropagation (BP) by reducing the reliance on network derivatives and enhancing stability in noisy systems. The authors propose modifications to the NP method, including iterative node perturbation and activity-based node perturbation, to improve performance and reduce computational overhead. The paper also explores the use of decorrelation techniques to enhance learning efficiency and proposes a new training algorithm, decorrelated activity-based node perturbation (DANP), which shows competitive performance with backpropagation in certain scenarios. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods, and the experimental results do not convincingly outperform traditional methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and concise explanations of the proposed methods.
- The use of node perturbation (NP) as an alternative to backpropagation (BP) for training neural networks is innovative and offers a different approach to learning in neural networks.
- The paper introduces a new training algorithm, decorrelated activity-based node perturbation (DANP), which shows promising results in improving performance and reducing computational overhead compared to traditional NP methods.
- The paper provides a theoretical foundation for node perturbation, which helps in understanding the mathematical underpinnings of the proposed methods.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, including a variety of network architectures and training settings, which help to validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as the modifications to the NP method, such as iterative node perturbation and activity-based node perturbation, are not significantly different from existing methods.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other existing methods, such as weight propagation (WP), which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods over traditional BP, especially in terms of performance and training efficiency.
- The paper does not adequately address the biological plausibility of the proposed methods, which is a significant claim made in the introduction.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous discussion on the computational complexity and training time of the proposed methods, especially in comparison to traditional BP.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and training time of the proposed methods compared to traditional BP?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the noise process is inaccessible, and what are the implications for practical applications?
- Can the authors clarify the biological plausibility of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of how they align with known biological mechanisms?
- What are the specific advantages of using NP over other gradient-based methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and biological plausibility?
- Could the authors elaborate on the training setup for the experiments, particularly how the learning rates were determined and how they affect the results?
- How does the proposed method compare to other gradient-free training methods, such as those based on evolutionary algorithms?
