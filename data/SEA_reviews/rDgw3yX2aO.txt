 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedGT, a novel framework for identifying malicious clients in federated learning (FL) through a group testing mechanism. FedGT groups clients into overlapping test groups, allowing the server to observe the aggregated model of clients within each group and perform a test to identify malicious clients. The framework is designed to balance privacy and security, with the server learning the aggregated model of clients in each group, representing a middle ground between traditional federated learning and secure aggregation. The paper presents experiments on various datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR-10, and ISIC2019, demonstrating FedGT's ability to identify malicious clients effectively. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its reliance on existing group testing techniques, and the scalability and practicality of the framework are concerns, particularly in large-scale settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed method.
- The idea of using group testing to identify malicious clients in federated learning is novel and innovative.
- The paper is supported by extensive experimental results on various datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR-10, and ISIC2019, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The integration of group testing with federated learning is a significant contribution to the field, addressing a critical issue of malicious client identification in federated learning.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the privacy-security trade-off, which is crucial for the practical implementation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is questioned due to its reliance on existing group testing techniques, which have been previously applied in similar contexts.
- The scalability of the proposed method is a concern, particularly in large-scale settings, where the overhead of group testing could become prohibitive.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the privacy implications of the proposed method, which is crucial given the sensitivity of federated learning to privacy concerns.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other existing methods for identifying malicious clients in federated learning, which could have strengthened the paper's argument for its effectiveness.
- The paper's assumptions about the number of malicious clients and the distribution of malicious clients are not clearly stated, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- There are several grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper, which detract from its professional quality.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the privacy implications of the proposed method, especially in terms of how it protects against potential privacy leaks?
- How does the proposed method perform in settings with a large number of clients, and what are the computational costs associated with group testing in such scenarios?
- Can you provide more details on the group testing protocol used in the proposed method, particularly how it is adapted from traditional group testing to work in a federated learning context?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of malicious clients colluding to manipulate the group testing results?
- Can you discuss the potential for extending the proposed method to other types of attacks, such as model poisoning attacks?
- What are the specific assumptions about the number and distribution of malicious clients that underlie the experimental results presented in the paper?
