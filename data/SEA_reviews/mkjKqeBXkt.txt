 **Summary:**
The paper introduces KITS, a novel inductive spatio-temporal kriging method that addresses the graph gap issue in existing inductive spatio-temporal kriging methods. KITS employs an increment training strategy by adding virtual nodes to the training graph to mimic unobserved nodes, thereby reducing the graph gap. The method also incorporates Reference-based Feature Fusion and Node-aware Cycle Regulation to enhance the learning of virtual nodes and ensure reliable pseudo labels. Extensive experiments across multiple datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of KITS, showing significant improvements over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant issue in existing inductive spatio-temporal kriging methods, namely the graph gap, and proposes a novel solution with the Increment training strategy, which inserts virtual nodes into the training graph.
- The proposed method, KITS, is well-motivated and clearly explained, with a focus on the inductive setting which is more practical and less restrictive compared to traditional transductive settings.
- The experiments are comprehensive, involving multiple datasets and baselines, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes detailed ablation studies that help in understanding the contributions of different components of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity on the definition and application of the graph gap, which could benefit from a clearer explanation or visual representation.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The training strategy and the design of the Increment training strategy, particularly the number and positioning of virtual nodes, are not well-explained, which could lead to confusion about their impact on the model's performance.
- There are minor typographical and formatting errors that need correction.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the graph gap and its impact on the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method handle different spatial distributions of unobserved nodes, and what are the implications for the model's performance in such scenarios?
- In the Increment training strategy, how are virtual nodes positioned, and what are the implications of this positioning on the model's performance?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these could affect its applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the model perform when different numbers of virtual nodes are added, and what are the implications of this on the model's performance and generalization?
