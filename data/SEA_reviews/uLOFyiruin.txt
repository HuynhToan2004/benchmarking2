 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Babel-ImageNet, a multilingual dataset derived from ImageNet, translated into 92 languages using BabelNet and WordNet synsets. This dataset is used to evaluate multilingual CLIP models across different languages, highlighting the performance disparities between high-resource and low-resource languages. The study demonstrates that models trained on high-resource languages often perform poorly in low-resource languages, suggesting a need for more robust cross-lingual models. The dataset is also used to investigate the effectiveness of different models and training strategies, including adapter training and language-specific fine-tuning. The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the performance of various models on this dataset, offering insights into the challenges of multilingual CLIP models and potential solutions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive analysis of multilingual CLIP models, including OpenCLIP, M-CLIP, and Sentence Transformer.
- It introduces a new benchmark, Babel-ImageNet, which is a valuable resource for the community and could be used to evaluate multilingual models more effectively.
- The authors provide a thorough analysis of the performance of different models across various languages, highlighting the challenges in multilingual CLIP models.
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the translation process, which is crucial for understanding the performance of models across different languages.
- The study shows that multilingual CLIP models perform worse in high-resource languages than in English, suggesting a need for more robust cross-lingual models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of translation quality, particularly the potential inaccuracies in translations which could affect the performance of the models.
- The evaluation is limited to zero-shot image classification, and it would be beneficial to include other tasks such as image-text retrieval to provide a more comprehensive analysis.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the dataset used for training the models, which could be crucial for understanding the performance disparities observed.
- The translation process used in the paper, which relies on machine translation, may not be the most effective or accurate method, especially for low-resource languages.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the potential biases in the translation process and how these might affect the performance of the models.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the translation process, particularly how they ensure the accuracy of translations and handle potential inaccuracies?
- How does the performance of the models vary when different translation methods are used?
- Could the authors include more languages in their analysis to provide a more comprehensive view of multilingual CLIP models?
- How does the performance of the models compare when different datasets are used for training?
- What are the potential biases in the translation process, and how might these affect the performance of the models?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of using more advanced translation methods, such as neural machine translation, to improve the accuracy of translations?
