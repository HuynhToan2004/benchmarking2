 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Chain of Hindsight (CoH) for training large language models (LLMs) using human feedback. CoH leverages a sequence of model outputs and their associated feedback to fine-tune the model, conditioning it to generate outputs that align with human preferences. The method is evaluated on two tasks, summarization and dialogue, showing improvements over existing methods like RLHF and supervised fine-tuning. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty, lack of clarity in some sections, and insufficient experimental validation. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through human evaluations, but the results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper does not sufficiently address the scalability and generalizability of the method across different model sizes and domains.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The proposed method, CoH, is straightforward and effective, requiring minimal modifications to existing training pipelines.
- CoH is evaluated on two different tasks, demonstrating its versatility and potential applicability across various domains.
- The method's simplicity and efficiency are highlighted, making it a potentially valuable tool for practitioners seeking to improve LLM performance.
- The experimental results show that CoH outperforms other methods like RLHF and SFT, indicating its effectiveness in aligning LLMs with human preferences.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear figures and a logical structure that aids in understanding the complexities of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it bears resemblance to existing techniques like HIR and RLHF, with the main difference being the use of a chain of hindsight feedback.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of CoH with different model sizes and its generalizability across various domains.
- There is a significant reliance on human evaluators, which can introduce bias and subjectivity, and the paper does not adequately address how these biases were mitigated.
- The paper does not include a detailed ablation study, which could help in understanding the impact of different components of the method on its performance.
- The paper's focus on specific tasks (summarization and dialogue) limits its applicability to other domains, and the method's effectiveness in these other domains is not explored.
- The method's simplicity might be seen as a weakness, as it lacks the complexity of other methods like HIR, which could limit its effectiveness in more complex scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the method's simplicity, which is both a strength and a weakness, impacts its effectiveness in more complex scenarios?
- How does the method handle the potential for bias in human evaluations, and what steps were taken to mitigate this bias?
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability of CoH with different model sizes and its generalizability across various domains?
- In the human evaluations, how were the evaluators selected, and what criteria were used to ensure their reliability and unbiased assessments?
- How does the method perform when compared to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Can the authors elaborate on the method's limitations and discuss potential directions for future research to address these limitations?
