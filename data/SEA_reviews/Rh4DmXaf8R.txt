 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the challenge of predicting multiple steps ahead in model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL), particularly focusing on the compounding errors that can occur in long-horizon predictions. The authors propose a novel training objective that involves a weighted sum of the loss function at various future horizons, with exponentially decaying weights leading to significant improvements in long-horizon R2 scores, especially in noisy environments. This method is tested using a soft actor-critic (SAC) agent in both pure batch and iterated batch RL scenarios, showing mixed results. The paper is praised for its clarity and detailed experimental analysis, although concerns are raised about the generalizability of the findings to more complex environments and the lack of a comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) by focusing on the compounding errors that can occur in long-horizon predictions.
- The proposed method of training one-step models to predict multiple steps ahead is innovative and well-explained, with a clear motivation for its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear structure and detailed experimental analysis that supports the claims made.
- The authors have conducted a thorough ablation study on different strategies for weighting the loss function at multiple future horizons, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is simple to implement and shows potential for improving the performance of MBRL agents in practical scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily uses the Cartpole environment for testing, which is a simple and low-dimensional system. It would be beneficial to see how the proposed method performs in more complex and high-dimensional environments.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, which makes it difficult to gauge the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the improvements are not statistically significant in some cases.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for overfitting in the models, which could limit their generalizability to different environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the weighting of the loss function at different horizons affects the performance of the model, especially in more complex environments?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in real-world applications?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the Cartpole environment for testing, and what steps are being taken to ensure the generalizability of the findings to more complex environments?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the number of seeds used and the statistical significance of the results?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to include more complex environments, such as those found in the MuJoCo simulator?
