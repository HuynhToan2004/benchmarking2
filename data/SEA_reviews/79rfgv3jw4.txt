 **Summary:**
The paper explores the compatibility between strong AI agents and weaker human or AI partners in a collaborative chess setting. It introduces three methodologies to enhance the compatibility of strong AI agents with weaker partners, aiming to improve performance in scenarios where the strong agent must adapt to the weaker partner's actions. The authors evaluate these methodologies using two frameworks: Stochastic Tag Team and Hand and Brain, and demonstrate that the proposed methods can outperform traditional AI agents in certain scenarios. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for broader applications in human-AI collaboration and the design of more compatible AI agents.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a novel and significant problem in the field of human-AI collaboration, focusing on the compatibility between strong AI agents and weaker human or AI partners.
- It introduces three methodologies to enhance the compatibility of strong AI agents with weaker partners, which are evaluated using two frameworks and shown to outperform traditional AI agents in certain scenarios.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the mechanisms by which the agents achieve skill-compatibility, which is a qualitatively and measurably distinct trait from raw performance.
- The proposed methodologies are generalizable to other domains and could potentially be applied to improve the compatibility of AI agents with weaker human or AI partners in other complex decision-making settings.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodologies and results, and includes detailed experimental setups and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence that the proposed methodologies are effective in scenarios where the strong agent must adapt to the weaker partner's actions. The experiments primarily focus on scenarios where the strong agent is already adapted to the weaker partner, which may not reflect real-world scenarios where the strong agent must adapt in real-time.
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the concept of "skill-compatibility," which is critical for understanding the contributions and implications of the research.
- The evaluation of the proposed methodologies is limited to chess and does not explore other domains, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the proposed methodologies with existing methods, which could help in understanding the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methodologies and the potential negative societal impacts of the research.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed methodologies be adapted to scenarios where the strong agent must adapt to the weaker partner's actions in real-time, such as in a real-time strategy game?
- How does the concept of "skill-compatibility" relate to the notion of "interpretability" in AI, and how does it contribute to the design of more compatible AI agents?
- How do the proposed methodologies compare with existing methods for improving the compatibility of AI agents with weaker partners, such as adversarial training or multi-agent reinforcement learning?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the experimental setups and results, particularly in sections where the results are not clearly explained?
- How do the authors plan to address the limitations of the proposed methodologies, such as the potential negative societal impacts and the limited generalizability of the findings?
