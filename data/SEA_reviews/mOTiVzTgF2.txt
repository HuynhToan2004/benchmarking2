 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Transformer architecture, ResiDual, which combines elements of both Pre-LN and Post-LN to address the challenges of gradient vanishing and representation collapse in deep transformer models. The authors provide theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of this approach, showing that ResiDual outperforms existing models in terms of convergence and representation capacity. The paper is well-written and presents a clear motivation for the proposed method, though it has been noted for its limited novelty and the incremental nature of its contributions. The experiments, while comprehensive, are criticized for their scope and depth, particularly in terms of the datasets and models used.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations that effectively motivate the proposed method.
- The proposed method, ResiDual, is simple and easy to implement, making it accessible for further research.
- Theoretical analysis and empirical evidence support the effectiveness of ResiDual in addressing the challenges of gradient vanishing and representation collapse in deep transformer models.
- The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of the ResiDual model, which is supported by empirical evidence, enhancing the credibility of the proposed method.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the ResiDual model, showing its ability to outperform existing models in terms of convergence and representation capacity.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as the proposed method is a combination of existing techniques (Pre-LN and Post-LN) without significant innovation.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with marginal improvements over existing methods and a lack of comprehensive evaluation across different datasets and model sizes.
- The theoretical analysis, while interesting, is seen as incremental and does not sufficiently differentiate the proposed method from existing models.
- The paper lacks a broader comparison with other models, such as those using different layer normalization methods or other architectural variations, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The empirical results are limited to specific datasets and model sizes, which may not generalize well to other scenarios or model architectures.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other models, including those that use different layer normalization methods or other architectural variations, to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations for the empirical results, particularly the marginal improvements observed over existing methods?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of convergence speed and stability across different datasets and model sizes?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical contributions of the paper, particularly how they differentiate from existing models and contribute significantly to the field?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including a broader range of datasets and model sizes, to better evaluate the generalizability and applicability of the proposed method?
