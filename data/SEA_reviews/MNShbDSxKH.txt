 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel generative neuro-symbolic visual reasoning model named GNSVR, which leverages large language models (LLMs) to generate and reuse modules for visual reasoning tasks. The model is structured into three stages: module initialization, module generation, and module execution. During the initialization stage, the LLM identifies the need for new modules and generates their signatures. In the generation stage, the LLM generates the code for the new modules, which are then evaluated against test cases. Finally, the generated modules are executed to reason about new images. The model is evaluated on various tasks such as visual question answering, referring expression comprehension, and image editing, demonstrating its ability to generalize and adapt to new tasks. However, the paper has been critiqued for its reliance on pre-defined modules and the need for extensive manual intervention, which may limit its practical applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear figures and a comprehensive appendix that aids in understanding the methodology.
- The approach of using LLMs to generate and reuse modules for visual reasoning tasks is innovative and could potentially reduce the need for extensive manual intervention.
- The method demonstrates competitive performance on standard tasks like visual question answering and referring expression comprehension, and the modules learned from one task can be transferred to new tasks.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the impact of the LLM model on the performance of the method, showing that the method's performance improves with the use of better LLMs.
- The paper is original in its approach, combining the strengths of neuro-symbolic approaches with the flexibility and generalization of large language models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not clearly distinguish its method from similar existing approaches such as ViperGPT and VisProg, which also use LLMs for module generation.
- The method relies heavily on pre-defined modules and requires extensive manual intervention, which may limit its practical applicability and scalability.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the LLM in generating code for new modules, which could lead to incorrect or incomplete code.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that the modules are reusable across different tasks, and it does not discuss the generalization ability of the method.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other neuro-symbolic approaches, which could help in understanding the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- How does the method ensure the correctness and completeness of the code generated by the LLM for new modules?
- Can the method be applied to other tasks beyond those evaluated in the paper?
- How does the method handle the generation of modules for tasks that are not directly related to the training data?
- How does the method compare to other neuro-symbolic approaches in terms of performance, generalization, and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the reusability of the modules across different tasks and the generalization ability of the method?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting to the training data, especially when the number of training examples is limited?
- How does the method ensure that the generated modules are not overly specific to the training data and can generalize to new, unseen data?
