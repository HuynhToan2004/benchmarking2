 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for amortized inference in probabilistic graphical models (PGMs) called Delta-AI. This approach leverages the sparsity of PGMs to optimize a local loss function, which is a variant of the GFlowNet objective, to learn amortized inference. Delta-AI aims to improve training efficiency by reducing the number of variables involved in each training step and by utilizing local credit assignment. The method is tested on synthetic PGMs and a real-world dataset (MNIST), demonstrating faster training times compared to existing methods. However, the paper's scope is limited to discrete variables, and the experimental evaluation is not extensive enough to convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness in more complex or continuous settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the methodology and its theoretical underpinnings, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, Delta-AI, is novel and innovative, offering a new approach to amortized inference that addresses the limitations of existing methods like GFlowNets.
- The method is supported by a strong theoretical foundation, including the derivation of a local constraint that matches the conditional distribution of a variable given its Markov blanket.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of Delta-AI in terms of faster training times compared to other methods like GFlowNets.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of related work, effectively positioning Delta-AI within the broader context of amortized inference and probabilistic graphical models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to discrete variables, which may not be representative of more complex or continuous settings where PGMs are commonly used.
- The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, with the method being tested only on a small number of synthetic PGMs and a single real-world dataset (MNIST).
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implications of the method, such as the scalability of the proposed method to larger and more complex PGMs.
- There is a lack of discussion on the societal impact of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for some applications of PGMs.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other methods, particularly those that use continuous variables or more complex PGMs.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method in depth, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when applied to PGMs with continuous variables, and what are the challenges or modifications required for such applications?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method to larger and more complex PGMs?
- How does the method compare to other amortized inference methods, particularly those that use different approaches or architectures?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their method, especially in applications where PGMs are used for decision-making or prediction?
- What are the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how might these affect its practical application in different scenarios?
