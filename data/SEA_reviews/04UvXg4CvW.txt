 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel knowledge distillation framework, EPIC, aimed at distilling deep Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) into shallower models while maintaining or even improving performance. The approach quantitatively analyzes the expressive power gap between deep and shallow GNNs, formulating it as an embedding regression problem based on polynomial approximation theory. This analysis provides an upper bound for the approximation error, which decreases rapidly with the number of student layers, indicating exponential convergence to zero. The methodology involves a three-term loss function and a novel algorithm for selecting the number of student layers, which is validated on large-scale benchmarks such as OGB. Despite the theoretical and experimental advancements, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach and its limited generalization to other datasets beyond those tested.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the expressive power gap between deep and shallow GNNs, which is a novel approach to knowledge distillation.
- It introduces a novel method for selecting the number of student layers, which is an innovative aspect of the proposed framework.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the theoretical analysis and experimental results.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed EPIC method can achieve comparable or better performance compared to the teacher model, while significantly reducing the number of layers.
- The paper provides a comprehensive ablation study, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not clearly distinguish between the "student" and "teacher" models, which could lead to confusion in understanding the roles and relationships between these models.
- The methodology for selecting the number of student layers seems limited to spectral-based GNNs, which may not generalize well to other types of GNNs.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a comparison with other baselines such as KD4GNN, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could be critical for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the improvements in performance are marginal in some cases.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the distinction between the "student" and "teacher" models, especially in the context of knowledge distillation?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to non-spectral-based GNNs, such as GCN and GAT?
- Can the authors provide more details on the methodology for selecting the number of student layers, especially in the context of non-spectral-based GNNs?
- Why was the KD4GNN baseline not included in the comparison, and how does the proposed method compare to this baseline?
- How does the proposed method handle the limitations discussed, such as the inability to generalize well to other datasets beyond those tested?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or proofs for the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly Assumption 1?
- How does the proposed method compare to other distillation methods that do not use polynomial approximation, such as those based on graph attention networks?
