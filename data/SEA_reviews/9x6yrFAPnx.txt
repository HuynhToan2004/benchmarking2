 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel risk-sensitive reinforcement learning (RL) algorithm, specifically designed for low-rank Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) with nonlinear function approximation. The algorithm, termed ELA, is an oracle-efficient representation learning algorithm that optimizes the Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR) metric. It leverages an MLE oracle to learn model dynamics and constructs Upper Confidence Bound (UCB)-type bonuses to encourage exploration. The paper also introduces ELLA, an algorithm that combines ELA with a computationally efficient planning oracle, LSVI, to enhance computational efficiency. The theoretical analysis of these algorithms includes sample complexity and computational efficiency, demonstrating the first provably efficient algorithm for CVaR RL in low-rank MDPs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of ideas.
- It introduces a novel algorithm, ELA, which is the first provably efficient algorithm for CVaR RL in low-rank MDPs, addressing a significant gap in the literature.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the algorithm, demonstrating that ELA achieves an epsilon-optimal CVaR with O(1/epsilon^2) samples.
- It introduces ELLA, a computationally efficient planning oracle that makes the algorithm only require polynomial running time with an MLE oracle.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the algorithm, demonstrating the sample complexity of the algorithm and its dependence on various parameters.
- The problem setting is significant and interesting, focusing on risk-sensitive RL in low-rank MDPs, which is a promising direction for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical results to validate the theoretical claims, which could enhance the paper's credibility and practical applicability.
- The computational complexity of the algorithm is high, and the paper does not discuss potential methods to reduce this complexity.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing algorithms, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and sample complexity.
- The algorithm's assumptions, such as the realizability assumption and the assumption that the learner has access to exact VI, are not discussed in detail, which could affect the understanding and applicability of the results.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by clarifying the definitions and notations used, especially in the preliminaries section.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the algorithm, which could provide a more balanced view of its strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide empirical results to validate the theoretical claims made in the paper?
- How does the algorithm perform in comparison to other existing algorithms in terms of computational efficiency and sample complexity?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their algorithm and potential directions for future research?
- What is the role of the realizability assumption in the algorithm's performance, and how does it affect the results?
- How does the algorithm handle the assumption that the learner has access to exact VI, and what are the implications if this assumption is not met?
- Can the authors clarify the definitions and notations used in the paper, particularly in the preliminaries section, to improve the paper's accessibility?
