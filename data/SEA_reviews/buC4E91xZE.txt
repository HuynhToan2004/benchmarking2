 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AnomalyCLIP, a method that adapts the CLIP model for zero-shot anomaly detection by learning object-agnostic text prompts to capture generic abnormality and normality in images. This approach leverages both global and local context optimization to learn object-agnostic text prompts, which are then used to optimize the CLIP model for anomaly detection. The method is tested on 17 real-world datasets across various domains, demonstrating superior zero-shot performance compared to existing methods. The paper also includes extensive ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of its components.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and clearly presents the proposed method and its experimental results.
- The motivation behind the proposed method is clear and reasonable, focusing on learning object-agnostic text prompts for zero-shot anomaly detection.
- The paper introduces a novel approach by learning object-agnostic text prompts, which is a significant advancement over previous methods that heavily relied on manual prompt engineering.
- Extensive experiments are conducted across 17 datasets from various domains, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the results and methodology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning its applicability to datasets with limited or no auxiliary data.
- There is a need for more visual results and detailed analysis, especially in the ablation study, to better understand the contributions of different components of the method.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other zero-shot anomaly detection methods, such as those using GPT-4 or other large language models.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential issues of overfitting to the auxiliary datasets used for training the text prompts.
- The paper's focus on zero-shot anomaly detection may limit its applicability to datasets with limited or no auxiliary data, which is a common scenario in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the selection of hyperparameters, particularly the number of learnable tokens in the text encoder?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of anomalies, such as missing objects or misplaced objects, which might not be adequately captured by the current method?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancy in the performance of AnomalyCLIP on different datasets, particularly the significant drop in performance on certain datasets like CVC-Clinicalb?
- How does the method handle datasets with limited or no auxiliary data? Could the authors discuss potential strategies for adapting the method to such scenarios?
- In light of the concerns about overfitting to auxiliary datasets, could the authors provide more details on how they addressed this issue in their experiments?
