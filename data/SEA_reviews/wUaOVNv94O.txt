 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for estimating spatial integrals by combining Monte Carlo methods with neural network integration techniques. The approach uses neural networks to approximate the anti-derivative of the integrand, thereby reducing variance and improving accuracy. The method is demonstrated to be unbiased and capable of producing lower variance estimations compared to traditional Monte Carlo and AutoInt methods. The authors show that this method can be particularly effective in solving PDEs and other scientific computing applications. Despite its theoretical contributions and experimental validation, the paper is criticized for its limited scope, lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and potential issues with scalability and computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach by combining Monte Carlo methods with neural network integration techniques, which is both theoretically sound and experimentally validated.
- The method is shown to be unbiased and capable of producing lower variance estimations compared to traditional Monte Carlo and AutoInt methods.
- The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method in solving PDEs and other scientific computing applications, providing a proof of concept that the proposed estimator is unbiased and experiences fewer variances.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The method's ability to approximate the anti-derivative of the integrand using neural networks is innovative and could be beneficial for a wide range of applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, which could limit its applicability in more complex or larger-scale problems.
- The method's reliance on neural networks for approximating the anti-derivative of the integrand might limit its effectiveness in scenarios where the integrand is not well-approximated by a neural network.
- The paper's focus on specific types of integrands, such as those with a known anti-derivative, may limit its general applicability to a broader range of integrals.
- There is a lack of discussion on the choice of the neural network architecture and the training parameters, which could affect the method's performance and scalability.
- The paper's experimental results are limited to a few specific applications, and it would be beneficial to see the method's performance on a more diverse set of problems to validate its broad applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the computational efficiency and scalability of your method compared to other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of runtime and memory usage?
- How does the method perform when the integrand is not well-approximated by a neural network? Are there any specific scenarios where the method might fail or produce inaccurate results?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the neural network architecture and the training parameters? How do these choices affect the method's performance and scalability?
- It would be interesting to see how the method performs on a more diverse set of problems, including those with different types of integrands and domains. Can you provide some examples or additional experimental results to support the method's broad applicability?
