 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the CODA framework, designed to simulate future data for training models in a data-centric approach to address concept drift. This approach utilizes feature correlation matrices to predict future data, which is then used to train various models including MLP, LightGBM, and FT-Transformer. The framework is evaluated across multiple datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing misclassification error rates. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the approach, including a theorem that establishes the reliability of predicted correlations under certain assumptions. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its limited scope in terms of the types of datasets used and the lack of comparison with more recent and varied baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel data-centric approach to tackle concept drift in temporal domain generalization, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method is simple, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The paper includes a thorough experimental analysis and sensitivity studies, providing a robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The use of feature correlation matrices to predict future data and the integration of this prediction into model training is a novel and effective approach.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and visualizations that aid in understanding the methodology and results.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to support the proposed method, enhancing the credibility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for a comprehensive understanding of its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, with only four datasets used, which may not adequately represent the diversity of real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and varied baselines, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalization ability of the model to unseen datasets, which is a critical aspect of domain generalization.
- The paper's assumptions, particularly regarding the boundedness and variance of features, may not hold true in all real-world scenarios, which could limit the applicability of the proposed method.
- The method's dependence on the accuracy of feature correlation matrix prediction raises concerns about its reliability, especially in scenarios where the prediction may not be accurate.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the feature correlation matrix prediction is inaccurate?
- Can the authors provide more details on the generalization ability of the model to unseen datasets?
- How does the method handle the issue of concept drift, particularly in terms of its ability to adapt to changing data distributions over time?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method compare to other data-centric approaches for temporal domain generalization, and what are the advantages or disadvantages compared to model-centric methods?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other recent baselines to establish the superiority of their method more convincingly?
- How does the method handle the assumption of similarity of data distribution across different time points, especially in scenarios where this assumption may not hold true?
