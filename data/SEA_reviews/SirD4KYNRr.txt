 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Invariant Attention mechanism designed to identify relationships across images while respecting their inherent structural properties. It addresses the issue of invariance to geometric transformations in vision architectures, which is typically learned from data rather than inherently present. The authors propose a kernel attention mechanism that is invariant to transformations, aiming to cluster images based on their underlying structure rather than their specific pixel arrangements. Theoretical proofs and empirical evidence are provided to support the claims, showcasing the method's ability to correctly cluster transformed data with intra-class variation. However, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental scope and lack of comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-understand notations and a clear motivation for the research.
- The authors have provided both theoretical proofs and empirical evidence to support their claims, enhancing the credibility of their findings.
- The proposed Invariant Attention mechanism is a novel approach to addressing the invariance to geometric transformations in vision architectures, which is a significant challenge in the field.
- The paper is innovative in its approach to kernel attention, which is typically learned from data, and instead, it proposes an invariant kernel that is optimized over transformations, allowing for the identification of clusters in transformed data.
- The theoretical results are well-presented, with proofs that are both interesting and non-trivial, demonstrating the depth of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly those that address the issue of invariance to geometric transformations in vision architectures.
- The experimental results are limited in scope, focusing primarily on simple datasets and not sufficiently demonstrating the method's effectiveness on more complex or varied datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including its computational efficiency and scalability.
- There are some minor issues with the presentation of the paper, such as unclear definitions and notations, which could be improved for better clarity and understanding.
- The paper's focus on kernel attention might limit its applicability to other attention mechanisms, such as self-attention, which are widely used in modern vision architectures.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on how the proposed method might be adapted to other attention mechanisms beyond kernel attention?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability with other existing methods?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, particularly on more complex or varied datasets, to demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of their method?
- In the theoretical proofs, why is the assumption that the infimal convex combination reach is bounded necessary? What are the implications if this assumption is not met?
- How does the paper address the issue of intra-class variation, especially in datasets where images within the same class might have significant differences in pixel arrangements?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and notations used in the paper, particularly those that are not standard or require additional explanation?
