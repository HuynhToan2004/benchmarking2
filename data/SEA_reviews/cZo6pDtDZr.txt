 **Summary:**
The paper presents two algorithms aimed at estimating and testing the collision probability of discrete distributions under local differential privacy (LDP) constraints. The first algorithm, a private estimation algorithm, is designed to efficiently estimate collision probability using fewer samples than previous methods, achieving a sample complexity of O~(1/α^2 ε^2). The second algorithm is a sequential testing algorithm that can adapt to different sample sizes based on the unknown parameter ε, offering a significant improvement in sample efficiency. The paper also provides theoretical guarantees and experimental validations, demonstrating the practical utility of these algorithms. Despite the theoretical advancements and experimental validations, the paper has been critiqued for its limited scope in terms of the number of distributions considered and the depth of theoretical analysis, particularly in the proof of Theorem 5.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem of estimating the collision probability of a discrete distribution, which is crucial in various scientific fields.
- The algorithms proposed are novel, well-explained, and supported by theoretical guarantees, providing significant improvements in sample complexity over existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-structured presentation of the results.
- The experiments conducted are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
- The paper provides a comprehensive literature review and positions its contributions within the context of existing research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to two distributions, which may not fully demonstrate the general applicability of the proposed algorithms.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the practical implications of the algorithms, such as the computational complexity and the scalability with the size of the support set.
- The proof of Theorem 5 is not convincing, with some parts appearing incorrect or unclear, and the overall proof structure is not well-explained.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed methods, such as the assumptions made about the uniformity of the support set and the scalability with the size of the support set.
- There is a lack of comparison with other recent works, particularly those that also address the estimation of collision probability under privacy constraints.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the societal impact of the proposed methods, which could be significant given the potential applications in areas like targeted advertising and data privacy.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the proof of Theorem 5, particularly the incorrect claims and the overall structure of the proof?
- How does the proposed algorithm perform when applied to distributions with a large support set, and what are the implications for computational complexity and sample efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of their algorithms with respect to the size of the support set?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare to other recent works that also address the estimation of collision probability under privacy constraints?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their work and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
- In the proof of Theorem 5, how does the use of martingales and the choice of δ affect the overall proof structure and the conclusions drawn?
