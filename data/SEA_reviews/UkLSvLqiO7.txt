 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the reproducibility of diffusion models, specifically focusing on the consistency of generated content from different models trained on the same dataset. It introduces a novel metric, the Reproducibility (RP) score, to quantify the similarity of samples generated from different diffusion models using the same initial noise. The study explores two regimes: the memorization regime where models are highly overparameterized and the generalization regime where models are trained on extensive datasets. The authors provide theoretical insights into the memorization regime and empirical evidence across various model architectures and training procedures. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for model generalization and the practical applications of diffusion models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and interesting problem of reproducibility in diffusion models, which is well-motivated and relevant to the current state of research in the field.
- The experimental design is comprehensive, involving a large number of diffusion models and training procedures, which supports the claims effectively.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear figures and a logical structure that aids in understanding complex concepts.
- The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the memorization regime, which is a significant contribution to the understanding of diffusion models.
- The study covers a wide range of diffusion models and training procedures, demonstrating the robustness of the findings across different settings.
- The paper includes a novel metric, the RP score, which effectively quantifies the reproducibility of diffusion models, contributing to the field's understanding of model performance and reliability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the experimental setup, particularly in the captions of figures and in the main text, to enhance the clarity and comprehensibility of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the study, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently explore the practical implications of the reproducibility of diffusion models, which could be a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is not well-connected to the empirical findings in Section 4, which could be improved by integrating the theoretical insights more directly with the experimental results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the relationship between the reproducibility and generalizability of diffusion models, as these concepts are closely linked.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup, particularly how the models were trained and the specifics of the training data used?
- How does the RP score compare to other metrics like the MAE score, and what are the implications of using different metrics to evaluate model reproducibility?
- In Figure 2, how does the model size affect the reproducibility and generalizability of diffusion models, and what are the theoretical implications of these findings?
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between the reproducibility and generalizability of diffusion models, and how these concepts influence each other in practical applications?
- How does the theoretical analysis in Section 3 relate to the empirical findings in Section 4, and what are the practical implications of these theoretical insights?
- Could the authors discuss the potential practical applications of the reproducibility of diffusion models, and how this property could be leveraged in various domains?
