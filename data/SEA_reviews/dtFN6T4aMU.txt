 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MAST, a novel framework for multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) that utilizes gradient-based topology evolution to train sparse networks for multiple agents. This methodology is supported by a hybrid TD-lambda target mechanism and a Soft Mellowmax operator to enhance learning targets and mitigate overestimation issues. The framework is evaluated across various benchmarks, showing significant improvements in training and inference FLOPs reduction while maintaining performance parity with dense models. However, concerns are raised about the incremental nature of the contributions, the lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, and the potential limitations in generalizability and scalability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear explanations of the methods and results.
- The proposed method, MAST, is innovative and well-motivated, particularly in its application of sparse training to multi-agent settings, which is not well-explored.
- The method achieves significant reductions in training and inference FLOPs, demonstrating its efficiency and potential for real-world applications.
- The paper includes a comprehensive ablation study that effectively isolates the contributions of different components of the method, providing valuable insights into their individual impacts.
- The results are compelling, showing significant improvements in performance and efficiency compared to existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of model size, computational efficiency, and performance under different sparsity levels.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the hyperparameters used in the experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the research, which is a significant oversight.
- There is a lack of discussion on the scalability and generalizability of the method, particularly in more complex or diverse multi-agent scenarios.
