 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel calibration method named Batch Calibration (BC) aimed at mitigating biases in large language models (LLMs) by estimating contextual biases from batched input. BC is designed to be a zero-shot, inference-only method that does not require additional training data, making it more practical and efficient compared to existing methods. The paper provides a systematic analysis of existing calibration methods, revealing their limitations and proposing BC as a unified solution. The method is validated on both natural language and image classification tasks, showing state-of-the-art performance. The paper also discusses the method's application to vision-language models and its robustness across various prompt engineering choices.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a good balance between theoretical analysis and empirical results.
- The proposed method, Batch Calibration (BC), is simple, effective, and can be applied in both zero-shot and few-shot settings.
- BC is a unified solution that addresses the limitations of existing calibration methods, and it is the first method to be evaluated on both natural language and image classification tasks.
- The paper provides a systematic analysis of existing calibration methods, which is crucial for understanding the current state of research in this area.
- The proposed method is validated on both natural language and image classification tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and versatility.
- The paper is well-motivated and well-organized, with a clear and concise presentation of the method and its implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of LLMs and the potential impact of prompt design choices.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other calibration methods, particularly those that use different approaches such as permutation-based prior estimation and Bayesian inference.
- The paper does not address the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the intuition behind the proposed method and a deeper analysis of the experimental results, including potential causes of observed performance degradation.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the impact of different prompt designs on the performance of BC, which is crucial for understanding the robustness of the method.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed method perform in different types of LLMs, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in applying BC to these models?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of prompt design choices on the performance of BC and provide recommendations for optimizing prompt design to improve BC's performance?
- How does the proposed method perform in different types of classification tasks, particularly those that are more complex or require different types of input data?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the experimental results, particularly the observed performance degradation in some cases, and suggest potential causes or solutions?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational cost and efficiency to other calibration methods, and what are the implications of these differences for practical implementation?
