 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training-free fine-tuning approach for sparse Large Language Models (LLMs), termed Dynamic Sparse No Training (DSQT). This method, inspired by Dynamic Sparse Training, updates sparse LLMs by iteratively pruning and growing weights to minimize the reconstruction error between dense and sparse models. DSQT does not require backpropagation or weight updates, making it computationally efficient and effective in enhancing the performance of sparse LLMs, especially at high sparsity levels. The method was tested on various models, including LLaMA-V1/V2, Vicuna, and OPT, showing improvements in perplexity on WikiText-2.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive review of related work that effectively positions the proposed method within the existing literature.
- The proposed method, DSQT, is simple yet effective, requiring no backpropagation or weight updates, which makes it computationally efficient and practical for on-device deployment.
- DSQT demonstrates strong empirical results, showing improvements in perplexity on WikiText-2 and zero-shot tasks, even at high sparsity levels, which is a significant advancement in the field of sparse LLMs.
- The paper includes a thorough analysis of the method's performance, including sensitivity analysis and robustness to calibration samples, which helps in understanding the method's effectiveness and limitations.
- DSQT is compatible with existing pruning methods like SparseGPT and Wanda, making it a versatile tool for enhancing the performance of sparse LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other training-free fine-tuning methods like LoRA and PEFT, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of DSQT's advantages and limitations.
- The methodology section could benefit from more detailed explanations, particularly regarding the selection of hyperparameters and the impact of different sparsity rates on performance.
- The paper's experimental setup is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on LLaMA-V1/V2 and not including other widely used models like Llama2 or Falcon, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the inference speed of DSQT with other pruning methods, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the specific contributions of DSQT compared to existing methods like SparseGPT and Wanda, which could lead to confusion about the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a detailed comparison of DSQT with other training-free fine-tuning methods like LoRA and PEFT? How does DSQT compare in terms of computational efficiency, performance, and practical deployment?
- How does the performance of DSQT vary with different sparsity rates, and what are the optimal settings for different model sizes?
- Could you provide more details on the selection of hyperparameters, particularly the maximum cycle T and the update threshold ε? How were these parameters chosen, and what impact do they have on the performance of DSQT?
- How does DSQT perform in terms of inference speed compared to other pruning methods? What are the practical implications of using DSQT in terms of inference efficiency?
- Could you clarify the specific contributions of DSQT compared to existing methods like SparseGPT and Wanda? What are the unique advantages of DSQT, and how does it compare in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does DSQT handle the issue of over-fitting, especially in scenarios where the calibration data might not be sufficient?
