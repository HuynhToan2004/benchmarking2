 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AttributeLLaMA, a model that utilizes a minimal set of expert-annotated attribution examples to fine-tune LLaMA models, enhancing their ability to provide attributions for generated responses. This model is evaluated across various tasks such as MMLU and StrategyQA, showing significant improvements in performance. The paper also explores the model's ability to generate attributions that align closely with Wikipedia documents, highlighting the model's memorization capabilities. Despite these advancements, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the model, as it is primarily evaluated on a single dataset (NQ).

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces AttributeLLaMA, a model that effectively utilizes minimal supervision to elicit attributions from LLaMA models, demonstrating a novel approach to improving the interpretability and reliability of LLMs.
- The evaluation of AttributeLLaMA on various tasks, including MMLU and StrategyQA, shows significant improvements in performance, highlighting the effectiveness of the proposed model.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the attributions generated by AttributeLLaMA, revealing its remarkable memorization capabilities and its ability to closely align with Wikipedia documents, enhancing the model's credibility.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental setup is clearly described, which facilitates reproducibility.
- The idea of using minimal supervision to train LLMs is innovative and could potentially reduce the reliance on large amounts of data, making the training process more efficient.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on the Natural Questions (NQ) dataset for evaluation, which might limit the generalizability of the findings to other datasets or scenarios.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis on the potential hallucinations in the generated attributions, which could affect the model's reliability.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the study, which could provide a more balanced view of the research.
- The paper's claims regarding the minimal supervision required for training LLMs are not convincingly supported, as the performance of the model might not be solely attributable to the supervision but also to other factors like the model's architecture or training methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other models, especially those that do not rely on minimal supervision, to better establish the effectiveness of the proposed model.

**Questions:**
- How does the model perform when evaluated on other datasets beyond NQ?
- Could you provide more detailed analysis on the potential hallucinations in the generated attributions and how these might affect the model's performance?
- What are the limitations of the study, and how do these impact the generalizability and applicability of the findings?
- How does the model compare with other models that do not rely on minimal supervision, and what are the specific advantages of using minimal supervision as claimed in the paper?
- Is there a possibility to use the model to enhance the performance of other models, such as those used in different domains or tasks?
- How does the model's performance compare with other models that use different training methods or supervision strategies, and what are the implications of these differences for the model's effectiveness?
