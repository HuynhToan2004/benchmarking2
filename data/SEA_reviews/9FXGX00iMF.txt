 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Best Window Selection (BWS) method, a novel data subset selection approach that addresses the challenges of selecting an optimal subset of training data for neural networks. BWS uses a window selection strategy to identify the best subset based on difficulty scores, which are calculated using the forgetting score. The method is evaluated across various datasets and selection ratios, demonstrating its effectiveness in achieving competitive performance across a broad range of settings. The paper also includes theoretical analysis and extensive experimental results to validate the method's efficiency and robustness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel window selection strategy for data subset selection, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is simple yet effective, demonstrated through extensive experiments and theoretical analysis.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method across various datasets and selection ratios, showing its effectiveness in achieving competitive performance.
- The theoretical analysis and experimental results are well-supported, enhancing the credibility of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and includes a detailed ablation study that supports the proposed method.
- The method is efficient, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the selection of the forgetting score as the difficulty score and does not compare it with other possible difficulty scores, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The computational complexity of the method is not thoroughly analyzed, which is crucial for understanding its scalability and efficiency.
- The paper does not include experiments on large-scale datasets like ImageNet-1k, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the method to larger datasets, which is a significant limitation given the increasing popularity of large-scale datasets.
- There is a lack of discussion on the robustness of the method to variations in data distributions and label noise, which are critical factors in real-world applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the sensitivity of the method to the selection of the kernel in the kernel ridge regression model, which could affect the accuracy and reliability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method?
- How does the method perform on larger datasets like ImageNet-1k, and what are the implications for its scalability and performance?
- Could the authors discuss the sensitivity of the method to variations in data distributions and label noise, and how the method could be adapted to handle these variations?
- How does the method perform when the kernel in the kernel ridge regression model is varied? What are the implications for the accuracy and reliability of the results?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the selection of the forgetting score as the difficulty score and compare it with other possible difficulty scores?
- How does the method perform in terms of robustness to variations in data distributions and label noise?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the selection of the kernel in the kernel ridge regression model and its impact on the results?
