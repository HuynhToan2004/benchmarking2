 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a unified framework, the Deep Variational Multivariate Information Bottleneck (DVMIB), which integrates various variational methods for dimensionality reduction. The framework is designed to optimize the trade-off between compression and reconstruction, and it includes the derivation of new methods like the Deep Variational Symmetric Information Bottleneck (DVSIB). The paper also demonstrates the application of this framework on benchmark datasets, showing that it can produce shared low-dimensional latent spaces that improve classification accuracy. However, the paper's experimental scope is limited, focusing mainly on MNIST variants, which might not fully showcase the framework's capabilities across diverse datasets. The paper's contribution is seen as significant in unifying existing methods and providing a new method, but the clarity of presentation and the depth of experimental validation need improvement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a logical flow.
- The unification of various variational methods into a unified framework provides a systematic and comprehensive approach to dimensionality reduction.
- The introduction of the Deep Variational Symmetric Information Bottleneck (DVSIB) as a novel method for dimensionality reduction is a significant contribution.
- The paper includes a thorough discussion of the unification of existing methods and provides a new method that shows potential.
- The authors have made a significant effort to make the code available, which is a commendable aspect.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental validation is limited, focusing primarily on MNIST variants, which might not fully demonstrate the framework's capabilities across diverse datasets.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework and the conditions under which it might not perform well.
- The presentation could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and the organization of content.
- The novelty of the proposed framework is questioned, as it seems to be a combination of existing methods without significant new insights.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed methods with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed framework.
- There are several typographical errors and inconsistencies in the notation, which could confuse readers.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty and the advantages of the proposed framework over existing methods?
- How does the proposed framework handle datasets with varying structures and complexities?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods to better establish the effectiveness of their framework?
- Is there a possibility to include more diverse datasets in future experiments to demonstrate the generalizability of the framework?
- How does the framework perform when the data is not as structured as in the experiments presented?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the beta parameter and its impact on the performance of the proposed methods?
