 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a Bayesian optimization (BO) framework based on Gaussian Cox processes, focusing on estimating the posterior mean and covariance of the Gaussian process. It utilizes a Laplace approximation and a change of kernel technique to transform the problem into a new reproducing kernel Hilbert space, enabling computationally tractable solutions. The framework is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods. The methodology includes the application of Nystrom approximations to compute posterior means and covariances, and the use of various acquisition functions for BO. Despite its novelty and potential, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, lack of comparison with more recent baselines, and unclear presentation in some sections.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a good balance of theoretical content and practical implementation.
- The introduction of Gaussian Cox processes into Bayesian optimization (BO) is a novel approach, providing a new perspective on estimating the posterior mean and covariance of Gaussian processes.
- The use of Laplace approximation and change of kernel techniques to transform the problem into a new reproducing kernel Hilbert space is innovative and computationally efficient.
- The paper is original in its application of Gaussian Cox processes to BO, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear theoretical results and practical implications, making it accessible and useful for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is somewhat limited, as it builds upon existing methods like Gaussian processes and Bayesian optimization, which have been extensively studied.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with more recent and relevant baselines, which could have provided a clearer picture of its relative performance.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in high-dimensional or large-scale datasets.
- There are some presentation issues, such as unclear definitions and notations, which could confuse readers and detract from the paper's clarity.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help readers understand its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in terms of how it scales with the size of the dataset and the dimensionality of the problem?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent and relevant baselines in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples for the acquisition functions used in the experiments, particularly those not commonly used in Bayesian optimization?
- How does the proposed method handle the estimation of the posterior mean and covariance when the number of observations is small?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and provide examples or scenarios where it might not be the most effective approach?
