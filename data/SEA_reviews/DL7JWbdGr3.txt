 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Pre-trained Epidemic Time-Series Models (PEMs), a novel approach to predicting the future dynamics of epidemics using a pre-trained model trained on diverse time-series datasets of various diseases. This model leverages self-supervised learning tasks such as random masking, last segment masking, peak masking, and seasonal detection to capture essential patterns of epidemic dynamics. The PEM model is trained on multiple datasets from different diseases and epidemics, showing significant improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods in various downstream tasks. The paper also discusses the challenges specific to pre-training for epidemic time-series and how the PEM model addresses these challenges.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of epidemic forecasting, which is crucial for public health decision-making.
- The methodology is clearly presented, and the experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper introduces a novel approach by leveraging prior knowledge from time-series data of other diseases to improve the performance of epidemic time-series prediction.
- The proposed self-supervised learning (SSL) tasks are carefully designed to learn important priors about the epidemic dynamics, which can be leveraged for fine-tuning to multiple downstream tasks.
- The results show that the PEM model outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods in various downstream time-series tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and utility.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the authors provide detailed information on the datasets used, which enhances the reproducibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the PEM model to other diseases or epidemics, which could limit its practical application.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the selection of SSL tasks and their impact on the model's performance. More detailed ablation studies and a discussion on the choice of tasks are needed.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect the reliability and generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the impact of the pre-training data on the model's performance, which is crucial for understanding the model's effectiveness.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the work, which could be significant given the application of the model in public health.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the scalability of the PEM model to other diseases or epidemics? How does the model adapt to new datasets, and what are the implications for its performance?
- How does the model perform when trained on a subset of the pre-training data? What are the implications for its generalization ability?
- Could you provide more insights into the choice of SSL tasks and their impact on the model's performance? Are there other SSL tasks that could be considered, and how might these affect the model's effectiveness?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how do these affect the reliability and generalizability of the findings?
- How does the model handle missing data in the input time series? What are the implications for the model's performance when dealing with incomplete data?
- Could you provide more details on the computational complexity and training time of the PEM model? How does this compare to other state-of-the-art methods, and what are the implications for the practical deployment of the model?
