 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel data augmentation method called SASSL, which utilizes neural style transfer to enhance self-supervised learning (SSL) by preserving semantic information while varying stylistic attributes. This method is designed to improve the robustness of representations and transferability across datasets. The paper presents experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of SASSL in improving downstream task performance, particularly on ImageNet, and in transfer learning scenarios. Despite its innovative approach, concerns were raised regarding the novelty and the clarity of the experimental setup, particularly the use of different datasets and the choice of hyperparameters.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method, SASSL, is innovative and straightforward, with a clear explanation of its components and their roles.
- The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through comprehensive experimental results, showing significant improvements over the baseline.
- The paper provides a detailed ablation study, which helps in understanding the impact of different components of the proposed method.
- The idea of using style transfer as a data augmentation technique is novel and has been shown to be effective in improving performance in various downstream tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how the proposed method compares to other data augmentation methods, especially those that also aim to improve the robustness of representations.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the method.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational cost of the method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with some results (e.g., transfer learning results) being confusing or not well-explained.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as it appears to be a simple combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims of improved transferability of the learned representations.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other data augmentation methods, especially those that aim to improve the robustness of representations?
- Can the authors clarify the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the method?
- How does the computational cost of the proposed method compare to other data augmentation methods, and what are the practical implications of this cost?
- In the transfer learning experiments, why were the results for some datasets (e.g., iNat21 and Retinopathy) not included, and how do the results for different style datasets compare?
- Could the authors provide more detailed evidence or experimental results to support the claims of improved transferability of the learned representations?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of degraded semantic information in augmented samples, and what steps are taken to ensure that the style transfer does not adversely affect the semantic content of the images?
