 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LEAP, a novel pose-free method for 3D modeling from sparse-view images. It challenges the traditional reliance on camera poses by using a neural volume shared across scenes, which is updated using a feature-similarity-driven approach. This method enables the synthesis of novel views without requiring camera poses, demonstrating superior performance and faster inference speeds compared to existing methods like PixelNeRF. LEAP shows significant improvements in generalization capability, handling both object-centric and scene-level datasets, and interprets learned priors that resemble epipolar geometry. The method's effectiveness is validated through extensive experiments and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating its potential for real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel pose-free approach, LEAP, which challenges the conventional use of camera poses for 3D modeling, providing a significant shift in the field.
- LEAP demonstrates superior performance in synthesizing novel views from 2-5 unposed images, surpassing existing generalizable NeRFs when using predicted poses from state-of-the-art pose estimators.
- The method achieves faster inference speed, running within one second on a single consumer-grade GPU, making it practical for real-world applications.
- LEAP shows strong generalization capability, able to model novel-category objects accurately and generalize well to novel categories and scenes, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes extensive experiments that validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The authors have committed to releasing the code for reproducibility and future research, which is a commendable effort towards advancing the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the description of the Multi-View Encoder, particularly how it determines the canonical view and how this affects the feature consistency across views.
- The method's dependence on the canonical view selection and the initialization of the neural volume in the local camera coordinate frame of the canonical view could limit its applicability to scenes where a single canonical view is not available.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, particularly the use of a neural volume representation that spans uniformly in 3D space, which could limit the method's applicability to unbounded scenes.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis and visualization of the learned neural volume, which could provide insights into the method's capabilities and limitations.
- The paper does not compare its method with the latest diffusion-based NeRFs, which could affect the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the Multi-View Encoder determines the canonical view and how this affects the feature consistency across views?
- How does the method handle the selection of the canonical view when multiple views are available?
- What is the impact of the initialization of the neural volume in the local camera coordinate frame of the canonical view on the method's performance?
- Could the authors provide a detailed explanation of the GCR layer and its role in the overall method?
- How does the method perform when the number of input images is less than 5, particularly when the number drops to 1 image?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the perceptual loss in the training process and its impact on the final results?
- What is the physical size of the neural volume, and how does it affect the method's applicability to unbounded scenes?
- How does the method compare with the latest diffusion-based NeRFs, and what are the advantages of using LEAP over these methods?
