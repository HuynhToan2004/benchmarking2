 **Summary:**
The paper introduces BATTLE, a novel adversarial attack method for deep reinforcement learning (DRL) agents, focusing on behavior-oriented attacks that manipulate the victim agent's behavior to align with human preferences. BATTLE employs a bi-level optimization approach, training an adversary to perturb the victim's observations and an intention policy to guide the victim towards desired behaviors. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments in various simulated environments, showing that BATTLE outperforms existing attack methods. However, the paper faces criticism for its lack of novelty in methodology, insufficient experimental validation, and unclear presentation of results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations and detailed illustrations that aid in understanding the proposed methods.
- The proposed BATTLE method is innovative, combining preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL) and adversarial attacks to create a novel approach.
- The empirical results demonstrate that BATTLE outperforms existing attack methods, showing significant improvements in attacking the victim's behavior.
- The paper provides theoretical analysis of the proposed method, enhancing the understanding of its theoretical underpinnings.
- The use of a weighting function to assign importance weights over each state is a novel approach that could potentially improve the performance of the attack.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, which is crucial for academic research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks sufficient experimental validation to convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. More comprehensive experiments, including a broader range of tasks and environments, are needed to substantiate the claims.
- The methodology of BATTLE is not significantly novel, as it resembles existing adversarial training methods, which could limit its perceived impact.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in more complex or real-world scenarios, which could limit its practical application.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of results, particularly in figures and tables, which could hinder the reader's understanding of the data and the conclusions drawn from it.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the ethical implications of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in real-world applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer discussion on the relationship between the proposed method and existing works.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the intention policy guides the victim agent towards desired behaviors? Specifically, how does the inner-level optimization ensure that the adversary policy learns to perturb states effectively to manipulate the victim's behavior?
- How does the weighting function affect the performance of the attack, and can the authors provide more detailed experimental results to support its effectiveness?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup, particularly how the tasks were selected and how the preference labels were collected?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and scalability?
- Could the authors address the ethical concerns raised in the paper and discuss how these issues were considered during the development and testing of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting, particularly in scenarios where the adversarial policy is trained on a small dataset?
