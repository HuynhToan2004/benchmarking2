 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the internal representations learned by neural networks in algebraic tasks, focusing on how margin maximization influences the features learned by these networks. Specifically, it explores the training dynamics of one-hidden-layer MLPs with quadratic activations on various algebraic tasks, including modular addition, sparse parity, and group operations. The authors employ a novel theoretical framework to characterize the maximum margin solutions for these tasks, demonstrating that these networks utilize Fourier features for modular addition and features corresponding to irreducible group-theoretic representations for other compositions. The paper provides empirical evidence supporting these theoretical findings, showing that trained networks closely align with the theoretical predictions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel theoretical framework for characterizing the maximum margin solutions for algebraic tasks, which is a significant contribution to the field of interpretability in neural networks.
- The theoretical results are well-supported by empirical evidence, showing a close alignment between the theoretical predictions and the actual behavior of trained networks.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The study of margin maximization in the context of algebraic tasks is innovative and addresses an important question in the field of neural network interpretability.
- The paper's findings are relevant and significant, particularly in understanding how neural networks arrive at specific computational strategies, which is a critical area of research in machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**
