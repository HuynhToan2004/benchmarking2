 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Subgraph-To-Node (S2N) translation, a novel approach for learning subgraph representations in a global graph context. This method involves transforming subgraphs into nodes, which are then used to construct a new graph where nodes represent subgraphs and edges represent relationships between them. This approach is designed to reduce memory and computational costs by approximating subgraph representations from the original global graph. The authors also propose a coarsened version of S2N (CoS2N) for data-scarce settings, which combines S2N with graph coarsening methods. The paper presents theoretical and empirical evidence demonstrating the efficiency and effectiveness of S2N and CoS2N in various real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to subgraph representation learning, which is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated.
- The methodology of S2N is simple yet effective, with a focus on reducing memory and computational costs compared to existing methods.
- The paper provides comprehensive theoretical and empirical evidence, including a detailed analysis of the efficiency of S2N and CoS2N in reducing memory and computational costs.
- The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of S2N and CoS2N in various real-world scenarios, showing that they can process significantly more subgraph samples than state-of-the-art models at similar or better performance levels.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, and includes a thorough analysis of the limitations and potential future work.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of their applicability to different types of subgraphs and the scalability of the approach.
- The theoretical analysis could be strengthened by including more detailed proofs and a deeper exploration of the theoretical underpinnings of S2N and CoS2N.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential loss of structural information when translating subgraphs into nodes, which could impact the effectiveness of the method.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, with some results being reprinted from previous works without adequate explanation or comparison.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the practical implications of the method, particularly in terms of its scalability and generalizability to different types of graphs and subgraphs.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the S2N graph encoder is designed, particularly in terms of its inputs and outputs?
- How does the method handle the potential loss of structural information when translating subgraphs into nodes?
- Could the authors provide more detailed proofs or theoretical analysis to support the claims made about the efficiency and effectiveness of S2N and CoS2N?
- In the context of the experiments, how do the authors ensure that the results are not biased by the use of pre-trained embeddings or other potentially confounding factors?
- How does the method perform when applied to graphs with different characteristics, such as those with varying densities or different types of nodes and edges?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of S2N and CoS2N with other existing methods, particularly in terms of their computational efficiency and memory requirements?
