 **Summary:**
The paper explores a federated bandit learning setup where clients are incentivized to participate, each reporting their own costs, which the server uses to compute incentives. The authors introduce a new mechanism, Truth-FedBan, which guarantees truthfulness and rationality, and ensures that clients receive incentives based on their reported costs, independent of the actual costs. The mechanism is designed to minimize social cost, which is a near-optimal solution. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for regret, communication cost, and social cost, and validates these through numerical studies. Despite the theoretical contributions, concerns were raised about the practicality of the assumptions and the clarity of the presentation, particularly in the proofs and the description of the communication protocol.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to incentivizing participation in federated bandit learning, where clients report their own costs, and the server pays based on these reports, ensuring truthfulness and rationality.
- The theoretical guarantees provided are strong, including near-optimal regret and communication cost, and a near-optimal social cost, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed mechanism and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed mechanism, showing improvements over existing methods in terms of social cost and other performance metrics.
- The authors have effectively addressed the issue of strategic misreporting by clients, which is a significant challenge in federated learning setups.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper makes strong assumptions about the clients' knowledge and strategic behavior, which may not be realistic in all practical scenarios.
- The communication protocol and the computation of critical values are not clearly described, which could lead to confusion about the practical implementation of the proposed mechanism.
- The proofs and theoretical results, while strong, are not always clearly presented or explained, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed mechanism, particularly in terms of its assumptions and potential negative societal impacts.
- There is a significant gap between the theoretical results and the practical implementation, which could limit the immediate applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its results with existing literature, which could undermine the perceived novelty and contribution of the work.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made about the clients' knowledge and strategic behavior, and discuss how these assumptions affect the practical applicability of the proposed mechanism?
- How does the proposed mechanism handle collusion among clients, and what are the implications of such collusion on the system's performance?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computation of critical values and the communication protocol to make these processes clearer and more accessible?
- How does the proposed mechanism compare to existing mechanisms in terms of computational efficiency and practical implementation?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential societal impacts of their work, particularly in terms of privacy and data security, and discuss how these impacts might be mitigated or addressed?
- In light of the strong assumptions and theoretical results, how do the authors envision the practical implementation of their findings, and what steps are being taken to bridge the gap between theory and practice?
