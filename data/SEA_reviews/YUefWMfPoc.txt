 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the selective classification problem in deep neural networks, focusing on the use of post-hoc confidence estimation methods to enhance model performance. The authors propose a novel metric, NAURC, to normalize the AURC metric, making it insensitive to model accuracy. This metric is applied to various neural network architectures to assess the effectiveness of different confidence estimation methods. The study reveals that certain models, such as EfficientNet-V2-XL and VGG16, respond differently to post-hoc calibration techniques, suggesting that model architecture might influence the effectiveness of these methods. The paper also explores the robustness of these methods under distribution shifts, demonstrating that the proposed methods can enhance model performance even in such scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The problem addressed, namely, the effectiveness of post-hoc calibration methods in selective classification, is both interesting and relevant.
- The proposed method of normalizing the AURC metric is novel and provides a useful tool for evaluating model performance.
- The experiments conducted are extensive, covering a variety of neural network architectures and datasets, which supports the robustness of the findings.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of existing methods and offers a new perspective on the problem of selective classification, which is understudied in the literature.
- The authors demonstrate that their method is effective not only in standard settings but also under distribution shifts, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of neural networks and datasets.
- The results presented in the paper are not always consistent across different models, which raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the factors influencing the performance of the proposed method, such as the architecture and training data of the models.
- There is a lack of novelty in the proposed method, as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods without significant new insights or theoretical contributions.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the proposed method, which might be a significant drawback for practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous experimental setup, including a broader range of datasets and a more detailed analysis of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the rationale behind the choice of the 0.01 threshold in the APG-NAURC metric? What would be the impact if this threshold were changed?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the model's accuracy is low, and what are the implications for the practical applicability of the findings?
- In light of the inconsistent results across different models, can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of why some models respond well to the proposed method while others do not?
- What are the computational costs associated with the proposed method, and how do these costs compare to other existing methods in terms of efficiency and scalability?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the NAURC metric over other potential metrics, such as the SAC metric, which might provide a more intuitive measure of model performance?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the model's training data is limited or biased, and what are the implications for the robustness and generalizability of the findings?
