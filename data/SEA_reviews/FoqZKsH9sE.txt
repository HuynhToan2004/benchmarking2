 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel block-structured pruning method for Vision Transformers (ViTs) aimed at reducing hardware consumption during inference. This method optimizes the pruning objective using a hardware-aware approach that balances accuracy and efficiency. It proposes a hardware-aware learning objective to maximize speedup and minimize power consumption, which is achieved through a lightweight algorithm that utilizes second-order Taylor approximation and empirical optimization. Extensive experiments across various ViT architectures demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing competitive performance and a balance between accuracy preservation and power savings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel block-structured pruning method for Vision Transformers (ViTs), which is a first in the field.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, with a clear hardware-aware learning objective that optimizes for both speedup and power consumption.
- The paper provides a lightweight algorithm for post-training pruning of ViTs, which is efficient and effective in reducing computational complexity.
- The experiments conducted are extensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various ViT architectures, including DeiT-B and DeiT-S.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making the complex methodologies accessible to readers.
- The proposed method achieves a remarkable balance between accuracy preservation and power savings, which is crucial for deploying ViTs on resource-constrained devices.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not discuss the impact of the proposed method on training time, which is a significant aspect of model compression.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it builds upon existing techniques like block-structured pruning, which have been explored in other domains such as NLP.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other pruning methods, particularly in terms of accuracy preservation and power savings.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the hardware-aware objective, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its applicability in different scenarios.
- The paper does not include experiments on hardware platforms, which is essential for validating the claimed hardware efficiency.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made in the method, particularly in relation to the hardware-aware objective.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on how the proposed method affects training time, especially in terms of the computational resources required?
- How does the proposed method compare to other pruning methods in terms of accuracy preservation and power savings?
- Could you elaborate on the hardware-aware objective and its practical implications for deploying the pruned models on specific hardware platforms?
- How do the assumptions made in the method impact the practical applicability of the proposed approach?
- What are the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how do these affect its applicability in different scenarios?
- Can you include experiments on hardware platforms to validate the claimed hardware efficiency of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger models, and what are the implications of using different block sizes in the pruning process?
