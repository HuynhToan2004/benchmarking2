 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a graph-based framework for jointly addressing out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization and detection. It formulates a graph where vertices are data points, and edges connect similar data points based on supervised and self-supervised signals. The authors propose a spectral contrastive loss to facilitate joint learning from labeled and unlabeled data, demonstrating theoretical insights and empirical results that showcase the framework's effectiveness. The paper provides a theoretical analysis of OOD generalization and detection using this graph-based approach, showcasing its utility in practical scenarios. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper's presentation, the novelty of the method, and the fairness of the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in machine learning, specifically focusing on out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization and detection, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, with a clear and well-structured presentation that makes the content accessible and understandable.
- The paper provides both theoretical and empirical evidence, supported by solid experimental results, which are essential for validating the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The use of a graph-based framework to address OOD generalization and detection is innovative and offers a novel approach to understanding and addressing these challenges.
- The paper is well-written, with clear descriptions of the methodology, experiments, and results, which are helpful for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation from existing works, particularly those by Bai et al. (2023), which might lead to questions about the novelty and distinctiveness of the proposed method.
- There is a notable absence of discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its broader implications.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed or clear, making it difficult to fully assess the robustness and generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer explanation of the theoretical connections between the graph-based approach and the OOD generalization and detection tasks.
- There are some typographical errors and unclear notations in the paper, which could confuse readers and detract from the overall clarity and professionalism of the work.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the main differences between their method and the approach by Bai et al. (2023), particularly in terms of novelty and methodological contributions?
- How does the graph-based framework specifically contribute to the understanding and resolution of OOD generalization and detection challenges?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of their proposed method?
- Is there a specific reason why the experimental results are not more detailed or comprehensive, and how do the authors ensure the robustness and generalizability of their findings?
- Can the authors address the typographical errors and unclear notations noted in the paper to enhance its clarity and professionalism?
