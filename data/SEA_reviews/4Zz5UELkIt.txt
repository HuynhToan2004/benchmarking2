 **Summary:**
The paper explores adaptive instrument design for indirect experiments to enhance sample efficiency in estimating conditional average treatment effects (CATE). The authors propose a computational procedure that utilizes influence functions to optimize data collection policies, aiming to minimize the mean-squared error of the desired estimator. The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, including experiments on semi-synthetic TripAdvisor and other datasets, demonstrating improvements over traditional uniform allocation strategies. Despite the theoretical and empirical contributions, the paper is critiqued for its limited empirical scope, lack of clarity in presentation, and insufficient discussion on the practical implications and limitations of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to adaptive instrument design for indirect experiments, focusing on optimizing data collection policies to improve sample efficiency.
- The use of influence functions to model the influence of different instruments on the estimation of treatment effects is a significant contribution to the field.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to support the proposed method, enhancing the credibility of the approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the theoretical concepts and empirical results.
- The proposed method is practical and can be implemented in real-world scenarios, showing potential for broader applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential drawbacks of the proposed method, which is crucial for a balanced assessment of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The empirical evaluation is limited, focusing on semi-synthetic TripAdvisor data and not extending to more diverse or real-world datasets, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved to enhance clarity and readability, particularly in the main text and figures.
- There is a lack of discussion on the assumptions and their implications, which are critical for understanding the conditions under which the proposed method is effective.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the proposed method, such as its scalability and computational complexity in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential drawbacks of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to more diverse or real-world datasets, and what are the implications of these findings?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions used in the theoretical analysis and discuss their implications for the practical applicability of the method?
- What are the specific computational complexities and scalability issues associated with implementing the proposed method in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of sample efficiency and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the figures and their implications, particularly in terms of the sample size and variance reduction?
