 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for aligning large multimodal models (LMMs) with human feedback, specifically addressing the issue of hallucination in vision-language models. The authors propose a new algorithm, Factually Augmented RLHF, which utilizes additional factual information to enhance the reward model and prevent reward hacking. This method is tested on the LLaVA-Bench and MMHal-Bench datasets, showing significant improvements over existing models. The paper also introduces a new benchmark, MMHal-Bench, to evaluate the performance of models in avoiding hallucinations. Despite these strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in the presentation of methods and results, and the generalizability of the proposed approach to other models and datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the methodology and results.
- The introduction of a new algorithm, Factually Augmented RLHF, which utilizes additional factual information to enhance the reward model, is a novel approach to addressing the issue of reward hacking in LMMs.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through experiments on two benchmarks, showing significant improvements in model performance.
- The authors have made the code and datasets publicly available, enhancing the reproducibility and accessibility of the research.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of multimodal models, specifically the issue of hallucination, and proposes a practical solution to improve the alignment of LLMs and LMMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the differences between the proposed method and existing methods, particularly in the area of RLHF.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the methodology, particularly in the sections describing the Fact-RLHF algorithm and the use of factual information in the reward model.
- The generalizability of the proposed approach is limited, as the experiments are primarily focused on the LLaVA model and its variants.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed experimental results and a broader evaluation on different datasets and models to strengthen its claims.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method to larger models, which could be a significant limitation for its practical application.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and existing methods, particularly in terms of the use of factual information in the reward model?
- How does the proposed method perform when scaled to larger models, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in doing so?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the performance of the Fact-RLHF algorithm across different datasets and models to strengthen the claims of its effectiveness?
- How is the factual information used in the Fact-RLHF algorithm integrated into the reward model, and what specific impact does it have on the model's performance?
- The paper mentions a potential issue with the scalability of the proposed method to larger models. Can the authors discuss any potential strategies or modifications that could be made to address this challenge?
