 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of different fine-tuning methods, specifically reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) and supervised fine-tuning (SFT), on the generalization and diversity of large language models (LLMs) in out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios. It examines the trade-off between these two aspects, demonstrating that while RLHF enhances generalization, it reduces output diversity. The study is based on two datasets and two tasks, showing that RLHF generally outperforms SFT in terms of generalization but falls short in diversity. The paper also discusses the potential for future research to address the limitations of current methods and improve the trade-off between generalization and diversity.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the results.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments, including a variety of models and datasets, which supports the robustness of their findings.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant topic in the field of large language models (LLMs), specifically the trade-off between generalization and diversity in RLHF and SFT.
- The experiments are thorough, covering different model sizes and datasets, which strengthens the validity of the findings.
- The paper provides valuable insights into the effects of different stages of the RLHF process on the generalization and diversity of LLMs, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of these models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive literature review, which could provide a better context for the research and help position the findings within the existing body of knowledge.
- The diversity metrics used in the study are limited to n-grams and sentence similarity, which may not fully capture the complexity of diversity in LLM outputs.
- The experimental setup and the rationale behind the choices made (e.g., using a reward model to select the best sample) are not adequately explained, which could limit the understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the diversity metrics used, such as the specific values of N in the metrics, which are crucial for interpreting the results.
- The results and their implications are not sufficiently discussed, particularly the findings on the generalization and diversity trade-offs, which could benefit from a more in-depth analysis.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation of the generalization and diversity aspects, possibly using a broader range of models and datasets to validate the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the rationale behind using a reward model to select the best sample and discuss the implications of this approach on the diversity and generalization of the models?
- How do the authors justify the choice of using GPT-4 as a proxy for human preference, especially given the potential biases and limitations of this model?
- Can the authors provide more details on the diversity metrics used, including the specific values of N in the metrics and how these metrics were chosen?
- How do the authors interpret the findings on the generalization and diversity trade-offs, and what are the implications of these findings for the practical applications of LLMs?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential reasons for the observed differences in generalization and diversity between RLHF and SFT, and discuss the implications of these differences for the development of more effective and diverse LLMs?
