 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel measure, magnitude, to evaluate the quality of embedding algorithms by capturing both local and global structures of a metric space. The authors propose two measures, magnitude profile difference and magnitude weight difference, to compare different embedding spaces. Theoretical properties of these measures are discussed, and their stability under perturbations and computational efficiency are demonstrated. The paper includes empirical evidence showing the effectiveness of these measures in identifying ground truth embeddings and in single-cell data analysis. However, the paper's contribution is seen as limited as it primarily extends existing definitions to different metrics without significant theoretical advancements.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed measures are simple, intuitive, and effectively capture the quality of embedding algorithms, particularly in the context of single-cell data analysis.
- The empirical evidence supports the utility of the measures in identifying ground truth embeddings and in single-cell data analysis.
- The paper includes theoretical proofs and empirical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed measures.
- The authors provide a clear motivation for the use of magnitude in representation learning tasks and discuss the stability of magnitude under perturbations of the data.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not clearly distinguish between the original contributions and the extensions from previous works, particularly in the definitions of magnitude and the stability results.
- The theoretical advancements are limited, and the paper primarily extends existing definitions to different metrics without significant theoretical advancements.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed measures, which could be a significant limitation for large-scale applications.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed measures.
- The paper's experimental results are not sufficiently detailed, and there is a lack of comparison with existing measures in terms of computational efficiency and stability under different conditions.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the original contributions of the paper and how they differ from previous works, particularly in the definitions and proofs of magnitude and stability?
- How do the proposed measures compare in terms of computational efficiency and stability under different conditions, such as varying levels of noise or perturbations?
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed measures and discuss potential strategies for reducing this complexity in large-scale applications?
- How do the proposed measures compare to existing measures in terms of their ability to capture different aspects of embedding quality, such as cluster, distance, and density preservation?
- Can you discuss the potential limitations and negative societal impacts of the proposed measures, particularly in the context of single-cell data analysis?
