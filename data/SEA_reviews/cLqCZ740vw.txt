 **Summary:**
The paper investigates methods aimed at mitigating high-frequency oscillations in reinforcement learning (RL) policies, focusing on two broad classes: loss regularization and architectural methods. It benchmarks existing techniques against a variety of environments, including classic RL environments and real-world robotics tasks, and introduces hybrid methods that combine elements from both classes. The paper presents a comprehensive review of literature, categorizing methods and proposing new hybrid approaches. It also includes experiments in both simulation and real-world settings, showcasing the practical application of these methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the hybrid methods and the clarity of the experimental results, particularly the real-world deployment details and the effectiveness of the proposed methods in complex or unstructured environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of reinforcement learning (RL), focusing on reducing high-frequency oscillations in RL policies, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- It provides a comprehensive review of existing literature on this topic, categorizing methods into two broad classes: loss regularization and architectural methods, and proposes new hybrid methods that combine elements from both classes.
- The paper includes experiments in both simulation and real-world settings, which adds practical relevance and validates the effectiveness of the proposed methods in real-world scenarios.
- The benchmarking of various existing methods in both simulation and real-world settings provides valuable insights into the performance of different methods across different environments.
- The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the hybrid methods proposed in the paper is questionable, as the combination of loss regularization and architectural methods might be seen as a straightforward application rather than a significant advancement.
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the real-world deployment details, particularly how the hybrid methods are adapted for real-world applications and what specific challenges were encountered in these deployments.
- The experimental results, especially the real-world deployment results, are not convincingly presented, with concerns about the clarity and depth of the results, particularly in the real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations or challenges of the proposed methods, especially in complex or unstructured environments, which could affect the practical applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods, particularly in real-world applications where hardware damage could occur.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the real-world deployment of the hybrid methods, including how the methods were adapted for these scenarios and what specific challenges were encountered?
- How do the hybrid methods compare to other existing methods in terms of effectiveness and practical applicability in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental results, particularly the real-world deployment results, to make them more clear and convincing?
- How do the proposed methods handle complex or unstructured environments, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in these scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods, particularly in real-world applications where hardware damage could occur?
