 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of synthetic data as a validation set to mitigate overfitting in medical imaging applications, specifically in liver tumor segmentation. It proposes a method where synthetic tumors are generated and inserted into healthy CT scans to create a validation dataset, which is then used to select the best model checkpoint. The approach is evaluated using a continual learning framework, demonstrating improvements in model performance on both in-domain and out-domain test sets. The paper also discusses the potential of synthetic data to enhance early cancer detection and reduce the need for extensive real data annotation. However, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the findings to other medical imaging tasks and the novelty of using synthetic data for validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The use of synthetic data to address the challenge of limited real-world data is a significant and innovative approach.
- The study demonstrates the effectiveness of synthetic data in enhancing model performance, particularly in medical imaging applications.
- The paper provides a clear and comprehensive methodology, including a detailed description of the tumor generation process and the continual learning framework.
- The experimental results are robust, showing significant improvements in model performance and early cancer detection capabilities.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of medical imaging, which is the limited availability of real-world data.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods for model selection and validation, particularly in the context of continual learning.
- The generalizability of the findings to other medical imaging tasks or different types of tumors is unclear.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential biases introduced by using synthetic data for validation.
- The novelty of the approach might be limited as similar methods have been previously explored, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing works.
- The paper does not discuss potential ethical implications or societal impacts of using synthetic data in medical imaging, which could be significant.
- There is a lack of detailed statistical analysis and error bars in the results, which could undermine the reliability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific contributions of their work in comparison to existing methods, particularly in terms of model selection and validation?
- How does the continual learning framework perform in different medical imaging tasks beyond liver tumor segmentation?
- What are the potential biases or limitations introduced by using synthetic data for validation, and how might these affect the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors provide more details on the synthetic data generation process, particularly how it is used to augment the training and validation sets?
- How does the paper address the ethical implications and societal impacts of using synthetic data in medical imaging?
- Can the authors provide more detailed statistical analysis and error bars to support the robustness of their findings?
