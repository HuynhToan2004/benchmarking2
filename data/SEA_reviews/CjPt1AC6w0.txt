 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the use of synthetic images derived from text-to-image generative models in transfer learning, focusing on the challenges of fine-tuning models on synthetic data and their subsequent adaptation to real-world datasets. The authors propose a two-stage framework called "Bridged Transfer," which involves initial fine-tuning on synthetic data and subsequent adaptation on real data, along with a "Dataset Style Inversion" strategy to improve stylistic alignment between synthetic and real images. The study evaluates the effectiveness of this approach across ten datasets and five models, demonstrating consistent improvements in model performance. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and insufficient comparison with other state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a timely and relevant topic, focusing on the generation and utilization of synthetic images derived from text-to-image generative models in facilitating transfer learning paradigms.
- The study is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, with a clear and concise presentation of the research.
- The paper includes extensive experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in enhancing transfer learning performance.
- The experiments are conducted across ten datasets and five distinct models, providing a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper introduces a novel two-stage framework called "Bridged Transfer," which effectively addresses the challenges of transfer learning by initially fine-tuning a pre-trained model on synthetic data and subsequently adapting it to real data.
- The "Dataset Style Inversion" strategy is proposed to improve the stylistic alignment between synthetic and real images, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive literature review and comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a better context for the proposed work.
- The paper primarily focuses on the ResNet architecture and does not explore the application of the proposed method to other architectures such as ViT, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as similar approaches have been previously explored, such as the use of synthetic data for fine-tuning and the Dataset Style Inversion strategy.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of data distribution gaps between synthetic and real images, which is a critical challenge in transfer learning.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly in the methodology and the evaluation of the proposed method against existing benchmarks.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its potential negative societal impacts.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the datasets used, the pre-training and fine-tuning processes, and the evaluation metrics employed?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other architectures, such as ViT?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the Dataset Style Inversion strategy and its effectiveness in improving the transfer learning performance?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of data distribution gaps between synthetic and real images?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their proposed method and how these impacts might be mitigated?
