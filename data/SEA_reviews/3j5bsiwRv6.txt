 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SparseRefine, a method designed to enhance semantic segmentation by integrating dense low-resolution predictions with sparse high-resolution refinements. This approach is aimed at improving the efficiency of high-resolution visual computing by reducing computational complexity while maintaining accuracy. The method involves an entropy selector to identify pixels with the least confidence, a sparse feature extractor to refine these pixels, and a gated ensembler to integrate the refined predictions with the initial coarse predictions. The paper claims significant speedups of 1.5 to 3.9 times while maintaining accuracy, as demonstrated on the Cityscapes dataset. However, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive experimental validation, and insufficient discussion on the computational efficiency and accuracy trade-offs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the critical issue of reducing computational complexity in high-resolution semantic segmentation, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The proposed method of enhancing dense low-resolution predictions with sparse high-resolution refinements is innovative and effectively reduces computational requirements while maintaining accuracy.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, making it accessible and understandable.
- The use of an entropy selector to identify pixels with the least confidence and a sparse feature extractor to refine these pixels is a novel approach that contributes to the field.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method and includes comprehensive ablation studies and visualizations, which enhance the understanding of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the computational efficiency and accuracy trade-offs, which are crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed method.
- The experimental validation is limited to the Cityscapes dataset, and it would be beneficial to see results on other datasets such as ADE20K and PascalVOC to validate the generalizability of the method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could help in evaluating the performance of the proposed method more comprehensively.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it builds upon existing works, and the main contribution appears to be the integration of existing modules rather than the development of new techniques.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding the broader implications of the work.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational overhead introduced by the additional modules, which could affect the overall efficiency of the method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets such as ADE20K and PascalVOC?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods to better understand the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the computational efficiency of the proposed method compare with other methods in terms of both MACs and latency?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the computational overhead introduced by the additional modules and how it affects the overall efficiency of the method?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of objects, especially those that are not easily detected by existing methods?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method?
