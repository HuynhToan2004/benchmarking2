 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for molecular generation, termed Hierarchical textual Inversion for Molecular generation (HI-Mol), which utilizes a hierarchical textual inversion scheme to capture hierarchical information of molecules. This method employs a pre-trained text-to-molecule model to generate molecules, optimizing low-level tokens to learn common concepts among molecules. The approach is validated on several datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in generating molecules with higher active ratios and improved FCD scores compared to existing methods. The methodology involves optimizing a single text token to capture the common concept among molecules, which is a departure from traditional methods that optimize multiple tokens. The paper also includes a discussion on the potential of the method for low-shot molecular property prediction.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for molecular generation, leveraging a hierarchical textual inversion scheme that captures hierarchical information of molecules, optimizing low-level tokens to learn common concepts among molecules.
- The method is well-explained and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The experiments are comprehensive and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method achieves good performance in terms of FCD, NSPDK, and Active ratio, outperforming existing methods in these metrics.
- The paper is well-organized and includes a detailed discussion of the methodology and its applications, such as molecular optimization and low-shot molecular property prediction.
- The method's potential for reducing the amount of training data required for molecular generation is highlighted, making it a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its applicability and potential challenges.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly the results of the ablation study, which could benefit from more comprehensive analysis and visualization.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the relationship between the proposed method and existing methods, such as the use of text-to-molecule models and the optimization of text tokens in textual inversion.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the method, which could be a significant concern given the potential applications in drug discovery.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the selection of hyperparameters and the sensitivity of the method to these parameters.
- The method's dependence on the quality of the pre-trained text-to-molecule model could limit its applicability and effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its potential challenges?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements?
- What are the specific roles and contributions of the intermediate tokens in the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when the number of clusters K is varied?
- How does the method handle the optimization of text tokens in textual inversion, and what are the implications of this for the learning of common concepts among molecules?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results and analysis of the ablation study, particularly in terms of the effects of different hyperparameters on the performance of the method?
- How does the method handle the potential negative societal impacts of molecular generation, such as the creation of hazardous molecules?
