 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DORSal, a novel method that integrates object-centric scene representations with a diffusion-based decoder to achieve controllable 3D novel-view synthesis. By leveraging pre-trained object-centric representations, DORSal enables precise rendering of novel views and supports object-level scene editing. The method uses a frozen object-centric scene representation, which is combined with a diffusion decoder to generate high-fidelity novel views. The experiments demonstrate that DORSal outperforms existing methods in terms of precision and editing capabilities, particularly in synthetic and real-world datasets. However, the paper is criticized for its limited editing capabilities and lack of end-to-end training, which affects the consistency of the generated views.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach by integrating object-centric scene representations with diffusion-based decoders, which is a significant advancement in the field of controllable 3D novel-view synthesis.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear figures and detailed descriptions, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The experiments are comprehensive and demonstrate that DORSal outperforms existing methods in terms of precision and editing capabilities.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear motivation and detailed discussion of the methodology, including the use of object-centric scene representations and the integration of a diffusion-based decoder.
- The results are promising, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method in generating high-quality novel views and enabling object-level scene editing.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other methods, particularly in terms of the number of training views and the editing capabilities.
- There is a lack of end-to-end training, which could affect the consistency of the generated views.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as the need for a large number of training views and the potential for flickering effects in the generated views.
- The editing capabilities of the method are limited, and the paper does not explore more complex editing operations like object merging or moving.
- The methodology is complex, involving multiple stages and components which may be difficult to implement and optimize.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of different numbers of object slots on the performance and generalization of the model, which is a critical aspect of the methodology.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the number of object slots on the performance and generalization of the model?
- How does the proposed method handle the flickering effect in the generated views, and what strategies could be employed to mitigate this issue?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and inference times for the proposed method, particularly in comparison to other methods?
- How does the method handle the absence of ground-truth object masks in real-world datasets, and what strategies are used to ensure the accuracy of the object-centric scene representations?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending the editing capabilities of the method to include more complex operations like object merging or moving?
