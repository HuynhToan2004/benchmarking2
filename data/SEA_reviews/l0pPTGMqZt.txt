 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the domain generalization (DG) problem where classes are only present in specific domains, a scenario often overlooked by existing DG methods. It introduces a new task, domain-linked domain generalization, and proposes the FOND algorithm to learn domain-invariant representations from domain-shared classes. The algorithm uses a contrastive loss to maximize mutual information between positive inter-domain samples and a fairness loss to ensure equalized odds between domain-shared and domain-linked classes. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive experiments on various benchmark datasets, showing significant improvements over existing methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its experimental setup and the clarity of its methodological explanations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a novel and important problem in domain generalization, specifically focusing on domain-linked classes which are often overlooked by existing methods.
- The proposed method is well-motivated and clearly explained, with a focus on learning domain-invariant representations from domain-shared classes and ensuring fairness between domain-shared and domain-linked classes.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to evaluate the proposed method, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods in domain generalization.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for understanding complex machine learning concepts.
- Theoretical insights are provided, which help in understanding the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough literature review, particularly in the area of contrastive learning, to better position its contributions within the existing body of work.
- There are concerns about the experimental setup, particularly the use of a fixed number of shared classes and the lack of baseline comparisons on datasets with different class distributions.
- The paper could be clearer in its explanations, particularly in sections where the methodology and experimental results are described.
- Some of the claims made in the paper, such as the necessity of learning domain-invariant representations from domain-shared classes, are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation, including a broader range of datasets and more detailed comparisons with existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the rationale behind the choice of the fixed number of shared classes and consider varying this number in future experiments?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, particularly in handling larger datasets?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the specifics of the datasets used and the methodology employed?
- How does the proposed method ensure that it does not inadvertently introduce biases or unfairness in the predictions, especially in scenarios where classes are not evenly distributed across domains?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the experimental results, particularly those that are not clearly explained in the text?
