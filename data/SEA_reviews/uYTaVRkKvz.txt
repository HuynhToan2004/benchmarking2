 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MuseGNN, a scalable graph neural network (GNN) designed for large-scale graph learning. MuseGNN employs an unfolded optimization method, integrating subgraph sampling and a sampling-based energy function to enhance scalability and maintain competitive accuracy. The model's training process involves iterative minimization of the energy function, which is extended to include auxiliary variables and a penalty term to manage node embeddings across different subgraphs. The paper provides convergence analysis and empirical evidence of MuseGNN's effectiveness on large-scale graph classification tasks, demonstrating its ability to handle graphs with over 100 million nodes. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach and the lack of detailed convergence analysis, particularly in terms of the role of hyperparameters and the comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The authors have effectively demonstrated the scalability of their approach by applying it to a large-scale graph with over 100 million nodes.
- The methodology is well-explained, and the convergence analysis is sound, providing a theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed method outperforms baseline models in terms of accuracy and speed.
- The paper includes a detailed appendix with proofs and additional experiments, enhancing the credibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as similar approaches using sampling and subgraph clustering have been previously explored in other works.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the role of hyperparameters in the convergence analysis, which is crucial for understanding the practical applicability of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of comparison with other sampling-based methods, which could provide a clearer picture of the method's effectiveness and novelty.
- The convergence analysis provided is not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of the specific conditions under which the results hold.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to other types of graphs or its performance on smaller graphs.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role of the hyperparameter γ in the convergence analysis and how it affects the practical application of the method?
- How does the proposed method compare with other sampling-based methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the convergence behavior of the energy function with different values of γ?
- In terms of scalability, how does the proposed method perform on smaller graphs, and what are the implications of this for its practical application?
- How does the method handle the integration of different subgraphs, and what are the implications of this for the model's performance and interpretability?
