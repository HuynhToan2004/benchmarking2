 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of label delays in online continual learning, proposing a new framework that explicitly models label delays between data and label streams. It introduces a novel method, Importance Weighted Memory Sampling (IWMS), which leverages labeled data from memory to mitigate the performance decline caused by label delays. The method was tested on various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in bridging the accuracy gap caused by label delays. The paper also discusses the challenges of using SSL and TTA techniques in the presence of label delays and proposes a baseline that rehearses from labeled memory samples most similar to the new unlabeled samples.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and practical problem in continual learning, specifically focusing on the impact of label delays, which is a significant issue in real-world applications.
- The proposed method, Importance Weighted Memory Sampling (IWMS), is well-motivated, simple, and effective, showing good performance in the continual learning setting.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experimental results and analyses, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The experiments are extensive, covering a wide range of SSL and TTA methods, which helps to establish the robustness and applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a novel perspective on the problem of label delays in continual learning, which is an interesting and under-explored area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the experimental setup and the description of the computational budget.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other baselines, such as those that use a larger computational budget or different sampling strategies.
- The paper's contribution to the field is somewhat limited as the proposed method, IWMS, is a simple extension of existing methods like experience replay.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how it might perform in different scenarios.
- The experimental results, particularly on the CLOC dataset, are not convincing, with low performance metrics that do not adequately demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper's setting and the assumptions made, such as the constant label delay factor, may not hold in more complex real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the computational budget and the experimental setup, particularly how the computational budget is normalized and how it affects the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when the computational budget is increased, and what are the implications for the performance of the naive baseline?
- In terms of the IWMS method, how is the memory buffer selected, and what are the implications of using different buffer sizes?
- How does the proposed method compare to other baselines, particularly in scenarios where the computational budget is not a constraint?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the datasets used and the computational resources required?
- Given the assumptions made in the paper, how do the authors address the potential limitations of their proposed method in more complex real-world scenarios?
