 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for clustering neurons based on maximally discriminative stimuli (MDS), using a digital twin model trained on neuron responses to various stimuli. This approach alternates between optimizing stimuli to maximally activate neurons within a cluster and reassigning neurons to clusters based on their responses to these stimuli. The method is applied to datasets from the mouse retina, marmoset retina, and macaque visual area V4, demonstrating its ability to identify functional cell types across different species and experimental settings. The paper claims that MDS can be used to quickly identify cell types in new experiments without requiring extensive model training, and that the resulting stimuli are interpretable and can be used to assign functional cell types on the fly. However, the paper has been criticized for its limited evaluation, lack of comparison to other methods, and potential over-reliance on specific model architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear introduction and explanation of the proposed method.
- The methodology is novel and innovative, particularly the use of maximally discriminative stimuli (MDS) for cell type identification in the visual system, which is a significant advancement over existing methods.
- The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness in identifying functional cell types in the visual system, with examples from mouse retina, marmoset retina, and macaque visual area V4.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the methodology, including a clear explanation of the algorithm and its application, which is beneficial for understanding and replicating the research.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons to ground truth and previous methods, and demonstrates the method's ability to quickly identify cell types in new experiments without requiring extensive model training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough evaluation of the method's performance, particularly in terms of how well it identifies functional cell types and its generalizability across different datasets and experimental conditions.
- There is a lack of comparison to other methods, which limits the ability to assess the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method against existing approaches.
- The paper's claims about the method's ability to identify functional cell types are not well-supported by the results presented, particularly in the mouse retina dataset where the method does not achieve perfect clustering.
- The paper's reliance on specific model architectures, such as the digital twin model, raises concerns about the method's robustness and generalizability.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as its inability to capture continuous cell types or its dependence on the specific model architecture used.
- There are issues with the clarity and detail in some sections of the paper, particularly in the description of the methodology and the results, which could be improved for better understanding and reproducibility.
