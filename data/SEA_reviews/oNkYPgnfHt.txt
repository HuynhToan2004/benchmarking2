 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Concept Bottleneck Memory Models (CB2Ms), an extension of Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) that incorporates a two-fold differentiable memory mechanism to generalize interventions to new situations. This model aims to improve model performance by learning from a few initial interventions and detecting potential mistakes in the CBM bottleneck, thereby requesting targeted interventions. The approach is evaluated on various datasets, demonstrating its ability to generalize interventions to unseen data and improve model performance. However, the paper's evaluation is criticized for its simplicity and lack of rigorous comparison with existing methods. The paper also faces criticism for its clarity and the need for more comprehensive experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed method.
- The introduction of Concept Bottleneck Memory Models (CB2Ms) is a significant advancement in the field, providing a novel approach to generalizing interventions to new situations.
- The paper demonstrates the potential of CB2Ms in improving model performance through experiments on challenging scenarios like handling distribution shifts and confounded data.
- The authors have provided a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, showcasing its ability to generalize interventions to unseen data and identify wrongly inferred concepts.
- The paper is well-motivated and provides a clear explanation of the method, which is supported by detailed experimental evaluations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency.
- The paper's evaluation is criticized for its simplicity and lack of rigor, with concerns about the choice of datasets and the methodology used.
- The paper's clarity needs improvement, particularly in explaining the methodology and the experimental setup.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the impact of noise in the memory module and the potential for overfitting due to the additional parameters introduced by the memory module.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by better organizing the content and providing more detailed explanations of the experimental results and methodology.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the term "mistake" as used in the paper? Is it referring to misclassified data points or incorrect concept representations?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the choice of datasets and the methodology used in the experiments?
- How does the proposed method handle noisy or incorrect interventions, and what measures are in place to prevent overfitting due to the additional parameters introduced by the memory module?
- Could the authors expand on the methodology used in the experiments, particularly in how they fill the memory module and how they measure the effectiveness of the interventions?
- How does the proposed method perform when combined with other concept-based models, and what are the potential applications of this approach in real-world scenarios?
