 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) methods for simulating wave functions of quantum systems, particularly focusing on fermionic systems. The authors propose a new antisymmetrization layer, termed "sortlets," which leverages sorting operations to achieve a computational complexity of O(NlogN), a significant improvement over the traditional O(N^3) complexity of determinant-based methods. This new layer is integrated with a neural network backbone, specifically the PsiFormer model, to enhance the flexibility and accuracy of the wave function representation. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the sortlet ansatz and its potential for achieving chemical accuracy in quantum chemistry. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope in terms of the systems studied and the lack of comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The introduction of the sortlet ansatz, which is based on sorting operations, is a novel and interesting approach to quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) methods.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the sortlet ansatz, including its mathematical properties and the construction of the sortlet ansatz.
- The paper includes a variety of numerical examples and comparisons with existing methods, demonstrating the practical applicability and potential of the sortlet ansatz.
- The paper addresses an important problem in quantum chemistry, specifically the antisymmetrization of wave functions in quantum Monte Carlo methods, which is crucial for accurately modeling quantum systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is limited in scope, focusing on only a few small molecules and not demonstrating the method's effectiveness on larger or more complex systems, such as transition metal complexes.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, particularly those that also employ neural network backbones, which could have provided a clearer picture of the sortlet ansatz's advantages and limitations.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of the wave function's nodal structure, which is critical for accurately modeling the quantum system's behavior.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the sortlet ansatz, such as its potential inaccuracies due to the number of sortlets used and its scalability to larger systems.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational resources required for implementing the sortlet ansatz, which could be a significant barrier for its adoption in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the sortlet ansatz perform on larger or more complex systems, such as transition metal complexes?
- Can the sortlet ansatz be combined with other neural network-based methods, such as those using Jastrow factors, to potentially improve its performance?
- What are the specific computational resources (e.g., CPU time, GPU memory) required for implementing the sortlet ansatz, and how do these compare to other existing methods?
- Is there a possibility to extend the sortlet ansatz to bosonic systems, and if so, what are the potential challenges or advantages in doing so?
- How does the sortlet ansatz compare in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy with other existing methods, such as those using Vandermonde determinants?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and conditions used in their numerical experiments, particularly the number of sortlets used and the optimization steps involved?
