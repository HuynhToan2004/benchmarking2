 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for evaluating dataset similarity in multi-source learning, leveraging a metric that does not require model training. The metric, based on a combination of cosine similarity and Hellinger distance, is used to quantify the similarity between datasets, which is crucial for assessing the performance of multi-source learning algorithms. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through extensive experiments across various datasets and learning algorithms, showing that the metric correlates well with model performance. The paper also addresses privacy concerns by proposing a privacy-preserving method for computing the metric. However, the paper's reliance on specific assumptions and its scalability to high-dimensional data are noted as limitations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of multi-source learning, focusing on the evaluation of dataset similarity which is crucial for effective data integration and model performance.
- The proposed method is innovative, utilizing a novel combination of cosine similarity and Hellinger distance to measure dataset similarity, which is both theoretically sound and empirically validated.
- The paper is well-structured, providing a clear and logical flow of ideas, and the experiments are comprehensive, covering a wide range of datasets and learning algorithms, demonstrating the robustness and applicability of the proposed method.
- The inclusion of a privacy-preserving method for computing the similarity metric is a valuable addition, addressing a critical concern in data-sharing scenarios.
- The paper provides a thorough literature review and presents a well-reasoned approach to the problem, including theoretical insights and practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that features across datasets are concordant, which may not hold true in real-world scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the results.
- The method's scalability is questionable, particularly in high-dimensional datasets, where the computational complexity could be a significant barrier.
- The method's reliance on specific assumptions, such as the Gaussian distribution of labels, may limit its applicability in more complex scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and potential future research directions.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, especially in the experimental results section.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when features across datasets are not concordant?
- Can the method be adapted to handle datasets with different feature spaces, such as text and image data?
- How sensitive is the method to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the regularization parameter λ?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the method and how it scales with the size of the datasets?
- What are the implications of the assumptions made in the method, particularly the assumption of Gaussian label distributions?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
