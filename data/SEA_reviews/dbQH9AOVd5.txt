 **Summary:**
The paper introduces I-STAR, a novel regularization method aimed at enhancing isotropy in the embedding space of large language models (LLMs) during training. The method, which utilizes a differentiable and stable measure of isotropy called IsoScore*, is designed to improve model performance by adjusting the isotropy levels in contextualized embeddings. The authors claim that decreasing isotropy in LLMs can lead to better performance on various downstream tasks, challenging the conventional belief that isotropy is beneficial. The paper presents extensive experimental results across multiple tasks and models, demonstrating the effectiveness of I-STAR in improving model performance by decreasing isotropy. However, concerns are raised regarding the clarity and detail of the experimental setup, the lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, and the potential over-generalization of results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, I-STAR, for measuring isotropy that is differentiable and stable on mini-batch computations, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experimental results demonstrate that decreasing isotropy in contextualized embeddings improves performance on most tasks and models considered, providing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the experimental results, which is helpful for understanding the implications of their findings.
- The proposed method, IsoScore*, is shown to be a more accurate measure of isotropy compared to existing methods, and it is demonstrated to be effective in improving model performance by decreasing isotropy in LLMs.
- The paper challenges the dominant belief in the NLP literature that encouraging isotropy improves performance on downstream tasks, which is an important contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some parts, particularly in the experimental setup and the description of the regularization methods used.
- There is a lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, which makes it difficult to assess the relative performance of the proposed method against state-of-the-art techniques.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of its findings across different models and datasets, which could limit the applicability of the results.
- The experimental design is not detailed enough, particularly in terms of the hyperparameters used and the specifics of the training setup, which could affect the reproducibility and validity of the results.
- The paper's claims about the importance of isotropy in LLMs are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a critical omission in ethical considerations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the hyperparameters used and the specifics of the training setup?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between isotropy and intrinsic dimensionality in LLMs, especially how the method influences these dimensions?
- What are the specific societal impacts of the proposed method, and how do the authors address these concerns?
- How does the method perform in different scenarios, such as with different models and datasets, and what are the implications of these variations on the results?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the method's limitations and potential applications beyond the scope of the current study?
