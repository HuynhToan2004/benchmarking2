 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called "logit bias" to address the issue of underfitting in Mean Absolute Error (MAE) loss, particularly in scenarios with a large number of classes. This method shifts the initial logit distribution to enhance the overlap with the derivative region of the loss function, thus improving learning performance. The authors empirically demonstrate the effectiveness of this method across various datasets, including Cifar-10, Cifar-100, Fashion-MNIST, and WebVision, showing that it can outperform or match the performance of other loss functions like Cross Entropy. The method is simple, requiring only a single parameter that is class-dependent, and is shown to be effective in noisy label settings.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is simple and effective, demonstrated by extensive experiments across different datasets and noise levels.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The method is novel and has the potential to be applied to other loss functions, showing promising results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the method, which is supported by empirical evidence.
- The proposed method does not require hyperparameter tuning, which is a significant advantage for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comparison with state-of-the-art methods for noisy label learning, such as DivideMix, which could have strengthened the evaluation of the proposed method.
- The method's effectiveness is not thoroughly evaluated in scenarios with a large number of classes, which is a critical application area for the method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the method performs in scenarios with different types of noise, such as label shift or out-of-distribution (OOD) noise.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed theoretical analysis, especially regarding the choice of the parameter 'C' in equation (4).
- The method's performance in terms of computational cost and scalability, particularly in large-scale datasets, is not discussed.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in the clarity of the figures and the explanation of certain equations and parameters.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with different types of noise, such as label shift or OOD noise?
- Can the proposed method be applied to other loss functions, and if so, how does it compare to other state-of-the-art methods?
- How is the parameter 'C' in equation (4) chosen, and what is its impact on the method's performance?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method performs in scenarios with a large number of classes, and how it scales with the size of the dataset?
- How does the method compare in terms of computational cost and training time to other methods, especially in large-scale datasets?
- Can the authors clarify the meaning and choice of the parameter 'C' in the equations, and provide a more detailed explanation of how the method is applied in practice?
