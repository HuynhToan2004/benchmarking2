 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel text-driven method for generating 3D indoor scenes, specifically focusing on generating layouts and textures from text prompts. It utilizes a two-stage approach, where the first stage generates a 3D layout using a diffusion model trained on a 3D dataset, and the second stage synthesizes textures using a fine-tuned ControlNet model. This method allows for interactive editing of the generated scenes, including resizing and moving objects, and is evaluated against existing methods such as Text2Room and MVDiffusion. The paper is praised for its clarity and the effectiveness of its methodology, although concerns are raised about the limited dataset used for training and the potential for overfitting.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The proposed method effectively solves the Penrose Triangle problem by separating layout and appearance generation, which is crucial for generating plausible indoor scenes.
- The interactive editing capability of the method is a significant advantage, allowing for real-time adjustments to the generated scenes.
- The method is original in its approach to generating 3D indoor scenes from text prompts, and the results are of high quality, as demonstrated in the supplementary material.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including user studies, which validate the effectiveness of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how the scene code is defined and how it is used to represent a scene layout, which could confuse readers unfamiliar with the method.
- The method's reliance on a specific dataset (Structured3D) for training raises concerns about potential overfitting and the generalizability of the method to other datasets.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the method, such as the lack of support for different room types and the potential for generating unrealistic scenes due to the limitations of the dataset.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with existing methods, such as MVDiffusion, to clarify the advantages of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process, including the size of the training dataset and the number of iterations required for training.
- The paper could be improved by including more diverse examples in the supplementary material to better demonstrate the capabilities of the method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the scene code is defined and used to represent a scene layout?
- How does the method handle different room types, and what are the implications of this limitation?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods like MVDiffusion, and how does it address the concerns raised about the method's reliance on a specific dataset?
- How does the method ensure that the generated scenes are realistic and plausible, especially when dealing with complex layouts and textures?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, including the size of the training dataset and the number of iterations required for training?
- How does the method handle different levels of detail in the generated scenes, and what are the implications of this for the quality and realism of the generated content?
