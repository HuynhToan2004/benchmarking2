 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel task in Temporal Graph Learning (TGL) termed "Recent Link Classification" (RLC), where the objective is to classify the labels of newly created edges in a temporal graph. This task is distinct from traditional link prediction tasks as it requires the prediction of labels based on existing edges, rather than predicting the existence of future edges. The authors propose a new benchmark dataset for RLC and evaluate several models using the Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) metric, which is more robust to imbalanced datasets. They also introduce a new model, Graph Profiler, which incorporates a readout layer to predict interaction class based on node features and recent interaction features. The paper explores the differences between RLC and existing TGL tasks and provides a detailed analysis of the challenges and potential solutions in RLC.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel task in Temporal Graph Learning (TGL) called "Recent Link Classification" (RLC), which is significant and relevant for real-world applications.
- The authors propose a new benchmark dataset for RLC and evaluate several models using the Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) metric, which is more robust to imbalanced datasets.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the differences between RLC and existing TGL tasks, which is valuable for understanding the unique challenges and potential solutions in RLC.
- The proposed Graph Profiler model is a simple and effective solution for RLC, incorporating a readout layer to predict interaction class based on node features and recent interaction features.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed methodologies and experimental results, making it easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed Graph Profiler model with other state-of-the-art models in RLC, which could provide a more robust validation of its effectiveness.
- The use of MCC as the primary evaluation metric may not be sufficient, as it does not provide a comprehensive view of the model's performance, particularly in terms of its ability to handle class imbalance.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The datasets used in the experiments are relatively small, which may not adequately represent the complexity and diversity of real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed model, which is essential for understanding its scalability and efficiency in larger, more complex datasets.

**Questions:**
- How does the Graph Profiler model perform when compared to other state-of-the-art models in RLC, particularly in terms of classification accuracy and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential ways to address these limitations?
- How does the Graph Profiler model handle class imbalance, and what are the implications of using MCC as the primary evaluation metric in this context?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the choice of MCC as the primary evaluation metric and consider including other metrics that could provide a more comprehensive view of the model's performance?
- How does the proposed Graph Profiler model scale with larger, more complex datasets, and what are the implications of its computational complexity for its practical application?
