 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel progressive subnetwork training method, termed RaPTr, aimed at enhancing the efficiency of training large language models (LLMs) by gradually increasing the complexity of the subnetworks during training. This methodology, which is a variation of the gradual stacking approach, is designed to maintain the full model throughout training while only training subnetworks within it at each step. Theoretical insights are provided to explain the stability of the loss function across different stages of training, supported by empirical results showing that RaPTr outperforms baseline training and stacking in terms of training efficiency and performance on downstream tasks. The paper also discusses the theoretical aspects of layer stability and provides empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to training large language models (LLMs) by focusing on the stability of the loss function across different stages of training, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method of progressive subnetwork training, specifically the RaPTr algorithm, is a simple yet effective method that outperforms baseline training and stacking in terms of training efficiency and performance on downstream tasks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of the proposed method, which is a strong point in validating the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience, including both experts and non-experts in the field.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves significant improvements over baseline training and stacking, showing its practical applicability and potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other recent and relevant works, such as "Accelerating Training of Large Language Models by Gradual Stack" and "Leveraging Data-Dependent Network Growth for Efficient Pre-Training of Large Language Models," which could provide a more comprehensive view of the proposed method's advantages and limitations.
- The paper's empirical results are somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on BERT and a 1.6B decoder-only LLM, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method across different types and sizes of LLMs.
- There is a lack of clarity in the experimental setup, particularly in the definition and implementation of the RaPTr algorithm, which could confuse readers and limit the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the implementation of the RaPTr algorithm, such as the specifics of the layer selection and the scaling mechanisms used, which could affect the reproducibility and applicability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and the practical implications of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the training time and the impact on downstream tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other recent and relevant works, such as "Accelerating Training of Large Language Models by Gradual Stack" and "Leveraging Data-Dependent Network Growth for Efficient Pre-Training of Large Language Models," to provide a more comprehensive view of the proposed method's advantages and limitations?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types and sizes of LLMs, such as those based on different architectures or trained on different datasets?
- Could the authors provide more details on the implementation of the RaPTr algorithm, particularly in terms of the layer selection and the scaling mechanisms used, to clarify the methodology and enhance the reproducibility of the results?
- How does the proposed method impact the training time and the computational resources required, and what are the practical implications of these requirements for large-scale training of LLMs?
- Can the authors elaborate on the theoretical insights provided in the paper, particularly the stability of the loss function across different stages of training, and how these insights inform the design of the RaPTr algorithm?
