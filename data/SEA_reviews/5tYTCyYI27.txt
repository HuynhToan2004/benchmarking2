 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the calibration problem in deep learning models, focusing on the impact of regularization techniques on calibration performance. It introduces the concept of calibratability, which refers to a model's ability to be recalibrated post-training, and argues that regularization can hinder this ability. The authors propose a method called progressively layer-peeled training (PLT) to enhance calibration without sacrificing accuracy. This method gradually freezes the top layers during training to prevent overtraining and improve calibration performance. The paper provides empirical evidence supporting these claims through various experiments, including comparisons with other methods and ablation studies. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the experimental setup, the lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, and the potential over-reliance on specific datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel concept of calibratability, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of deep learning models.
- The experiments are well-designed, providing a clear demonstration of the impact of regularization on calibration performance.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and it includes comprehensive ablation studies to support the proposed method.
- The proposed method, PLT, is simple yet effective, and the experiments show that it can improve calibration performance without sacrificing accuracy.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of deep learning, focusing on the calibration issue which is often overlooked.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the experimental setup and the rationale behind certain choices, such as the selection of regularization methods and the number of epochs for each phase.
- The comparison with other methods is limited, and the paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation against existing calibration techniques.
- The paper's claims about the impact of regularization on calibration performance are not convincingly supported by the data presented, and the correlation between calibratability and discriminability is not adequately explained.
- There is a lack of discussion on the implications of the findings, particularly in relation to the generalizability of the results and the potential limitations of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, including a comparison with other methods that address similar issues.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of regularization methods and the number of epochs for each phase?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where regularization is not used during training?
- Can the authors provide more details on the calculation of the Pearson correlation coefficients and the choice of layers for calibration in the experiments?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods that aim to improve calibration performance, such as those mentioned in the literature?
- Could the authors elaborate on the implications of their findings for the broader community, particularly in terms of the practical applications and potential limitations of the proposed method?
