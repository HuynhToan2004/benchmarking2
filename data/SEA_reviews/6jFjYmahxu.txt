 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a differentiable sound simulation framework called DiffSound, which integrates differentiable shape representation, high-order finite element analysis, and a differentiable audio synthesizer to optimize material properties and object shapes from sound signals. The framework is designed to model the physical properties of objects and their gradients, enabling inverse reasoning for various applications. The methodology includes a differentiable tetrahedral mesh representation, a high-order finite element analysis module, and a differentiable additive synthesizer. Experiments demonstrate the framework's effectiveness in estimating material properties, object shapes, and impact positions from sound signals. However, concerns about the complexity of the method, the need for more realistic sound quality, and the generalizability to different materials and shapes are highlighted.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework that integrates differentiable shape representation, high-order finite element analysis, and a differentiable audio synthesizer, which enables the optimization of material properties and object shapes from sound signals.
- The method is well-explained, with clear mathematical derivations and a detailed explanation of the optimization process.
- The proposed method is innovative, combining existing techniques in a novel way to address the problem of inverse reasoning from sound signals.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, and includes supplementary materials that enhance the understanding of the methodology.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various applications, including material parameter estimation, impact position estimation, and object shape estimation.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of modal sound synthesis, which has practical implications in areas such as robotics and multisensory applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method is complex and difficult to follow, particularly the sections on differentiable tetrahedral representation and high-order finite element analysis.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as the inability to model complex shapes or non-linear sounds, and the high computational cost of the differentiable tetrahedral mesh representation.
- The sound quality produced by the method is not realistic, with a noticeable lack of naturalness and complexity in the sound signals.
- The generalizability of the method to different materials and shapes is questionable, as the results are heavily dependent on the material properties and shapes used in the experiments.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as the potential for overfitting due to the complexity of the model and the need for a large dataset for training.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other existing methods, such as those using neural networks for modal analysis or differentiable shape representations.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the scalability and applicability of the method in real-world scenarios, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific advantages of using a high-order tetrahedral element over a lower-order element, and explain why the method is described as differentiable when it only differentiates the mass and stiffness matrices?
- How does the method handle the optimization of material properties and object shapes, particularly in scenarios where the sound signal is noisy or incomplete?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the method, especially in terms of the time required for each simulation step?
- How does the method handle the optimization of material properties and object shapes when the sound signal is not available, or when the sound signal is not sufficient for accurate optimization?
- Can the authors provide more examples or demonstrations of the method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios, such as in robotics or multisensory applications?
- How does the method compare with other existing methods in terms of accuracy, efficiency, and applicability in different scenarios?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and suggest potential areas for future research and improvement?
