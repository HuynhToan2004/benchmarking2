 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called prompt gradient projection (PGP) for continual learning, specifically in the context of prompt-based continual learning. PGP integrates gradient projection with prompt-tuning to prevent forgetting in class and task incremental settings. The method leverages Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to ensure the gradient is orthogonal to the subspace spanned by old features, thereby preserving previous knowledge. The paper validates its approach through experiments on various benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing forgetting and improving performance. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, insufficient theoretical analysis, and lack of comparison with recent prompt-based continual learning methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The proposed method, PGP, is innovative and integrates gradient projection with prompt-tuning effectively, which is a novel approach in continual learning.
- The paper provides theoretical guarantees against forgetting for prompt-tuning, enhancing the reliability and predictability of the model's performance.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PGP in reducing forgetting and improving performance, validating the theoretical claims.
- The methodology is simple and easy to implement, making it accessible to a broader audience and practical for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other recent prompt-based continual learning methods, such as DyTox, Coda-Prompt, and LGCL, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned as it primarily combines existing methods without significant innovation.
- The theoretical analysis provided is insufficient, particularly in explaining the mathematical formulations and the practical implications of the equations used.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity introduced by the method, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to specific datasets and does not include a broader range of benchmarks, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's methodology and results are not sufficiently novel or differentiated from existing methods, which could limit its impact and contribution to the field.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other recent prompt-based continual learning methods to better contextualize the contributions of PGP?
- How does the computational complexity of PGP compare to other methods, and what are the practical implications of this complexity in terms of runtime and memory usage?
- Could the authors clarify the mathematical formulations and the practical implications of the equations used in the paper, particularly the orthogonal condition and its impact on performance?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between stability and plasticity, and what are the implications of this trade-off in practical applications?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the hyperparameters used and the specifics of the training and evaluation procedures, to enhance reproducibility and transparency?
