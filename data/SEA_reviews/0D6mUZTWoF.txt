 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a continual graph learning framework named TACO, which addresses the challenges of catastrophic forgetting in graph neural networks (GNNs) by storing information from previous tasks as a reduced graph. This framework is designed to maintain topological information of nodes and capture correlations between old and new tasks through a graph coarsening method called RePro. The methodology involves a three-step process: combining, reducing, and generating, where nodes are grouped into clusters and assigned to super-nodes, and the graph is coarsened to a target size. The paper presents a theoretical analysis and empirical results, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing baselines in terms of performance and efficiency. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the paper's presentation, and the empirical evaluation, which was criticized for its limited scope and lack of comprehensive comparisons.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and challenging problem of continual graph learning, focusing on the realistic setting of streaming graphs where the model is trained on a subgraph for each task.
- The proposed method is novel and technically sound, introducing a dynamic continual graph learning framework that efficiently preserves topological information of nodes and takes advantage of the correlation between old and new tasks.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the proposed method and the experiments conducted.
- The theoretical analysis provided supports the effectiveness of the proposed method, and the empirical results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing baselines.
- The paper includes a comprehensive ablation study and extensive experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed method, which helps in understanding the impact of different components of the model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods in the field of continual graph learning, which could provide a better understanding of the proposed method's advantages and limitations.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and readability, especially in sections with complex technical details.
- The empirical evaluation is limited in scope, focusing only on node classification and not exploring other tasks such as link prediction or graph-level classification.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential future research directions.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed method, especially in terms of its scalability and applicability to large-scale datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and existing methods in the field of continual graph learning, particularly in terms of novelty and technical contributions?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of catastrophic forgetting in the context of continual graph learning, and what are the implications for the performance of the model over time?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed method, especially in terms of its scalability and applicability to large-scale datasets?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and efficiency with other existing methods, particularly in scenarios where the dataset size and complexity vary?
- Can the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential directions for future research to address these limitations?
