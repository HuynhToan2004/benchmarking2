 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LIRE, a novel approach to optimizing rewards through a listwise paradigm for preference alignment in large language models (LLMs). LIRE directly incorporates the rewards of multiple candidates into the loss function, optimizing against it in a compact and effective framework. The method is designed to enhance the model's ability to generate responses that align with human preferences, potentially improving the stability and consistency of model performance. The paper presents extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of LIRE, comparing it against state-of-the-art methods and showing its superiority in preference alignment tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to optimizing rewards through a listwise paradigm, which directly incorporates the rewards of multiple candidates into the loss function, optimizing against it in a compact and effective framework.
- The proposed self-enhancement algorithm is designed to progressively optimize the reward through iterative training, enhancing the model's ability to generate responses that align with human preferences.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the stability and consistency of the model performance, with results showing that LIRE outperforms state-of-the-art methods in preference alignment tasks.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The method is simple and effective, with a straightforward approach that is easy to implement.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed method, comparing it with previous methods and demonstrating its superior performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the self-enhancement algorithm works, particularly in terms of its iterative training process and the role of the temperature parameter T.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential issues with the stability of the model's performance and the sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of reward hacking, which could lead to models that generate responses that align with the reward function but do not truly reflect human preferences.
- The methodology of the self-enhancement algorithm is not clearly explained, and the paper does not provide sufficient details on how it is implemented.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with some of the latest and most relevant methods, such as RAFT, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the self-enhancement algorithm work, and what is the role of the temperature parameter T in this process?
- Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the self-enhancement algorithm, including the specifics of the iterative training process?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of reward hacking, and what steps have been taken to ensure that the model's responses truly reflect human preferences?
- Why was the decision made to not include a discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the dataset used in the experiments, including the size and the specifics of how it was collected and processed?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that use similar approaches but have not been directly compared in the paper?
