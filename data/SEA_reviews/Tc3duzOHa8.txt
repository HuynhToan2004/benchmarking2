 **Summary:**
The paper introduces RODEO, a data-centric method aimed at enhancing out-of-distribution (OOD) detection by generating diverse and near-distribution outliers. The method leverages both image and text domains to guide the diffusion model, which generates OOD data that closely resembles the in-distribution data. This approach is supported by theoretical insights and extensive experiments, demonstrating that RODEO outperforms existing methods in both clean and adversarial settings. The paper also discusses the challenges of OOD detection in adversarial settings and proposes a novel methodology that addresses these challenges.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, RODEO, is simple, intuitive, and effective, as demonstrated by strong experimental results.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of related works and a detailed explanation of the methodology, including theoretical insights and extensive ablation studies.
- RODEO's adaptive outlier exposure (OE) method is innovative, utilizing both text and image domains to generate near-distribution and diverse outliers, which is crucial for robust OOD detection.
- The method achieves significant results on various datasets, including medical and tiny datasets, and establishes a state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in adversarial settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the description of the "CLIP Guidance" and "Data Filtering" steps, which could benefit from additional explanation or visual aids.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the reliance on a large amount of in-distribution data and the potential for overfitting.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with other methods, especially in adversarial settings, and more extensive evaluations on different datasets.
- The paper does not address how the method performs in scenarios where the in-distribution data is not diverse or when the OOD data is significantly different from the in-distribution data.
- The method's reliance on a large amount of in-distribution data might limit its applicability in scenarios where such data is not available or is difficult to obtain.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the "CLIP Guidance" and "Data Filtering" steps are implemented?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the in-distribution data is not diverse, or when the OOD data is significantly different from the in-distribution data?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how do these limitations impact its practical applicability?
- How does the method perform when the in-distribution data is not diverse, and what are the implications of this for the effectiveness of the method?
- Could you provide more insights into the method's performance in adversarial settings, and how does it compare to other methods in terms of robustness?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the OOD data is not semantically or stylistically related to the in-distribution data?
- How does the method ensure that the generated outliers are not too similar to the in-distribution data, which could lead to false positives in OOD detection?
