 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the neural correlates of multimodal integration by comparing the predictive capabilities of multimodal models against unimodal models using SEEG recordings from patients with epilepsy. The study employs a variety of models, including vision-only, language-only, and multimodal models, to predict neural activity in response to movies. The authors find that multimodal models outperform unimodal models in predicting neural activity in certain brain regions, particularly around the temporoparietal junction. The paper also explores the impact of different model architectures and training methods on the predictive accuracy. Despite the innovative approach and the potential implications for understanding brain function, the paper has been critiqued for its limited scope, lack of rigorous statistical analysis, and unclear presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a novel and important question regarding the neural correlates of multimodal integration, using a large-scale dataset of SEEG recordings.
- The study employs a variety of multimodal models, including vision-only, language-only, and multimodal models, to predict neural activity, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a detailed description of the methodology used to analyze the data.
- The use of multimodal models to predict neural activity in the brain is a promising approach that could lead to new insights into brain function and multimodal integration.
- The study demonstrates that multimodal models can outperform unimodal models in predicting neural activity, which is a significant finding.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to a single dataset, which may not be representative of other populations or conditions.
- The statistical analysis used in the study is not rigorous, with concerns about the reliability of the results due to the small sample size and potential bias in the data.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential confounding factors that could influence the results, such as the use of the same models for both predicting and analyzing the data.
- The presentation of the results is unclear, with insufficient detail provided about the specific brain regions and models used, making it difficult to replicate or build upon the findings.
- The paper lacks a clear definition of what constitutes a "multimodal" site, which could lead to ambiguity in the interpretation of the results.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the models used, which could help in understanding the relative strengths and weaknesses of different model architectures.
- The paper does not adequately address the ethical considerations of using invasive brain recordings, particularly in the context of patient privacy and informed consent.

**Questions:**
- How were the models selected for use in the study, and how were the hyperparameters tuned?
- What specific models were used to predict the neural activity, and how were these models chosen?
- How were the neural data analyzed, and what statistical tests were used to determine the significance of the results?
- Can the authors clarify the definition of a "multimodal" site and how this definition was used to identify the regions of interest?
- How does the paper address the potential confounding factors, such as the use of the same models for both predicting and analyzing the data?
- What are the implications of the findings for understanding brain function and multimodal integration, and how might these findings be applied in clinical or therapeutic contexts?
- Could the authors provide more detail about the data collection and processing methods, particularly how the SEEG recordings were obtained and processed?
