 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to learning Nash equilibria in monotone games using a perturbation method termed "slingshot perturbation." This approach involves adding a perturbation term to the gradient, which guides the learning process towards a perturbed equilibrium. The authors demonstrate the convergence of this method under both full and noisy feedback conditions, showing that the equilibrium achieved is close to the original equilibrium. The paper also discusses the convergence rates and empirical results, comparing the proposed method with existing approaches. Despite its novelty, the paper's contribution is seen as incremental, as it largely builds upon existing works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel perturbation method, termed "slingshot perturbation," which guides the learning process towards a perturbed equilibrium, demonstrating its effectiveness in both full and noisy feedback settings.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, with detailed proofs provided in the appendix, which supports the claims made about the convergence rates and the closeness of the perturbed equilibrium to the original equilibrium.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the algorithms and their implications.
- The empirical results show that the proposed algorithm outperforms existing methods, such as Multiplicative Weight Update (MWU) and Optimistic Multiplicative Weight Update (OMWU), in terms of convergence speed.
- The paper covers a broad range of existing payoff-regularized algorithms, providing a unified framework for understanding their convergence properties.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is seen as incremental, primarily focusing on extending existing works rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach.
- The convergence rates and the practical implications of the perturbation method are not thoroughly explored, particularly in terms of how the perturbation strength affects the convergence rate and the practical applicability of the method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for slower convergence due to the perturbation term.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with some figures and results not adequately explained or justified.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other existing methods that also demonstrate last-iterate convergence in monotone games, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of the perturbation strength parameter in the convergence rates and the practical implications of the perturbation method?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods that also demonstrate last-iterate convergence in monotone games, such as those mentioned in the literature review?
- In the experimental results, how does the convergence speed of the proposed method compare with that of other methods under different feedback conditions?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or justifications for the experimental setup and results, particularly in figures where the convergence rates are not clearly depicted?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of noise in the feedback, and what are the implications of this for the convergence rates and the practical applicability of the method?
