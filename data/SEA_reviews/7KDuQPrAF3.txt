 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel foundation model for error correction codes (FECCT), which is designed to be universal across different error correction codes (ECCs) and can generalize to unseen codes. The model leverages a code-invariant initial embedding, learned modulation of attention maps conditioned on the Tanner graph, and a length-invariant code-aware noise prediction module based on the parity-check matrix. It is trained on multiple short- and medium-length codes and demonstrates performance that matches or surpasses state-of-the-art methods. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its lack of clarity in certain sections, particularly in the mathematical notation and the explanation of key concepts. Additionally, the novelty of the approach is questioned due to similarities with existing methods like ECCT.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel foundation model for error correction codes, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The model is designed to generalize to unseen codes, demonstrating its applicability beyond specific training data.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The proposed model outperforms the state-of-the-art ECCT model, showing a practical advantage.
- The authors have conducted a thorough ablation study to understand the impact of each component of the proposed method, providing valuable insights into the effectiveness of the model's design.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear problem setting and background sections that aid in understanding the complex concepts presented.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the training and testing methods, particularly the training data and the specifics of the training process.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned due to similarities with existing methods like ECCT, which might lead to concerns about the originality of the contributions.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed model, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There are some inconsistencies and unclear notations in the mathematical formulations, which could confuse readers and detract from the paper's clarity.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalization capabilities of the model, which is a critical aspect for the model's utility in practical scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and testing methods, particularly the training data and the specifics of the training process?
- How does the proposed model compare to other existing methods in terms of performance and efficiency, especially in scenarios where the model is fine-tuned for specific codes?
- Can the authors clarify the notations and mathematical formulations to improve the paper's readability and understanding?
- How does the model handle different types of noise beyond additive Gaussian noise, and can it be adapted to handle other types of noise effectively?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed model over existing methods, particularly in terms of generalization and performance on unseen codes?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed model and how these might impact its practical application?
