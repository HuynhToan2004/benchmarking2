 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of neural collapse on differential privacy (DP) in the context of fine-tuning pre-trained models. It explores how the misclassification error in a binary classification task remains constant under idealized conditions of perfect neural collapse, regardless of the dimensionality of the private training set or the learning rate. However, when perturbations are introduced, the misclassification error becomes dependent on the dimensionality. The paper proposes strategies to mitigate these issues, such as pre-processing features or employing dimension reduction methods. It also discusses the theoretical implications of these phenomena and provides experimental validations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic of enhancing privacy in machine learning models, focusing on the interesting phenomenon of neural collapse in representation learning.
- The theoretical analysis provided is sound, and the proofs are rigorous, contributing to the understanding of differential privacy (DP) in the context of neural collapse.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear structure that facilitates understanding of the complex concepts discussed.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and well-presented, supporting the theoretical claims and demonstrating the impact of perturbations on neural collapse.
- The paper introduces novel insights into the relationship between neural collapse and DP, potentially leading to new strategies for improving the utility of DP models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes a strong and potentially unrealistic condition of perfect neural collapse, which might not reflect real-world scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the practical implications and limitations of the proposed strategies for mitigating the non-robustness of neural collapse under perturbations.
- The assumptions regarding the class balance and the nature of the loss function are not sufficiently justified or explained, which could affect the applicability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing works, particularly in terms of the novelty and depth of the theoretical contributions.
- The experimental setup and the choice of parameters (such as the learning rate and the perturbation level) are not sufficiently justified, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper could be improved by including a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer discussion on the limitations and potential future research directions.

**Questions:**
- How does the choice of the learning rate impact the behavior of NoisySGD under different conditions of neural collapse?
- Can the results of this study be extended to more complex scenarios, such as multi-class classification problems or more general neural network architectures?
- What are the specific advantages of using the proposed strategies for mitigating the non-robustness of neural collapse under perturbations, and how do they compare to other existing methods?
- How does the paper's analysis of neural collapse in the context of differential privacy differ from or build upon existing works, particularly those that explore the relationship between neural collapse and generalization error?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly regarding the class balance and the nature of the loss function, and discuss how these assumptions affect the applicability of the results?
