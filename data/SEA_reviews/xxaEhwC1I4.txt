 **Summary:**
The paper explores the convergence properties of the last iterate of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) in the context of composite optimization problems with convex functions. It extends previous work by Zamani & Glineur (2023) to handle sub-Gaussian noise and provides convergence rates for both the expected value and high probability settings. The analysis is unified across various settings, including general domains, composite objectives, non-Euclidean norms, Lipschitz conditions, smoothness, and strong convexity. The paper also discusses the convergence rates for both smooth and strongly convex cases, showing that the last iterate can converge at rates comparable to those of averaged iterates. However, it relies on the assumption of a convex objective function and does not consider non-convex cases.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a unified analysis that can be applied to various settings simultaneously, offering a comprehensive approach to the study of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) in different scenarios.
- The results are significant as they demonstrate the convergence of the last iterate of SGD, which is crucial for understanding the performance of SGD in practical applications.
- The proofs are novel and well-organized, providing a clear and structured presentation of the theoretical contributions.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and concise introduction and a logical flow that makes it easy to follow.
- The convergence rates for both smooth and strongly convex cases are presented, which are not only interesting but also potentially useful for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the objective function is convex, which may limit its applicability to non-convex optimization problems, a significant area of interest in machine learning.
- The proof techniques used are similar to those in previous works, and the paper does not clearly distinguish its novel contributions from existing methods.
- There is a lack of discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, which could limit the paper's impact on the broader machine learning community.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing works, particularly in terms of the convergence rates and the assumptions made.
- The assumptions regarding the boundedness of the gradient and the strong convexity of the objective function are quite strong and may not hold in many practical scenarios.
- The paper does not address the issue of non-smoothness, which is a significant limitation given the prevalence of non-smooth optimization problems in machine learning.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the practical implications of the theoretical results, especially in terms of their applicability to real-world machine learning problems?
- How do the convergence rates compare with those of other stochastic gradient descent algorithms, such as those using momentum or averaging techniques?
- Can the proof techniques be extended to handle non-convex optimization problems, and if so, how might this be achieved?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their assumptions, particularly the strong convexity and boundedness assumptions, and how these might affect the applicability of their results?
- How does the paper's approach compare to other recent works on stochastic gradient descent, such as those by Zamani & Glineur (2023) and Liu et al. (2023)?
