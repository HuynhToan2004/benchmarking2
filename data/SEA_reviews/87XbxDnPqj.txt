 **Summary:**
The paper explores a novel approach to CT reconstruction by directly modeling the nonlinear measurement process using a nonconvex optimization problem, which is solved using gradient descent. The authors prove that this method can converge to the global optimum, providing a theoretical framework for reducing metal artifacts in CT images. The approach is tested on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing artifacts compared to traditional methods. The paper also discusses the practical implications of using a Gaussian model for the measurement matrix and provides a theoretical analysis of the convergence rate.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to CT reconstruction by directly modeling the nonlinear measurement process, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The authors prove that their method can converge to the global optimum, providing a theoretical framework for the effectiveness of their approach.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise language that makes the content accessible and understandable.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate that the proposed method can effectively reduce metal artifacts in CT images, which is a significant practical application of the theoretical results.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the convergence rate of gradient descent, which is an interesting and potentially impactful contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the measurement matrix is Gaussian, which may not be realistic in practical applications of CT imaging, where the measurement matrix is typically structured and dependent on the ray directions.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to the case where the measurement matrix is Gaussian, which may not generalize well to more practical scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of metal artifacts in CT images, which is a significant limitation given the claimed reduction in artifacts.
- The paper's theoretical results are not clearly linked to the practical applications, which makes it difficult to understand the implications of the theoretical findings for real-world CT reconstruction.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, which limits the ability to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed method in more complex or realistic scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the assumption of a Gaussian measurement matrix affects the practical applicability of their results?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods for handling metal artifacts in CT imaging?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including quantitative measures of artifact reduction and comparisons to existing methods?
- How does the proposed method handle noise in the measurements, and what are the implications of this for the effectiveness of the reconstruction?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending their theoretical results to more practical scenarios, such as those involving structured measurement matrices?
