 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the two-sided matching problem in a multi-armed bandit setting where both players and arms are uncertain about their preferences. It introduces a new algorithm called Round-Robin ETC, which allows players to explore and learn their preferences without direct communication, and aims to achieve optimal stable regret. The paper provides theoretical analysis of the regret bounds, comparing them to existing results in the literature. However, the paper is criticized for its clarity and organization, with some reviewers expressing concerns about the assumptions, the novelty of the algorithm, and the depth of the regret analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a novel and interesting problem setting, where both players and arms are uncertain about their preferences, which is a significant departure from traditional bandit learning models.
- The proposed algorithm, Round-Robin ETC, is well-designed to handle the complexities of the two-sided matching problem, and the regret analysis is thorough, providing a clear understanding of the algorithm's performance.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and understandable, and the presentation is clear, which aids in following the complex algorithmic descriptions.
- The paper introduces a new algorithm that does not require strong assumptions about the structure of preferences, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key terms and concepts, which could hinder understanding for readers unfamiliar with the specific problem setting.
- There is a notable absence of empirical results, which could have strengthened the paper by demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness in practical scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the novelty of the algorithm, as it seems to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The regret analysis is limited to the player side, which is a significant omission given the importance of understanding the impact of the algorithm on both sides of the market.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which could detract from its professional presentation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of the event E^a in the algorithm, particularly how it affects the learning process?
- How does the paper handle the case where arms may misreport their preferences after learning them, and what implications does this have for the stability of the matching?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of their algorithm with existing approaches, highlighting the specific advantages and innovations of their method?
- In terms of the regret analysis, could the authors consider extending the analysis to include the arm-pessimal stable regret, and how might this impact the overall assessment of the algorithm's performance?
- How does the paper address the potential for strategic behavior by arms, and what measures are in place to prevent or mitigate the effects of such behavior?
