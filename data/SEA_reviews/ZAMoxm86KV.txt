 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FZooS, a novel federated optimization algorithm that leverages trajectory-informed surrogate gradients to address the challenges of query and communication round inefficiencies in federated zeroth-order optimization (ZOO). FZooS uses a trajectory-informed derived Gaussian Process for gradient estimation and employs random Fourier features to approximate the mean of the Gaussian Process, thereby allowing the transfer of gradient surrogates across clients and the server. This approach aims to reduce the number of function queries and improve communication efficiency. Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate that FZooS outperforms existing methods in terms of query and communication efficiency, providing a significant contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in federated optimization by focusing on the challenges of query and communication round inefficiencies in federated zeroth-order optimization (ZOO).
- The proposed algorithm, FZooS, is novel and innovative, leveraging trajectory-informed surrogate gradients for query-efficient gradient estimation and adaptive gradient correction for communication round efficiency.
- The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical results that demonstrate the superiority of FZooS over existing methods in terms of query and communication efficiency.
- The use of Gaussian processes to model local functions and the application of random Fourier features to approximate the mean of the Gaussian Process are highlighted as significant contributions to the field.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the algorithm and its components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the local functions are Gaussian processes, which might limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where this assumption may not hold.
- The convergence analysis in the paper is limited, and the conditions for convergence are not clearly stated or justified.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its assumptions and the conditions under which it might not perform optimally.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some figures showing marginal improvements over existing methods, and the significance of these improvements is not clearly demonstrated.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the proposed method with the number of clients and the dimensions of the problem.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of the number of function queries required and the communication costs.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made about the local functions being Gaussian processes and discuss the implications of this assumption on the applicability of the method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the local functions are not Gaussian processes?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods in terms of the number of function queries required and the communication costs?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and under what conditions might it not perform optimally?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the scalability of the proposed method with the number of clients and the dimensions of the problem?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the convergence analysis, particularly the conditions for convergence and their justification?
