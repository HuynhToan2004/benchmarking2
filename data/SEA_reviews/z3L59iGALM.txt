 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to scalable Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) for route optimization, leveraging techniques such as graph compression, spatial parallelization, and improved initialization conditions inspired by eigenvector algorithms. The authors propose a new algorithm, rhip, which generalizes classic IRL algorithms and enables fine-grained control over performance characteristics. This method is tested on a large-scale route planning problem involving hundreds of millions of states and demonstration trajectories, achieving significant improvements in route quality. The paper also provides theoretical insights and empirical evaluations, demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness in handling complex routing tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The proposed algorithm, rhip, is novel and provides a generalization of classic IRL algorithms, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper presents a scalable solution to a large-scale route planning problem, demonstrating its practical applicability and potential for real-world impact.
- The authors provide a comprehensive empirical evaluation, including a large-scale real-world application, which supports the robustness and effectiveness of their approach.
- The paper is original and addresses an important problem in the field of IRL, with a focus on scalability which is a critical issue in many real-world applications.
- The authors have provided a theoretical analysis of MaxEnt, which is a valuable contribution to the understanding of this algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other existing methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the algorithm's advantages and limitations.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method across different types of MDPs, particularly those with high-dimensional states or continuous actions.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations and societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its broader implications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed methods, especially in relation to the size of the MDP and the number of demonstration trajectories.
- The paper's experimental setup and the choice of metrics used for evaluation are not clearly explained, which could limit the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The paper's reliance on specific hardware configurations (e.g., V100 machines) for achieving state-of-the-art results may not be accessible to all researchers, and the results may not generalize to other hardware platforms.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other existing methods, including a discussion on the scalability and performance of these methods in different scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform in other types of MDPs, particularly those with high-dimensional states or continuous actions?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations and societal impacts of their proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability and computational complexity issues as the size of the MDP and the number of demonstration trajectories increase?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and the choice of metrics used for evaluation, and provide more detailed results on the performance of the algorithm across different scenarios?
- Is there a possibility to extend the proposed method to other types of MDPs, and if so, what are the potential challenges and limitations?
