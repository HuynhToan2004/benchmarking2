 **Summary:**
The paper explores the possibility of achieving uniformly tight generalization bounds in the overparameterized setting, a critical aspect of understanding the generalization capabilities of neural networks. It introduces the concept of estimability, demonstrating that no generalization bound can be uniformly tight under common assumptions, such as the VC dimension of the hypothesis class being larger than the training set size. The paper further discusses the limitations of algorithm-dependent generalization bounds, showing a trade-off between learning performance and estimability. Despite these negative results, the paper provides insights into the challenges of achieving tight generalization bounds and suggests potential directions for future research.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the main question and a structured presentation of the results.
- The paper provides a novel perspective on the generalization of neural networks, particularly in the context of overparameterized settings, which is an important and timely topic.
- The authors introduce a new notion of estimability, which is both intuitive and useful for understanding the generalization capabilities of neural networks.
- The paper offers a clear and insightful discussion of the implications of the results, which are significant in the context of generalization theory.
- The negative results presented are strong, providing a compelling argument against the possibility of achieving uniformly tight generalization bounds under common assumptions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion of existing literature, particularly works that have addressed similar questions or results. This omission could mislead readers about the novelty and relevance of the paper.
- The assumptions made in the proofs, such as the overparameterized setting and the nature of the learning algorithm, are not sufficiently justified or explained, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper's conclusions are somewhat limited by the specific assumptions and definitions used, which might not apply universally across different settings or models.
- There is a lack of experimental validation or empirical evidence to support the theoretical claims, which could strengthen the paper's impact and practical relevance.
- The paper's scope is somewhat narrow, focusing primarily on the overparameterized setting and specific types of generalization bounds, which might not be representative of more general scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions and implications of the overparameterized setting, particularly in relation to the number of parameters in the network and the number of examples in the training set?
- How do the results of this paper compare or contrast with similar works, such as those by Bartlett and Long (2021)?
- In the context of algorithm-dependent generalization bounds, how does the paper's approach differ from or build upon existing works, such as those by Hardt et al. (2016)?
- Could the authors provide more details or examples of how the results might apply to specific types of generalization bounds, such as those based on the VC dimension or other complexity measures?
- How do the authors envision the practical implications of their findings, particularly in terms of designing new generalization bounds or improving existing ones?
