 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Preference-Conditioned Inversion (PCI) framework, a novel method for multi-objective molecular optimization (MOMO), focusing on the efficient exploration of the Pareto front. PCI leverages a differentiable surrogate oracle to generate Pareto optimal molecules, guided by a non-dominated gradient. Theoretical analysis and empirical evaluations demonstrate the method's effectiveness in capturing the Pareto front and generating diverse molecules that satisfy specific preferences. The approach is compared to existing methods such as linear scalarization and evolutionary algorithms, showing superior performance in terms of diversity and average property scores. However, the paper lacks a detailed comparison with more recent and diverse MOMO methods, and the experimental setup could be expanded to include a broader range of objectives and datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise descriptions of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The introduction of the PCI framework for preference-conditioned MOMO is novel and effectively addresses the challenges of finding Pareto optimal solutions in multi-objective molecular optimization.
- The method is supported by a comprehensive theoretical analysis, which demonstrates its ability to converge towards preference-conditioned Pareto optimal solutions in discrete chemical space.
- The empirical results show that the PCI method significantly outperforms existing baselines in terms of diversity and average property scores, indicating its practical utility in generating diverse molecules that satisfy specific preferences.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the method, including the differentiable surrogate oracle and the preference-guided Pareto molecular optimization, which are crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other recent and diverse MOMO methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the PCI method's performance.
- The experimental setup is limited to a few datasets and objectives, which may not fully demonstrate the method's generalizability across different chemical spaces and objective functions.
- The method's reliance on a differentiable surrogate oracle for generating Pareto optimal molecules may limit its applicability to more complex or less predictable systems.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the PCI method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key terms and concepts, which could hinder the reader's understanding.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the discrepancy in the Pareto front coverage between PCI and Linear Scalarization, as shown in Figure 3?
- How does the PCI method handle conflicting or correlated objectives, especially when the oracle is not differentiable?
- Is there a possibility to extend the PCI method to handle more complex or less predictable systems, such as those found in early-stage drug discovery?
- Can you provide more details on the computational cost and efficiency of the PCI method, especially in comparison to other existing methods?
- How does the PCI method perform when the preference vector is not well-defined or when the objectives are not clearly defined?
- Could you explain the rationale behind the choice of the differentiable surrogate oracle and how it contributes to the method's effectiveness?
