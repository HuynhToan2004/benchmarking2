 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework that integrates control-theoretic modeling with machine learning to analyze animal behavior, specifically focusing on zebrafish and C. elegans locomotion. The approach employs an iLQR-VAE to learn latent dynamics from behavioral data, which are then used to reconstruct observed movements. This methodology not only identifies the control inputs that drive behavior but also provides insights into the underlying dynamics of movement. The framework is validated using zebrafish behavioral data and extended to C. elegans, demonstrating its applicability across different species. The paper also discusses the potential of this method in disentangling different categories of movements and providing a more interpretable representation of behavioral dynamics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework for understanding animal behavior by integrating control-theoretic modeling with machine learning, which is particularly relevant for analyzing complex behaviors such as those observed in zebrafish and C. elegans.
- The methodology is well-documented, with clear explanations of the approach and its application to real-world data, which enhances the paper's accessibility and reproducibility.
- The paper presents a comprehensive analysis of the learned latent dynamics, including the identification of control inputs and the disentanglement of different categories of movements, which provides valuable insights into the neural mechanisms underlying behavior.
- The paper effectively uses a sparse control signal to drive a latent Recurrent Neural Network (RNN), preserving key kinematic features and disentangling different categories of movements.
- The methodology is innovative, combining linear model reduction with a nonlinear model to identify the latent dynamics underlying behavior, which is particularly relevant for understanding complex behaviors.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology, results, and implications, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's methodology, while innovative, does not sufficiently differentiate itself from existing approaches, particularly in terms of the use of linear model reduction to identify latent dynamics.
- The paper's application to C. elegans behavioral data is limited, with only a brief discussion on the results, which raises questions about the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and efficiency of the model, particularly in terms of training and inference times, which could be a significant limitation for broader application.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the methodology, including the assumptions made and the potential biases or errors introduced by the model.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with other models and methods to better understand the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the results, particularly in terms of their implications and the potential for further research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of the proposed methodology compared to existing approaches, particularly in terms of the use of linear model reduction?
- How does the model handle the complexity of behavioral data, particularly in terms of the dimensionality and sparsity of the control inputs?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other models and methods, particularly in terms of the computational complexity and the effectiveness of the model in different behavioral contexts?
- How does the model perform in terms of predicting behavioral outcomes, particularly in scenarios where the control inputs are not sparse or where the behavior is not well-defined?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the model and how these might affect the interpretation of the results?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications of the model in understanding neural activity related to movement?
