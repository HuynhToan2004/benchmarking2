 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for training classifiers that can abstain from making predictions when certain conditions are met, such as fairness and accuracy constraints. This is achieved through a two-stage process involving an integer programming (IP) problem that determines optimal abstention rates and a learning-to-abstain model that predicts these rates for unseen data. The framework aims to balance fairness and accuracy, providing theoretical insights into the feasibility of achieving specific fairness definitions under given constraints. The paper's approach is supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results, demonstrating its effectiveness in real-world datasets. However, the paper's assumptions, such as the accessibility of true labels and the computational complexity of the IP problem, are noted as significant limitations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in fairness-aware learning, specifically focusing on the use of classifiers that abstain, which is a less explored area in fairness research.
- The proposed framework is innovative, integrating two stages: an integer programming (IP) stage for training and a learning-to-abstain model for inference, which is novel and interesting.
- The paper provides theoretical insights into the feasibility of achieving specific fairness definitions under given constraints, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework, showing improvements in fairness and accuracy compared to existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, which enhances its accessibility and readability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions, such as the availability of true labels during training and the computational complexity of the IP problem, are not adequately addressed.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning its applicability in scenarios where true labels are not accessible.
- The experimental setup is limited to binary classification and does not include multi-class settings, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing methods like LTD and FSCS, which could have provided a clearer picture of its advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to demographic parity and does not extend to other fairness definitions, which could limit the applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not discuss the societal impact of the proposed method, which is a significant omission in the ethical discussion of AI systems.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the true labels are accessed during training and whether this is a common assumption in similar studies?
- How does the computational complexity of the IP problem scale with the size of the dataset, and what are the practical implications of this complexity?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods like LTD and FSCS, particularly in terms of the fairness and accuracy trade-offs?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where true labels are not accessible, and what are the potential impacts on the fairness and accuracy of the predictions?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential societal impacts of their proposed method, particularly in terms of fairness and bias in AI systems?
- In the experimental setup, how does the proposed method handle scenarios with more than two groups, and what are the implications of this extension on the fairness and accuracy of the predictions?
