 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Error Norm Truncation (ENT), a method for training text generation models that utilizes the error norm between the predicted and true one-hot distributions to identify and remove noisy tokens. This approach is compared against traditional methods such as Loss Truncation and Tailr, showing improved performance on various tasks like language modeling, machine translation, and text summarization. The method is claimed to be robust to data noise, particularly in scenarios where the data is noisy or synthetic, and has been evaluated on both synthetic and real-world noisy data. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the method and its potential for practical application.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the proposed method is simple and intuitive.
- The idea of using error norm to estimate the quality of data is innovative and has been validated through comprehensive experiments across different tasks, including language modeling, machine translation, and text summarization.
- The method is shown to be effective in improving the performance of models trained on noisy data, as demonstrated by improvements in BLEU scores and perplexity.
- The paper provides a theoretical connection between error norm and other metrics like TVD and KL-Divergence, which helps in understanding the theoretical underpinnings of the method.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed method outperforms previous methods in various scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the sensitivity of the proposed method to the choice of error norm threshold, which is a crucial hyperparameter.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definition and use of the error norm in the context of the method, particularly how it is calculated and how it relates to the training data.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the theoretical underpinnings of the method, including a clearer explanation of how the error norm is derived and its relation to other metrics like TVD and KL-Divergence.
- The method's applicability to different types of noise, such as adversarial noise, is not discussed, which could limit its practical utility.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which could be a significant concern when using data truncation methods.
- The paper's claims about the method's effectiveness in handling different types of noise (e.g., untranslated text, misordered words) are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- How sensitive is the proposed method to the choice of error norm threshold, and what are the implications of choosing different thresholds?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of how the error norm is calculated and how it relates to the training data?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of noise, particularly adversarial noise?
- Could the authors clarify the method's performance in scenarios where the data is not noisy but rather contains rare or out-of-distribution examples?
- How does the method compare to other data truncation methods, particularly those that use different metrics like KL-Divergence?
- What are the implications of using the error norm as a measure of data quality, and how does it compare to other metrics like TVD and KL-Divergence?
- How does the method handle the issue of overfitting, and what steps have been taken to mitigate this risk?
