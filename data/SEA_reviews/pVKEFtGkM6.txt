 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the calibration of pre-trained language models (LMs) and their aligned counterparts, focusing on the multiple-choice question (MCQ) setting. It demonstrates that pre-trained LMs are underconfident in their predictions, while aligned LMs are overconfident, which affects their calibration. The study reveals that the alignment process, particularly in-context learning, can alter the predictive distribution and lead to overconfidence. The paper proposes a post-hoc calibration method using temperature scaling to mitigate these issues. However, it is noted that the method's effectiveness varies across different tasks and requires further validation on more diverse datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a thorough empirical analysis of the differences between pre-trained LMs and aligned LMs, specifically focusing on their calibration in the multiple-choice question (MCQ) setting.
- The authors identify two distinct types of uncertainties in LMs under the MCQ setting, which are responsible for answer decision and format preference, respectively.
- The paper introduces a simple yet effective post-hoc calibration method that can be applied to any pre-trained LM to improve its calibration performance.
- The findings of the paper, such as the impact of alignment processes on calibration and the role of in-context learning in calibration, are significant and could be of interest to the broader community.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive empirical studies that provide insights into the calibration of pre-trained and aligned LMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a direct comparison with other calibration methods such as KDE, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The methodology and results are limited to the MCQ setting, which might not generalize well to other types of questions or tasks.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.
- The post-hoc calibration method, while simple, may not be as effective as other methods like KDE across all tasks, and its effectiveness varies depending on the task.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications for certain assumptions and methodologies used, such as the decomposition of predictive probability and the choice of post-hoc calibration methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a direct comparison with KDE in their experiments to better evaluate the effectiveness of their proposed method?
- How does the proposed post-hoc calibration method compare to other calibration methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- In the context of the MCQ setting, how does the proposed method perform on other types of questions or tasks beyond those tested?
- How does the alignment process affect the calibration of pre-trained LMs in open-ended settings, and could the findings from the MCQ setting be generalized to such settings?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of post-hoc calibration methods and provide more detailed justifications for their selection?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of overconfidence in aligned LMs, and what are the implications of this overconfidence in practical applications?
