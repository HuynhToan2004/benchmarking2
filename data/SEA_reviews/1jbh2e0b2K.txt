 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the theoretical and practical aspects of multitask fine-tuning (MTF) for foundation models, focusing on its effectiveness in improving performance on target tasks with limited labeled data. It introduces a task selection algorithm aimed at choosing related fine-tuning tasks that enhance model performance on target tasks. Theoretical analyses and empirical evidence are provided to support the claims, showing that MTF with diverse and consistent tasks can lead to reduced errors in target tasks compared to directly adapting pretrained models. The paper also discusses the potential of MTF in bridging domain gaps and its applicability to various datasets and models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis on multitask fine-tuning (MTF) for foundation models, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of MTF in various applications.
- The proposed task selection algorithm is practical and achieves better performance compared to directly fine-tuning on all possible tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes both theoretical and empirical studies, which support the claims effectively.
- The authors discuss the potential of MTF in bridging domain gaps and its applicability to various datasets and models, highlighting its relevance and importance in the field.
- The paper includes detailed experimental results and ablation studies, which provide insights into the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some parts, particularly in the definitions and notations used, which could make it difficult for readers to fully understand the theoretical analysis and the experimental setup.
- The selection of fine-tuning tasks is not adequately discussed, raising concerns about the practicality and effectiveness of the proposed method in real-world scenarios where relevant tasks might not be readily available.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to binary classification tasks, which might not generalize well to other types of tasks, such as multi-class classification.
- The empirical results are not convincing, with some experiments showing only marginal improvements or even lower accuracy compared to baseline methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational costs and the scalability of the proposed method, which could limit its practical application in resource-constrained environments.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definitions and notations used in the paper, particularly in the theoretical analysis sections, to make the content more accessible to readers?
- How does the proposed task selection algorithm perform when applied to different datasets and models? Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results or comparisons to better illustrate the method's effectiveness?
- What are the specific computational costs and scalability issues associated with the proposed method, and how do these impact the practical application of the method in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the theoretical analysis to include more complex tasks, such as multi-class classification, and how this might affect the overall findings?
- How does the paper address the issue of task selection, particularly in scenarios where relevant tasks are not readily available or where the selection process might introduce biases or errors?
