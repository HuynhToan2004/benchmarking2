 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedAU, a novel method for federated learning (FL) that addresses the issue of unknown participation statistics among clients. FedAU dynamically adjusts the weights of client contributions based on their participation history, which is crucial for mitigating the impact of heterogeneous client behavior on the FL process. Theoretical analysis and experimental results demonstrate that FedAU converges to the optimal solution of the original objective and achieves linear speedup, outperforming baseline methods in various participation patterns. The paper is well-structured, with clear contributions and a comprehensive theoretical framework, although it has some limitations such as strong assumptions and potential scalability issues.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and presents a novel approach to address the unknown participation statistics in federated learning, which is a significant and practical issue in real-world scenarios.
- The proposed FedAU algorithm is simple, intuitive, and effective, demonstrating linear speedup and outperforming baseline methods in various participation patterns.
- The convergence analysis of FedAU is rigorous and includes a novel methodology that connects estimation error and convergence, providing insights into the behavior of the algorithm.
- The paper is well-motivated, with a clear presentation of the problem and a logical structure that effectively communicates the contributions and methodology.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing the superior performance of FedAU over existing methods in various scenarios, including the ability to handle heterogeneous participation rates effectively.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies on strong assumptions, such as the independence of the client participation random variable across time, which may not hold in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the computational complexity and memory requirements.
- The theoretical analysis assumes that the client participation random variable is independent across time, which may not be realistic in practical federated learning settings.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other baseline methods, especially those that address similar issues in federated learning, such as variance reduction methods.
- The convergence analysis is complex and may be difficult for readers to fully understand without additional explanations or simplifications.
- There is a need for more extensive experimental validation, including more datasets and different participation patterns to confirm the robustness and effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more insights into the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of computational complexity and memory requirements?
- How does the proposed method perform when the client participation random variable is not independent across time?
- Could the authors discuss the relationship between the proposed method and other baseline methods, especially those that address similar issues in federated learning?
- Can the convergence analysis be simplified to make it more accessible to readers without a background in convergence analysis?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the participation statistics are not only unknown but also uncontrollable and heterogeneous?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle different types of participation patterns, such as those that are not independent or not Bernoulli distributed?
