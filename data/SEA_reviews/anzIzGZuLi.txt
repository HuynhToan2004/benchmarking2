 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TP-BERTa, a pre-trained language model tailored for tabular data prediction. It employs novel techniques such as relative magnitude tokenization and intra-feature attention to effectively handle numerical and categorical features. The model is trained on a large-scale dataset and demonstrates competitive performance against traditional methods like Gradient Boosted Decision Trees. The paper also discusses the challenges in adapting language models to tabular data and provides extensive experimental results, including comparisons with existing models and ablation studies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, providing a clear and concise explanation of the proposed methods.
- The problem of adapting language models to tabular data is addressed, which is a significant and relevant issue in the field.
- The paper introduces novel techniques such as relative magnitude tokenization and intra-feature attention, which are well-motivated and effectively address the challenges of handling numerical and categorical features in tabular data.
- Extensive experiments are conducted, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods and providing a comprehensive evaluation of the model's performance.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the model.
- The experimental setup and comparisons could be improved, particularly in terms of using more recent and relevant baselines and ensuring fair comparisons.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed model over existing methods are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which could benefit from more rigorous testing and validation.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected to improve the paper's professional presentation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the relative magnitude tokenization technique handles continuous values, especially in scenarios where the range of values is large?
- How does the intra-feature attention module manage the potential for interference between feature names and values, especially when they are highly correlated?
- The paper mentions a need for more powerful base LMs for handling classification and regression tasks jointly. Could the authors elaborate on this point and discuss potential future improvements?
- In the experimental setup, could the authors provide more details on the datasets used and the methodology for selecting the datasets for pre-training and testing?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed methods compare to other models, and what are the practical implications of this complexity in terms of real-world applications?
