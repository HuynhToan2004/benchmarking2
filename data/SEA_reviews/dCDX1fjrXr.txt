 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the Sparse Labels Node Classification (SLNC) problem, where the number of labeled nodes is very limited and not evenly distributed across classes. It proposes a framework named Estimating Label Information (ELI) to estimate node labels and improve semi-supervised learning performance. The framework involves four steps: label distribution estimation, key node selection, label distribution incorporation, and optimization. Experiments are conducted on several benchmark datasets, showing that ELI can enhance the performance of existing semi-supervised node classification baselines. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of novelty, as it heavily relies on existing methods and does not sufficiently address the challenge of class imbalance. The methodology's complexity and the clarity of its presentation are also questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a novel setting in node classification, where the number of labeled nodes is very small and not evenly distributed across classes, which is a significant problem in real-world applications.
- The proposed framework, ELI, is well-articulated and presents a clear methodology for estimating node labels, which is a crucial aspect of the research.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and comprehensive experiments conducted on multiple benchmark datasets.
- The performance of ELI is robust, showing a significant improvement over existing methods in the semi-supervised node classification task.
- The paper is generally well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for academic research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it heavily relies on existing methods such as AGC and label propagation, with only minor modifications.
- The motivation behind the research is not clearly articulated, making it difficult to understand the significance and relevance of the study.
- The complexity of the framework is high, with multiple steps and hyperparameters, which could limit its practical application.
- The paper does not adequately address the challenge of class imbalance, which is a significant issue in the SLNC problem.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with some results (e.g., in Figure 1) being misleading or not clearly explained.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The writing quality needs improvement, with several typos and grammatical errors that detract from the professionalism of the paper.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the motivation behind the research and how it contributes to the advancement of knowledge in the field of node classification?
- How does the proposed method handle the challenge of class imbalance, especially in scenarios where the number of labeled nodes per class is very small?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup and results, especially in relation to the performance of the ELI framework in different scenarios?
- Is there a possibility to simplify the framework by reducing the number of steps or hyperparameters, making it more accessible for practical applications?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of their method and discuss potential directions for future research?
