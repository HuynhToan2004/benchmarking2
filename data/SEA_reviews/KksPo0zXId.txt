 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel post-training structured pruning framework that aims to prune pre-trained models without retraining, focusing on structured pruning. This framework is designed to optimize the sparsity of channels, using a two-phase layer reconstruction strategy to minimize the accuracy drop induced by pruning. It groups coupled structures across layers based on their dependencies, measures and marks the least important channels in a group, and then performs a two-phase layer reconstruction strategy using a small amount of unlabeled data to recover the accuracy drop. The method is evaluated on various datasets including CIFAR-10/100 and ImageNet, showing promising results in terms of accuracy and speed. However, the paper lacks a detailed comparison with some existing methods and does not provide sufficient experimental validation on larger datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive review of related work.
- The proposed post-training structured pruning framework is innovative and effective, achieving good results in terms of accuracy and speed.
- The method is straightforward and easy to implement, with the potential to be applied to other architectures like Transformers.
- The paper includes thorough experiments and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method can be applied to large models, and the authors plan to release the code, which could benefit the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with some existing methods, particularly those that also use a two-phase layer reconstruction strategy.
- The method's performance on larger datasets such as ImageNet is not convincingly demonstrated, with the results showing significant accuracy drops compared to retraining-based methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental validation on larger datasets, which could undermine the claims of its effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method to larger models or datasets, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- There are some minor issues with the clarity and presentation of the paper, such as the organization of figures and the explanation of certain experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between their method and other existing methods that use a similar two-phase layer reconstruction strategy?
- How does the method perform on larger datasets such as ImageNet, and what are the potential reasons for the observed accuracy drops?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to larger models, and if so, what are the expected challenges and limitations?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the use of calibration data and its impact on the results?
- How does the method handle the scalability issues, and what are the potential solutions for improving its applicability to larger models and datasets?
