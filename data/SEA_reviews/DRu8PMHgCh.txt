 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedTrans, a novel client utility estimation method designed to guide client selection in federated learning (FL) scenarios with noisy data. FedTrans leverages a Bayesian framework to model client utility and its relationships with weight parameters and local model performance. It employs a variational inference algorithm to infer client utility at the FL server, utilizing auxiliary data to enhance model performance. The method is evaluated against several baseline methods, demonstrating improvements in accuracy and robustness in noisy scenarios. However, concerns about the clarity of presentation, the complexity of the method, and the practicality of the assumptions underpinning the method's effectiveness are highlighted.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a detailed description of the proposed method and the experimental setup.
- The problem addressed in the paper is significant, focusing on improving the performance of federated learning (FL) in noisy scenarios, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The proposed method, FedTrans, is novel and provides a practical solution by utilizing auxiliary data to estimate client utility, which is transparent to clients and does not require additional operations on the client side.
- The paper includes comprehensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in accuracy and robustness compared to existing methods.
- The theoretical analysis provided supports the proposed method, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the assumptions made in the method, particularly concerning the auxiliary data and its quality, which are crucial for the method's effectiveness.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with some sections being unclear or confusing, which could hinder understanding of the proposed method.
- The method's complexity, particularly in terms of the number of hyperparameters and the computational demands, is not adequately addressed, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to specific datasets and does not include a broader range of datasets or baseline methods, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the mathematical formulations and experimental results, which need to be addressed for clarity and accuracy.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the method, which could affect its applicability in different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when the auxiliary data is of low quality or not representative of the client data?
- What are the specific roles and impacts of the hyperparameters in the method, particularly in terms of their sensitivity and tuning?
- Can the method be adapted to handle different types of noise, such as adversarial attacks or different distributions of label noise?
- How does the method handle clients with different levels of noise in their data, and what are the implications for the overall performance of the FL system?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of the auxiliary dataset and the parameters used in the method, and how are these choices justified?
- Can the method be extended to handle different types of federated learning systems, such as decentralized FL, and how might this affect its performance?
