 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel evaluation metric, SharpeRatio@k, designed to assess the risk-return tradeoff in off-policy evaluation (OPE) for reinforcement learning (RL). The metric is inspired by portfolio evaluation in finance and measures the efficiency of policy portfolios formed by an OPE estimator under varying online evaluation budgets. The authors demonstrate that SharpeRatio@k can effectively distinguish between conservative and high-stakes OPE estimators, identifying the most efficient estimator capable of forming superior portfolios of candidate policies. The paper also provides a comprehensive benchmark experiment using SharpeRatio@k to evaluate various OPE estimators, highlighting its potential utility in policy selection and deployment in online A/B tests. The proposed metric is implemented in an open-source software, enhancing its practical applicability and utility.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel evaluation metric, SharpeRatio@k, which effectively measures the risk-return tradeoff in off-policy evaluation (OPE) and provides a comprehensive benchmark experiment to evaluate various OPE estimators.
- The proposed metric is inspired by portfolio evaluation in finance, which is a novel approach in the RL community, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of SharpeRatio@k in distinguishing between conservative and high-stakes OPE estimators.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined problem.
- The implementation of SharpeRatio@k in an open-source software enhances its practical utility and accessibility, making it a valuable tool for the RL community.
- The paper provides a thorough benchmark experiment using SharpeRatio@k to evaluate various OPE estimators, which can help in identifying the most efficient estimator capable of forming superior portfolios of candidate policies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the proposed metric, particularly in terms of its sensitivity to different hyperparameters and its performance across various problem instances.
- The paper's motivation and the rationale behind using SharpeRatio@k are not clearly articulated, especially in the introduction and early sections of the paper.
- The paper's claims regarding the limitations of existing metrics and the effectiveness of SharpeRatio@k are not convincingly supported by empirical evidence or theoretical arguments.
- The paper's experimental setup and the choice of environments are somewhat limited, which might not fully demonstrate the generalizability or applicability of the proposed metric.
- The paper's discussion on the relationship between SharpeRatio@k and the performance of the behavior policy is not clear, which could lead to confusion about the metric's utility in practical scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the proposed metric, particularly in terms of its sensitivity to different hyperparameters and its performance across various problem instances?
- How does the proposed metric compare to existing metrics in terms of its ability to distinguish between conservative and high-stakes OPE estimators?
- Can the authors clarify the rationale behind using SharpeRatio@k and how it addresses the limitations of existing metrics in the context of OPE?
- How does the proposed metric perform in more complex or realistic scenarios, such as those involving continuous action spaces or more varied policy selections?
- Can the authors provide more detailed empirical evidence or theoretical arguments to support their claims about the limitations of existing metrics and the effectiveness of SharpeRatio@k?
- How does the proposed metric relate to the performance of the behavior policy, and how should this relationship be interpreted in practical scenarios?
