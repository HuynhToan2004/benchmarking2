 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new metric framework, Calibrated Loss, aimed at reducing variance in neural network evaluation metrics, which is crucial for accurate comparisons between different deep learning pipelines. The authors argue that traditional methods, such as running the training process multiple times, are not practical due to computational limitations. The proposed framework, supported by theoretical justifications and extensive experimental validations across various datasets, aims to enhance the reliability of performance evaluations in deep learning. However, concerns are raised about the theoretical soundness, practical applicability, and the novelty of the proposed method compared to existing variance reduction techniques.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of deep learning by proposing a new metric framework, Calibrated Loss, which aims to reduce variance in neural network evaluation metrics.
- The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of the problem, the proposed solution, and the theoretical justifications, making the content accessible and understandable.
- Extensive experimental validations are conducted across various datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed metric in enhancing the reliability of performance evaluations in deep learning.
- The paper provides a theoretical guarantee under a particular setting of linear regression, showing that the proposed metric has a smaller variance than its vanilla counterpart.
- The authors have conducted experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing the effectiveness of the proposed metric.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical soundness is questionable, particularly concerning the proof of Theorem 4.1 and the assumptions made regarding the distribution of features and labels.
- The practical applicability of the proposed method is limited, as it requires a test-validation dataset and a remaining test dataset, which might not be feasible in all scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing variance reduction techniques, which could have provided a clearer picture of the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical feasibility.
- The paper's contribution is not clearly defined, particularly in terms of its novelty and the extent to which it advances the state of the art.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability in scenarios where the test-validation dataset and a remaining test dataset are not available.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in Theorem 4.1 regarding the distribution of features and labels?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing variance reduction techniques, particularly in terms of computational cost and effectiveness?
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of neural networks, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and transformers?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these limitations affect its practical applicability?
