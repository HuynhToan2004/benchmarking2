 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for blind face restoration from low-quality images, leveraging a dual-branch framework named DAEFR. This framework incorporates an auxiliary LQ branch specifically trained on LQ data to capture domain-specific information, which is then aligned with HQ features using a cross-attention module. The method aims to address the domain gap between LQ and HQ images, thereby improving restoration performance. The proposed method is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in restoring facial details. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its reliance on existing techniques and the lack of detailed experimental validation and comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel dual-branch framework, DAEFR, which effectively addresses the domain gap between low-quality (LQ) and high-quality (HQ) images, a significant challenge in blind face restoration.
- The methodology is well-structured, with clear explanations of the method's components and their contributions to the restoration process.
- The paper provides extensive evaluations, including comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of a dual-branch framework, where one branch is dedicated to LQ data, is a novel approach that enhances the restoration process by leveraging domain-specific information from LQ inputs.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for readers to understand the proposed methodology and its implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from existing methods, such as CLIP, which may lead to questions about the novelty of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of detailed experimental validation, particularly in terms of quantitative metrics and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the credibility of the results.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other methods, including those that use similar approaches like CLIP or other multi-head attention mechanisms.
- The methodology, while innovative, is somewhat limited by the use of pre-trained encoders and decoders, which may not fully address the domain gap between LQ and HQ images.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities and potential improvements.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method distinguishes from existing methods, particularly in terms of its novel contributions?
- How does the paper address the domain gap between LQ and HQ images, especially in terms of the feature alignment and fusion processes?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and a broader range of quantitative metrics?
- How does the paper ensure that the auxiliary LQ branch effectively captures domain-specific information from LQ inputs?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and suggest areas for future research or improvements?
- What are the computational requirements of the proposed method, and how do they compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of efficiency and scalability?
