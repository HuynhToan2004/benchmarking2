 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel decentralized multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) algorithm named Anticipation Sharing (AS), which addresses the challenge of coordination in environments where agents have individual rewards and no global perspective. AS enables agents to learn without sharing sensitive information such as rewards or model parameters, instead relying on anticipation sharing. This method allows agents to learn from each other's anticipated actions, facilitating decentralized cooperative learning. The paper includes theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of AS in various tasks. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the methodology, the absence of a detailed algorithm, and the lack of comparison with recent related works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear motivation and a clear presentation of the proposed method.
- The problem of decentralized MARL with individual rewards is well-motivated and relevant, with the proposed method showing promise in addressing this issue.
- The theoretical analysis is thorough, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.
- The paper includes empirical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The approach is innovative, focusing on anticipation sharing which is a novel concept in MARL.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed algorithm for the proposed method, which makes it difficult to fully understand the implementation.
- The comparison with related works is insufficient, particularly with recent studies that address similar issues in decentralized MARL.
- The empirical results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the choice of baselines seems outdated.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability in different scenarios and its scalability.
- The method's novelty is questioned due to similarities with existing methods like value sharing and teammate modeling.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions and limitations of the theoretical results.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition of $A_i^{\pi'}(s,a)$ and its relationship with the potential joint policy $\pi'$?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the anticipation sharing might not be effective, such as in environments with heterogeneous agents or in scenarios with complex social dilemmas?
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the number of agents and the size of the environment?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent decentralized MARL methods that do not require sharing rewards or values?
- Could you elaborate on the assumptions made in the theoretical results and discuss how these assumptions might affect the applicability and robustness of the proposed method?
