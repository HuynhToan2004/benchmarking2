 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel in-context learning strategy, termed SuperContext, which integrates the outputs of small, supervised language models (SLMs) into the prompt of a large language model (LLM). This approach leverages the discriminative knowledge of SLMs to enhance the performance of LLMs, particularly in out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios. The methodology involves the insertion of SLM outputs between the question and answer in a QA task, and between the question and the predicted label in a classification task. The paper provides empirical evidence of SuperContext's effectiveness by comparing it to traditional in-context learning and other baselines. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the experimental setup, the novelty of the approach, and the potential for data leakage.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel in-context learning strategy, SuperContext, which integrates the outputs of small, supervised language models into the prompt of a large language model, enhancing its performance in out-of-distribution scenarios.
- The approach is straightforward, general, and applicable to various tasks, with potential to improve the performance of large language models.
- The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in both zero-shot and few-shot settings, and in both natural language understanding and question-answering tasks.
- The results show that SuperContext can outperform traditional in-context learning methods, suggesting that the integration of supervised knowledge from small models can significantly enhance the performance of large models.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, and includes detailed analyses of the performance of the model across different datasets and tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods like HuggingGPT, which also uses small models to enhance the performance of large models.
- The methodology section could be clearer, especially in explaining the roles and interactions of different components such as r_i and s_i.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other methods that use small models to enhance large models, which could help establish the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper's reliance on the outputs of small models raises concerns about data leakage, especially since the models are fine-tuned on the same datasets as the large models.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the findings, as the experiments are limited to specific datasets and tasks.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the areas of in-context learning and knowledge integration in large language models.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use small models to enhance large models, such as HuggingGPT?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the methodology, particularly the roles and interactions of different components like r_i and s_i?
- How does the performance of the model vary when using different small models for different tasks, and what are the implications of this for the generalizability of the findings?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the potential for data leakage and the selection of datasets for in-context learning examples?
- How does the model handle the integration of supervised knowledge from small models, and what are the implications of this for the overall performance and generalizability of the findings?
