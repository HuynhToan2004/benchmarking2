 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the goal representations and decision-making processes in a pretrained maze-solving policy network. It identifies eleven channels within the network that track the location of the cheese, showing that these channels are redundant and distributed. The authors demonstrate that by modifying these channels, they can control the policy's behavior without retraining, showing that the policy can be steered towards different objectives. The study also highlights the context-dependent nature of the policy's goals and the redundancy in the representation of these goals across the network. Despite the novel findings, the paper has been criticized for its limited scope, lack of generalizability, and insufficient comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- It introduces a novel approach to understanding and controlling the behavior of a maze-solving policy network, specifically by identifying eleven channels that track the location of the cheese.
- The paper presents a compelling case for the existence of redundant and distributed goal representations in the network, which are not only identifiable but also controllable through activation engineering.
- The findings have implications for the broader field of AI safety, as understanding and controlling the goals of AI systems is crucial for ensuring their safe and beneficial deployment.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the network's behavior, including how it responds to different inputs and how this behavior can be influenced by modifying specific activations within the network.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to a single environment (maze-solving), which may limit the generalizability of the findings to other, more complex tasks.
- The paper's reliance on visual inspection and hand-designed interventions may not be sufficient for robust validation of the findings, as it lacks a broader range of experimental validations.
- The paper's contribution is seen as somewhat limited as the findings are based on a single policy network trained on a specific task, which may not be representative of more complex or varied scenarios.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, which could help contextualize the paper's findings and highlight their novelty or superiority.
- The paper's focus on a specific policy network and task raises questions about the scalability and applicability of the findings to other policy networks or tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role of the 11 channels in the network and how they are specifically related to the cheese-tracking behavior observed?
- How do the findings of this study apply to other policy networks, and what implications might this have for the broader field of AI safety and interpretability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the decision-making process of the policy network, particularly how it balances the pursuit of different goals and how this balance might be influenced by different inputs or settings?
- How does the paper's approach to controlling the policy network through activation engineering compare to other methods, and what are the advantages or disadvantages of this approach compared to others?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential societal impacts of their findings, particularly in terms of how understanding and controlling the goals of AI systems could be used to ensure their safe and beneficial deployment?
