 **Summary:**
The paper explores the relationship between graph convolution and mixup, proposing that graph convolution can be viewed as a specialized form of mixup under certain conditions. Specifically, it demonstrates that graph convolution can be expressed as a mixup of node features and labels during training and testing, with the target node's label assigned to all its neighbors. The authors also propose two methods, HMLP and TMLP, which mimic the behavior of GCNs by replicating the homophily relabel and test-time mixup strategies. These methods are tested on several benchmark datasets, showing that they can match or surpass the performance of GCNs in some cases. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of novelty, as the proposed methods are seen as straightforward applications of known techniques, and for its limited experimental evaluation and theoretical analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their connections to graph convolution and mixup.
- The paper introduces two methods, HMLP and TMLP, which can be used to mimic the behavior of GCNs, and shows that these methods can achieve comparable performance to GCNs in certain scenarios.
- The paper provides a novel perspective on understanding graph convolution through mixup, which is both interesting and potentially impactful for the field of graph neural networks.
- The experimental results, although not extensive, are promising and suggest that the proposed methods can be effective in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough theoretical analysis and comparison with existing works, particularly those that have explored similar ideas in the context of GNNs and mixup.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, focusing only on a few datasets and not comparing the proposed methods against a broader range of baselines or state-of-the-art methods.
- The novelty of the paper is questionable, as the proposed methods are seen as straightforward applications of known techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper's claims about the equivalence of graph convolution and mixup are not convincingly supported, and the experimental results do not consistently outperform GCNs.
- The paper's presentation could be improved to better highlight the contributions and to clarify the methodology and results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed methods, particularly HMLP and TMLP, differ from existing works that have explored similar ideas?
- How do the authors justify the choice of datasets and the experimental setup, especially considering the limited scope of the evaluation?
- Can the authors provide more detailed theoretical insights into the equivalence of graph convolution and mixup, and discuss the implications of this equivalence for the design and analysis of GNNs?
- Given the limitations of the experimental evaluation, how do the authors plan to extend their research to include a more comprehensive set of benchmarks and comparisons?
- Can the authors elaborate on the practical implications of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially in large-scale graph applications?
