 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel computational framework for inferring synaptic plasticity rules from neural activity and behavioral data using gradient-based optimization. The method employs Taylor series expansions and MLPs to approximate plasticity rules, adjusting parameters through gradient descent to match observed neural and behavioral data. The framework is validated against established rules and applied to real-world data from Drosophila, revealing an active forgetting mechanism in mushroom body circuits for decision making, learning, and memory. The approach, which can learn complex rules with nonlinear time dependencies, is shown to be robust to noise and sparsity in neural recordings. However, the method's generalizability to other types of neural data and its assumptions about the form of the plasticity rule are noted as limitations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and the methodology is clearly explained, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The application of the model to real-world data from Drosophila is a significant contribution, providing insights into the computational principles governing synaptic plasticity and learning in the brain.
- The methodology is versatile, capable of inferring synaptic plasticity rules from experimental data, and can be adapted to fit any parameterized plasticity rule.
- The paper includes an open-source, extensible implementation, which enhances its utility and reproducibility.
- The framework is validated through simulations, accurately recovering established rules, and demonstrates its ability to identify an active forgetting component of reward learning in flies, enhancing the predictive accuracy of previous models.
- The methodology is general, capable of inferring synaptic plasticity rules from experimental data, and can be adapted to fit any parameterized plasticity rule.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on rate-based modeling may limit its applicability to spiking data, which could be a significant limitation given the prevalence of spiking data in neuroscience research.
- The method's generalizability to other types of neural data and its assumptions about the form of the plasticity rule are not thoroughly explored, which could limit its utility in broader applications.
- The paper's methodology is similar to previous work, and the novelty of the approach is not clearly articulated.
- The paper does not adequately address the challenges posed by unknown initial synaptic weights or the necessity of exact connectome information, which could limit the practical applicability of the method.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the method's performance across different types of neural data and different learning scenarios, which could affect the robustness and generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific assumptions made about the form of the plasticity rule and discuss how these assumptions might limit the applicability of the method?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of neural data, such as spiking data, and what are the implications of this for the method's generalizability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the method's robustness to noise and sparsity in neural recordings, particularly in real-world applications?
- What are the implications of the method's reliance on gradient-based optimization for its scalability and applicability to large-scale neural recordings and connectomics?
- How does the method handle the potential challenges posed by unknown initial synaptic weights, and what strategies could be employed to address this limitation?
- Could the authors discuss the potential implications of the method's assumptions about the form of the plasticity rule on the inferred rules, particularly in terms of their biological plausibility and generalizability?
