 **Summary:**
The paper explores the problem of eliciting confidence scores from large language models (LLMs) that do not provide probability outputs. It introduces two methods: linguistic confidences, which are generated by prompting the LLM to express its confidence, and surrogate model probabilities, which are derived from a separate model that predicts the confidence of the LLM's output. The paper evaluates these methods across various datasets and models, showing that surrogate model probabilities can sometimes outperform linguistic confidences. The authors propose a mixture of these two methods, which combines the strengths of both approaches. The paper also discusses the limitations of linguistic confidences, particularly their coarseness, and how surrogate models can improve confidence estimation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem of eliciting confidence scores from LLMs, which is crucial for ensuring trust in AI systems.
- The study is comprehensive, involving 12 datasets and 5 models, which provides a robust evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the results, including a comparison of surrogate model probabilities with linguistic confidences and an examination of the calibration of linguistic confidences.
- The paper introduces a novel approach by using surrogate models to estimate the confidence of LLMs, which is a creative solution to the lack of probability outputs in some models.
- The experiments are extensive, covering a wide range of models and datasets, which strengthens the validity of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on question-answering tasks, which might limit the generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks.
- The linguistic confidences are shown to be less reliable than model probabilities, which raises questions about their practical utility.
- The paper does not adequately address how the surrogate model probabilities are calculated or how they are used to estimate the confidence of the LLM's output.
- The methodology of using surrogate models to estimate confidence is not sufficiently explained, particularly the rationale behind using a different model for confidence estimation.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, such as the potential for overfitting or the generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, which could benefit from additional examples or clarification to enhance understanding.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the surrogate model probabilities are calculated and how they are used to estimate the confidence of the LLM's output?
- How do the authors ensure that the surrogate model's predictions are not biased towards the training data, especially in cases where the model might be overfitting?
- Is there a possibility to adapt the surrogate model to generate confidence scores for a specific LLM, rather than relying on a separate model?
- How do the authors address the potential for overfitting in the surrogate model, especially when it is used to estimate the confidence of an LLM that it has not been trained on?
- Could you clarify the discrepancy in the confidence scores reported for different models, particularly between GPT-4 and Claude, which have similar accuracy but different confidence scores?
- How do the authors ensure that the linguistic confidences are not biased by the training data, and what steps are taken to mitigate this potential issue?
