 **Summary:**
The paper investigates link prediction in graphs, focusing on three critical factors: local structural proximity, global structural proximity, and feature proximity. The authors explore the relationships among these factors, demonstrating that global structural proximity is effective when local structural proximity is insufficient, and feature proximity can be incompatible with structural proximity. The study proposes a novel decoupled SEAL model to address these incompatibilities, aiming to enhance link prediction performance. The paper provides theoretical and empirical evidence to support its claims, including a comprehensive analysis of various datasets and heuristic algorithms. Despite its contributions, the paper faces criticism for its assumptions, lack of novelty, and the need for more robust experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear structure that facilitates understanding.
- It addresses a fundamental problem in link prediction, providing a comprehensive analysis of the relationships among three factors (local structural proximity, global structural proximity, and feature proximity).
- The authors propose a new decoupled SEAL model to address the incompatibility between feature proximity and local structural proximity, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper includes a thorough theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support the claims, with a focus on the importance of data-centric perspectives in link prediction.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the relationships among the factors and propose a new model that could potentially improve link prediction performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that feature proximity is an additional noise parameter rather than adaptively combining that information in the same subspace, which may not be a realistic assumption in real-world scenarios.
- The proposed decoupled SEAL model lacks detailed experimental validation, particularly in terms of its performance on other datasets beyond those used in the study.
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as the proposed decoupled SEAL model is an extension of existing methods like SEAL, and the theoretical analysis does not provide significant new insights.
- The paper does not adequately address the fairness issues in the selection of datasets for benchmarking, which could lead to biased conclusions.
- The paper's scope is limited to undirected graphs, and the proposed model may not be directly applicable to directed graphs, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The theoretical analysis and empirical evidence do not convincingly support the claims about the incompatibility between feature proximity and local structural proximity, and the paper lacks a broader experimental validation across different datasets and models.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental validation of the decoupled SEAL model on other datasets to support its effectiveness?
- How does the proposed decoupled SEAL model handle the issue of fairness in dataset selection for benchmarking?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly regarding the adaptive combination of feature proximity and local structural proximity?
- How does the proposed decoupled SEAL model perform when applied to directed graphs, and what modifications are necessary for its application in such scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more intuitive examples or real-world applications to illustrate the incompatibility between feature proximity and local structural proximity, as discussed in Section 3.4?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of their theoretical analysis, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions may not hold true?
