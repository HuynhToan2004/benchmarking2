 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel probabilistic framework for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection, specifically focusing on the use of natural OOD signals from the background of in-distribution (ID) images. This framework, termed SSOD, is designed to optimize the detection of OOD data by leveraging the local properties of convolution. The methodology is evaluated across various datasets, including ImageNet, and shows promising results, outperforming existing methods in some scenarios. However, concerns about the novelty of the approach and the clarity of the method's theoretical underpinnings are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel probabilistic framework for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection, which provides a comprehensive overview of existing OOD methods and identifies their main differences and key limitations.
- The proposed method, SSOD, is simple yet effective, with impressive experimental results that outperform the state-of-the-art (SOTA) in many scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive evaluation on various benchmarks and model architectures.
- The method's ability to detect OOD data using natural OOD signals from the background of ID images is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited, as it primarily uses existing techniques such as DS evidence theory and the local property of convolution, without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a detailed theoretical analysis on why the proposed method works effectively, which could enhance the understanding of its effectiveness.
- The method's reliance on the background information of ID images for OOD detection may not be effective in scenarios where the background information is similar to the OOD data.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with recent OOD detection methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of its performance.
- There are inconsistencies in the results reported, such as the performance on certain datasets like iSUN, which raises concerns about the method's robustness.
- The paper's reliance on a specific model (ResNet) and a specific dataset (ImageNet) for training could limit the generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the method handles scenarios where the background information is similar to the OOD data?
- Could the authors provide more details on the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method, particularly why it is effective in detecting OOD data?
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent OOD detection methods, especially those that use different models or datasets?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the inconsistencies observed in the results across different datasets?
- How does the proposed method perform when the OOD data is significantly different from the ID data, and what are the implications of this on the method's effectiveness?
