 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the relationship between disentanglement and compositional generalization in deep learning models, focusing on the use of vector-valued representations. It argues that vector-valued representations, which encode multiple aspects of data into a single vector, can enhance both disentanglement and compositional generalization capabilities. The authors demonstrate this through experiments on the Shapes3D dataset, showing that vector-valued representations can achieve better disentanglement and compositional generalization compared to scalar-valued representations. The paper also discusses the impact of vector size and regularization strength on these abilities. However, concerns were raised about the generalizability of the findings to more complex datasets and the potential for overfitting due to the increased dimensionality of the latent space.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic in deep learning research, focusing on the relationship between disentanglement and compositional generalization.
- The writing is clear and easy to follow, with a comprehensive review of related work and a thorough experimental setup.
- The experiments conducted are well-designed, with clear results and interpretations, and the findings are supported by a variety of metrics and visualizations.
- The paper includes a novel approach to vectorizing representations, which could be beneficial for future research in this area.
- The authors demonstrate that vector-valued representations can achieve better disentanglement and compositional generalization than scalar-valued representations, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claims about the superiority of vector-valued representations over scalar-valued representations are not convincingly supported by the experiments. The results on the Shapes3D dataset do not generalize well to other, more complex datasets like MPI3D-Real.
- The experiments are limited to simple datasets and do not include more complex, real-world datasets, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed methods.
- There is a lack of theoretical justification for the observed phenomena, which could strengthen the paper's arguments.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and a more detailed analysis of the results, particularly the discrepancies between different metrics and datasets.
- The paper's claims about the relationship between disentanglement and compositional generalization are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which could undermine the paper's main arguments.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies between the results on the Shapes3D dataset and those on the MPI3D-Real dataset, particularly the lack of generalizability of the findings to more complex datasets?
- How do the authors justify the use of a single metric (R2) to evaluate compositional generalization, especially given the potential biases and limitations of this metric?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations for the observed phenomena, such as the positive correlation between disentanglement and compositional generalization, and the negative correlation between disentanglement and classification accuracy?
- How do the authors address the potential for overfitting due to the increased dimensionality of the latent space in their models?
- Can the authors provide more theoretical insights into why vector-valued representations might be more effective for disentanglement and compositional generalization than scalar-valued representations?
