 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the application of Borda count in preference learning, particularly focusing on the effects of hidden contexts that are not directly observable in the data. It argues that the Borda count method, which aggregates preferences across different contexts, can lead to biased results, especially when the contexts are not well understood. The paper introduces a distributional preference learning (DPL) framework to address these issues by estimating the distribution of utility values for each alternative, thereby providing a more nuanced understanding of preference dynamics. The DPL method is validated through experiments on real-world datasets, showing potential in mitigating the effects of hidden contexts on preference learning outcomes.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant issue in preference learning, particularly the handling of hidden contexts which can bias the results of standard methods like Borda count.
- The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the problem and its implications, supported by theoretical analysis and empirical evidence.
- The introduction of distributional preference learning (DPL) is a novel and promising approach for detecting hidden contexts and mitigating their effects on preference learning outcomes.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear structure and good presentation of the content, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The experiments, including both synthetic and real-world data, are well-designed and provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly in Theorem 3.2, to clarify their practical applicability.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other existing methods for handling hidden contexts in preference learning, which could strengthen the argument for the superiority of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of their scalability and computational complexity, which could be a concern for large-scale applications.
- The experimental results, while convincing, are somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on specific datasets and not generalizing well to other settings or datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the findings for real-world applications, particularly in the context of large language models (LLMs) and the ethical considerations involved.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the assumptions made in Theorem 3.2 and discuss their practical applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed DPL method compare with other existing methods for handling hidden contexts in preference learning?
- In the experimental setup, how were the parameters of the DPL method chosen, and what were the considerations behind these choices?
- How does the DPL method perform when applied to different datasets or in different settings, and what are the implications of these variations on the results?
- Could the authors discuss the potential ethical implications of using the DPL method in real-world applications, particularly in the context of LLMs and the potential for bias or unfairness in preference learning outcomes?
