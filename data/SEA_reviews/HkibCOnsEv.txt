 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new optimization method for training large neural networks using the KFAC approach, which is inverse-free and memory-efficient. This method, termed SINGD, is a structured inverse-free natural gradient descent that extends the INGD method to incorporate sparse Kronecker factors. The authors demonstrate that SINGD is competitive with other optimizers like AdamW and KFAC, showing its effectiveness in low-precision training environments. The paper also discusses the theoretical foundations and practical implications of the proposed method, including its application to various neural network architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear figures that aid in understanding the content.
- The authors effectively bridge the gap between INGD and KFAC, providing a clear explanation of how the proposed method extends and improves upon existing techniques.
- The paper is well-motivated, with a clear focus on addressing the challenges of second-order methods in deep learning, particularly in low-precision training environments.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method is competitive with other optimizers like AdamW, showing that it can be as effective as state-of-the-art methods while being memory-efficient.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with other optimizers, including KFAC, and shows that SINGD can achieve similar or better performance with less memory usage.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the choice of the sparsity parameter k, which is critical for the effectiveness of the proposed method. More experimental results and analysis would be beneficial to understand the impact of different sparsity levels.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the paper, particularly in the preliminaries section, which could be improved by moving some content to the appendix to make the main text more accessible.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for numerical instability or the need for careful tuning of hyperparameters.
- The experimental results are somewhat limited, focusing primarily on small-scale datasets and not including larger, more complex models or datasets, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the existing INGD method, which could have been used as a baseline for comparison.

**Questions:**
- How does the choice of the sparsity parameter k affect the performance of the proposed method? Are there any experimental results or theoretical insights that could guide the selection of this parameter?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific values of hyperparameters used for each optimizer and the training configurations for different models?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational cost and efficiency with other optimizers, particularly in large-scale training scenarios?
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between the INGD method and the KFAC method, especially in terms of the updates and the role of the Kronecker factors?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for numerical instability, particularly in low-precision training environments?
- Are there any plans to extend the experimental validation to include larger, more complex models or datasets, such as those used in large-scale image classification or language modeling tasks?
