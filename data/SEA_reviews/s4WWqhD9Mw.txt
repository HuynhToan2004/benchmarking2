 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Holmex, a method designed to address spurious correlations in black-box models by integrating human expertise. Holmex employs a two-step process: first, it detects spurious correlations using a novel algorithm that constructs concept vectors from pre-trained vision-language models. These vectors are then used to identify correlations between concepts and labels, which are evaluated by humans. In the second step, Holmex proposes a transfer editing technique to fix biased black-box models by adjusting interpretable models. This method is tested on various real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in detecting and correcting spurious correlations. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods like DISC and PCBM.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with comprehensive experiments that support the proposed method.
- The use of human expertise to guide the process of spurious correlation detection and model fixing is a novel and practical approach.
- The methodology is straightforward and effective, with a clear motivation for the use of human expertise in model debugging.
- The paper provides a novel technique, transfer editing, which is compatible with arbitrary black-box models and can transfer the revision in interpretable models to black-box models.
- The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method is effective in detecting and fixing spurious correlations, and the results show that it outperforms existing methods in certain scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the scalability of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques like DISC and PCBM, with limited new insights or theoretical contributions.
- The method's effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated across a wide range of datasets or real-world scenarios, and the paper does not discuss potential negative societal impacts.
- The method's reliance on human expertise for detecting spurious correlations and fixing biased models may limit its applicability in large-scale or automated settings.
- The paper does not adequately address how the method handles scenarios where spurious correlations are not immediately apparent or where human expertise may not be available or reliable.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the method performs when applied to different types of datasets or real-world scenarios, and how it scales with the size of the dataset or the complexity of the model?
- How does the method handle scenarios where spurious correlations are not immediately apparent, and what steps are taken to ensure that the detected correlations are indeed spurious rather than genuine?
- What are the computational requirements of the method, and how does it compare in terms of efficiency with other existing methods?
- Can you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the method, particularly in terms of fairness and bias in machine learning models?
- How does the method ensure the reliability and accuracy of the human expertise used in the spurious correlation detection and model fixing processes?
