 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an interleaving multi-task neural architecture search (MTNAS) framework aimed at optimizing loss weights across multiple tasks. It proposes a novel method where tasks are trained in an interleaving manner, allowing for the transfer of knowledge between tasks, thus avoiding the need for fine-tuning loss weights. This method is evaluated on datasets like CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet, showing promising results. The approach leverages a distribution matching method to enhance knowledge transfer between tasks, and the methodology is supported by extensive experiments. However, the paper's presentation is criticized for being confusing, and the method's applicability to non-differentiable NAS methods is limited.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to multi-task neural architecture search by interleaving the training of tasks, which is an interesting and innovative method.
- The proposed method is well-supported by extensive experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness in various scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly explained.
- The use of distribution matching for knowledge transfer is an effective and interesting method, which is well-explained and compared against other methods.
- The method is shown to be effective in reducing the need for fine-tuning loss weights, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, such as those mentioned in related works, which could provide a clearer picture of the method's advantages.
- The presentation of the paper is confusing, with some sections and figures not being clearly explained or labeled, which could hinder understanding.
- The applicability of the method is limited to differentiable NAS methods, which may not be suitable for all NAS scenarios.
- The method's effectiveness in scenarios with a large number of tasks or complex datasets is not thoroughly explored, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the interleaving mechanism, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, including those mentioned in related works, to better establish the advantages of your method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with a large number of tasks or more complex datasets, such as those used in large-scale NAS studies?
- Can you clarify the computational cost of the interleaving mechanism and how it compares to other methods in terms of efficiency?
- In the context of the experiments, could you explain the choice of datasets and the specific conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the method handle the transfer of knowledge between tasks when the tasks are not closely related or when the data distributions vary significantly?
