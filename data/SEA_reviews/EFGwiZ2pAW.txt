 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SimTeG, a method for learning node representations in textual graphs using a pre-trained language model (LM) and a Graph Neural Network (GNN). SimTeG involves a two-stage process: first, the LM is fine-tuned on the textual graph, and then the GNN is trained on the embeddings generated by the LM. The methodology aims to improve the performance of GNNs by leveraging advanced text features extracted by the LM. The paper presents extensive experiments across various benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of SimTeG in enhancing GNN performance, particularly in tasks like node classification and link prediction. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the approach, as similar methods have been explored in prior works, and the empirical validation was questioned due to the lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow and understand.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, SimTeG.
- The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies, which help in understanding the impact of different components of the method.
- The methodology is straightforward and could be applied to various GNN models, making it adaptable and accessible.
- The paper provides a clear motivation for the study and includes a detailed literature review, which is helpful in understanding the context of the research.
- The paper includes several interesting observations and insights, which could be valuable for further research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as similar methods using LM-based node representation learning have been explored in prior works.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's effectiveness and positioning.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method, which is a significant concern for larger and more complex datasets.
- The paper's contribution to the field is not clearly articulated, particularly in terms of its novelty and how it advances the state of the art.
- The paper's reliance on a two-stage approach, where the LM is fine-tuned on the textual graph and then the GNN is trained on the embeddings generated by the LM, may not be the most efficient or effective approach.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the proposed method differs from similar methods discussed in prior works, particularly in terms of its technical contributions?
- How does the proposed method compare with state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of its applicability to larger and more complex datasets?
- How does the method handle the issue of overfitting, particularly in the context of the fine-tuning of the LM on the downstream task?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the method and provide insights into potential future directions for research in this area?
