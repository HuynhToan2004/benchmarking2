 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to improving Linear Mode Connectivity (LMC) in neural networks by aligning neurons during training through a process called training-time neuron alignment. This method involves masking some neurons and training multiple models, which are then aggregated to achieve better linear interpolation of weights. The approach is tested on both vanilla training and federated learning settings, showing improvements in LMC and model fusion. The paper also explores the application of this method in federated learning, where it can potentially enhance model generalization. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited theoretical justification, unclear presentation, and insufficient empirical evidence, particularly in larger-scale datasets and different network architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the interesting problem of linear mode connectivity (LMC) and proposes a novel method for training-time neuron alignment, which is a new perspective on improving LMC.
- The methodology is well-articulated, and the paper is generally well-written, with clear figures that aid in understanding the proposed method.
- The paper conducts extensive experiments on various tasks and datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR, and a subset of ImageNet, which showcase the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving LMC and model fusion.
- The introduction of training-time neuron alignment is a significant contribution to the field, providing a new approach to addressing the LMC problem.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure that makes it easy to follow, and the related work section is comprehensive.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed theoretical explanation for why the proposed method works, particularly in relation to the hypothesis presented in Theorem 3.2.
- The experimental results are limited to small-scale datasets and simple network architectures, which may not generalize well to larger, more complex models or datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different network architectures and the potential impact of varying initialization methods.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications for the experimental settings and results, particularly in the federated learning experiments.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of permutation invariance, which is a critical aspect of LMC, and the proposed method's effectiveness in this regard is not clearly demonstrated.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in the clarity of the figures and the organization of the content.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the initialization method used in the experiments and how it affects the results?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different network architectures, such as Transformers, and on larger datasets like ImageNet?
- Could the authors clarify the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method, particularly in relation to Theorem 3.2 and the hypothesis it supports?
- How does the proposed method compare to other post-matching methods in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness?
- Can the authors elaborate on the experimental settings, particularly in the federated learning experiments, to help clarify the results and their implications?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of permutation invariance, and what are the implications of this approach for the broader understanding of LMC?
