 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a self-training framework named ST-SGG for scene graph generation (SGG), aimed at addressing the challenges of long-tailed predicate distributions and missing annotations in benchmark datasets. It proposes a model-agnostic pseudo-labeling technique, CATM, which adaptively adjusts the threshold for pseudo-labeling based on the learning state of each predicate class. The framework is designed to be applied to any existing SGG models and is supported by extensive experiments on datasets like Visual Genome and Open Images V6, demonstrating its effectiveness in enhancing performance on fine-grained predicate classes. However, concerns about the novelty and clarity of the method's presentation were raised, with suggestions for a more detailed comparison with existing methods and clearer explanations of the proposed techniques.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant problem of long-tailed predicate distributions in scene graph generation (SGG), which is a critical issue in the field.
- The proposed model-agnostic framework, CATM, is designed to be applied to any existing SGG models, making it versatile and adaptable.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive experimental setup that includes comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.
- The authors have conducted a thorough ablation study to validate the effectiveness of their proposed techniques, which provides a robust foundation for their claims.
- The paper introduces a novel pseudo-labeling technique, Class-specific Adaptive Thresholding with Momentum (CATM), which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments conducted on two datasets, Visual Genome and Open Images V6, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework in enhancing the performance on fine-grained predicate classes.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the effectiveness of the proposed CATM thresholding technique compared to other methods like Motif-taucon and Motif-taucl.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the proposed techniques, particularly in the explanation of the CATM method and the graph structure learner (GSL).
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The novelty of the proposed framework is questioned due to its similarity to existing self-training methods, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how it differs from these methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the models used and the training parameters, which could affect the reproducibility and validation of the results.
- There are several grammatical errors and inconsistencies in the paper that need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the CATM thresholding technique with other methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness?
- How does the proposed CATM technique compare to other methods in terms of handling the long-tailed predicate distribution issue in SGG?
- Could the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the graph structure learner (GSL) in the context of the proposed framework?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed framework over existing methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and effectiveness?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the performance metrics and comparisons with other methods on different datasets?
- How does the proposed framework address the issues of semantic ambiguity and the long-tailed distribution of predicate classes in SGG?
