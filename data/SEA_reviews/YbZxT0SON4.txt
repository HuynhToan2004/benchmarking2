 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework named SOFE (Stationary Objectives For Exploration) aimed at addressing the non-stationarity in intrinsic rewards, a significant challenge in reinforcement learning. By augmenting state representations with sufficient statistics, SOFE transforms non-stationary rewards into stationary rewards, simplifying optimization and improving exploration performance. The framework is demonstrated to be effective across various environments and tasks, showing improvements in exploration efficiency and performance in both reward-free and sparse reward settings. However, the paper's novelty and the depth of its contributions are questioned due to its similarity to existing methods and its limited scope in terms of the number of environments and tasks tested.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear motivation for the problem of non-stationarity in exploration rewards.
- The proposed framework is simple yet effective, with empirical results showing improvements over existing methods.
- The paper is well-motivated, with a clear problem statement and a well-designed framework that is straightforward and easy to implement.
- The experiments are well-designed, with a good range of environments and tasks, including both reward-free and sparse reward settings.
- The paper provides a comprehensive literature review, positioning the proposed framework within the existing body of work.
- The proposed framework is simple yet effective, with empirical results showing improvements over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed framework is limited as it shares similarities with existing methods such as E3B, particularly in the use of augmented state representations.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The experiments are limited in scope, focusing primarily on navigation tasks and not exploring a wider range of tasks or environments, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the framework to more complex tasks or environments, raising concerns about its generalizability.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the methodology, particularly in the description of the SOFE algorithm and the role of the auxiliary model in computing the intrinsic reward.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the experimental results and the methodology used, especially in the supplementary materials.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the auxiliary model in computing the intrinsic reward, particularly in the context of the SOFE framework?
- How does the proposed framework perform in more complex tasks or environments beyond those tested in the experiments?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the experimental results, including the methodology used and the role of the auxiliary model in these experiments?
- What are the limitations of the proposed framework, and under what conditions might it not be effective?
- How does the proposed framework compare to other existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the ability to handle more complex tasks?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of environments and tasks for the experiments, and consider expanding the scope to include a more diverse set of tasks?
