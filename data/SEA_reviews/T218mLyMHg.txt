 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph fusion framework named SMGF, designed to handle multi-view graphs with both graph and attribute views. The framework aggregates view-specific graph structures to approximate underlying entity connections and optimizes view weights using spectral properties of the Laplacian matrix. It employs two objectives, eigengap and connectivity, to determine view weights and construct a multi-view Laplacian for unsupervised learning. The proposed method is evaluated on six real-world datasets, showing superior performance in node embedding and clustering results. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods, and the methodology's complexity and computational efficiency are not thoroughly discussed.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and effective, with a novel approach to unsupervised learning on multi-view graphs.
- The method is evaluated on six real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness across different scenarios.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear introduction, methodology, and evaluation, which makes it easy to understand and replicate.
- The proposed method outperforms several baselines in terms of clustering and embedding quality, indicating its practical utility.
- The use of two objectives to determine view weights and the optimization scheme for these weights are innovative and contribute to the interpretability of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable as it bears a strong resemblance to existing methods, particularly in the use of the eigenvalue gap and connectivity objectives.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the complexity and computational efficiency of the method, which could be a significant drawback for its practical implementation.
- The method's scalability and performance on large-scale datasets are not thoroughly discussed, which is crucial for assessing its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis to support the effectiveness of the proposed method, which could strengthen the paper's claims.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, which could undermine its claims of superior performance.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of hyperparameter tuning, which is critical for the method's performance.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability with other state-of-the-art methods, especially on large-scale datasets?
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the method's scalability and its performance on datasets with a large number of nodes?
- How does the proposed method handle noisy data in graph views, and what are the potential implications for its performance?
- Could the authors provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, including the time complexity of each step in the algorithm?
- How sensitive is the proposed method to the choice of hyperparameters, and what are the potential implications for its performance in practical scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the hardware used and the specific hyperparameters tuned for each baseline method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of interpretability and explainability with other methods, especially in scenarios where the results need to be justified or explained to stakeholders?
