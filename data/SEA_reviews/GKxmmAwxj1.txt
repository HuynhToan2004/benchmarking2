 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for training Boltzmann generators (BGs) using a combination of maximum likelihood and reverse KL divergence, alongside a 2-Wasserstein loss to ensure the generation of realistic protein conformations. The approach employs a split flow architecture that processes internal coordinates separately for backbone and side chains, utilizing gated attention units and rotary positional embeddings to model long-range interactions. The methodology is evaluated on two small proteins, showing improvements over existing methods in generating low-energy conformations. However, the paper's scope is limited to small proteins, and the generalizability to larger, more complex systems remains unclear. Additionally, the paper's presentation could benefit from clearer explanations and improved figures to aid in understanding the complex methodologies and results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for training Boltzmann generators using a combination of maximum likelihood, reverse KL divergence, and 2-Wasserstein loss, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method is evaluated on two small proteins, showing improvements over the baseline method in generating low-energy conformations.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise descriptions of the proposed method and its evaluation.
- The use of internal coordinates and the split flow architecture for backbone and side chains is innovative and helps in capturing the distribution of backbone internal coordinates, which is crucial for modeling protein conformations.
- The methodology is supported by a robust evaluation, including a detailed comparison of the proposed method against existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to small proteins, which may not generalize well to larger, more complex systems, such as those found in drug design applications.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to only two small proteins, which does not convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness on larger, more diverse sets of proteins.
- The paper could benefit from clearer explanations and improved figures, particularly in Figure 1, to aid in understanding the complex methodologies and results.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the transferability of the trained model to other protein systems and the scalability of the method to larger, more complex systems.
- The method's dependence on the quality of the training data and the potential for overfitting to specific protein families is not adequately addressed.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the method to larger, more complex systems and how the method could be adapted for such applications?
- How does the method perform on a wider range of proteins, including those with different structural properties?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the 2-Wasserstein loss in the method, especially in relation to the energy minimization process?
- How does the method handle the transferability of the trained model to other protein systems, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the results?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational efficiency of their method against existing methods, particularly in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the method ensure the quality of the training data and mitigate the risk of overfitting to specific protein families?
