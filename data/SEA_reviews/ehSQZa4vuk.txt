 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the concept of "policy confounding," where reinforcement learning (RL) agents develop habits that are effective only when specific policies are followed, leading to incorrect outcomes when these policies deviate. The authors demonstrate this phenomenon through a series of examples and experiments, highlighting the limitations of current RL methods that rely on spurious correlations. They propose that using Markov state representations could potentially mitigate these issues, although the paper does not provide a clear solution or extensive empirical evidence. The theoretical framework is well-articulated, but the paper's experimental scope is limited, and the practical implications of the proposed solutions are not thoroughly explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and intuitive examples that effectively illustrate the problem of policy confounding.
- The concept of policy confounding is novel and addresses a significant issue in RL, particularly the reliance on spurious correlations which can limit generalization.
- The theoretical framework is solid, and the paper provides a clear mathematical characterization of the problem, which is well-motivated and supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed appendix that provides additional background and proofs.
- The authors have demonstrated that policy confounding can occur in both deterministic and partially observable environments, showing that the issue is not limited to specific types of environments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive solution to the problem of policy confounding. While it proposes some potential solutions, such as using Markov state representations, these are not thoroughly explored or validated.
- The experimental scope is limited, focusing primarily on simple grid-world environments, which may not adequately represent real-world applications where policy confounding could be more prevalent.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its approach or compare its findings with existing literature, which could help position the work within the broader context of RL research.
- The paper does not provide a clear, practical method for addressing policy confounding, which could limit its applicability in more complex or realistic scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how to prevent policy confounding, especially in terms of practical strategies that could be implemented in real-world RL systems.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the proposed solutions, such as using Markov state representations, could be practically implemented to mitigate policy confounding in RL systems?
- How does the paper address the issue of policy confounding in environments where the policy is not fixed, such as in continuous control tasks?
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between policy confounding and other related concepts, such as spurious correlations and the use of history in RL environments?
- How does the paper's theoretical framework apply to more complex or realistic environments, such as those involving continuous control or high-dimensional state spaces?
- Could the authors provide additional empirical evidence or examples to demonstrate the practical implications of policy confounding in more complex or varied scenarios?
