 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Progressive Image Editing (PIE), a method that leverages text-to-image generative models to simulate disease progression in medical imaging. By iteratively refining disease-related image features, PIE allows for precise and realistic disease progression simulations, enhancing clinical diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment. The method is validated through experiments in three medical imaging domains, demonstrating its superiority over existing methods. However, the paper is critiqued for its lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, insufficient detail on the experimental setup, and potential overclaiming of novelty. The methodology is also questioned for its scalability and the clarity of its contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for simulating disease progression using text-to-image generative models, which is a significant advancement in medical imaging.
- The proposed method is technically sound, and the experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in generating realistic disease progression images.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a user study with physicians to evaluate the effectiveness of the generated progressions.
- The method's ability to simulate disease progression is highlighted as a valuable tool for medical research and clinical practice.
- The theoretical analysis of the iterative refining process being equivalent to gradient descent with an exponentially decayed learning rate provides a deeper understanding of the method's operation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in the context of medical image editing and disease progression simulation.
- The experimental setup and details are insufficiently described, which limits the reproducibility and transparency of the results.
- The method's novelty is questioned, as it appears to be a straightforward application of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper's claims of scalability and the method's ability to handle complex diseases are not convincingly supported by the presented evidence.
- The paper overclaims its novelty and contribution, particularly in terms of the theoretical analysis and the method's ability to handle personalized and complex diseases.
- The method's limitations, such as the need for high-quality input images and the potential for overfitting to specific datasets, are not adequately addressed.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their method compared to existing works, particularly in terms of disease progression simulation and medical image editing?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance, especially in medical image editing and disease progression simulation?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific datasets used and the parameters of the model?
- How does the method handle personalized and complex diseases, and what are the limitations in terms of scalability and generalizability?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical analysis and its practical implications for the method's operation?
- How does the method ensure the accuracy and reliability of the generated disease progressions, especially in cases where the input data is of poor quality or incomplete?
