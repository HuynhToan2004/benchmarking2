 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to self-supervised anomaly detection, focusing on the selection of data augmentation hyperparameters to optimize performance. The authors propose a differentiable validation loss and new differentiable augmentations to improve the alignment between augmented training data and unsupervised test data. The methodology is tested on 41 anomaly detection tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed techniques. The paper also addresses the challenge of recognizing and tuning augmentation as a hyperparameter, which is often overlooked in existing methods. However, concerns were raised about the novelty and clarity of the paper, with some reviewers questioning the technical contributions and the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and challenging problem of recognizing and tuning augmentation as a hyperparameter for self-supervised anomaly detection (SSAD), which is often overlooked in existing methods.
- The proposed unsupervised validation loss and differentiable augmentation formulations are novel and effective, enhancing the alignment between augmented training data and unsupervised test data.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and detailed theoretical analysis, making it accessible and easy to understand.
- Extensive experiments are conducted on 41 anomaly detection tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed techniques.
- The paper provides a new perspective on augmentation selection in SSAD, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty in some areas, particularly in the application of differentiable augmentation and the use of a differentiable validation loss, which have been previously explored in other contexts.
- The experimental setup and results are not clearly explained, with some tables and figures being hard to interpret.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the proposed methods to other data modalities beyond image data.
- The theoretical analysis and proofs provided are not convincing, with some claims, such as the proof of Lemma 2, being potentially misleading or incorrect.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its methods with existing state-of-the-art methods, which limits the ability to assess the true impact and improvement over existing techniques.
- There are issues with the clarity and organization of the paper, with some sections being hard to follow and the presentation of results being confusing.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed methods can be applied to other data modalities beyond image data?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical performance?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations and proofs for the theoretical claims made in the paper, particularly the proof of Lemma 2?
- How are the hyperparameters for the differentiable augmentation functions initialized, and what is the impact of these initializations on the performance of the method?
- In the experimental setup, how are the results presented in Tables 1 and 2 interpreted, and what do the asterisks denote in these tables?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of the choice of augmentation functions and the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance?
