 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two methods, Concept Controller (CC) and Concept Controller CBM (CC-CBM), aimed at mitigating catastrophic forgetting in continual learning. CC utilizes sparsity regularization to identify and freeze concept-related neurons, while CC-CBM leverages a concept bottleneck model to learn and preserve concepts across tasks. Both methods aim to improve interpretability by identifying neurons that correspond to specific concepts, which can be frozen to prevent forgetting. The paper presents extensive experiments on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and TinyImageNet datasets, comparing the proposed methods with various baselines. However, the paper has been criticized for its lack of novelty, unclear methodological descriptions, and insufficient experimental validation, particularly in terms of the scalability and generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach by combining the concept bottleneck model with continual learning, which is a novel application of this model in the context of continual learning.
- The methodology is well-described, making it easy to follow and understand, which is crucial for reproducibility and adoption by other researchers.
- The paper provides a comprehensive comparison with various baselines, which helps in evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed methods against existing approaches.
- The use of concept-related neurons and the application of sparsity regularization to identify these neurons are innovative and well-explained, contributing to the interpretability of the model.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a logical structure, which enhances the reader's understanding of the content.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the methodology, particularly in sections 3 and 4, which could confuse readers and hinder the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed methods, especially in terms of the number of tasks and the complexity of the datasets used.
- The novelty of the work is questioned, as the main contributions seem to be the application of existing methods (e.g., Dynamic Expandable Networks and Concept Bottleneck Model) to continual learning without significant modifications or improvements.
- The paper suffers from inconsistencies in terminology and notation, which can be misleading and confusing for readers.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of concept drift, which is critical in continual learning settings.
- The paper's experimental results are not convincing, with marginal improvements over baselines and insufficient validation on larger datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the methodology, particularly in sections 3 and 4, to make it more detailed and understandable?
- How do the authors address the scalability of their methods, especially in terms of the number of tasks and the complexity of the datasets?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other continual learning baselines, especially those that have been recently proposed?
- How do the authors ensure that the concept-related neurons identified by their methods truly represent human-understandable concepts, and not just arbitrary neuron groupings?
- Could the authors explain the rationale behind the choice of datasets and the task distributions used in their experiments?
- How does the paper address the issue of concept drift, which is crucial in continual learning settings?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the computational cost and training time of their methods compared to baselines?
