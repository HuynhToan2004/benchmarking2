 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Heuristic Blending (HUBL), a novel technique for enhancing the performance of offline Reinforcement Learning (RL) algorithms by blending heuristic values with bootstrapped ones. HUBL modifies the Bellman operators in offline RL algorithms by partially replacing bootstrapped values with heuristic ones, which are estimated using Monte-Carlo returns. This method is designed to be compatible with various existing offline RL algorithms and aims to improve their performance by reducing complexity and increasing finite-sample performance. The paper provides both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of HUBL, demonstrating improvements across multiple benchmarks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The proposed HUBL method is simple, effective, and easy to implement, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the HUBL method, which is both novel and insightful.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the HUBL method across various benchmarks, showing consistent improvements over existing methods.
- The method's integration with existing offline RL algorithms is seamless, making it a versatile tool for enhancing performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with similar methods, such as those by Wilcox et al., which also address the issue of bootstrapping in offline RL.
- The theoretical analysis assumes that the heuristic values are accurate, which might not be the case in practical scenarios.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental details, such as the specific hyperparameters used for HUBL and the base offline RL algorithms, which could affect the reproducibility and understanding of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the HUBL method, such as its applicability to different types of offline RL algorithms and its performance in various environments.
- The empirical results are not comprehensive enough, and the paper does not include a sufficient number of seeds for statistical significance.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a detailed comparison with similar methods, such as those by Wilcox et al., to highlight the unique contributions and advantages of the HUBL method?
- How does the HUBL method perform when the heuristic values are not accurate? What are the implications for the performance and reliability of the method?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific hyperparameters used for HUBL and the base offline RL algorithms, to enhance the reproducibility and understanding of the results?
- How does the HUBL method perform in different types of offline RL environments, such as those with stochastic environments or those where the data is not collected by a behavior policy?
- Could you clarify the definition and role of the heuristic function in the HUBL method, especially in relation to the behavior policy and the data distribution?
- How does the HUBL method handle the trade-off between bias and regret, particularly in scenarios where the heuristic values are less accurate?
