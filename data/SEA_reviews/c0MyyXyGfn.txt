 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to solving complex reinforcement learning (RL) tasks by decomposing them into simpler subtasks, each prioritized by lexicographic constraints. This method, termed Prioritized Soft Q-Decomposition (PSQD), uses a soft Q-learning algorithm to learn a Q-function for each subtask, which is then combined to form a global Q-function. The approach is tested in a 2D navigation task and a Franka Panda manipulation task, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods. The paper also discusses the challenges of scaling the method to more complex environments and the need for further research on the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem in the field of reinforcement learning (RL) by proposing a novel method for decomposing complex RL tasks into simpler subtasks, which can be solved independently.
- The method is well-motivated, clearly explained, and supported by empirical results that demonstrate its effectiveness in solving complex RL tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the supplementary material provides additional details that enhance understanding.
- The method's scalability to high-dimensional action spaces and its ability to learn and adapt subtask solutions under lexicographic priorities are highlighted as significant contributions.
- The empirical results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods, indicating its practical utility and potential impact on the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the proposed method to more complex environments and how it would perform in real-world applications.
- There is a need for more extensive testing in diverse environments to validate the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the subtask decomposition is performed, which could limit the reproducibility and applicability of the method.
- The method's reliance on the manual selection of threshold values for subtask priorities could limit its practical utility, especially in scenarios where these values are not easily quantifiable.
- The paper does not discuss the computational efficiency of the method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- The method's novelty is questioned as it seems to be an extension of existing methods, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from prior work.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex environments, and what are the challenges in scaling it to such environments?
- Could you provide more details on how the subtask decomposition is performed, including any heuristics or guidelines that could aid in its application?
- How do the threshold values for subtask priorities affect the performance of the method, and are there any automated methods for setting these values?
- Can the method be adapted to handle more than two subtasks, and how would the performance compare to a baseline that uses all subtasks at once?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the subtasks are not independent, and what are the implications for the overall performance of the system?
- Could you clarify the role of the entropy term in the MaxEnt RL objective and its impact on the learning process?
