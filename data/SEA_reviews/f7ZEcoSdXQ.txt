 **Summary:**
The paper explores the incentive mechanisms in federated learning (FL) to address the issue of heterogeneous data distribution among clients, which can affect model performance and convergence. The authors propose a mechanism that quantifies the heterogeneity using Wasserstein distance and introduces a two-stage Stackelberg game to model the interactions between the central server and clients. The paper presents a theoretical analysis of the convergence bound and equilibrium existence, alongside empirical evaluations that demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed mechanism. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive theoretical support, and inadequate empirical evidence.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue in federated learning, specifically the incentive mechanism for clients to contribute to the federated learning process.
- The use of Wasserstein distance to quantify data heterogeneity is innovative and provides a new perspective on the problem.
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and presents clear theoretical results and empirical evaluations.
- The proposed incentive mechanism is theoretically sound and effectively demonstrated through empirical studies.
- The paper is well-written and provides a novel approach to the incentive problem in federated learning, offering a theoretical analysis of the convergence bound and equilibrium existence.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as the use of Wasserstein distance to quantify data heterogeneity and the incentive mechanism design are not entirely new.
- The theoretical support for the proposed mechanism is insufficient, particularly in the convergence analysis and the assumptions made about the data distributions.
- The paper lacks comprehensive empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of the proposed mechanism across different scenarios and datasets.
- There are inconsistencies in the theoretical assumptions and their practical implications, such as the assumption of a complete information game which is not practical in federated learning scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of data heterogeneity, particularly in the context of non-iid data, and does not provide a detailed discussion on how the proposed mechanism handles this issue.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the related works and a clearer explanation of the assumptions and their implications.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed mechanism handle the issue of data heterogeneity, particularly in scenarios where the data distributions are significantly different?
- Can the authors provide more detailed empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of the proposed mechanism across different scenarios and datasets?
- What are the practical implications of assuming a complete information game in federated learning scenarios?
- How does the proposed mechanism ensure truthful reporting from clients, and what measures are in place to prevent malicious behavior?
- In the context of federated learning, how does the proposed mechanism handle the issue of non-iid data, and what are the potential impacts on model performance and convergence?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the assumptions about the data distributions and their practical implications?
