 **Summary:**
The paper explores the generalization performance of linear estimators under matrix norm constraints on the bias operator, extending the Gauss-Markov theorem to include non-zero and bounded bias. The authors derive the optimal estimators for different Schatten norms, including Nuclear and Spectral norms, and compare their performance with Ridge regression. Theoretical analysis is provided for different matrix ensembles, and the paper includes simulations that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The main contributions include the derivation of explicit formulas for the optimal estimator under various norms and the demonstration of how these estimators compare in terms of generalization error and test error.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and interesting problem in the field of linear estimation, providing a novel approach by allowing non-zero and bounded bias, which is a departure from traditional methods.
- The authors have derived explicit formulas for the optimal estimator under different Schatten norms, which are applicable to a wide range of linear models and can be extended to other norms.
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the theoretical results and providing a clear presentation of the findings, which enhances its readability and accessibility.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of simulations that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, providing empirical evidence to support the theoretical claims.
- The authors have made a substantial effort to compare the proposed estimators with Ridge regression, showing how the Nuclear estimator can outperform Ridge in certain scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of their applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The theoretical results are primarily focused on specific matrix ensembles, which may not generalize well to other types of data distributions, such as those found in real-world applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which could be a significant limitation in practical implementations.
- The paper's focus on linear models may limit its relevance to more complex, non-linear models that are commonly used in machine learning applications.
- There is a lack of clarity in some of the theoretical results, particularly in the proofs and the definitions of key terms, which could make it difficult for readers to fully understand the contributions of the paper.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and definition of the bias operator B in the context of the paper, especially in relation to the norm of the operator?
- How do the authors justify the choice of matrix ensembles used in the theoretical analysis, and what implications does this choice have for the generalizability of the results?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons between the proposed methods and other regularization techniques, such as Lasso, to better understand the relative advantages and disadvantages of each approach?
- What are the specific computational complexities associated with the proposed methods, and how do these compare to the computational demands of other estimators like Ridge regression?
- In the context of the simulations, how were the hyperparameters chosen, and what impact do these choices have on the results?
- Can the authors provide more detailed justifications for the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly in relation to the assumptions about the distribution of the data and the noise?
