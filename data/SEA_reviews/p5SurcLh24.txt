 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for integrating model-based and model-free reinforcement learning (RL) through the concept of "equivalent policy sets" (EPS). The EPS is a set of policies that are not provably suboptimal, and the authors propose a method to maintain this set by alternating between model-based and model-free policy updates. The method is evaluated on various benchmarks, showing that it outperforms existing methods in some scenarios. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the methodology, the practicality of the assumptions, and the robustness of the results. The paper's contributions include a novel approach to combining model-based and model-free RL, but the practical implications and theoretical rigor of the approach are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to combining model-based and model-free reinforcement learning (RL) by maintaining a set of policies that are not provably suboptimal, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the theoretical underpinnings and practical applications of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a logical structure that facilitates understanding of the complex concepts presented.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in various scenarios, showing that it can outperform existing methods in certain benchmarks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive literature review and discusses the limitations of the proposed approach, which adds depth to the analysis and enhances the credibility of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the model-free RL policy is trained on the same data as the model-based policy, which may not be practical in all scenarios, especially when data collection for the two policies is not synchronized.
- The theoretical analysis of the proposed approach is not robust, with some sections of the paper lacking rigorous proofs and detailed explanations, which could undermine the credibility of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the proposed approach, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the scalability of the method to more complex environments.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key concepts such as the "equivalent policy set" and the "Bayes-optimal policy."
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other existing methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of its effectiveness and limitations.
- The paper suffers from some typographical and grammatical errors, which detract from its professional quality and readability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the model-free RL policy is trained on the same data as the model-based policy, and discuss the practical implications of this assumption?
- How does the proposed method ensure that the model-free policy is trained on the same data as the model-based policy, and what are the implications of this for the overall effectiveness of the approach?
- In the experiments section, why is there a discrepancy between the reported results in Figure 2 and those in Table 1, particularly in the performance of the SAC algorithm?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, especially in terms of the computational resources required to maintain the equivalent policy set?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the model-based and model-free policies are not well-aligned, and what are the implications of this misalignment for the overall performance of the agent?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method in more detail, particularly in terms of its applicability to more complex environments and its scalability to larger, more diverse datasets?
