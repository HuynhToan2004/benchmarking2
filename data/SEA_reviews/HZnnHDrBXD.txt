 **Summary:**
The paper investigates action-manipulation attacks in continuous reinforcement learning (RL) settings, focusing on both white-box and black-box scenarios. It introduces a novel attack method, LCBT, which utilizes a binary tree structure to discretize action spaces and estimate worst-case Q-values. Theoretical analyses and empirical results demonstrate that LCBT can force the RL agent to learn specific policies with sublinear attack costs, showing effectiveness across various RL algorithms like DDPG, PPO, and TD3. The paper also discusses the practicality and computational efficiency of the attack, although it faces criticisms regarding the assumptions and the necessity of the attack in continuous state and action spaces.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic of action-manipulation attacks in reinforcement learning (RL), which is relevant given the increasing use of RL in critical applications.
- The theoretical analysis provided is comprehensive and well-organized, contributing to a deeper understanding of the proposed attack method.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for readers to engage with the content.
- The proposed attack method, LCBT, is innovative and shows effectiveness in both white-box and black-box scenarios, demonstrating its applicability and robustness.
- The paper includes empirical results that validate the theoretical claims, providing a solid foundation for the proposed attack method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and justification for the attack model, which is crucial for understanding the motivations and practical implications of the proposed attack.
- There is a significant assumption that the attacker can manipulate the action space, which may not be practical or realistic in many real-world scenarios.
- The computational efficiency of the attack method is a concern, particularly in black-box settings where the attacker lacks prior knowledge of the MDP.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the knowledge of the worst-case action and the action space, are strong and may not be practical or realistic in many real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the attack method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in various scenarios.
- The paper's related work section is inadequate, missing significant recent studies on adversarial RL, which could provide a more comprehensive context for the research.
