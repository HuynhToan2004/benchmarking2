 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to selecting the most suitable Large Language Model (LLM) for a new task by leveraging benchmark datasets to train a "router" model. This router model is designed to predict the performance of LLMs on new tasks, thereby assisting in the efficient selection of the most effective model for specific use cases. The methodology involves transforming the LLM selection problem into a collection of binary classification tasks, using benchmark datasets to learn the strengths and weaknesses of different LLMs. The paper also discusses the challenges and potential improvements in the methodology, such as the generalization of correctness predictors to new tasks and the importance of a larger pool of benchmarks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology.
- The approach of using benchmark datasets to learn the strengths and weaknesses of different LLMs is novel and practical, particularly in the context of the increasing number of open-source LLMs.
- The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation, including a variety of benchmark datasets and models, which adds to the robustness of the findings.
- The proposed method of learning a router to select the best LLM for new tasks is innovative and could be a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper discusses potential limitations and future directions for research, which demonstrates a thoughtful approach to the problem and a commitment to ongoing improvement.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the methodology would perform with smaller or less powerful LLMs, which could limit its applicability.
- The assumptions made in the methodology, such as the accuracy of the correctness predictor and the generalization of the model to out-of-distribution data, are not thoroughly validated.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methodology, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of LLMs and tasks.
- The paper could be clearer in its presentation, particularly in explaining the methodology and the results, to enhance understanding and reproducibility.
- The paper's experimental setup and results could be improved by including more detailed comparisons with other models and a more thorough discussion of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the methodology perform with smaller or less powerful LLMs? Are there any specific challenges or limitations to consider in this context?
- How sensitive is the methodology to the choice of benchmark datasets? Could the selection of different datasets lead to different outcomes or recommendations?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly in terms of how the benchmark datasets were selected and how the LLM routers were trained?
- How does the methodology handle tasks or datasets that are significantly different from those in the benchmark datasets?
- Could the authors elaborate on the assumptions made in the methodology, particularly in terms of the accuracy of the correctness predictor and the generalization of the model to out-of-distribution data?
- How does the methodology compare to other approaches for selecting the best LLM for a new task? Are there any specific advantages or disadvantages compared to these other methods?
