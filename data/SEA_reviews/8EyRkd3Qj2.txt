 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel learning scenario called checkerboard learning, where clients hold different modalities of data, and the goal is to learn a model that can impute missing modalities based on the data available at each client. This is addressed through a collaborative adaptation framework that includes both a modality VAE and a client-adaptation VAE, aimed at handling statistical heterogeneity and modality combination heterogeneity. The framework is evaluated on various datasets, including a real-world clinical dataset, and the results show that the proposed method outperforms existing related methods. The paper also discusses the challenges of imputing missing modalities and the potential biases introduced by statistical heterogeneity.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel learning scenario called checkerboard learning, which is a practical and up-to-date approach to learning from distributed multimodal data.
- The proposed framework, CLAP, is well-designed to address the challenges of imputing missing modalities in a distributed learning setting, with a focus on handling statistical heterogeneity and modality combination heterogeneity.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the challenges and proposed solutions.
- The experiments conducted on both benchmark and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a good motivation and justification for the proposed framework, which is both intuitive and well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing works in the field of federated learning, which could help to better position the proposed method within the existing literature.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently detailed, with some results (e.g., Table 3) being difficult to interpret, and the absence of error bars makes it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the results.
- The paper does not address the privacy concerns associated with learning from distributed multimodal data, which is a significant limitation given the potential risks associated with sharing sensitive data across clients.
- The paper does not provide sufficient implementation details, which could limit the reproducibility and practical application of the proposed method.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
