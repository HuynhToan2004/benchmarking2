 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AdaICl, a novel method for active learning in in-context learning (ICL), which combines uncertainty and diversity sampling to select examples that the model is most uncertain about and that represent different semantic regions. AdaICl addresses the challenge of limited budgets for annotating examples by using a model-adaptive optimization-free algorithm that identifies hard examples and performs semantic diversity-based example selection. The method is evaluated across nine datasets and seven large language models (LLMs), demonstrating improvements in performance, budget efficiency, and fewer ICL examples needed. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and lack of significant comparison with other active learning baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly explained.
- The proposed method, AdaICl, effectively combines uncertainty and diversity sampling, which is novel and effective in active learning for in-context learning (ICL).
- The paper provides extensive experiments across various datasets and LLMs, demonstrating the effectiveness of AdaICl in improving performance, budget efficiency, and reducing the number of ICL examples needed.
- AdaICl addresses the challenge of limited budgets for annotating examples, which is a significant issue in practical applications.
- The paper includes a thorough analysis of the impact of hyperparameters and the choice of semantic similarity measures, providing insights into the method's effectiveness.
- The method's ability to identify hard examples and perform semantic diversity-based example selection is highlighted as a key strength, particularly in scenarios with limited budgets for annotating examples.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks significant novelty as the idea of combining uncertainty and diversity sampling is not new.
- There is a lack of significant comparison with other active learning baselines, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the method with larger datasets or the computational costs associated with the method.
- The method's effectiveness in scenarios with limited budgets for annotating examples is not convincingly demonstrated, as the results on smaller datasets do not clearly show an advantage over larger datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of datasets and the scalability of the method with varying dataset sizes.
- The paper's methodology and results sections are somewhat disjointed, making it difficult to follow the logic and the contributions of the study.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other active learning baselines to better establish the novelty and effectiveness of their method?
- How does the method perform on larger datasets, and what are the computational costs associated with its implementation?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies demonstrating the method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the budget for annotating examples is very limited, and what are the implications for its effectiveness in such cases?
- The paper mentions that the method is effective in reducing the number of ICL examples needed. Could the authors provide more detailed results or comparisons to support this claim?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the LLM's feedback is not reliable, and what are the implications for the method's effectiveness in such cases?
