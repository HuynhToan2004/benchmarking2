 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the exposure bias in diffusion models, proposing a novel metric and a training-free method called Epsilon Scaling to address this issue. The authors analyze the sampling distribution and derive a metric to quantify exposure bias, demonstrating that the prediction error at each sampling step is the root cause. They propose Epsilon Scaling, which scales down the network output to mitigate the input mismatch between training and sampling, and show its effectiveness across various diffusion models. The method is validated through extensive experiments on datasets like CIFAR-10 and LSUN, demonstrating improvements in metrics like FID. However, concerns about the novelty and the generalizability of the findings to more complex datasets are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the exposure bias problem in diffusion models, which is a significant issue in the field.
- It introduces a novel training-free method, Epsilon Scaling, which effectively mitigates the exposure bias and shows significant performance improvements.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which enhances its readability and comprehensibility.
- The methodology is well-motivated, with a clear and logical structure, which aids in understanding the proposed solutions and their implications.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of Epsilon Scaling across various diffusion models and datasets, which supports the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the differences between the proposed metric and other metrics used to quantify exposure bias, which could confuse readers about the novelty and utility of the proposed metric.
- The empirical results, particularly the FID scores, are not convincingly superior to existing methods, which raises questions about the practical benefits of the proposed solutions.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the findings to more complex datasets or models, which limits the scope of the research.
- The paper does not sufficiently explore the implications of the proposed solutions on other metrics such as precision and recall, which could affect the overall assessment of the method's effectiveness.
- The paper's claims about the root cause of exposure bias and the effectiveness of Epsilon Scaling are not convincingly supported by the experimental evidence, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between the proposed metric and other metrics used to quantify exposure bias, and discuss the advantages of their metric in practical applications?
- How does the proposed method perform on more complex datasets or models, and what are the implications of these findings for the broader applicability of the method?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including FID scores for different datasets and models, to better demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed solutions?
- How does the proposed method impact other metrics such as precision and recall, and what are the implications of these impacts on the overall performance of the diffusion models?
- Can the authors elaborate on the design of the scaling schedule and its impact on the performance of the method, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical implementation?
