 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of randomized matrix multiplication to reduce memory consumption during backpropagation in neural networks, specifically focusing on linear layers. It proposes a method that computes gradients by multiplying a random matrix with the input, thereby reducing memory requirements. Theoretical analysis and empirical evidence are provided to support the reduction in memory usage and the impact on variance. The method is tested on the GLUE benchmark, showing mixed results in terms of memory efficiency and model accuracy. However, the paper's claims about memory reduction are questioned, as the actual memory savings are not as significant as claimed. The method's empirical evaluation is also criticized for not adequately demonstrating the benefits across different datasets and model sizes.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method of using randomized matrix multiplication for memory reduction in neural networks is innovative and potentially beneficial for reducing memory usage.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the variance introduced by randomized matrix multiplication, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, covering various aspects of the method's performance, including memory efficiency, computational complexity, and model accuracy.
- The paper introduces a novel method for reducing memory usage in neural networks, which is a significant issue in training large models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper claims that the method reduces memory usage by a factor of 10, but the actual reduction in memory usage is not as significant as claimed, particularly for smaller batch sizes.
- The empirical evaluation of the method is limited to the GLUE benchmark, and it would be beneficial to see how the method performs on other datasets and models of different sizes.
- The theoretical analysis of the method's impact on model accuracy and performance is not convincingly demonstrated, particularly in the context of the GLUE benchmark.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational overhead introduced by the randomized matrix multiplication, which could be a significant drawback for large-scale training.
- The method's performance is highly dependent on the batch size, which may not be practical for all training scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancy between the claimed memory reduction and the actual reduction observed in the experiments?
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets and models of different sizes?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the impact of the randomized matrix multiplication on computational time and model accuracy across different batch sizes?
- What are the specific advantages of using randomized matrix multiplication over other methods for reducing memory usage in neural networks?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods for reducing memory usage in neural networks in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated?
