 **Summary:**
The paper introduces "Don't Trust: Verify" (DTV), a novel method that leverages large language models (LLMs) to autoformalize mathematical statements and solutions, which are then verified using automated theorem provers. The approach aims to enhance the accuracy of LLM-generated solutions by integrating formal verification into the reasoning process. The method is tested on various datasets, showing significant improvements over traditional methods like majority voting. However, concerns were raised about the scalability of the method, the quality of formalization, and the clarity of the presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach that leverages LLMs to formalize mathematical statements and solutions, which is a significant advancement in the field of automated theorem proving.
- The methodology is clearly presented, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed approach.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through rigorous testing, showing that it outperforms traditional methods in quantitative reasoning tasks.
- The use of formal verification to enhance the accuracy of LLM-generated solutions is a promising direction for improving the reliability of AI systems.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including an ablation study and a discussion on the limitations of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of how it would perform on larger, more complex datasets or in more complex mathematical domains.
- The quality of formalization generated by the LLM is a concern, with potential issues such as incorrect formalization leading to inaccurate solutions.
- The paper's presentation could be improved to enhance clarity and understanding, particularly in figures and tables that are currently confusing or unclear.
- There is a lack of comparison with other advanced LLMs like GPT-4, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the approach, particularly in terms of the reliability of the formalization process and the potential for false positives in the verification step.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to assess the robustness and generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the formalization process is evaluated for quality, particularly in terms of how incorrect formalization could lead to inaccurate solutions?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger, more complex datasets or in more complex mathematical domains?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly in terms of the specific datasets used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the proposed method compare to other advanced LLMs like GPT-4, and what are the implications of using these models for the formalization and verification process?
- Could the authors discuss potential strategies for improving the reliability of the formalization and verification processes, particularly in terms of reducing false positives and enhancing the accuracy of the method?
