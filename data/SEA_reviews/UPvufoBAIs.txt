 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) in category-level 3D pose estimation using only RGB images, without requiring 3D annotations or source data. The method leverages the observation that certain object sub-parts remain stable across out-of-domain scenarios, allowing for the strategic use of these invariant subcomponents for effective model updates. It represents object categories as simple cuboid meshes and utilizes a generative model of neural feature activations to learn the mesh vertex features. The model iteratively updates these features based on their proximity to corresponding features in the target domain, even when the global pose is incorrect. The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness through extensive experiments on real and synthetic data, showing significant improvements over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) problem in 3D pose estimation, which is a significant and relevant research topic.
- The method proposed is straightforward, intuitive, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broader audience.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to understand, with detailed explanations of the method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to UDA in 3D pose estimation using only RGB images, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is simple yet effective, and the paper provides a thorough analysis of the method's limitations and potential improvements.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the method handles the stability of object sub-parts across out-of-domain scenarios and how this stability is leveraged for effective model updates.
- The theoretical analysis of the method's convergence to the target domain is not discussed, which could enhance the paper's credibility.
- The method's reliance on the assumption that certain object sub-parts remain stable across out-of-domain scenarios may not always hold true, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could provide more detailed visualizations and comparisons, particularly in the appendix, to better illustrate the method's effectiveness and limitations.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the method handles occlusion and the impact of occlusion on the pose estimation accuracy.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the method and potential directions for future research.

**Questions:**

- Could the authors discuss the theoretical analysis of the method's convergence to the target domain and its implications for practical applications?

- Could the authors provide more detailed visualizations and comparisons, particularly in the appendix, to better illustrate the method's effectiveness and limitations?
