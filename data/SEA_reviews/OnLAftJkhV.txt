 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel data-driven approach to crystal structure prediction (CSP) using a latent conservative objective model (LCOMs). This method leverages a graph diffusion auto-encoder (CD-VAE) to convert crystal structures into a vector-based search space and trains a surrogate model to optimize the crystal structure in the latent space. The LCOMs approach is shown to be competitive with state-of-the-art methods while significantly reducing computational costs. The methodology involves a conservative training of the surrogate model to prevent exploitation of its errors by the optimizer. Despite its effectiveness and efficiency, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its reliance on existing methodologies like CD-VAE and COMs, which have been previously used in similar contexts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, LCOMs, is novel and innovative, combining existing techniques such as CD-VAE and COMs in a new and effective way.
- The methodology is supported by thorough experimental validation, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in crystal structure prediction (CSP), which is crucial for various applications in materials science.
- The use of conservative training to prevent exploitation of errors in the learned surrogate model is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it heavily relies on existing methodologies such as CD-VAE and COMs, which have been previously used in similar contexts.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of its method, which could be crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- The evaluation of the method is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on a subset of compounds and not comparing the results across different datasets or against a broader range of baseline methods.
- There is a lack of discussion on the generalizability of the method to other datasets or crystal structures, which could be a significant concern for its practical application.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential societal impacts of its work, which could be a critical consideration for some readers.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the term "conservative objective models" and its relevance to the methodology described in the paper?
- How does the method ensure that the crystal structures predicted are not only the global optimum but also stable and synthesizable?
- Is there a possibility to apply the method to other datasets or crystal structures, and if so, what are the potential challenges or limitations?
- Can you discuss the potential societal impacts of your work, particularly in the context of crystal structure prediction and materials science?
- How does the method handle the optimization of crystal structures in the latent space, and what are the implications of this approach for the accuracy and efficiency of the predictions?
