 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two new federated optimization algorithms, GradSkip and GradSkip+, which aim to reduce communication and computational costs in federated learning environments. GradSkip allows clients to perform a varying number of local gradient steps, thereby reducing communication frequency, while GradSkip+ extends this approach to include unbiased compression operators. The theoretical analysis of these algorithms demonstrates linear convergence rates under certain conditions, and empirical results are presented to validate these claims. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty, insufficient empirical validation, and unclear presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear and intuitive description of the algorithms, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed algorithms, GradSkip and GradSkip+, show promising results in reducing communication complexity and local computational costs, which are significant in federated learning scenarios.
- The theoretical analysis of the algorithms is comprehensive, with detailed proofs and a clear explanation of the underlying mathematical concepts.
- The paper includes a variety of algorithms, including GradSkip, GradSkip+, and VR-GradSkip+, which are well-described and have been empirically evaluated.
- The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in federated learning, focusing on reducing communication costs, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks sufficient empirical validation, with the experiments being limited to a few datasets and not sufficiently comprehensive to support the claims made.
- The novelty of the work is questioned, as the algorithms appear to be a combination of existing methods without significant innovation.
- There are concerns about the fairness of the comparison in the experiments, particularly in terms of the computational costs and the assumptions made about the data and model conditions.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made and their implications, as well as a clearer explanation of the theoretical results and their practical implications.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with some sections being difficult to follow and the figures being small and hard to read.
