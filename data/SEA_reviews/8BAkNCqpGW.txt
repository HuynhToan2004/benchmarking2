 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel policy gradient method for POMDPs in the offline setting, focusing on finite-horizon problems with continuous state and observation spaces. It establishes statistical and computational guarantees for the proposed method, addressing challenges such as the unobserved state, bias estimation, and function approximation. The method leverages a min-max estimating procedure to compute policy gradients non-parametrically using offline data, which is then used to optimize a history-dependent policy. The paper provides theoretical analysis on the statistical and computational convergence of the proposed algorithm, supported by a toy example and simulation studies. However, the practical applicability of the method in real-world scenarios and its scalability remain concerns.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel policy gradient method for POMDPs in the offline setting, which is the first of its kind to provide statistical and computational guarantees.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis on the statistical and computational convergence of the proposed algorithm, which is supported by a toy example and simulation studies.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise presentation of ideas, and includes a comprehensive review of related literature.
- The proposed method is technically sound and addresses the challenges of bias estimation, function approximation, and global convergence in POMDPs, which are significant in the field of offline reinforcement learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical studies on real-world applications, which could demonstrate the practical applicability and effectiveness of the proposed method in more complex and varied scenarios.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other existing methods for POMDPs, which could provide a better understanding of its relative performance and advantages.
- The paper's reliance on a fixed-parameter policy class may limit its applicability in more complex scenarios where history-dependent policies are required.
- The computational complexity of the proposed method is high, which might limit its scalability to larger or more complex POMDPs.
- The paper's assumptions and theoretical results, such as the convergence rate and the relationship between POMDPs and MDPs, are not clearly explained or justified.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the practical applications of the proposed method in real-world scenarios, and how it compares to other existing methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of unobserved states in POMDPs, and what are the specific advantages of using a history-dependent policy class over a fixed-parameter policy class?
- Can you clarify the assumptions and theoretical results, particularly the convergence rate and the relationship between POMDPs and MDPs, and how these assumptions affect the applicability and effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the unobserved state is not confounded, and how does it compare to other methods in such scenarios?
- Could you discuss the potential of extending the proposed method to handle more complex POMDPs, such as those with infinite state and action spaces, and how the computational complexity might be addressed in such cases?
