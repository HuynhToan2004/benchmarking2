 **Summary:**
The paper explores the generalization capabilities of decision-focused learning (DFL) models, particularly focusing on how they perform on altered optimization tasks. It introduces two methods: implicit generalization, which trains models on a distribution of tasks, and explicit generalization, which incorporates task representations into the model's input. The paper demonstrates these methods through experiments on various decision-making problems, showing that explicit generalization can outperform traditional methods like SPO. However, concerns are raised about the limited scope of the experiments, the clarity of the presentation, and the lack of a broader evaluation across different types of tasks and optimization problems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-structured presentation.
- The research question about task generalization in decision-focused learning is both interesting and relevant, especially considering the practical implications of generalizing decisions across different tasks.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are clearly presented, with the paper effectively demonstrating the benefits of task representations in explicit generalization.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of decision-focused learning, which has been under-explored in previous research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough literature review, missing key references and discussions on related works, which could have enriched the context and depth of the research.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, focusing only on a few types of tasks and not including a broader range of optimization problems such as vehicle routing or scheduling.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its methods, particularly the scalability and applicability of the proposed solutions in more complex or varied scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed methods, especially in relation to the size and complexity of the tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of its research, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the term "explicit generalization" and its implications for the model's performance and generalization capabilities?
- How does the paper ensure that the model's predictions are not overly influenced by the specific features or constraints of the training tasks, especially in the case of implicit generalization?
- In the context of the experiments, how are the test tasks selected, and what criteria are used to ensure that they are representative of the generalization performance across different tasks?
- Could you discuss the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed methods, particularly in relation to the size and complexity of the tasks?
- How do the proposed methods compare to other state-of-the-art decision-focused learning methods, and what are the potential advantages or disadvantages of using these methods over traditional approaches?
