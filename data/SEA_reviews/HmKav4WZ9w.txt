 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to handling numerical features in Factorization Machines (FM) by encoding them into vectors of basis functions, specifically using B-Splines. This method allows for the approximation of segmented output functions, potentially improving accuracy and reducing sparsity issues. The approach is evaluated against traditional binning methods on both synthetic and real datasets, showing improvements in performance. The paper also discusses the integration of this method into existing FM systems, enhancing their ability to handle numerical features. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticisms regarding its experimental scope, potential generalizability, and the clarity of its presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear and concise presentation of the proposed method.
- The method is theoretically justified and empirically supported, demonstrating improvements over traditional binning methods in both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The approach is simple and can be integrated into existing systems, potentially improving performance and reducing computational costs.
- The use of B-Splines for encoding numerical features is novel and offers a more flexible and theoretically grounded approach compared to traditional binning methods.
- The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, including an A/B test on a real-world advertising system, which supports the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other methods for handling numerical features, such as neural networks or other encoding methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to a small set of datasets, and it is unclear how the method would perform on larger, more diverse datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the method across different types of numerical features or recommendation scenarios.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and self-containment, as it relies heavily on references and external material which may not be accessible to all readers.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration in the context of recommendation systems.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other methods for handling numerical features, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- How does the method perform on larger, more diverse datasets, and what are the implications for its scalability and generalizability?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup, including the specific datasets used and the methodology employed?
- How does the method handle categorical features, and what are the implications for its performance in scenarios where numerical features are less important?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental results, particularly in terms of the significance of the improvements observed and the statistical significance of the results?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting, especially in scenarios where the basis functions used may not adequately represent the data?
