 **Summary:**
The paper explores a strategic classification setting where individuals can manipulate or improve their features, affecting the decision-making process in a Stackelberg game context. It introduces a novel probabilistic model that captures both manipulation and improvement behaviors, decomposing the objective difference between non-strategic and strategic policies into three interpretable terms. The authors explore how adjusting the decision-maker's preferences can impact the optimal policy and its fairness properties. The paper provides theoretical insights and experimental validations, including on real-world datasets, to demonstrate the practical implications of these theoretical findings. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its limited empirical evaluation, lack of detailed experimental setup, and unclear presentation of certain results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel probabilistic model that captures both manipulation and improvement behaviors, which is a significant extension of existing models.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis on the impact of adjusting the decision-maker's preferences on the optimal policy and its fairness properties.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes both theoretical and experimental results to validate the findings.
- The model is applied to real-world datasets, demonstrating its practical applicability.
- The paper explores a strategic classification setting where individuals can manipulate or improve their features, which is a novel approach to the problem of strategic classification.
- The theoretical results are supported by experimental results on real-world datasets, enhancing the credibility of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's empirical evaluation is limited, with only a few experiments conducted on synthetic and real datasets, which may not adequately demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- There is a need for more clarity in the presentation of results, particularly in figures and tables, to enhance the readability and comprehension of the data.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the proposed methods can be applied to high-dimensional feature spaces, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which is an important consideration for their practical implementation.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made and their implications, as well as a more comprehensive comparison with existing literature.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the experimental setup, including the data sources and preprocessing steps?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the cost of manipulation/improvement is a deterministic function of feature changes?
- Can the authors clarify the meaning and implications of the parameters q and ε in the context of the proposed model?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between manipulation and improvement, especially in scenarios where both behaviors are possible?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed methods and discuss potential practical applications in high-dimensional feature spaces?
- How do the authors address the issue of fairness in their model, particularly in scenarios where group fairness notions are used?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their approach and provide suggestions for future research directions?
