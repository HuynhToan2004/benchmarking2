 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Align With Purpose (AWP), a novel framework designed to enhance specific properties in models trained with the Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC) criterion. AWP achieves this by integrating an additional loss term that prioritizes alignments based on a desired property, such as low latency or minimum word error rate. The framework is demonstrated to be effective in various scenarios, including automatic speech recognition, where it shows improvements in latency and word error rate. The paper is praised for its clarity, ease of implementation, and potential for broader application across different domains.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed method.
- The AWP framework is versatile and can be applied to different models and tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in low latency and minimum word error rate (mWER) scenarios.
- The method is simple, straightforward, and requires minimal code changes, making it easy to implement.
- The paper provides extensive experiments that validate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in latency and word error rate.
- The method can be applied to other alignment-free loss functions, demonstrating its general applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader comparison with other methods for achieving low latency and minimum word error rate, such as those mentioned in the literature.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The method's reliance on the choice of the property function (fprop) could limit its effectiveness, as it may not be applicable to all scenarios or may require extensive tuning.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational overhead introduced by the additional loss term, which could impact the practical applicability of the method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and its broader impacts, which could be crucial for understanding its real-world applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the method performs in scenarios where the property function (fprop) is not well-chosen or not applicable?
- How does the method compare to other methods for achieving low latency and minimum word error rate, especially in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to other domains beyond automatic speech recognition, and if so, what are the potential challenges and opportunities?
- Could you provide more insights into the computational overhead introduced by the additional loss term, and how this affects the practical applicability of the method?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the property function (fprop) may introduce biases or other negative impacts on the model's performance?
