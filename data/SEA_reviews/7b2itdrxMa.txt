 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of causal curriculum learning in both children and reinforcement learning (RL) agents using Procgen environments. It demonstrates that children use their current level competence to determine the next level in the curriculum, which is akin to how RL agents use auxiliary rewards to mitigate catastrophic forgetting. The study involves both observational data from children and RL experiments, showing that auxiliary rewards can improve learning efficiency and reduce the likelihood of divergence. However, the paper's experimental setup and the generalizability of the findings are questioned due to the limited scope of the tasks and the lack of rigorous evaluation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach by applying causal curriculum learning to both children and RL agents, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The findings from the study are intriguing, particularly the observation that children use their current level competence to determine the next level in the curriculum, which is akin to how RL agents use auxiliary rewards.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The inclusion of empirical data from children's behavior in the study adds a practical and real-world dimension to the research.
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic in the field of curriculum learning, which is crucial for advancing the capabilities of AI agents.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental setup is limited to a few tasks and does not adequately address the generalizability of the findings to a broader range of tasks.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the RL experiments, particularly in terms of the statistical significance of the results and the comparison with existing methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implications of the findings, such as how the observations of children's behavior can be translated into effective RL algorithms.
- The paper's claims about the generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks and environments are not well-supported, which limits the applicability of the research.
- There is a lack of detail in the RL experiments, particularly concerning the environment used and the hyperparameters employed, which could affect the reproducibility and validity of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its findings with existing literature, which could help contextualize the contributions of the study.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the meaning of "global advancement" and "global competence" as used in the paper?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- What are the specific hyperparameters used in the RL experiments, and how do they affect the results?
- Can you provide more details on the environment used in the RL experiments, including the number of lanes and the difficulty levels?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of catastrophic forgetting in RL, and what are the implications for the generalization of the findings to other types of tasks?
- Could you elaborate on the practical implications of the findings for the development of RL algorithms that mimic the learning behaviors observed in children?
- How do the findings from the RL experiments compare with those from the observational data, particularly in terms of the generalizability of the observations to other types of tasks?
