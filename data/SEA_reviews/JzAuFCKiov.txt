 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Pairwise Proximal Policy Optimization (P3O), a novel policy gradient algorithm designed for aligning large language models (LLMs) with human preferences through relative feedback. P3O operates by directly optimizing comparative rewards, addressing the limitations of existing methods like Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) and Direct Policy Optimization (DPO). Theoretical analysis and empirical evaluations demonstrate that P3O outperforms PPO and DPO in terms of the KL-Reward trade-off and can align better with human preferences. The methodology involves a comparative approach to reward modeling, which simplifies the algorithm's design and avoids the need for complex reward scaling.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear and detailed explanation of the proposed method, P3O.
- The theoretical analysis is solid, and the paper is well-motivated, addressing the issue of reward equivalence in PPO and proposing a new method, P3O, to optimize rewards trained from comparison-based loss.
- The empirical results demonstrate that P3O outperforms PPO and DPO in terms of the KL-Reward trade-off, showing a better alignment with human preferences.
- The paper introduces a novel policy gradient algorithm, P3O, which simplifies the design of RL algorithms for LLM alignment.
- The authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis, showing that P3O is invariant to equivalent rewards and avoids the complexity of PPO, making it a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that P3O can be generalized to accommodate more than two ranked responses, which could enhance the trade-off between human effort and AI alignment.
- The empirical evaluations are limited to two datasets, which might not be sufficient to validate the effectiveness of P3O across a broader range of tasks.
- There is a lack of clarity on how P3O handles the generation of multiple responses in a single prompt, which is crucial for evaluating the method's performance.
- The paper does not provide code for reproducibility, which is essential for validating the results and replicating the experiments.
- The paper does not compare P3O with other state-of-the-art methods like RAFT, RLAIF, and ReST, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of P3O's effectiveness.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of P3O, which is crucial for understanding the method's applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- How does P3O handle the generation of multiple responses in a single prompt?
- Can the authors provide more evidence or experimental results to support the generalization of P3O to more than two ranked responses?
- Could the authors provide code for reproducibility and share the code for generating the curves in Figures 2 and 3?
- How does P3O compare with other state-of-the-art methods like RAFT, RLAIF, and ReST?
- What are the limitations of P3O, and how do these limitations affect its applicability and effectiveness?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the potential societal impacts of P3O?
