 **Summary:**
The paper examines the effectiveness of selective mixup in addressing distribution shifts, specifically focusing on how this method affects the training distribution. It highlights that the observed improvements in selective mixup are often due to a resampling effect rather than the mixup itself. The authors demonstrate this through empirical studies on several datasets and theoretical analysis, showing that selective mixup can be seen as a form of resampling. Despite the paper's clear presentation and interesting insights, concerns were raised about the novelty of the findings and the limited scope of the experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear figures and detailed explanations of the experiments.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support their claims, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of selective mixup in addressing distribution shifts.
- The paper provides a new perspective on the mechanism of selective mixup, which can be seen as a resampling effect, and offers a novel combination of sampling and mixup methods.
- The authors compare the results of selective mixup with those of resampling, demonstrating that selective mixup is a more effective method for addressing distribution shifts.
- The paper is significant as it addresses the mechanisms of selective mixup, which is a widely used method for addressing distribution shifts in various domains.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is limited as the findings on the resampling effect of selective mixup are not entirely new, as similar effects have been observed in other studies.
- The experiments are limited in scope, focusing on a small number of datasets and not sufficiently exploring the effects of selective mixup in more complex or varied settings.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of selective mixup, particularly in scenarios where the "regression toward the mean" effect does not occur or is less pronounced.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the implications of the findings, especially in terms of practical applications and future research directions.
- The theoretical analysis is somewhat simplistic and does not fully capture the complexities of selective mixup in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the datasets used in their experiments, particularly the specific mixup methods and selection criteria employed?
- How do the authors address the limitations of selective mixup, especially in scenarios where the "regression toward the mean" effect is not present?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of their findings, particularly in terms of how selective mixup can be applied to improve model generalization in real-world scenarios?
- What are the potential future directions for research on selective mixup, and how might these findings influence the development of new methods or applications in the field?
