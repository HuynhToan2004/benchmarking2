 **Summary:**
The paper explores the alignment of large language models (LLMs) with human feedback through reinforcement learning (RL) while maintaining differential privacy (DP). It introduces a framework that ensures the final model satisfies DP over the entire training process, validated through experiments on tasks such as sentiment generation and summarization. The approach uses a reward model trained on a private dataset, which is then used to fine-tune the LLM's policy to maximize the reward. The paper discusses the challenges in maintaining DP while training the reward model and proposes a novel DP adaptation of the Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) algorithm. However, the paper's experimental results are not convincing, with marginal improvements in utility and privacy, and the scalability of the approach to larger models and datasets is not adequately addressed.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a timely and relevant topic, focusing on the alignment of large language models (LLMs) with human feedback while maintaining differential privacy (DP), a critical aspect given the increasing adoption of LLMs in user interactions.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive literature review and a detailed description of the proposed framework and algorithms.
- The experimental results demonstrate the feasibility of achieving privacy-preserving alignment of LLMs using RL, even though the utility and privacy improvements are marginal.
- The paper is the first to explore the alignment of LLMs with human feedback while ensuring DP, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the effectiveness of the proposed DP adaptation of PPO and the impact of the reward model on the privacy and utility trade-off.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with marginal improvements in utility and privacy, and the scalability of the approach to larger models and datasets is not adequately addressed.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed approach, particularly the scalability and generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks or larger models.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the hyperparameter tuning and the sensitivity of the results to these parameters, which is crucial for reproducibility and validation of the findings.
- The paper does not address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed approach, which is a significant omission in the discussion of limitations.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify why the reward model needs to be trained with DP, and how this affects the privacy and utility trade-off?
- How does the proposed DP adaptation of PPO compare in terms of utility and privacy with other existing methods, particularly in terms of the impact on the training process and the utility of the final model?
- Could the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning and its impact on the results, including the sensitivity of the privacy-utility trade-off to different hyperparameters?
- How does the proposed approach scale to larger models and datasets, and what are the expected performance improvements or challenges in these scenarios?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed approach and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
- How does the proposed approach compare in terms of utility and privacy with other methods that do not use DP, such as those that use data synthesis or other privacy-preserving techniques?
