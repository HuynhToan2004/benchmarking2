 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, LaLiGAN, for discovering symmetries in data using a generative model that learns nonlinear symmetries by decomposing group transformations into nonlinear mappings between data space and latent space. The model learns a latent space where symmetries become linear, thereby expanding the search space beyond linear symmetries. Theoretical guarantees are provided for the expressive power of the proposed decomposition, and the method is demonstrated to discover well-structured latent spaces with interpretable symmetries in high-dimensional and nonlinear dynamical systems. The paper also discusses the potential applications of this method in equation discovery and long-term forecasting.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for learning nonlinear symmetries in data, expanding the search space beyond linear symmetries, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method is theoretically grounded, with theoretical guarantees provided for the expressive power of the proposed decomposition.
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the methodology and its applications, including equation discovery and long-term forecasting.
- The methodology is original and the paper is well-motivated, addressing the important problem of automatic symmetry discovery in data.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis that shows the proposed decomposition and neural network parametrization can approximate nonlinear group actions under certain conditions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential failure modes of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and reliability.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the methodology, particularly in the explanation of the training objectives and the role of various components such as the discriminator and the reconstruction loss.
- The paper does not provide sufficient quantitative evaluation, relying heavily on qualitative results which may not adequately demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
- The paper does not discuss the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of its computational complexity and scalability to larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the hyperparameters used in the experiments, which is crucial for reproducibility and understanding the robustness of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the potential societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle non-compact Lie groups, such as SO(2)?
- What are the training objectives, and how are they optimized?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the discriminator in the training process and its impact on the final results?
- How does the method handle different types of symmetries, such as those found in different dynamical systems?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the method to larger, more complex datasets?
- How does the method perform when the symmetry group is not known a priori?
- Can the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its application in real-world scenarios?
