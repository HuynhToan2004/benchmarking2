 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel generative model for image synthesis using a diffusion model in the wavelet domain. The approach leverages the sparsity and non-Gaussian nature of high-frequency wavelet coefficients by conditioning on low-frequency coefficients, which are less sparse and more Gaussian-like. This model, termed the Wavelet Multi-Scale Generative Model (WMGM), aims to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of generative modeling by addressing the challenges of ill-conditioning and non-Gaussianity in high-frequency coefficients. The paper provides theoretical and empirical evidence supporting the duality between the spatial and wavelet domains, and demonstrates the model's effectiveness through comparisons with existing models on datasets like CelebA-HQ and AFHQ.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by applying a diffusion model in the wavelet domain, which is both theoretically interesting and practically relevant.
- The proposed WMGM framework demonstrates a faster sampling speed and better generation quality compared to existing methods, which is supported by empirical results.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the wavelet domain, which is beneficial for understanding the underlying principles of the proposed method.
- The paper presents a pioneering framework for implementing generative models in the wavelet domain, which is significant for advancing the field of diffusion modeling techniques.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the sparsity and non-Gaussianity of high-frequency wavelet coefficients, which are crucial aspects of the proposed method.
- There is an inconsistency in the description of the model's architecture, particularly the handling of high-frequency wavelet coefficients, which is not adequately explained.
- The paper does not include a detailed comparison with existing methods, such as WSGM, which could have provided a clearer understanding of the proposed model's advantages.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the training and sampling processes, which are critical for understanding the practical implementation of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its ability to handle various types of images and its robustness to different conditions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the GANO model handles high-frequency wavelet coefficients, especially in terms of sparsity and non-Gaussianity?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of training and sampling time with other existing methods, such as WSGM?
- Can the authors clarify the inconsistencies in the description of the model's architecture, particularly the handling of high-frequency wavelet coefficients?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of handling different types of images, such as those with varying levels of sparsity and non-Gaussianity?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and sampling processes, including the specifics of how the model is trained and the computational resources required?
