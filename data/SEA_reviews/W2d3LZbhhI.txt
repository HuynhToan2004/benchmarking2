 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a unified sampling framework for diffusion probabilistic models, aiming to optimize the solver schedule to improve sample quality with fewer function evaluations (NFE). The framework, named USF, allows for the selection of various strategies at different timesteps, including timestep discretization, prediction type, starting point, Taylor expansion order, derivative estimation method, and the use of correctors. The paper also presents a predictor-based search method, S^3, which optimizes the solver schedule to achieve a better trade-off between time and quality. The method was tested on various datasets, demonstrating significant improvements in sample quality with reduced NFE, although concerns about the clarity of the presentation and the generalizability of the results to high-resolution datasets persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a comprehensive review of related work.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, and the framework is well-designed, offering a unified perspective for all sampling methods based on exponential integral.
- The experiments are well-designed, showing that the proposed method can achieve much higher sample quality than baselines, especially with very few steps.
- The proposed method can be easily integrated with existing methods, as shown in Table 1, and it achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance with low NFE.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of different components of the framework, which is helpful for understanding the impact of various choices on sample quality.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the proposed method, particularly the role and operation of the predictor in the search process.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods, especially in high-resolution datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost and time complexity of the proposed method, which could affect its practical applicability.
- The generalizability of the results to different types of diffusion models is not adequately addressed, particularly in relation to stable diffusion models.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the experimental settings and results, including the number of runs for each experiment and the variance of the results.
- The paper could be improved by providing more detailed insights into the search space and the impact of different settings on the performance of the sampler.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and operation of the predictor in the search process? How does it guide the search towards better solver schedules?
- How does the proposed method perform in high-resolution datasets, such as ImageNet-256? Can you provide more detailed results or comparisons with existing methods?
- How does the computational cost and time complexity of the proposed method compare to existing methods? Are there any specific optimizations that could be made to improve efficiency?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental settings, such as the number of runs for each experiment and the variance of the results?
- How does the proposed method generalize to different types of diffusion models, particularly stable diffusion models?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the NFE budget and its impact on the performance of the sampler?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between computational efficiency and sample quality, especially in terms of the number of function evaluations required?
