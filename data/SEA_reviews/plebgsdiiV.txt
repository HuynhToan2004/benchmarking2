 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for off-policy evaluation (OPE) in reinforcement learning (RL) with deterministic target policies, focusing on continuous action spaces. It proposes a kernel relaxation and metric learning approach to address the challenges of zero importance sampling ratios in deterministic policies. The methodology involves learning kernel metrics to minimize the mean squared error of the estimated temporal difference update vector, which is then used to evaluate the performance of the target policy using offline data. Theoretical analyses are provided to derive the bias and variance of the estimation error, and empirical studies demonstrate the method's effectiveness in various test domains.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach for off-policy evaluation (OPE) with deterministic target policies, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, with clear and detailed derivations of bias and variance, supported by empirical studies.
- The empirical results show that the proposed method significantly outperforms other baselines, indicating its practical effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, and the methodology is clearly described, aiding in reproducibility.
- The use of kernel relaxation and metric learning in OPE for deterministic policies is a novel approach, which could potentially inspire further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the support of the behavior policy contains the actions determined by the target policy, which may not be a realistic assumption in many practical scenarios.
- The empirical evaluation is limited to simple environments like the pendulum, which may not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in more complex settings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the method, which could be a significant concern in large-scale applications.
- The theoretical analysis and proofs are complex and may not be accessible to readers without a strong background in mathematical proofs, which could limit the paper's accessibility.
- The paper does not adequately address how the method performs in scenarios with multiple behavior policies or how it scales to handle larger datasets.
- The comparison with existing methods such as SR-DICE and FQE is not comprehensive, and the paper does not sufficiently discuss the advantages of the proposed method over these approaches.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the assumptions regarding the support of the behavior policy and how these assumptions affect the practical applicability of the method?
- How does the proposed method perform in environments with multiple behavior policies, and what are the implications for the computational complexity in such scenarios?
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of the method and how it scales with the size of the dataset?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods like SR-DICE and FQE, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and empirical performance?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle stochastic target policies, and if so, what are the implications for the method's effectiveness and computational requirements?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of kernel functions and their impact on the method's performance?
