 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the STIMULUS algorithm, a stochastic multi-gradient recursive estimator designed for multi-objective optimization (MOO) problems. The algorithm is claimed to achieve a linear convergence rate, with theoretical analysis provided for both non-convex and strongly convex settings. The STIMULUS algorithm is compared against existing methods such as MGD, SMGD, and MOCO, demonstrating superior performance in terms of convergence rate and sample complexity. The paper also discusses the practical implications of the algorithm, including its potential to handle large-scale multi-objective problems. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as the approach appears similar to existing methods like MGDA, and for its reliance on strong assumptions that may not be realistic in practical scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear problem definition and a comprehensive literature review.
- The proposed algorithm, STIMULUS, is simple, effective, and achieves a linear convergence rate, which is a significant improvement over existing methods.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the STIMULUS algorithm, including both non-convex and strongly convex settings, and establishes a linear convergence rate.
- The experimental results demonstrate the superiority of the STIMULUS algorithm over existing methods, showing a significant reduction in sample complexity.
- The paper addresses the practical challenges of multi-objective optimization, particularly in handling large-scale multi-objective problems, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the practicality of its assumptions, such as the boundedness of the minimum value of the objective function, which may not hold in real-world scenarios.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned as the approach appears similar to existing methods like MGDA, with the main difference being the use of a recursive framework for updating stochastic gradient estimates.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the computational complexity of STIMULUS with existing methods, which is crucial for assessing its practical efficiency.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the choice of baselines is questionable, as they may not be the most relevant or competitive.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implementation challenges of the STIMULUS algorithm, particularly in terms of its computational complexity and the practicality of its assumptions.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the practical implications of the assumption that the minimum value of the objective function is bounded? How does this assumption affect the applicability of the STIMULUS algorithm in real-world scenarios?
- How does the computational complexity of STIMULUS compare to existing methods, particularly in terms of the number of iterations required to achieve a given level of convergence?
- In the experimental results, why were MGD and SMGD not compared on the same batch size as STIMULUS and STIMULUS-M?
- Can you provide more details on the implementation of STIMULUS in practical scenarios, particularly in terms of its computational efficiency and the practicality of its assumptions?
- How does the STIMULUS algorithm perform in scenarios where the objectives are not strongly convex? What are the implications of this for the practical applicability of the algorithm?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of baselines in the experimental comparisons? Why were specific methods chosen over others, and how do these choices affect the interpretation of the results?
