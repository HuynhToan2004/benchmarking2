 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework, Language-Guided Abstraction (LGA), which leverages natural language descriptions to guide state abstraction in imitation learning. This approach aims to improve sample efficiency and robustness by filtering out irrelevant features, thereby reducing the need for extensive human intervention. The framework involves a pre-trained language model that translates task descriptions into state abstraction functions, which are then used to train policies using a small set of demonstrations. The methodology is tested in both simulated and real-world environments, demonstrating its effectiveness in various tasks, including mobile manipulation with a Spot robot. The paper also discusses the potential limitations and ethical considerations of the approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning state abstractions using natural language descriptions, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it easy to follow and understand, and the paper provides a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in both simulated and real-world environments.
- The use of a pre-trained language model to translate task descriptions into state abstraction functions is a novel and interesting approach, particularly in the context of imitation learning.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the methodology, results, and limitations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the language model (LM) is trained and how it handles different task descriptions, which could affect the robustness and generalizability of the method.
- There is a potential limitation in the method's ability to handle complex, multi-step tasks or tasks with non-segmentable features, which could affect its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the ethical implications of the approach, such as the potential biases that could be introduced by the language model.
- The evaluation of the method is somewhat limited, with a focus on simple tasks and a lack of comparison to more recent or complex methods, which could affect the perceived effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method to more complex environments or the computational cost of using large language models.

**Questions:**
- How does the LM handle different task descriptions, and how is it trained to ensure consistency and accuracy in generating state abstraction functions?
- Can the LM be fine-tuned to improve its performance on specific types of tasks, and how would this affect the generalizability of the method?
- How does the method perform when the language model is not able to accurately translate task descriptions into state abstraction functions?
- In the real-world experiments, how were the policies trained and evaluated, and what were the specific challenges encountered in implementing the method in these settings?
- How does the method compare to other recent methods in terms of performance and computational cost, and what are the potential limitations or drawbacks of the proposed method compared to these approaches?
