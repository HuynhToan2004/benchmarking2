 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for modeling partial orders in hyperbolic space using "shadow cones," a concept inspired by physical phenomena such as light casting shadows. This framework generalizes the entailment cone methodology and offers advantages in optimization and empirical performance. The shadow cones are defined using light sources and opaque objects, with the size and shape of the shadow determining the hierarchy of elements. The paper provides a detailed mathematical analysis and experimental results demonstrating the framework's effectiveness, particularly in handling hierarchical data and representing partial orders. Despite its strengths, the paper is critiqued for its limited scope in terms of application scenarios and the lack of comparison with other recent methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed proofs and explanations that enhance understanding.
- The introduction of the shadow cones framework is innovative and provides a novel perspective on modeling partial orders in hyperbolic space, which is both theoretically interesting and practically useful.
- The empirical results are strong, showing that the shadow cones outperform existing methods in terms of accuracy and generalization.
- The paper is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the methodology and its advantages over existing methods.
- The mathematical analysis is robust, with detailed proofs and explanations that support the claims made about the framework.
- The paper is original, introducing a new framework for modeling partial orders in hyperbolic space, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the shadow cones framework, such as its applicability to other types of hierarchical data or its scalability to larger datasets.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, with only a few datasets used for comparison, which might not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the work, which is an important consideration for any research in this field.
- The paper could be improved by including more diverse examples and case studies to illustrate the practical applications of the shadow cones framework.
- The paper is somewhat dense in places, with some sections requiring more detailed explanations or examples to aid understanding.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other recent methods, particularly those that use different models or approaches to partial order embedding.

**Questions:**

- How does the shadow cones framework handle different types of hierarchical data, such as those with varying branching factors or different types of relationships?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications of the shadow cones framework? How might it be used in real-world scenarios to improve the representation or analysis of hierarchical data?
- How does the shadow cones framework compare to other methods that use different models or approaches to partial order embedding? Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison in terms of performance, scalability, and other relevant metrics?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their work, and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
