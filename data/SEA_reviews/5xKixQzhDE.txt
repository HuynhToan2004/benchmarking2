 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for dataset condensation, specifically designed for hyperparameter search. It aims to generate a synthetic validation dataset by matching the hyperparameter gradients computed via implicit differentiation and efficient inverse Hessian approximation, thereby preserving the validation-performance rankings of models across different hyperparameters. The proposed method, termed hyperparameter-calibrated dataset condensation (HCDC), is evaluated on both image and graph datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in speeding up hyperparameter/architecture search. The paper presents a theoretical framework that aligns hyperparameter gradients to ensure the validation performance rankings remain consistent, which is a significant advancement in the field of dataset condensation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to dataset condensation that is specifically tailored for hyperparameter search, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that focus on maintaining generalization performance.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, clearly written, and the experiments are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach across various datasets and tasks.
- The use of implicit differentiation and the efficient inverse Hessian approximation for computing hyperparameter gradients is a novel and effective approach, enhancing the efficiency and accuracy of the method.
- The paper presents a theoretical framework that aligns hyperparameter gradients, ensuring the validation performance rankings remain consistent, which is a significant advancement in the field of dataset condensation.
- The method is well-explained, with clear explanations of the challenges and solutions, and the experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms other methods in terms of performance ranking preservation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, which are crucial for assessing the practical applicability of the proposed method.
- The method's performance in scenarios where the training data is small or when the hyperparameter space is large is not adequately addressed, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the method's ability to generalize to unseen architectures and the potential impact of differentiable NAS models on its effectiveness.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the method's limitations, such as its applicability to different types of datasets and its computational complexity.
- The method's performance in terms of test accuracy and its ability to maintain the performance ranking of architectures across different hyperparameters is not consistently demonstrated across all experiments.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when the training data is small, and what are the implications for its effectiveness in such scenarios?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- How does the method perform when the hyperparameter space is large, and what are the implications for its practical applicability?
- Can the authors clarify how the method generalizes to unseen architectures and what are the potential limitations in this regard?
- How does the method compare to other methods in terms of test accuracy, and what are the implications of this for its practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the method's computational complexity and its limitations in terms of dataset types?
