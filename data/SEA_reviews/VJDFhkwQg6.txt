 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel federated learning framework for GFlowNets, a class of generative models that can sample from compositional objects such as sequences and graphs. The proposed method, FC-GFlowNet, enables each client to learn a GFlowNet based on their local rewards, which are then aggregated by a server to ensure federated balance. This approach ensures the correctness of the global sampling distribution and provides theoretical guarantees for the convergence of local models. The paper also introduces a new training criterion, contrastive balance, which is shown to be beneficial in both federated and centralized settings. The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various tasks, including grid-world, sequence generation, multiset generation, and Bayesian phylogenetic inference.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the motivation, methodology, and results.
- The proposed method is novel, practical, and theoretically sound, providing a new approach to federated learning of GFlowNets.
- The paper is original, introducing a new training criterion, contrastive balance, which is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated.
- The experiments are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various tasks, showing a good balance between theoretical guarantees and practical performance.
- The paper is the first to explore federated learning for GFlowNets, a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially in large-scale or more complex scenarios.
- The paper's reliance on the assumption that local GFlowNets are perfectly trained might not hold in real-world scenarios, and the robustness of the method under imperfect training conditions is not thoroughly explored.
- The comparison with other federated learning methods is limited, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are somewhat limited, with the experiments conducted primarily on toy datasets. More complex or real-world datasets could better demonstrate the generalizability and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper's theoretical contributions, while interesting, are somewhat disconnected from the main focus of the paper, which is the application of GFlowNets in federated learning.

**Questions:**
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed method scale with the size and complexity of the tasks?
- Can the proposed method be adapted to handle scenarios where local GFlowNets are not perfectly trained? What are the implications of this in terms of the accuracy and robustness of the global model?
- How does the proposed method compare with other federated learning methods in terms of performance, scalability, and computational efficiency?
- In the experiments, how are the rewards combined, and what are the implications of this combination on the overall performance and applicability of the method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the datasets used, the training parameters, and the evaluation metrics?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for catastrophic failure in federated learning settings, and what measures can be taken to mitigate this risk?
