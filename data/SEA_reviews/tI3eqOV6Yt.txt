 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Hyper-UT, a novel transformer-based architecture that combines dynamic function generation from hyper networks with adaptive depth from Universal Transformers. This architecture is designed to enhance generalization in tasks that require handling examples with varying complexities, particularly in multi-step reasoning tasks. The authors evaluate Hyper-UT on a new task called conditional pointer value retrieval (C-PVR), which involves recursive retrieval until a condition is met. The paper also explores the use of adaptive depth and modularity in transformers to improve generalization and proposes a new benchmark to test these capabilities. Despite the innovative approach and the new benchmark, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper's contributions, the fairness of comparisons, and the practical applicability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the experimental setups and results.
- It introduces a novel task called C-PVR to probe the generalization ability of models across different complexities, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is the first to study the generalization ability of transformers across different complexities, which is a timely and relevant topic given the increasing use of transformers in various applications.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed Hyper-UT model in enhancing generalization in transformers.
- The paper provides a comprehensive literature review, which helps in understanding the current state of research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the motivation and benefits of the proposed task and model, making it difficult for readers to grasp their significance and relevance.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed model with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and generalization performance.
- The paper's claims about the generality of the improvements in accuracy and efficiency of adaptive-depth, modular transformers are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- There are several minor issues with the presentation of the paper, such as unclear figures and typographical errors, which could detract from its professional quality.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and practical applicability of the proposed model, particularly in terms of its computational cost and the size of the training data required.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the motivation and benefits of the proposed task and model, and how they contribute to the broader goals of improving generalization in transformers?
- How does the proposed model compare with existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and generalization performance, especially when applied to more complex tasks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the size and diversity of the training data used, and how this affects the model's performance and generalization?
- How does the proposed model perform when scaled up to larger models, and what are the computational costs associated with this scaling?
- In light of the concerns about the fairness of comparisons and the practical applicability of the results, how do the authors justify the claims made in the paper, particularly regarding the generalizability and efficiency of the proposed model?
