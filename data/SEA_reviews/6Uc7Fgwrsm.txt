 **Summary:**
The paper introduces OmniMixup, a novel method that generalizes the Mixup technique by allowing for arbitrary sampling distributions, enhancing its applicability across various domains. This method, OmniMixup, is supported by a theoretical framework that establishes a relationship between the Mixup objective and the empirical risk minimization objective, providing a regularization term. The paper also presents OmniEval, a method for evaluating different sampling distributions, which is crucial for selecting the most effective distribution. The empirical results demonstrate that OmniMixup outperforms traditional Mixup methods on image classification and molecular property prediction tasks. However, the paper has been criticized for its lack of clarity in presentation, the limited novelty of its contributions, and the inadequacy of its experimental evaluations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a generalized version of Mixup, OmniMixup, which encompasses various existing Mixup-like methods as special cases, providing a unified framework for understanding these methods.
- OmniMixup is supported by a theoretical framework that establishes a relationship between the Mixup objective and the empirical risk minimization objective, enhancing its theoretical underpinning.
- The paper provides a novel evaluation framework, OmniEval, which is used to measure the effectiveness of a given Mixing-Pair Sampling Distribution (MPSD), contributing to the method's practical applicability.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of OmniMixup, showing its potential to improve over traditional Mixup methods in various applications.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making the complex concepts accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, with some sections being difficult to follow, which could hinder understanding of the methodology and results.
- The novelty of the contributions is limited, as the generalization of Mixup and the use of Mahalanobis distance for evaluating Mixup strategies are not new ideas.
- The experimental evaluations are inadequate, with insufficient baseline comparisons and a lack of detailed analysis on the effectiveness of OmniMixup across different datasets and models.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could be crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a clearer explanation of the MPSD and its role in OmniMixup?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing Mixup-like methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- What are the specific advantages of OmniMixup over traditional Mixup methods, and how does it address the limitations of existing Mixup methods?
- How does the choice of the sampling distribution affect the performance of OmniMixup, and can the authors provide more insights into this aspect?
- Could the authors clarify the notation and terminology used in the paper to improve readability and understanding?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger and more complex datasets, and what are the potential implications of its use in such scenarios?
