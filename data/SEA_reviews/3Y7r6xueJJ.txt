 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of spurious correlations in continual learning (CL) settings, focusing on how these correlations can influence model predictions and transfer between tasks. It introduces a novel metric, Bias-flipped Mis-classification Rate (BMR), to quantify the bias in model predictions and demonstrates that CL methods can transfer biases both forward and backward. The study employs three benchmark datasets and various CL methods to systematically analyze the transfer of biases and proposes a baseline method, Group-class Balanced Greedy Sampling (BGS), to mitigate these biases. The paper also discusses the limitations of existing CL methods and suggests a new approach to address these issues in future research.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue in continual learning (CL) by focusing on the transfer of spurious correlations, a topic that has been overlooked in previous research.
- It introduces a novel metric, Bias-flipped Mis-classification Rate (BMR), which provides a useful tool for measuring bias in CL models.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and includes extensive experiments and analyses that are well-documented, enhancing its reproducibility.
- The authors provide a thorough analysis of the impact of spurious correlations on CL, highlighting the need for more robust CL methods that can handle such biases.
- The paper is significant as it demonstrates the transfer of bias in CL models, which is a critical issue in real-world applications where biased data is common.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental results are not convincing, particularly in demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed baseline method, BGS, in mitigating biases. The results in Table 1 suggest that BGS does not significantly improve over existing methods like Group DRO.
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the biases used in the experiments, which could lead to confusion about the nature and impact of these biases.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its methodology, particularly the assumption that spurious correlations are consistent across all tasks, which may not be realistic in many real-world scenarios.
- The paper's methodology and results sections are somewhat disconnected, with the methodology section focusing on two-task CL settings, while the results section evaluates methods across multiple tasks, which could mislead the reader about the applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its approach, particularly the potential for overfitting and the generalizability of the results to different types of biases and datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition and nature of the biases used in your experiments, especially in the context of the CelebA dataset?
- How does the proposed BGS method compare to other existing methods in terms of effectiveness in mitigating biases in CL models?
- Can you provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical implications of the biases you have identified in CL models?
- In light of the concerns about the experimental setup and the potential for overfitting, how do you ensure that your findings are robust and generalizable to different types of biases and datasets?
- Could you discuss the limitations of your approach and suggest potential directions for future research to address these limitations?
