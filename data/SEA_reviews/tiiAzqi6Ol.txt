 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to training preference models (PMs) by decomposing global preference assessments into interpretable features, which are then evaluated using a prompted language model (LM). The features are aggregated using a logistic regression classifier to predict human preferences. This method aims to address issues such as overfitting and lack of transparency in traditional PMs. The approach is evaluated using two datasets, HH-RLHF and SHP, and demonstrates improved robustness and interpretability compared to standard PMs. However, the method's generalizability and the specificity of the features used are questioned, as well as the method's dependence on the initial LLM's capabilities.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem of training preference models (PMs) for language models (LLMs), focusing on robustness and interpretability.
- The proposed method of decomposing preference assessments into interpretable features and using a prompted LM for scoring is novel and innovative.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in terms of robustness and interpretability.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and the methodology is clearly explained.
- The approach is evaluated on two datasets, HH-RLHF and SHP, which helps in validating the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the specific features used in the experiments, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The method's reliance on the capabilities of the initial LLM is not adequately addressed, raising concerns about the method's applicability to different LLMs.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential limitations or drawbacks of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its contributions.
- There is a lack of comparative analysis with other methods, which could have strengthened the paper's claims of superiority.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential for the method to be influenced by the specific features used in the experiments, which could limit its applicability to other datasets or scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the specific features used in the experiments and how they were selected?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different LLMs, and what are the implications for its applicability and effectiveness?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations or drawbacks of the proposed method, and how these might impact its practical implementation?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting, especially in scenarios where the training data might be limited?
- Could the authors provide more details on the selection of the reference PMs used in the experiments, and how these choices might influence the results?
- How does the method handle the potential for the LLM to provide biased or inaccurate feature scores, and what measures are in place to mitigate these risks?
