 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GRASP, a novel method for detecting out-of-distribution (OOD) nodes in graphs, focusing on the propagation of OOD scores across neighboring nodes. It theorizes that the effectiveness of OOD score propagation depends on the ratio of intra-edges to inter-edges in the graph. To enhance this ratio, GRASP proposes an edge augmentation strategy. The method's efficacy is validated through extensive experiments on various benchmark datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods. The paper is commended for its clarity, thoroughness, and the novelty of its approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a critical and under-explored problem in graph OOD detection, which is essential for various applications such as recommender systems and bioinformatics.
- The proposed method, GRASP, is simple, intuitive, and well-explained, making it easy to understand and implement.
- The paper provides both theoretical and empirical evidence, enhancing its credibility and practical applicability.
- The methodology is flexible and can be integrated with various existing OOD scoring functions, demonstrating its adaptability and versatility.
- The experimental results are robust and convincing, showing significant improvements over existing baselines.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and structured, making it accessible and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review, particularly regarding related works on OOD detection in graphs and graph data augmentation.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including a broader range of datasets, including more recent and large-scale datasets.
- The method's reliance on the assumption of a pre-trained model could limit its applicability in scenarios where training from scratch is necessary.
- The paper's theoretical analysis, while insightful, may not fully capture the complexity of real-world graph structures and their OOD detection challenges.
- The paper does not discuss potential limitations or societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be crucial for broader adoption and understanding.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the intuition behind the "naive" propagation approach and why it does not always help graph OOD detection?
- How does the GRASP method handle the selection of the subset G, and what are the implications of using different subsets?
- Can the authors elaborate on the computational efficiency of GRASP compared to other methods, especially in large-scale graph applications?
- Is there a possibility to extend the GRASP method to handle graph-level OOD detection, and what would be the implications of such an extension?
- How does the GRASP method perform when applied to different types of graph structures, such as directed or weighted graphs?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations or societal impacts of the proposed method, and how these might be addressed or mitigated?
