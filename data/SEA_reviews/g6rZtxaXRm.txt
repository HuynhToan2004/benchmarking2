 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel zero-shot approach called Follow-up Differential Descriptions (FuDD) that leverages large language models (LLMs) to generate class descriptions tailored for image classification tasks. FuDD identifies ambiguous classes and generates descriptions that differentiate them from others, enhancing the performance of vision-language models like CLIP. The method was tested across 12 datasets, showing significant improvements over naive LLM-generated descriptions and generic description ensembles. The paper also discusses the potential of FuDD in few-shot settings, comparing it to other methods like CoOp and SuS-X. Despite its strengths in methodology and experimental validation, concerns were raised about the scalability and generalization of the method, particularly in large-scale datasets and different types of visual tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, FuDD, is innovative and addresses a significant problem in the field by providing a novel approach to improving image classification performance using LLMs.
- The experimental setup is robust, with a comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and comparisons against various baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of FuDD.
- The paper provides detailed insights into the importance of differential descriptions and the impact of fine-tuning, which are crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness.
- The methodology is straightforward and the paper is well-organized, making it easy to understand and implement the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the scalability and generalization of the method, especially in large-scale datasets or different types of visual tasks.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with other methods like CoOp and SuS-X, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency.
- The method's reliance on the initial prediction from the vision-language model (VLM) could limit its effectiveness in scenarios where the VLM's initial prediction is inaccurate.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the method could be adapted to other types of visual tasks or datasets, which could limit its applicability.
- The paper does not address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in certain applications.
