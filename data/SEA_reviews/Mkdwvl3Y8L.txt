 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the existence of "knowledge-critical" subnetworks within pretrained language models (LMs) by proposing a method that utilizes differentiable weight masking to identify these subnetworks. The approach involves optimizing a multi-objective loss function to suppress specific knowledge while maintaining the model's performance on other relational knowledge and language modeling tasks. The method is demonstrated across various GPT2 models and knowledge graphs, showing that the identified subnetworks are highly sparse and contribute to the model's capacity to express specific knowledge relationships. Despite these findings, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the method to other models and its practical utility in real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to identifying "knowledge-critical" subnetworks in pretrained language models.
- The methodology is well-supported by experiments, which show that the identified subnetworks are highly sparse (>98%) and contribute to the model's capacity to express specific knowledge relationships.
- The paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the method's effectiveness across different model sizes and knowledge graphs, and includes an ablation study to validate the contributions of each component of the loss function.
- The findings could be useful for understanding how knowledge is encoded in pretrained language models and for improving the interpretability of these models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the generalizability of the method to other models and knowledge graphs, and does not provide a theoretical analysis of the method.
- The method's practical utility is not clearly demonstrated, particularly in terms of how it could be applied to improve the interpretability or performance of language models in real-world applications.
- The paper does not address the limitations of the method, such as its dependency on the specific pretraining dataset and the potential for overfitting to the training data.
- The method's reliance on perplexity as a metric for evaluating knowledge suppression and maintenance is questionable, as it may not accurately reflect the model's ability to express or understand specific knowledge relationships.
- The paper does not include a comparison with other methods for identifying knowledge-critical subnetworks, such as those based on attention weights or other model components.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the generalizability of the method to other models and knowledge graphs? How might the method need to be adapted for different types of models or knowledge graphs?
- How might the method be applied in practice to improve the interpretability or performance of language models?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of perplexity as a metric for evaluating knowledge suppression and maintenance? How does this metric align with the goals of the method, and what are the potential limitations of using perplexity in this way?
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the method, such as its dependency on the specific pretraining dataset and the potential for overfitting to the training data?
- How does the method compare to other methods for identifying knowledge-critical subnetworks, such as those based on attention weights or other model components?
