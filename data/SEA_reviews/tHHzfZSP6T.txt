 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the compositional generalization capabilities of Transformers by training them on a synthetic dataset that includes compositions of predefined functions. The authors explore how the model generalizes to new combinations of functions, focusing on both in-order and out-of-order compositions. The study reveals that the model can generalize to a vast number of compositions, even with minimal training data, and that attention layers in the latter half of the model play a crucial role in this generalization. The paper also discusses the impact of training data diversity and the role of attention in enabling compositional generalization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a synthetic task designed to evaluate compositional generalization, which is both well-designed and provides a clear explanation of the experimental setup.
- The authors demonstrate that a Transformer can generalize to a vast number of compositions with minimal training data, highlighting the model's ability to compose capabilities.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering various aspects of compositional generalization, and the results are well-presented, making the paper easy to follow.
- The paper provides a novel perspective on the compositional generalization capability of Transformers, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The use of linear probing to analyze the internal mechanisms of Transformers is an innovative approach that adds depth to the understanding of the model's functioning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the implications of the findings, particularly the limitations of the synthetic data and the generalizability of the results to real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other model architectures, such as LSTMs, to better understand the specific advantages of Transformers in compositional generalization.
- The experimental setup, while innovative, is somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on permutations and bijections, which might not fully represent the complexities of real-world compositional generalization tasks.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential biases in the training data and how these might affect the model's generalization capabilities.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential biases in the training data and how these might affect the model's generalization capabilities.
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the generalizability of the findings to more complex or realistic compositional tasks, which might limit the practical applicability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the implications of the findings for real-world applications of Transformers? How can the insights gained from this study be applied to improve the performance of Transformers in practical scenarios?
- How do the authors address the potential biases in the training data, and what steps were taken to ensure that the model's generalization capabilities are not solely due to these biases?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the synthetic data and the potential biases in the training data?
- How do the authors justify the choice of using permutations and bijections in the experimental setup, and what steps were taken to ensure that the results are generalizable to more complex compositional tasks?
- Could the authors clarify the role of attention layers in enabling compositional generalization and how this relates to the overall performance of the model?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of the experimental setup, such as the simplicity of the compositional tasks and the potential for overfitting due to the small dataset size?
