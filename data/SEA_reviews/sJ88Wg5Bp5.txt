 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for Continual Test-Time Adaptation (CTTA) by proposing a Visual Domain Adapter (ViDA) with both high-rank and low-rank embedding spaces. The ViDA leverages these features to adapt to current domain distributions and maintain continual domain-shared knowledge. The authors also introduce a Homeostatic Knowledge Allotment (HKA) strategy to dynamically merge knowledge from these adapters, enhancing the model's ability to handle domain shifts. The method was tested on various benchmarks and reportedly outperforms existing methods, demonstrating its effectiveness in classification and segmentation tasks. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the methodology, and the empirical evidence supporting the claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.
- The proposed method of using high-rank and low-rank embedding spaces is innovative and effectively addresses the challenges of continual test-time adaptation.
- The experiments are thorough, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various benchmarks and tasks, including classification and segmentation.
- The use of visualization tools such as t-SNE and CAM provides valuable insights into the feature extraction capabilities of the proposed method.
- The paper presents a novel approach by combining low-rank and high-rank adapters to manage domain-specific and task-relevant knowledge, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and comprehensive explanation of the Homeostatic Knowledge Allotment (HKA) strategy, making it difficult to understand how it contributes to the overall methodology.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable as similar ideas have been previously explored in other works.
- There is a lack of detailed experimental results and comparisons, particularly in the context of foundation models and large-scale datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the challenges of continual test-time adaptation, particularly in terms of error accumulation and catastrophic forgetting.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive ablation studies to better understand the contributions of different components of the proposed method.
- The methodology and the rationale behind certain design choices, such as the use of high-rank and low-rank adapters, are not sufficiently justified or explained.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and operation of the Homeostatic Knowledge Allotment (HKA) strategy in the context of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method address the challenges of continual test-time adaptation more effectively than existing methods?
- Can you provide more detailed experimental results, particularly in the context of foundation models and large-scale datasets?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could you provide more insights into the design choices, such as the use of high-rank and low-rank adapters, and how these choices contribute to the overall effectiveness of the method?
