 **Summary:**
The paper explores the transfer of knowledge from large Vision Foundation Models (VFMs) to smaller task-specific models, focusing on scenarios where labeled data is limited. It introduces two distinct approaches: task-agnostic and task-oriented knowledge transfer, employing different loss functions and distillation strategies. The task-oriented method, which involves fine-tuning the VFM with a small labeled dataset and then distilling the knowledge to the target model, demonstrates superior performance across various downstream tasks. The paper also discusses the curation of transfer sets to enhance the effectiveness of the knowledge transfer. The experimental results show that the task-oriented approach outperforms other methods, including task-agnostic knowledge transfer, web-scale CLIP pretraining, and supervised ImageNet pretraining.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-structured experimental setup.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, demonstrating significant performance improvements over existing methods such as task-agnostic knowledge transfer, web-scale CLIP pretraining, and supervised ImageNet pretraining.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison of the proposed method with various alternatives, including a comprehensive ablation study and the use of task-specific transfer sets.
- The approach is novel and could be useful in scenarios where labeled data is limited, offering a practical solution for training small task-specific models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the biases that might be inherited from the foundation models.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of different loss functions and their effects on the final results, which could be crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness.
- There is a lack of discussion on the generalizability of the findings across different domains and the potential limitations of the method in scenarios where large transfer sets are not available.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other recent works that explore similar ideas, such as those mentioned in the related works section.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the biases that might be inherited from the foundation models and how these could affect the performance of the task-specific models?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and memory requirements with other methods, particularly in resource-constrained settings?
- What are the specific computational and memory requirements of the proposed method, and how do these compare with other methods in terms of practical deployment?
- Can the authors elaborate on the choice of loss functions and their impact on the final results?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other recent works that explore similar ideas, particularly those mentioned in the related works section?
