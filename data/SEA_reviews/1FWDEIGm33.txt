 **Summary:**
The paper examines the context-dependent nature of Large Language Models (LLMs) by proposing a new metaphor, "LLM as a superposition of perspectives," which challenges the traditional view of LLMs as individuals with stable values and personalities. The authors demonstrate that LLMs can exhibit unexpected perspective shifts in response to different contexts, which can significantly alter their expressed values and personality traits. This phenomenon, termed the "unexpected perspective shift effect," is explored through various experiments using psychological questionnaires and different model versions. The paper also introduces the concept of "perspective controllability" to measure the extent to which a model can adopt specific perspectives, suggesting that some models are more controllable than others. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in defining key terms, potential misleading claims, and insufficient discussion on the implications of its findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and intriguing perspective on Large Language Models (LLMs) as a superposition of perspectives, which challenges the traditional view of LLMs as individuals with stable values and personalities.
- It provides a systematic and comprehensive study of the unexpected perspective shift effect in LLMs, demonstrating that different contexts can significantly alter the expressed values and personality traits of LLMs.
- The paper includes a thorough comparison of various models, revealing interesting insights into the controllability of LLMs and their susceptibility to context-induced shifts.
- The concept of "perspective controllability" is introduced, offering a new metric for evaluating the extent to which a model can adopt specific perspectives.
- The experiments are well-designed, with clear results that support the claims made, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claims about the "unexpected perspective shift effect" are potentially misleading as the term "unexpected" is not clearly defined or justified, and the experiments primarily involve contexts that are not truly unexpected.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the implications of its findings, particularly in terms of how these insights can be applied to improve the development and use of LLMs.
- There is a noticeable absence of related work on LLMs' susceptibility to context-induced shifts, which could have provided a richer context for the study.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of its experiments, such as the use of only a few models and a small number of contexts, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The concept of "perspective controllability" is not well-explained, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed exposition of this concept and its implications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and justification of the term "unexpected" in the context of the "unexpected perspective shift effect"?
- How do the authors envision the practical applications of the insights gained from studying LLMs as a superposition of perspectives?
- Could the authors elaborate on the implications of their findings for the development and use of LLMs, particularly in terms of improving their controllability and reducing context-induced shifts?
- What are the main differences between the perspective shift effect and the hallucination effect, and how do these phenomena relate to the findings presented in the paper?
- How do the authors address the limitations of their experiments, particularly the small sample size and the potential generalizability of the results?
- Could the authors provide more details on the calculation of the perspective controllability score and how it relates to the overall controllability of a model's behavior?
