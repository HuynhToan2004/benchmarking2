 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SPADE, a novel architecture that integrates a state space model (SSM) into the bottom layer of a Transformer model to enhance its ability to handle long-range dependencies. The SSM is used to provide coarse global information, which is refined by local attention mechanisms in subsequent layers. The model is evaluated on the Long Range Arena benchmark and language modeling tasks, demonstrating improvements in both efficiency and effectiveness. The paper also explores the scalability of the model through pre-training and fine-tuning on various tasks. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty, insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art models, and unclear justification for the choice of S4 as the SSM.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodologies and experiments.
- The integration of a state space model (SSM) into the bottom layer of a Transformer model to address the limitations of full attention in handling long-range dependencies is an innovative approach.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed model across various tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling long sequences.
- The use of a state space model to provide coarse global information and local attention mechanisms to refine this information is a reasonable approach that shows potential.
- The paper includes a thorough experimental analysis, which supports the claims made about the model's effectiveness and efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear justification for the choice of S4 as the SSM, particularly in terms of its ability to capture local information effectively.
- The novelty of the approach is limited as the integration of SSM into the bottom layer of a Transformer model has been explored in prior works.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance on long-range dependency tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the model, especially in terms of the size of the SSM and its impact on training and inference times.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the model's performance on different sequence lengths and the choice of the S4 model, which is not extensively used in recent state-of-the-art models.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance on long-range dependency tasks?
- How does the size of the SSM affect the training and inference times, and what are the specific computational costs associated with using S4 in the model?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of S4 as the SSM and discuss the implications of using a different SSM, such as those based on recurrent neural networks?
- In terms of the model's performance on different sequence lengths, how does the model handle varying dependencies across different lengths?
- How does the model perform when the S4 module is not initialized using Eq. 5, and what are the implications of this on the model's performance?
