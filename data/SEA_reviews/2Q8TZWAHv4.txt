 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Graph Output Attribution (GOA), a novel method for explaining the decision-making process of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by attributing graph outputs to input graph features. GOA represents GNNs as a sum of scalar products involving node features, edge features, and activation patterns, which are used to compute the contribution of each node or edge feature to each scalar product. This method is evaluated on synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in terms of fidelity, discriminability, and stability. The paper also introduces new metrics to assess these properties, showing that GOA outperforms existing methods in these aspects.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is novel and provides a fresh perspective on explaining the decision-making process of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), particularly in terms of its focus on the role of activation patterns.
- The method is simple and computationally efficient, which is beneficial for practical applications.
- The paper introduces new metrics for evaluating the performance of GNN explainers, providing a more comprehensive evaluation framework.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in terms of fidelity, discriminability, and stability, showing that it outperforms existing methods.
- The paper provides a detailed mathematical formulation and theoretical analysis, which adds depth to the understanding of the method's underpinnings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the activation patterns and their role in the method.
- The assumption of equal contribution of product terms may not hold in practical scenarios, which could limit the applicability of the method.
- The paper's evaluation primarily focuses on synthetic datasets, which may not fully represent real-world scenarios.
- The method's reliance on the assumption of equal contribution of product terms and its applicability to other types of GNNs, such as those that use concatenation or weighted sums, is not thoroughly discussed.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- There is a lack of discussion on the scalability of the method, especially in terms of its applicability to large-scale datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the activation patterns and their role in the method?
- How does the method handle the assumption of equal contribution of product terms in practical scenarios?
- Is there a way to modify the method to handle other types of GNNs that use concatenation or weighted sums?
- How does the method perform on other types of GNNs, such as those using different aggregation functions like mean, max, min, etc.?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and its applicability in more detail?
- How does the method scale to larger datasets, and what are the computational costs associated with its application?
