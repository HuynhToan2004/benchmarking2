 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an innovative approach to Bayesian filtering by framing it as an optimization problem, leveraging gradient-based optimization methods to approximate the MAP estimate of the posterior distribution. This methodology, referred to as the Implicit MAP Filter, is demonstrated to be effective in both linear and nonlinear systems, with a focus on high-dimensional settings. The approach is compared against various classical Bayesian filtering methods, showing competitive performance in certain scenarios. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, as similar approaches have been previously explored, and the paper's presentation and clarity need improvement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-motivated, with a clear and interesting problem formulation that is well-suited for high-dimensional systems.
- The proposed method is novel and innovative, providing a new perspective on Bayesian filtering by framing it as an optimization problem.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results, which are supported by a solid theoretical foundation.
- The experiments conducted are robust and comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in both low-dimensional and high-dimensional settings.
- The paper provides a clear and concise explanation of the methodology, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear distinction from existing works, particularly those by Santos (1996) and Khan & Rue (2023), which also explore similar methodologies.
- The paper's presentation and clarity could be improved, with some sections being overly complex and difficult to follow.
- The novelty of the paper is questionable, as the approach of using optimization to approximate the MAP estimate of the posterior distribution is not new.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in high-dimensional settings, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, particularly in more complex scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer discussion on the limitations and potential applications of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the main differences between their work and the works by Santos (1996) and Khan & Rue (2023)?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and practical applicability to existing methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings?
- Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the particle filter used in the experiments, including the number of particles and other relevant hyperparameters?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of misspecification of the process noise and dynamics, which is known to be a significant challenge in Bayesian filtering?
- Can the authors discuss the potential of extending the proposed method to more complex scenarios, such as non-stationary or nonlinear systems?
- How does the choice of the learning rate matrix affect the performance of the algorithm, and what are the implications of using different optimization algorithms like Adam or RMSprop?
