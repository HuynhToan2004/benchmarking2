 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GT-BEHRT, a novel method that integrates graph transformers with BERT-based models to address the challenges of electronic health record (EHR) data analysis. GT-BEHRT leverages temporal visit embeddings extracted from a graph transformer and uses these to enhance patient representations in BERT, effectively capturing both graphical and temporal relationships in EHR data. The approach is evaluated on various predictive medical tasks across multiple datasets, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods. The paper also includes a two-step pre-training strategy to enhance the model's ability to capture the intrinsic graphical structure of EHR data and the temporal relationships between visits.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach by integrating graph transformers with BERT-based models, which effectively captures the intrinsic graphical structure of EHR data and the temporal relationships between visits.
- The methodology is supported by extensive experiments on multiple datasets, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes detailed descriptions of the dataset and predictive tasks, ensuring reproducibility.
- The use of graph transformers and BERT-based models allows for the capture of both graphical and temporal relationships in EHR data, which is crucial for accurate predictions.
- The paper includes a two-step pre-training strategy that enhances the model's ability to capture the intrinsic graphical structure of EHR data and the temporal relationships between visits.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms other state-of-the-art baselines, especially when dealing with longer medical sequences.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the novelty and contribution of the proposed method compared to existing methods.
- The computational complexity of the proposed method is high, and there is no analysis or comparison of computational efficiency with other methods.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The pre-training tasks and their impact on the final performance are not adequately explained, and the necessity and effectiveness of these tasks are not clearly justified.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of datasets and the potential impact of dataset size and diversity on the model's performance.
- There are some typographical and formatting errors that need correction.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the specific contributions of the proposed method compared to existing methods, particularly in terms of novelty and effectiveness?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed method compare to other methods, and what are the implications of this complexity for practical applications?
- Can you provide more details on the pre-training tasks, particularly how they contribute to the final performance and whether there are alternatives that could be more effective?
- How does the model handle missing data in EHR records, and what are the implications of this for the model's performance?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of datasets and the potential impact of dataset size and diversity on the model's performance?
- How does the model perform when different pre-training tasks are used, and what are the implications of these choices for the model's performance on downstream tasks?
