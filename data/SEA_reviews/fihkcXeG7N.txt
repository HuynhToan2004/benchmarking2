 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Gate-guided and Subgraph-aware Bilateral Fusion (GSBF) model for molecular property prediction, which integrates both atom-wise and subgraph-wise information through two distinct branches within the model. The GSBF model employs a gate-guided mechanism to control the utilization of these branches and a decomposition-polymerization GNN architecture for the subgraph-wise branch. The authors propose cooperative node-level and graph-level self-supervised learning strategies to enhance the model's generalization. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the GSBF model, although it requires a large number of parameters and the selection of subgraphs is not adequately addressed.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by integrating both atom-wise and subgraph-wise information into two distinct branches within the model, which is a significant advancement in molecular property prediction.
- The Gate-guided and Subgraph-aware Bilateral Fusion (GSBF) model is proposed, which dynamically selects atomic-level or subgraph-level information, enhancing adaptability in information processing for molecular properties prediction.
- A decomposition-polymerization GNN architecture is introduced for the subgraph-wise branch, which can effectively extract invariant subgraph features.
- Cooperative node-level and graph-level self-supervised learning strategies are proposed to improve the GSBF model's generalization.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and results.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, and the results are promising.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the selection of subgraphs, which is crucial for the performance of the model.
- The novelty of the proposed method is not clearly articulated, and it appears to be a simple combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art models, which could undermine the credibility of the results.
- The computational cost of the proposed method is high due to the need to train two branches simultaneously, which could limit its practical application.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially when dealing with large molecules or a large number of subgraphs.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential improvements.

**Questions:**
- How are the subgraphs selected, and what is the impact of this selection on the model's performance?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art models to validate the effectiveness of their proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues when dealing with large molecules or a large number of subgraphs?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their proposed method and suggest potential areas for future research?
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent methods that also use gate-guided mechanisms for molecular property prediction?
