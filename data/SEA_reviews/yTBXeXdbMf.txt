 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the problem of preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL) where an agent learns a reward function from human feedback. The authors propose a method that separates the data collection phase from the human feedback phase, which is novel in the context of PbRL. They introduce a reward-agnostic algorithm that can handle linear reward parametrization and unknown transitions, showing that it requires fewer samples than previous algorithms. The paper also explores the action-based comparison setting, where the agent learns an advantage function directly, and demonstrates a sample complexity that scales with the bound of the advantage function. Theoretical results show that the proposed method can learn near-optimal policies with fewer samples compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the algorithms and theoretical results.
- The introduction of a reward-agnostic algorithm that can be applied to linear reward parametrization is a significant contribution to the field of preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL).
- The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the proposed algorithm, which is a significant advancement in the understanding and application of PbRL.
- The experimental design of the algorithm is novel, allowing for the separation of data collection from human feedback, which is a practical advantage in real-world applications.
- The paper presents a clear and concise explanation of the algorithms and theoretical results, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.
- The theoretical results show that the proposed algorithm can learn near-optimal policies with fewer samples compared to existing methods, which is a significant improvement in sample efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes a strong oracle that can learn an accurate model with a small error, which may not be realistic in practical scenarios where such oracles are not available.
- The assumption that the transition model is known in certain parts of the paper contradicts the claim of reward-agnostic data collection, as the algorithm requires knowledge of the transition model to collect data.
- The paper lacks empirical results, which could strengthen the claims made about the practical applicability and efficiency of the proposed algorithm.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed algorithm, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities and potential shortcomings.
- The paper does not adequately address how the proposed algorithm compares to existing methods in terms of computational complexity and scalability, which are crucial factors in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the assumption of a known transition model is consistent with the claim of reward-agnostic data collection?
- How does the proposed algorithm handle the case where the true reward function is not linear, or where the linear parametrization is not accurate?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed algorithm over existing methods in terms of computational complexity and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and results, particularly how the algorithm was tested in practical scenarios and how it compares to existing methods in terms of sample efficiency and computational requirements?
- How does the proposed algorithm handle the potential issues of label noise, which are common in PbRL applications?
