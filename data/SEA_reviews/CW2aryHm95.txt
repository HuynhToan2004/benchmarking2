 **Summary:**
The paper introduces WOLFI, a novel approach to adaptive bitrate selection (ABR) in video streaming using a combination of deep Q-learning and a supervised predictive model. The methodology involves training a Transmission Time Predictor (TTP) and a deep Q-network (DQN) on historical network traces to predict download times and optimize bitrate selection. The paper compares WOLFI with existing MPC-based methods and demonstrates its superiority in terms of quality of experience (QoE) metrics. The approach leverages the predictive capabilities of TTP to inform the DQN's decision-making process, aiming to enhance the user experience in video streaming. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its reliance on existing methodologies and its limited scalability and generalizability to different network conditions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, WOLFI, is simple and effective, with a straightforward architecture that is easy to implement.
- The evaluation is thorough and convincing, demonstrating that WOLFI outperforms existing MPC-based methods in terms of quality of experience (QoE) metrics.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including a comparison with other ABR algorithms and a discussion on the limitations and future research directions.
- The authors have released the code for their experiments, enhancing the reproducibility and transparency of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily combines existing methodologies (TTP and DQN) without significant innovation.
- The method's scalability and generalizability are questioned, as it relies heavily on specific network traces and conditions which may not be applicable in diverse or dynamic network environments.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and scalability of the TTP and DQN components, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios with large and varied network conditions.
- The paper's focus on a specific video streaming application may limit its broader applicability in other areas of network traffic optimization.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of its work, which could be critical for understanding the broader implications of the research.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the TTP and DQN components, especially in terms of how they handle varying network conditions and the size of the training dataset?
- How does WOLFI perform in scenarios where the network conditions are significantly different from those used in its training?
- Could you elaborate on the potential negative societal impacts of your work and how these were considered in your research?
- What are the specific challenges in extending the method to other types of network traffic optimization, and how does the method compare to other approaches in these contexts?
- How does the method handle the trade-off between video quality and buffering time, and what are the implications of this trade-off for user experience?
