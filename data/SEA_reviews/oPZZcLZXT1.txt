 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Transition Discriminator-based Imitation Learning (TDIL), a novel method for single-demonstration imitation learning in sparse-reward environments. TDIL addresses the challenge of learning from a single expert demonstration by approximating a surrogate reward function using a transition discriminator, which distinguishes between valid and non-valid transitions based on the reachability of states. This method, which is validated through experiments on MuJoCo benchmarks, aims to guide the agent towards expert states and improve the density of reward signals, thereby enhancing the learning process. The paper also discusses the potential of TDIL in practical applications such as autonomous vehicles, where the availability of multiple demonstrations might be limited.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed method and the experiments conducted.
- The proposed method is novel and technically sound, introducing a new approach to single-demonstration imitation learning (IL) by using a transition discriminator to approximate surrogate rewards.
- The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it outperforms existing IL methods across multiple benchmarks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive overview of the challenges in single-demonstration IL and introduces a practical and non-adversarial approach using a transition discriminator to implement the surrogate reward.
- The authors provide a blind model selection method for their proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how the discriminator is trained and the specifics of the training process, which could be crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness.
- The paper's experiments are limited to MuJoCo environments, and it is unclear how the proposed method would perform in more complex, real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to continuous control tasks and its reliance on the availability of a transition discriminator.
- The paper does not include a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could help in understanding the method's advantages and disadvantages more clearly.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the challenges and potential solutions in single-demonstration IL, particularly in high-dimensional, continuous environments.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed method perform in more complex, real-world scenarios, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in such environments?
- Can you provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, including their advantages and disadvantages in comparison to the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method address the challenges and limitations in single-demonstration IL, particularly in high-dimensional, continuous environments?
- Could you discuss the potential applications of the proposed method in more detail, particularly in practical scenarios such as autonomous vehicles?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of reward sparsity, and what are the implications of this for the learning process?
