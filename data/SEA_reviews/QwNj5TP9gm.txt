 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Evidential Conservative Q-Learning (ECQL) framework for dynamic recommendations, integrating evidence-based uncertainty and conservative learning to address the challenges of short-term focus in existing recommendation systems. ECQL leverages a sequential state encoder to aggregate historical information and current user interactions, along with newly recommended items, to generate a current state for the environment. The CEAC module, a novel conservative evidential-actor-critic, uses this state to perform evidence-based rating predictions and explores the item space based on an uncertainty-aware ranking score. The framework is evaluated on multiple real-world dynamic datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance and its ability to capture users' long-term interests. However, the paper lacks clarity in some sections, and there are concerns about the computational efficiency and the clarity of the experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and the authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis to justify the convergence behavior and recommendation quality of the proposed ECQL framework.
- The proposed ECQL framework integrates reinforcement learning with evidential learning to provide uncertainty-aware diverse recommendations that may reflect users' long-term interests.
- The experimental results show that the proposed ECQL framework outperforms other state-of-the-art methods on multiple real-world dynamic datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.
- The paper introduces a novel evidential uncertainty guided exploration to maximize information gain, which is a significant contribution to the field of recommendation systems.
- The CEAC module, a novel conservative evidential-actor-critic, is designed to perform evidence-based explorations and is integrated into the ECQL framework, enhancing its capability to discover items that align with users' long-term interests.
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis to justify the convergence behavior and recommendation quality of the proposed ECQL framework, which is a valuable addition to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the introduction and the theoretical analysis, which could benefit from clearer explanations and more detailed descriptions.
- The computational efficiency of the proposed ECQL framework is not discussed, which is crucial for practical applications where computational resources are limited.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the scalability of the ECQL framework, which is essential for understanding its applicability in large-scale recommendation systems.
- The experimental results are not convincing, particularly the comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, and the paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method.
- There are some grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper, which need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the societal impact of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for the deployment of such systems.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the sequential state encoder and the CEAC module, particularly how they are integrated into the ECQL framework?
- How does the ECQL framework handle the scalability issues in large-scale recommendation systems?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational efficiency of the ECQL framework?
- How does the proposed CEAC module compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, including the performance metrics and the experimental setups used?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these limitations might affect the practical deployment of the ECQL framework?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the societal impact of the proposed method, particularly in terms of privacy concerns and the potential for bias in recommendations?
