 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel hybrid distillation framework that integrates both supervised/contrastive learning (CL) and masked image modeling (MIM) to enhance the diversity and discrimination of student models. The framework leverages the strengths of both CL and MIM teachers, with the MIM teacher providing token relations at intermediate layers and the CL teacher providing final features. This methodology is supported by extensive experimental results that demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing distillation approaches. The paper also includes a comprehensive analysis of attention distance and mutual information, providing insights into the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel hybrid distillation framework that combines the strengths of both supervised/contrastive learning (CL) and masked image modeling (MIM) teachers, providing a new perspective on model training.
- The proposed methodology is well-motivated, clearly presented, and supported by extensive experimental results, demonstrating significant improvements in diversity and discrimination compared to previous methods.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of attention distance and mutual information, which is crucial for understanding the impact of different training strategies on model performance.
- The proposed progressive redundant token masking strategy effectively reduces distillation costs and prevents the model from falling into local optima.
- The experimental results are strong, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing distillation approaches on various downstream tasks, including classification, detection, and segmentation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of why the proposed method can effectively transfer both diversity and discrimination to the student side, especially considering the different pre-training paradigms of CL and MIM.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of its effectiveness.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential improvements.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and memory requirements of the hybrid distillation process.
- The paper's claims about the limitations of single-teacher distillation and the need for a hybrid approach are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of why the proposed method can effectively transfer both diversity and discrimination to the student side, especially in light of the different pre-training paradigms of CL and MIM?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational complexity and memory requirements?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential areas for future research?
- In the experimental results, why does the hybrid distillation method not show significant improvements in some cases, such as on the ImageNet-1K dataset?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential issues of overfitting, especially in the distillation process?
