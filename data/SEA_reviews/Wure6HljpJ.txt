 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CoSDA, a novel method for continual source-free domain adaptation, focusing on mitigating catastrophic forgetting in multi-target sequential adaptation. CoSDA employs a dual-speed optimized teacher-student model pair, incorporating consistency learning and mutual information loss to prevent forgetting. Extensive experiments across four benchmarks demonstrate that CoSDA outperforms state-of-the-art methods, showing significant improvements in transferability-stability trade-offs. The methodology is supported by a unified framework for evaluating various domain adaptation methods, providing a comprehensive analysis of existing approaches and their limitations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, CoSDA, which effectively addresses the challenges of continual source-free domain adaptation, particularly focusing on the mitigation of catastrophic forgetting.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with a clear presentation of the problem and a logical approach to designing the solution.
- The paper provides extensive experimental results and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various benchmarks and scenarios.
- The unified framework for evaluating domain adaptation methods is a significant contribution, facilitating a systematic analysis of existing approaches and their limitations.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the problem, methodology, and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in the context of continual domain adaptation and source-free domain adaptation.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques, such as mixup and mutual information loss, without significant innovation.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- There are some inconsistencies and potential errors in the experimental results, particularly in the presentation of accuracy metrics and the comparison with other methods.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed explanations and justifications for the methodological choices, such as the use of dual-speed optimization and the choice of hyperparameters.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in the context of continual domain adaptation and source-free domain adaptation?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability to other methods, such as those using dynamic architecture or parameter regularizations?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the mutual information loss in the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where it is not effective?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential issues of overfitting, especially in scenarios where the student model is updated more frequently?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the selection of hyperparameters and the impact of different choices on the results?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with a larger number of target domains, and what are the implications for the performance and generalization of the model?
