 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel disentanglement metric, LSEPIN, to address the limitations of Mutual Information Skill Learning (MISL) in unsupervised reinforcement learning (URL). It proposes a new skill-learning objective, WSEP, and an algorithm, PWSEP, based on Wasserstein distance to enhance the diversity and separability of learned skills. Theoretical analysis is provided to support the proposed methods, showing how they can potentially discover more optimal skills for downstream tasks. The paper also discusses the practical implications of these theoretical results, though some reviewers noted the complexity and potential impracticality of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel disentanglement metric, LSEPIN, which is a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised reinforcement learning (URL).
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust and supports the proposed methods, showing how the new objectives can potentially discover more optimal skills for downstream tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their theoretical underpinnings.
- The authors address a practical issue in URL by focusing on the diversity and separability of learned skills, which is crucial for task adaptation.
- The paper includes empirical examples to validate the theoretical results, enhancing the credibility of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is quite complex and dense, making it difficult to follow without extensive background knowledge in the field.
- The practical implications of the proposed methods are not clearly articulated, raising concerns about their applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from more concrete examples and simplified explanations to make the theoretical concepts more accessible to a broader audience.
- The paper's focus on the diversity and separability of learned skills is important but does not sufficiently address how these skills can be effectively applied in downstream tasks.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are somewhat limited by the existing literature, which may not sufficiently differentiate the proposed methods from prior works.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed methods, such as the potential for the learned skills to be less effective in downstream tasks compared to those learned by other methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the LSEPIN metric can be used to design better URL objectives?
- How does the proposed WSEP objective compare to existing methods like MISL, and what are the specific advantages of using WSEP?
- Could the authors provide more concrete examples or simplified explanations to help illustrate the practical implications of their theoretical results?
- How does the proposed method ensure that the learned skills are not only diverse and separable but also effective in downstream tasks?
- What are the specific contributions of the paper that differentiate it from prior works in the field of unsupervised skill learning?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential limitations of their proposed methods and how these might be addressed in future research?
