 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel evaluation framework for assessing the performance of large language models (LLMs) in the context of climate change information. The framework encompasses eight dimensions and 30 issues, covering both presentational and epistemological adequacy of answers. It leverages human annotators with relevant educational backgrounds and AI assistance from GPT-4 to assess the quality of LLM responses to climate change questions. The framework is designed to provide a scalable oversight mechanism for evaluating the accuracy, completeness, and clarity of LLM outputs. The paper also explores the utility of LLMs in generating responses to climate change questions and evaluates their performance against established criteria.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a timely and important topic, focusing on the use of large language models (LLMs) to address climate change information needs.
- The evaluation framework is comprehensive, covering eight dimensions and 30 issues, which provides a thorough assessment of LLM responses.
- The study is well-organized, clearly articulating the research question and methodology, making it accessible and understandable.
- The paper includes a diverse set of questions from different sources, which enhances the robustness of the evaluation.
- The authors provide a detailed description of their evaluation protocol, including the recruitment and training of human annotators, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The paper includes a novel protocol for scalable oversight that leverages AI assistance, which is a significant advancement in the evaluation of LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the challenges associated with evaluating LLMs, such as the difficulty in defining success and the potential for bias in the evaluation process.
- The evaluation framework primarily focuses on LLMs' ability to generate responses to climate change questions, without considering other potential applications like summarization or generation of answers.
- The paper does not explore the potential of using LLMs to generate answers directly from sources like Wikipedia, which could enhance the accuracy and completeness of the responses.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of bias in the evaluation process, which is crucial for ensuring the reliability and validity of the results.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a small set of general information needs, which may not adequately represent the full spectrum of climate change information needs.
- The paper's findings are not sufficiently generalizable, as it primarily evaluates responses to a limited set of questions and does not consider the impact of different LLM sizes or architectures.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the recruitment and training of human annotators, including the criteria used for selecting candidates and the process of training them to ensure consistency and accuracy in their evaluations?
- How do the authors address potential biases in the evaluation process, and what steps are taken to ensure the reliability and validity of the results?
- Given the importance of accuracy and completeness in the evaluation of LLM responses, how do the authors ensure that the generated responses are based on up-to-date and accurate information?
- What are the potential applications of LLMs in the context of climate change information, and how might these applications impact the effectiveness of the evaluation framework?
- How do the authors plan to address the limitations of the evaluation framework, particularly in terms of its generalizability and the potential for bias in the evaluation process?
