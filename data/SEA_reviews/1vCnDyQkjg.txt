 **Summary:**
The paper introduces UniHSI, a framework that enables human-scene interaction (HSI) by translating language commands into task plans using a large language model (LLM). The framework, named "Chain of Contacts (CoC)," represents interactions as ordered sequences of human-object contact pairs. UniHSI is composed of a high-level LLM Planner and a low-level Unified Controller, which convert language prompts into task plans and execute them, respectively. The paper also presents a new dataset, ScenePlan, which encompasses thousands of task plans based on diverse scenarios. The framework demonstrates versatility in task execution and generalizability to real-world scenes. However, concerns were raised about the complexity of the method, the limited evaluation on real-world scenes, and the lack of comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, UniHSI, which supports unified control of diverse interactions through language commands, representing interactions as "Chain of Contacts (CoC)."
- The methodology is clearly presented, with a well-structured paper that is easy to follow, and the results are well-presented with clear figures and tables.
- The introduction of a new dataset, ScenePlan, which encompasses thousands of task plans based on diverse scenarios, provides a valuable resource for the research community.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed framework in versatile task execution and generalizability to real scanned scenes.
- The framework's ability to translate language prompts into task plans in the form of CoC and execute these plans using a Unified Controller shows promise in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The methodology is complex and requires a significant amount of human effort to design and train, which may limit its practical applicability.
- The evaluation is limited to synthetic data, which may not adequately reflect the performance of the method in real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of interaction annotation-free training and long-horizon transition control.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as the complexity of the method and the potential for errors in LLM-generated task plans.
- The paper does not sufficiently address how the method handles multi-object interactions, which is a critical aspect of human-scene interaction.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as the complexity of the method and the potential for errors in LLM-generated task plans.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the role of the TaskParser in the methodology, particularly how it interprets LLM-generated task plans and translates them into actionable steps?
- How does the method handle multi-object interactions? Can you provide examples or additional details on how this is managed?
- In the evaluation, how are the metrics calculated, and how do they reflect the performance of the method in real-world scenarios?
- Could you provide more details on the training process for the LLM Planner and the Unified Controller? How long does it take, and what resources are required?
- How does the method ensure the physical plausibility of the generated motions, particularly in real-world scenarios where the environment is not perfectly simulated?
- Could you discuss the potential impact of using different LLMs on the performance of the method? How does the method handle errors or inaccuracies in the LLM-generated task plans?
