 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to text generation by formalizing it as a future-constrained generation problem, aiming to minimize undesirable behaviors and enhance faithfulness to instructions. The authors propose a method that utilizes a pre-trained language model to estimate future constraint satisfaction scores, which guide the generation process. This method is evaluated across three distinct text generation tasks: keyword-constrained generation, toxicity reduction, and factual correctness in question-answering. The results show that the proposed method outperforms baseline methods in these tasks, although the evaluation primarily relies on automatic metrics and lacks comprehensive human evaluations. The paper also discusses the method's computational efficiency and its ability to guide the generation process towards desired behaviors, although it does not address the limitations of the approach, such as the potential for overfitting and the generalizability of the method across different types of constraints.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive introduction of the proposed approach.
- The proposed method is simple, efficient, and effective, as demonstrated by experimental results on keyword-constrained generation, toxicity reduction, and factual correctness in question-answering.
- The use of LLMs to estimate the future constraint satisfaction score is innovative and provides a novel approach to text generation.
- The paper includes a human evaluation on a challenging long-form QA task, which supports the effectiveness of the proposed decoding method.
- The method is applicable to a wide range of text generation tasks and can be integrated into existing text generation pipelines.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation primarily relies on automatic metrics, which may not fully capture the quality of the generated text, particularly in terms of fluency and coherence.
- There is a lack of comprehensive human evaluations to assess the quality of the generated text, which is crucial for understanding the practical utility of the proposed method.
- The method's generalizability across different types of constraints and its ability to handle complex or contradictory constraints is not thoroughly explored.
- The computational cost of the proposed method is higher than traditional sampling methods, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as potential overfitting or the impact of different types of constraints on the effectiveness of the method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the human evaluation, such as the number of annotators, the inter-annotator agreement, and the specific questions or criteria used for evaluation?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the constraint is complex or contradictory?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the effectiveness of the method in real-world applications?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use LLMs for constraint satisfaction estimation, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method, such as overfitting or the impact of different types of constraints on the method's effectiveness?
