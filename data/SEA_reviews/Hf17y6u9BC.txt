 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the effects of different hyperparameters and methods on the interpretability of language models using activation patching. It examines two corruption methods, Gaussian Noise (GN) and Symmetric Token Replacement (STR), and two metrics, probability and logit difference, to assess the impact on the localization of model components. The study explores how these variations affect the model's performance and interpretability, particularly focusing on the GPT-2 model. The paper also discusses the potential for Gaussian Noise to introduce out-of-distribution (OOD) data, which could affect the reliability of the interpretability results. It concludes by recommending STR over GN and logit difference over probability for better interpretability outcomes.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of the existing literature on activation patching and its applications in language model interpretability, highlighting the need for standardized methods and metrics.
- The study is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the experimental setups and results, which are supported by intuitive visualizations and a clear presentation of findings.
- The authors have conducted a systematic study on the effects of different hyperparameters and methods in activation patching, which is significant for the field of mechanistic interpretability.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear recommendations for best practices in activation patching, which could be beneficial for practitioners seeking to improve the interpretability of their language models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The study primarily focuses on the GPT-2 model, which might limit the generalizability of the findings to other models or architectures.
- The paper could benefit from a broader range of models and tasks to validate the proposed best practices more comprehensively.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential biases introduced by the selection of prompts for corruption, which could affect the reliability of the interpretability results.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the proposed best practices, such as the potential for overfitting or the impact of different model architectures.
- The paper's recommendations for best practices are not sufficiently justified with empirical evidence, which could undermine their credibility.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods, such as the potential for misuse in biased or discriminatory applications.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the selection of prompts for corruption affects the reliability of the interpretability results, and how this might be mitigated?
- How do the proposed best practices perform on larger or more complex models, such as GPT-3 or larger models?
- Are there any potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods, and how might these be mitigated?
- How do the authors address the potential for overfitting in the models used in their experiments, and what steps have been taken to ensure the generalizability of the findings?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup and results for the "greater-than" task, and how these relate to the overall findings of the study?
- How do the authors justify the use of specific metrics, such as logit difference, over others like probability or KL divergence, and what empirical evidence supports these choices?
