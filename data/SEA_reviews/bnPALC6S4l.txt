 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, SNAP-DDM, which leverages domain decomposition methods (DDMs) to solve partial differential equations (PDEs) using neural operators. This approach decomposes the global domain into smaller subdomains, each solved by specialized neural operators trained for specific subdomain problems. The method is tested on electromagnetic problems, demonstrating its ability to handle complex PDEs with high accuracy and efficiency. The authors claim that this method can potentially scale to larger and more complex PDEs, although the evaluation is currently limited to 2D electromagnetic simulations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The methodology, including the use of domain decomposition methods and neural operators, is innovative and shows potential for significant impact in the field.
- The experiments are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in the context of 2D electromagnetic simulations.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the methodology and includes a comprehensive evaluation, which supports the claims made.
- The use of neural operators to solve PDEs is a promising approach, and the paper provides a significant contribution to this area by introducing a novel method that combines domain decomposition with neural operators.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of scalability and generalizability to more complex PDEs.
- The evaluation is limited to 2D electromagnetic simulations, which may not fully demonstrate the method's capabilities in more complex or higher-dimensional scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the related work, particularly in the area of neural operators and domain decomposition methods.
- The methodology section could be expanded to provide more details on the implementation and training of the neural operators, including the specifics of the neural network architectures used.
- There is a need for more extensive experiments to validate the claims made about the method's scalability and generalizability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the method and how these might affect its scalability and applicability to more complex PDEs?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of accuracy and efficiency, particularly in more complex or higher-dimensional scenarios?
- Can the authors elaborate on the specific neural network architectures used in the training of the neural operators and how these were chosen?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the boundary conditions are not known in advance, or where the PDEs are more complex than those tested in the current experiments?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending the method to include adaptive subdivision of domains based on the complexity of the PDEs?
- How does the method handle the variability in PDE coefficients and boundary conditions, particularly in scenarios where these are not known in advance?
