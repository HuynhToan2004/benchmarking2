 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for variational inference using a diffusion model in the latent space, which involves the introduction of auxiliary latent variables to enhance the expressiveness of the variational posterior. This approach is inspired by the wake-sleep algorithm and aims to optimize a lower bound on the marginal likelihood. The method, referred to as denoising diffusion variational inference, is tested on various datasets including MNIST, CIFAR-10, and 1000 Genomes, showing improvements over existing methods. The paper also discusses extensions to semi-supervised learning and clustering. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the method's implementation, the necessity of the proposed objective, and the generalizability of the results, particularly in more complex datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to variational inference using diffusion models in the latent space, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it accessible and understandable, which is crucial for reproducibility.
- The paper demonstrates empirical improvements over existing methods such as VAE, AAE, and IAF-VAE, showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of diffusion models in latent space for variational inference is a novel and interesting approach that has the potential to enhance the expressiveness of variational posteriors.
- The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method, which is beneficial for readers to understand the contributions and implications of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear justification for the necessity of the proposed objective, particularly in terms of its impact on the expressiveness of the variational posterior and the overall performance of the model.
- There are several unclear aspects in the methodology, such as the role and definition of auxiliary latent variables and the specifics of the diffusion process in the latent space.
- The experimental results are limited to simpler datasets like MNIST and CIFAR-10, raising concerns about the generalizability of the method to more complex datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its method from existing approaches, particularly those that use similar components like auxiliary latent variables and diffusion models in latent space.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for a complete understanding of its applicability and potential challenges.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and definition of auxiliary latent variables in the context of the proposed method?
- How does the diffusion process in the latent space affect the expressiveness of the variational posterior, and what are the specific advantages of using this approach over other methods?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the training time and inference speed, to better assess the computational efficiency of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform on more complex datasets, such as those used in large-scale applications like ImageNet?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential limitations of the proposed method and discuss how these might affect its practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of expressiveness and computational efficiency, particularly in scenarios where computational resources are limited?
