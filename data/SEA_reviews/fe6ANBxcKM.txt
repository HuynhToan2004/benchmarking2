 **Summary:**
The paper explores federated reinforcement learning (FL) in the context of tabular episodic Markov Decision Processes (MDPs), where multiple agents collaborate to learn optimal policies without sharing raw data. It introduces two federated Q-learning algorithms, FedQ-Hoeffding and FedQ-Bernstein, which achieve linear regret speedup and logarithmic communication cost. These algorithms employ event-triggered synchronization mechanisms and adaptive exploration policies to optimize learning efficiency. The paper also discusses the technical challenges in proving linear regret speedup and logarithmic communication cost, and provides theoretical guarantees for these properties.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces two new federated Q-learning algorithms, FedQ-Hoeffding and FedQ-Bernstein, which achieve linear regret speedup and logarithmic communication cost in the federated learning setting.
- The algorithms are well-designed, with a focus on reducing communication cost by requiring each client to send only the empirical average of the estimated values of the next states after visiting a state-action pair.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the algorithms and their theoretical guarantees, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors demonstrate a linear regret speedup compared to their single-agent counterparts, which is a significant achievement in the field of federated reinforcement learning.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison of the proposed algorithms with related works, highlighting their contributions and improvements over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the server and agents are fully synchronized, which may not hold in practical federated learning scenarios where agents may have different data arrival rates.
- The communication cost analysis is limited to the number of scalars communicated, which might not adequately capture the full communication cost, especially in terms of bandwidth and latency.
- The algorithms are only applicable to tabular MDPs, which limits their applicability to more complex environments.
- The paper lacks empirical evaluations, which could help validate the theoretical claims and provide practical insights into the algorithms' performance.
- There is a lack of discussion on the convergence of the algorithms, which is crucial for understanding their practical applicability.
- The paper does not adequately address the challenges of non-i.i.d. data in federated learning, which is a significant issue in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- How do the algorithms perform in scenarios where agents have different data arrival rates?
- Can you provide more details on the convergence of the algorithms, especially in terms of the number of rounds required for convergence?
- How does the proposed event-triggered synchronization mechanism impact the performance of the algorithms?
- Can the algorithms be extended to more complex environments beyond tabular MDPs?
- How do the algorithms handle non-i.i.d. data, which is common in federated learning scenarios?
- Could you clarify the notation and definitions used in the paper, especially those related to the MDP and the algorithms' parameters?
- How does the paper address the potential issues of non-i.i.d. data in the federated learning setting?
