 **Summary:**
The paper explores the relationship between adversarial examples and the eigenvalues of the kernel matrix associated with a neural network. It argues that adversarial examples, particularly those that are imperceptible and close to original data points, can be characterized by their ability to induce near-zero eigenvalues in the kernel matrix. This is supported by theoretical proofs and numerical experiments on a subset of the MNIST dataset. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for understanding the generalization capabilities of neural networks and the vulnerability of models to adversarial attacks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-explained theoretical framework.
- The theoretical results are novel and contribute significantly to the understanding of adversarial examples and their relationship to the eigenvalues of the kernel matrix.
- The paper includes rigorous proofs of theorems and provides a clear explanation of the mathematical underpinnings, making it accessible to readers not specialized in the field.
- The connection between adversarial examples and the eigenvalues of the kernel matrix is innovative and provides a new perspective on the behavior of neural networks in the presence of adversarial inputs.
- The paper includes a thorough discussion of related work and positions itself well within the existing literature, highlighting its contributions and the novelty of its approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions, such as training to zero error and the assumption of a kernel, might limit the generalizability of the results to other types of neural networks or datasets.
- The experiments are limited in scope, using a small subset of MNIST, which might not fully validate the theoretical claims.
- The paper's reliance on specific assumptions, such as the Lipschitz continuity of the empirical feature map, might not hold for all types of neural networks, limiting the applicability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its assumptions, such as the assumption of a kernel and the training of the network to zero error, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the assumptions and their implications, as well as a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the results would generalize to other types of neural networks, such as convolutional neural networks?
- How does the paper's approach relate to existing works on adversarial examples, particularly those that suggest adversarial examples are not out-of-distribution?
- Could the authors provide more detailed justification for the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the assumption of a kernel and the training to zero error?
- How does the paper's approach differ from or complement other theoretical works on adversarial examples, such as those that focus on the linearity hypothesis or the role of spurious features?
- Can the authors elaborate on the practical implications of their findings, particularly how they might be applied to improve the robustness of neural networks against adversarial attacks?
