 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel feature shaping method for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection, leveraging a piecewise constant shaping function optimized to maximize the expected difference between the maximum logit values for in-distribution (ID) and OOD data. This approach aims to improve the generalization ability of OOD detection across various datasets and model architectures, even when OOD data is inaccessible. The methodology includes a formulation of an abstract optimization framework and a reduction to a concrete problem with a closed-form solution for the piecewise constant shaping function. Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing feature-shaping methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear and concise explanation of the proposed methods.
- The proposed method is novel and addresses an important problem in OOD detection, specifically focusing on the generalization ability of feature shaping methods.
- The optimization problem and the closed-form solution are well-defined, and the paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper includes a thorough analysis of the impact of different feature values on OOD detection, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of the feature shaping function.
- The proposed method is shown to outperform existing feature shaping methods across a variety of datasets and model architectures, highlighting its versatility and applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with the most recent feature-shaping methods, which could limit the understanding of its novelty and effectiveness.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the piecewise constant approximation for the shaping function, may not hold true for all feature-shaping methods, particularly those that use whole-vector mappings.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other OOD detection methods, such as classifier training with surrogate OOD datasets.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets and model architectures, which might not fully demonstrate the generalization ability of the proposed method across different domains.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the choice of the shaping function and its impact on the performance of the feature shaping method?
- How does the proposed method perform when combined with other OOD detection methods like ReAct and DICE, as mentioned in the paper?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly regarding the piecewise constant approximation for the shaping function?
- How does the proposed method handle the generalization ability across different datasets and model architectures, especially those not included in the experiments?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of the proposed method in improving the performance of OOD detection methods that use surrogate OOD datasets for training?
