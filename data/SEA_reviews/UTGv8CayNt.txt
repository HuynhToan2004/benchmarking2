 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to hierarchical imitation learning for low-level control tasks, leveraging a chain-of-thought (CoT) decoding strategy to predict subgoals from demonstrations. The methodology involves an unsupervised discovery of subgoals using a PELT-like method and a transformer architecture that learns to predict these subgoals alongside actions. The approach is evaluated on various tasks including Moving Maze, Franka Kitchen, and ManiSkill2, demonstrating superior performance over baseline methods. The paper also includes ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of the proposed components.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the methodology and experiments, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed method, CoTPC, shows superior performance over existing baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness in solving low-level control tasks.
- The use of an unsupervised discovery of subgoals and the transformer-based architecture for learning from demonstrations is innovative and contributes to the field of hierarchical imitation learning.
- The paper includes thorough ablation studies that validate the effectiveness of the proposed components, such as the CoT decoder and the hybrid masking strategy.
- The experimental results are robust, showing the method's ability to handle a variety of tasks and settings, including unseen and 0-shot scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where it might not perform optimally.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and scalability to more complex tasks.
- There is a need for more quantitative and qualitative evaluations, particularly in the ablation studies and on more complex tasks, to fully validate the effectiveness of the proposed components.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the CoT discovery process across different tasks and environments, which could limit the practical applicability of the method.
- The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the CoT discovery process and its implications for the performance of the method in various scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the CoTPC method perform on more complex tasks such as those in the D4RL benchmark, and what are the potential challenges in scaling up the method?
- Could you provide more details on how the CoT discovery process is adapted for different tasks and environments, and how this affects the performance of the method?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the subgoals are not clearly defined or where the action space is continuous?
- What are the specific challenges in extending the method to more complex tasks, and how do these compare to the challenges faced by other hierarchical imitation learning methods?
- Can you elaborate on the method's ability to handle different types of demonstrations, such as those generated by planners versus those from humans or RL agents?
- How does the method handle the generalization of the CoT discovery process across different tasks, and what are the implications for the scalability and applicability of the method?
