 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the effectiveness of Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) and Maximum Weighted Likelihood Estimation (MWLE) in scenarios of covariate shift, a type of out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization problem. It demonstrates that under specific conditions, such as well-specified models and bounded density ratios, MLE can achieve minimax optimality. The paper also explores the mis-specified model scenario, where MLE may not be optimal, and MWLE emerges as the preferred choice. The study is supported by theoretical analyses and examples, including linear and logistic regression, and phase retrieval, to illustrate the practical implications of these theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel perspective on the use of Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) and Maximum Weighted Likelihood Estimation (MWLE) in scenarios of covariate shift, which is a significant contribution to the field of out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-defined assumptions and clear proofs, which are supported by examples in linear and logistic regression and phase retrieval.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed discussion on the assumptions and their implications, which aids in understanding the results.
- The paper addresses a fundamental question in machine learning, specifically concerning the most effective algorithms for OOD generalization in covariate shift scenarios, and provides a clear answer to this question.
- The paper is of significant interest to the community, as it tackles a fundamental question in machine learning and provides a clear answer, which is expected to be of interest to both academia and industry.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions are quite strong, which might limit the generalizability of the results. For instance, the assumption of a well-specified model might not hold in all practical scenarios, and the assumption of bounded density ratios is not necessary for MLE to be minimax optimal.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the implications of the assumptions, particularly Assumption A.3, which is crucial for the results. More detailed discussion on the practical implications and potential relaxations of this assumption would be beneficial.
- The paper lacks empirical validation of the theoretical results, which could strengthen the claims made about the performance of MLE and MWLE in practical settings.
- The paper's scope is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on linear regression and logistic regression, which might not be generalizable to more complex models like deep neural networks.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the assumptions, which could affect the applicability of the results in more general or realistic scenarios.
