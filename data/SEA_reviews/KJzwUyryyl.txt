 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ALMANACS, a novel benchmark for evaluating the simulatability of explanations generated by language models. The benchmark utilizes a large language model (LLM) to predict the behavior of a model based on the explanations, aiming to assess the effectiveness of various explanation methods such as counterfactuals, rationalizations, attention, and Integrated Gradients. The benchmark includes 12 safety-relevant topics and 15 templates for each, generating a total of 18,000 test examples. Despite the innovative approach, the paper's evaluation is limited to a single model, GPT-4, and does not include a direct comparison with human evaluators, raising concerns about the generalizability and validity of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmark, ALMANACS, which is the first to evaluate the simulatability of explanations generated by language models, using a large language model (LLM) to predict the model's behavior based on the explanations.
- The benchmark includes a diverse set of 12 safety-relevant topics and 15 templates for each, generating a total of 18,000 test examples, which can be used to evaluate various explanation methods.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of different explanation methods such as counterfactuals, rationalizations, attention, and Integrated Gradients, which can help in understanding the effectiveness of these methods in improving the simulatability of explanations.
- The use of a large language model (LLM) for automatic evaluation of explanations is innovative and could potentially reduce the time and cost associated with human studies.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and provides a detailed description of the benchmark design, including the simulatability pipeline and the evaluation metrics used.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a single model, GPT-4, which may not generalize to other models or human evaluators, raising concerns about the validity and generalizability of the results.
- The use of KL divergence as a metric for evaluating the simulatability of explanations is questionable, as it does not necessarily correlate with human evaluations of the explanations' quality or simulatability.
- The paper does not provide a direct comparison between the LLM-based evaluation and human evaluations, which is crucial for validating the reliability of the results.
- The paper does not consider the impact of different model sizes and capabilities on the simulatability of explanations, which could be a significant factor in the effectiveness of the explanations.
- The benchmark's focus on simulatability as the primary metric for evaluating explanations may not fully capture the complexity of the explanations, as it does not consider other important properties such as faithfulness, plausibility, and minimalism.
- The paper's assumption that LLMs can accurately evaluate explanations is not convincingly supported, and the reliability of LLMs in this role needs further validation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the process of selecting the 15 templates per topic where generalization is most difficult?
- How does the paper ensure that the explanations generated by the LLM are not overly simplistic or biased towards specific types of explanations?
- What are the specific instructions given to the LLM when it is used to predict the model's behavior based on the explanations?
- How does the paper address the potential biases or limitations of the LLM in evaluating the simulatability of explanations?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison between the LLM-based evaluation and human evaluations to validate the reliability of the results?
- How does the paper ensure that the explanations generated by the LLM are not overly simplistic or biased towards specific types of explanations?
- Could the authors consider incorporating other metrics beyond simulatability to evaluate the explanations, such as faithfulness, plausibility, and minimalism?
