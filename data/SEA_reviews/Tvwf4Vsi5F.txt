 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel threat model, Transfer Attack with Public Models (TAPM), which assumes an adversary with limited resources can only query a target model without access to its weights. The authors propose a defense mechanism, PubMed, which is trained using a game-theoretic approach to maximize robustness against transfer attacks from publicly available models. The defense is evaluated against 24 public models and 11 attack algorithms across three datasets, demonstrating significant improvements in robustness with minimal clean accuracy loss. The paper also explores the generalization of adversarial examples across different models and training methods, suggesting a potential low-dimensional subspace for transfer adversarial examples.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel threat model, TAPM, which is realistic and practical, addressing the challenges of defending against transfer attacks from publicly available models.
- The proposed defense method, PubMed, is innovative and effective, demonstrating significant improvements in robustness against transfer attacks without significant loss in clean accuracy.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides extensive experimental results, making it accessible and informative.
- The introduction of game theory to analyze the attacker's strategy is a novel approach that adds depth to the defense mechanism.
- The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed defense across various datasets and attack algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed defense, which is crucial for understanding its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The assumption of equal access to both the attacker and the defender regarding the set of publicly available models could be a significant limitation, as it might not hold in practical scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the findings to other types of attacks or datasets beyond those tested.
- The paper's reliance on a single defense mechanism, PubMed, might limit its applicability in more complex or varied attack scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational costs and practical implications of implementing the defense mechanism.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed defense mechanism, PubMed, perform against other types of attacks beyond those tested in the paper?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational costs and practical implications of implementing the defense mechanism in real-world scenarios?
- Is there a possibility to extend the game-theoretic analysis to other defense mechanisms, and how might this affect the defense's effectiveness?
- How does the paper address the issue of generalizability, especially in scenarios where the attacker might have access to different or more diverse models?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the effectiveness of the defense mechanism against a wider range of attack algorithms, including those not tested in the current study?
