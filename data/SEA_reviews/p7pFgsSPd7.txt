 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Sample-aware RandAugment (SRA), a search-free data augmentation method designed to enhance the performance of neural networks by dynamically adjusting the augmentation policy based on the difficulty of the training data. SRA uses a heuristic score-based module, the Magnitude Instructor Score (MIS), to evaluate the difficulty of each sample and guide appropriate augmentation strategies. This method is tested across various datasets, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet, demonstrating competitive or better performance compared to search-based methods. The method's simplicity and effectiveness have been validated through extensive experiments, showing improvements over existing methods like RandAugment. However, concerns about the novelty of the approach and the need for further validation on larger datasets like JFT-350 are highlighted.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, Sample-aware RandAugment (SRA), is simple, effective, and shows promising results, especially on the ImageNet dataset.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with various baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of SRA.
- The method's heuristic score-based module, Magnitude Instructor Score (MIS), is innovative and provides a new approach to sample-aware augmentation.
- The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies and analysis, which support the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method's simplicity and ease of implementation make it attractive for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it seems to be a simple modification of existing methods like RandAugment.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity or the time cost of the proposed method, which could be critical for practical applications.
- The method's effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated on larger datasets like JFT-350, which could undermine the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of detailed theoretical analysis to support the claims made about the method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for overfitting due to the augmentation of hard samples, which could lead to a biased represented distribution of the original training data.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to illustrate how the proposed method differs from existing methods like RandAugment?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting due to the augmentation of hard samples?
- Can the authors provide more experimental results, such as on larger datasets like JFT-350, to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method on a broader scale?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and time cost with other existing methods?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the hyperparameter γ in the proposed method?
- Could the authors provide more detailed theoretical analysis or experimental evidence to support the claims made about the method's effectiveness?
