 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel pre-training method called Split and Merge Proxy (SMP) for protein inter-chain contact prediction, leveraging monomer data to create pseudo multimer data. This approach aims to address the data volume bottleneck in multimer data, which is typically more limited than monomer data. The method involves splitting monomer data into pseudo multimers, training the model to merge them back together, and then fine-tuning the model on real multimer data. The SMP method shows significant improvements over existing methods like GeoTrans, particularly on the DIPS-Plus and CASP-CAPRI benchmarks. The approach is also evaluated on other multimer-related tasks such as docking and interaction site prediction, demonstrating its versatility.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of monomer data into multimer-related tasks is a novel and interesting approach that effectively utilizes existing data resources.
- The proposed method, SMP, is straightforward and effective, demonstrating significant improvements in protein inter-chain contact prediction tasks compared to existing methods.
- The paper provides extensive experiments and ablation studies, which validate the effectiveness of the proposed method and demonstrate its applicability to other multimer-related tasks.
- The code, model, and pre-training data will be released, ensuring reproducibility and facilitating further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the SMP method is limited as it primarily involves splitting monomer data into pseudo multimers and training the model to merge them back together, which is a standard data augmentation technique.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other pre-training paradigms, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the SMP's effectiveness.
- There is a concern about the potential data leakage due to the use of the same dataset for pre-training and testing, which could affect the fairness and validity of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the SMP method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the specific architecture used for the contact prediction head and the parameters of the interleave module.
- The paper does not compare the SMP method with other pre-training methods like masked language model (MLM) proxy tasks, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the SMP's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific architecture used for the contact prediction head and the parameters of the interleave module?
- How does the SMP method compare with other pre-training methods like masked language model (MLM) proxy tasks, and what are the implications of using different pre-training paradigms?
- How does the SMP method handle the potential data leakage due to using the same dataset for pre-training and testing?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the SMP method and its applicability in different scenarios?
- How does the SMP method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements?
