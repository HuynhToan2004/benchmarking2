 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FLASK, a fine-grained evaluation protocol for assessing the performance of large language models (LLMs) in instruction-following tasks. FLASK decomposes coarse-level scoring into skill set-level scoring, enabling a more detailed analysis of LLM performance. It evaluates both open-source and proprietary models using a set of 12 skills, which are further divided into 4 primary abilities. The evaluation is conducted through both human and automated evaluations, and the results show a high correlation between these two methods. The paper also discusses the limitations of current evaluation methods and proposes a new approach to address these issues.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The authors propose a novel evaluation protocol that considers a wide range of skills, which is a significant improvement over previous methods.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of various models, including both open-source and proprietary models, which is valuable for understanding the performance of different models.
- The use of LLMs for annotation and evaluation is innovative and potentially cost-effective, although the paper acknowledges the limitations of this approach.
- The paper presents a large-scale evaluation using both human and automated methods, which is valuable for understanding the performance of LLMs in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the "skills" used in the evaluation, which could lead to confusion about what is being measured.
- The methodology for selecting the skills and the criteria for their selection is not clearly explained, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its approach, particularly the potential biases introduced by the use of LLMs for annotation and evaluation.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of central tendency bias in human annotation, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the quality control measures used in the human evaluation, which is crucial for ensuring the validity of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the evaluation process, particularly in terms of the time and resources required for manual evaluation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and selection criteria for the "skills" used in the evaluation?
- How do the authors ensure the reliability and validity of the evaluations conducted by LLMs, particularly in cases where the LLMs may not fully understand the instructions or criteria for evaluation?
- What steps are taken to address the potential biases introduced by the use of LLMs for annotation and evaluation?
- How does the paper ensure the quality of the human evaluations, particularly in terms of inter-rater agreement and the reliability of the annotations?
- Could the authors provide more details on the time and resource requirements for conducting the manual evaluations, and discuss potential strategies for scaling up the evaluation process?
- How does the paper address the potential for central tendency bias in human annotation, and what steps are taken to mitigate this bias?
