 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an offline actor-critic framework named Offline-AC, specifically designed for traffic signal control (TSC) applications. It proposes an adaptive decision-making model named OfflineLight, which leverages offline RL techniques to address challenges in TSC, such as data distribution shifts and generalization capabilities. The framework is evaluated against various state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness. The paper also introduces a new dataset, TSC-OID, which is crucial for further research in this area. Despite its innovative approach and the release of a new dataset, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty and lack of detailed comparative analysis with other offline RL methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel offline actor-critic framework (Offline-AC) specifically designed for traffic signal control (TSC), which is a significant contribution to the field.
- It proposes a new adaptive decision-making model named OfflineLight, which leverages the Offline-AC framework to address challenges in TSC.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the presentation is clear, with figures and tables effectively illustrating the results.
- The release of the new dataset, TSC-OID, is a valuable contribution to the community, providing a valuable resource for further research.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness and robustness of the proposed method through extensive evaluations against state-of-the-art methods, showing the superiority of the OfflineLight model.
- The introduction of offline RL to TSC is a promising approach, as it can potentially reduce the computational and environmental costs associated with traditional online RL methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other offline RL methods, which could have provided a clearer understanding of the relative advantages of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing methods (CQL and TD3) without significant new contributions.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational costs and scalability of the proposed method, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the data distribution issues and the choice of KL divergence over other distance functions.
- The generalizability of the proposed method to other domains beyond TSC is not clearly established.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other offline RL methods to better understand the relative advantages of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method address the data distribution issues in TSC, and what are the potential implications for other domains?
- Why was KL divergence chosen over other distance functions, and how does it impact the performance and generalizability of the method?
- Can the authors clarify the computational costs and scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of its applicability to larger, more complex TSC problems?
- How does the proposed method compare to other offline RL methods in terms of training speed and convergence, and what are the implications for practical deployment in real-world scenarios?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their proposed method, especially in terms of privacy and security concerns related to the use of traffic data?
