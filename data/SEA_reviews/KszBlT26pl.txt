 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) method that leverages the Fokker-Planck equation to estimate the reward function in a Markov Decision Process (MDP) without requiring the transition function. This approach is based on the assumption that the dynamics of the system can be described by the Fokker-Planck equation, which implies a specific form of the reward function. The method is tested on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in estimating the reward function. However, the paper's theoretical underpinnings and the practical applicability of the method, especially in complex or high-dimensional systems, are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) by using the Fokker-Planck equation to estimate the reward function, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear theoretical derivations and empirical results that support the claims made.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The application of the method to real-world problems, such as cancer cell migration, demonstrates the practical utility of the approach.
- The paper provides a comprehensive overview of related work, showing a thorough understanding of the existing literature in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the system dynamics can be described by the Fokker-Planck equation, which may not be true for all systems, especially complex or high-dimensional ones.
- The empirical results are not convincing, with some figures, such as Figure 2, showing significant discrepancies between the inferred reward function and the ground truth.
- The methodology section lacks clarity in explaining how the reward function is inferred from the Fokker-Planck equation, which could be improved with additional examples or clarifications.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical guarantees or empirical evidence to support the claim that the method can handle high-dimensional or complex systems.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincing, with some figures showing significant discrepancies or insufficient detail to support the claims made.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive experimental validation, including comparisons with existing methods and more detailed analysis of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the reward function is inferred from the Fokker-Planck equation, possibly with additional examples or clarifications?
- How does the method handle high-dimensional or complex systems, and what theoretical or empirical evidence supports this claim?
- What are the specific advantages of using the Fokker-Planck equation over other methods for estimating the reward function in IRL?
- How does the method perform in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially in high-dimensional or complex systems?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, possibly including a benchmark against a variety of IRL methods?
- How does the method handle the assumption of periodic boundary conditions, and what implications does this have for the applicability of the method in different domains?
