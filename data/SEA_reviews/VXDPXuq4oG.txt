 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Order-Preserving GFlowNets (OP-GFNs), a novel approach to multi-objective optimization (MOO) in generative flow networks (GFlowNets). OP-GFNs do not require a predefined scalar reward function; instead, they learn a reward function based on a partial order on candidates, which is consistent with a provided partial order on the candidates. This approach allows for efficient exploration and exploitation in both single-objective maximization and multi-objective Pareto approximation tasks. The paper presents theoretical proofs and empirical evaluations demonstrating the advantages of OP-GFNs over traditional GFlowNets and other methods in various environments, including synthetic datasets, molecule generation, and neural architecture search.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The introduction of Order-Preserving GFlowNets (OP-GFNs) is novel and significant, providing a new approach to multi-objective optimization (MOO) in GFlowNets without requiring a predefined scalar reward function.
- The theoretical proofs provided are sound, and the empirical evaluations are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of OP-GFNs across various environments.
- The paper addresses the limitations of existing GFlowNet methods by proposing a new training criterion that does not require a scalar reward function, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the theoretical underpinnings and empirical results, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods in all cases, particularly in the molecular design experiments, where the performance of OP-GFNs is not significantly better than the baselines.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the novelty of the proposed method, particularly in relation to existing works such as "preference-conditioning" and "goal-conditioning" GFlowNets.
- The paper could be improved by providing more detailed explanations and examples of the order-preserving reward function and its implications for the exploration-exploitation trade-off.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the novelty of their work in relation to existing methods like "preference-conditioning" and "goal-conditioning" GFlowNets?
- How does the proposed method handle the exploration-exploitation trade-off, particularly in terms of the order-preserving reward function?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, especially in the molecular design experiments, to better understand the performance of OP-GFNs compared to other methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, particularly in large-scale or complex environments?
- Can the authors provide more examples or detailed explanations of how the order-preserving reward function is learned and applied in practice?
