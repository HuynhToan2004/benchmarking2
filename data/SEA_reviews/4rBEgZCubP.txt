 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Visual Particle Dynamics (VPD), a method for learning 3D particle-based simulators from RGB-D videos, which includes a neural simulator for latent particle dynamics and a renderer for generating images from arbitrary viewpoints. VPD is designed to support 3D state editing and multi-material simulations, making it a significant advancement over traditional analytic simulators that struggle with capturing realistic dynamics. The method is trained end-to-end using posed RGB-D videos and does not require privileged information, making it more accessible and practical for various applications such as robotics and animation. The paper also discusses the challenges and limitations of VPD, including its dependence on RGB-D videos and the potential for blurring over long rollouts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for learning simulators directly from observations, which is a significant advancement over traditional analytic simulators that struggle to capture sufficiently realistic simulations.
- The method is designed to support 3D state editing and multi-material simulations, which are crucial for applications such as robotics and animation.
- The method is trained end-to-end using posed RGB-D videos, which eliminates the need for privileged information, making it more accessible and practical.
- The paper demonstrates the method's ability to capture realistic dynamics and provides examples of scene editing and long-term predictions, which are crucial for applications in robotics and animation.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear illustrations and a comprehensive review of related work.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on rigid and soft-body dynamics, which might limit its applicability to other types of dynamics such as fluid dynamics.
- The method requires access to RGB-D videos, which are not always available, and the paper does not discuss the implications of this requirement.
- The paper's reliance on RGB-D videos raises concerns about the method's generalizability to real-world scenarios, where such data might not be readily available.
- The method's performance on more complex scenes with multiple objects and more realistic lighting conditions is not thoroughly evaluated, which could limit its practical utility.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method, including the potential for blurring over very long rollouts and the need for RGB-D videos, which could affect the method's applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, which could help in understanding its practical applicability and potential future improvements.

**Questions:**
- How does the method handle rigid-soft body interactions, and what are the challenges associated with modeling such interactions?
- Can the method be extended to handle more complex scenes with multiple objects and more realistic lighting conditions?
- How does the method perform when trained on a single RGB-D camera, and what are the implications of this setup for the method's performance and applicability?
- What are the limitations of the method, and how do these limitations affect its practical utility and potential future improvements?
- How does the method compare to other learned simulators in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency?
- How does the method handle the "sim-to-real" gap, and what are the implications of this gap for the method's applicability in real-world scenarios?
