 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SASC, a method designed to generate natural language explanations for black-box text modules, which are functions that map text to scalar continuous values. SASC operates in two steps: first, it identifies candidate explanations by summarizing the top n-grams using a pre-trained language model (LLM). Second, it evaluates these explanations by generating synthetic text based on each candidate explanation and measuring the response of the module to this text. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on synthetic modules, BERT, and fMRI voxels, showing that it can recover ground truth explanations in synthetic scenarios and provide explanations for BERT and fMRI voxels that are comparable to human-generated explanations. However, concerns are raised about the method's reliance on LLMs for explanation generation, the clarity of the evaluation process, and the generalizability of the findings across different types of text modules.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue by proposing a method for explaining the internal workings of black-box text modules, which is crucial for understanding and interpreting the outputs of large language models (LLMs).
- The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and the results are promising, showing that the method can recover ground truth explanations in synthetic scenarios and provide explanations for BERT and fMRI voxels that are comparable to human-generated explanations.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments are thorough, including a variety of settings and comparisons to human-generated explanations, which provides a robust evaluation of the method.
- The paper demonstrates the potential of the proposed method in practical applications, such as explaining the responses of individual fMRI voxels to language stimuli, which could be beneficial for fine-grained brain mapping.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on LLMs for explanation generation raises concerns about the method's reliance on these models and the potential for over-reliance on their capabilities, which could limit the method's applicability and understanding of the true nature of the explanations.
- The evaluation process is not clearly described, particularly the method used to compare the generated explanations with human-generated explanations, which is crucial for assessing the method's effectiveness.
- The paper's scope is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on text modules and not addressing the broader issue of interpretability in LLMs, which could be a significant drawback for practitioners seeking to understand the behavior of LLMs in more complex scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as the potential for the method to only explain the top responses of a module and its inability to capture low-level text patterns or patterns requiring long context.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing methods, which could provide a better understanding of its strengths and weaknesses in the context of existing research.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods for explaining the behavior of LLMs, particularly those that do not rely on LLMs for explanation generation?
- Could you clarify the evaluation process, particularly how the generated explanations were compared with human-generated explanations?
- How does the method handle the potential for LLMs to generate explanations that are not grounded in reality or that are not based on the true behavior of the module being explained?
- Given the limitations of the method, what steps could be taken to improve its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the method was applied to different types of text modules and the specific conditions under which it was tested?
