 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the effectiveness of graph classification benchmarks in distinguishing the advancements of graph neural networks (GNNs) over other methodologies. It proposes a novel metric to quantify the effectiveness of a dataset by utilizing performance gaps and considering dataset complexity. The study involves 16 real-world datasets and employs a new metric to evaluate dataset effectiveness, focusing on the correlation between intrinsic graph properties and task labels. The paper also introduces a method for generating synthetic datasets to control these correlations and provides a comprehensive evaluation of the performance of GNNs against other methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the performance of GNNs against other methods, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of GNNs in graph classification tasks.
- The introduction of a new metric to quantify the effectiveness of a dataset is a significant contribution, offering a new perspective on dataset selection and design processes.
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and the results are clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors have proposed a pioneering metric to quantify dataset effectiveness, which is a novel approach in the field of graph learning.
- The paper conducts a thorough investigation into the effectiveness of graph classification datasets, which is essential for assessing the advancements of GNNs compared to other methodologies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on binary classification datasets, which may not fully represent the diversity of graph classification tasks. Expanding the study to include datasets with more classes could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The motivation behind using GNNs for graph classification is questioned, as it is unclear whether GNNs are truly necessary or if simpler methods like MLPs could suffice.
- The correlation between graph properties and class labels is not adequately explained, and the paper lacks a detailed analysis of how this correlation impacts the effectiveness of GNNs.
- The paper's scope is limited to binary classification, which may not fully capture the complexity and diversity of real-world graph classification problems.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to datasets with more than two classes or its effectiveness in scenarios where GNNs may not perform optimally.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for any technological advancement.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors elaborate on the motivation behind using GNNs for graph classification, especially considering the potential of simpler methods like MLPs?
- How does the paper address the limitations discussed, such as the focus on binary classification and the lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the generation of controllable synthetic datasets and how these datasets are used in the study?
- How does the paper ensure that the results are not influenced by the specific GNN models used in the experiments?
- Is there a possibility of extending the study to include datasets with more classes and different types of graph properties to better represent the diversity of real-world graph classification tasks?
