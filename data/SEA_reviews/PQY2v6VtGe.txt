 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Confidential-DPproof, a framework designed to enhance privacy auditing in machine learning models by providing a certificate of privacy during training. This framework employs a zero-knowledge proof (ZKP) protocol to verify that a DP-SGD algorithm was executed correctly, ensuring that the privacy guarantees claimed by the model trainer are valid. The paper addresses the challenges of post-hoc privacy auditing, including the need for extensive data and computational resources, by offering a more efficient and less intrusive method. The framework is evaluated on the CIFAR-10 dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness in achieving state-of-the-art test accuracy with a certified privacy guarantee.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the critical issue of verifying privacy claims in machine learning models, which is crucial for enhancing trust in AI systems.
- It proposes a novel approach using zero-knowledge proofs to certify privacy, which is a significant advancement in the field of differential privacy.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is practical and could potentially reduce the need for extensive post-hoc auditing, thereby improving the efficiency and accuracy of privacy assessments.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, demonstrating its effectiveness in achieving state-of-the-art test accuracy with a certified privacy guarantee.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and applicability to more complex models or larger datasets.
- There is a need for a more detailed comparison with existing privacy auditing methods to highlight the advantages of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of computational costs associated with the use of zero-knowledge proofs, which could be a significant drawback for practical implementation.
- The experimental setup is limited to specific datasets and models, which may not fully represent the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss potential negative societal impacts, which is a critical aspect of ethical considerations in AI research.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be extended to other types of differentially private algorithms beyond DP-SGD?
- How does the computational cost of using zero-knowledge proofs compare to the benefits in terms of privacy and accuracy?
- Is there a potential for the proposed method to be used for other types of privacy auditing, such as verifying the privacy of data processing or data storage?
- Could the authors provide more details on the implementation and scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger and more complex datasets?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of computational complexity, especially in scenarios where the computational resources are limited?
- Can the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their work, particularly in terms of how the method might be used to enhance or undermine privacy protections in AI systems?
