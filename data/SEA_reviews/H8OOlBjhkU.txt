 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel algorithm for sparse optimization under mixed constraints, specifically addressing the challenge of handling support-preserving constraints. The authors propose a two-step projection operator that first identifies the top k components of a vector and then projects it onto the convex constraint set. This approach is validated through theoretical analysis and numerical experiments, showing the algorithm's effectiveness in maintaining the support of sparse vectors and providing convergence guarantees in various settings. The paper also extends the analysis to stochastic and zeroth-order settings, offering a theoretical framework that is both novel and practical. Despite some concerns about the practicality of the algorithm in real-world scenarios and the clarity of certain proofs, the paper is generally well-received for its technical contributions and potential applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in sparse optimization by introducing a novel algorithm that can handle support-preserving constraints, a feature not commonly found in existing methods.
- The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including detailed proofs and convergence guarantees, which are supported by numerical experiments that demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical flow that facilitates understanding of the complex theoretical concepts.
- The extension of the algorithm to stochastic and zeroth-order settings is a notable contribution, as it broadens the applicability of the method in real-world scenarios.
- The paper is original in its approach, providing a novel perspective on handling sparsity constraints in optimization problems, and offers a theoretical framework that can be extended to other areas of machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the practical implications and limitations of the proposed algorithm, particularly in scenarios where the constraints are not support-preserving.
- There are some minor errors and inconsistencies in the proofs and notations, which could confuse the readers and detract from the paper's credibility.
- The paper does not adequately compare its results with existing literature, which could undermine the perceived novelty and significance of the contributions.
- The algorithm's performance and convergence guarantees are not thoroughly validated in practical scenarios, which could limit the paper's impact in real-world applications.
- The paper's reliance on certain assumptions, such as the strong convexity of the loss function, might limit its applicability in more general scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the practical implications and limitations of the proposed algorithm, especially in scenarios where the constraints are not support-preserving?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical performance?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing literature to better establish the novelty and significance of their contributions?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed algorithm over existing methods, particularly in terms of convergence guarantees and practical performance?
- How does the algorithm handle the trade-off between sparsity and sub-optimality gap, and what are the implications of this trade-off in practical applications?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of parameters and their impact on the algorithm's performance and convergence guarantees?
