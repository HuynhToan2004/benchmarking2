 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AutoCLIP, a method for auto-tuning zero-shot classifiers in vision-language models. It modifies the weighting of prompt templates during inference to enhance classification accuracy by adjusting the weight of each prompt based on the image it is applied to. This method uses a logsumexp function to aggregate class-image similarities, which is claimed to improve performance over standard zero-shot classifiers. The experiments conducted across various datasets and models demonstrate that AutoCLIP outperforms standard zero-shot classifiers in most cases, with a notable average improvement of 0.45%. The paper also explores the use of different prompt templates and models, including CLIP, DCLIP, and WaffleCLIP, to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is intuitive, easy to implement, and can be applied to existing zero-shot classifiers without significant modifications.
- The method is simple yet effective, as demonstrated by its performance improvement across multiple datasets and models.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to understand and follow, and includes comprehensive experiments that validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method does not require additional training or access to pre-training data, making it applicable in various settings.
- The results show that the proposed method outperforms standard zero-shot classifiers in most cases, with an average improvement of 0.45% and up to 3% in some settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of datasets or models, and the potential negative societal impacts.
- The experiments are limited to zero-shot image classification tasks and do not explore the method's effectiveness in other tasks like object detection or retrieval.
- The paper does not include a detailed comparison with other methods like ZPE, which also tunes weights at test time, and lacks an ablation study on the number of iterations and the choice of hyperparameters.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed visualizations and explanations of the weighting process to enhance understanding and reproducibility.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational overhead and memory requirements introduced by the proposed method, which could impact its practical implementation in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform on other tasks beyond zero-shot image classification?
- Can the authors provide more detailed visualizations and explanations of the weighting process to enhance understanding and reproducibility?
- How does the method handle different types of datasets or models that might not benefit from the proposed method?
- Is there a potential for the method to introduce biases or unfairness in the classification process?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of prompts, such as those generated by large language models?
- Could the authors clarify the computational overhead and memory requirements introduced by the proposed method and discuss potential strategies for mitigating these issues?
