 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called selective recalibration, which aims to improve the calibration of neural network predictions by selectively rejecting a portion of data based on the confidence in the predictions. This method is achieved through a combination of a selection model and a recalibration model, both optimized to enhance the reliability of predictions. Theoretical analysis and empirical evaluations on various datasets, including medical imaging and zero-shot classification tasks, demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach in reducing calibration errors. The methodology involves a joint training of a selection model and a recalibration model, which is shown to outperform existing methods in terms of calibration error reduction.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a good balance between theoretical and empirical contributions.
- The proposed method, selective recalibration, is a novel approach that effectively reduces calibration error by combining a selection model and a recalibration model.
- Theoretical analysis provides insights into the benefits of selective recalibration, enhancing the understanding of its effectiveness.
- Extensive experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate the practical applicability and superiority of the proposed method over existing baselines.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear presentation of the methodology, results, and theoretical analysis.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include potential biases introduced by the selection process and the impact on model fairness.
- There is a lack of comparison with the most recent recalibration methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method's performance.
- The paper does not address the computational complexity or the scalability of the proposed method, which could be a concern for larger or more complex datasets.
- The selection of datasets for evaluation is somewhat limited, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The theoretical analysis is somewhat simplistic and does not fully capture the complexity of the calibration process, particularly in scenarios where the classifier is not well-calibrated.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant in applications where calibration is critical, such as medical diagnosis.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to the most recent recalibration methods, such as those mentioned in the paper by Minderer et al. (2021)?
- Could you provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, particularly in relation to the size and complexity of the datasets used?
- How does the selection process affect the model's fairness and accuracy across different subgroups of data, especially in applications where fairness is a critical concern?
- Can you clarify the experimental setup, particularly the selection of datasets and the parameters used in the experiments?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of calibration errors, such as those related to the average calibration error or the worst-case calibration error?
- Could you elaborate on the societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in applications where calibration is critical, such as medical diagnosis?
