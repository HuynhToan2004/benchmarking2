 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Newborn Embodied Turing Test (NETT), a benchmark designed to compare the object segmentation abilities of newborn chicks and artificial agents. The study involves raising chicks in controlled environments and training artificial agents in virtual replicas of these environments. The results demonstrate that while chicks can generalize object segmentation across different backgrounds and viewpoints, artificial agents struggle with this task. The paper highlights the challenges in developing machine learning algorithms that mimic the learning capabilities of newborn chicks, emphasizing the need for more effective methods in AI that can match the rapid and robust learning of biological systems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research.
- The study is significant as it addresses the need for a developmentally-inspired benchmark to compare the learning capabilities of newborn chicks and artificial agents.
- The use of a Newborn Embodied Turing Test (NETT) to compare the learning capabilities of newborn chicks and artificial agents is innovative and well-executed.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation of various machine learning algorithms and architectures, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The inclusion of a "noise band" in the results to account for individual differences in chick behavior is a thoughtful and insightful approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The study is limited to a single species (chicks) and a single task (object segmentation), which may not fully capture the breadth of developmental capabilities in animals or the range of AI applications.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the implications of the results for the development of machine learning algorithms that could mimic the learning capabilities of newborn chicks.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations or ethical considerations of using animal models in AI research.
- The paper's methodology and results are not sufficiently detailed or explained, making it difficult to fully understand the experimental setup and the implications of the findings.
- The paper's title and some of its claims are overly ambitious or misleading, which could detract from its credibility and relevance.
