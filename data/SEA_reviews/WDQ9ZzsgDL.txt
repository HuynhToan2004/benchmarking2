 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PromptNER, a novel approach for few-shot and cross-domain Named Entity Recognition (NER) that leverages Large Language Models (LLMs) to perform NER tasks. It utilizes a set of entity definitions and few-shot examples to prompt the LLM to generate potential entities with explanations, which are then used to justify the compatibility of these entities with the provided entity type definitions. The method has been evaluated on various datasets, including CoNLL, GENIA, FewNERD, FaBNER, TweetNER, and CrossNER, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance in few-shot and cross-domain NER scenarios. Despite its promising results, the paper has been critiqued for its incremental innovation, lack of comparison with more advanced baselines, and potential issues with data contamination.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the main ideas and methodology.
- The proposed method, PromptNER, is innovative and effective, particularly in its ability to leverage few-shot examples and entity definitions to enhance the performance of Large Language Models (LLMs) in Named Entity Recognition (NER) tasks.
- The paper provides extensive experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the method across various datasets and scenarios.
- The method's flexibility and ease of application across domains make it a valuable contribution to the field of NER.
- The paper includes detailed analyses of the impact of different components of the method, such as the number of examples and the size of the model, which helps in understanding the factors influencing the performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with more advanced baselines, such as those using pre-trained models specifically for NER tasks, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it seems to be an incremental improvement over existing methods like chain-of-thought prompting and few-shot prompting, with the primary difference being the use of a set of entity definitions and explanations.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of data contamination, which could lead to overestimation of the method's effectiveness if the test data has been seen during the pretraining of the LLM.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the data used for training and testing, which could affect the reproducibility and evaluation of the results.
- The method's performance is heavily dependent on the quality and diversity of the few-shot examples and entity definitions provided, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where such data might not be readily available.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the few-shot examples and entity definitions were selected and how they were validated to ensure their quality and relevance to the task?
- How does the method perform when using different types of LLMs, such as those trained for sequence-to-sequence tasks versus sequence-to-label tasks?
- Can the authors clarify how the method handles overlapping entities in the same sentence and how the entity boundaries are determined?
- How does the method handle entity types that are not explicitly mentioned in the provided definitions or examples?
- Could the authors provide more details on the data contamination issue and how they addressed it in their experiments?
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with more advanced baselines, such as those using pre-trained models specifically for NER tasks?
