 **Summary:**
The paper introduces InsertNeRF, a method that leverages hypernetworks to generalize NeRF representations across multiple scenes without retraining. By integrating hypernetworks into the NeRF framework, InsertNeRF dynamically generates weights for each scene based on reference features, enhancing the model's ability to handle new views and scenes. The method employs a multi-layer dynamic-static aggregation strategy to fuse features from multiple reference views, which is crucial for the model's generalization capabilities. The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's components and their impact on performance, supported by comprehensive experimental results and ablation studies. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism regarding its novelty and the clarity of its contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to generalizing NeRF representations across multiple scenes.
- The proposed method of integrating hypernetworks into NeRF is novel and innovative, providing a new perspective on generalizing NeRF models.
- The paper includes comprehensive experimental results and ablation studies that effectively demonstrate the impact of each component of the proposed method.
- The method's integration with existing NeRF frameworks like mip-NeRF and NeRF++ is highlighted, showcasing its applicability and versatility.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's components and their impact on performance, which is supported by both quantitative and qualitative results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is questioned due to similarities with existing works, particularly the use of hypernetworks and multi-layer dynamic-static aggregation strategies.
- The method's dependence on the quality and quantity of reference views and its sensitivity to the number of reference views are not adequately addressed.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and the time taken for training and inference, which are crucial for practical applications.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently robust or comprehensive, with insufficient comparisons to state-of-the-art methods and a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications for certain design choices, such as the use of hypernetworks and the dynamic activation functions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific advantages of their method over existing works, particularly in terms of novelty and computational efficiency?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the reference views are of poor quality or insufficient in number?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and the time taken for training and inference, especially in comparison to other methods?
- Could the authors elaborate on the design choices of the hypernetworks and the dynamic activation functions, and how these contribute to the method's effectiveness?
- In terms of practical applications, how does the method perform when applied to real-world scenes or scenarios with complex lighting conditions?
