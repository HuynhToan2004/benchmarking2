 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a Bayesian preference elicitation framework aimed at personalizing recommendations by learning individual cost preferences from feedback on pairwise comparisons. This framework involves maximizing mutual information between user responses and cost function parameters, using a Wishart distribution as a prior for the cost matrix. The methodology includes a sequential question selection scheme and a graph-based sequential guidance system to minimize expected recourse cost. The paper presents extensive numerical experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, concerns were raised about the lack of clarity in the presentation, the strong assumptions made, and the need for more robust experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to personalized recourse recommendations using a Bayesian preference elicitation framework, which is both innovative and relevant to the field.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with a clear explanation of the question selection process and the use of a Wishart distribution as a prior for the cost matrix.
- The paper provides a comprehensive literature review, effectively situating the research within the broader context of the field.
- The use of mutual information for question selection and the sequential guidance system to minimize expected recourse cost are highlighted as significant contributions to the field.
- The paper includes extensive numerical experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from poor presentation, with some sections being hard to follow and a lack of clarity in the presentation of the main ideas.
- There are several typographical and grammatical errors that need correction.
- The paper makes strong assumptions, such as the linear relationship between user responses and the cost matrix, which may not hold in real-world scenarios.
- The experimental validation is not robust enough, with insufficient details provided on the experimental setup and the results not convincingly demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches.
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the assumptions made, which could affect the generalizability and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the meaning and role of the Wishart distribution in the context of the proposed method?
- How is the question selection process implemented in practice, and what are the computational implications of this process?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the datasets used and the specifics of the recourse generation process?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness in real-world scenarios?
- Could you elaborate on the assumptions made in the model and discuss how these assumptions might limit the applicability of the findings?
