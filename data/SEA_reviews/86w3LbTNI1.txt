 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the issue of reward hacking in reinforcement learning (RL) by proposing a novel regularization technique based on occupancy measures rather than traditional action distribution regularization. This approach aims to address the limitations of action distribution regularization, which can lead to significant changes in return despite minor changes in action distribution. The authors introduce a theoretical framework and empirical evidence to support their approach, demonstrating its effectiveness through experiments in various RL environments. The paper also discusses the challenges of tuning the regularization parameter and provides insights into how this method might improve the robustness of RL policies against misaligned proxy rewards.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to addressing reward hacking in reinforcement learning (RL) by regularizing the occupancy measure of policies, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that focus on action distributions.
- The theoretical analysis provided in the paper is robust and supports the proposed method, particularly in demonstrating the limitations of action distribution regularization and the effectiveness of occupancy measure regularization.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and includes a clear explanation of the theoretical results and their implications.
- The empirical results presented in the paper are compelling, showing that the proposed method outperforms traditional methods in preventing reward hacking and improving performance.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the challenges associated with regularizing action distributions, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of computing the occupancy measure, which is a critical aspect of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definition and usage of the occupancy measure, particularly in the context of its application in different environments and its relationship to the proxy reward.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive experimental setup, including a broader range of environments and a more rigorous comparison with other regularization methods.
- The theoretical analysis, while robust, does not fully address the practical challenges of tuning the regularization parameter, which is a significant limitation for the practical application of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability in different RL frameworks and its scalability to more complex environments.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the computational complexity involved in computing the occupancy measure?
- How does the occupancy measure regularization approach handle scenarios where the proxy reward is learned, and how does this affect the effectiveness of the regularization?
- Can you clarify the definition and usage of the occupancy measure in different environments, especially in scenarios where the reward function is only a function of the state?
- How does the proposed method compare with other regularization methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Could you provide more details on the tuning of the regularization parameter and how this affects the performance of the regularized policies?
- How does the proposed method handle the problem of reward hacking in more complex environments, such as those involving continuous state and action spaces?
