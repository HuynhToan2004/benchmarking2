 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for semantic segmentation using diffusion models, focusing on leveraging low-resolution feature maps to construct high-resolution segmentation masks. The approach involves analyzing the generation process of diffusion models to identify semantic correspondences between image pixels and spatial locations of low-dimensional feature maps. This analysis is used to construct image-resolution segmentation masks, which are shown to perform well on various scene-centric datasets. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive experiments, although the paper has been critiqued for its reliance on pre-trained models and the lack of detailed comparisons with other methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and effective, leveraging the semantic knowledge of diffusion models to construct segmentation masks without additional training.
- The method demonstrates impressive results on scene-centric datasets, outperforming existing unsupervised semantic segmentation methods.
- The paper is well-motivated and includes a detailed analysis of the diffusion model's generation process to identify semantic correspondences between image pixels and spatial locations of low-dimensional feature maps.
- The method is applicable to any diffusion model, and the proposed method is simple and easy to implement.
- The paper includes extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper heavily relies on the pre-trained diffusion model, which could limit its applicability in scenarios where such models are not available or feasible.
- The method's reliance on low-resolution feature maps for constructing high-resolution segmentation masks might not be effective in scenarios where these maps do not contain sufficient semantic information.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other unsupervised semantic segmentation methods, particularly those that do not rely on pre-trained models.
- The paper's evaluation protocol might not be fair, as it uses a different number of masks per image compared to other methods, which could affect the results.
- The paper's methodology and results are not clearly presented, particularly in figures and tables, which could make it difficult for readers to understand the contributions and findings.
- The paper's claims about the effectiveness of the proposed method are not convincingly supported by the results presented, and there are concerns about the method's scalability and applicability to different datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the number of masks per image was determined and how it affects the results?
- How does the proposed method compare to other unsupervised semantic segmentation methods that do not rely on pre-trained models?
- Can the authors clarify the evaluation protocol and ensure that the results are comparable across different methods?
- How does the proposed method perform on different datasets, especially those that are not scene-centric?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the methodology and results, particularly in the figures and tables, to enhance the clarity and understanding of the paper?
- What are the implications of using different pre-trained diffusion models on the performance of the proposed method?
