 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel model stealing attack in the context of reinforcement learning (RL) policies, specifically designed to steal policies without access to the environment or knowledge of the input range. This approach involves a method that estimates the state distribution using a reward model, allowing for the estimation of the victim policy's behavior in different scenarios. The paper presents a countermeasure aimed at mitigating the effectiveness of the attack, and includes empirical results demonstrating the attack's efficacy across various control tasks. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental scope, potential overestimation of the attack's effectiveness, and lack of comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a novel and relevant problem in the context of model stealing attacks, focusing on the control domain where knowledge of input ranges is typically unavailable.
- The proposed method is well-explained, with clear presentation of the algorithm and its components, including the use of a reward model and a distribution evaluator.
- The empirical results are convincing, showing that the attack is effective in reducing the KL divergence between the estimated state distribution and the victim policy's distribution.
- The paper includes an ablation study that demonstrates the effectiveness of each component of the proposed method.
- The method is applicable in scenarios where the attacker does not have access to the environment or the victim's input range, which is a significant limitation in many real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental scope is limited, with only three tasks used for evaluation, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the method.
- The paper overestimates the effectiveness of the attack, particularly in its claims about the attack's ability to steal policies without access to the environment or knowledge of the input range.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, which could help contextualize the contributions of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in scenarios where the attacker does not have complete knowledge of the environment or the victim's behavior.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the method to more complex or high-dimensional state spaces.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed attack, which is a significant omission in the ethical considerations section.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when the attacker has some knowledge of the environment or the victim's behavior?
- Can the method be adapted to work with different types of reward functions, such as sparse rewards?
- How does the method scale to more complex or high-dimensional state spaces?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the method, particularly in terms of the time and resource requirements for training the attacker policy?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the attacker has only black-box access to the victim policy, and cannot directly query the policy with actions?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and roles of the distribution evaluator and the reward model in the attack process?
- How does the method ensure that the attacker's policy accurately imitates the victim policy, and what measures are in place to prevent the attacker from simply memorizing the victim's behavior?
