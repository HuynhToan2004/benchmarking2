 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AnoMem, a novel two-stage anomaly detection method designed to operate in both one-class and outlier-exposure settings. AnoMem leverages multi-scale normal prototypes during training to compute anomaly deviation scores, integrating memory modules into contrastive learning. The method is evaluated across various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in detecting anomalies and maintaining exceptional performance across different scales of anomalies. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and insufficient justification for certain design choices.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel two-stage method for anomaly detection, which effectively captures subtle features of normal data and adapts to varying levels of anomaly complexity.
- The proposed method leverages multi-scale normal prototypes during training to compute an anomaly deviation score, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is evaluated on various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in detecting anomalies and maintaining exceptional performance across different scales of anomalies.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental results are impressive, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of multi-scale normal prototypes for representation learning and anomaly detection is a novel approach that enhances the model's ability to detect both low-scale, texture-oriented anomalies and higher-scale, object-oriented anomalies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage.
- There is a lack of justification for certain design choices, such as the use of a Hopfield layer for memorization and the specifics of how multi-scale normal prototypes are used in anomaly detection.
- The paper does not clearly define the term "anomaly" and does not include a comparison with some state-of-the-art anomaly detection methods, which could have strengthened the paper's argument.
- The paper's experimental evaluation is limited to a few datasets, which may not be sufficient to convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across a broader range of scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential areas for improvement.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition of "anomaly" used in the paper and how it differs from other definitions in the literature?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage with other state-of-the-art anomaly detection methods?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of using a Hopfield layer for memorization, and how does it compare to other memory modules in terms of efficiency and effectiveness?
- Can you provide more details on how the multi-scale normal prototypes are used in the anomaly detection process, and how the anomaly deviation score is computed from these prototypes?
- The paper mentions a potential issue with the model's sensitivity to the number of anomaly samples. Could you elaborate on this and discuss potential strategies for addressing this issue?
- How does the proposed method compare to other memory-augmented anomaly detection methods, and what are the specific advantages of using multi-scale normal prototypes over other memory mechanisms?
