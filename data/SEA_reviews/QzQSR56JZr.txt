 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MALLS, a new dataset of 28K NL-FOL pairs generated by GPT-4 and verified by human annotation. The dataset is designed to enhance the performance of LLMs in translating natural language to first-order logic. The authors also present LogicLLaMA, a model fine-tuned on MALLS, which demonstrates competitive performance with GPT-4 and outperforms GPT-3.5 on various NL-FOL benchmarks. The paper explores the use of iterative correction and reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF) to enhance the model's correction capabilities. Despite its strengths, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty and the use of GPT-4 to generate the dataset, which could limit its broader applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new dataset, MALLS, which is significantly larger and more diverse than existing datasets like LogicNLI and FOLIO.
- The dataset is well-curated, with careful filtering and human annotation to ensure high quality.
- The paper presents a novel approach using a GPT-4-generated dataset and a fine-tuned LLM, LogicLLaMA, which achieves competitive performance with GPT-4 and outperforms GPT-3.5 on various NL-FOL benchmarks.
- The iterative correction and reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF) methods are novel and contribute to the state-of-the-art in NL-FOL translation.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and good organization, making it easy to follow.
- The use of RLHF for correction is an interesting approach that could be beneficial for other tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the prompting pipeline, which is crucial for understanding the dataset's quality and diversity.
- The dataset's size (28K) is relatively small compared to larger datasets like Wikipedia, which could limit its impact and utility.
- The use of GPT-4 to generate the dataset raises concerns about the dataset's generalizability and applicability to different LLMs, as it might be overly specialized to GPT-4's capabilities.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the dataset and the potential biases introduced by its generation using GPT-4.
- There is a lack of detailed comparison with other datasets and methods, which could help in understanding the dataset's strengths and weaknesses.
- The paper's reliance on GPT-4 for dataset generation and the use of RLHF for correction might not be generalizable to other LLMs, which could limit its broader applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the prompting pipeline, including the specific prompts used and the frequency of different prompts in the dataset?
- How does the dataset's size (28K) compare to larger datasets like Wikipedia, and what are the implications of this size for the dataset's utility and impact?
- How does the dataset's quality compare to other datasets like LogicNLI and FOLIO, and what steps were taken to ensure the dataset's diversity and generalizability?
- Could you discuss the potential biases introduced by using GPT-4 to generate the dataset and how these might affect the dataset's applicability to other LLMs?
- How do the authors plan to address the limitations of the dataset, such as its size and potential biases, in future work?
- Could you provide more details on the RLHF correction process, including the specifics of the reward model and how it affects the model's performance?
