 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an automated sampler search algorithm, termed the Swift Sampler (SS), aimed at optimizing data sampling strategies for deep learning models. The SS method maps samplers to a low-dimensional hyperparameter space and utilizes Bayesian optimization to search for optimal samplers. This approach reduces the dimensionality of the search space and the computational cost of evaluating samplers, making it suitable for large-scale datasets. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on various tasks, including image classification, showing improvements over baseline methods. However, concerns about the novelty of the method, the clarity of the experimental setup, and the generalizability of the findings to other datasets and model architectures remain.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components, including the use of Bayesian optimization and the reduction of the search space to a small number of hyperparameters.
- The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in accuracy and computational efficiency compared to baseline methods.
- The paper addresses a practical and relevant problem in deep learning, focusing on the efficient learning of samplers for large-scale datasets, which is crucial for improving model performance and reducing computational costs.
- The use of Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter optimization is a promising approach, and the paper effectively applies this method to the search for optimal samplers.
- The paper includes a variety of experiments across different datasets and model architectures, demonstrating the generalizability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There is a notable absence of comparisons with state-of-the-art methods in the field, which makes it difficult to assess the relative performance of the proposed method against current best practices.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the Bayesian optimization hyperparameters and the training conditions for the baseline methods.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of different hyperparameter choices on the performance of the method, which is crucial for reproducibility and understanding the robustness of the results.
- The paper's claims regarding the efficiency and effectiveness of the proposed method are not adequately supported by the experimental results, particularly in terms of the computational cost savings and the generalizability of the findings across different datasets and model architectures.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for any technological advancement.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the specifics of the Bayesian optimization hyperparameters and the training conditions for the baseline methods?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to datasets with different characteristics, such as varying class imbalance or different types of noise?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in the field, including a discussion on the relative performance and the specific advantages of the proposed method?
- How does the choice of hyperparameters, such as the number of segments and the number of feature functions, impact the performance of the method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost savings of the proposed method compared to baseline methods, and how does this translate into practical benefits for large-scale training scenarios?
- How generalizable is the method across different model architectures, and what are the specific conditions under which the method performs best?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how these impacts might be mitigated or addressed?
