 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SABER, a novel algorithm designed for federated learning under second-order data heterogeneity. SABER samples a subset of clients and tasks, incorporates an estimate of the global update direction, and regularizes each client's subproblem to reduce client drift. Theoretical analysis shows that SABER achieves state-of-the-art communication complexity, and empirical results demonstrate its superior performance compared to standard baselines. The algorithm's stateless design and support for partial participation make it a strong candidate for real-world deployment.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel algorithm called SABER for federated learning under second-order data heterogeneity, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The authors have demonstrated the superiority of SABER over existing methods through extensive empirical results, showing improvements in communication complexity and convergence rates.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The algorithm's stateless design and support for partial participation make it a strong candidate for real-world deployment, enhancing its practical applicability.
- The paper contributes to the field by addressing a critical issue in federated learning, namely the handling of heterogeneous data, and proposes a novel approach to tackle this challenge.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithm, particularly in terms of its scalability and applicability to more complex datasets or models.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, focusing on a few datasets and not adequately comparing the results with other existing methods under similar conditions.
- There is a lack of comprehensive discussion on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, which could affect the generalizability and applicability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the computational and communication costs associated with the SABER algorithm, especially in comparison to other federated learning methods.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of SABER over other methods like SCAFFOLD, which raises questions about the robustness and reliability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the scalability of the SABER algorithm, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger, more complex datasets or models?
- How does the SABER algorithm perform in scenarios where the data heterogeneity is not as pronounced as in the experiments described?
- Could you elaborate on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and discuss their implications for the practical applicability of the results?
- How does the computational and communication cost of SABER compare to other federated learning methods, especially under similar conditions?
- Can you provide more insights into the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
