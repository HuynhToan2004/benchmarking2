 **Summary:**
The paper explores aligning agents in 3D environments using a method inspired by the training of large language models (LLMs). It begins by training a general imitation learning agent using a large dataset of human gameplay, then fine-tunes this agent to perform a specific task using synthetic preference labels. The agent's behavior is further refined using a reward model trained on these preferences, and the policy is fine-tuned using reinforcement learning. The approach is validated through experiments in a console game, showing that the agent can be trained to consistently perform a desired behavior. The paper also discusses the potential scalability and applicability of this method to other complex environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to training agents in complex 3D environments, leveraging a method inspired by the training of large language models (LLMs).
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the dataset, environment, and the training process, including detailed ablations and evaluations.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental setup is thorough, with a clear demonstration of the approach's effectiveness in aligning an agent to perform a specific behavior in a game environment.
- The alignment process is detailed, showing how the agent's behavior can be refined through a series of steps, including supervised fine-tuning, preference learning, and reinforcement learning.
- The paper provides a clear demonstration of the approach's applicability to other complex environments, suggesting that it could be scaled up for broader application.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is somewhat limited, as the alignment process closely follows existing methods for aligning LLMs, which might not be seen as a significant advancement in the field.
- The evaluation is limited to a single game environment, which might not convincingly demonstrate the generalizability of the approach across different environments.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the choice of the reward model and the impact of synthetic preferences on the alignment process.
- The paper's reliance on synthetic preferences raises concerns about the authenticity and applicability of the findings to real-world scenarios where human preferences might not be as easily quantified.
- The paper could provide more examples or additional experiments to demonstrate the approach's effectiveness in more complex or varied environments.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the agent's behavior is refined through each step of the alignment process, particularly in terms of the specific changes observed in the agent's behavior?
- How does the choice of reward model affect the alignment process, and could the authors elaborate on this aspect of the methodology?
- How do the authors ensure that the synthetic preferences accurately reflect human preferences, and what steps are taken to validate the authenticity of these preferences?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of using different types of preference data, such as human annotations or other forms of synthetic data, on the alignment process?
- How does the approach perform when applied to more complex or varied environments, and what steps are taken to ensure that the agent's behavior remains aligned with the desired outcomes in these scenarios?
