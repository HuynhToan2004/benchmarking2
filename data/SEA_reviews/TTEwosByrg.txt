 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CoBBLEr, a benchmark designed to evaluate the biases in Large Language Models (LLMs) when used as evaluators. It focuses on six different cognitive biases and tests them across 15 LLMs, ranging from 3B to 175B parameters. The benchmark includes both implicit and induced biases, and it uses a pairwise evaluation method to compare the LLMs' evaluations with human preferences. The study reveals that most LLMs exhibit significant biases, with an average Rank-Biased Overlap (RBO) score of 49.6% indicating a significant misalignment with human preferences. The benchmark aims to provide a comprehensive evaluation of LLMs as evaluators, contributing to the understanding of their biases and potential for use in automatic annotation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new benchmark, CoBBLEr, for evaluating the biases in Large Language Models (LLMs) when used as evaluators, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The study covers a wide range of models, from 3B to 175B parameters, providing a comprehensive evaluation across different model sizes.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear presentation of the methodology and results, which facilitates understanding and reproducibility.
- The evaluation of cognitive biases in LLMs is an important and timely topic, and the paper provides a thorough examination of these biases, including both implicit and induced biases.
- The paper includes a human study to compare LLM evaluation with human preferences, which adds depth to the analysis and provides insights into the alignment between LLMs and human evaluators.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the reasons for the observed biases, which could provide a deeper understanding of the phenomena.
- The choice of models for the study seems somewhat arbitrary, and the paper does not sufficiently explain why these models were chosen.
- The benchmark's focus on QA responses limits its generalizability to other types of responses, which could be a significant limitation.
- The paper's claims about the LLMs' biases being stronger than humans are not well-supported, and the methodology used for human preference studies is not clearly explained.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the findings to other domains or tasks, which could limit the practical applicability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential mitigation strategies for the identified biases, which could enhance the practical utility of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations for the observed biases, particularly the induced biases?
- How were the models chosen for the study, and what were the criteria for their selection?
- How do the authors ensure the generalizability of the findings to other types of responses beyond QA?
- Could the authors clarify the methodology used for the human preference studies, especially regarding the calculation of RBO scores and the IAA metrics?
- How do the authors address the potential for LLMs to learn to mitigate their biases over time, and what implications might this have for the benchmark's utility?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending the benchmark to include more types of responses or tasks, and how this might affect the benchmark's utility and applicability?
