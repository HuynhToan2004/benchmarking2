 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel temperature scaling function for the InfoNCE loss in self-supervised contrastive learning. This function is designed to dynamically adjust the temperature parameter based on the cosine similarity of samples, aiming to enhance the performance of contrastive learning methods. The proposed method, DySTreSS, has been tested on various datasets including ImageNet and CIFAR, showing improvements over existing methods. The paper provides a theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of the proposed method, although it faces criticism for its limited novelty and the need for more comprehensive experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow content, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis and mathematical proofs, which are supported by empirical evidence.
- The proposed method, DySTreSS, is simple, intuitive, and effective, as demonstrated through extensive experiments on multiple datasets.
- The paper is structured well, with clear explanations of the motivation, methodology, and results, including a comprehensive ablation study.
- The use of a cosine function for temperature scaling is novel and provides a new perspective on temperature control in contrastive learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of temperature scheduling and other advanced techniques like ARB.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as similar approaches have been previously explored, such as the temperature function in MACL.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive experimental validation, including additional datasets and different model architectures.
- There are some unclear descriptions and missing details, such as the definition of "local structure" and the absence of certain experimental results (e.g., transfer learning results and the effect of temperature on different datasets).
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, especially in large-scale or long-term training scenarios.
- There are some typographical errors and formatting issues that need to be corrected.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other temperature scheduling methods, such as those in ARB and Kukleva et al. (2023)?
- Could you provide more details on the temperature scheduling in the proposed method and how it affects the performance and training efficiency?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of the cosine function for temperature scaling, and how does it compare with other possible functions?
- Can you clarify the definition and role of "local structure" in your methodology, and how it relates to the global structure?
- How does the proposed method perform in transfer learning scenarios, and what are the implications for downstream task performance?
- Could you provide the results of DySTreSS with different temperature ranges and compare them to the current settings?
- What are the implications of the proposed method for long-term training scenarios, and how does it compare with other methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
