 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel reinforcement learning (RL) framework aimed at fine-tuning pre-trained retrieval models for task-specific goals, such as optimizing recall or novelty in recommendation tasks. This framework converts the action space from a large set of items to a binary action space, which simplifies optimization and addresses the challenge of large action spaces. The methodology involves initializing the policy with a supervised model and using a large language model (LLM) as a reward model to simulate human feedback. The paper presents theoretical and empirical results, showing that the proposed method can improve recall and novelty in retrieval tasks, though it faces limitations in generalization and the complexity of the reward function.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of a novel reinforcement learning (RL) framework for fine-tuning pre-trained retrieval models is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is supported by both theoretical and empirical results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper addresses the challenge of large action spaces by reducing them to a binary space, which is a practical and innovative approach.
- The use of a large language model (LLM) as a reward model to simulate human feedback is an interesting and potentially impactful application of LLMs in retrieval tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on a single RL algorithm (REINFORCE) might limit the generalizability of the findings, as other RL algorithms like PPO or Actor-Critic could provide different insights.
- The use of a binary action space might oversimplify the problem and not adequately address the complexity of real-world retrieval tasks.
- The paper's empirical results are limited to specific datasets and tasks, which might not generalize well to other types of retrieval tasks or datasets.
- The reward function used in the experiments is complex and not well-explained, which could hinder the reproducibility and understanding of the results.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are limited, with some theoretical results (e.g., Proposition 1) being straightforward extensions of existing work.
- The paper's experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the improvements are not consistently significant across different tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the reward function used in the experiments and how it was derived?
- How does the proposed method compare to other RL algorithms like PPO or Actor-Critic, especially in terms of performance and generalizability?
- Can the authors elaborate on the choice of using a binary action space and discuss how this might affect the performance in more complex retrieval tasks?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle more complex reward functions that might better reflect the nuances of different retrieval tasks?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational efficiency compared to other methods, especially in large-scale retrieval systems?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific LLM used as the reward model and the training parameters for the RL algorithm?
