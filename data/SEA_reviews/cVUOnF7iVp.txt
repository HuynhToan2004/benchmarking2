 **Summary:**
The paper explores the sparse linear regression problem under the local differential privacy (LDP) model, focusing on both the non-interactive and sequentially interactive settings. It provides novel theoretical insights by deriving lower and upper bounds for the estimation error, which are crucial for understanding the performance of LDP algorithms in this context. The paper addresses the challenges of extending previous results to the general k-sparse case and proposes new algorithms to improve the utility of LDP estimators. The authors also discuss the implications of their findings, including the potential for further research in this area.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the sparse linear regression problem under the local differential privacy (LDP) model, addressing both the lower and upper bounds of the estimation error.
- The authors propose novel algorithms that are efficient and demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods, particularly in the non-interactive setting.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and logical flow, making it accessible to readers.
- The results are novel and provide a better understanding of the LDP model's limitations and potential improvements, which could be useful for further research in the field.
- The paper's contribution is significant as it addresses a critical gap in the understanding of sparse linear regression under LDP, providing a more complete picture of the problem's complexities and potential solutions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the data distribution is i.i.d., which may not hold in practical scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the results.
- The proofs rely heavily on assumptions such as the boundedness of the 2-norm of the data, which may not hold in real-world applications, and the results might not be applicable in such cases.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the results, particularly how they translate into real-world applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed algorithms, such as their applicability in scenarios where the assumptions do not hold.
- The paper's contribution is somewhat limited as it mainly extends existing results to the k-sparse case and improves the utility of LDP estimators, which might not be seen as a significant advancement by some.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the results would hold in scenarios where the data distribution is not i.i.d.?
- How do the authors address the potential issues when the assumptions, such as the boundedness of the 2-norm of the data, do not hold?
- Can the proposed algorithms be adapted to handle scenarios where the data is not uniformly distributed?
- How do the authors justify the use of the soft-thresholding operator in their algorithms, and what are the specific benefits of this approach?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of their results, particularly in terms of their applicability in real-world scenarios?
