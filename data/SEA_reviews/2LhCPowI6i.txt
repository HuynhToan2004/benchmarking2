 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for filtering generated data in a generative replay framework to mitigate catastrophic forgetting in class-incremental learning scenarios. The method involves a classifier that assesses the quality of generated data by rejecting samples it cannot classify with sufficient confidence, thereby preventing the retraining on poor-quality data. This approach is tested across various neural network architectures and datasets, showing that the method's effectiveness grows with the number of tasks. However, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental scope, focusing on a single dataset and not adequately comparing its approach with existing methods. The presentation of the paper is also noted as needing improvement, with issues such as poor figure quality and unclear explanations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear and straightforward methodology that is easy to implement.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and does not require additional training of the classifier or generator, making it practical for real-world applications.
- The method is evaluated across various neural network architectures and datasets, showing its effectiveness and scalability.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the experimental setup and the methodology used, which is crucial for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks sufficient experimental evidence to support its claims, with only one dataset used and limited testing across different model configurations.
- The presentation of the paper is poor, with figures and tables that are difficult to read and understand, and a lack of clear explanations and definitions.
- The paper does not adequately compare its approach with existing methods, missing an opportunity to demonstrate the superiority or novelty of the proposed method.
- The method's effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated, with some results showing only marginal improvements or even decreases in performance.
- The paper does not address the limitations of its approach, which could include the potential for overfitting or the impact of different classifier configurations on performance.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods for mitigating catastrophic forgetting in generative replay frameworks?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including different model configurations and datasets, to better support the claims made in the paper?
- How does the performance of the classifier vary with different classifier configurations, and what are the implications for the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- What are the specific benefits of using the proposed method in scenarios where the number of tasks is large, and how does it compare to other methods in terms of scalability and effectiveness?
- Can the authors clarify the definitions and roles of different components in the proposed method, such as the "solver" and the "generator," to improve the clarity and understanding of the paper?
