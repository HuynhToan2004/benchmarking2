 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a meta-learning approach for solving inverse imaging problems, focusing on the training of a meta-model that can be fine-tuned for specific tasks with minimal steps. The methodology involves a bilevel optimization problem where the outer level is supervised and the inner level can be either supervised or unsupervised, leveraging the measurement operator. The paper provides theoretical insights and experimental validations, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in various imaging tasks, including super-resolution and MRI imaging. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned, as it primarily adapts existing meta-learning frameworks without significant innovation. The paper also lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods and does not provide sufficient details on the training process, which could affect the reproducibility of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive literature review and a clear explanation of the meta-learning approach.
- The proposed method is innovative in adapting the meta-learning framework to solve inverse imaging problems, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided in the paper is insightful and supports the proposed method, particularly in the context of linear inverse problems.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing promising results in super-resolution and MRI imaging tasks.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy for readers to understand the proposed method and its implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it primarily adapts existing meta-learning frameworks without significant innovation, particularly in the application of bilevel optimization to inverse imaging problems.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could help in understanding the advantages and limitations of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of detail in the experimental setup, particularly concerning the training process and the specifics of the PDNet architecture, which could affect the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of computational complexity and resource requirements, which could limit its applicability to larger or more complex imaging tasks.
- The theoretical analysis and experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method recovers the Bayes optimal estimator, which is a significant claim made in the paper.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the training process, particularly how the PDNet architecture is trained and the specifics of the fine-tuning process for different tasks?
- How does the proposed method compare to other meta-learning approaches, especially in terms of computational efficiency and generalization to new tasks?
- Can you clarify the role of the regularization term in the meta-learning formulation and its impact on the generalization of the model to new tasks?
- In the context of the unsupervised setting, how does the proposed method handle the absence of ground truth data, and what are the implications for the recovery of the Bayes optimal estimator?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex or realistic imaging scenarios, such as MRI with motion artifacts or CT imaging with high noise levels?
