 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FreeLM, a novel fine-tuning-free language model that integrates both language and task-aware signals for training. The model is designed to unify multiple tasks into a "proposition correctness judgment" task, thereby reducing the need for task-specific fine-tuning. This approach allows the model to learn from a unified data format, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that rely on task-specific datasets. The paper evaluates FreeLM on various benchmarks and demonstrates its effectiveness in language understanding and generation tasks, showing that it outperforms existing models like GPT-2 and InstructGPT in zero-shot and few-shot settings. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope in terms of model size and the generalizability of its results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by integrating both language and task-aware signals for training, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that rely on task-specific datasets.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the training process and the experimental setup.
- The proposed method demonstrates strong performance on various benchmarks, including GLUE and Story Cloze, and shows potential for reducing the need for fine-tuning in language models.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed analysis of the training process and the effects of different components of the model.
- The approach of unifying multiple tasks into a unified format and the use of a teacher signal to guide the model's learning are innovative and could be useful for other tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other models in terms of the number of parameters and the computational cost, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The evaluation of the model is limited to a few datasets, which may not fully demonstrate its generalizability across different tasks and domains.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims of its generalizability to unseen tasks, and the results on the Story Cloze task are not convincing.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its scalability and the potential impact of using different model sizes or training strategies.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art models, which could have provided a clearer picture of the model's relative performance.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost and the number of parameters of the FreeLM model compared to other models like GPT-2 and InstructGPT?
- How does the model perform on other tasks beyond those evaluated in the paper, and what are the implications of using different model sizes or training strategies?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies in the results reported on the Story Cloze task and provide more evidence to support the claims of the model's generalizability?
- Is there a possibility of extending the FreeLM model to other tasks or domains, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in doing so?
- How does the model handle the trade-off between language signal and task-aware signal during training, and what are the implications of this trade-off for the model's performance?
