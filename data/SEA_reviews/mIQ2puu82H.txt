 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel loss function, KC loss, aimed at improving the naturalness of generated images by enforcing a constant kurtosis across different band-pass filtered versions of images. The KC loss is applied to various diffusion-based generative tasks such as personalized few-shot finetuning, unconditional image generation, and image super-resolution. The proposed method is evaluated using a combination of automatic and human evaluations, showing improvements in perceptual quality and naturalness. The paper is well-written and the experiments are robust, although some reviewers have pointed out a lack of theoretical justification and a need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, KC loss, is simple, intuitive, and can be easily integrated into existing diffusion-based generative models.
- The method shows significant improvements in image quality and naturalness as evidenced by both automatic metrics and human evaluations.
- The paper is novel in its approach to using kurtosis concentration to improve the naturalness of generated images, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments are robust and well-executed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various generative tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough theoretical justification for the proposed method, particularly in explaining why a constant kurtosis across different band-pass filtered versions of images is beneficial for naturalness.
- The experimental results are limited to specific datasets and models, and it is unclear how the method would perform on other datasets or models.
- There is a lack of comparison with other naturalness-enhancing methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the potential limitations or societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for some applications.
- The paper does not include code, which could limit the reproducibility of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more theoretical justification for the proposed method, specifically explaining why a constant kurtosis across different band-pass filtered versions of images is beneficial for naturalness?
- How does the proposed method compare with other naturalness-enhancing methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly how the method was applied to different models and datasets?
- What are the potential limitations or societal impacts of the proposed method, and how might these be addressed in future work?
- Could the authors provide additional results or comparisons that would help to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method in different scenarios?
