 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Min-k% Prob, for detecting whether a piece of text has been included in the pretraining data of a large language model (LLM). This method is applied to a benchmark dataset, WikiMIA, which includes texts before and after model training to support gold truth detection. The method is based on the hypothesis that unseen examples are likely to contain a few outlier words with low probabilities under the LLM, while seen examples are less likely to have such words. The paper also explores the practical implications of this method in real-world scenarios such as copyrighted book detection and contaminated downstream example detection. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation, potential over-reliance on a single metric (AUC), and lack of detailed theoretical justification.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Min-k% Prob, which is a simple and effective way to detect whether a text has been included in the pretraining data of a large language model (LLM), without requiring any knowledge about the pretraining corpus or additional training.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, including a detailed comparison with existing methods and a case study that demonstrates the practical applicability of the method.
- The method is clearly described, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand.
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue of detecting pretraining data in LLMs, which is crucial for understanding the provenance of these models and ensuring the ethical use of data.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's evaluation is limited, focusing only on the Area Under the Curve (AUC) metric, which might not fully capture the effectiveness of the method in real-world scenarios.
- The method's reliance on a single metric (AUC) raises concerns about the robustness of the method, especially in scenarios where other metrics such as accuracy or precision might be more relevant.
- The paper does not provide a detailed theoretical justification for why the proposed method works, which could strengthen the paper's argument.
- The paper's focus on a single dataset (WikiMIA) and a limited set of models (LLaMA, GPT-Neo, Pythia) limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for false positives or false negatives in the method, which could be critical in practical applications.
- The method's performance might be influenced by the specifics of the pretraining data, such as the size and diversity of the dataset, which could affect the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when applied to different datasets or models? What are the implications of using different datasets or models on the method's effectiveness?
- Can the method be adapted to handle longer texts, such as those found in books or other large documents?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the pretraining data is not uniformly sampled from a single distribution? What are the implications of using data from multiple distributions on the method's effectiveness?
- What are the implications of using different models for the reference-based methods, and how might this affect the method's performance?
- Can the authors provide more detailed theoretical justification for why the proposed method works, and how does it compare to other methods in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- How does the method handle false positives and false negatives, and what are the implications of these errors in practical applications?
