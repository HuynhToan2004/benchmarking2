 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for training neural networks to approximate solutions of partial differential equations (PDEs) by integrating a deep generative model to adjust the training set. The method aims to optimize the residual induced by the neural network model and minimize the Wasserstein distance between the residual-induced distribution and the uniform distribution. This is achieved by formulating a min-max problem that adjusts the random samples in the training set to maintain a smooth residual profile. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on several benchmark problems, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues in its presentation and lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and logical structure.
- The proposed method, AAS, is novel and interesting, integrating a deep generative model to optimize the training set, which is a unique approach.
- The theoretical analysis provided is solid, and the methodology is well-explained, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The paper demonstrates a significant improvement in performance over existing methods, such as DAS, as shown in the experimental results.
- The integration of optimal transport into the training process of neural networks for PDEs is a novel and interesting approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and generalization capabilities.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with some sections being difficult to understand without additional clarification.
- The paper's contribution is not clearly articulated, and the motivation behind the use of a deep generative model for adjusting the training set is not convincingly justified.
- There are concerns about the scalability of the proposed method to higher-dimensional problems and its applicability to more complex PDEs.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental setup and results could be more detailed and comprehensive, including a broader range of benchmark problems and a comparison with more recent and relevant methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and significance of the deep generative model in the AAS method?
- How does the AAS method perform in higher-dimensional problems, and what are the specific challenges it faces in such scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency and scalability of the AAS method compared to other existing methods?
- In terms of generalization capabilities, how does the AAS method compare to other methods that optimize the training set?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the AAS method and provide examples or scenarios where it might not be effective?
- It would be helpful if the authors could provide a more detailed comparison with other methods that optimize the training set, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the convergence properties of the AAS method, especially in relation to the uniform residual profile?
- How does the AAS method perform on more complex PDEs, such as those involving non-linear terms or more complex boundary conditions?
