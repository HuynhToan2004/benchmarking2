 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for 3D single object tracking (SOT) by unifying the tracking of objects across various categories using a single network with shared model parameters. This is achieved through the introduction of a new network, AdaFormer, which adaptively encodes shape and size information from cross-category data. The paper also proposes two category-unified models, SiamCUT and MoCUT, which demonstrate improved generalization and training stability compared to category-specific models. The methodology is supported by extensive experiments on the KITTI and NuScenes datasets, showing significant performance gains over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to unify tracking across different categories, which is a significant advancement in the field of 3D single object tracking.
- The proposed method demonstrates superior performance compared to existing category-specific models, showing a clear improvement in tracking accuracy and efficiency.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methodologies and experimental results.
- The introduction of a unified representation network and the use of vector-attention to facilitate feature learning and interaction of points within each group are innovative and well-explained.
- The experimental results are strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods in both Siamese and motion-centric paradigms.
- The paper provides a clear motivation for the need to track objects across different categories and offers a unified learning objective to balance the distribution of positive and negative samples.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the method might not perform optimally.
- There is a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods on the NuScenes dataset, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not address the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant omission in the discussion of ethical considerations.
- The experimental results, while strong, are not significantly better than some existing methods, and the paper does not provide a thorough ablation study to understand the individual contributions of each component.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and inference speeds of the proposed method, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- The paper's claims about the generalization of the proposed method across different categories are not adequately supported by empirical evidence, particularly in scenarios where the method might struggle with object categories not commonly found in the datasets used.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential scenarios where it might not perform optimally?
- Why was there no comparison with state-of-the-art methods on the NuScenes dataset? Could the authors provide an explanation or additional results to address this gap?
- How do the authors address the potential societal impacts of their method, particularly in terms of privacy and security concerns?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods in terms of training and inference speeds to better understand the practical applicability of their method?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly in scenarios where the proposed method shows significant improvements over existing methods?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of varying object sizes and shapes across different categories, especially in scenarios where the method might struggle with less common object categories?
