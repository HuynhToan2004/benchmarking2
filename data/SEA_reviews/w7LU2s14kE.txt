 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the internal knowledge representations in transformer language models (LLMs) by examining how linear relational embeddings (LREs) are utilized for predicting object tokens given subject tokens. The authors propose a method to estimate LREs and evaluate their faithfulness and causal influence on LLM predictions. The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments, including the creation of an "attribute lens" tool to visualize the object distribution at each layer for a given relation. Despite the paper's innovative approach and the utility of the attribute lens, concerns were raised about the limited scope of the experiments and the potential overestimation of linearity in LLM predictions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of how linear relational embeddings (LREs) can be used to predict object tokens based on subject tokens, which is a significant advancement in understanding the internal knowledge representations in transformer language models (LLMs).
- The authors have developed a novel method for estimating LREs and conducted a thorough set of experiments to evaluate their faithfulness and causal influence on LLM predictions.
- The paper introduces an "attribute lens" tool, which is a valuable contribution to the field, allowing for the visualization of hidden states and the identification of falsehoods in LLM outputs.
- The experiments are well-designed, with the authors conducting a comprehensive set of experiments to evaluate the faithfulness and causal influence of LREs on LLM predictions.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's findings might be overestimated as it only evaluates the linearity of LLM predictions on a limited set of relations, which could be more diverse.
- The experiments are limited in scope, focusing only on a subset of relations, which might not generalize well to other types of relations or across different LLMs.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger models or different types of relations.
- The evaluation metrics used in the paper, such as faithfulness and causality, are not well-defined or clearly explained, which could lead to confusion about the results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the results might apply to other types of relations or in different contexts, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of relations, and what are the implications for the scalability and applicability of the findings?
- Could the authors clarify the evaluation metrics used in the paper, particularly how faithfulness and causality are measured, and how these metrics relate to the overall findings?
- How might the proposed method be adapted to address other types of relations or in different contexts, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in doing so?
- The paper mentions that the linearity of LLM predictions might be overestimated due to the limited scope of the experiments. How might the findings be influenced if a more diverse set of relations were included in the study?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the models used and the methodology for estimating LREs, to help reproducibility and better understanding of the results?
