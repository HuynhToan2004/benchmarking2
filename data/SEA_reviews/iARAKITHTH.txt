 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for detecting machine-generated text using a ratio of perplexity scores from two pretrained language models (LLMs). This approach, termed "Binoculars," utilizes a "performer" LLM to predict next tokens and a "observer" LLM to measure perplexity, which is then normalized by the cross-perplexity. The method aims to address the "capybara problem" by accounting for the influence of prompts on perplexity scores. The paper claims that this method can detect LLM-generated text without training data and has been tested against various baselines, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance. However, concerns were raised about the method's ability to generalize to other LLMs and its reliance on the specific models used.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and logical structure that aids in understanding the proposed method.
- The proposed method, which uses a ratio of perplexity scores to detect LLM-generated text, is novel and well-explained, showing a good understanding of the challenges in this field.
- The method is supported by a thorough evaluation across a variety of datasets and benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness against various conditions and datasets.
- The paper addresses the "capybara problem" by normalizing perplexity scores with cross-perplexity, which is a novel approach that effectively reduces the impact of prompts on perplexity scores.
- The method is capable of detecting LLM-generated text without training data, which is a significant advantage over other methods that require specific training data.
- The paper includes a detailed discussion of the limitations and potential biases of the method, which is commendable for its transparency and honesty.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's reliance on specific LLMs, particularly the "observer" and "performer" models, limits its generalizability and applicability to other LLMs.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for adversarial attacks, which could manipulate the perplexity scores to mislead the detection system.
- The method's effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated across different LLMs, especially those that are significantly different from the models used in the study.
- The paper's claims about the method's ability to detect LLM-generated text without training data are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which primarily compare the method to other detectors rather than directly demonstrating its effectiveness.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential for LLMs to imitate human writing styles, which could affect the accuracy of the method.
- The paper does not adequately address the ethical implications of using LLM detectors, which could potentially be used to discriminate against individuals or groups based on their writing styles.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when applied to LLMs that are significantly different from those used in the study?
- Could the method be adapted to work with different pairs of LLMs, or is it limited to the specific models used in the study?
- How does the method handle adversarial attacks that manipulate the perplexity scores to mislead the detection system?
- Can the method be used to detect LLM-generated text in different languages, and how does it handle variations in writing styles across different languages?
- How does the method handle cases where LLMs are used to generate text that closely mimics human writing styles?
- What are the potential applications of the method, and how does it compare to other existing detection methods in terms of accuracy, speed, and computational resources required?
- How does the method handle cases where the prompt is not known, and what are the implications of this for the reliability of the detection system?
