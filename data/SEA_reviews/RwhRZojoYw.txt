 **Summary:**
The paper examines the impact of dropout methods on oversmoothing in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), specifically focusing on DropEdge and DropMessage. It argues that these methods, while effective in training, do not significantly reduce oversmoothing during testing, which can lead to poor performance. The paper introduces a novel approach, Learn2Drop, which optimizes the information bottleneck to dynamically drop messages during message passing, aiming to mitigate oversmoothing. The empirical evaluation across various datasets shows that Learn2Drop outperforms traditional dropout methods in terms of oversmoothing reduction and model performance. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in the definition of oversmoothing, the insufficiency of experimental validation, and the unclear relationship between oversmoothing and performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Learn2Drop, which optimizes the information bottleneck to dynamically drop messages during message passing, potentially reducing oversmoothing in GNNs.
- The authors critically evaluate the effectiveness of existing dropout methods in reducing oversmoothing, providing a detailed analysis of the limitations of these methods.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive related work section that effectively positions the study within the existing literature.
- The empirical evaluation is thorough, including a variety of datasets and metrics to assess the impact of dropout methods on oversmoothing.
- The paper addresses an important issue in GNNs, namely oversmoothing, which is crucial for improving the performance of GNNs in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the definition and measurement of oversmoothing, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, particularly in terms of the depth and breadth of the experiments. The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experiments, including different architectures and datasets.
- There is a lack of clarity in the relationship between oversmoothing and performance, which is essential for understanding the impact of the proposed methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential negative societal impacts.
- The paper's focus on GCNs might limit its applicability to other types of GNNs, and the evaluation could be broadened to include a more diverse set of models.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and measurement of oversmoothing used in the paper? How does this definition compare to existing definitions in the literature?
- How does the proposed method, Learn2Drop, compare to other methods that aim to mitigate oversmoothing in GNNs? What are the specific advantages of Learn2Drop over these methods?
- The paper mentions that oversmoothing can be mitigated by DropEdge and DropMessage during training. Can the authors provide a theoretical explanation for why these methods do not effectively mitigate oversmoothing during testing?
