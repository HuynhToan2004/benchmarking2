 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel one-shot privacy estimation technique for federated learning (FL) systems, specifically for the Gaussian mechanism used in DP-FedAvg. The methodology involves inserting canary vectors into the training process, allowing for the estimation of privacy loss without requiring knowledge of the model architecture or training data. The approach is empirically validated across various datasets and threat models, demonstrating its effectiveness in estimating privacy loss in a single training run. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the method, including the use of Gaussian distributions to approximate privacy parameters.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in federated learning (FL), focusing on the estimation of privacy loss without requiring extensive retraining or specific model architectures.
- The proposed method is straightforward and empirically validated across various datasets and threat models, demonstrating its effectiveness in estimating privacy loss in a single training run.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The approach is model and dataset agnostic, making it applicable to various FL tasks without modification.
- The paper provides a comprehensive empirical validation of the method, demonstrating its effectiveness in estimating privacy loss in a single training run.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of federated learning systems and its assumptions about client participation patterns.
- The theoretical underpinnings of the method, particularly the use of Gaussian distributions to approximate privacy parameters, are not sufficiently justified, especially in high-dimensional spaces.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing privacy estimation techniques, such as CANIFE, which could provide a better understanding of the method's advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper's reliance on the assumption that canaries can be inserted according to the same distribution as real clients may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where client participation patterns are not uniform.
- The paper's experimental results are limited to a single dataset and a specific model architecture, which may not generalize well to other types of models or datasets.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing privacy estimation techniques, such as CANIFE, in terms of accuracy and efficiency?
- Can the method be adapted to other types of federated learning systems, such as those using different mechanisms or architectures?
- How sensitive is the method to variations in the number of canaries used, and what are the implications of using different numbers of canaries in different scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more detailed justification for the use of Gaussian distributions to approximate privacy parameters, particularly in high-dimensional spaces?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the client participation pattern is not uniform, and what are the implications of this for the accuracy of the privacy estimates?
