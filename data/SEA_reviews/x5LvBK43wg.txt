 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel test-time adaptation (TTA) method named PROGRAM, which leverages a Prototype Graph Model (PGM) for reliable pseudo-label generation and a Robust Self-Training (RST) technique for test-time adaptation with noisy pseudo-labels. The PGM utilizes both global and local information to generate more accurate pseudo-labels, while RST incorporates consistency regularization and pseudo-labeling to enhance robustness. Extensive experiments on domain generalization and image corruption benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of PROGRAM, showing improvements over existing TTA methods. The method is designed to be easily integrated into existing baselines and can handle noisy pseudo-labels effectively.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly presented.
- The proposed method, PROGRAM, is a novel approach that combines both prototype-based and nearest-neighbor-based methods to generate reliable pseudo-labels, which is a significant advancement in test-time adaptation (TTA).
- The methodology is flexible and can be integrated into existing baselines, making it adaptable and practical for various applications.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing consistent improvements over existing TTA methods.
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in TTA by handling noisy pseudo-labels effectively, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The methodology is simple, efficient, and easy to implement, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant works in the field of TTA, which could provide a clearer context for the proposed method's contributions.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for real-time applications.
- The experimental results are not consistently superior across all datasets, and the paper does not provide sufficient insights into why the method performs differently on certain datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of overfitting to the test set, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis and discussion on the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the impact of the batch size on the performance of the method, which is crucial for practical applications.
