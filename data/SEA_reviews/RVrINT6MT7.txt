 **Summary:**
The paper explores the reactivation of neural activity during quiescent periods in recurrent neural networks (RNNs) trained to perform spatial navigation tasks. It derives sufficient conditions for the emergence of reactivation dynamics in these networks, focusing on the impact of noise and the optimization of network dynamics. The theoretical framework is supported by numerical simulations, demonstrating that the network dynamics during quiescence closely resemble those during active behavior. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for neural network training and the reactivation dynamics observed in biological systems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel mathematical framework for understanding the reactivation dynamics in recurrent neural networks (RNNs) during quiescent periods, which is supported by rigorous mathematical derivations and empirical simulations.
- The authors effectively combine mathematical analysis with empirical simulations to demonstrate the reactivation dynamics in trained networks, showing how these dynamics can be explained by the optimization of network dynamics.
- The paper provides a clear and well-structured presentation, with detailed explanations of the theoretical results and empirical simulations, which are supported by supplementary materials that enhance the reproducibility of the work.
- The results of the paper are significant and demonstrate the potential for the proposed framework to explain reactivation phenomena in biological systems, which could be useful for understanding and modeling neural network dynamics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's reliance on specific assumptions, such as the optimization of network dynamics and the use of noisy RNNs, may limit the generalizability of the findings to other types of networks or tasks.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are somewhat limited by the assumptions made, particularly the assumption that the network dynamics are optimally trained, which may not hold in all real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the results might apply to different types of networks or tasks, which could limit the practical utility of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the assumptions and the implications of these limitations for the applicability of the results.
- The paper's claims regarding the emergence of reactivation dynamics as a consequence of noise compensation and task optimization are not adequately supported by empirical evidence, which could weaken the paper's arguments.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed framework apply to other types of networks or tasks, and what are the specific conditions under which the reactivation dynamics are expected to emerge?
- Could you elaborate on the role of noise in the emergence of reactivation dynamics and how this relates to the optimization of network dynamics?
- How do the results of this study compare with previous models that explain reactivation dynamics based on attractor dynamics in recurrent networks?
- In the context of the reactivation dynamics observed in biological systems, how do the results of this study contribute to a better understanding of these dynamics, and what are the implications for modeling and predicting neural activity in different behavioral contexts?
