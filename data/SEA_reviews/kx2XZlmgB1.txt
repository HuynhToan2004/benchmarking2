 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of contrastive learning for ordinal regression, addressing the challenges posed by traditional data augmentation techniques that can overshadow the ordinal content information. It proposes a novel method, Ordinal Content Preserving Contrastive Learning (OCP-CL), which utilizes a generative model and the principle of minimal change to generate images with desired ordinal content information while minimizing the influence of non-ordinal factors. The method is evaluated on various ordinal regression tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving performance over existing methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and lack of comprehensive comparative analysis with other contrastive learning methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is innovative, particularly in its application of the minimal change principle to preserve ordinal content information in data augmentation.
- The experimental results are compelling, showing significant improvements in performance across various ordinal regression tasks.
- The method is flexible and can be integrated into existing ordinal regression methods as a plug-and-play solution.
- The paper addresses a relevant and under-explored problem in the field of ordinal regression, which is often overlooked in contrastive learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is somewhat limited as it builds upon existing techniques like contrastive learning and GANs, with the main innovation being the application of the minimal change principle.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other contrastive learning methods, which could have provided a clearer understanding of the method's advantages and limitations.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The method's effectiveness in scenarios where ordinal content information is not as subtle, such as in tasks with more significant ordinal content, is not explored.
- There is a lack of discussion on the generalizability of the method to other types of ordinal regression datasets or tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant omission in ethical considerations.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other contrastive learning methods to clarify the advantages of their proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where ordinal content information is not as subtle, such as in tasks with more significant ordinal content?
- Is the method generalizable to other types of ordinal regression datasets or tasks, and if so, how does it perform in these scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their method and how these impacts could be mitigated?
- How does the method handle class imbalance issues, which are common in ordinal regression datasets?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency and scalability of their method, especially in comparison to other methods?
