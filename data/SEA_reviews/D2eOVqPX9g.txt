 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedSARSA, a novel federated on-policy reinforcement learning (FRL) algorithm that leverages linear function approximation to address challenges such as Markovian sampling, heterogeneity in reward functions and transition kernels, and continuous state-action spaces. It provides a finite-time error analysis, demonstrating that FedSARSA converges to a near-optimal policy for all agents, with the extent of near-optimality proportional to the level of heterogeneity. The algorithm shows linear speedups as the number of agents increases, which holds for both fixed and adaptive step-size configurations. The paper also discusses the challenges in federated learning, including client drift and nonstationarity, and presents a comprehensive theoretical analysis and empirical validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel federated on-policy reinforcement learning scheme, FedSARSA, which integrates linear function approximation to address various challenges in federated learning.
- It provides a comprehensive finite-time error analysis, demonstrating that FedSARSA converges to a near-optimal policy for all agents, with the extent of near-optimality proportional to the level of heterogeneity.
- The paper shows that FedSARSA leverages agent collaboration to enable linear speedups as the number of agents increases, which holds for both fixed and adaptive step-size configurations.
- The authors conduct a comprehensive theoretical analysis and empirical validation, providing a comprehensive comparison of the proposed method with existing approaches.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and intuitive explanations, making it accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the policy improvement operator, which is crucial for understanding the algorithm's behavior.
- The experimental setup is limited, and the results are not compared with existing methods, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the mathematical formulations and assumptions, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on how the proposed method handles client drift and nonstationarity, which are critical issues in federated learning.
