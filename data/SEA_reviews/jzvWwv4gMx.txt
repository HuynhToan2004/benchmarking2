 **Summary:**
The paper explores the reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) by evaluating their performance in symbolic reasoning tasks, where semantics are decoupled from language. The study reveals that LLMs struggle with generalization in such contexts, often relying on template matching rather than formal reasoning. The authors also investigate the impact of fine-tuning on LLMs' performance, suggesting that while some improvements are observed, the models still lack the ability to generalize to new logic rules effectively. The paper includes experiments with different LLMs, including ChatGPT and GPT-4, and employs datasets like Symbolic Trees and ProofWriter to evaluate reasoning abilities. Despite the comprehensive nature of the study, the findings suggest that LLMs are not yet capable of generalizable logical reasoning, raising questions about the effectiveness of current models in this domain.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue by evaluating the reasoning abilities of large language models (LLMs) in symbolic reasoning tasks, where semantics are decoupled from language.
- The study is comprehensive, involving multiple LLMs, including ChatGPT and GPT-4, and employing datasets like Symbolic Trees and ProofWriter.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The findings are significant, highlighting the limitations of current LLMs in symbolic reasoning and providing insights into the challenges of generalizing from semantic to symbolic reasoning.
- The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate a clear separation between semantic and symbolic reasoning, which is critical for understanding the capabilities of LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of why LLMs struggle with symbolic reasoning tasks, particularly in the context of template matching.
- The experiments are primarily focused on symbolic reasoning tasks, which may not fully capture the capabilities of LLMs in other types of reasoning, such as inductive or abductive reasoning.
- There is a need for more diverse datasets and tasks to ensure that the findings are not overly influenced by the specific datasets used.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential impact of semantic knowledge on LLMs' performance in symbolic reasoning tasks.
- The experiments do not sufficiently explore the role of pre-training on semantic reasoning tasks in shaping LLMs' performance in symbolic reasoning.
- The paper does not include a comparison with other symbolic reasoning models, which could provide a more robust evaluation of LLMs' performance.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on why LLMs struggle with symbolic reasoning tasks, particularly in terms of template matching?
- How do you explain the observed performance differences between different LLMs in symbolic reasoning tasks?
- What are the implications of these findings for the development of LLMs capable of generalizable logical reasoning?
- Could you provide more details on the human study, including the sample size and the demographics of the participants?
- How do you address the potential confounding factors, such as the impact of semantic knowledge on LLMs' performance in symbolic reasoning tasks?
- What steps are being taken to ensure that the findings are not overly influenced by the specific datasets used?
- Could you discuss the potential impact of pre-training on semantic reasoning tasks on LLMs' performance in symbolic reasoning?
