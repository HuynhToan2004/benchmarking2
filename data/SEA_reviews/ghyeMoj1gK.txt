 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel client-centric federated learning (CCFL) framework, where clients take the lead in initiating model uploads and updates, while the server maintains a repository of cluster models, iteratively refining them using received client models. This approach allows for rapid adaptation to new tasks and high performance. The authors propose strategies for accurate server estimation of clients' data distributions and provide theoretical analysis on the convergence of CCFL. Extensive experiments across various datasets and system settings demonstrate the advantages of CCFL in model performance and computation efficiency over baselines. However, the paper faces criticism for its reliance on strong assumptions, lack of clarity in presentation, and limited novelty compared to existing works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods.
- The proposed method is novel and practical, particularly in its client-centric approach, which is innovative in the field of federated learning.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the convergence of the proposed method, which is a significant contribution.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showcasing its advantages in terms of model performance and computation efficiency.
- The paper discusses the challenges of the proposed method and provides some solutions, contributing to the understanding and application of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with existing works, particularly in terms of the novelty and advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches.
- The theoretical analysis provided is limited, and the paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in terms of communication and computation costs.
- The paper assumes a strong knowledge of federated learning, which may not be accessible to all readers.
- The paper's presentation is confusing, particularly in the descriptions of algorithms and the organization of content.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with some results (e.g., Figure 5) being unclear or misleading.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of the number of clients and the complexity of the data distributions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the proposed method and existing approaches, particularly in terms of novelty and advantages?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational complexity, especially in terms of communication and computation costs?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing works, highlighting the specific contributions and improvements of their proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with a large number of clients and more complex data distributions?
- Can the authors provide a clearer explanation of the experimental results, particularly in Figure 5, to better understand the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method ensure the accuracy and effectiveness of the server's estimation of clients' data distributions?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the datasets used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
