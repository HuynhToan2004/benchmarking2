 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel autoencoder-based approach, Dual Corruption Denoising AutoEncoders (DC-DAE), for handling missing data in machine learning applications. DC-DAE employs a dual corruption strategy during training, combining masking and additive noise, to prevent overfitting and enhance generalization. This methodology is evaluated against various baselines on tabular datasets, showing state-of-the-art performance. The paper also discusses the potential of DC-DAE in improving generalization across diverse missingness scenarios and provides a plug-and-play approach for practical applications. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the lack of comparison with more recent and diverse datasets, and the absence of a discussion on the societal impact of the work.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using dual corruption for imputation, which is both simple and effective.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it easy to understand and replicate.
- The proposed DC-DAE demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on multiple tabular datasets, outperforming GAN, DAE, and VAE baselines under varied missingness scenarios.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear presentation and good clarity in the writing, making it accessible to readers.
- The method is straightforward and easy to implement, with a plug-and-play approach that can be applied to various datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with recent works, particularly those that have shown significant improvements in missing data imputation, such as IM-GAN and DAEMA.
- The experimental setup is limited to tabular datasets, which may not adequately represent the performance of the proposed method in more complex or diverse data types.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as similar methods have been previously explored, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing literature.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the handling of mixed data types and the scalability of the method to larger datasets.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the societal impact of the work, which is crucial for assessing the broader implications of the research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with recent works, particularly those that have shown significant improvements in missing data imputation?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to more complex or diverse data types, such as image or text data?
- Can the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential future directions for improving its performance and applicability?
- How does the proposed method handle mixed data types, and what are the implications of this for the generalization of the results?
- Can the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their work and how these might be mitigated or addressed in future research?
