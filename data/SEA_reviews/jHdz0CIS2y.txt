 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training strategy, SelfVC, for voice conversion models using self-synthesized examples derived from self-supervised learning and speaker verification models. The approach aims to enhance the speaker similarity of generated speech by iteratively improving the synthesis model through a challenging training objective. The methodology is evaluated on both English and multilingual datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art results in zero-shot voice conversion. The paper also explores the use of SSL representations for prosodic information and proposes a training strategy to iteratively enhance the synthesis model. However, concerns about the clarity of the methodology and the need for more robust quantitative evaluations are highlighted.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel training strategy for voice conversion models using self-synthesized examples, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The methodology is clearly explained, making it easy to understand and follow, and the experiments are well-designed, providing a solid foundation for the research.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and the experiments, which are well-designed and well-executed.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) results in zero-shot voice conversion for English and can be easily scaled to multiple languages in a text-free manner.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the training pipeline, including feature extraction, duration prediction, and pitch prediction, which are crucial for the effectiveness of the voice conversion model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the methodology, particularly in how the SSL representations are used for prosodic information and how the pitch contour is normalized to remove speaker information.
- The results presented in the paper, such as those in Table 2, are not convincing, and the paper could benefit from more robust quantitative evaluations to support the claims made.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the components used, such as the TitaNet speaker verification model and the HiFiGAN vocoder, to enhance reproducibility and understanding.
- The paper does not provide audio samples or comparisons with other methods, which are essential for evaluating the quality and effectiveness of the voice conversion model.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the SSL representations are used for prosodic information and how the pitch contour is normalized to remove speaker information?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods in terms of speaker similarity and intelligibility, particularly in multilingual scenarios?
- Can you provide more details on the training data used for the SSL model and how it is fine-tuned for the specific task?
- How does the training time compare between the proposed method and other methods, and what are the computational costs associated with the proposed method?
- Could you elaborate on the use of the TitaNet speaker verification model and the HiFiGAN vocoder, and how these components contribute to the effectiveness of the voice conversion model?
