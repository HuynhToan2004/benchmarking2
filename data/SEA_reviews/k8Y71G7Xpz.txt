 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FORKS, a second-order online kernel learning algorithm that leverages incremental sketching techniques to manage computational complexity and maintain linear time complexity with respect to the budget. The methodology employs a fast sketching approach to construct a time-varying explicit feature mapping, which is crucial for efficient updates. Theoretical analysis demonstrates a logarithmic regret guarantee, and empirical results show that FORKS outperforms existing methods on real-world datasets. The paper also introduces TISVD, a novel incremental SVD method, and discusses its application in online learning environments. Despite the strengths in computational efficiency and theoretical analysis, the paper faces criticism for its lack of novelty in algorithmic contributions and the need for clearer presentation and more rigorous experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and easy to follow.
- The introduction of the TISVD method is novel and provides a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed FORKS algorithm demonstrates a logarithmic regret bound, which is a notable achievement.
- The paper provides extensive empirical evaluations that validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of sketching techniques to enhance computational efficiency in online kernel learning is a well-explored area, and the paper effectively addresses the computational challenges associated with this approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the algorithmic contributions is limited, as the main ideas appear to be derived from existing works.
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not provide a detailed comparison with existing methods.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of concept drift in online learning, which is a significant challenge in practical applications.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with insufficient details and a lack of statistical significance testing.
- The computational complexity and time complexity of the algorithm are not thoroughly analyzed, and the paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other methods in terms of these metrics.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of the algorithmic contributions and discuss how they differentiate from existing works?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of concept drift in online learning?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other methods in terms of computational complexity and time complexity?
- Is there a possibility to include a statistical significance test to validate the results presented in the paper?
- How does the paper address the limitations discussed, particularly in terms of practical applicability and scalability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the choice of datasets, hyperparameters, and the rationale behind the experimental design?
