 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Vision ELECTRA (VE), a novel self-supervised learning framework for vision tasks inspired by the ELECTRA model in natural language processing. VE employs a generator-discriminator paradigm, with a generator that masks image patches and reconstructs them, and a discriminator that differentiates between original and reconstructed patches. The model incorporates adversarial training, hierarchical discrimination, and Gaussian noise injection to enhance the model's ability to learn contextual representations at both macro and micro levels. Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate VE's effectiveness in various vision tasks such as image classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive ablation studies, and unclear experimental setups.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel self-supervised learning framework for vision tasks, leveraging the generator-discriminator paradigm, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The use of adversarial training, hierarchical discrimination, and Gaussian noise injection enhances the model's ability to learn contextual representations at both macro and micro levels, which is crucial for vision tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework in various vision tasks.
- The integration of ELECTRA's discriminator-based approach into the vision domain is a novel and interesting concept, showcasing a new perspective in self-supervised learning for vision tasks.
- The paper includes an ablation study that provides insights into the individual contributions of different components of the model, which is essential for understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study, particularly concerning the role of the discriminator in the proposed method. This oversight undermines the clarity of the paper's claims regarding the necessity and effectiveness of the discriminator.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it primarily adapts the ELECTRA model from natural language processing to the vision domain without significant modifications.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently robust or convincing. For instance, the results in Table 1 are inconsistent with those reported in the original papers, raising concerns about the reproducibility and validity of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of model collapse in adversarial training, which could significantly impact the model's performance and reliability.
- There is a lack of clarity and detail in the experimental setup, particularly concerning the training and testing datasets, which could affect the generalizability and applicability of the findings.
- The paper's contribution is seen as limited due to its reliance on existing models like MAE and SimMIM, with the proposed modifications not being sufficiently distinct or innovative.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the discriminator in the proposed method contributes to the learning process, especially in terms of its role in differentiating between original and reconstructed patches?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of model collapse in adversarial training, and what steps have been taken to mitigate this risk?
- Can you clarify the discrepancies in the results reported in Table 1 compared to those in the original papers, and explain why these differences exist?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and model size with other existing methods, such as MAE and SimMIM?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly concerning the training and testing datasets, to ensure the reproducibility and applicability of your findings?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of model overfitting, and what measures have been taken to prevent this?
