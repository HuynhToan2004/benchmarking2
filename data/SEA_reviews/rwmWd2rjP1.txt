 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, MoreRed, for denoising unstable molecules using a diffusion model trained on stable molecules. This approach aims to reduce the computational cost and time required for training by leveraging a pseudo-energy function based on Gaussian mixture distributions. The method is validated against a baseline force field model on datasets such as QM7-X and QM9, demonstrating its effectiveness in predicting diffusion time steps and improving the accuracy of relaxed molecules. The paper also explores the application of MoreRed in data generation tasks, showing its potential in molecular structure generation and image generation. However, concerns are raised about the method's generalization to larger, more complex molecules and its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive related work section that effectively positions the research within the current literature.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, leveraging the power of diffusion models to predict the diffusion time step, which is a novel approach in molecular relaxation.
- The method shows promising results in both molecule relaxation and data generation tasks, demonstrating its versatility and potential impact.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the methodology and its components, including the use of pseudo-energy functions and the design of the diffusion model, which are crucial for understanding the proposed method.
- The method's potential for reducing computational costs and time required for training is highlighted, making it a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is somewhat limited as it primarily builds on existing methods like PaiNN and DDPM, with the main innovation being the application of these models to molecule relaxation.
- The method's performance on larger, more complex molecules is not evaluated, which limits its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The method's generalization to other datasets and its comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in molecule relaxation are not thoroughly discussed, which could affect the understanding of its effectiveness and limitations.
- There are concerns about the method's scalability and applicability to other types of molecules beyond those tested, such as organic molecules, which could limit its utility in broader applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its assumptions and potential failure cases.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the method performs on larger, more complex molecules? Are there any plans to evaluate its effectiveness on such molecules in future work?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in molecule relaxation? Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the results to better understand the method's relative performance?
- What are the potential failure cases of the proposed method? How do these compare to the failure cases of the baseline force field model?
- Can you provide more details on how the method handles different types of molecules, such as organic molecules, which were not evaluated in the current study?
- How does the method handle the generation of multiple stable conformers for a given molecule? Is this a limitation of the method or a characteristic that could be leveraged for specific applications?
- Could you clarify the term "unstable molecules" used in the paper? Is this a term used in quantum chemistry, or is it a term specific to the methodology used in the study?
- How does the method perform when applied to other types of datasets or molecular structures beyond those tested? Are there any assumptions or limitations that could affect its applicability in these contexts?
