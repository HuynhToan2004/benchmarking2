 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CoLiDE, a novel framework for learning linear Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) by simultaneously estimating both the DAG structure and exogenous noise levels. This approach is designed to enhance the robustness of DAG learning, particularly in heteroscedastic scenarios, by integrating a concomitant scale estimation. The framework leverages ideas from concomitant scale estimation in sparse linear regression and is supported by extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The methodology aims to improve the identification of DAGs in the presence of noise and heteroscedasticity, showing superior performance compared to existing methods. However, the paper has been critiqued for its incremental nature and lack of comprehensive theoretical analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to DAG learning by integrating the estimation of noise levels, which is crucial for handling heteroscedasticity effectively.
- The methodology is supported by a comprehensive set of experiments, including both synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method over existing techniques.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and experimental results.
- The use of concomitant estimation in DAG learning is innovative, addressing a significant challenge in the field by decoupling the sparsity parameter from noise levels.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the algorithm and experimental setup, enhancing reproducibility and transparency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and time efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The theoretical analysis of the method is insufficient, particularly in terms of statistical guarantees and the identification of DAGs.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to nonlinear SEMs and its sensitivity to hyperparameter choices.
- The experimental results, while promising, do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing techniques in all scenarios, especially in more complex or nonlinear settings.
- The paper's reliance on certain assumptions, such as the homoscedasticity of noise, may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where noise is often heteroscedastic.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity and time efficiency of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in nonlinear SEMs, and what are the potential limitations in such settings?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of scalability and computational efficiency with other state-of-the-art techniques, especially in large-scale or complex datasets?
- What are the main challenges in extending the method to nonlinear SEMs, and how might these be addressed?
- Can the authors provide more detailed theoretical analysis, particularly in terms of statistical guarantees and identifiability of DAGs?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of identifiability in heteroscedastic scenarios, and what are the implications for the practical applicability of the method?
