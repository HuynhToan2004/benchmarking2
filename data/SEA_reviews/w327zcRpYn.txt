 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SUBER, a novel framework for training and evaluating RL-based recommender systems using synthetic data generated by Large Language Models (LLMs). The framework simulates human behavior by utilizing LLMs to predict user ratings and preferences, thus providing a realistic environment for RL agents to learn and optimize their recommendations. The paper includes ablation studies and experiments on movie and book recommendations, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, concerns were raised about the realism of the LLM-generated data and the generalizability of the results to different LLMs and real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem by proposing a novel framework for training and evaluating RL-based recommender systems using synthetic data.
- The framework's design is well-structured, with clear and detailed explanations, making it easy to follow.
- The paper includes extensive ablation studies and experiments, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in movie and book recommendations.
- The authors provide detailed descriptions of the experimental setup, including the LLMs used, which helps in understanding the methodology and results.
- The availability of the code and the open-source nature of the framework facilitate reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's main contribution, the use of LLMs to simulate human behavior, is not sufficiently novel or differentiated from existing approaches that use LLMs for generating synthetic data.
- The quality and realism of the LLM-generated data are questionable, with concerns about the accuracy of user ratings and preferences, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing simulation environments, which could have provided a clearer context for the proposed framework's advantages and limitations.
- The experiments are limited to a few LLMs and do not explore the impact of different LLMs on the performance of the framework, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper's claims about the framework's ability to simulate human behavior and accurately predict user preferences are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential biases introduced by the LLM-generated data, which could affect the fairness and effectiveness of the RL-based recommender systems.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed framework compare with existing simulation environments, particularly in terms of data quality and realism?
- What are the specific advantages of using LLMs over other methods for generating synthetic data for RL-based recommender systems?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to demonstrate how the framework can effectively simulate human behavior and predict user preferences?
- How do the authors address the potential biases introduced by the LLM-generated data, and what steps are taken to ensure fairness and effectiveness in the RL-based recommender systems?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the reward perturbation and shaping components on the performance of the RL agents?
- How do the authors ensure the generalizability of the results to different LLMs and real-world applications?
