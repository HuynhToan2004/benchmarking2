 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, ALS, aimed at improving the performance of existing label shift methods by utilizing an intermediate distribution derived from both labeled source and unlabeled target samples. This method aims to reduce the variance in importance weight estimation and improve generalization guarantees. Theoretical analyses are provided to support the effectiveness of ALS, and empirical results on various datasets demonstrate its superiority over existing methods. However, the paper is criticized for its presentation clarity, limited novelty, and insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods. Concerns are also raised about the practical applicability of the method due to its reliance on known target labels and the theoretical assumptions that may not hold in practical scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, ALS, that utilizes an intermediate distribution to address label shift, which is a significant challenge in real-world applications.
- Theoretical analyses are provided to support the effectiveness of the proposed method, which includes a detailed derivation of the ALS method and its application to existing methods like BBSE and RLLS.
- The paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow, and includes extensive experiments that demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods.
- The authors have made significant efforts to ensure the reproducibility of their work by providing a detailed description of their experimental setup and results.
- The paper addresses a crucial problem in the field of machine learning, specifically the label shift problem, which is of great importance in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, making it difficult to follow, especially in sections where the methodology and theoretical results are discussed.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it appears to be a combination of existing methods like BBSE and RLLS, without significant innovation.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed approach.
- The theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions that may not hold in practical scenarios, which could limit the applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in large-scale applications.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, which could hinder the reproducibility and validation of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and how they relate to practical scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance, computational complexity, and practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons and evaluations, especially in scenarios where the proposed method may not perform optimally?
- What are the specific computational requirements of the proposed method, and how do they compare to other methods in terms of efficiency and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed information on the experimental setup, including the parameters used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
