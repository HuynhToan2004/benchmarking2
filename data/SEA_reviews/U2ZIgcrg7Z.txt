 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ZooPFL, a method for personalized federated learning (FL) with large foundation models, which typically have black-box access. ZooPFL utilizes zeroth-order optimization to adapt inputs and learn transformations on the inputs and mappings of the outputs, reducing computational costs and enhancing personalization. The method is evaluated on both computer vision and natural language processing tasks, showing promising results. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, the clarity of its advantages over existing methods, and the robustness of the experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The motivation behind the research is clear and well-articulated, highlighting the challenges of personalized federated learning with large foundation models.
- The proposed method, ZooPFL, is simple and effective, utilizing zeroth-order optimization to adapt inputs and learn transformations on the inputs and mappings of the outputs, which is innovative.
- The paper provides theoretical support and empirical validation, demonstrating the effectiveness of ZooPFL.
- The experiments are comprehensive, including both computer vision and natural language processing tasks, and the results show that ZooPFL outperforms baseline methods in most cases.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the advantages of using zeroth-order optimization over other methods like FedPrompt and FedCP.
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques such as input surgery and semantic re-mapping, which have been used in other federated learning methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of distribution shifts in federated learning, which is a critical challenge in personalized federated learning.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the impact of different hyperparameters and the sensitivity of the method to these parameters.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper's experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the results are sometimes inconsistent with the claims made in the paper.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the advantages of using zeroth-order optimization over other methods?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of distribution shifts in federated learning, which is a critical challenge in personalized federated learning?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the method and provide insights into how these limitations might affect the practical applicability of the proposed approach?
- How does the proposed method perform when the number of clients or the amount of training data per client varies?
- Could the authors provide more detailed insights into the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the method?
- Is there a possibility to include more recent and larger foundation models in the experiments to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method on more advanced models?
