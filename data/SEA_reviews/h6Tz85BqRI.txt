 **Summary:**
The paper introduces VQGraph, a novel approach for GNN-to-MLP distillation, leveraging a graph tokenizer to learn discrete codes for node neighborhoods. This method allows for more effective distillation of structural knowledge from GNNs to MLPs, demonstrating improved performance and inference speed across various datasets. The approach involves training a GNN encoder to predict node neighborhoods from discrete codes, which are then used to distill MLPs. The method is supported by extensive experiments and visualizations, showcasing its effectiveness and efficiency. However, concerns were raised about the novelty and the clarity of the method's contributions, particularly in how it differs from existing VQ-VAE methods and other graph neural network distillation approaches.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise descriptions of the proposed method and its components, making it easy to understand.
- The proposed method, VQGraph, demonstrates strong empirical performance across various datasets, showing improvements over existing methods like NOSMOG and GLNN.
- The method is innovative in its application of VQ-VAE for graph neural network distillation, which is a novel approach that has not been explored extensively in this context.
- The paper provides extensive visualizations and ablation studies, which support the claims made about the method's effectiveness.
- The method achieves significant improvements in inference speed, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the graph tokenizer and the method's ability to generalize to graphs with different topologies.
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as it appears to be a straightforward extension of VQ-VAE to graph data, with limited differentiation from existing methods like GLNN.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other relevant methods, such as those mentioned in the literature, which could have provided a more robust evaluation.
- The method's reliance on a fixed number of discrete codes might limit its expressiveness and applicability to graphs with more complex topologies.
- There are concerns about the method's scalability and its ability to handle large-scale graphs, which are critical for practical applications.
- The paper's focus on node classification tasks might limit the method's applicability to other types of graph learning tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the graph tokenizer works and how it handles different topologies in graphs?
- How does the proposed method compare to other graph neural network distillation methods, especially those that use similar approaches like GLNN?
- Can you provide more details on the training process, particularly how the MLP representations are obtained and how they are used in the distillation process?
- How does the method handle graphs with varying numbers of nodes and edges?
- Could you elaborate on the method's scalability and its ability to handle large-scale graphs?
- How does the method perform when applied to other types of graph learning tasks, such as link prediction or graph classification?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the method's performance is evaluated and compared to other methods?
