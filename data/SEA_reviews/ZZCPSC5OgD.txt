 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LipVoicer, a novel lip-to-speech synthesis method that leverages text inferred from a lip-reading model to guide the generation of natural audio. The approach involves a two-stage process: first, a lip-reading model predicts text from a silent video, and then a diffusion model generates mel-spectrograms conditioned on both the video and the predicted text. This method is tested on the LRS2 and LRS3 datasets and demonstrates superior performance over existing methods, particularly in terms of human evaluations and objective metrics such as word error rate (WER). The paper also discusses the use of a classifier-free guidance mechanism to enhance the model's performance, although concerns are raised about the novelty and clarity of the method's contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed method and its evaluation.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) results on the LRS2 and LRS3 datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The use of a classifier-free guidance mechanism to enhance the performance of the model is a novel and interesting approach.
- The method's ability to generate high-quality speech from silent videos, which is crucial for applications in fields like education, accessibility, and entertainment, is highlighted.
- The paper includes thorough ablation studies that help in understanding the method's performance and the impact of different components.
- The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, including both quantitative metrics and human evaluations, which strengthens the credibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its practical applicability and potential shortcomings.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as the use of text inferred by lip-reading to guide lip-to-speech synthesis is not a new concept.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the text inferred by the lip-reading model guides the diffusion model, which could lead to confusion about the method's operation.
- The method's performance on other datasets such as LRS1 and GRID is not evaluated, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the potential societal impacts of the technology, which is a significant omission given the potential applications in areas like accessibility and entertainment.
- The method's reliance on a lip-reading model for accurate text prediction could limit its performance in scenarios where the model fails to accurately transcribe the video.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the text inferred by the lip-reading model guides the diffusion model, especially in terms of the specific training and inference processes involved?
- How does the method perform on other datasets such as LRS1 and GRID, and what insights can be gained from these evaluations?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential societal impacts of the technology, especially in terms of its applications in accessibility and entertainment?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the lip-reading model fails to accurately transcribe the video, and what are the implications of such failures on the overall performance of the system?
- Can the authors discuss the potential for extending the method to handle different accents or languages, and what challenges might be encountered in such extensions?
