 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for generating natural language explanations for an agent's behavior in safety-critical settings, leveraging a decision tree distilled from the agent's policy to represent behavior. This behavior representation is then used to generate explanations using a large language model (LLM), which can be interacted with to answer follow-up questions. The methodology is tested in a multi-agent search-and-rescue environment, showing that the explanations generated by the LLM are preferred over those from human experts and other baselines. The paper also discusses the challenges of hallucination and its impact on explanation quality.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach for generating natural language explanations for an agent's behavior using a decision tree distilled from the agent's policy, which is then used to generate explanations with a large language model (LLM).
- The method is model-agnostic and does not require fine-tuning the LLM, making it adaptable to various models.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear and intuitive algorithm and a well-designed experiment.
- The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of explainable AI by proposing a method that can be applied to any agent policy, making it a valuable tool for understanding agent behavior.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation that includes both quantitative and qualitative results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the LLM is conditioned on the behavior representation to generate explanations, particularly in terms of the prompt used.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the distillation process, which could be a significant limitation for larger or more complex models.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods for generating natural language explanations, which could provide a more robust evaluation of its effectiveness.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method, such as its applicability to different types of policies or environments, which could affect its generalizability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the hallucination phenomenon, which is a significant issue in the use of LLMs for explanation generation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the LLM is conditioned on the behavior representation to generate explanations, specifically what prompts are used?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods for generating natural language explanations, particularly in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the distillation process and how it scales with the complexity of the model?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of policies or environments, and what are the limitations of its applicability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the agent's behavior is not explicitly represented in the decision tree, and what are the implications for the quality of the explanations generated?
