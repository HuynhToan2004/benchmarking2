 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel prompting strategy, Self-Contemplation Prompting (SEC), aimed at enhancing the in-context learning (ICL) abilities of large language models (LLMs) by allowing them to generate their own few-shot examples based on test inputs. This strategy, which does not require human-crafted demonstrations, has been evaluated across multiple tasks including arithmetic reasoning, commonsense reasoning, multi-task language understanding, and code generation. The results show that SEC can achieve comparable or superior performance to traditional ICL methods and zero-shot learning. The paper also discusses the limitations and potential societal impacts of this approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed self-contemplation prompting (SEC) strategy is simple, effective, and broadly applicable, requiring minimal human intervention.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of SEC across various tasks and models, showing comparable or superior performance to traditional in-context learning (ICL) and zero-shot learning.
- The paper includes detailed ablation studies and error analysis, providing valuable insights into the performance of SEC.
- The paper presents a novel approach to in-context learning, which could potentially reduce the reliance on human-crafted demonstrations, making the process more efficient and cost-effective.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of SEC, such as its reliance on the quality of generated few-shot examples and the potential for misleading demonstrations.
- The experimental results are somewhat inconsistent, with SEC sometimes underperforming compared to traditional ICL methods, especially in specific datasets like 3-digit base-5 addition problems.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare SEC with other automatic prompting strategies, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of its effectiveness.
- The paper does not discuss the potential societal impacts of SEC, which could be significant given its potential applications in various domains.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of SEC over traditional ICL methods are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which are sometimes inconsistent or inconclusive.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples and analysis of the generated few-shot examples, particularly in terms of their quality and how they influence the performance of SEC?
- How does the performance of SEC compare to other automatic prompting strategies, and what are the key differences between SEC and these other methods?
- Can the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of SEC, particularly in terms of its applications in areas like education or healthcare?
- Could the authors clarify the inconsistencies in the experimental results, especially the underperformance of SEC in specific datasets like 3-digit base-5 addition problems?
- How does the SEC strategy handle the potential for misleading demonstrations, and what steps are taken to ensure the reliability and accuracy of the generated examples?
