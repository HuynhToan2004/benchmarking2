 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for protein generation, focusing on antibody design, using a combination of discrete walk-jump sampling and a distributional conformity score. The method leverages an energy-based model (EBM) to learn a smoothed data manifold and a denoising model to transform noisy samples back to clean samples. This approach aims to address the challenges of training and sampling from discrete generative models, particularly in the context of protein design. The method is evaluated against several baselines and shows promising results in both in-silico and in-vitro experiments, demonstrating the generation of functional antibodies with improved binding affinity. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in explaining certain methodological details and its limited generalization to other types of proteins beyond antibodies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is novel and innovative, introducing a new paradigm for modeling discrete data distributions using a neural empirical Bayes framework.
- The methodology is robust, as demonstrated by its ability to generate functional antibodies with improved binding affinity in both in-silico and in-vitro experiments.
- The introduction of the distributional conformity score provides a useful metric for evaluating protein generative models.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear and concise figures that aid in understanding the proposed methods.
- The method is efficient, requiring only a single noise level for training, which simplifies the training process and reduces the need for complex noise schedules.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the distributional conformity score, which is a new metric introduced for evaluating protein generative models. More detailed explanations and examples would be beneficial.
- The generalization of the method to other types of proteins beyond antibodies is not clear. The paper should discuss whether the method can be extended to other types of proteins and how it might be adapted for different types of data.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the context of discrete diffusion models and energy-based models.
- There are some inconsistencies in notation and definitions throughout the paper, which could confuse readers.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and scope.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing methods, which could help in understanding the relative performance of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the distributional conformity score compare to other metrics for evaluating protein generative models?
- Can the proposed method be generalized to other types of proteins beyond antibodies?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods for protein design, particularly in terms of efficiency and effectiveness?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how do these limitations affect its applicability and performance?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the method was adapted for antibody design, particularly in terms of the input and output representations?
- How does the method handle the alignment of sequences, and what are the implications of this alignment for the generation of valid protein sequences?
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies in notation and definition throughout the paper, particularly in the context of the energy-based model and the denoising process?
