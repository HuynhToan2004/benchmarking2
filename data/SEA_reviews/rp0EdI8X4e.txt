 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Faithful Vision-Language Concept (FVLC) model, an extension of the Label-Free Concept Bottleneck Model (CBM) aimed at enhancing the stability and interpretability of machine learning models. The FVLC model is designed to mitigate the instability issues associated with label-free CBMs, which can be sensitive to perturbations in concept sets. The paper proposes a formal definition for FVLC models, emphasizing four critical properties: similarity, stability, prediction result stability, and anti-interference ability. The authors also introduce a novel metric for evaluating the faithfulness of vision-language interpretation, which is tested against various perturbations and baseline models. The experimental results show that FVLC maintains accuracy and stability, outperforming baseline models in certain scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant problem of instability in label-free CBMs, which is a critical issue in the field of machine learning.
- It provides a clear and formal definition of the Faithful Vision-Language Concept (FVLC), which is crucial for ensuring the stability and interpretability of machine learning models.
- The paper introduces a novel metric for evaluating the faithfulness of vision-language interpretation, which is an innovative approach to assessing the performance of machine learning models.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of their method, showing that FVLC maintains accuracy and stability under various perturbations.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental results presented in the paper are somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on a single baseline model and a few datasets. More comprehensive experiments involving a variety of baseline models and datasets would strengthen the paper's claims.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the proposed method across different types of models and datasets, which is a significant concern for its practical application.
- The paper does not include a discussion on how the method performs on models that are not trained on the same dataset as the concept set, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of the surrogate loss used in the optimization process, which is crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness and reliability.
- The paper does not include a comparison with other interpretability models, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results on the generalizability of the proposed method across different types of models and datasets?
- How does the proposed method perform on models that are not trained on the same dataset as the concept set?
- Could the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the surrogate loss in the optimization process?
- How does the proposed method compare to other interpretability models, particularly in terms of accuracy and stability under perturbations?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential future directions for research?
