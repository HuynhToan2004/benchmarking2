 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to modeling probability distributions over tree-structured graphs using Sum-Product-Set Networks (SPSNs). SPSNs extend Sum-Product Networks (SPNs) by incorporating a set unit that models finite random sets, enabling the representation of tree-structured graphs. The authors demonstrate the tractability of SPSNs under mild assumptions and show that they can perform comparably to neural network-based models in graph classification tasks. The paper also discusses the exchangeability of SPSNs and their robustness to missing values. However, the experimental results show that SPSNs perform worse than neural networks, and the paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing models and a thorough discussion on the tractability and expressivity of SPSNs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of the set unit in Sum-Product-Set Networks (SPSNs) is a significant contribution to the field, allowing for the modeling of tree-structured graphs with heterogeneous nodes.
- SPSNs are tractable under mild assumptions, which is a significant advantage over other models.
- The paper provides a thorough discussion on the exchangeability of SPSNs, which is an important aspect of their design.
- The experiments are well-conducted, with results showing that SPSNs perform comparably to intractable models based on neural networks, demonstrating their competitiveness.
- The paper is innovative in its approach to modeling tree-structured graphs, which is a challenging problem in probabilistic modeling.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing models, particularly those that are also designed for tree-structured graphs, which could provide a better understanding of the advantages and limitations of SPSNs.
- The experimental results show that SPSNs perform worse than neural networks, which raises questions about the practical utility of SPSNs.
- The paper does not discuss the tractability and expressivity of SPSNs in detail, which are crucial aspects of the model's design.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of missing values in the experiments, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of SPSNs and how these limitations might affect their practical application.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed explanations of the experimental setups and results, particularly in the appendix.
