 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Divided Attention (DivA), a novel method for unsupervised multi-object motion segmentation, leveraging an adversarial conditional encoder-decoder architecture. DivA modifies the Slot Attention architecture to use the image as context to decode optical flow, without attempting to reconstruct the image. This method allows for the segmentation of independently moving regions in real-time, without requiring ground truth or manual supervision. The approach has been evaluated on various datasets, including DAVIS, SegTrack, and others, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency. The method's key contributions include the use of adversarial training to separate objects and its ability to handle different numbers of objects and image resolutions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to unsupervised multi-object motion segmentation.
- The methodology is well-explained, with a clear description of the proposed method and its implementation, including the use of adversarial training to separate objects.
- The paper is organized effectively, with a comprehensive literature review that positions the research within the current state of the art.
- The experimental results are robust and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, with a thorough ablation study that validates the design choices.
- The method is flexible and can handle different numbers of objects and image resolutions, which is a significant advantage over some existing methods.
- The paper includes code and pre-trained models, enhancing reproducibility and accessibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from more qualitative results and a broader range of datasets to demonstrate the generalizability of the method.
- There are some inconsistencies in the experimental results and the methodology, such as the use of different metrics and settings across different datasets, which could confuse the reader.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of over-segmentation, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.
- The paper's discussion on the role of adversarial training in improving segmentation accuracy is not convincing, and the paper could benefit from more detailed ablation studies to clarify its impact.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of runtime and computational efficiency.

**Questions:**

- Can the method be adapted to handle different types of objects, such as static objects in a video?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the number of slots is not known in advance?
