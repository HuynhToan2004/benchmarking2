 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel feature selection method for robust novelty detection, termed "STYLIST," which focuses on identifying and discarding environment-biased features. This method aims to enhance the generalization capabilities of novelty detection models by reducing the impact of spurious correlations. The approach is validated through experiments on three datasets, demonstrating improved performance in detecting novel content while ignoring irrelevant style shifts. The paper also introduces COCOShift, a synthetic benchmark designed to evaluate robust novelty detection across varying levels of spuriousness. Despite its innovative approach and the introduction of a new benchmark, the paper has been critiqued for its limited scope in terms of the datasets used and the depth of experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel feature selection method, STYLIST, which is capable of identifying and discarding environment-biased features, enhancing the generalization capabilities of novelty detection models.
- The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method on three datasets and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method through various experiments.
- The paper introduces COCOShift, a synthetic benchmark designed to evaluate robust novelty detection, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors provide a clear and well-written presentation, with detailed experimental results and a well-structured methodology.
- The paper addresses an important and practical problem of robust novelty detection, which is relevant in many real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited, as it only evaluates the proposed method on three datasets and does not compare it with other state-of-the-art methods.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, and the paper lacks a broader range of experiments, such as those involving sub-population shift setups and different types of datasets.
- The paper's assumptions and the practical applicability of the method are not thoroughly discussed, particularly concerning the identification of style and content in practical scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity and detail in some sections of the paper, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.
- The paper's contribution to the field is not clearly articulated, and the benefits of the proposed method are not convincingly demonstrated across a variety of datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the assumptions made in the paper, particularly regarding the identification of style and content in practical scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the training and testing datasets are drawn from different distributions?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of datasets and the rationale behind their selection?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the practical implications and limitations of the proposed method?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and distinctions made in the paper, particularly between "novelty detection" and "anomaly detection"?
