 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Message Passing Link Predictor (MPLP), a novel link prediction model that utilizes quasi-orthogonal vectors to estimate link-level structural features, thereby addressing the limitations of existing models like GCN and SAGE in capturing joint structural features. MPLP is designed to approximate the Common Neighbor (CN) heuristic, which is a fundamental aspect of link prediction. The paper presents a theoretical analysis and empirical evaluations demonstrating that MPLP outperforms existing methods in link prediction tasks, particularly in non-attributed graphs. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques, and the practical utility of the method is questioned due to its limited applicability to attributed graphs and its reliance on specific graph properties.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel approach to link prediction by leveraging the orthogonality of input vectors, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed MPLP in link prediction tasks.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is computationally efficient, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper introduces a new link prediction paradigm that consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines across a variety of graph benchmarks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques such as DotHash and distance encoding, without significant new contributions.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its scalability and efficiency.
- The method's reliance on specific graph properties, such as the presence of high-degree nodes or hubs, may limit its applicability to different types of graphs.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical utility of the method in attributed graphs, which are common in real-world applications.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its practical utility and applicability.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the graph does not have hubs, or the hubs are not well-connected to the rest of the graph?
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method?
- How does the method perform in attributed graphs, and what are the specific challenges in applying it to these types of graphs?
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical utility and applicability?
- How does the method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- What are the specific challenges in applying the method to graphs with different properties, such as graphs with low-degree nodes or graphs with no hubs?
