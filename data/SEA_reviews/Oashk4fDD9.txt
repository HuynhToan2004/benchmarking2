 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for pre-training a seq2seq Transformer model using synthetic data derived from Finite State Transducers (FSTs). This method aims to inject structural inductive biases into the model, which are then evaluated on tasks such as few-shot text editing and grapheme-to-phoneme conversion. The authors demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms several baselines, including a naive pre-training method, Set, and task embeddings. The methodology involves pre-training on FSTs and fine-tuning on natural data, showing improvements in systematic generalization and few-shot learning. However, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks and the scalability of the method to larger models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical structure that makes it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is simple, adjustable, and efficient, making it easy to reproduce and extend to other tasks.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are convincing, demonstrating that the proposed method outperforms several baselines.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to injecting structural inductive biases into a Transformer, which is crucial for learning from little data and improving generalization outside the training distribution.
- The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, including systematic generalization and few-shot learning for FST-like tasks, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on downstream tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks, raising concerns about the method's applicability beyond the specific tasks evaluated.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, such as LIME, to provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The method's scalability to larger models and different architectures, such as encoder-only models, is not discussed, which limits the understanding of its applicability in diverse settings.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights into its strengths and weaknesses.
- The method's focus on deterministic FSTs might limit its applicability to more complex or non-deterministic tasks, which could be a significant drawback in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods like LIME, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the method to larger models or different architectures, such as encoder-only models?
- What are the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how do these affect its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability of the method to larger models or different architectures, and how these might affect the method's performance and applicability?
