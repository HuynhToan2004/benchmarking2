 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for identifying backdoor data in training datasets, aiming to address the limitations of existing methods that either require access to clean data or manual threshold settings. The proposed method, named Mask-aware Scaled Prediction Consistency (MSPC), utilizes a hierarchical data splitting optimization problem and a bi-level optimization-based approach to identify backdoor data effectively. The method is evaluated across various datasets and attack scenarios, demonstrating its effectiveness in identifying backdoor samples without the need for additional clean data or manual threshold settings. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the method in real-world scenarios and its generalizability across different types of backdoor triggers.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a practical problem by focusing on identifying backdoor data without requiring access to clean data or manually setting detection thresholds, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the limitations of existing methods and how the proposed approach addresses these issues.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental results are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is novel, leveraging a mask-aware SPC loss and a hierarchical data splitting optimization method, which is a significant advancement over existing methods.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results, which enhances its readability and accessibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's practicality is questionable, particularly in scenarios where the attacker has access to the model and can optimize the mask to evade detection, which may limit its effectiveness.
- The method's generalizability is limited, as it relies on the assumption that the attacker's trigger is located within a specific region of the image, which may not hold true for all types of backdoor triggers.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The experimental setup could be improved, particularly in terms of the variety of attacks used and the inclusion of more recent and diverse backdoor attacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed method and a discussion on how these limitations could be addressed in future work.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform against adaptive attacks where the attacker has full knowledge of the detection method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method, including the time required for training and inference?
- How does the proposed method handle backdoor attacks that do not rely on a specific region of the image, such as those that use noise-like triggers?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of attacks and the parameters used in the experiments?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that do not require access to clean data or manual threshold settings, such as those discussed in the related work section?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their method and propose potential avenues for future research to address these limitations?
