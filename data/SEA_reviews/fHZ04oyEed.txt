 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the problem of learning representations from interventional data, focusing on how these representations can be made robust to distribution shifts. It introduces RepLIn, a method that enforces statistical independence between features during interventions to enhance predictive performance on interventional data. The paper presents several experiments, including synthetic and real-world datasets, to demonstrate the effectiveness of RepLIn in improving predictive accuracy under different conditions. The method is evaluated against various baselines, showing improvements in predictive performance and statistical independence. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in defining key terms, such as "interventional data," and for not sufficiently distinguishing its contributions from existing literature.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear motivation and a strong narrative that guides the reader through the proposed method and its evaluation.
- The experiments are comprehensive, including both synthetic and real-world datasets, and are well-designed to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning representations from interventional data, which is a significant and relevant problem in machine learning.
- The method is well-motivated and the experimental results are convincing, showing improvements over baseline methods in most cases.
- The use of kernel canonical correlation analysis to evaluate the independence of learned representations is a novel and interesting approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining key terms, such as "interventional data," which could confuse readers and limit the understanding of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the relationship between its contributions and existing literature, particularly in the area of causal representation learning.
- The experiments are somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on specific types of data and not adequately demonstrating the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implications of enforcing statistical independence in learned representations, particularly in real-world applications where causal graphs may not be known or easily identifiable.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how it might perform in more complex or varied data scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the definition and usage of "interventional data" throughout the paper?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in causal representation learning, particularly in terms of learning meaningful representations that can be interpreted or used in downstream tasks?
- What are the specific advantages of using the proposed method over other methods that might also achieve statistical independence in learned representations?
- Could you provide more details on how the method performs in more complex or varied data scenarios, such as those with multiple interventional variables or more complex causal graphs?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the causal graph is not known or easily identifiable, and what are the implications for the effectiveness and interpretability of the learned representations?
