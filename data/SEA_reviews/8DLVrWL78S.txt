 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FastSBDD, a novel approach to structure-based drug design that optimizes for binding affinity by predicting ligand graphs and atom types. It uses a two-phase method where the first phase predicts ligand graphs from a database, and the second phase predicts atom types and coordinates. This method aims to achieve significant computational efficiency and parameter reduction compared to existing methods. Theoretical analysis is provided to demonstrate the expressivity of GNNs in this context, showing that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art methods in terms of efficiency and accuracy. However, the paper lacks comprehensive comparisons with other generative models and does not discuss the limitations of its approach in detail.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The methodology is innovative, particularly in its approach to using a database of unlabelled graph structures, which is not commonly used in similar tasks.
- The paper demonstrates significant improvements in efficiency and accuracy compared to existing methods, as evidenced by the experimental results.
- The theoretical analysis is thorough and provides valuable insights into the limitations of GNNs in structure-based drug design, which is an important and timely area of research.
- The use of a database of unlabelled graph structures allows for a more focused approach to drug design, which could potentially lead to more effective and efficient drug discovery processes.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other generative models, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The discussion on the limitations of the proposed method is not detailed enough, which could leave readers with a limited understanding of its practical applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The method's reliance on a database of unlabelled graph structures may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where such data is not readily available.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of GNNs in structure-based drug design, which could provide a more balanced view of the method's capabilities and challenges.
- The paper's focus on computational efficiency and parameter reduction might not be the primary concern for many drug design applications, where other factors like accuracy and generalization might be more critical.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method performs in scenarios where the database of unlabelled graph structures is not available or not comprehensive enough?
- How does the method handle the trade-off between binding affinity and other molecular properties, such as drug-likeness?
- Can the authors clarify how the method generalizes to different protein-ligand pairs and how it handles the variability in molecular structures?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the method in real-world applications, such as the availability of diverse and comprehensive databases of unlabelled graph structures?
- How does the method compare to other generative models in terms of efficiency, accuracy, and parameter reduction?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency and parameter reduction claims, possibly including a comparison with other methods in terms of runtime and model size?
