 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Equivalent Effect Abstraction (EEA) aimed at constructing MDP homomorphisms without requiring prior knowledge of symmetries. EEA operates by predicting the next state given a hypothetical action and then inferring the abstract state based on this prediction. This method is tested across various environments, including simple gridworlds and more complex Atari games, demonstrating improvements in sample efficiency over traditional methods like DQN. The approach is based on the concept of homomorphisms, which map from experienced state-action pairs to abstract states, and is supported by theoretical analysis and empirical evidence.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible.
- The methodology is straightforward and effective, with a focus on reducing the size of the state-action space, which can improve sample efficiency.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the proposed method, which is supported by empirical evidence across various environments, including tabular and deep RL settings.
- The approach is innovative, utilizing a novel concept of homomorphism that allows for the reduction of state-action pairs, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its implementation, including the use of algorithms and equations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the method scales to more complex environments, such as those with continuous action spaces.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the method performs in environments with stochastic dynamics, where predicting the next state is challenging.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could help in understanding the relative performance of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on the assumption of deterministic dynamics may limit its applicability in more complex, stochastic environments.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as its inability to handle environments with stochastic dynamics or unpredictable transitions.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in the clarity of figures and the organization of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform in environments with continuous action spaces, and what modifications are necessary for such environments?
- Can the method be extended to handle environments with stochastic dynamics, and if so, how is this handled?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of sample efficiency and overall performance?
- Is there a way to integrate the proposed method with existing homomorphic MDP methods that rely on symmetries to further reduce the size of the abstract state-action space?
- How does the method handle environments where the reward function depends on the previous action, and what implications does this have for the applicability of the method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process for the forward and backward models, particularly in terms of the model architectures used?
- How does the method ensure that the state-action pairs predicted by the forward and backward models are valid and meaningful in the context of the environment?
