 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the use of convex combinations of adversarial loss and Interval Bound Propagation (IBP) loss to optimize the trade-off between adversarial robustness and standard accuracy in neural networks. The approach, which includes two methods, CC-IBP and MTL-IBP, is shown to achieve state-of-the-art performance on several benchmarks, including MNIST, CIFAR-10, TinyImageNet, and downscaled ImageNet. The authors propose that the expressivity of the loss function, determined by a parameter alpha, is crucial for achieving optimal robustness-accuracy trade-offs. The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the relationship between the over-approximation coefficient and performance across different expressive losses, demonstrating the importance of expressivity in achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the motivation, contributions, and methodology, making it accessible and understandable.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and convincing, showing that the proposed methods achieve state-of-the-art performance on several benchmarks.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to combining adversarial loss and Interval Bound Propagation (IBP) loss, which is a significant contribution to the field of adversarial robustness.
- The analysis of the over-approximation coefficient and its impact on performance across different expressive losses provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between robustness and accuracy.
- The paper is well-positioned within the literature, effectively summarizing the state-of-the-art and demonstrating the superiority of the proposed methods over existing techniques.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed methods, particularly in comparison to other state-of-the-art methods.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of the adversarial attack methods used and the hyperparameters involved.
- The novelty of the proposed methods is questionable, as similar approaches have been previously explored, and the paper does not adequately distinguish its contributions from existing literature.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of training time and the computational overhead of the proposed methods, which could limit their practical applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer differentiation from existing methods, particularly in the area of multi-task learning and loss functions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed methods, especially in comparison to other state-of-the-art methods?
- How does the choice of adversarial attack method (e.g., PGD, CW, etc.) affect the results, and how sensitive are the results to different hyperparameters?
- In the experimental setup, how were the hyperparameters chosen, and what impact do they have on the results?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of training time and computational overhead to other state-of-the-art methods?
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of their approach and how it differs from existing methods, particularly in the area of multi-task learning and loss functions?
- How does the proposed method perform against different types of adversarial attacks, such as black-box attacks, and what are the implications for the robustness of the models?
