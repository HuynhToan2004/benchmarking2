 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to offline reinforcement learning (RL) by refining the behavior policy used in behavior regularization, aiming to improve performance and reduce computational overhead. This approach, termed Conservative Policy Iteration (CPI), iteratively refines the reference policy, ensuring policy improvement while avoiding out-of-distribution actions. The paper provides both theoretical and empirical evidence, demonstrating that CPI can outperform existing methods on benchmark datasets like D4RL. The method's simplicity and ease of implementation are highlighted, with the authors providing a detailed algorithm and experimental setup. However, the paper's experimental scope is limited, and the empirical results do not convincingly surpass state-of-the-art methods in all cases.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to offline RL by refining the behavior policy used in behavior regularization, which is both theoretically sound and empirically effective.
- The proposed method, CPI, is simple to implement and can be integrated with existing methods like TD3+BC with minimal modifications.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and includes a detailed algorithm and experimental setup.
- The empirical results show that CPI can outperform existing methods in most cases, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the convergence of CPI, which supports the method's robustness and reliability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental scope is limited, focusing primarily on D4RL benchmarks, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The empirical results do not convincingly surpass state-of-the-art methods in all cases, particularly in the Mujoco tasks where EDAC shows better performance.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for more complex environments or larger datasets.
- The paper's experimental results are not consistently superior to existing methods, and the performance gains are sometimes marginal or inconsistent across different datasets.
- There is a lack of comparison with other baseline methods, such as CQL, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed insights into how the proposed method performs in more complex environments or with larger datasets?
- How does the proposed method compare to other baseline methods, such as CQL, in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the choice of using a Gaussian policy with a learnable mean and its impact on the method's performance?
- How does the method perform in different settings, such as discrete action spaces, and what are the implications for its applicability and effectiveness?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the method to offline-to-online tasks and the challenges or opportunities this might present?
