 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel federated learning approach for fine-tuning multilingual language models (LLMs) across multiple clients, focusing on low-resource languages. The approach, termed "Federated Prompt Tuning," involves fine-tuning a "prompt encoder" at each client, which encodes task-specific prompts into a vector, while the base LLM remains frozen. The global prompt encoder is then averaged across clients, enhancing model performance across languages. Experiments conducted on benchmarks like XGLUE demonstrate the method's effectiveness, particularly in low-resource settings. The paper also discusses the reduction in communication and computational costs compared to traditional methods. However, concerns were raised regarding the clarity of the method's motivation and its comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the novelty of the approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical structure that effectively communicates the ideas.
- The proposed method, Federated Prompt Tuning, is a novel approach to fine-tuning multilingual LLMs, which is particularly beneficial for low-resource languages.
- The experiments conducted on the XGLUE benchmark demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in accuracy and efficiency.
- The paper addresses a significant issue in the field by focusing on fine-tuning LLMs for low-resource languages, which is an important and under-explored area.
- The use of a prompt encoder to fine-tune LLMs is an innovative approach that could potentially reduce computational costs and improve data efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the method's design, particularly in the use of a prompt encoder and the decision to average global prompts.
- The comparison with other parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods like LoRA is insufficient, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the different methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially concerning the potential for overfitting and the impact on model generalization.
- The evaluation is limited to specific benchmarks and tasks, which may not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness across different scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for ensuring ethical considerations in the deployment of AI technologies.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the design choices of the prompt encoder and the decision to average global prompts?
- How does the proposed method compare to other parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, such as LoRA, in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness?
- Could the authors expand the evaluation to include more diverse benchmarks and tasks to better demonstrate the generalizability of the method?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where data is highly imbalanced or where there are significant differences in data distribution across clients?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the datasets used and the model architectures employed?
- How does the method address the potential for overfitting, especially in low-resource settings where data availability is limited?
