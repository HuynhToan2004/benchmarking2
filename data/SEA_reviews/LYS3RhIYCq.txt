 **Summary:**
The paper investigates scaling laws for imitation learning in single-agent games, specifically focusing on Atari and NetHack environments. It explores the relationship between model size, dataset size, and performance metrics such as validation loss and mean return, demonstrating that these metrics scale as a power law with respect to the compute budget. The authors extend prior work on scaling laws in reinforcement learning to imitation learning, showing that the optimal model and dataset sizes for a given compute budget can be predicted. The study is supported by experiments on various Atari games and NetHack, highlighting the potential of scaling up model and data sizes to improve performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive and systematic analysis of the scaling laws for imitation learning in single-agent games.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of scaling up model and dataset sizes in improving performance.
- The paper provides a novel approach to forecasting the training requirements for compute-optimal neural BC agents in NetHack, which could potentially lead to significant advancements in the field.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear presentation of the problem, methodology, and results, and includes a detailed discussion on the limitations of the study.
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of the scaling laws for imitation learning in single-agent games, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the "compute budget," which is a critical concept used throughout the study.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the scaling laws for the field of imitation learning, particularly in terms of practical applications and future research directions.
- The experimental setup and methodology are not sufficiently detailed, which could limit the reproducibility and transparency of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach, such as the potential for overfitting and the generalizability of the findings to other types of games or imitation learning tasks.
- The paper's claims about the universality of the scaling laws are not well-supported, and the limitations of the study are not adequately discussed.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of the "compute budget" in their study?
- How do the authors address the potential for overfitting in their experiments, particularly in light of the large dataset sizes used?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specific hyperparameters used and the training conditions for the different models?
- How do the authors ensure that the scaling laws observed in their study are not specific to the games or models used, but rather represent a general trend in imitation learning?
- Could the authors discuss the implications of their findings for the broader field of imitation learning, particularly in terms of practical applications and future research directions?
