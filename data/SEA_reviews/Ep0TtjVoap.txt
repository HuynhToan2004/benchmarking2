 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ToRA, a novel method that integrates natural language reasoning with external tool usage to solve complex mathematical problems. It utilizes a dataset of interactive tool-use trajectories for mathematical problems, which are used to fine-tune open-source models, achieving state-of-the-art performance on 10 mathematical reasoning datasets. The approach involves training models to generate natural language rationales and programs, which are then executed using external tools to solve mathematical problems. The methodology includes a novel approach called output space shaping, which allows for diverse plausible reasoning steps and improves model performance. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its reliance on a single dataset, potential issues with generalization, and lack of clarity in certain sections.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for training language models to solve mathematical problems by integrating natural language reasoning with tool usage, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method, ToRA, shows strong performance on various mathematical reasoning tasks, outperforming existing methods.
- The use of output space shaping is a novel and effective strategy for improving model performance by allowing for diverse plausible reasoning steps.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing significant improvements over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies heavily on a single dataset (ToRA-Corpus) for training, which might limit the generalization of the results to other datasets or problem domains.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the descriptions of the training process and the roles of different models.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for hallucination in the generated natural language rationales, which could mislead or confuse users.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other methods, particularly those that use different types of reasoning or tools.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the training process, particularly how different models are used and how the outputs are combined?
- How do you ensure that the generated natural language rationales are accurate and do not lead to hallucination?
- Can you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of your method, especially in areas like education or financial modeling?
- How does your method compare to other methods that use different types of reasoning or tools, such as symbolic reasoning or knowledge-based systems?
- Could you provide more details on the generalization of your method to other types of mathematical problems or datasets?
