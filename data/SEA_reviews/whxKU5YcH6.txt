 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SGOOD, a novel graph-level representation learning method aimed at detecting out-of-distribution (OOD) graphs. SGOOD constructs a super graph of substructures for each graph, leveraging a two-level graph encoding pipeline to encode task-agnostic substructures and their relationships into expressive representations. This method is supported by three substructure-preserving graph augmentation techniques to enhance the model's ability to distinguish between in-distribution (ID) and OOD graphs. The approach is validated through extensive experiments, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods on various datasets. However, concerns are raised regarding the clarity and depth of the experimental results, the novelty of the approach, and the practical applicability of the method in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel graph-level representation learning method, SGOOD, which effectively encodes task-agnostic substructures to learn powerful representations for graph-level out-of-distribution (OOD) detection.
- SGOOD proposes a two-level graph encoding pipeline and three graph augmentation techniques, which are well-motivated and supported by theoretical analysis.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with extensive experiments demonstrating the superior performance of SGOOD over existing methods.
- The methodology is straightforward and the proposed techniques are simple yet effective, making the approach accessible and understandable.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods and a comprehensive set of experiments that validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scalability and efficiency.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as similar methods have been proposed previously, and the paper does not adequately distinguish its contributions from existing works.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper does not sufficiently address the practical applicability of the proposed method in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity in the experimental results, particularly in the ablation studies and the comparisons with baseline methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational requirements and the efficiency of the proposed method, which could limit its practical implementation in large-scale applications.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the computational complexity of the proposed method and provide more details on its scalability and efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of in-distribution (ID) classification accuracy? What are the implications of prioritizing OOD detection over ID accuracy?
- Can you provide more details on the training and inference time of the proposed method, and compare it with other baseline methods?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of substructure preservation during graph augmentation, and what are the implications of this approach for the overall performance of the model?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the super graph construction method and its impact on the model's performance?
- How does the model handle the potential for overfitting, especially in scenarios where the training data is limited?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and the methodology used for OOD graph generation?
