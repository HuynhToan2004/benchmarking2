 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of Entropic Wasserstein Regression (EWR) in network pruning, aiming to address the challenges of noisy gradients in large-scale neural networks. EWR is proposed as a robust alternative to traditional linear regression methods, leveraging the Wasserstein distance to balance noise reduction and covariance information preservation. The approach is evaluated across various network architectures and noise levels, showing comparable or superior performance to existing methods. The theoretical analysis and experimental results support the effectiveness of EWR in handling noisy gradients and maintaining network performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed method.
- The use of Entropic Wasserstein Regression (EWR) is novel and effectively addresses the issue of noisy gradients in large-scale neural networks.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust and supports the effectiveness of EWR in network pruning.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various network architectures and noise levels.
- The application of EWR to network pruning is innovative and shows potential for improving the robustness and efficiency of neural network training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- The comparison with existing methods like CBS is not fair, as the experimental setup does not include the same level of noise as reported in the original CBS paper.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous experimental setup, including a broader range of noise levels and different types of noise to better evaluate the robustness of the method.
- The novelty of the method is somewhat limited, as it builds on existing works like linear regression and Wasserstein distance.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the experimental results compare when using the same noise levels as reported in the original CBS paper?
- How does the computational complexity of EWR compare to other methods like CBS, especially in large-scale applications?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical application?
- Is there a possibility to extend the theoretical analysis to include more complex network architectures, such as those used in large-scale applications like image recognition?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of noise, such as adversarial attacks or noisy labels?
