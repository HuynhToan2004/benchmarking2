 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel variational inference framework for stochastic differential equations (SDEs) driven by fractional Brownian motion (fBM), focusing on the Markov approximation of fBM to enhance the computational efficiency and effectiveness of variational inference. The framework leverages neural networks to learn the drift, diffusion, and control terms within the variational posterior, optimizing the Hurst index to model the nature of fractional noise. The paper includes both synthetic and real-world experiments, such as video prediction, to demonstrate the method's effectiveness. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity, particularly in the presentation of mathematical details and the need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to variational inference for stochastic differential equations (SDEs) driven by fractional Brownian motion (fBM), which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The authors have derived a closed-form expression for the optimal approximation coefficients, which is a valuable contribution to the community.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable to readers, and includes a variety of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of neural networks to learn the drift, diffusion, and control terms within the variational posterior is innovative and adds a modern approach to the field.
- The paper is the first to apply variational neural-SDEs to video perception, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from poor writing and lacks clarity in some sections, making it difficult for readers to follow the mathematical derivations and the experimental setups.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the accuracy of the Markov approximation.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the Markov approximation, which is a critical aspect of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not convincing enough, and the paper lacks a detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the computational complexity and the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of the number of OU processes used.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the results, particularly in the experiments involving video prediction?
- How does the paper address the limitations of the Markov approximation, and what are the implications of these limitations for the practical application of the method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of the number of OU processes used?
- In the experimental results, how do the authors ensure that the improvements observed are not due to the use of more parameters in the neural network architecture rather than the Markov approximation?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the choice of the Hurst index in the experiments, and how it impacts the results?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy with other methods, particularly in scenarios where the Markov approximation might not be as effective?
