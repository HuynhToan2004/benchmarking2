 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DPFormer, a novel framework designed to enhance the training of Transformer models under differential privacy constraints. It addresses the challenges of gradient clipping and attention distraction through innovative techniques like Phantom Clipping and Re-Attention Mechanism. These techniques aim to reduce computational overhead and mitigate the effects of long-tailed data distributions on attention mechanisms. The paper presents theoretical analyses and empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of these methods, showing improvements in computational efficiency and model performance on recommendation datasets. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the proposed methods and their effectiveness in more complex or diverse data scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, which is supported by empirical evidence, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper addresses significant challenges in the training of Transformer models under differential privacy constraints, focusing on the reduction of computational costs and mitigation of attention distraction.
- The proposed Phantom Clipping technique is innovative and provides a significant speedup in model training, which is a notable contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, showing the advantages of the proposed methods in terms of performance and efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty in some of its contributions, as the Phantom Clipping technique is similar to existing methods like Ghost Clipping, and the Re-Attention Mechanism is an adaptation of existing methods in Bayesian deep learning.
- The effectiveness of the proposed methods is not convincingly demonstrated across a broader range of datasets, particularly those with different characteristics such as long-tailedness and sparsity.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed methods with larger models or in more complex scenarios, which could limit their applicability.
- The paper's claims about the impact of long-tailed data on attention distraction are not well-supported by empirical evidence, particularly in more diverse or complex data scenarios.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need correction to improve the paper's professionalism and readability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of the computational efficiency and performance improvements claimed by the proposed methods?
- How do the proposed methods perform when scaled up to larger models, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in this regard?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of the Re-Attention Mechanism, particularly how it mitigates attention distraction in the context of differential privacy?
- Is there a possibility to extend the proposed methods to other types of data or tasks beyond recommendation systems, and how might this affect their effectiveness or applicability?
- How does the paper address the potential for overfitting or other training instabilities that could arise from the use of differentially private data?
