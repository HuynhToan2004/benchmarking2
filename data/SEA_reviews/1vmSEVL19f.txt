 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Direct Reward Fine-Tuning (DRaFT), a method for fine-tuning diffusion models to maximize differentiable reward functions, such as those derived from human preference models. DRaFT leverages gradient backpropagation through the full sampling process, which is optimized using LoRA and gradient checkpointing to reduce computational costs. The method is evaluated on various reward functions, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency compared to reinforcement learning-based approaches. DRaFT also includes variants such as DRaFT-K and DRaFT-LV, which truncate backpropagation to the last K steps of sampling and provide lower-variance gradient estimates, respectively. The paper provides extensive experiments and ablation studies, showing that DRaFT outperforms existing methods in terms of efficiency and performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a good literature review.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and efficient, with the ability to apply differentiable rewards to the final image, which is more straightforward than existing methods.
- The paper provides extensive experiments and ablation studies, including comparisons with various baselines and different reward functions, demonstrating the method's effectiveness and efficiency.
- The authors have conducted a comprehensive set of experiments, including on different reward functions and various sampling methods, which are well-documented and provide valuable insights into the method's performance and behavior.
- The paper introduces several novel methods, such as DRaFT-K and DRaFT-LV, which are simple and effective, and can be applied to a wide range of reward functions.
- The method's efficiency is highlighted by its ability to be 200x faster than RL algorithms, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other baseline methods, particularly in terms of the specifics of how the baseline models were implemented and tuned.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for reward hacking and the generalization of the method to other tasks or domains.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational costs and memory requirements of the DRaFT-K and DRaFT-LV methods, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- The method's applicability to other types of diffusion models, such as text-to-image diffusion models, is not discussed, which could limit its practical utility.
- The paper does not compare the method with other recent methods that optimize the diffusion model directly, such as those mentioned in the literature.
- The method's generalization ability to other types of tasks, such as image-to-image translation, is not explored, which could limit its applicability.

**Questions:**

- How does the method handle the potential for reward hacking, and what steps can be taken to mitigate this issue?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its generalization to other tasks or domains?
- How does the method compare in terms of computational costs and memory requirements with other methods, such as those mentioned in the literature?
- Can the method be applied to other types of diffusion models, such as text-to-image diffusion models, and if so, what are the implications for performance and efficiency?
