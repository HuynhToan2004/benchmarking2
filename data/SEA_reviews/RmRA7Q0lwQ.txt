 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the application of Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) in text generation, specifically in language models, to enhance prompt adherence and improve model performance. CFG, which was initially developed for text-to-image generation, is adapted to text-to-text models, demonstrating its effectiveness across various tasks such as code generation, question answering, and machine translation. The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of CFG across different prompting styles and models, showcasing its potential to reduce the need for larger models and enhance the faithfulness of responses to system prompts. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, as similar methods have been previously explored, and the paper's presentation could be improved for better clarity and comprehension.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations that make it easy to follow.
- The adaptation of Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) from text-to-image to text-to-text models is a significant contribution, demonstrating its effectiveness across various tasks such as code generation, question answering, and machine translation.
- The evaluation of CFG is thorough and comprehensive, covering a wide range of tasks and prompting styles, including zero-shot prompting, chain-of-thought prompting, long-form generation, and chatbot-style prompting.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the computational cost of CFG and demonstrates that a model using CFG can perform as well as a model twice its size, which is a crucial finding for practical applications.
- The use of CFG to enhance the faithfulness of responses to system prompts and its ability to reduce the need for larger models are significant contributions to the field of language modeling.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as CFG has been previously applied to text-to-text models, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the CFG technique is adapted from text-to-image to text-to-text models, which could limit the understanding of the method's effectiveness and applicability.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved to enhance clarity and comprehension, particularly in the explanation of the CFG technique and its impact on model performance.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of CFG, which is a significant concern for practical applications, especially in resource-constrained environments.
- The evaluation of CFG is limited to specific models and tasks, which may not generalize well to other models or tasks, reducing the paper's applicability and relevance.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the CFG technique is adapted from text-to-image to text-to-text models, and what specific modifications were made to the technique to make it effective in text-to-text scenarios?
- How does the CFG technique affect the diversity of generated text, particularly in scenarios where it might lead to repetitive or predictable outputs?
- In terms of computational cost, how does the CFG technique compare to other inference-time techniques, and what are the practical implications of using CFG in resource-constrained environments?
- The paper mentions that CFG can be used alongside other inference-time methods like Chain-of-Thought and Self-Consistency. Could you provide more details on how these methods are combined and what benefits this approach offers?
- Could you elaborate on the negative prompting technique used in the paper, particularly how it affects the model's performance and how it is implemented in practice?
- How does the paper address the potential for CFG to be misused, and what steps have been taken to ensure the ethical use of this technique?
