 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training-free decoding algorithm for language models (LLMs) that addresses the discrepancies between generative and discriminative LLM decoding procedures. This approach formulates decoding as a signaling game between a generator and a discriminator, aiming to find a Nash equilibrium that reconciles these incompatible scoring procedures. The method, termed "equilibrium-ranking," is evaluated across various benchmarks, showing improvements over existing decoding methods in question answering tasks. The paper proposes a novel application of game theory to language model decoding, highlighting the potential for better alignment between generative and discriminative outputs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of the consensus game and the equilibrium-ranking method is innovative and addresses a significant problem in language model decoding, particularly in reconciling generative and discriminative outputs.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms existing baselines on multiple benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.
- The integration of game theory with language model decoding is a novel and interesting approach that has the potential to advance the field.
- The paper is well-motivated and addresses a significant issue in language model decoding, which is crucial for advancing the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods like deliberation methods, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method's advantages and limitations.
- The experimental setup is limited to specific tasks, which might not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method across different types of language model applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the convergence of the equilibrium-ranking method and its computational efficiency, especially in larger language models.
- There is a lack of clarity on the definition and role of the "correctness parameter" in the model, which could lead to confusion about the model's functionality and assumptions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods like deliberation methods to better understand the advantages and limitations of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform on different types of language model applications beyond question answering tasks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the convergence of the equilibrium-ranking method and its computational efficiency, especially in larger language models?
- What is the definition and role of the "correctness parameter" in the model, and how does it affect the model's functionality and assumptions?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of language model scale, and could it potentially improve performance in larger models?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and its applicability in different scenarios?
