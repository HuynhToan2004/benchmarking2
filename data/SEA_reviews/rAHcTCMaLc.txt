 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Maximum Entropy Reinforcement Learning (MaxEnt RL) algorithm named S2AC, which utilizes Stein Variational Gradient Descent (SVGD) to optimize the policy distribution. This method allows for the computation of entropy in a closed-form, which is crucial for MaxEnt RL. The algorithm models the policy as a parameterized sampler from an Energy-Based Model (EBM) over Q-values, enhancing the expressivity of the policy distribution. Empirical results demonstrate that S2AC outperforms other MaxEnt RL algorithms such as SQL and SAC in various environments, including a new multi-goal environment and the MuJoCo benchmark. Despite its theoretical contributions, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty, lack of rigorous proofs, and unclear exposition in certain sections.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The proposed method, S2AC, is simple, efficient, and effective, with a clear explanation of its components and their roles in the algorithm.
- The empirical results are strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of S2AC in various environments, including a new multi-goal environment and the MuJoCo benchmark.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that validate the proposed method's performance and demonstrate its ability to recover the ground truth entropy in different settings.
- The use of SVGD to estimate the entropy of the policy distribution is a novel approach that provides a closed-form expression for the entropy of the policy distribution.
- The paper includes a thorough discussion on the theoretical aspects of the method, including the invertibility of SVGD and the computation of the trace term in the entropy formula.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a rigorous proof for some of the theoretical claims, such as the invertibility of SVGD and the computation of the trace term in the entropy formula.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it builds upon existing works like SQL and SAC, with the main difference being the use of SVGD for entropy estimation.
- The exposition in some sections of the paper is unclear, particularly in the explanation of the SVGD algorithm and the computational complexity of the entropy formula.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the method, such as the computational cost and the potential for mode collapse in the policy distribution.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other MaxEnt RL algorithms and a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the entropy formula's computational complexity.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition of the function $h$ in Algorithm 1 and its relationship to the function $\Delta f$ in SVGD?
- How does the entropy formula depend on the number of particles in SVGD, and what are the implications of this dependence for the computational complexity of the algorithm?
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of the entropy formula and how it scales with the number of particles in SVGD?
- How does the proposed method compare to other MaxEnt RL algorithms, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and sample complexity?
- Could you elaborate on the practical implications of the mode collapse issue in SVGD and how it affects the performance of the policy distribution?
- How does the paper address the potential issue of mode collapse in SVGD, and what measures have been taken to mitigate this risk?
