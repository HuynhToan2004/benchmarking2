 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TS-LLM, a novel tree-search framework that leverages a learned value function to guide LLMs' decoding ability, applicable to various tasks beyond reasoning. TS-LLM utilizes AlphaZero-like tree-search algorithms to enhance LLM's reasoning and planning capabilities, potentially improving performance in tasks like RLHF alignment. The framework's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive experiments, showcasing improvements over baseline methods like CoT. However, the paper's clarity and organization need significant improvement, with concerns regarding the fairness of comparisons and the practical applicability of the method in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive related work section that effectively situates the research within the current landscape.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and well-motivated, with a clear focus on improving the performance of LLMs through a novel approach to tree search.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, comparing it against various baselines and demonstrating its effectiveness across multiple tasks.
- The method's potential for guiding LLM training and its applicability to different tasks and LLM sizes are highlighted, suggesting a versatile approach that could be widely adopted.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and a detailed appendix that provides additional information and analysis.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear, cohesive structure, with some sections and figures (e.g., Figure 1) not effectively contributing to the overall narrative.
- The comparison with other methods like CoT is potentially unfair due to differences in the search space and the computational resources required.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly concerning the computational demands of the proposed tree-search algorithms.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could undermine the credibility of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the practical applicability of the method in real-world scenarios, especially in terms of its scalability and the practicality of its assumptions about the availability of training data.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and function of Figure 1 in the context of the paper, particularly in relation to the overall narrative and the methodology described?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational resources and search space to other methods like CoT, especially in scenarios where the search space is not identical?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or use cases where the method's scalability has been demonstrated, particularly in large-scale or real-world applications?
- How does the method handle the issue of over-reliance on well-designed prompts, and what steps have been taken to mitigate this risk?
- Could the authors discuss the practical implications of the method's assumptions about the availability of training data, and how these assumptions might affect the method's applicability in different scenarios?
