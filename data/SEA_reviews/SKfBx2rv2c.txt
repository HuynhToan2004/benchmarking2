 **Summary:**
The paper presents a novel approach to algorithmic recourse, focusing on the generation of counterfactual explanations that preserve causal relationships in the absence of explicit causal graphs. The authors propose two methods, AR-L2 and AR-Nuc, which leverage non-linear Independent Component Analysis (ICA) to identify and constrain the variation of exogenous noise, thereby preserving causal relationships among input features. These methods are evaluated against various baselines on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating their effectiveness in generating actionable and feasible counterfactual explanations. The paper also discusses the scalability and computational efficiency of the proposed methods, although some concerns about the assumptions and the clarity of the methodology are noted.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to algorithmic recourse by leveraging non-linear Independent Component Analysis (ICA) to identify and constrain the variation of exogenous noise, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed methods, AR-L2 and AR-Nuc, are well-motivated and theoretically grounded, providing a solid foundation for the research.
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the problem, methodology, and results, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods in generating counterfactual explanations that preserve causal relationships and are actionable and feasible.
- The paper addresses a critical issue in the field of algorithmic recourse, which is the generation of counterfactual explanations that are not only interpretable but also actionable, thus contributing to the development of more effective and trustworthy machine learning systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper makes several strong assumptions, such as the additive, non-recursive, and Markovian nature of the Structural Causal Model (SCM), which may not hold in all practical scenarios.
- The methodology relies heavily on the availability of a causal graph, which is often not available in real-world applications, making the proposed methods less practical.
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the causal graph is obtained, which is a critical component of the proposed method.
- The scalability of the proposed methods to high-dimensional datasets is not adequately addressed, and the computational efficiency of the methods is not discussed in detail.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its methods with existing approaches, such as those that do not require a causal graph, which could limit the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed methods and a clearer explanation of the assumptions and methodologies used.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the causal graph is obtained and how it is used in the proposed methods?
- How do the authors ensure that the assumptions made in the paper hold in practical scenarios, especially when the data may not be generated by an additive SCM?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of their methods with existing approaches, particularly those that do not require a causal graph?
- How do the proposed methods compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability to other state-of-the-art methods?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the causal graph used and the parameters of the SCM?
- How do the authors address the potential issues of overfitting in the model, especially when the data used for training is limited?
