 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel accelerated stochastic optimizer named NAG-GS, which leverages a semi-implicit Gauss-Seidel type discretization of an accelerated Nesterov-like Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE). This method aims to enhance the stability and convergence of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) in training machine learning models. The authors provide theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, demonstrating the robustness and competitive performance of NAG-GS against state-of-the-art methods. The method's robustness to learning rate selection and its memory efficiency are highlighted. However, the paper's scope is limited to specific datasets and models, and it does not sufficiently address the computational complexity or the generalization of the method to broader applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel semi-implicit Gauss-Seidel type discretization of an accelerated Nesterov-like Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE), which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors provide both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, demonstrating the robustness and competitive performance of the proposed method.
- The method is shown to be robust to the selection of learning rate values and is memory-efficient compared to other optimizers.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear and concise presentation of the proposed method and its theoretical foundations.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of their method, showing that it outperforms other optimizers in terms of robustness and efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The method's generalization to broader applications is not adequately discussed, and it is unclear whether the proposed method can be applied to other optimization problems beyond those tested.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other optimizers, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and memory requirements.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to the quadratic case, and it is not clear how the results would generalize to other types of functions.
- The paper does not address the issue of convergence speed, which is a significant drawback compared to other optimizers like Adam.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and scope.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the dataset or the complexity of the model?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the gradient noise has unbounded variance?
- Can the authors discuss the potential for applying the proposed method to other optimization problems beyond those tested in the paper?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and memory requirements to other optimizers like Adam?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the Gauss-Seidel splitting scheme and its impact on the method's performance?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of convergence speed, and what steps could be taken to improve this aspect?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the hardware used and the specifics of the hyperparameter tuning process?
