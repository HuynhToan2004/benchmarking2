 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel hierarchical likelihood learning framework for subject-specific predictions using deep neural networks (DNNs) with Poisson outputs and gamma random effects. The framework, termed h-likelihood, is designed to handle clustered count data with high-cardinality categorical features, improving prediction performance by capturing both nonlinear effects of input variables and subject-specific cluster effects. The methodology optimizes a single objective function, which yields maximum likelihood estimators for fixed parameters and best unbiased predictors for random effects. The paper also explores feature selection methods using multi-head attention layers and sparsemax functions, enhancing practical performance and learning efficiency. Extensive experiments and real-data analyses are conducted to validate the proposed method's effectiveness, although concerns are raised about the clarity of presentation and the depth of experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel hierarchical likelihood learning framework for subject-specific predictions using deep neural networks with Poisson outputs and gamma random effects, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology optimizes a single objective function, which yields maximum likelihood estimators for fixed parameters and best unbiased predictors for random effects, enhancing the practical application of the model.
- The paper provides a clear and concise presentation of the methodology, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- Extensive experiments and real-data analyses are conducted to validate the proposed method's effectiveness, demonstrating its applicability and utility in real-world scenarios.
- The introduction of feature selection methods using multi-head attention layers and sparsemax functions is a valuable addition to the field, potentially improving the efficiency and effectiveness of data analysis.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the h-likelihood and its role in the model, which could confuse readers unfamiliar with the concept.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the identifiability of the model and the potential impact of different choices of random effects on the model's performance.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly in the real data analysis section, which could limit the reproducibility and evaluation of the proposed method.
- There is a need for a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, such as Gaussian random effects and other non-Poisson models, to establish the superiority of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a clearer presentation of the main contributions and a more straightforward explanation of the technical aspects, such as the use of sparsemax functions and the derivation of the h-likelihood.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the h-likelihood and its role in the model, possibly with a worked-out example to illustrate its use?
- How does the choice of random effects distribution (gamma in this case) affect the model's performance, and are there other distributions that could be considered?
- Can the authors elaborate on the identifiability of the model and how it is ensured in the proposed framework?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and predictive accuracy?
- Could the authors provide more details on the real data analysis experiments, including the dataset used, the experimental setup, and the results obtained?
- What are the main contributions of this paper, and how do they differ from existing methods?
