 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FLEA, a federated learning framework designed to address the challenges of data scarcity and label skew while preserving data privacy. It proposes a novel approach where clients exchange privacy-protected features, such as activations from intermediate layers of models, to aid local training. This framework is evaluated against various benchmarks and shows significant improvements over existing methods. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in methodology and experimental results, and for not adequately addressing the computational and communication costs associated with feature sharing.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and under-explored problem in federated learning (FL) where clients have limited and highly label-skewed data, which is often overlooked by existing methods.
- The proposed framework, FLEA, is innovative in its approach to privacy-protected feature sharing, which is a novel concept in FL.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of FLEA, showing significant improvements over existing methods.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The methodology is well-motivated and provides a good analysis of the problem, which is relevant in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its methodology, particularly in how the feature buffer is updated and the role of different hyperparameters in the model.
- The experimental results are not convincingly presented, with insufficient details on the experimental setup and the lack of standard deviation in reported results.
- The computational and communication costs associated with feature sharing are not adequately addressed, which could limit the scalability and practical applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with other existing methods that also address data scarcity and label skew, such as FedGen and VHL.
- The privacy risks associated with feature sharing are not adequately discussed, which is a critical concern in federated learning.
- The paper's motivation and the necessity of the proposed method are not convincingly argued, especially in scenarios where the data scarcity and label skew are not severe.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the feature buffer is updated, especially in terms of the data it contains and how it affects the training process?
- How do the authors justify the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the weighting parameters in the loss function?
- What are the specific computational and communication costs associated with the feature sharing process, and how do these costs compare to other federated learning methods?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods that address data scarcity and label skew, such as FedGen and VHL?
- Can the authors provide more details on the privacy risks associated with feature sharing, and how these risks are mitigated in the proposed method?
- In the experimental results, why are the standard deviations not reported, and how do the authors ensure the reliability and reproducibility of their results?
