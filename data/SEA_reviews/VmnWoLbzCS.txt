 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Lumos, a modular framework designed to enhance the performance of language agents using smaller, open-source Large Language Models (LLMs) such as LLAMA-2. Lumos employs a modular architecture consisting of planning, grounding, and execution modules, each trained on specific tasks using annotated datasets. The framework leverages GPT-4 to generate annotations from existing benchmarks, enabling the models to decompose tasks into subgoals and execute them efficiently. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as it primarily extends existing methodologies to smaller models. The experimental results, while promising, are not sufficiently compelling to outweigh these concerns.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces Lumos, a modular framework that effectively utilizes smaller, open-source LLMs for language agents, demonstrating that these models can perform on par with larger, closed models.
- The framework's modular design allows for the separation of tasks into manageable subgoals, which aids in the efficient execution of complex tasks.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive evaluation that includes comparisons with other open-source agent formulations and closed models like GPT-4.
- The approach of using smaller LLMs to generate annotations for training is innovative and could potentially reduce the reliance on large, closed models.
- The paper demonstrates the potential of open-source LLMs in performing complex tasks, which could lead to more accessible and affordable solutions in the future.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily extends existing methodologies to smaller models, which may not be seen as a significant advancement.
- The performance of Lumos on tasks where it underperforms compared to larger models is not adequately explained, leaving a gap in understanding the model's effectiveness.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other LLM-based agents, which could have provided a clearer picture of Lumos's relative performance.
- There is a noticeable absence of a discussion on the limitations of the proposed approach, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the framework.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the action space is defined for new tasks, which could limit the generalizability of the framework.
- The annotation conversion process, while innovative, is not thoroughly validated, raising concerns about the accuracy and reliability of the generated annotations.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the discrepancies in performance between Lumos and larger models on certain tasks? What specific factors contribute to these differences?
- How does Lumos handle the generation of annotations for new tasks, especially in terms of defining the action space?
- What are the specific challenges or limitations of the proposed approach that were not addressed in the paper?
- How does the framework handle the generalizability of the models across different tasks and domains?
- Can you provide more details on the annotation conversion process, particularly how the accuracy and reliability of the generated annotations were validated?
