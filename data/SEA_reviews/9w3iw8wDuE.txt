 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DeYO, a novel test-time adaptation (TTA) method designed to address the limitations of using entropy as a confidence metric in TTA. It proposes a new metric, Pseudo-Label Probability Difference (PLPD), which measures the influence of shape information on predictions. The method leverages this metric to prioritize samples that incorporate shape information, thereby improving the robustness of TTA against biased scenarios. The paper presents extensive experiments demonstrating the superiority of DeYO over existing methods across various scenarios, including mild and wild distributions. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of comprehensive theoretical backing, unclear presentation, and limited novelty in the use of object-destructive transformations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel approach to TTA by proposing a new confidence metric, Pseudo-Label Probability Difference (PLPD), which effectively measures the influence of shape information on predictions.
- The methodology is supported by extensive experiments that demonstrate the superiority of DeYO over existing methods, showcasing its effectiveness in both mild and wild scenarios.
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method, which is significant given the complexity of TTA.
- The introduction of the PLPD metric and the use of object-destructive transformations to identify harmful samples are innovative and contribute to the advancement of TTA research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough theoretical explanation for the use of PLPD, particularly in the context of TTA, which could enhance its credibility and applicability.
- The presentation of the paper is criticized for being unclear and difficult to understand, with inconsistencies and ambiguities in the use of notation and terminology.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as the use of object-destructive transformations is not entirely novel, and the paper does not adequately distinguish its contributions from existing methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the model might not be robust to certain types of distribution shifts.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine its claims of superiority.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed theoretical explanation for the use of PLPD in TTA, specifically addressing how it measures the influence of shape information and how this contributes to the robustness of the method?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of distribution shifts, particularly those that might not be effectively addressed by the object-destructive transformations used?
- In the context of the experiments, how does the performance of DeYO compare to state-of-the-art methods, and what are the specific advantages of DeYO in terms of computational efficiency or other metrics?
- Can the authors clarify the use of notation and terminology throughout the paper to improve readability and understanding?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the model might be biased towards certain types of data, and what steps are taken to mitigate these biases?
