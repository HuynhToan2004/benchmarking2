 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel continual learning method named Time-Sensitive Replay (TSR), which selectively reintroduces training examples from previous tasks to prevent forgetting. TSR operates by monitoring the prediction distribution of a validation set and replaying examples when a significant shift in distribution is detected. The method is further extended to a task-free setting, termed Task-Free Time-Sensitive Replay (TF-TSR), which does not require task labels and instead groups examples based on similarity to existing tasks. The paper claims that TSR and TF-TSR reduce the number of replayed examples, thereby improving efficiency and accuracy. However, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty, inadequate experimental validation, and unclear presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the critical issue of catastrophic forgetting in continual learning, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The proposed method, Time-Sensitive Replay (TSR), is novel and innovative, focusing on replaying data when it is necessary, which is more efficient than traditional methods.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, making it easy to follow.
- The method is evaluated on various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness across different scenarios.
- The paper provides a new metric, Accuracy per Replay (APR), which helps in understanding the impact of each replayed example on model performance.
- The methodology is simple yet effective, with the potential to reduce computational costs by replaying fewer examples.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the proposed method, particularly in explaining why it is more efficient than existing methods.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some results showing no significant improvement over baselines.
- The paper suffers from poor writing and lacks clarity in explaining the methodology, especially in the task-free setting.
- There is a lack of discussion on related work, particularly on task-free continual learning, which is crucial for contextualizing the research.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the need for a validation set and the potential for overfitting due to replaying examples.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational costs and the scalability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental evidence to support the claims made, such as the effectiveness of the task-free setting and the reduction in computational costs.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition of the prediction sequence and the role of the moving average in the forgetting detection process?
- How does the paper ensure that the validation set is representative of the data distribution, especially in the task-free setting?
- Can you explain the rationale behind the choice of the Hoeffding test in task identification and how it compares to other statistical tests?
- How does the paper address the potential for overfitting due to replaying examples, especially in the task-free setting?
- Could you provide more details on the computational costs and scalability of the proposed method, especially in comparison to other continual learning methods?
- How does the paper address the issue of forgetting when the validation set is limited or not representative of the data distribution?
- Could you clarify the discrepancies in the experimental results, particularly the significant performance drop observed in some datasets?

**Paper Decision:**
- Decision: Reject
- Reasons: The paper, while addressing a relevant problem in continual learning, lacks novelty and sufficient experimental validation. The proposed method, Time-Sensitive Replay (TSR), is seen as an incremental improvement over existing methods without a clear demonstration of its advantages. The presentation of the paper is also criticized for being unclear and not convincingly explaining the methodology, especially in the task-free setting. The reviewers also noted a lack of discussion on related work and insufficient experimental evidence to support the claims made. The decision to reject is based on these significant issues and the need for a more robust experimental validation and clearer presentation to support the claims made.
