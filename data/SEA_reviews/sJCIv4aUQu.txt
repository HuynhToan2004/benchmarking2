 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ADOPT, a novel adaptive gradient method designed to converge with an optimal rate (O(1/\u2212T)) for smooth nonconvex optimization problems, regardless of the choice of hyperparameters. ADOPT modifies the Adam algorithm by removing the current gradient from the second moment estimate and changing the order of momentum calculation and scaling operations. Theoretical analysis is provided to support the convergence, and empirical results show that ADOPT outperforms existing methods such as Adam and AMSGrad in various practical applications, including image classification, generative modeling, natural language processing, and deep reinforcement learning. The paper also discusses the limitations and potential future improvements of the algorithm.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and understandable.
- The proposed ADOPT algorithm achieves an optimal convergence rate (O(1/\u2212T)) without relying on specific hyperparameter choices or bounded stochastic gradient assumptions.
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, including a detailed discussion on the causes of non-convergence in Adam and RMSprop.
- The empirical results demonstrate that ADOPT outperforms existing methods such as Adam and AMSGrad in various practical applications, showing improvements in accuracy and performance.
- The paper is significant in its contribution to the field of adaptive gradient methods, potentially leading to more robust and efficient optimization algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical contributions are limited to the constant learning rate case, which may not be practical in real-world applications where diminishing learning rates are commonly used.
- The empirical results are somewhat limited, with insufficient comparisons to other adaptive gradient methods and a lack of comprehensive experimental validation across different datasets and models.
- The paper's claims of novelty and superiority over existing methods are not convincingly supported by the experimental evidence provided.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the uniform bound on the second moment of the gradient, may not hold in practical scenarios, which could limit the general applicability of the results.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithm and potential areas for future research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of ADOPT with other adaptive gradient methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical performance?
- How does the choice of hyperparameters, such as \u03b1t and \u03b12, affect the performance of ADOPT in different applications?
- Can the authors elaborate on the choice of the toy problem and its relevance to real-world applications?
- What are the implications of the uniform bound on the second moment of the gradient for the practical applicability of the results?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare in terms of convergence speed and stability with other adaptive gradient methods, particularly in scenarios where the learning rate is not constant?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including a broader range of datasets and models, to more convincingly demonstrate the superiority of ADOPT over existing methods?
