 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of catastrophic forgetting in large language models (LLMs) under instruction-tuning and safety fine-tuning. It proposes a novel method called "conjugate prompting" to mitigate this issue by artificially making the task look farther from the fine-tuning distribution while requiring the same capability. This approach is tested through both synthetic and real-world experiments, showing that conjugate prompting can recover pre-trained capabilities in LLMs by translating prompts to different languages. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the broader understanding and application of LLMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel perspective on catastrophic forgetting in language models, specifically focusing on the phenomenon of instruction tuning.
- The proposed conjugate prompting method is innovative and shows potential for recovering pre-trained capabilities in LLMs.
- The experiments are well-designed and include both synthetic and real-world settings, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and visualizations that aid in understanding the complex concepts.
- The paper addresses a significant issue in the field of LLMs, which is the phenomenon of catastrophic forgetting, and proposes a method to mitigate this issue.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining key terms such as "catastrophic forgetting" and "continuous loss," which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with other existing approaches, such as continual learning methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup is somewhat simplified and may not fully reflect real-world scenarios, particularly in the linear regression experiments.
- The paper's claims about the effectiveness of the conjugate prompting method are not convincingly supported by the data presented, particularly in the real-world experiments.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its method, such as the potential for overfitting to specific prompts or the generalizability of the findings to other LLMs or scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definitions of "catastrophic forgetting" and "continuous loss" as used in the paper?
- How does the conjugate prompting method compare with other continual learning methods in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- In the real-world experiments, how does the conjugate prompting method perform when applied to different types of LLMs or in different scenarios, such as multilingual or multimodal LLMs?
- What are the specific prompts used in the experiments, and how were they chosen?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the results were validated and the statistical significance of the findings?
- How does the conjugate prompting method address the issue of catastrophic forgetting in LLMs, and what are the implications of this approach for the broader application of LLMs?
