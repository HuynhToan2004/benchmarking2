 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel vision-language pre-training framework named Tag2Text, which utilizes image tagging to enhance the performance of vision-language models. The framework employs a text semantic parser to extract tags from image-text pairs, which are then used for supervised pre-training. This approach allows for the integration of diverse tag categories beyond objects, enhancing the model's ability to recognize and generate tags. The framework is evaluated across various vision-language tasks, including image captioning and retrieval, showing significant improvements over existing methods. Despite its innovative approach, concerns were raised about the novelty and the clarity of the experimental setup, particularly the use of a text semantic parser and the impact of tagging guidance on model performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to vision-language pre-training by integrating image tagging, which enhances the learning of visual-linguistic features and provides semantic guidance.
- The proposed framework, Tag2Text, demonstrates superior zero-shot performance on image tagging tasks, rivaling fully supervised models, and shows effectiveness across a wide range of downstream benchmarks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental results are convincing, showcasing the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches.
- The integration of tagging guidance into vision-language models allows for the recognition of diverse tag categories, which can enhance the performance of both generation-based and alignment-based tasks.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the controllability of tagging guidance, demonstrating its effectiveness in image captioning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is questionable as the use of tags as anchor points for vision-language models is not entirely new, and the approach of using a text semantic parser to extract tags from image-text pairs is not particularly innovative.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as the impact of tagging guidance on model performance and the potential for overfitting due to the use of image tags from texts.
- The experimental setup is not clearly described, particularly the use of a text semantic parser and the impact of different tagging guidance on model performance.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and inference time of the proposed method, which could be crucial for evaluating its practical applicability.
- The paper does not discuss the potential for overfitting due to the use of image tags from texts, which could limit the generalizability of the model.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with other existing methods that use tags as anchor points for vision-language models?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting due to the use of image tags from texts?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the use of a text semantic parser and the impact of different tagging guidance on model performance?
- How does the proposed method perform when trained on different datasets, such as those used by other methods like BLIP-2?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and inference time of the proposed method, and discuss the computational complexity of the model?
- How does the model handle the potential for noise in the tags extracted from texts, and what measures are in place to mitigate this issue?
