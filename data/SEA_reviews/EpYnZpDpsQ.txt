 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel self-supervised representation learning (SSL) method named Learning from Randomness (LFR), which employs random data projections to learn representations without the need for domain-specific augmentations. The method uses a combination of a representation model, a set of random data projection functions, and a set of simple predictors to optimize the representation model and predictor model using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) method. The LFR method is evaluated across various data modalities including image, sequential, and tabular data, demonstrating competitive performance against state-of-the-art SSL baselines. However, the paper lacks detailed analysis on the diversity of random projectors and does not explore the impact of varying batch sizes and predictor training epochs on performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and well-presented experimental results.
- The proposed method, Learning from Randomness (LFR), is novel and innovative, utilizing random data projections to learn representations without the need for domain-specific augmentations, which makes it applicable to various data modalities.
- The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through comprehensive experiments on various data modalities, showing that it outperforms multiple state-of-the-art SSL baselines.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's performance across different data modalities, including medical applications, and includes an ablation study to analyze the impact of hyperparameters on performance.
- The use of Expectation-Maximization (EM) to train the model is highlighted as an effective approach for learning from randomness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed analysis on the diversity of random projectors and does not explore the impact of varying batch sizes and predictor training epochs on performance.
- The performance of LFR on tabular data is not as good as some other methods, and the reasons for this are not discussed.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method, which could include the potential for increased risk of sensitive features being misused due to the limited human input to the learning process.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational resources required to implement the LFR method, which could be crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The method's effectiveness is not compared against other state-of-the-art methods in terms of fine-tuning performance, which is a significant metric in SSL.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method, which could include the potential for increased risk of sensitive features being misused due to the limited human input to the learning process.

**Questions:**

- How does the choice of batch size impact the performance of LFR, and what are the implications of using different batch sizes?
- What is the impact of varying predictor training epochs on the performance of LFR, and how does this compare to the performance when all models are trained jointly?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational resources required to implement the LFR method, and how does this compare to other SSL methods in terms of computational efficiency?
- Why does LFR perform worse on tabular data compared to other methods, and what are the reasons for this?
