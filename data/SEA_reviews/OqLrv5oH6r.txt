 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Weak Supervision Heuristics for Federated Learning (WSHFL), a method that leverages labeling functions to encode expert knowledge into federated learning models. This approach is designed to enhance the performance of federated learning models by utilizing weak supervision, which can be more practical and less intrusive compared to traditional methods that require direct data annotations. The methodology involves generating labeling functions on client devices and aggregating them at the server, allowing for the selection of effective labeling functions by domain experts. The paper presents a framework for mining labeling functions and integrates them into the federated learning process, demonstrating competitive performance compared to fully supervised baselines. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope in terms of data types and lack of comprehensive evaluation against state-of-the-art federated learning methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a clear presentation of the problem.
- The proposed approach is novel and has the potential to improve the performance of federated learning models, particularly in scenarios where expert knowledge is critical.
- The paper introduces a new approach to federated learning that leverages weak supervision, which is a less intrusive method compared to traditional federated learning approaches.
- The proposed method is evaluated on a diverse set of datasets, including both text and time-series data, demonstrating its applicability across different domains.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including comparisons with baseline methods and ablation studies, which help in understanding the impact of different components of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art federated learning methods, which limits the understanding of how the proposed method performs against the current best practices in federated learning.
- The experimental setup is not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of hyperparameters used for different models and datasets, which could affect the reproducibility and the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide a clear discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential challenges in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the societal impact of the proposed method, especially in terms of data privacy and security, which are critical concerns in federated learning.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a few datasets and does not include a broader range of applications, which could help in validating the method's effectiveness across different domains.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the rationale behind the selection of specific hyperparameters for different models and datasets?
- How does the proposed method compare to other semi-supervised or self-supervised methods in federated learning, particularly in terms of performance and data efficiency?
- What are the specific challenges and limitations of the proposed method, and how do these impact its practical applicability in real-world scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the datasets used, the hyperparameters chosen, and the results of different experiments?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of data privacy and security in federated learning, particularly in scenarios where sensitive data is involved?
- Could the authors elaborate on the method's scalability and its potential impact on the efficiency and effectiveness of federated learning processes?
