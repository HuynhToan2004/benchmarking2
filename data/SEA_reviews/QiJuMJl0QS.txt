 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel meta-learning framework named Meta Routing Modulation (MRM) for handling heterogeneous task distributions. MRM employs a Gumbel-Benes layer to learn task-specific channel shuffling in convolutional neural networks, which is more efficient than previous methods. The framework is tested across multiple datasets, including jigsaw-Omniglot and jigsaw-MiniImageNet, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods. The approach is highlighted for its ability to learn task-specific modulation on the architecture level, which is more efficient than learning task-specific modulation on the parameter level. The paper also discusses the limitations of the proposed method and suggests potential improvements for future work.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning task-specific modulation on the architecture level, which is more efficient than learning task-specific modulation on the parameter level.
- The method is well-explained, and the experimental results are promising, showing an improvement in performance over existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly articulated.
- The idea of using a permutation matrix to modulate the channel routing is innovative and effectively demonstrated through the use of the Gumbel-Benes layer.
- The proposed method is efficient in terms of parameter complexity, which is a significant advantage over previous methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential improvements for future work.
- The experimental results are not convincing, particularly in the unimodal setting, where the proposed method underperforms compared to existing methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient justification for the choice of the Gumbel-Sinkhorn layer over other routing layers, and the performance of the Gumbel-Benes layer is not convincingly superior.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experiments, including additional datasets and different architectures to strengthen the claims made.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and parameter complexity, which are crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify why the proposed method underperforms in the unimodal setting compared to existing methods?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and parameter complexity with other methods, especially in large-scale datasets?
- Can you provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method, particularly in terms of training and inference times?
- Why was the Gumbel-Sinkhorn layer chosen over other routing layers, and what are the specific advantages of using this layer in your model?
- Could you elaborate on the design of the Gumbel-Benes layer and its impact on the overall performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method handle the complexity of learning task-specific modulation on the architecture level, and what are the implications for scalability and generalization to new tasks?
