 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method to address the atomic clash issue in geometric diffusion models for structure-based drug design. It employs a proximal operator to enforce physical constraints within the Langevin Dynamics of the Geometric Diffusion Model, thus improving the generation of molecules that adhere to established physical principles. The proposed method aims to enhance the accuracy of molecular generation by reducing the occurrence of atomic clashes, which are often seen as a drawback of data-driven methods. The paper includes theoretical analysis, empirical evaluations, and an ablation study to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant issue in molecular generation, specifically the atomic clash problem, which is crucial for structure-based drug design.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis to support the convergence of their approach, which enhances the credibility of their method.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, requiring no modifications to the training process of Geometric Diffusion Models (GDMs), making it easy to apply to existing models.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive ablation study that helps understand the impact of various components of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is technically sound and achieves the goal of reducing clashes, as demonstrated by empirical evaluations.
- The paper introduces a novel constrained sampling process designed to obviate undesirable collisions, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that address similar issues such as DecompDiff.
- The method's reliance on a specific threshold (2Å) for enforcing constraints might limit its applicability in more complex scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method, which could impact its practical application.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, especially in the context of drug design.
- The paper's experimental validation is limited to a single dataset, which might not fully demonstrate the method's generalizability.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of drug design and its potential misuse.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method compared to existing methods?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and efficiency with other methods that address similar issues in molecular generation?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the threshold (2Å) and discuss how this might affect the method's applicability in different scenarios?
- In terms of the experimental validation, could the authors consider using additional datasets to demonstrate the method's generalizability?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential negative societal impacts, particularly in the context of drug design and its potential misuse?
