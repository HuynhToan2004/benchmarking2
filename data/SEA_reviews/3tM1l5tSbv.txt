 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for solving non-convex optimization problems by learning a mapping from input to a distribution of solutions, rather than learning a single-valued input-solution mapping. This methodology is supported by a generative learning framework that leverages a RectFlow model to approximate the vector field of the Fokker-Planck equation, which is used to generate solutions from the learned distribution. The approach is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling multi-valued input-solution mappings and achieving faster solution generation compared to traditional methods. The paper also provides theoretical analysis on the optimality loss and runtime complexity associated with the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to solving non-convex optimization problems by learning a mapping from input to a distribution of solutions, which is a departure from traditional methods that focus on learning a single-valued input-solution mapping.
- The proposed method is supported by a theoretical analysis on the optimality loss and runtime complexity, which provides a solid foundation for understanding the performance of the method.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The methodology is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling multi-valued input-solution mappings and achieving faster solution generation compared to traditional methods.
- The paper provides a detailed characterization of the optimality loss and runtime complexity associated with the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its applicability and potential challenges.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to a few datasets, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the method across different types of problems.
- There is a lack of comparison with other methods that also learn a distribution of solutions, such as diffusion models, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on how the method handles the trade-off between optimality and runtime complexity, as well as the practical implications of these trade-offs in real-world applications.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed experimental results and a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential applications of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method handles the trade-off between optimality and runtime complexity, and discuss potential strategies for optimizing this trade-off in practical applications?
- How does the method perform when applied to more complex datasets or problems that are not well-suited for the assumptions made in the model?
- Can the authors discuss the potential of extending the method to handle discrete variables or other types of constraints, and how this might impact the performance of the method?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, such as the number of samples used in the experiments and the statistical significance of the results?
- How does the method compare to other methods that also learn a distribution of solutions, such as diffusion models, and what are the key differences in terms of performance and applicability?
