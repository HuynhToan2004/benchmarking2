 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called SubTuning for parameter-efficient fine-tuning of pretrained models, which selectively fine-tunes a subset of layers while leaving the rest frozen. The method aims to balance the computational cost and performance by training only a few layers, which is particularly beneficial when data is scarce or corrupted. The authors provide empirical evidence that SubTuning can achieve comparable or better performance than full fine-tuning and linear probing in such scenarios. The methodology is supported by theoretical analysis and extensive experiments across various datasets and network architectures. The paper also explores the practical implications of SubTuning in multi-task learning and inference efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and presents a clear, well-motivated methodology.
- The proposed method, SubTuning, is simple yet effective, and offers a novel approach to fine-tuning by selectively training a subset of layers.
- Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the method across various tasks, datasets, and network architectures, showing that SubTuning can achieve comparable or better performance than full fine-tuning and linear probing.
- The method is compatible with other parameter-efficient transfer learning methods, which could be a valuable addition to existing techniques.
- Theoretical justification is provided for the method, which enhances its credibility and applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other parameter-efficient transfer learning methods, such as LoRA and Head2Toe, which could have strengthened the argument for the effectiveness of SubTuning.
- The experiments are primarily conducted on smaller datasets like CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and VTAB, which may not adequately represent the performance on larger, more complex datasets.
- The method's efficiency in terms of parameter count is not clearly demonstrated, as it is not compared against other methods in terms of the number of parameters used for fine-tuning.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The method's effectiveness in scenarios where data is abundant or not corrupted is not thoroughly explored, which limits the understanding of its general applicability.

**Questions:**
- How does the performance of SubTuning compare when fine-tuning more layers, such as groups of 2 or 3 consecutive blocks?
- What are the implications of using SubTuning in scenarios where data is not corrupted, and how does it compare to full fine-tuning in such cases?
- Could you provide more details on the computational cost of SubTuning compared to full fine-tuning, especially in terms of the number of parameters used?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the data distribution is significantly different from the pretraining dataset, such as ImageNet to iNaturalist or ImageNet to MS COCO?
- Is there a potential for SubTuning to be applied to other tasks beyond classification, such as detection or segmentation?
