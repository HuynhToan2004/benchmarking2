 **Summary:**
The paper explores the semi-supervised heterogeneous domain adaptation (SHDA) problem, focusing on the transferability of noises from source domains to target domains. The study employs various methods to analyze the impact of label and feature information, the transferability of noises, and the discriminability of source samples. The authors claim that noises from simple distributions, such as Gaussian, Uniform, and Laplace distributions, can be effectively transferred to improve performance in the target domain. The paper presents a series of experiments to validate these claims, though it faces criticism for its clarity, novelty, and the generalizability of its findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by investigating the transferability of noises from source domains to target domains in the context of semi-supervised heterogeneous domain adaptation (SHDA), which is an interesting and relevant area of study.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive literature review, highlighting the relevance and significance of the problem.
- The experimental setup is robust, involving a large number of tasks, and the results are clearly presented, contributing to the paper's credibility.
- The paper raises an interesting question about the transferability of knowledge from source domains to target domains, which is crucial for understanding domain adaptation in heterogeneous scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, with some sections being difficult to understand due to unclear definitions and grammatical errors.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned as it seems to reiterate existing concepts without significant advancement or innovation.
- The experiments are limited to a few datasets, and the results are not convincingly generalizable to other scenarios or datasets.
- The paper's claims about the transferability of noises are not well-supported by the evidence provided, and the methodology used to generate and analyze these noises is not sufficiently detailed or convincing.
- The paper's contribution to the field is seen as limited, as it primarily reiterates existing knowledge without offering substantial new insights or practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of "source noises" in the context of their study?
- How do the authors justify the claim that noises from simple distributions can be effectively transferred to improve performance in the target domain?
- Can the authors provide more detailed information on how the noises are generated and analyzed, particularly in the context of the experiments?
- Why was the decision made to focus only on Gaussian distributions for generating noises, and what are the implications of this choice for the generalizability of the findings?
- How do the authors address the concerns regarding the clarity and novelty of their work, especially in light of the existing literature on similar topics?
