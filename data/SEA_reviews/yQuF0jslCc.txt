 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the online fractional knapsack problem (OFKP) where items can be fractionally accepted, which is a relaxation of the traditional online knapsack problem. The authors propose algorithms that leverage predictions about the optimal offline solution to achieve competitive ratios, which are matched by lower bounds. The algorithms are designed to handle both perfect and imperfect predictions, with the latter being probabilistically correct. The paper provides theoretical analyses and experimental results, demonstrating the practical applicability and effectiveness of the proposed methods. The algorithms are shown to outperform existing methods in both worst-case and average-case scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an interesting and important problem, the online fractional knapsack problem (OFKP), which is a natural relaxation of the online knapsack problem.
- It introduces a novel prediction model for the OFKP, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The algorithms proposed in the paper are well-analyzed, with matching lower bounds, and the experimental results demonstrate the practical applicability of the methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The theoretical results are solid, and the paper provides a clear presentation of the algorithms and their analysis.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its work from previous studies, such as those by Sun et al. (2021), which also consider the OFKP with predictions.
- The assumption that the unit values of items are bounded is not convincingly justified, especially given the potential for items with arbitrarily small values.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications and assumptions of the prediction models, particularly how they align with real-world scenarios.
- The experimental setup and results are somewhat limited, and the paper could benefit from a broader range of experimental scenarios and a more thorough comparison with existing methods.
- There are some minor issues with the presentation and clarity of the paper, such as the use of abbreviations and the placement of definitions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how their work differs from that of Sun et al. (2021) in terms of prediction models and algorithmic approaches?
- How does the paper justify the assumption that the unit values of items are bounded, and what are the implications of this assumption on the practical applicability of the results?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or scenarios where the prediction models might be applicable in real-world settings?
- In the experimental results, why are the performance metrics for the TA algorithm worse than those for the prediction-based algorithms? Can this be explained by the assumptions made in the TA algorithm?
- How does the paper address the potential for items with arbitrarily small values in the prediction models, and what are the implications of this for the practical applicability of the results?
