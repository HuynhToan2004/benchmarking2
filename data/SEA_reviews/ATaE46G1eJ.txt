 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new adversarial attack method, CosPGD, designed for pixel-wise prediction tasks such as semantic segmentation, optical flow estimation, and image restoration. CosPGD leverages cosine similarity to scale the loss function, aiming to optimize adversarial perturbations more effectively than previous methods like PGD and SegPGD. The method is evaluated across various datasets and architectures, showing improved attack efficacy and efficiency compared to existing methods. However, the paper's novelty and the depth of its theoretical contributions are questioned, with concerns about the scalability and the generalization of the method to different models and settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a strong methodology.
- The proposed CosPGD is a simple yet effective method that outperforms other existing methods like PGD and SegPGD in terms of attack efficacy and efficiency.
- The method is versatile and can be applied to various pixel-wise prediction tasks, including semantic segmentation, optical flow estimation, and image restoration.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with previous methods and includes comprehensive evaluations on multiple datasets and architectures, which supports the claims of the proposed method.
- The authors have made their code publicly available, which enhances the reproducibility and accessibility of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as it primarily extends the SegPGD method by using cosine similarity instead of pixel-wise PGD, which might not significantly advance the state-of-the-art in adversarial attacks.
- The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the method's limitations, particularly in scenarios where it might not be as effective as other methods like PGD.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and generalization of the method to different models and settings, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The method's dependency on the cosine similarity metric might not be robust enough, as it does not consider other potential loss scaling methods.
- There are concerns about the method's ability to maintain its effectiveness against different types of adversarial defenses, such as those based on gradient obfuscation.
- The paper's claims about the method's effectiveness are sometimes overstated, which could mislead readers about the true capabilities of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the method performs against different types of adversarial defenses, particularly those based on gradient obfuscation?
- How does the method compare in terms of attack efficacy and efficiency when applied to different models and settings, such as those involving different types of adversarial defenses?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the method's limitations and potential failure cases?
- How does the method scale with different models and settings, and what are the implications for its practical applicability?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to include other loss scaling methods, or to modify the cosine similarity metric to better capture the loss scaling in different scenarios?
