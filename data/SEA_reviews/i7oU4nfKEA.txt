 **Summary:**
The paper explores the effects of multilingual pre-training on language modeling performance across 252 languages, varying in resource levels and linguistic similarity. It pre-trains over 10,000 models, including monolingual and multilingual models, to assess how different pre-training language distributions influence model performance. The study reveals that adding multilingual data can benefit low-resource languages, especially when the added languages are syntactically similar. However, high-resource languages typically experience a decline in performance. The paper also discusses the impact of multilingual pre-training on model capacity and the "curse of multilinguality."

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive and extensive experimental setup.
- It investigates the effects of multilingual pre-training on language modeling performance, a topic of significant interest in the NLP community.
- The study includes a large number of languages, which is beneficial for understanding the impact of multilingual pre-training across diverse linguistic landscapes.
- The paper provides insights into the curse of multilinguality and the effects of linguistic similarity on performance, which are valuable for future research and practical applications.
- The results are robust, demonstrating that multilingual pre-training can be beneficial for low-resource languages, especially when the added languages are syntactically similar.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on language modeling performance and does not address the effects of multilingual pre-training on other tasks such as machine translation or text generation.
- The study only includes models up to 45M parameters, which may not adequately represent the behavior of larger models.
- The selection of languages is somewhat arbitrary, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not explore the effects of pre-training on high-resource languages in more detail, which could provide valuable insights into the limitations of multilingual pre-training.
- The results are not surprising and do not offer significant new insights, as similar effects have been observed in previous studies.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its methodology, such as the use of perplexity as a metric and the potential for data leakage in the evaluation.

**Questions:**
- How does the paper address the potential for data leakage in the evaluation, particularly given the use of publicly available datasets?
- Can the authors elaborate on the selection process for the languages included in the study and discuss the implications of this selection for the generalizability of the findings?
- How do the authors justify the use of perplexity as a metric for evaluating model performance, especially given the potential for confounding factors such as data size and language complexity?
- In light of the findings, what are the practical implications for the design and deployment of multilingual language models in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the linguistic similarity metrics used and discuss how these metrics were chosen and validated?
- How do the authors respond to the concerns regarding the novelty and significance of the findings, given that similar effects have been observed in previous studies?
