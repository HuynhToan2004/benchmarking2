 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Quack, an automated testing framework designed to evaluate the security of large language models (LLMs) by generating jailbreak prompts. Quack utilizes a knowledge graph to structure and iteratively refine these prompts, aiming to bypass LLM safety constraints. The framework is evaluated against various LLMs, including open-source and commercial models, and demonstrates improved success rates in jailbreaking compared to existing methods. The approach leverages a role-playing mechanism where each LLM plays a specific role in the testing process, and the results are validated using a similarity score. However, the paper faces criticisms for its lack of novelty, insufficient evaluation, and unclear methodological contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.
- The proposed framework is innovative, utilizing a knowledge graph to structure and iteratively refine jailbreak prompts, which is a novel approach.
- The evaluation of the framework is thorough, covering a wide range of models and scenarios, and demonstrates improved success rates in jailbreaking compared to existing methods.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the eight characteristics of jailbreak prompts, which are well-structured and easy to understand.
- The framework's ability to automatically generate jailbreak prompts reduces the need for human expertise and labor, making it a valuable tool for testing the security of LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it primarily focuses on generating jailbreak prompts, which is a well-explored area in previous studies.
- The evaluation of the framework is insufficient, with limited diversity in the models and scenarios tested, and a lack of comparison with other existing methods.
- The paper's methodology and contributions are not clearly defined, with unclear explanations of how the framework operates and what specific advancements it offers over existing methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the experimental setup, such as the number of prompts tested and the specifics of the knowledge graph used.
- The framework's reliance on a similarity score for evaluating jailbreak success raises concerns about the validity and reliability of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed framework compare to other existing methods for generating jailbreak prompts, particularly in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies of the jailbreak scenarios and their corresponding characteristics, to illustrate the framework's capabilities more clearly?
- How is the knowledge graph used in the framework constructed, and what specific role does it play in the jailbreak prompt generation process?
- What are the specifics of the experimental setup, such as the number of prompts tested and the criteria for determining a successful jailbreak?
- Could the authors clarify the methodology and contributions of the framework, particularly how it differs from existing methods and what unique advantages it offers?
