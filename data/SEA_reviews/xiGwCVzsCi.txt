 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the challenge of ensuring fairness in insurance pricing when sensitive attributes are privatized, a common scenario due to regulatory constraints. It introduces a novel method where a trusted third party (TTP) holds the sensitive attributes, while the insurer receives privatized versions. The TTP trains a model using these privatized attributes, ensuring that the insurer does not directly access the sensitive data. The paper explores two scenarios: known and unknown noise rates, and provides theoretical guarantees for these cases. The methodology is evaluated on real-world datasets, showing that it can effectively handle privacy-preserving data while maintaining fairness and accuracy in pricing models. The paper is commended for its clarity, originality, and practical applicability, though concerns are raised about the novelty of the technical contributions and the practical implications of the assumptions made.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and novel approach to addressing the problem of fairness in insurance pricing with privatized sensitive attributes.
- The theoretical analysis is sound, and the paper provides a clear and intuitive explanation of the methodology, making it accessible to readers.
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue, as the use of machine learning in insurance pricing raises concerns about fairness and privacy, especially with the handling of sensitive attributes.
- The paper is original in its approach, particularly in its application to the insurance domain, and introduces a method that is practical and adaptable to different levels of transparency required.
- The paper includes real-world datasets and experiments, which add practical relevance and support the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implications of the assumptions made, such as the boundedness of T(X) and the finiteness of the parameters of f in the hypothesis class.
- The technical contribution of the paper is limited, with the proofs and theoretical results being largely standard in the field of learning with noisy labels.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its method from existing works, particularly in terms of the use of noisy labels and the handling of privacy concerns.
- There is a lack of experimental details, such as the choice of hyperparameters and the specifics of the data preprocessing, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and a comparison with other methods in the field.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on the practical implications of the assumptions made in the paper, particularly regarding the boundedness of T(X) and the finiteness of the parameters of f in the hypothesis class?
- How does the method handle the privacy concerns beyond the use of a trusted third party, especially in terms of ensuring that the privatized sensitive attributes are truly randomized and unbiased?
- What are the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments, and how were they chosen?
- How does the method perform when the noise rate is unknown, and what are the implications of this for the accuracy and fairness of the pricing models?
- Could you provide more details on the data preprocessing and the choice of datasets used in the experiments?
- How does the method compare with other methods in terms of accuracy, fairness, and computational efficiency?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle continuous sensitive attributes, and what would be the challenges in implementing this?
