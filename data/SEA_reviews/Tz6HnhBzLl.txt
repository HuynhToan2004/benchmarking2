 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of positional differential game theory to robust reinforcement learning (RRL), specifically focusing on zero-sum differential games. It introduces two new algorithms, Isaacs Deep Q-Networks (IDQN) and Decomposed Isaacs Deep Q-Networks (DIDQN), which utilize a shared Q-function to solve these games. The theoretical analysis includes the proof that the Q-function can be used to approximate both minimax and maximin Bellman equations under certain conditions. The paper presents a centralized Q-learning approach and demonstrates the algorithms' effectiveness through experiments in both simple and complex MuJoCo environments. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the algorithms, the clarity of the theoretical explanations, and the practical applicability of the positional differential game framework in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by combining positional differential game theory with robust reinforcement learning (RRL), providing a new framework for understanding and addressing RRL problems.
- The theoretical analysis is well-structured, providing a clear explanation of the shared Q-function concept and its application in solving zero-sum differential games.
- The introduction of new algorithms, Isaacs Deep Q-Networks (IDQN) and Decomposed Isaacs Deep Q-Networks (DIDQN), offers a fresh perspective on solving RRL problems, especially in the context of zero-sum differential games.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, with a clear exposition of the mathematical concepts and their implications.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, showing that they outperform other RRL and multi-agent RL baselines in both simple and complex environments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive review of related work, particularly in the area of RRL and positional differential game theory, which could have provided a better context for the proposed research.
- The novelty of the algorithms (IDQN and DIDQN) is questionable as they seem to be a combination of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The theoretical analysis and proofs, especially in sections 3 and 4, are not clearly explained or intuitive, making it difficult for readers to understand the implications and assumptions.
- The practical applicability of the positional differential game framework in real-world scenarios is not convincingly demonstrated, raising concerns about the framework's utility in addressing real-world RRL problems.
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key concepts such as the shared Q-function and the positional differential game framework.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with existing algorithms and a broader range of environments to validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of the shared Q-function in the context of positional differential games?
- How do the authors justify the use of the positional differential game framework in solving RRL problems, especially in scenarios where the environment is non-stationary?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed algorithms with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- How do the authors address the concerns regarding the novelty and practical applicability of their approach, especially in light of existing works in RRL and differential game theory?
- Can the authors provide a more intuitive explanation of the theoretical results and assumptions, particularly in sections 3 and 4, to help readers better understand the implications and limitations of the proposed methods?
