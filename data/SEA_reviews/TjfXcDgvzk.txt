 **Summary:**
The paper introduces NOLA, a method that extends the LoRA technique by replacing the fixed matrices A and B with linear combinations of random matrices, which are optimized for each task. This approach aims to reduce the storage requirements of LLMs by compressing the model's parameters. NOLA was evaluated on various tasks including NLP and computer vision, showing comparable or better performance with fewer parameters than LoRA. The method was also tested on different model architectures like GPT-2 and ViT, demonstrating its applicability across various domains. The paper also discusses the potential of NOLA to address the limitations of LoRA, such as the rank-one decomposition and the quantization of parameters.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the motivation is clearly articulated.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, showing significant improvements in performance and storage reduction compared to LoRA.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves comparable performance to LoRA with fewer parameters, and it can be applied to different model architectures like GPT-2 and ViT.
- The method is computationally efficient, reducing the computational overhead by optimizing the low-rank matrices.
- The paper includes a variety of experiments that demonstrate the method's effectiveness across different tasks and model architectures.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other methods like LoRA, especially in terms of the number of parameters used and the performance metrics.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis to support the empirical observations, which could enhance the understanding of the method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of low rank in the training of LLMs, which could affect the method's applicability in certain scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the method's limitations and potential negative societal impacts.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with some results showing a significant drop in performance when the number of parameters is reduced.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the rank of the matrices A and B is chosen and how it affects the performance of the method?
- How does the method perform when applied to different model architectures, such as those used in the PRANC paper?
- Can you provide more details on the training process, such as the learning rate and other hyperparameters used?
- Why was the decision made to use a rank of 8 for the experiments, and how does this compare to other ranks in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the method handle the storage and retrieval of the random matrices, especially in scenarios where the model needs to be retrieved quickly?
- Could you provide more details on the computational overhead and memory requirements of the method, especially in comparison to LoRA?
