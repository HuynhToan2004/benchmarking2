 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for differentiating through infeasible quadratic programs using an extended conservative Jacobian (ECJ) approach. This method is applied to quadratic programming layers in neural networks, allowing for the training of infeasible programs, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that require feasible programs. The paper proposes a new approach that can handle both feasible and infeasible quadratic programs, providing a more robust and flexible framework for training optimization layers. The method is validated through experiments on classic learning tasks such as Sudoku, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling infeasible programs and improving predictive performance. The paper also introduces a C++ software package called QPLayer, which can be integrated with modern learning frameworks to facilitate the use of this method in practical applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly presents the proposed method, making it accessible and easy to understand.
- The authors have successfully tackled the problem of differentiating through infeasible quadratic programs, which is a significant challenge in machine learning.
- The methodology is novel and innovative, introducing a new approach to handling infeasible programs that could be particularly useful in scenarios where traditional methods fail.
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, which is supported by both theoretical and practical demonstrations.
- The inclusion of a C++ software package, QPLayer, is a valuable contribution that enhances the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experiments are somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on a single learning task (Sudoku) and not exploring a broader range of applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other existing methods, such as the OptNet, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed approach.
- There are some unclear aspects in the paper, such as the definitions and roles of certain variables and assumptions, which could benefit from further clarification.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.

**Questions:**
