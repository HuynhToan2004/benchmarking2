 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an adaptive planning budget for the Differentiable Neural Computer (DNC) model, allowing the model to adjust the number of planning steps based on input size during training. This approach is tested on graph problems such as shortest path and minimum cut, demonstrating improved generalization and memory utilization compared to fixed planning budgets. The authors propose that this adaptive budget allows the model to learn more general algorithms and utilize memory more efficiently, thus enhancing its performance on larger inputs. However, the paper's experimental scope is limited to these specific graph problems, and the generalizability of the findings to other tasks remains unclear.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant issue in the field of memory-augmented neural networks by focusing on the impact of the planning phase and the use of a fixed planning budget, which is a novel approach.
- The proposed adaptive planning budget is innovative and shows potential for improving generalization and memory utilization, as demonstrated through empirical results on graph problems.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments are well-designed and provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach, with a focus on the generalization of the model to larger inputs.
- The paper includes a thorough discussion on the potential limitations and challenges of the proposed method, which enhances its credibility and applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental scope is limited to graph problems, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the findings to other tasks or domains.
- The motivation behind the choice of a linear planning budget is not well-explained, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the selection of this parameter.
- The paper lacks a broader comparison with other models or algorithms, which could help contextualize the contributions of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational overhead or the practical implications of using an adaptive planning budget, particularly in terms of training time and resource requirements.
- There is a lack of clarity in some figures and results, which could be improved with more detailed explanations or additional visual aids.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on the choice of a linear planning budget and discuss how this choice impacts the generalization and performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare with other models or algorithms that do not use a fixed planning budget? What are the advantages and disadvantages of each approach?
- Can you provide more details on the computational overhead and training time required for using an adaptive planning budget?
- How does the proposed method perform on other tasks or domains beyond the graph problems tested?
- In the figures and results, can you clarify any ambiguities or uncertainties, such as the sudden drops in performance observed in certain figures?
- How does the adaptive planning budget affect the memory utilization and efficiency of the DNC model, especially when the memory size is not fixed?
