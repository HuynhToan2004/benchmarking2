 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called PDE-Diffusion, which integrates physics-based priors and a two-stage diffusion model to address the challenges of generalization, interpretability, and long-horizon predictive performance in solving partial differential equations (PDEs). The method involves training a neural network to learn the residual between the predicted and true PDE solutions, with the residual used to guide the diffusion process. This approach is evaluated against existing methods such as FNO, UNet, and VDM using datasets like CFD2D and DR2D. The results show that PDE-Diffusion outperforms these methods in terms of mean squared error and mean absolute error, indicating its effectiveness. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, unclear presentation, and insufficient comparisons with other relevant methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to solving partial differential equations (PDEs) using a diffusion model, which is a departure from traditional methods like FNO, UNet, and VDM.
- The methodology integrates physics-based priors and a two-stage diffusion model, which enhances the interpretability and generalization capabilities of the model.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments conducted are thorough, providing a robust validation of the proposed method.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance in all cases, as demonstrated in the results section.
- The introduction of two new simulated datasets specifically designed to emulate real-world challenges enhances the availability of resources for deep learning in the PDE-solving domain.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is questioned as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods like video diffusion models and FNO, with limited technical innovation.
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, particularly in the description of the methodology and the experimental setup.
- There is a significant absence of comparisons with other relevant methods, such as PhyDNet and DeepONet, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on the training of a separate autoencoder for latent representation and the use of a diffusion model for residual prediction may limit its generalizability and applicability to other PDE types.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and scope.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and impact of the initial condition and boundary condition embeddings in the model's performance?
- How does the method handle different types of PDEs, and what are the implications of this limitation on the model's applicability?
- Can you provide more details on the training process, particularly how the autoencoder is trained and the role of the diffusion model in the overall process?
- Why was the decision made to use a diffusion model for residual prediction, and how does this approach compare to other methods like Fourier neural operators?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of datasets and the rationale behind their selection?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially in larger and more complex PDE scenarios?
