 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of spikes in the training loss during stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and their impact on generalization. It establishes a connection between these spikes and the catapult phenomenon, which occurs in the top eigenvectors of the neural tangent kernel (NTK). The authors show that these spikes promote feature learning and generalization by aligning the Average Gradient Outer Product (AGOP) of the trained network with the true AGOP. The paper also explores the relationship between the number of catapults and the batch size, suggesting that smaller batch sizes lead to more catapults and potentially better generalization. The study extends the understanding of the catapult phenomenon from gradient descent to SGD and provides empirical evidence for its effects on generalization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly presents the relationship between the catapult phenomenon and the training loss spikes, along with their implications for generalization.
- The experiments are comprehensive and provide strong empirical evidence to support the claims made in the paper.
- The paper connects the catapult phenomenon to generalization, offering a new perspective on how catapults might affect generalization performance.
- The authors have demonstrated that catapults can occur in SGD, which is a significant extension of the catapult phenomenon from gradient descent to SGD.
- The paper presents a novel perspective on the relationship between the number of catapults and the batch size, showing that smaller batch sizes lead to more catapults and potentially better generalization.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a deeper theoretical analysis of the mechanisms behind the catapult phenomenon and its impact on generalization.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the relationship between the catapult phenomenon and other phenomena such as the edge of stability and the sharpness of the loss landscape.
- The paper's claims about the correlation between the number of catapults and generalization performance are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which are limited to specific datasets and architectures.
- The paper's discussion on the connection between the catapult phenomenon and the NTK regime is not sufficiently clear, and the implications of this connection for practical applications are not adequately explored.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the catapult phenomenon in real-world applications, such as the choice of learning rates and batch sizes.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a deeper theoretical analysis of the mechanisms behind the catapult phenomenon and its impact on generalization?
- How does the catapult phenomenon relate to other phenomena such as the edge of stability and the sharpness of the loss landscape?
- What are the implications of the catapult phenomenon for practical applications, such as the choice of learning rates and batch sizes?
- How does the catapult phenomenon affect the generalization performance of different architectures, and what are the specific conditions under which these effects are most pronounced?
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between the catapult phenomenon and the NTK regime, and discuss the implications of this relationship for practical applications?
