 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Adaptive Wasserstein Variational Optimization (AWaVO), a novel method for constrained reinforcement learning that leverages Wasserstein variational optimization and distributional reinforcement learning to enhance interpretability. AWaVO integrates PPO-DR for policy optimization and Wasserstein Variational Inference (WVI) for variational inference, aiming to achieve interpretability through a probabilistic inference framework. The method's efficacy is demonstrated through experiments in both simulated and real-world environments, including a quadrotor drone, showcasing its practical applicability. However, the paper is critiqued for its lack of clarity in presentation, particularly in the explanation of key components and the experimental setup, which makes it difficult to fully understand the method's contributions and its comparative advantages over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and detailed experimental setup.
- The authors provide a novel approach to constrained RL by integrating Wasserstein variational inference and distributional reinforcement learning, which is both innovative and theoretically grounded.
- The paper includes a probabilistic inference approach to constrained RL, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear theoretical justifications and empirical results that support the claims made.
- The paper includes a real-world hardware implementation, which adds credibility to the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with other methods, particularly in terms of interpretability and performance. It would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of how AWaVO compares to other interpretable RL methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup, particularly the implementation of AWaVO and the choice of hyperparameters.
- There are several instances where the paper is unclear or confusing, particularly in the definitions and uses of key terms and concepts.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could limit its practical applicability in more complex environments.
- The paper's claims about interpretability and the advantages of the proposed method over existing methods are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the proposed method, AWaVO, compares to other interpretable RL methods in terms of interpretability and performance?
- How does the choice of hyperparameters impact the performance of AWaVO, and what guidelines can be provided for selecting these parameters in different environments?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how AWaVO was implemented and the specifics of the hardware used?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational complexity associated with the Wasserstein distance and the distributional representation?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the convergence rates and the interpretability of the learned policies, possibly including visualizations or additional data?
