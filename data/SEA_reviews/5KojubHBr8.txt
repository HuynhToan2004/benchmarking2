 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MMICL, a novel approach for vision-language models (VLMs) that leverages in-context learning (ICL) to enhance their ability to process complex multi-modal prompts, particularly those involving multiple images and text. MMICL utilizes a new dataset, the Multi-modal In-Context Learning (MIC) dataset, which is designed to improve the VLM's understanding of intricate relationships between images and text. The model, trained on this dataset, demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on various vision-language benchmarks, showing significant improvements in zero-shot performance and few-shot learning capabilities. The paper also explores the potential of MMICL in reducing language bias in VLMs, which is a significant challenge in current models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, MMICL, which addresses the limitations of current vision-language models (VLMs) in understanding complex multi-modal prompts, particularly those involving multiple images and text.
- MMICL proposes a new context scheme and constructs a new dataset, the Multi-modal In-Context Learning (MIC) dataset, which enhances the VLM's ability to understand intricate relationships between images and text.
- The paper presents extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of MMICL, showing significant improvements in zero-shot performance on various vision-language benchmarks.
- MMICL effectively tackles the challenge of complex multi-modal prompt understanding and emerges the impressive ICL ability, alleviating language bias in VLMs.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and provides a comprehensive analysis of the limitations of current VLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how MMICL mitigates language bias in VLMs, and the results presented do not convincingly demonstrate this claim.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate MMICL from existing models like FLAN-T5 and InstructBLIP-FLAN-T5, and the performance improvements over these models are not significant.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the limitations of the MIC dataset, which could affect the generalizability and robustness of MMICL.
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for hallucination and the generalizability of the results across different datasets and tasks.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare MMICL with other state-of-the-art models, particularly those that also utilize in-context learning (ICL) techniques.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the potential negative societal impacts.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed explanation of how MMICL mitigates language bias in VLMs and provide more convincing results to support this claim?
- How does MMICL differ from existing models like FLAN-T5 and InstructBLIP-FLAN-T5, and what are the specific advantages of MMICL over these models?
- Can the authors provide more details on the limitations of the MIC dataset and how these limitations affect the performance and generalizability of MMICL?
- How does MMICL perform on other vision-language tasks beyond those evaluated in the paper, and what are the potential limitations of the model in handling different types of visual and textual inputs?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the ablation studies conducted, particularly on the impact of different components of the model on performance?
- How does MMICL handle the potential for hallucination, and what steps have been taken to mitigate this issue?
