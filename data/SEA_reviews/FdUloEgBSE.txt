 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TEDMol, a text-guided diffusion model designed for 3D molecular generation. TEDMol integrates the capabilities of language models with diffusion models, allowing for textual conditions to guide the reverse process, enhancing the stability and diversity of generated molecules. It utilizes a reference geometry derived from textual prompts to guide the denoising process of a pre-trained equivariant diffusion model, which is then used to generate 3D molecular structures. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on various tasks, showing improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach and the lack of comparison with more recent baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by integrating the capabilities of language models with diffusion models, providing a deeper level of language understanding in 3D molecule generation.
- The experimental results demonstrate that TEDMol enhances the stability and diversity of generated molecules, capturing and utilizing information derived from textual descriptions.
- The method is well-organized, easy to follow, and the code is provided, which facilitates reproducibility.
- The paper provides a detailed background on diffusion-based 3D molecule generation and equivariant diffusion models, making it accessible to readers without prior knowledge in these areas.
- The authors have curated a subset of molecules from QM9 and associated them with real-world descriptions sourced from PubChem, enriching the content of the dataset and supplementing textual context for those molecules lacking real-world descriptions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods, such as those using value guidance for 3D molecular generation.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as it primarily involves the integration of a text-to-molecule model and a diffusion model, which have been previously explored.
- The paper lacks a comparison with more recent baselines, which could have provided a clearer picture of TEDMol's performance relative to current state-of-the-art methods.
- The method's performance in terms of atom and molecule stability is not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the stability metrics reported in the paper do not align with those typically used in the field.
- There is a lack of detailed comparison in the experimental section, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of TEDMol's capabilities.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the reference geometry is obtained from the textual prompt and how it is converted into a molecular graph?
- How does the model ensure that the generated molecules are valid and stable, especially in terms of the stability metrics reported?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training data and the specific textual prompts used in the experiments?
- How does the model handle the generation of molecules with multiple desired properties, and what are the specific textual descriptions used for these cases?
- Could the authors explain the discrepancy in the stability metrics reported and provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods?
