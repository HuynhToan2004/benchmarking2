 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the automatic selection of data for instruction tuning, focusing on three key dimensions: complexity, quality, and diversity. It proposes a new method for data selection, DEITA, which uses a combination of existing metrics and novel approaches to evaluate data samples. The method is evaluated against various baselines and shown to perform well or on par with state-of-the-art models using significantly less data. The paper also introduces new metrics and methodologies for data selection, aiming to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of instruction tuning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methods and results.
- The authors conduct a comprehensive empirical study, exploring the effectiveness of different data selection strategies across various models and datasets.
- The proposed method for data selection, DEITA, is simple, intuitive, and has been shown to perform well in experiments, outperforming other methods in some cases.
- The paper provides a novel approach to data selection by integrating multiple metrics and methodologies, which could be beneficial for practitioners in the field.
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of instruction tuning, which is crucial for improving the performance of large language models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental design could be improved by including more diverse models and datasets, as well as varying the data budgets to better understand the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced perspective on its applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for the field.
- The novelty of the proposed method is somewhat limited as it primarily combines existing metrics and methodologies without significant innovation.
- The paper's claims about the effectiveness of DEITA are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which show only marginal improvements over baseline methods in some cases.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the term "budget" as used in the paper?
- How does the performance of DEITA vary with different data budgets, and what are the implications of this for the generalizability of the findings?
- Can the authors provide more details on the data selection process and how it impacts the performance of the models?
- How does the proposed method compare to other data selection methods that have been proposed in the literature, particularly those that use different metrics or methodologies?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
- What are the specific advantages of DEITA over other data selection methods, particularly in terms of its simplicity and practicality?
