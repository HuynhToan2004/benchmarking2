 **Summary:**
The paper explores the capabilities of encoder-only BERT-family models for instruction following tasks, specifically using the XML-R model fine-tuned with a dataset derived from xP3. The authors employ a non-autoregressive mask prediction objective to generate outputs, which is evaluated against baseline models like BLOOMZ and mT0. Despite the innovative approach and the model's potential to outperform in some tasks, the paper suffers from several limitations. These include the lack of comprehensive evaluation against stronger baselines, the absence of ablation studies, and the potential for unfair comparisons due to different training data sizes. The paper also does not adequately address the limitations of the model in handling long-generation tasks and the reliability of predicted lengths.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach by applying the BERT family of models to instruction following tasks, which is a novel direction in language model research.
- The methodology is clearly explained, making it easy to follow and understand the experimental setup.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the model's capabilities, including an analysis of its performance on held-out tasks and its ability to generalize to new languages.
- The authors have conducted a detailed analysis of the model's performance, including a discussion on the scaling effects and the impact of different training data sizes.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear presentation of the research and its findings, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the model's capabilities, particularly in comparison to stronger baselines such as decoder-only models like BLOOM and encoder-decoder models like mT0.
- There is a notable absence of ablation studies, which could provide insights into the impact of different model configurations and training strategies.
- The evaluation of the model's performance is not robust enough, with some results (such as those in Table 2) being potentially misleading due to the model's performance on different tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the model, particularly in its handling of long-generation tasks and the reliability of predicted lengths.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison of the model's training data size to ensure fairness in evaluating its performance against other models.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the performance of the model on the XNLI task, especially in comparison to the baseline models?
- How does the model perform on the XCOPA task, and what are the specific challenges it faces in this domain?
- Can you provide more details on the training data size used for the XML-R model and how it compares to the baseline models?
- How does the model handle the length prediction task, and what are the implications of using a fixed length for all tasks?
- Could you elaborate on the model's performance in few-shot prompting learning, and what steps are being taken to address this limitation?
- How does the model compare to other encoder-only models like BLOOM, and what are the specific advantages or disadvantages of using an encoder-only model for instruction following tasks?
