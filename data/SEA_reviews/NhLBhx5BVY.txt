 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel topological loss function aimed at improving neuron segmentation in lightsheet microscopy images by addressing split and merge errors. This loss function is designed to penalize critical supervoxels, thereby enhancing the segmentation accuracy. The methodology is theoretically grounded and computationally efficient, with the potential to generalize to other biomedical image segmentation tasks. The paper presents results from both 2D and 3D datasets, showcasing its effectiveness in handling complex topological structures. Despite its strengths, concerns are raised regarding the novelty of the approach, the limited scope of validation, and the lack of comparison with more recent and relevant methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions and a logical structure that enhances understanding.
- The proposed method is novel, providing a new perspective on topological loss functions by focusing on supervoxels rather than individual voxels.
- The method's computational efficiency is a significant advantage, with a time complexity of O(n), which is much faster than most existing methods.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear definitions and a detailed explanation of the proposed method.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in neuron segmentation.
- The use of supervoxels instead of individual voxels is a novel approach that potentially enhances the method's applicability to other biomedical image segmentation tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for a comprehensive understanding of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The method's novelty is questionable, as similar approaches using supervoxels have been previously explored in other fields, such as medical image segmentation.
- The paper's experimental validation is limited, with only two datasets used, and the results do not consistently outperform state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with more recent and relevant methods in the field, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed approach.
- The paper's focus on neuron segmentation in lightsheet microscopy images may limit its applicability to other types of biomedical image segmentation tasks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, particularly how it compares to other methods in terms of runtime and resource usage?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other biomedical image segmentation tasks beyond neuron segmentation?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical application?
- How do the hyperparameters α and β affect the performance of the proposed method, and have they been optimized for different datasets or segmentation tasks?
- Could the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the handling of topological structures?
- How does the proposed method compare with more recent and relevant methods in the field, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the validity of the results presented?
