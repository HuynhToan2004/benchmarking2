 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph neural network (GNN) architecture designed to model anisotropic materials by preserving directional information during message passing. The method encodes edge features with a directional encoding scheme that decomposes edge features along material-space basis vectors, allowing for the modeling of anisotropic materials with minimal changes to standard mesh-based GNNs. The approach is validated through experiments that demonstrate the model's ability to outperform existing mesh-based GNNs for modeling anisotropic materials. The paper also addresses the limitations of existing methods that discard directional information, which can lead to inaccurate modeling of anisotropic materials.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant limitation of existing methods in the field by introducing a novel feature encoding scheme that preserves directional information during message passing, enhancing the ability to model anisotropic materials.
- The proposed method is easy to implement and can be integrated into existing frameworks, making it accessible and practical for various applications.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and logical presentation that aids in understanding the methodology and results.
- The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the method's ability to outperform the state-of-the-art method for capturing material anisotropy.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the method and its advantages over existing methods, supported by empirical results that show the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method's performance and advantages.
- There is a lack of discussion on the generalizability of the method to other types of anisotropic materials or different material properties.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive ablation study to demonstrate the effectiveness of each component of the proposed method.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of the number of samples used and the statistical significance of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide insights into its applicability and potential future research directions.
- The paper's focus on specific types of anisotropic materials (transversely isotropic elastic materials) limits the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be extended to other types of anisotropic materials or different material properties?
- How does the method perform when applied to other types of deformations or different types of materials?
- How sensitive is the method to the choice of material properties, such as Young's modulus or Poisson's ratio?
- Is there a possibility of overfitting the model to the training data, and how does the model generalize to new, unseen materials or geometries?
- Could you provide more details on the training and inference times for the proposed method and compare them with existing methods to assess computational efficiency?
- How does the method perform when the material properties are not known a priori, and how does it handle different types of external forces or boundary conditions?
