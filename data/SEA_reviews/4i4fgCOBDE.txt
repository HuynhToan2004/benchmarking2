 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the fairness in link prediction through Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) by focusing on the preferential attachment bias, which is a significant issue in within-group fairness. It theoretically demonstrates that GCNs with symmetric normalized graph filters have a bias towards nodes with higher degrees, which can lead to unfairness in link prediction. The paper proposes a new within-group fairness metric and a training-time strategy to mitigate this bias. The authors conduct extensive experiments to validate their findings, showing that the proposed method can effectively reduce the fairness gap. Despite the theoretical and empirical contributions, the paper faces criticism for its presentation clarity, limited novelty, and potential over-reliance on certain assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important issue in fairness in link prediction, which is often overlooked in existing literature.
- It provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis on the preferential attachment bias in GCNs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments are well-designed and conducted, with clear results that validate the theoretical findings.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, which is a practical advantage for real-world applications.
- The paper provides a new within-group fairness metric and a training-time strategy to mitigate the bias in link prediction, which are valuable contributions to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The presentation of the paper is not clear and could benefit from better organization and clarity in the figures and tables.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the assumption that each path from node i to j in the computation graph of Φs is independently activated with probability ρs(i), are not well-justified or explained.
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as the proposed fairness regularizer and the use of a GCN for link prediction have been previously explored.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the potential societal impacts of the findings.
- The experimental results are not convincing, particularly the lack of a baseline comparison and the inconsistency in the effectiveness of the proposed method across different datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the proposed method performs in real-world scenarios and a broader evaluation of its effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed justification for the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis?
- How does the proposed method perform in real-world scenarios, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in practical applications?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and fairness metrics?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of preferential attachment bias in GCNs, and what are the implications for fairness in link prediction?
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies observed in the experimental results, particularly the inconsistency in the effectiveness of the proposed method across different datasets?
