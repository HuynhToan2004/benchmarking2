 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multi-objective inverse reinforcement learning (MOIRL) approach that leverages demonstrations from multiple experts to learn a common reward function, which is then used to train policies that can generalize across different expert preferences. The approach is evaluated in both discrete and continuous environments, demonstrating its effectiveness in transferring learned policies to different preferences. The method is supported by a theoretical framework and experimental results, which showcase its potential in real-world applications where multiple experts provide diverse demonstrations. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in methodological details, insufficient comparisons with existing methods, and limited evaluation environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to multi-objective inverse reinforcement learning (MOIRL) that effectively utilizes demonstrations from multiple experts to learn a common reward function, enhancing the generalization of learned policies across different expert preferences.
- The methodology is supported by a theoretical framework and experimental results, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in both discrete and continuous environments.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for advancing research in the field of imitation learning.
- The approach of learning from diverse expert demonstrations is significant and relevant, especially in scenarios where multiple experts provide different preferences for the same task.
- The paper addresses a critical gap in the literature by focusing on the transferability of learned policies across different expert preferences, which is a crucial aspect of imitation learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the methodological details, particularly in the explanation of how the reward function is learned and the role of the consensus ADMM in this process.
- There is a significant gap in the literature review, with insufficient discussion and comparison with existing methods such as those by Beliaev et al., Kishikawa & Arai, and Chen et al.
- The paper primarily focuses on the DST environment and does not evaluate the proposed method in other complex, continuous control environments, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in scenarios involving a large number of experts or diverse preferences.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly robust, with insufficient comparisons to existing methods and a lack of detailed analysis on the performance of the learned policies across different expert preferences.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of the preference vector and its impact on the learned policy, which is crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the reward function is learned, specifically in terms of the role of the consensus ADMM in this process?
- How does the method perform when the number of experts increases, and what are the implications for the scalability of the approach?
- In the continuous control environments, how does the method handle the stochastic nature of the expert demonstrations?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the preference vector and its impact on the learned policy?
- How does the method compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and the robustness of the learned policies across different expert preferences?
- The paper mentions the transferability of learned policies across different expert preferences. Can the authors provide more insights into how this transferability is achieved and what it means for the practical application of the method?
