 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of equivariance in reinforcement learning (RL) and planning tasks, specifically focusing on Geometric MDPs that exhibit Euclidean group symmetries. It proposes a novel equivariant TD-MPC algorithm for continuous action spaces, leveraging sampling-based MPPI for equivariant control. Theoretical insights are provided on the reduction of parameters in linearized dynamics and optimal control policies, and empirical evaluations are conducted on standard RL benchmarks, demonstrating improved performance. The paper also discusses the integration of symmetry into RL algorithms, aiming to enhance model-based RL and planning in robotic applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel equivariant TD-MPC algorithm, which is a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning and planning, particularly in continuous action spaces.
- Theoretical insights are provided on the reduction of parameters in linearized dynamics and optimal control policies, which are critical for understanding the benefits of equivariance in model-based RL.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of concepts and theoretical results, making it accessible to readers.
- Empirical evaluations are conducted on standard RL benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm in improving performance.
- The paper discusses the integration of symmetry into RL algorithms, which is a crucial aspect of improving model-based RL and planning in robotic applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where equivariance might not be beneficial or practical.
- There is a need for more comprehensive experimental validation, including comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods and more diverse tasks to fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
- The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the theoretical concepts, especially the definitions and implications of G-equivariant and G-invariant functions.
- The empirical results are not sufficiently robust, with some tasks showing marginal improvements or no significant differences compared to non-equivariant methods.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational costs associated with implementing equivariance in the algorithm, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the computational costs associated with implementing equivariance in the algorithm, and how these costs compare to non-equivariant methods?
- How does the proposed method perform when the symmetry group is unknown or not fully known?
- In the empirical evaluations, why were only certain tasks chosen for comparison, and what were the specific reasons for these choices?
- Could you elaborate on the theoretical assumptions and their implications for the practical application of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of tasks and the rationale behind the experimental design?
