 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel evaluation framework for synthetic tabular data, focusing on the distribution matching between synthetic and real data. It proposes a spectrum of metrics that assess the quality of synthetic data by comparing various aspects of their distributions. The framework includes both model-free and model-based metrics, with the latter employing a probabilistic cross-categorization (PCC) model as a surrogate for the true data-generating process. The framework is evaluated across different datasets and synthesizer models, demonstrating that explicit modeling of tabular structure can lead to better synthetic data. Despite its strengths in unifying existing metrics and proposing new metrics, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive evaluation, and potential over-reliance on certain assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel evaluation framework that unifies existing metrics for synthetic tabular data under a coherent structure, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors propose a new set of metrics that can be used to evaluate the quality of synthetic data, which is a valuable addition to the existing body of knowledge.
- The framework is well-explained, with clear definitions and logical connections between the metrics, making it accessible and understandable.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of multiple synthesizers and datasets, which is crucial for understanding the performance of different methods in practical scenarios.
- The authors have made their code and datasets publicly available, which enhances the reproducibility and accessibility of their research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation between model-free and model-based metrics, which could lead to confusion about the evaluation methodology.
- The evaluation is limited to a small number of datasets and synthesizers, which may not sufficiently demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and time requirements of the proposed metrics, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The novelty of the framework is questioned, as it seems to resemble existing frameworks such as SDMetrics, with only minor modifications.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed framework, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential shortcomings.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the relationship between the proposed metrics and existing metrics, particularly in terms of their theoretical foundations and practical implications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the distinction between model-free and model-based metrics, and how they are applied in the evaluation process?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the datasets used in the evaluation, and what criteria were considered in their selection?
- What are the computational and time requirements for implementing the proposed metrics, and how do these compare to existing metrics?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and sampling times for the different synthesizers, and how these affect the practical applicability of the proposed framework?
- How do the authors address the potential for overfitting in their model-based metrics, and what steps have been taken to mitigate this risk?
- Could the authors elaborate on the relationship between the proposed metrics and existing metrics, particularly in terms of their theoretical foundations and practical implications?
