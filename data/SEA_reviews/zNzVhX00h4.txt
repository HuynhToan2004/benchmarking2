 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the loss landscape of two-layer ReLU neural networks in the mildly overparameterized regime, focusing on the existence of good local minima and the role of the rank of the Jacobian in determining their presence. The authors demonstrate that most activation regions in parameter space have no bad differentiable local minima, and that these regions often contain a high-dimensional space of global minima. They provide theoretical analysis and experimental evidence to support these claims, using random matrix theory and combinatorial techniques. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the optimization landscape of neural networks, suggesting that a large fraction of the loss landscape is favorable to optimization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the loss landscape of two-layer ReLU neural networks, focusing on the existence of good local minima and their relationship to the rank of the Jacobian.
- The theoretical results are supported by experimental evidence, which demonstrates the practical relevance and applicability of the theoretical findings.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the theoretical results and their implications for the optimization of neural networks.
- The analysis is novel and contributes to the understanding of the optimization landscape of neural networks, particularly in the context of mildly overparameterized networks.
- The paper is well-positioned within the existing literature, providing a clear discussion of related work and its contributions to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's results are limited to two-layer networks and do not generalize to deeper networks, which may limit the practical applicability of the findings.
- The experimental results are based on synthetic data and do not include real-world data, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a clear discussion on how the results could be applied in practical settings, such as training neural networks with gradient descent.
- The paper does not address the impact of different initialization schemes on the optimization landscape, which could be a significant factor in the training of neural networks.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the theoretical results and their practical implications, which could be addressed in the conclusion section.

**Questions:**
- How do the results of this paper compare to those of Soudry & Carmon (2016)? Specifically, can the results be extended to include the dropout noise used in their study?
- Can the results be generalized to deeper networks, and if so, how might the optimization landscape change with additional layers?
- In the context of the experimental results, how does the rank of the Jacobian relate to the optimization landscape of the network?
- How does the paper's analysis of the loss landscape apply to different initialization schemes, and what implications does this have for the practical training of neural networks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific dataset used and the initialization scheme employed?
