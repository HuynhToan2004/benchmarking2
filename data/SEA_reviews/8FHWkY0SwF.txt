 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach, FedSDR, for personalized federated learning (PFL) that addresses the shortcut trap problem by utilizing a shortcut discovery and removal method. This method is designed to extract personalized invariant features from local training data and eliminate shortcut features that may lead to over-reliance on non-informative data. The approach is theoretically grounded in structural causal models and demonstrates superior out-of-distribution generalization performance on various datasets. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of empirical comparison with existing invariant learning methods and its limited applicability to more complex or realistic settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the shortcut trap problem in personalized federated learning (PFL), a significant issue in the field.
- The theoretical analysis is solid, and the proofs are provided, enhancing the credibility of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is novel and innovative, providing a new perspective on addressing the shortcut trap problem in PFL.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.
- The empirical results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and discussion of the shortcut trap problem, which could confuse readers unfamiliar with the concept.
- The empirical comparison is limited, particularly lacking comparisons with existing invariant learning methods, which are crucial for validating the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The applicability of the proposed method is limited, as it assumes a specific data generating process that might not be realistic in practical scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these could be addressed in future work.
- The experimental setup is somewhat simplistic, using only one training environment per client, which may not reflect real-world federated learning scenarios where multiple training environments are common.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical implementations.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a clearer definition and discussion of the shortcut trap problem in the introduction to help readers unfamiliar with the concept better understand the paper?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing invariant learning methods, particularly in terms of OOD generalization performance?
- Can you elaborate on the data generating process assumed by your method and discuss its realism and practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the data generating process varies across different clients, potentially leading to different shortcut features?
- Could you discuss the limitations of your method and suggest potential directions for future research to address these limitations?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed method compare with existing methods, and what are the implications for its practical deployment in federated learning scenarios?
