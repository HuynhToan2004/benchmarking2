 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Hierarchical Importance Weighted Encoding (HIWE), a novel grid-based positional encoding technique aimed at accelerating NeRF training in large outdoor scenes. HIWE allocates model parameters based on scene importance, prioritizing regions with more detailed information. This method leverages a bounding box hierarchy to store learned parameters, enabling efficient indexing and faster training. The approach is evaluated on the drone-deploy dataset, showing improved rendering quality and reduced training times compared to state-of-the-art methods. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the method's presentation, the absence of comparisons with some relevant methods, and the limited evaluation to only one dataset.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel importance-weighted positional encoding, which is a significant contribution to the field of NeRFs.
- The methodology is clearly explained, with a well-structured presentation that aids in understanding the proposed approach.
- The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness in reducing training time while maintaining high rendering quality, as evidenced by visual comparisons.
- The use of a bounding volume hierarchy for efficient feature indexing is a novel approach that could potentially enhance the performance of NeRFs in large outdoor scenes.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the importance distribution function is generated and its impact on the training process.
- The evaluation is limited to a single dataset, which may not generalize well to other scenarios or datasets.
- There is a lack of comparison with other relevant methods such as F2Nerf, HollowNerf, and other fast-training NeRF methods.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of training times, which is crucial for evaluating the efficiency of the proposed method.
- The presentation could be improved to better illustrate the method's technical contributions and to provide more detailed explanations of the experimental setup and results.
- The paper's title may be misleading as it claims to be an accelerated training method, but the evaluation primarily focuses on rendering quality rather than training speed.

**Questions:**
- How is the importance distribution function generated, and how does it influence the training process?
- Can you provide more details on the training time of the proposed method compared to other methods?
- Why was the evaluation limited to a single dataset, and how does the method perform on other datasets such as those used in the F2Nerf paper?
- Could you clarify the role of the importance distribution in the training process, and how it affects the performance of the method?
- How does the proposed method handle different levels of importance in the scene, and what are the implications for the training and rendering processes?
