 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called "Thought Propagation" (TP), which leverages analogous problems to enhance the reasoning abilities of large language models (LLMs). The method involves a three-step process: LLM Propose, LLM Solve, and LLM Aggregate. LLM Propose generates analogous problems, LLM Solve solves these problems, and LLM Aggregate uses the solutions to refine the reasoning process. The approach is evaluated across three tasks: shortest-path reasoning, creative writing, and LLM-agent planning, showing significant improvements over baseline methods. Despite its novelty and the effectiveness demonstrated, the paper is critiqued for its complexity, limited evaluation scope, and potential for over-reliance on analogical reasoning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and detailed explanations of the proposed method, making it accessible and easy to understand.
- The methodology of Thought Propagation (TP) is novel, providing a new approach to reasoning tasks by leveraging analogous problems, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of TP across various tasks, including shortest-path reasoning, creative writing, and LLM-agent planning, showing significant improvements over existing methods.
- The method is compatible with existing prompting methods, making it a versatile tool for enhancing reasoning abilities in LLMs.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of TP, demonstrating its effectiveness through rigorous testing and comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of TP, particularly in scenarios where analogical reasoning might not be effective or applicable.
- The evaluation scope is limited, focusing primarily on LLM-based reasoning tasks. It would be beneficial to see how TP performs in other domains, such as vision or robotics, to assess its broader applicability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed complexity analysis, which is crucial for understanding the computational demands of TP, especially in more complex scenarios.
- The method's reliance on analogical reasoning might limit its effectiveness in tasks where the relationship between the input and analogous problems is not strong, potentially leading to inaccurate or ineffective solutions.
- The paper's experimental setup and results could benefit from more detailed descriptions and comparisons, particularly in the context of different LLM backends and task variations.

**Questions:**
- How does TP handle scenarios where the analogous problems are not directly related to the input problem? What measures are in place to ensure the accuracy and relevance of the solutions derived from these analogous problems?
- Could you provide more details on the computational complexity of TP, especially in scenarios where multiple layers of analogous reasoning are required?
- How does TP compare to other methods that leverage analogical reasoning, such as those mentioned in the related work section? What are the specific advantages or disadvantages of TP compared to these methods?
- Can you clarify the process of LLM Aggregate in more detail, particularly how the LLM determines which solutions to aggregate and how this affects the final output of TP?
- How does TP perform in tasks where the relationship between the input and analogous problems is weak? Are there any scenarios where TP might not be effective, and if so, how might these be addressed?
