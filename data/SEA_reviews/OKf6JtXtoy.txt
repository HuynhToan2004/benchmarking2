 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel dimensionality reduction method, MAP IT, which aims to align distributions over marginal probabilities in input and target spaces, thereby avoiding the need for normalization. This approach is distinct from existing methods like t-SNE and UMAP, which typically align based on pairwise probabilities. MAP IT utilizes a Cauchy-Schwarz divergence and employs a kernel smoothing technique to enhance the representation of local neighborhoods. The method's theoretical underpinnings are explored, and experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness in visualizing datasets like MNIST and COIL-20. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in certain sections, the limited scope of experimental validation, and the absence of a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, MAP IT, which aligns distributions over marginal probabilities, a significant departure from traditional methods like t-SNE and UMAP that focus on pairwise comparisons.
- MAP IT offers a dual perspective through continuous densities and kernel smoothing, enhancing the representation of local neighborhoods.
- The theoretical framework of MAP IT is robust, with clear theoretical explanations and a comprehensive mathematical derivation.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, with clear explanations of the theoretical concepts and practical implications.
- MAP IT has been shown to outperform other methods in visualizing datasets such as MNIST and COIL-20, demonstrating its effectiveness in capturing class structures.
- The paper provides a new perspective on dimensionality reduction and visualization, which could be valuable for future research and applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the explanation of the MAP IT framework and the role of neighborhoods in the optimization process.
- The experimental validation is limited, focusing primarily on small datasets and not comparing MAP IT with a broader range of state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity or the scalability of MAP IT to larger datasets, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the societal impact of the proposed method, which is an essential consideration for any new technology.
- There are several typographical and grammatical errors that need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role of neighborhoods in the optimization process and how they are defined and utilized in the MAP IT framework?
- How does MAP IT handle datasets with a large number of dimensions or a large number of samples? What are the computational implications of scaling up the method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of parameters and the methodology used in the visualization experiments?
- How does MAP IT compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency, scalability, and visualization quality?
- Can the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their method and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
- Is there a possibility to include more detailed visualizations or comparisons with other datasets to better demonstrate the effectiveness and generalizability of MAP IT?
