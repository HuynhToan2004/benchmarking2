 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SCARCE, a unified optimization framework for active learning in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). SCARCE integrates both structural and feature-based criteria to select nodes for labeling, aiming to improve the performance and fairness of GNNs. The framework is validated through extensive experiments on various datasets, showing that it can outperform existing methods in terms of both accuracy and fairness metrics. The paper also discusses the challenges of hyperparameter tuning and proposes a gradient balance method to address this issue. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and the lack of detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and fairness metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and challenging problem of active learning on graphs, which is crucial for advancing the field of graph learning.
- The proposed SCARCE method is well-motivated, easy to follow, and well-written, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The methodology integrates both structural and feature-based criteria, providing a comprehensive approach to active learning in GNNs.
- Extensive experiments are conducted on a variety of datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving both performance and fairness.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly presented, with a clear introduction of the motivation and methodology.
- The proposed gradient balance method is simple yet effective, and the paper includes a complexity analysis, which is beneficial for understanding the computational efficiency of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing active learning methods on graphs, which could help position the proposed method more effectively within the existing literature.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the computational complexity of SCARCE with other methods, which could be crucial for understanding its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed experimental results, particularly on larger datasets, to demonstrate the scalability and robustness of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help readers better understand its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the fairness metrics used in the experiments, which could lead to confusion about the actual fairness improvements claimed by the method.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by including more detailed experimental results and a clearer explanation of the proposed method, particularly in the methodology section.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity of SCARCE with other methods, including a discussion on the practical implications of this complexity in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in scaling the method to such datasets?
- Could the authors clarify the fairness metrics used in the experiments and provide a more detailed discussion on how these metrics were chosen and how they relate to the actual fairness improvements claimed by the method?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of hyperparameter tuning, particularly in terms of balancing the different criteria used in the optimization process?
- Could the authors provide a clearer explanation of the proposed method, particularly in the methodology section, to help readers better understand the technical details and the intuition behind the method?
