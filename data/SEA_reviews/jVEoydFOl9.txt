 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ULTRA, a novel method for learning universal and transferable graph representations, specifically designed for knowledge graph (KG) reasoning. ULTRA constructs a graph of relations using a labeling trick and employs a graph neural network (GNN) to learn relative representations of these relations. These representations are then used to predict links in the original graph, demonstrating strong zero-shot inductive inference performance on various KGs. The method is evaluated against state-of-the-art baselines and shows promising results, though it faces some limitations such as computational efficiency and scalability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, ULTRA, is simple yet effective, demonstrating strong zero-shot inductive inference performance on various knowledge graphs (KGs).
- The methodology is supported by a thorough evaluation against state-of-the-art baselines, showing that ULTRA outperforms these models in many cases.
- The paper is technically solid, with a focus on learning universal and transferable graph representations, which is a significant advancement in the field of knowledge graph reasoning.
- The experimental results are promising, demonstrating the effectiveness of ULTRA in handling different types of knowledge graphs and showing that it can generalize well to unseen graphs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The computational complexity of the method is not discussed, which is crucial for understanding its scalability and practical applicability.
- The paper does not adequately compare ULTRA with other state-of-the-art methods like MTDEA and InGram, which could provide a clearer picture of its relative performance.
- There is a lack of clarity in the experimental setup, particularly in the description of how the method performs on different types of knowledge graphs and the impact of different training setups.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the datasets used and the training parameters, which could affect the reproducibility and validation of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of ULTRA and discuss its scalability?
- How does ULTRA compare to other state-of-the-art methods like MTDEA and InGram, especially in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly how the method performs on different types of knowledge graphs and the impact of different training setups?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, especially when applied to larger and more complex knowledge graphs?
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of ULTRA and discuss potential future directions for improving the method?
