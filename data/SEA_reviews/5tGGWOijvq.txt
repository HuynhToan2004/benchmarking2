 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a framework named Prompt Risk Control (PRC) aimed at selecting prompts for Large Language Models (LLMs) that adhere to user-specified risk constraints. This framework utilizes statistical techniques to derive risk bounds from validation data, allowing for the selection of prompts that minimize risk across various scenarios. The paper provides a theoretical foundation and empirical evidence to support the use of PRC, showcasing its application in real-world settings like medical question summarization and chatbot applications. Despite its novelty and practical utility, the paper has been critiqued for its limited empirical evaluations, lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, and insufficient discussion on the societal impacts of its applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and accessible, making complex concepts understandable to a broad audience.
- It introduces a novel framework, Prompt Risk Control (PRC), which offers a systematic and rigorous approach to prompt selection based on risk constraints, a significant contribution to the field of large language models (LLMs).
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of existing techniques, such as Hoeffding bounds and quantile-based risk measures, and extends these to address distribution shifts in deployment scenarios.
- The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of PRC in practical applications like code generation, chatbot applications, and medical question summarization.
- The paper addresses a critical and timely issue in the deployment of LLMs, focusing on the control of risk through prompt selection, which is a crucial aspect of LLM deployment.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the societal impacts of the proposed framework, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of its application.
- There is a need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, such as those that use different risk measures or different approaches to risk control.
- The empirical evaluations are limited, and the paper could benefit from more extensive testing and validation to strengthen its claims.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the PRC framework, such as its applicability in scenarios where risk constraints cannot be met, which could limit its practical utility.
- There are concerns about the generalizability of the findings due to the limited scope of the experiments, which primarily focus on specific tasks and datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the technical challenges and potential solutions for handling distribution shifts, which are critical for the practical application of the framework.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the societal impacts of the proposed framework, particularly in terms of fairness and bias in LLM deployment?
- How does the PRC framework compare with other existing methods for risk control in LLM deployment?
- What are the specific technical challenges in applying the PRC framework to handle distribution shifts, and how can these be addressed?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the PRC framework and discuss potential solutions or modifications to address these limitations?
- In the context of the experiments, how do the results generalize to other tasks or datasets, and what are the implications of these findings for the broader applicability of the framework?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the selection of datasets and the choice of risk measures used in the experiments?
