 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel mathematical framework, JoMA, to analyze the training dynamics of multilayer Transformer architectures. It integrates out the self-attention layer, focusing on the dynamics of MLP layers only, and predicts attention patterns in multilayer Transformers. The framework is supported by theoretical findings that suggest attention becomes sparse during training, followed by a dense phase, and provides insights into the learning hierarchy in multilayer Transformers. Experiments are conducted on real-world datasets and pre-trained models, demonstrating the framework's applicability and relevance to practical scenarios. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental validation, the lack of comparison with existing methods, and the need for a more rigorous theoretical analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the main results and theoretical findings.
- The proposed framework, JoMA, is novel and provides a fresh perspective on the training dynamics of multilayer Transformer architectures.
- The theoretical findings are supported by experiments, showing a correlation between the theoretical predictions and the practical behavior of transformer models.
- The paper is significant as it addresses the dynamics of multilayer Transformer architectures, which is crucial for understanding and improving the performance of these models.
- The authors have provided a novel theoretical framework that integrates out the self-attention layer in Transformers, which is a novel approach that could lead to new insights and understandings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed framework.
- The experimental validation is limited, focusing primarily on a single dataset (Wikitext2/Wikitext103) and a few pre-trained models (OPT and Pythia). More diverse experiments are needed to validate the generalizability of the findings.
- The theoretical analysis is not rigorous, with some theorems and assumptions not being well-explained or justified, which could undermine the credibility of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed framework, which could have provided a more balanced view of the contributions and potential shortcomings.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly linked to the theoretical claims, which could weaken the overall impact of the study.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed framework compare with existing methods in terms of effectiveness and novelty?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons and discussions with related works to clarify the contributions and differences of the proposed framework?
- How does the proposed framework handle different types of attention mechanisms, such as self-attention and cross-attention?
- Can the authors provide more experimental results to validate the findings across different datasets and pre-trained models?
- How does the proposed framework address the limitations and potential biases in the training data, such as the orthonormality of embedding vectors?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations and justifications for the theoretical assumptions and theorems, particularly in the appendix?
