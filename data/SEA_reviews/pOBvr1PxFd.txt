 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel layerwise sparsity method for large language models (LLMs), specifically targeting non-uniform layerwise sparsity. This approach, termed Outlier Weighed Layerwise sparsity (OWL), is designed to preserve outlier features, which are critical for maintaining model performance. The method is tested across various LLMs, including LLaMA-V1 and OPT, and shows significant performance improvements over existing methods like Wanda and SparseGPT. The paper also explores the relationship between outlier features and layerwise sparsity, providing empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of OWL. Despite some concerns about the novelty and computational efficiency, the paper is commended for its thorough empirical analysis and clear presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow content, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors have conducted a thorough empirical analysis, which includes a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies.
- The paper introduces a novel layerwise sparsity method, OWL, which effectively preserves outlier features, leading to improved performance in LLM pruning.
- The methodology is supported by a detailed empirical study, which provides valuable insights into the relationship between outlier features and layerwise sparsity.
- The paper offers a fresh perspective on LLM pruning by highlighting the importance of layerwise sparsity ratios, which is a departure from traditional uniform sparsity.
- The proposed method demonstrates significant performance gains, surpassing state-of-the-art methods like Wanda and SparseGPT.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the OWL method, particularly in comparison to other pruning methods.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it appears to be an extension of existing methods like Wanda, which also considers outlier features.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodology section, particularly in the explanation of how the OWL method is applied across different layers.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the impact of OWL on the computational efficiency or the practical deployment of LLMs.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are somewhat limited, focusing primarily on a few large models and not sufficiently exploring the generalizability of the findings across different model sizes or architectures.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with other pruning methods, particularly those that also consider outlier features, to establish the superiority of the OWL method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the OWL method, including the time and space required for pruning?
- How does the OWL method compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical deployment to other pruning methods, especially those that also consider outlier features?
- Can the authors clarify the methodology section, particularly how the OWL method is applied across different layers and how the sparsity ratios are determined?
- How does the OWL method perform when applied to different model sizes or architectures, and what are the implications for performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors elaborate on the empirical findings regarding the non-uniform distribution of outliers across layers and its impact on model performance?
- How does the OWL method compare in terms of generalizability and applicability to other types of models or tasks beyond LLM pruning?
