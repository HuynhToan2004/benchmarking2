 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to video-language learning, specifically addressing the multi-granularity noisy correspondence (MNC) problem in instructional videos. The authors employ a unified optimal transport framework, incorporating video-paragraph and clip-caption contrastive losses to capture long-term dependencies based on optimal transport. This method aims to improve temporal learning and video understanding by addressing both coarse-grained and fine-grained misalignments between video clips and captions. The proposed method, NOise Robust Temporal Optimal traNsport (Norton), is evaluated across various tasks including video retrieval, videoQA, and action segmentation, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling noisy instructional videos.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant problem of multi-granularity noisy correspondence in instructional videos, which is crucial for improving the temporal learning and video understanding.
- The proposed method, NOise Robust Temporal Optimal traNsport (Norton), is novel and innovative, employing video-paragraph and clip-caption contrastive losses to capture long-term dependencies based on optimal transport.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments conducted are extensive and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various tasks such as video retrieval, videoQA, and action segmentation.
- The use of optimal transport (OT) for handling misalignment issues in video-language learning is novel and effective, particularly in addressing the challenges of coarse-grained and fine-grained misalignments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The use of the term "multi-granularity" is confusing and not well-defined, which could lead to misunderstandings and misinterpretations of the method's focus.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing approaches, such as TAN and December, which also address noisy correspondence learning in video-language pre-training.
- The paper does not release the code, which limits the reproducibility of the results and the ability for other researchers to build upon the work.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of using optimal transport for handling misalignment issues in video-language learning, which could provide a more balanced view of the method's effectiveness and applicability.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with existing approaches like TAN and December in terms of effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might impact its practical application?
- How does the method handle the issue of coarse-grained misalignment, particularly in scenarios where the method may not be able to effectively address this issue?
- Could the authors clarify the definition and use of the term "multi-granularity" in the context of the proposed method?
- How does the method perform when the video-text alignment is not well-aligned, and what are the implications of this for the overall effectiveness of the method?
