 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MILLET, a novel framework for time series classification that integrates Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) with deep learning techniques. It proposes a conjunctive pooling method to enhance interpretability and introduces a new synthetic dataset, WebTraffic, to evaluate the model's interpretability. The framework is designed to be compatible with existing deep learning models and is evaluated across various datasets, showing improvements in interpretability and predictive accuracy. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the paper, the novelty of the method, and the empirical evaluation, which was criticized for not adequately demonstrating the superiority of MILLET over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to using MIL for time series classification.
- The proposed method is simple, easy to implement, and can be applied to existing models with minimal modification, making it accessible and practical.
- The paper introduces a new dataset, WebTraffic, which is useful for evaluating the interpretability of models.
- The authors provide a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, including both predictive accuracy and interpretability, which is crucial for understanding the model's behavior.
- The paper includes a variety of deep learning models and datasets, which enhances the robustness of the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the synthetic dataset, including how it is generated and what it represents.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods rather than a fundamentally new approach.
- The empirical evaluation is not convincing, with the proposed method not consistently outperforming existing baselines, and the improvements are sometimes marginal.
- There are concerns about the fairness of the evaluation, particularly in how the results of MILLET are presented compared to other methods.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, especially in terms of interpretability, and does not sufficiently address the computational efficiency of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and a more thorough analysis of the results, including a discussion on the statistical significance of the improvements.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the synthetic dataset, WebTraffic, is generated and what it represents?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and interpretability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the results were evaluated and how the models were compared?
- In light of the concerns about the novelty and the empirical evaluation, how do the authors respond to the criticisms regarding the contribution of their work?
- Is there a possibility to include more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of interpretability and computational efficiency?
