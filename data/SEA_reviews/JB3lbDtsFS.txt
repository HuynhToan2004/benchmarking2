 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel meta-learning framework that models human annotator simulation as a zero-shot density estimation problem. This framework leverages a normalizing flow to estimate the conditional distribution of human annotations, allowing for the generation of human-like annotations for unlabeled test inputs. The method is evaluated on three real-world human evaluation tasks, demonstrating superior capability and efficiency in predicting aggregated behaviors of human annotators and simulating inter-annotator disagreements. The approach is designed to capture the inherent variability in human judgments, which is crucial for ethical AI practices. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticisms regarding the clarity of its methodological descriptions, the choice of evaluation metrics, and the generalizability of its findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel meta-learning framework that models human annotator simulation as a zero-shot density estimation problem, which is a creative and innovative approach.
- The proposed method demonstrates superior capability and efficiency in predicting the aggregated behaviors of human annotators, matching the distribution of human annotations, and simulating the level of inter-annotator agreement.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and examples that help in understanding the proposed method.
- The evaluation is thorough, covering a variety of datasets and tasks, which demonstrates the robustness and applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses the significant issue of human annotator simulation, which is crucial for ethical AI practices, and provides a novel solution to this problem.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed description of the methodology, particularly in Section 3, which currently lacks sufficient detail.
- The choice of evaluation metrics and their justification is not convincingly explained, which could affect the perceived effectiveness of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the generalizability of the findings, especially in terms of how well the proposed method performs across different types of data and tasks.
- The paper could benefit from a broader range of experiments, including more diverse datasets and tasks, to better demonstrate the robustness and applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of computational complexity, which is a significant concern given the potential for high computational costs.
- There are some unclear notations and equations, which could confuse readers and detract from the clarity of the paper.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed description of the methodology, particularly in Section 3, to help clarify the proposed approach?
- How do the authors justify the choice of evaluation metrics, and could they provide more detailed explanations or additional metrics to support their claims?
- Could the authors discuss the generalizability of their findings, especially in terms of how well the proposed method performs across different types of data and tasks?
- How does the proposed method compare to other approaches in terms of computational complexity and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results and discussions, especially for the speech quality assessment task, where the results are not as clear-cut?
- How do the authors address the issue of computational complexity, and what strategies or optimizations have been considered to make the method more practical for real-world applications?
