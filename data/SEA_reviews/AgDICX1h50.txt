 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel prompting method called "analogical prompting" for large language models (LLMs) to guide their reasoning process. This method prompts LLMs to self-generate relevant exemplars or knowledge in context, which is then used to solve new problems. The approach is inspired by human analogical reasoning and aims to avoid the need for labeled exemplars or retrieval. The paper evaluates this method across various reasoning tasks, including math problem solving, code generation, and logical reasoning, demonstrating its effectiveness compared to 0-shot and few-shot CoT. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the method's distinction from existing methods, and the potential for the LLM to generate incorrect or irrelevant exemplars.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel prompting method, "analogical prompting," which guides LLMs to self-generate relevant exemplars or knowledge in context, enhancing their ability to solve new problems.
- The method is evaluated on various reasoning tasks, including math problem solving, code generation, and logical reasoning, demonstrating its effectiveness compared to 0-shot and few-shot CoT.
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and provides detailed experimental results, including comparisons with different prompting methods and various base LLMs.
- The idea of using LLMs to generate exemplars for reasoning tasks is innovative and addresses the challenge of providing relevant guidance or exemplars for LLMs in complex tasks.
- The paper is significant in its contribution to the field of LLM prompting, providing a method that can potentially reduce the need for manual labeling and simplify the process of retrieving relevant exemplars.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its method from existing methods such as "self-consistency" and "least-to-most," which also aim to enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs.
- The method's effectiveness may depend on the quality and relevance of the generated exemplars, and there is a risk that LLMs might generate incorrect or irrelevant exemplars, which could mislead the reasoning process.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other prompting methods like "self-consistency" and "least-to-most," which could provide a clearer picture of the method's advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper's novelty is questioned as similar methods have been explored in prior works, and the proposed method appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of the work.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the differences between your method and "self-consistency" and "least-to-most"? How does your method address the limitations or challenges of these existing methods?
- How does the method ensure that the generated exemplars are relevant and correct, especially when LLMs are not 100% accurate?
- Could you provide more details on the retrieval-based CoT method used in the experiments? How does it compare to the proposed method in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- How do you address the potential for LLMs to generate incorrect or irrelevant exemplars, and what measures are in place to mitigate this risk?
- Could you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how these impacts might be mitigated or addressed?
- How does the method perform when compared to other methods like self-consistency and least-to-most in terms of inference time and memory usage?
