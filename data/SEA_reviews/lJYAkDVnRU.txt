 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Context-Aware Meta-Learning (CAML), a novel approach to meta-learning that emulates in-context learning in Large Language Models (LLMs) for visual tasks. CAML leverages a frozen pre-trained feature extractor, such as CLIP, and ELMES encoding for labels to represent both image and label embeddings in a joint embedding space. The Transformer encoder is trained to predict the class of a query image given this sequence of joint embeddings. The authors claim that CAML can learn new visual concepts during inference without fine-tuning, which is demonstrated through empirical results on multiple benchmarks. However, the paper has been critiqued for its reliance on frozen backbones and the lack of novelty in its approach, as similar methods have been explored previously.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a strong theoretical analysis of the proposed method.
- The proposed method, CAML, is simple yet effective, and the theoretical analysis is solid, with proofs provided in the appendix.
- CAML achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) results on multiple benchmarks, showing its effectiveness in meta-learning tasks.
- The idea of using frozen pre-trained feature extractors is innovative, reducing the need for extensive training data and potentially improving performance.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and a well-presented theoretical framework that supports the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's reliance on frozen backbones may limit its applicability in scenarios where fine-tuning is necessary for better performance.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as similar approaches have been explored previously, such as using frozen pre-trained feature extractors and ELMES encoding for labels.
- The method's performance heavily depends on the quality of the pre-trained feature extractor, which may limit its effectiveness in certain scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of the support set's class distribution affecting the performance of CAML, which could limit its practical application.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other meta-learning algorithms that do not require pre-training, such as those that use frozen feature extractors?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the ELMES encoding for labels is learned and how it affects the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the support set's class distribution is not balanced or biased?
- Is there a way to adapt the method to handle scenarios where the class distribution at inference time is different from that at training time?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential strategies to address these limitations?
