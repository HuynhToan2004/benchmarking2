 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Decomposed Prompt Tuning (DePT), a novel method for parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) that decomposes soft prompts into shorter soft prompts and low-rank matrices. This approach aims to reduce memory and computational costs by up to 20% compared to traditional prompt tuning methods. The DePT method has been evaluated across various natural language processing (NLP) and vision-language (VL) tasks, showing competitive or better performance than state-of-the-art PEFT methods. The methodology involves optimizing the shorter soft prompt and the low-rank matrices with different learning rates, which allows for efficient training and inference. Despite its strengths, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with other PEFT methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Decomposed Prompt Tuning (DePT), which decomposes soft prompts into shorter soft prompts and low-rank matrices to optimize training and inference efficiency.
- The methodology is supported by comprehensive evaluations on 23 natural language processing (NLP) and vision-language (VL) tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and versatility.
- The experiments show that DePT outperforms state-of-the-art PEFT approaches in certain scenarios and integrates seamlessly with parameter-efficient transfer learning (PETL) approaches.
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and provides clear explanations of the methodology and experimental results.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of DePT, including comparisons with vanilla prompt tuning (PT) and other PEFT methods.
- The paper is significant as it addresses the challenge of parameter-efficient fine-tuning, which is crucial for large language models (LLMs) that are subject to heavy daily querying.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for decomposing soft prompts into shorter soft prompts and low-rank matrices, and the rationale behind the use of different learning rates for the shorter soft prompt and the pair of low-rank matrices is not well-explained.
- The novelty of the DePT method is questionable as similar approaches have been previously proposed, such as the use of low-rank matrices in prompt tuning.
- The paper does not compare DePT with other PEFT methods like LoRA, Adapter, or BitFit, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of its effectiveness.
- The paper lacks detailed explanations and justifications for certain experimental settings, such as the choice of parameters and the impact of different hyperparameters on performance.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights into its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical analysis or insights into the behavior of the DePT method, which could enhance the understanding of its effectiveness and efficiency.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the motivation behind the DePT method and the rationale behind the use of different learning rates for different components of the method?
- How does DePT compare with other PEFT methods like LoRA, Adapter, or BitFit in terms of performance, memory usage, and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations and justifications for the experimental settings and parameter choices in the DePT method?
- How does the DePT method perform when combined with other PEFT methods, such as LoRA or Adapter?
- Can the authors provide more theoretical analysis or insights into the behavior of the DePT method, particularly in terms of its efficiency and effectiveness across different model architectures and sizes?
