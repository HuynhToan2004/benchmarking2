 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Neural Field Classifier (NFC) framework, which transforms existing Neural Field Regressors (NFRs) into classification variants through a novel Target Encoding module and a classification loss. This approach is designed to enhance the robustness and generalization capabilities of neural fields, particularly in scenarios with sparse inputs, corrupted images, and dynamic scenes. The methodology involves encoding continuous regression targets into a high-dimensional discrete encoding, enabling a multi-label classification task. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the NFC framework, showing improvements in novel view synthesis and geometry reconstruction. However, the paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and overlooked question in neural fields by comparing regression versus classification, which is a novel approach.
- The proposed method, NFC, is simple to implement and has shown impressive improvements in various tasks such as novel view synthesis, geometry reconstruction, and robustness to sparse inputs and image noise.
- The paper is well-written, making the methodology and results easy to understand, and includes comprehensive experiments and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of binary encoding to transform neural fields into classification tasks is a novel approach that shows promise in improving the generalization capabilities of neural fields.
- The paper's experiments are thorough, including novel view synthesis, geometry reconstruction, and robustness to sparse inputs and image noise, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scope and applicability.
- The comparison between the proposed method and existing methods like DVGO and NeuS is not fair as the proposed method uses a different loss function, which might give an unfair advantage.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational costs of the proposed method compared to existing methods, which is important for practical implementation.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant omission in the ethical considerations of the research.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method, particularly how it improves generalization capabilities.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by adding more visuals and explanations, especially in the methodology section, to aid in understanding the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data is not corrupted or sparse?
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the computational costs of the proposed method versus existing methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these impacts could be mitigated?
- How does the proposed method perform in dynamic scenes, and can the authors provide more detailed results and analysis in this area?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of how the proposed method improves generalization capabilities?
