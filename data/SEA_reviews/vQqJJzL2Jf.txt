 **Summary:**
The paper explores the extrapolation capabilities of Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) by investigating the effects of spectral shifts in the Fourier spectrum of PDE solutions. The authors introduce a novel metric, the Weighted Wasserstein-Fourier distance (WWF), to quantify these shifts and demonstrate its utility in predicting extrapolation performance. They also propose a transfer learning strategy to mitigate the effects of larger spectral shifts, reducing extrapolation errors by up to 82%. The paper is structured around a series of experiments, each designed to isolate the impact of different factors on extrapolation performance. Despite some concerns regarding the depth of theoretical analysis and the generalizability of the findings, the paper is commended for its clear writing, logical structure, and significant contributions to the understanding of PINN extrapolation behavior.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and logical structure, and provides a comprehensive overview of the current state of research on PINN extrapolation.
- The introduction of the Weighted Wasserstein-Fourier distance (WWF) as a new metric for quantifying extrapolation performance is innovative and potentially useful for future research.
- The experiments are thorough, covering a variety of PDEs and demonstrating the impact of different factors on extrapolation performance.
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of PINNs, which is crucial for advancing the application of these models in real-world scenarios.
- The findings, particularly those related to the role of spectral shifts in extrapolation performance, are both surprising and insightful, contributing to a better understanding of PINN behavior.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a deeper theoretical analysis, which could provide a more robust foundation for the empirical observations.
- There is a noted absence of a high-dimensional analogue to the experiments, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the transfer learning approach, particularly in scenarios where the transfer learning is not effective.
- The experiments are limited to specific PDEs, and the results may not generalize to other types of PDEs or more complex scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the theoretical implications of the findings and a more comprehensive comparison with existing literature.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis to support the empirical observations?
- How does the transfer learning approach perform in scenarios where it is not effective, and what are the implications of these failures?
- Could the authors extend the experiments to include high-dimensional scenarios and more complex PDEs to validate the generalizability of the findings?
- How do the results compare with other methods that aim to improve the extrapolation capabilities of PINNs, such as those mentioned in related works?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the transfer learning approach in mitigating the effects of spectral shifts and how it impacts the overall performance of the PINNs?
