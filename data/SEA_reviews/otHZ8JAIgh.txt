 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multimodal learning framework, PIBD, designed to address redundancy issues in multimodal data for cancer survival prediction. The framework comprises two key modules: Prototypical Information Bottleneck (PIB) and Prototypical Information Disentanglement (PID). PIB addresses intra-modal redundancy by modeling prototypes to select discriminative instances within a modality, while PID disentangles multimodal data into modality-common and modality-specific knowledge. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of PIBD through extensive experiments on five cancer benchmark datasets, showing superior performance compared to existing methods. Despite the innovative approach and experimental validation, concerns about the clarity of the methodology, the lack of detailed comparisons, and the need for more comprehensive evaluations persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of multimodal learning, specifically in cancer survival prediction, which is of significant interest and relevance to the medical community.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, with a clear and logical structure, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable.
- The experiments are well-designed and comprehensive, involving multiple datasets and various baseline methods, and the results are well-presented, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper introduces a novel approach using prototypical information bottleneck and disentanglement, which is well-articulated and provides a clear rationale for its design.
- The methodology is supported by a theoretical framework, which enhances the credibility of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed comparisons with recent state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a more robust validation of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The experimental results, particularly in the Kaplan-Meier analysis, are not convincingly superior to existing methods, raising questions about the practical utility of the proposed method.
- The methodology section is somewhat unclear, particularly the explanation of the prototypical information bottleneck and disentanglement modules, which could benefit from more detailed exposition or additional illustrative examples.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include issues such as computational complexity, scalability, and generalizability to other types of data or tasks.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis, which are crucial for understanding the robustness and applicability of the method.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments and how they were tuned?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational complexity and scalability?
- Can you provide more details on the data preprocessing steps, particularly how the genomic data is extracted from the TCGA database?
- How does the method handle different types of data, such as textual or audio data, and what modifications would be required to adapt the method to these types of data?
- Could you provide more detailed explanations or examples to clarify the operation of the prototypical information bottleneck and disentanglement modules?
- How does the method handle different types of redundancy in the data, and what are the implications for the performance of the method in real-world applications?
