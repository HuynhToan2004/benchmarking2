 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LOGRL, a novel approach to lead optimization in drug discovery using goal-conditioned reinforcement learning (GCRL). LOGRL differentiates from traditional methods by training on a distinct task, source-to-target path prediction, and employing a beam search strategy during molecule generation. This approach aims to enhance the generation of molecules with high Tanimoto similarity to target molecules, without direct exposure to this metric during training. LOGRL also incorporates a negative action selection strategy to address sparse rewards and a post-curation process for property optimization. The method's novelty lies in its ability to generate molecules without relying on similarity metrics, which is crucial for drug discovery. However, the paper lacks comprehensive experimental validation and a detailed discussion on the limitations of the approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to lead optimization using goal-conditioned reinforcement learning, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that rely on molecular similarity metrics.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear and concise writing that makes the paper easy to follow.
- The use of offline reinforcement learning to address the sparse reward problem in GCRL is a novel approach, which is not commonly seen in the literature.
- The paper demonstrates the potential of using GCRL to address the challenge of lead optimization, showing promising results in the context of drug discovery.
- The integration of reaction rules into the generation process is a notable contribution, as it enhances the synthesisability of the proposed candidates and suggests a possible route of synthesis.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive experimental validation. The experiments are limited to five trypsin inhibitors, which may not adequately represent the broader applicability of the method.
- The evaluation metrics used are not robust enough to fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the method. More comprehensive metrics such as docking scores and biological activity scores are missing.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the approach, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the method.
- The method's reliance on a negative action selection strategy and a post-curation process for property optimization raises concerns about the scalability and efficiency of the approach.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the generation process, such as the number of molecules generated and the time required for each step, which are crucial for assessing the practicality and efficiency of the method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the approach, including its scalability, efficiency, and applicability to different types of drug discovery scenarios?
- How does the method handle the sparsity of rewards in the offline dataset, and what steps were taken to mitigate the effects of this sparsity on the learning process?
- Could the authors elaborate on the role of the negative action selection strategy and the post-curation process in the overall performance of the method?
- How does the method ensure that the generated molecules are diverse enough to meet the desired properties, and what steps are taken to mitigate the risk of overfitting to the training data?
- Can the authors provide more details on the generation process, such as the number of molecules generated and the time required for each step, to better understand the practicality and efficiency of the method?
