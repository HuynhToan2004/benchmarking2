 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the effectiveness of process supervision versus outcome supervision in training reward models for large language models (LLMs) in mathematical reasoning tasks. It demonstrates that process supervision, which involves grading each step of the reasoning process, outperforms outcome supervision, which only evaluates the final outcome. The study is based on a large dataset of 800,000 step-level human feedback labels, collected using GPT-4, which is used to train a process reward model. The paper also explores the use of active learning to improve the efficiency of process supervision. It presents a detailed analysis of the performance of these models on various datasets, including the MATH dataset, and compares the results with state-of-the-art models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations and visualizations that aid in understanding the results.
- The study provides a comprehensive comparison between outcome supervision and process supervision, demonstrating that process supervision outperforms outcome supervision in mathematical reasoning tasks.
- The paper is significant in its contribution to the field of large language models (LLMs) by highlighting the importance of process supervision in training reward models, which is crucial for improving the reliability and performance of LLMs.
- The dataset of step-level human feedback labels is valuable for future research and could be beneficial for the development of more reliable LLMs.
- The use of active learning in process supervision is highlighted as a promising method for reducing the amount of data needed for training, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the findings, which is crucial for a comprehensive understanding of the implications of the research.
- There is a need for more extensive experiments to verify the conclusions, especially in terms of the generalizability of the findings across different domains and datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential biases introduced by the human annotators in the process supervision data, which could affect the reliability and validity of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training data for the ORMs and PRMs, which is crucial for reproducibility and verification of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for test set contamination, which could affect the validity of the results and the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the ethical implications of the research, particularly in relation to the use of human annotators and the potential biases in the data.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the training data for the ORMs and PRMs, including the specific datasets used and the training procedures?
- How do the authors address the potential for test set contamination in their experiments, and what steps were taken to mitigate this issue?
- Could the authors discuss the potential biases introduced by the human annotators in the process supervision data, and how these biases were addressed or mitigated?
- How do the authors ensure the reliability and validity of the process supervision data, especially in light of the potential for annotator bias and subjectivity?
- Could the authors provide more details on the ethical considerations involved in the use of human annotators and the potential biases in the data?
- How do the authors plan to address the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of their findings, particularly in terms of the generalizability and applicability of the results?
