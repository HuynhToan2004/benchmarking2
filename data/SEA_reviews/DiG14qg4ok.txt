 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Low-coherence Subspace Projection (LcSP) to enhance the learning capacity of Gradient Orthogonal Projection (GOP) in continual learning. LcSP projects features and gradients into subspaces with low coherence, aiming to reduce mutual interference between tasks and improve model expressiveness. The method is evaluated on several benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness in maintaining learning capacity even with a large number of tasks. However, the paper is criticized for its unclear writing, lack of comprehensive comparison with existing methods, and insufficient experimental validation, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method, LcSP, is a novel approach to enhancing the learning capacity of Gradient Orthogonal Projection (GOP) in continual learning by projecting features and gradients into low-coherence subspaces.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis to show the advantages of the proposed method in terms of stability and plasticity.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can maintain learning capacity even with a large number of tasks.
- The method is a plug-and-play module that can be easily extended to most GOP methods, which enhances its applicability and flexibility.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the method is clearly described, which facilitates reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- The writing of the paper is unclear in some sections, particularly the descriptions of figures and the methodology used.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental evidence to support the claims made, such as the learning capacity degradation problem of GOP-based methods.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The method's novelty is questioned as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques, which may not be sufficiently distinct to warrant a new method classification.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some results, such as the performance on the miniImageNet dataset, being significantly lower than expected.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational efficiency and scalability of LcSP with other methods, particularly in terms of training time and memory usage?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the number of tasks is significantly larger, such as in the continual learning setting with a large number of tasks?
- Can the authors provide more details on the methodology used in the experiments, particularly the choice of hyperparameters and the training conditions?
- How does the method handle the learning capacity degradation problem of GOP-based methods, and can the authors provide more experimental evidence to support this claim?
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of the "babel function" in the method, and how it contributes to the overall effectiveness of the LcSP approach?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its applicability in different scenarios?
