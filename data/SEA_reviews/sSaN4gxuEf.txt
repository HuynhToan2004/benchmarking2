 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for test-time domain adaptation using a few unlabeled samples from the target domain. The method leverages pre-trained models, specifically CLIP, to learn domain-specific knowledge and generate domain-specific prompts. These prompts are then used to guide the model's inference, enabling adaptation to new, unseen domains. The paper presents extensive experiments on several benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, the paper has been critiqued for its complexity, lack of clarity in certain methodological details, and potential over-reliance on CLIP for domain-specific knowledge.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem of test-time domain adaptation, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The method proposed is novel and innovative, leveraging recent advancements in pre-trained models and foundation models.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and detailed experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The methodology is supported by extensive ablation studies that show the effectiveness of each component, providing a robust validation of the proposed approach.
- The paper includes a variety of benchmarks and comparisons with existing methods, which helps in positioning the work within the current state of the art.
- The proposed method is computationally efficient, requiring minimal additional computational resources at test time.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is complex and difficult to follow, with some sections requiring more clarity and detailed explanations.
- The methodology relies heavily on the pre-trained model (CLIP), which might limit its applicability to other models.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential shortcomings.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, especially in terms of the number of source domains and the size of the knowledge bank.
- There are concerns about the novelty of the method, as it seems to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with other methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and performance.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential shortcomings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the knowledge bank is initialized and updated?
- How does the method handle the scalability issues, especially in terms of the number of source domains and the size of the knowledge bank?
- Can the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the domain-aware contrastive loss in the method?
- How does the method perform when applied to other models besides CLIP?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- How does the method ensure the robustness of the domain prompts, especially in scenarios where the number of samples is limited?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations on how the method handles the domain-specific knowledge acquisition and transfer?
