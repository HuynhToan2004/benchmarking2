 **Summary:**
The paper explores the interaction between different modalities in multimodal learning, specifically focusing on quantifying multimodal interactions in a semi-supervised setting. It introduces two lower bounds: one based on shared information between modalities and another on disagreement between unimodal classifiers, and derives an upper bound using connections to approximate algorithms for min-entropy couplings. The authors validate these bounds through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating their accuracy in tracking true interactions. The theoretical results are used to estimate multimodal model performance, guide data collection, and select appropriate multimodal models for various tasks. The paper is well-received for its theoretical contributions and practical applications, although some concerns are raised about the experimental validation and the practical implications of the theoretical results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a strong theoretical foundation and rigorous proofs.
- The introduction of lower and upper bounds for quantifying multimodal interactions is innovative and provides a solid theoretical framework for understanding the interactions between different modalities.
- The paper provides a clear and intuitive explanation of the relationships between synergy and other interactions, which are crucial for understanding the nature of multimodal interactions.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are convincing, demonstrating the practical applicability of the theoretical results.
- The paper is original in its approach to quantifying multimodal interactions using information theory, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and assumptions of the theoretical results, which could affect the practical applicability of the findings.
- The experimental validation is limited, with only a few datasets used, which might not be sufficient to generalize the results across different domains.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which is crucial for understanding the practical feasibility of the proposed approaches.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review, especially in the area of multimodal interactions, to better position its contributions within the existing body of knowledge.
- There is a lack of discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of how they can be applied to improve the performance of multimodal models in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of how they can be used to improve the performance of multimodal models?
- How do the proposed bounds compare to other existing measures of multimodal interactions, and what are the advantages of using the proposed bounds over other methods?
- In the experimental validation, how were the unimodal classifiers trained, and what impact did this have on the results?
- Could the authors discuss the computational complexity of the proposed methods in more detail, and how this might affect the practical feasibility of using the methods in large-scale applications?
- How do the authors address the limitations and assumptions of the theoretical results, and what are the implications of these limitations for the practical applicability of the findings?
