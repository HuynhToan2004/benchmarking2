 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SpaCE, a novel toolkit designed to address spatial confounding in causal inference. SpaCE generates semi-synthetic datasets with realistic spatial structures, enabling the evaluation of causal inference methods. It utilizes state-of-the-art machine learning ensembles to create counterfactuals and outcome variables, which are then masked to create benchmark datasets. The toolkit is built on a well-structured pipeline that includes data generation, evaluation, and benchmarking, with a focus on enhancing the reproducibility and comparability of causal inference methods. The paper also discusses the limitations of current benchmark datasets and proposes a new approach that could potentially improve the evaluation of causal inference methods in spatial settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing a comprehensive toolkit for evaluating causal inference methods in spatial settings, focusing on the unique challenges posed by spatial confounding.
- The dataset generation process is well-structured, with clear documentation and reproducibility, which enhances the transparency and reusability of the results.
- The paper includes a variety of datasets from different domains, which adds to the toolkit's utility and applicability across different research areas.
- The authors have provided a detailed description of the dataset generation process, including the choice of predictive models and the spatial autocorrelation of residuals, which helps in understanding the dataset's characteristics.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The inclusion of code and detailed documentation facilitates the reproducibility and application of the toolkit by other researchers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the generated datasets, which could impact the generalizability and applicability of the results.
- There is a need for a more comprehensive evaluation of the toolkit, including a broader range of causal inference methods and more diverse datasets to validate the toolkit's effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential biases introduced by the synthetic data generation process, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the ethical implications of the toolkit, particularly concerning the use of synthetic data in real-world applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate the concept of spatial confounding from other types of confounding, which could lead to confusion about the toolkit's specific focus and utility.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential societal impacts of the toolkit, particularly concerning the use of synthetic data in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the generated datasets and how these limitations might affect the generalizability and applicability of the results?
- How does the toolkit handle different types of causal inference methods, particularly those that might not be directly applicable to spatial data?
- What are the specific ethical considerations related to the use of synthetic data in real-world applications, and how does the toolkit address these concerns?
- How does the toolkit ensure the comparability of results across different datasets, especially when the datasets have varying spatial characteristics?
- Could the authors provide more details on the process of selecting the variables to be masked in the datasets and how this affects the performance of different causal inference methods?
- How does the toolkit handle the potential biases introduced by the synthetic data generation process, and what steps are taken to mitigate these biases?
