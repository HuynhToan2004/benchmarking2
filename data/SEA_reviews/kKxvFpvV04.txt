 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for quantifying inductive bias, focusing on the amount of information required to specify a well-generalizing model within a hypothesis space. It employs a sampling-based algorithm to estimate inductive bias, which can be applied to various model classes and datasets. The authors provide an upper bound on the approximation error of their estimation approach and demonstrate its application across different model classes and datasets, showing that neural networks encode a massive amount of inductive bias. The methodology involves modeling the test error distribution using a scaled non-central chi-squared distribution and provides a theoretical framework for understanding inductive bias in machine learning models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and interesting problem of quantifying inductive bias, which is crucial for understanding and improving machine learning models.
- The proposed method is straightforward and well-explained, with a clear presentation that aids in understanding the concepts and the proposed solutions.
- The paper provides a theoretical framework and empirical evidence that neural networks encode a massive amount of inductive bias, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is general and applicable to different model classes and datasets, showing its versatility and potential for broader application.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and illustrations that enhance the reader's comprehension of the concepts and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and justification of the hypothesis space, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on sampling from the hypothesis space raises concerns about its effectiveness and the accuracy of the results, especially when the hypothesis space is large and complex.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is essential for assessing its practicality and scalability.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, particularly those that quantify inductive bias, which could help in understanding the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper's assumptions and limitations are not clearly stated, which could limit the understanding of the method's applicability and scope.
- The empirical results are not convincingly demonstrated to be robust or generalizable across different datasets and model classes.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and justification of the hypothesis space, particularly how it affects the quantification of inductive bias?
- How does the method handle the computational complexity, especially when the hypothesis space is large and complex?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the assumptions and limitations of their method, and how these affect the results and applicability?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods that quantify inductive bias, and what are the advantages or disadvantages compared to these approaches?
- Can the authors provide more details on the empirical results, particularly how they were obtained and what they imply about the generalizability and robustness of the findings?
