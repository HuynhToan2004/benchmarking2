 **Summary:**
The paper explores the local group testing problem, where each hidden element is an autonomous entity that can analyze feedback for queries it is part of, aiming to learn about all other hidden agents. It introduces a deterministic non-adaptive sequence of queries that allows non-hidden elements to learn about all other hidden agents. The paper presents polynomial-time constructions for both local selectors and local avoiding selectors, along with near-matching lower bounds. The algorithms are designed to operate in a distributed manner, with each agent only querying elements it is part of, and avoiding elements from "interfering" clusters. The paper also discusses extensions to fault-prone testing environments and different feedback functions, showing how the results can be applied to codes.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to the group testing problem by allowing each element to analyze feedback for queries it is part of, which is a significant departure from traditional group testing models.
- The algorithms presented are designed to operate in a distributed manner, which could be beneficial in practical applications where privacy and security are concerns.
- The paper provides both upper and lower bounds for the local group testing problem, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The results are shown to be nearly tight, indicating that the algorithms are close to optimal.
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions and proofs that are easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The problem addressed is relevant and has practical implications, particularly in the context of local testing and privacy concerns.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the motivation behind the local group testing problem, which could limit the understanding and relevance of the results.
- There is a lack of experimental validation or real-world applications discussed, which could strengthen the paper's claims and impact.
- The paper is quite technical, which could limit its accessibility to readers not familiar with the group testing literature.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications and potential applications of the local group testing algorithms.
- The presentation could be improved by including more intuitive explanations and visual aids to help illustrate the complex concepts.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the local group testing algorithms.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the motivation behind the local group testing problem and provide examples or scenarios where this model would be particularly useful?
- How do the results of this paper compare to existing works in the field, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Are there any potential negative societal impacts of the local group testing algorithms, and how might these be mitigated?
- Could the authors provide more details on the practical implementation and scalability of the local group testing algorithms, particularly in terms of computational resources and time complexity?
- How do the results of this paper apply to other feedback functions beyond the ones considered, and what are the implications for extending the results to other settings?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending the local group testing algorithms to more general settings or different types of group testing problems?
