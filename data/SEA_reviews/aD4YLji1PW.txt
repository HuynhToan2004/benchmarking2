 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to curriculum learning in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) using a genetic algorithm to optimize a population of opponents and environments. This method aims to address the challenges of overfitting and instability in training by generating scenarios that are similar to those encountered during training, thus enhancing the learning process. The approach is evaluated against various baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness in competitive two-player benchmarks, particularly in continuous control settings. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is critiqued for its lack of clarity in the problem formulation, insufficient theoretical analysis, and unclear presentation of experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to curriculum learning in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), which is both innovative and relevant to the field.
- The use of a genetic algorithm for curriculum generation is highlighted as a significant contribution, offering a new perspective on how to optimize opponents and environments in MARL.
- The empirical results presented in the paper demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showing that it outperforms several strong baselines across various competitive two-player benchmarks.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The inclusion of an ablation study provides valuable insights into the effectiveness of the different components of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the problem formulation, particularly in the definitions and notations used, which could confuse readers and hinder understanding.
- There is a significant absence of theoretical analysis, which could have strengthened the paper by providing a deeper understanding of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The presentation of experimental results is not clear, with crucial details such as the number of seeds used and the standard deviation of the results missing, which could affect the reliability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which is a critical concern in MARL.
- The related work section is inadequately detailed, missing out on significant existing literature that could have provided valuable context and comparisons.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which are crucial for understanding its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and notations used in the paper to improve clarity and understanding?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of overfitting in MARL?
- What are the specific contributions of the genetic algorithm in the context of curriculum learning for MARL?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of their method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing approaches in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors elaborate on the differences and similarities between the proposed method and other existing methods, particularly in the context of multi-agent RL?
- What are the implications of using a genetic algorithm for curriculum generation in MARL, and how does it compare to other methods like domain randomization?
