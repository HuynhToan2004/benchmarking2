 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the federated domain adaptation problem by proposing a novel aggregation rule, FedGP, and an auto-weighting scheme to enhance the performance of federated learning in scenarios with limited target data and significant domain shifts. The authors develop a theoretical framework to analyze the performance of server aggregation rules and propose a lightweight aggregation rule, FedGP, which significantly improves target performance under domain shift and data scarcity. The paper is well-structured, providing a comprehensive review of related literature and conducting extensive experiments to validate the proposed methods. Despite its strengths, the paper suffers from some clarity issues in the presentation of theorems and assumptions, which could affect its readability and comprehensibility.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and challenging problem in federated learning (FL) by focusing on federated domain adaptation (FDA) with limited data in the target domain.
- It introduces a novel aggregation rule, FedGP, and an auto-weighting scheme, which are theoretically grounded and provide a principled approach to solving the FDA problem.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions, theorems, and proofs that support the proposed methods.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments to validate their methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach across various datasets.
- The paper provides a theoretical framework for analyzing the performance of server aggregation rules in the FDA setting, which is both novel and relevant.
- The proposed methods are simple, intuitive, and have been shown to be effective in various experiments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the computational and communication costs associated with the proposed methods, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- There are several minor errors and inconsistencies in the paper, such as incorrect citations and formatting issues.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of domain shift, which is a critical aspect of federated domain adaptation.
- The assumption of data scarcity in the target domain may not be practical, as it is not common for data to be scarce for only one client in a federated learning setting.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the theoretical framework and the intuition behind the proposed methods.
- The experimental results, while showing improvements, are not consistently better than existing methods, and the paper could benefit from more rigorous comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper could be improved by including more real-world datasets and addressing the practical implications of the proposed methods in more detail.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the computational and communication costs associated with the proposed methods, and how these costs compare to existing methods?
- How do you address the practical issue of domain shift in federated domain adaptation?
- In the experimental results, why are the improvements not consistently better than existing methods?
- Can you provide more details on how the proposed methods perform in real-world scenarios, particularly in terms of practical implementation and scalability?
- How do the proposed methods handle the issue of data scarcity in the target domain, and what are the implications of this assumption in practical applications?
- Could you elaborate on the intuition behind the proposed methods, particularly the FedGP and auto-weighting schemes?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed methods over existing methods in terms of performance and practical applicability?
