 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for imitation learning from observation (ILFO) that addresses the challenge of learning progress-dependent tasks, where the agent needs to learn earlier behaviors before advancing to later ones. The method employs a dynamic discount factor scheduling, which gradually increases the discount factor over time, allowing the agent to focus on early rewards initially and gradually incorporating later rewards. This approach is evaluated against several baseline methods and reported to outperform them on several tasks from the Meta-World benchmark. The methodology is supported by ablation studies and theoretical justifications, although concerns are raised about the clarity of the experimental setup and the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are solid, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is intuitive and simple, yet effective, particularly in tasks with progress dependency.
- The ablation studies conducted are thorough, providing insights into the impact of different components of the proposed method.
- The method's approach to discount scheduling is novel and addresses a significant challenge in imitation learning from observation, particularly in tasks where earlier behaviors need to be learned before progressing to later ones.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear presentation of the problem, methodology, and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the method applies to tasks without progress dependency, raising concerns about its generalizability.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, especially in tasks where the proposed method performs worse or similarly to baselines.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the need for a cost function or the potential for the method to be less effective in tasks with complex dependencies.
- There are inconsistencies in the presentation of results and figures, which could mislead the reader about the performance of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the related work, particularly in the area of curriculum learning in RL.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the proposed method applies to tasks without progress dependency?
- How does the method perform in tasks where it does not outperform the baselines, and what insights can be gained from these results?
- Is there a specific reason why the method performs worse in certain tasks, such as the door-lock task?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use inverse models or different reward functions?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational costs of their method compared to the baselines?
- Can the authors discuss the potential for extending the method to include more complex dependencies or to handle tasks where the expert's trajectory is not strictly monotonically increasing?
