 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training paradigm for machine translation using large language models (LLMs), specifically focusing on the translation task. The approach involves a two-stage fine-tuning process: initial fine-tuning on monolingual data and subsequent fine-tuning on a small set of high-quality parallel data. This method, applied to Llama-2 models, shows significant improvements in translation quality, outperforming both zero-shot Llama-2 and existing models like NLLB-54B and GPT-3.5-D. The paper also explores the impact of monolingual data and parallel data quality on translation performance, concluding that high-quality monolingual data and parallel data are crucial for effective fine-tuning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel training paradigm for machine translation using LLMs, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed training strategy achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on WMT'21 and WMT'22 test sets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the impact of monolingual data and parallel data quality on translation performance, which is crucial for understanding the factors influencing translation quality.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is essential for a broad audience.
- The experimental results are robust, showing significant improvements over zero-shot Llama-2 and existing models, highlighting the practical applicability of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed training paradigm, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of computational costs across different stages of the training process, which is essential for evaluating the efficiency of the proposed methods.
- There is a lack of detailed information on the monolingual data used, which is crucial for reproducibility and validation of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the potential societal impact of the proposed methods, which is an important consideration for the ethical implications of the work.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the generalizability of the findings to other LLMs and translation tasks, as well as a broader set of experiments to validate the claims.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational costs associated with each stage of the training process?
- How does the proposed training paradigm compare in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements to other existing methods?
- Could the authors elaborate on the selection process for the high-quality parallel data used in the second stage of fine-tuning?
- How does the proposed training paradigm perform with different LLMs, and are there any specific LLMs that might not benefit as much from this approach?
- Could the authors provide more details on the monolingual data used, including the languages and volumes, to enhance the reproducibility and transparency of the results?
- How does the proposed training paradigm compare in terms of translation quality and efficiency with other existing methods, such as those using encoder-decoder models or other LLM-based approaches?
