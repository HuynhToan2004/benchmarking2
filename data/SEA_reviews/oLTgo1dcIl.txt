 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the convergence of stochastic extragradient methods (SEG) in the context of minimax problems, focusing on the convergence of SEG-RR and SEG-FF, which are shown to diverge in the convex-concave setting. To address this issue, a new method, SEG-FFA, is introduced, which incorporates a flip-flop shuffling and anchoring technique to ensure convergence. The paper provides theoretical analysis and experimental validations, demonstrating that SEG-FFA outperforms other shuffling-based methods in both strongly-convex-strongly-concave and convex-concave settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations that make the content accessible and understandable.
- The authors have successfully identified the issues that prevent SEG from converging in the convex-concave setting and have provided a novel solution through the introduction of anchoring.
- The convergence analysis of SEG-FFA is robust and rigorous, demonstrating a significant improvement over existing methods in terms of convergence rates and theoretical guarantees.
- The paper is original in its approach to shuffling-based SEG, providing a new perspective on how to improve convergence rates in stochastic optimization.
- The theoretical results are supported by a comprehensive set of experiments that validate the theoretical findings, showing the practical applicability of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions of strong monotonicity and bounded gradient variance may not hold.
- The experimental results are limited to specific cases, which might not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could provide more insights into the practical implications of the theoretical results, especially in terms of how the proposed method might perform in real-world applications.
- There is a lack of comparison with other methods that might also benefit from the anchoring technique, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's effectiveness.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the monotonicity of the saddle gradient operator, are quite strong and might limit the applicability of the results to more general cases.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of how the proposed method might perform in real-world applications?
- How does the anchoring technique compare to other methods that might also benefit from similar techniques?
- Can you clarify the role of the anchoring technique in the convergence analysis, especially in light of the concerns about its practical applicability?
- What are the specific challenges in extending the analysis to non-monotone problems, and how might these be addressed?
- Can you elaborate on the experimental results, particularly in terms of how they validate the theoretical findings and what insights they provide into the practical performance of the proposed method?
