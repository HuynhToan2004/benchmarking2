 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of transformer models in computing the greatest common divisor (GCD) of two positive integers, trained on a supervised translation task where input pairs are translated into their GCD. The authors investigate how the model learns to cluster input pairs with the same GCD, and how different training distributions affect the model's performance and explainability. The experiments reveal that models trained on uniform operands predict a limited range of GCDs, while those trained on log-uniform operands and outcomes perform better and maintain explainability. The paper also discusses the impact of training distributions on model performance and the model's ability to generalize across different input distributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the experimental setup and results.
- The problem of learning the greatest common divisor (GCD) is an interesting and challenging mathematical task, and the experiments are well-designed to explore the effects of different training distributions on model performance and explainability.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to model explainability by interpreting model predictions on selected mathematical tasks, leveraging theoretical understanding of the underlying problems.
- The findings on the impact of training distributions on model performance and explainability are significant and could be applied to other arithmetic tasks, particularly in fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) on mathematical problems.
- The paper demonstrates that transformers can be trained to perform exact calculations involving integer divisibility, which is a central task in integer arithmetic and number theory.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to the GCD task, which might not generalize well to other mathematical or arithmetic tasks.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned, as similar findings have been reported in previous works on transformers for arithmetic tasks.
- The experiments are limited to a single model architecture (transformer), which might not fully support the claims made about the generalizability of the findings to other architectures.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the findings, such as the potential impact of different model architectures or the generalizability of the results to other mathematical or arithmetic tasks.
- The paper's claims about the model's ability to generalize to other tasks are not convincingly supported by the experimental evidence provided.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the model's ability to generalize to other mathematical or arithmetic tasks?
- How do the findings on the impact of training distributions on model performance and explainability apply to other mathematical or arithmetic tasks?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of their findings, particularly in terms of the generalizability of the results to other model architectures or mathematical tasks?
- How do the findings on the model's performance on the GCD task compare to other mathematical or arithmetic tasks?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential implications of their findings for the development of more robust and explainable models in mathematics and arithmetic?
