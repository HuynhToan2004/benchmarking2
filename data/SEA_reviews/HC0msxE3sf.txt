 **Summary:**
This paper explores the application of the beta-Variational Autoencoder (beta-VAE) framework to the Lewis's signaling game in the context of emergent communication. By redefining the objective function as the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO), the authors aim to influence the statistical properties of emergent languages, particularly focusing on Zipf's law of abbreviation and Harris's articulation scheme. The study proposes a novel approach by using beta-VAE to model the sender's encoding process, which is optimized to maximize the ELBO. The paper also discusses the potential of using this approach to improve the meaningfulness of word segments in emergent languages. Despite the innovative approach, the paper faces criticisms regarding its experimental setup, the clarity of its contributions, and the novelty of its findings, which are seen as not sufficiently distinct from existing literature.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the background and related work.
- The idea of using a beta-VAE to model the sender's encoding process in emergent communication is novel and innovative, providing a new perspective on the modeling of emergent languages.
- The paper is well-motivated, with a clear rationale for using a beta-VAE in the context of emergent communication, and the proposed approach is well-explained.
- The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of the model, including the use of a prior message distribution to influence the statistical properties of emergent languages.
- The paper addresses an important problem in emergent communication, specifically the issue of meaningful word segmentation, and proposes a solution using a beta-VAE framework.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental setup is not clearly described, and the paper lacks a detailed explanation of the experimental conditions and the results, making it difficult to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper's contributions are not clearly articulated, and the novelty of the findings is questionable as similar approaches have been explored in previous works.
- The paper does not adequately compare its results with existing methods, such as those using a Bernoulli distribution, which limits the ability to assess the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper's reliance on a fixed prior message distribution might limit the generalizability of the findings, and the use of a neural language model as a prior could provide more insight into the statistical properties of emergent languages.
- The paper's claims regarding the influence of the prior message distribution on the statistical properties of emergent languages are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the conditions under which the experiments were conducted and the results observed?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing approaches, particularly those using a Bernoulli distribution as a prior?
- Could you clarify the role and influence of the prior message distribution on the statistical properties of emergent languages?
- In light of the concerns about the novelty and clarity of contributions, could you elaborate on how this paper advances the understanding or application of emergent communication compared to existing works?
