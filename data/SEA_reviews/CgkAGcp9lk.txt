 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel methodology for generating stable crystal structures in multi-component alloys using a Crystal Diffusion Variational Autoencoder (CDVAE) model. The model, named P-CDVAE, incorporates a neural network for predicting crystal phase from latent vectors, enhancing the denoising performance and material generation capabilities. The methodology is demonstrated through experiments involving NiFeCr alloys, showcasing improvements in denoising and material generation compared to existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the method to other types of alloys and the lack of comprehensive experimental validation, including the absence of comparative analysis with other generative models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem in the field of materials science by proposing a method for generating stable crystal structures in multi-component alloys, which is crucial for material discovery and optimization.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the dataset creation, training, and evaluation processes, making it accessible to readers.
- The paper includes a variety of experiments to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method, such as the denoising performance and material generation capabilities, which are supported by DFT validation.
- The authors provide a detailed description of the dataset creation, which is crucial for understanding the method's applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results, which enhances its readability and comprehensibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other generative models, which could provide a better understanding of the method's advantages and limitations in the context of other approaches.
- The generalizability of the method to other types of alloys is unclear, as the experiments are limited to NiFeCr alloys.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive experimental validation, particularly in terms of the number of generated structures and the diversity of the generated materials.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the local search process, which is a crucial component of the method.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, which is necessary for a complete understanding of its applicability and potential challenges.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other generative models in terms of denoising and material generation capabilities?
- Can the method be generalized to other types of alloys, and if so, how does the performance vary across different alloy types?
- What is the computational cost of the method, and how does it compare with other methods in terms of efficiency?
- How diverse are the generated materials, and what are the implications of this diversity for the practical applicability of the method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the local search process, particularly how it affects the generation of materials with desirable properties?
- How does the method perform when optimizing for different material properties, and what are the implications of this optimization for the stability and other properties of the generated materials?
