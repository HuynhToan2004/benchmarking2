 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to simultaneous machine translation (SiMT) using a decoder-only large language model (LLM) fine-tuned on causally aligned source-target sentence pairs. The model, which utilizes a wait token to control input segmentation, is evaluated on English-German and English-Russian language pairs, showing competitive performance against state-of-the-art baselines. The methodology involves using a small dataset of causally aligned sentence pairs to fine-tune the LLM, which is then tested in both text-to-text and speech-to-text translation scenarios. The paper also explores the potential of zero-shot performance of LLMs in SiMT tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to SiMT using a decoder-only LLM, which is a departure from traditional encoder-decoder models.
- The methodology is straightforward and effectively demonstrates the utility of decoder-only LLMs for SiMT tasks.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results are promising, showing that the proposed method can achieve competitive performance against state-of-the-art baselines.
- The use of wait tokens to control input segmentation is innovative and could be a significant advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of generalizability and scalability.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a small dataset of causally aligned sentence pairs, which may not fully represent the diversity and complexity of real-world translation scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for data leakage due to the use of overlapping content between the training data and the pre-training data.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, with insufficient comparisons to other baselines and a lack of quantitative data on inference speed and latency.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed technology.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify whether the datasets used for training and testing are the same, and if so, how this was addressed to avoid data leakage?
- How does the proposed method perform when scaled to larger datasets or when applied to a wider range of languages?
- Could the authors provide more details on the inference speed and latency of the model, particularly in comparison to other baselines?
- How does the model handle the trade-off between quality and latency, especially in scenarios where the model may need to wait for more context to make accurate translations?
- Can the authors discuss the potential for adapting the proposed method to end-to-end SiMT systems, which might be more practical for real-world applications?
- How does the model handle the translation of complex structures such as clauses and sentences, especially when the source sentence is incomplete?
