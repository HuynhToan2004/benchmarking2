 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Fiber Monte Carlo (FMC) for estimating integrals with discontinuous integrands, which are prevalent in applications such as physical simulation and design. FMC samples line segments rather than points from the domain, allowing for differentiable estimates of integrals, which is crucial for gradient-based optimization. The method is demonstrated to be effective in various applications, including image stylization, topology optimization, and computational geometry. The paper provides theoretical justifications and practical demonstrations, showcasing the method's versatility and applicability. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the presentation, the theoretical assumptions, and the experimental validation, particularly in higher-dimensional settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method called Fiber Monte Carlo (FMC), which is a differentiable variant of the simple Monte Carlo estimator that samples line segments rather than points from the domain. This method is well-motivated, clear, and well-written, with a clear structure and logical flow.
- FMC is a general methodology that can be applied in various applications such as image stylization, topology optimization, and computational geometry, demonstrating its versatility and potential impact.
- The paper is well-researched, with thorough literature reviews and comparisons to existing methods, highlighting the novelty and effectiveness of FMC.
- The method is theoretically sound, with proofs provided in the appendix, and it is demonstrated to be effective in practical applications, as shown in the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the theoretical justifications and the experimental setups. Some figures and equations are not well-explained or referenced, which could confuse readers.
- The method's assumptions and limitations are not clearly stated, which could lead to misunderstandings or misapplications of the method. For instance, the method's applicability to high-dimensional domains is not adequately addressed.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare FMC to existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and theoretical guarantees.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implementation of FMC, including the choice of hyperparameters and the computational complexity of the method.
- There is a need for more rigorous experimental validation, especially in higher-dimensional settings and with more complex integrands, to demonstrate the robustness and scalability of the method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the theoretical justification for the FMC method, particularly in terms of the assumptions and the conditions under which the method is applicable?
- How does the FMC method compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and theoretical guarantees?
- Can the authors provide more details on the practical implementation of FMC, including the choice of hyperparameters and the computational complexity of the method?
- How does the FMC method perform in higher-dimensional settings, and what are the implications of this for the practical applicability of the method?
- Can the authors provide more examples or case studies to illustrate the versatility and effectiveness of FMC in different applications?
