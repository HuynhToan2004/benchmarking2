 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to reconstructing dynamic scenes from multi-view videos using a Signed Distance Function (SDF) flow. This method represents the change in SDF over time, which is then used to infer scene flow, allowing for the reconstruction of 3D geometry. The approach is validated through experiments on the CMU Panoptic dataset, showing improvements over existing methods. The paper also explores the mathematical relationship between SDF flow and scene flow, which is crucial for understanding the dynamics of the scene. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticisms regarding its clarity, the practicality of its assumptions, and the limited evaluation on only two datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to 3D reconstruction of dynamic scenes using a Signed Distance Function (SDF) flow, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The mathematical relationship between SDF flow and scene flow is clearly articulated, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and understandable, which is crucial for a complex topic like 3D reconstruction.
- The methodology is straightforward and easy to follow, which is beneficial for both academic and practical applications.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in terms of accuracy and completeness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of key concepts such as "the first-order derivative of the SDF with respect to time" and the relationship between SDF flow and scene flow.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the assumption of rigid motion in local regions, may not hold true in practical scenarios involving non-rigid deformations.
- The paper is limited to specific datasets (CMU Panoptic and Tensor4D), which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the method, particularly the time required for training and inference.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its results with other state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the concept of "the first-order derivative of the SDF with respect to time" and its relationship with the SDF flow?
- How does the method handle non-rigid deformations, and what are the implications for the assumption of rigid motion in local regions?
- What is the computational complexity of the proposed method, and how does it compare to other methods in terms of training and inference times?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their method and how these might affect its practical application?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of accuracy, completeness, and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors elaborate on the choice of datasets and the rationale behind their selection?
- Could the paper benefit from additional evaluations, such as using synthetic datasets or comparing with other recent methods like Tensor4D?
