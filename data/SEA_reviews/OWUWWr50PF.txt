 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to Euclidean clustering by providing a closed-form solution that avoids local minima issues associated with traditional methods like Lloyd's algorithm. The approach involves decomposing the data matrix into singular value decomposition (SVD) and thresholding the projection matrix to recover cluster assignments. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that under certain conditions, this method can deterministically recover the true clustering. Experiments on both synthetic and real data show that this method outperforms existing algorithms, although it may not be as competitive in all scenarios. The paper also discusses potential relaxations of the method to improve its practical applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel closed-form solution to Euclidean clustering that avoids local minima issues, which is a significant advancement over traditional methods like Lloyd's algorithm.
- The theoretical analysis is well-supported, with clear and elegant proofs that demonstrate the deterministic recovery of true clustering under certain conditions.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and illustrations that aid in understanding the complex mathematical concepts.
- The proposed method shows strong performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets, outperforming existing methods in some cases.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that verify the theoretical results and demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and scalability, especially for large datasets.
- The method's reliance on the SVD of the data matrix might introduce computational challenges, especially for high-dimensional data.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the existence of a subspace for the data and the uniqueness of the projection operator, may not hold in all practical scenarios, which could limit the method's applicability.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is based on strong assumptions, such as the distinctness of clusters and the absence of noise, which may not hold in real-world datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, especially how they translate into practical applications.
- The paper's experimental results are not consistently superior across all datasets, and the method's performance varies depending on the dataset characteristics.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the method performs in scenarios where the assumptions of distinct clusters and absence of noise do not hold?
- How does the method's performance scale with the dimensionality of the data, and what are the computational implications for high-dimensional data?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to existing methods?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art clustering methods, particularly in terms of scalability and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors discuss the potential implications of the method's assumptions on its practical applicability?
- How do the authors justify the choice of parameters in the method, particularly the threshold parameter lambda, and what are the implications of different choices on the method's performance?
