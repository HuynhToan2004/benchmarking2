 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Variational Autoencoder (VAE) architecture, named Policy Disentangled Variational Autoencoder (PDVAE), aimed at learning policy representations from video data. The PDVAE is designed to generate diverse videos aligned with specific policies, with the ability to alter the policy during generation. The architecture integrates a lossy compression module and a policy module, which utilize Hierarchical Quantized Autoencoders (HQA) to extract policy representations from video data. The model is evaluated on datasets such as Moving MNIST, KTH action dataset, and VizDoom, demonstrating its effectiveness in generating videos that adhere to specified policies. However, the paper's methodology and the clarity of its presentation have been critiqued, with concerns about the novelty of the approach and the depth of experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-designed architecture that effectively learns policy representations from video data.
- The proposed method is novel and innovative, introducing a new approach to video generation by integrating reinforcement learning and variational autoencoder concepts.
- The paper presents a comprehensive experimental setup, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method through various experiments and comparisons with existing models like MoCoGAN.
- The use of Hierarchical Quantized Autoencoders (HQA) for policy extraction from video data is highlighted as a significant strength, contributing to the overall effectiveness of the model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing models, particularly in terms of video quality metrics and the use of different datasets for evaluation.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as similar methods have been previously explored in the literature, such as learning policy representations from video data.
- There is a lack of clarity in the paper's presentation, particularly in the methodology section, which could benefit from more detailed explanations and figures to aid understanding.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the method across different datasets and environments, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with some figures and results being misleading or insufficiently explained.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to existing methods, particularly in terms of learning policy representations from video data?
- How does the proposed method handle the generalization to new, unseen policies or environments?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations and figures to clarify the methodology and the experimental setup, especially in sections where the paper is currently unclear or misleading?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of video quality metrics, such as FID and FVD, to other state-of-the-art models in video generation?
- Can the authors provide more comprehensive comparisons with other models, including both qualitative and quantitative evaluations, to better establish the effectiveness and superiority of their method?
