 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to evaluating the robustness of neural networks against image corruptions, termed visually-continuous corruption robustness (VCR). It proposes two new metrics, Human-Relative Model Robustness Index (HMRI) and Model Robustness Superiority Index (MRSI), to compare neural network performance with human perception across a range of corruption levels. The study involves 7,718 human participants and various image transformations, revealing a significant gap between neural network and human robustness. The authors also explore the concept of visually similar corruption functions and their impact on human and neural network performance. Despite the paper's comprehensive approach and significant findings, concerns about the clarity of the methodology, the limited scope of corruption types, and the lack of detailed results and discussions have been raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive evaluation involving 7,718 human participants and a variety of image transformations.
- It introduces two novel metrics, HMRI and MRSI, which effectively compare neural network performance with human perception, offering a new perspective on neural network robustness.
- The paper identifies visually similar corruption functions and their impact on human and neural network performance, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The study provides valuable insights into the robustness gap between neural networks and humans, particularly in the context of image classification tasks.
- The authors have made significant efforts to ensure the reproducibility of their work by providing the validation set, human robustness data, and evaluation code as a toolbox and benchmark.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the methodology, particularly the choice of corruption functions and the sampling strategy, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a limited scope of corruption types considered, focusing primarily on pixel-level corruption, which may not adequately represent the full range of image corruptions.
- The paper does not discuss or compare its findings with existing benchmarks or metrics, which could help contextualize the results and validate the proposed metrics.
- The results and discussions are not sufficiently detailed, with some figures and tables lacking clarity and explanation, making it difficult to interpret the findings.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed metrics, nor does it discuss the limitations of its approach, which could impact the credibility and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- How were the corruption functions chosen for the study, and what criteria were used to select them?
- Could you explain the rationale behind the choice of 200ms as the time limit for human classification, and how this affects the results?
- In the context of the results presented, what do the values of HMRI and MRSI represent, and how do they relate to the performance of neural networks and humans under different conditions?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing benchmarks or metrics, and what are the advantages or disadvantages of using VCR over other metrics?
- Can you provide more details on the evaluation of the proposed metrics, particularly how they were validated and what the results indicate about the robustness of neural networks?
- What are the limitations of the proposed approach, and how do these affect the applicability and generalizability of the findings?
