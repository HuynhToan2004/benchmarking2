 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of contrastive learning, specifically the Bradley-Terry loss, in estimating fitness functions from experimental data, particularly focusing on global epistasis models. The authors demonstrate that the MSE loss, while effective in sparse epistatic representations, struggles with dense representations induced by global epistasis. They propose that contrastive losses, such as Bradley-Terry loss, can effectively learn sparse latent functions from limited data, providing a novel approach to estimating fitness functions from experimental data. The paper also presents theoretical insights into the sparsity of fitness functions and empirical evidence supporting the use of contrastive losses over MSE loss.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in estimating fitness functions from experimental data, which is crucial for protein engineering.
- The authors provide a novel approach by using contrastive losses, particularly the Bradley-Terry loss, to learn sparse latent functions, which is a promising direction for improving model performance.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The theoretical insights into the sparsity of fitness functions and the relationship between global epistasis and the density of fitness functions in the epistatic domain are well-explained and supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper demonstrates that minimizing contrastive loss functions, such as the Bradley-Terry loss, is an effective technique for extracting sparse latent functions, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors provide a comprehensive empirical evaluation of the proposed approach, showing that it outperforms other loss functions in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and training time of the proposed method compared to other methods, which could be crucial for practical implementation.
- The comparison of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency is insufficient.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly how it might perform in scenarios with high noise or when the data is not sufficiently informative.
- The paper's focus on specific types of fitness functions (e.g., those with global epistasis) might limit the generalizability of the findings to other types of fitness functions.
- The theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method, particularly the use of contrastive losses and their relationship to global epistasis, are not thoroughly explored, leaving some gaps in the theoretical justification.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with other ranking loss functions and a broader range of experimental setups to validate the proposed method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and training time of the proposed method compared to other methods?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and computational efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods in practical applications?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential avenues for future research to address these limitations?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with high noise or when the data is not sufficiently informative?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other ranking loss functions, particularly in terms of their theoretical foundations and practical applications?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the data might not fit the assumptions of global epistasis?
- Could the authors discuss the implications of their findings for other types of fitness functions, particularly those not characterized by global epistasis?
