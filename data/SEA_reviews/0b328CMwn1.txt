 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Activation Prompt (AP) for visual prompting in vision models, which extends the traditional visual prompting (VP) by applying perturbations to intermediate activation maps rather than directly to input images. The authors argue that AP can improve the performance and efficiency of VP by optimizing the perturbations at each layer, which is a departure from the direct application of VP to the input layer. The paper provides a theoretical analysis and extensive experiments to support the effectiveness of AP, comparing it with various visual prompting techniques and parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods. The experiments demonstrate that AP can outperform VP and other PEFT methods across multiple datasets and model architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Activation Prompt (AP), which extends the traditional visual prompting (VP) by applying perturbations to intermediate activation maps, offering a new approach to enhance the performance of VP.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the effectiveness of AP across various model architectures and datasets, demonstrating its superiority over VP in many scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes detailed experimental setups, making it accessible and reproducible.
- Theoretical insights are provided to explain the behavior of AP in different layers of vision models, enhancing the understanding of the proposed method.
- The experiments are extensive, covering a wide range of datasets and models, and the results are convincing, showing that AP outperforms VP in many cases.
- The paper includes a thorough discussion of the limitations and potential negative societal impacts, which is commendable for its transparency and ethical considerations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with other parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency metrics.
- The choice of AP layers is not uniform across different model architectures, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the number of parameters used in AP with other PEFT methods, which could affect the fairness of the comparisons.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of different learning rates on the performance of AP, which is a critical hyperparameter for training stability and convergence.
- The theoretical analysis and experimental results are not sufficiently connected, which could limit the understanding of the theoretical contributions.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational overhead introduced by AP, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of AP, particularly in terms of its applicability to different model architectures and datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the inconsistency in the number of parameters used in AP across different model architectures?
- How does the choice of AP layers affect the performance of the model, and could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of this effect?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons of the computational overhead introduced by AP with other PEFT methods?
- How does the choice of learning rate affect the performance of AP, and could the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter search used in the experiments?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of AP, particularly in terms of its applications in various domains?
- How does AP compare to other PEFT methods in terms of performance and efficiency, and could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of these metrics?
