 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GraphECL, a novel contrastive learning method for graphs that leverages an MLP and GNN to learn node representations. The method aims to achieve fast inference by using MLP for inference, while GNN is used for training to capture structural information. The paper provides theoretical insights into how GraphECL learns node representations and demonstrates its effectiveness through extensive experiments on various datasets. It also discusses the scalability and efficiency of GraphECL, highlighting its potential in practical applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes comprehensive theoretical analysis and empirical results.
- The proposed method is simple, efficient, and achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in most cases.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including theoretical insights and empirical evidence, which supports the effectiveness of the method.
- The paper demonstrates the scalability of the proposed method, showing that it can handle large-scale datasets and achieve significant inference speed-ups.
- The motivation behind the proposed method is clear, and the experiments are comprehensive, including a variety of datasets and baselines.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of graphs and its scalability to larger, more complex datasets.
- There are some unclear notations and definitions in the paper, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational overhead of the proposed method, especially in comparison to other methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the scalability and efficiency of the proposed method.
- There are some minor typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method to larger, more complex datasets?
- How does the proposed method perform on graphs with different homophily levels?
- Could the authors clarify the notations and definitions used in the paper to improve readability?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational overhead and efficiency with other methods, especially in large-scale applications?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over other existing methods, particularly in terms of scalability and efficiency?
