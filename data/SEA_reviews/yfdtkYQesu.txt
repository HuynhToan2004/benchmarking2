 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to learning-rate-free optimization by scaling the parameters of deep neural networks in adaptive gradient methods, such as Adam, to make them equivalent to steepest descent methods. This method, termed PS-DA-SGD, is tested across various tasks including supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and self-supervised learning. The approach is shown to perform comparably to hand-tuned learning rates and demonstrates effectiveness in scenarios where traditional methods struggle. The paper also discusses the limitations of existing learning-rate-free methods and proposes new methods to address these challenges. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of theoretical analysis, the need for more extensive experimental validation, and the clarity of its presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning-rate-free optimization by scaling the parameters in adaptive gradient methods, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method is shown to be effective in various tasks including supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and self-supervised learning, demonstrating its versatility and applicability.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for disseminating complex research findings.
- The method is innovative, providing a new perspective on how adaptive gradient methods can be adapted to be learning-rate-free, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, covering a variety of tasks and datasets, which helps to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough theoretical analysis, which could strengthen the claims made about the equivalence of adaptive gradient methods to steepest descent methods.
- The experimental results, while showing some improvements, are not consistently superior to existing methods, and the paper could benefit from more robust and diverse experimental validations.
- The paper could benefit from a clearer presentation, particularly in the algorithm section, to make the methodology more accessible and understandable.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method to larger datasets, which is a significant limitation given the potential applications of the method in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not provide sufficient discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical feasibility.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis to support the claims made about the equivalence of adaptive gradient methods to steepest descent methods?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets, and what are the computational requirements for scaling the method to such datasets?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might impact its practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing learning-rate-free methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and scalability?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including standard deviations and additional datasets, to better validate the claims made about the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the method handle the scaling of the learning rate in different scenarios, and what are the implications of this scaling on the convergence and stability of the training process?
