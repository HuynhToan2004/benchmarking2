 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection called Diverse Outlier Sampling (DOS), which clusters OOD samples and selects the most informative sample from each cluster for model training. This approach is designed to address the issue of biased OOD sample selection, which can lead to ineffective training. The method is tested across multiple datasets and shows promising results, although concerns about the novelty of the approach and the clarity of experimental results are raised. The paper is praised for its thorough empirical analysis and clear presentation, but criticized for its limited novelty and potential lack of generalizability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and concise descriptions of the proposed method.
- Extensive experiments are conducted, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in OOD detection.
- The method is straightforward and effective, with a simple and intuitive approach that is easy to understand.
- The paper provides a comprehensive ablation study, which helps in understanding the impact of different components of the method.
- The paper is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the motivation behind the proposed method.
- The method is simple to implement and can be easily adopted in practical settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and justification for the term "diversity" used in the title and throughout the paper.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some results showing no significant improvement over existing methods.
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as similar approaches have been previously proposed, such as clustering OOD samples and selecting the most informative one.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not provide sufficient empirical evidence to support the claims made.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential bias in the clustering process, which could affect the performance of the method.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the proposed method, which is a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and justification for the term "diversity" used in the paper?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational cost and efficiency?
- How is the decision made regarding the number of clusters in the clustering process, and what impact does this have on the performance of the method?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, particularly for the large-scale datasets used in the study?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential bias in the clustering process, and what steps are taken to mitigate this bias?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and provide more empirical evidence to support the claims made in the paper?
