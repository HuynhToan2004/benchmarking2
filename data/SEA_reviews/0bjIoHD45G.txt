 **Summary:**
The paper explores the performance gap between deep learning (DL) and tree-based methods on tabular data, focusing on the handling of categorical features. It introduces two techniques: Categorical Feature Detection (CFD) and Learned Fourier Features (LFF), aimed at enhancing the performance of deep learning models on tabular data. The CFD method identifies implicitly categorical features by applying statistical tests, and the LFF method uses Fourier embeddings to model non-linear interactions. The paper benchmarks these methods against XGBoost on a comprehensive tabular data benchmark, showing that the proposed methods can close the performance gap in some cases. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty, inadequate theoretical backing, and unclear presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant issue of closing the performance gap between deep learning and tree-based methods on tabular data, focusing on the handling of categorical features.
- The authors provide a comprehensive experimental evaluation, comparing their proposed methods to XGBoost on a comprehensive tabular data benchmark.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes an ablation study that provides insights into the performance of the proposed methods.
- The authors demonstrate that their methods can significantly boost the performance of deep learning models on tabular data, achieving results that closely match or surpass XGBoost in some cases.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their implementation details.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as the proposed methods, such as CFD and LFF, are not significantly different from existing methods in the field.
- There is a lack of theoretical backing for the proposed methods, which could strengthen the paper's argument.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed methods, which could affect the reliability and generalizability of the results.
- The presentation of the paper is not clear, with some sections being difficult to follow due to complex terminology and lack of clarity in method descriptions.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The paper's focus on specific datasets and tasks may limit the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the proposed methods, particularly CFD and LFF, differ significantly from existing methods in the field?
- How do the authors address the computational complexity of their proposed methods, especially in large-scale applications?
- Can the authors provide more detailed theoretical backing for the proposed methods, particularly in terms of their effectiveness and generalizability?
- How do the authors ensure that the proposed methods can handle different types of categorical features effectively?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed methods with XGBoost, especially in terms of computational efficiency and performance on different types of datasets?
- How do the authors plan to address the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of their applicability to different types of tabular data and tasks?
