 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MissDiff, a novel diffusion-based framework designed to handle missing data in tabular datasets. MissDiff employs a denoising score matching approach to model the score of the complete data distribution, thereby avoiding the biases associated with traditional "impute-then-generate" and "generate-then-impute" pipelines. The framework is theoretically justified and empirically validated across various datasets, showing improvements in both data imputation and generation tasks. However, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental scope, focusing only on tabular data and not exploring more complex data types like images or videos. The methodology is also seen as potentially overly complex and not sufficiently compared against existing state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly presents the motivation, methodology, and results.
- The proposed method, MissDiff, is novel and offers a principled approach to handling missing data, potentially reducing biases associated with traditional "impute-then-generate" and "generate-then-impute" pipelines.
- The paper provides a theoretical justification for MissDiff, demonstrating its effectiveness in recovering the oracle score function of the complete data and upper bounding the negative likelihood of the observed data.
- MissDiff outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods in most settings on both imputation tasks and generation tasks on multiple real tabular datasets under different missing mechanisms.
- The paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and scalability.
- The experiments are limited to tabular data, and it is unclear how MissDiff would perform on more complex data types like images or videos.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential impact of the assumptions made on the performance and scalability.
- The methodology is complex, and the paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples, especially in the experimental sections.
- There are concerns about the method's applicability to more complex data types and its scalability to larger datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including computational complexity and scalability?
- How does MissDiff perform on more complex data types like images or videos?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its performance and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations and examples in the experimental sections to clarify the methodology and results?
- How does the method perform when dealing with different types of missing data, such as missing completely at random (MCAR) versus missing not at random (NMAR)?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated?
