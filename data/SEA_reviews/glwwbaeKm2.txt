 **Summary:**
The paper introduces VertiBench, a novel benchmarking tool designed for Vertical Federated Learning (VFL), focusing on synthetic dataset generation. VertiBench utilizes two key factors, feature importance and feature correlation, to evaluate VFL algorithms across diverse scenarios. The paper presents a theoretical framework and empirical results, demonstrating the effectiveness of VertiBench in capturing a broad spectrum of VFL scenarios. It also proposes new metrics and dataset splitting methods to assess VFL performance, providing valuable insights into the field. However, concerns were raised regarding the limited scope of VFL tasks evaluated, the lack of comparison with existing benchmarks, and the need for more comprehensive evaluations of VFL algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the problem, proposed methodology, and results, making it accessible and understandable.
- It introduces a novel dataset splitting method that can generate synthetic datasets with varied party importance and correlation, capturing a broad scope of VFL scenarios.
- The proposed metrics are innovative, offering a new perspective on evaluating VFL algorithms, and the experiments are comprehensive, covering a wide range of VFL algorithms.
- The theoretical framework presented in the paper is robust, providing a solid foundation for the empirical results.
- The paper addresses an important problem in VFL by proposing a new benchmarking tool, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing benchmarks, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The scope of VFL tasks evaluated is limited, focusing primarily on classification tasks, which might not adequately represent the full spectrum of VFL applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed metrics and dataset splitting methods, which could affect the generalizability and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not include real-world datasets in its experiments, which could have provided more practical insights into the performance of VFL algorithms.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, such as the assumption of feature independence in Shapley value calculations, which might not hold in all VFL scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on how the proposed metrics compare to existing benchmarks, and how this comparison could be integrated into the evaluation process?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to VFL tasks beyond classification, such as regression or time-series forecasting?
- Can the authors provide more details on the assumptions made in the Shapley value calculations, particularly regarding feature independence, and discuss potential scenarios where these assumptions might not hold?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability of VFL algorithms, especially in large-scale deployments?
- Could the authors clarify the role of feature importance and correlation in VFL, and how these factors are balanced in practical scenarios?
- The paper mentions the use of synthetic datasets; how do these datasets compare in terms of realism and applicability to real-world VFL scenarios?
