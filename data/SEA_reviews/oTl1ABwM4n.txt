 **Summary:**
The paper explores the problem of length generalization in transformer models, focusing on the sorting task where the model predicts the sorted sequence from an unsorted one. The authors introduce a multi-task training framework that incorporates auxiliary tasks to enhance the model's performance on longer sequences. The proposed method, termed "task hinting," involves adding a small number of length-dependent parameters to the model to further boost its performance. The paper provides empirical evidence of the effectiveness of this approach through experiments on a synthetic dataset, showing that models trained with auxiliary tasks can generalize better to sequences of length 100, compared to those trained without such tasks. The theoretical analysis supports the findings, suggesting that the proposed method could be applicable to other tasks beyond sorting.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem in the field of machine learning, focusing on the generalization of transformer models to longer sequences, a significant issue in many practical applications.
- The proposed method of using auxiliary tasks to improve generalization is innovative and shows promise in enhancing the performance of transformer models.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear visualizations and explanations that aid in comprehending the proposed methods and their effects.
- The empirical evidence provided is strong, showing that the proposed method can effectively improve generalization on the sorting task, with significant gains in performance.
- The theoretical analysis provided offers a deeper understanding of the observed phenomena and supports the practical findings, providing a solid foundation for further research and application.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on the sorting task, which might limit the generalizability of the findings to other tasks. More experiments on other tasks could help validate the effectiveness of the proposed method across a broader range of applications.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The related work section is brief and could benefit from more detailed discussions on existing methods and their comparisons with the proposed approach.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of the computational complexity and resource requirements of the proposed method, particularly in terms of training and inference times.
- The paper's focus on synthetic data might limit the generalizability of the findings to real-world applications, where data distributions are more complex and varied.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and inference times for the proposed method, particularly how these compare to other methods and in practical scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform on other tasks beyond sorting, and what are the specific tasks where it shows the most significant improvements?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of auxiliary tasks and how they were selected for the experiments? What criteria were used to determine their effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that address the length generalization problem in transformer models, particularly those that use different architectural or training strategies?
- Given the focus on synthetic data, how do the authors plan to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method on real-world datasets, and what steps are being taken to address the challenges posed by such datasets?
