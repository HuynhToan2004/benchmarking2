 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DPO-Diff, a framework for optimizing discrete prompts in text-to-image diffusion models. It formulates prompt optimization as a discrete optimization problem over the text space, employing a family of compact search spaces and a gradient-based algorithm to efficiently search for improved prompts. The framework is designed to address the challenges of vast domain space and high memory-runtime complexity by introducing a "Shortcut Gradient" method. The methodology is tested on various datasets, including DiffusionDB, ChatGPT, and COCO, and the results indicate that DPO-Diff can effectively improve the faithfulness of images generated by text-to-image diffusion models. However, the paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods and does not include a human evaluation, which could limit the understanding of its effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and presents a novel method for prompt optimization in text-to-image diffusion models.
- The introduction of a gradient-based framework for prompt optimization is innovative and addresses a significant challenge in the field.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation on diverse datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The methodology is supported by a thorough ablation study, which helps in understanding the contributions of different components of the model.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, and includes a detailed algorithm in the appendix, which aids in reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the effectiveness of the proposed Shortcut Gradient method.
- The evaluation metrics used, such as CLIP loss, may not fully capture the quality of the generated images, especially in terms of visual fidelity.
- The paper does not include a human evaluation, which is crucial for assessing the practical utility and user-friendliness of the optimized prompts.
- The method's performance is limited by the size and quality of the negative prompt library, which could affect its generalizability and applicability.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method comprehensively, which could hinder the understanding of its scope and applicability.
- The optimization process heavily relies on the quality of the negative prompt library, which may not be readily available or of high quality in all applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the effectiveness of the proposed Shortcut Gradient method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of memory and runtime efficiency with other gradient-based methods for prompt optimization?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the negative prompt library on the method's performance and generalizability?
- How do the authors ensure the quality and diversity of the negative prompts used in the method, and what strategies are in place to handle scenarios where high-quality negative prompts are not available?
- Could the authors provide more details on the human evaluation process, including the criteria used to assess the effectiveness of the optimized prompts and the sample size of the evaluation?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of faithfulness in text-to-image diffusion models, and what are the implications of this approach in terms of the model's ability to generate high-quality, faithful images?
