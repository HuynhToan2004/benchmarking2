 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Fourier Neural ODEs (FNODEs) for modeling complex dynamical systems, particularly focusing on the estimation of temporal and spatial gradients using Fourier analysis. The method leverages Fourier analysis to estimate gradients from noisy observational data, which are then used as inputs to a neural network for training. This approach aims to improve the training speed and robustness of neural ODEs by reducing the need for simulation-based training. The methodology is validated through experiments on various ODE and PDE systems, showing promising results in terms of prediction accuracy and training efficiency. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the experimental setup, the generalization of the method to more complex systems, and the novelty of the approach compared to existing methods like FNO.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method using Fourier analysis to estimate gradients for training Neural ODEs, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method demonstrates strong empirical performance in modeling complex systems, particularly in the context of ODE and PDE systems, as shown in the experimental results.
- The approach is well-explained and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand.
- The methodology is straightforward and simple, which is advantageous for its practical application.
- The paper includes a thorough experimental evaluation, showing the method's superiority in training time, prediction accuracy, and robustness compared to existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from clearer explanations and examples, especially in the experimental setup and the use of Fourier analysis for gradient estimation.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as it appears similar to existing methods like FNO, which also use Fourier analysis for gradient estimation.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to high-dimensional systems and the potential for overfitting due to the use of Fourier analysis.
- There is a need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of training time and performance metrics like MSE and RMSE.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the Fourier transform, which could be a significant limitation for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role of the control function u(t) in the model and its impact on the model's performance?
- How does the method perform when the sampling frequency is not high enough, and what are the implications for the model's accuracy?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training procedure, particularly how the model is trained using the estimated gradients?
- How does the method handle noisy data, and what are the implications for the model's performance in such scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for overfitting due to the use of Fourier analysis for gradient estimation, and how this is addressed in the model?
- Can the authors provide more comparative analysis with other methods like FNO, particularly in terms of training time and performance metrics?
- How does the method perform on more complex systems, and what are the implications for its practical application in real-world scenarios?
