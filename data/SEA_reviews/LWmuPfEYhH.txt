 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ACORM, a novel framework for multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) that utilizes an attention-guided contrastive learning approach to enhance role representation learning. ACORM leverages an attention mechanism to guide the global state towards learned role representations, promoting heterogeneity and knowledge transfer among agents. The framework is evaluated on challenging tasks in StarCraft II and demonstrates state-of-the-art performance. However, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty, insufficient comparisons with existing methods, and the need for more comprehensive evaluations across diverse environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to role representation learning in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) using an attention-guided contrastive learning method, which effectively tackles agent homogenization and facilitates efficient knowledge transfer.
- The method is compatible with existing MARL algorithms such as QMIX and MAPPO, and has been evaluated on challenging StarCraft II micromanagement tasks, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and detailed explanations of the proposed methods, which aid in understanding the complex concepts presented.
- The use of an attention mechanism to guide the global state towards learned role representations is innovative and has been shown to improve coordination and performance in MARL tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it builds upon existing works such as ROMA and RODE, with similarities in the role encoder and attention-guided value decomposition.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of role-based MARL algorithms and other recent methods that address agent heterogeneity.
- The evaluation is limited to the StarCraft II environment, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method across diverse environments.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the method to large-scale MARL tasks, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential challenges.
- The paper does not provide sufficient empirical evidence to support the claims of knowledge transfer and skillful coordination, which are crucial for the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of the proposed method compared to existing works such as ROMA and RODE?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other recent methods that address agent heterogeneity in MARL?
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability of the method to large-scale MARL tasks and discuss any potential challenges or limitations?
- In the context of the StarCraft II environment, how does the method perform when the number of agents or the complexity of the tasks varies?
- How does the method ensure that the attention mechanism effectively guides the global state towards learned role representations, and what are the potential implications if this guidance is not optimal?
- Can the authors provide more empirical evidence to support the claims of knowledge transfer and skillful coordination, possibly through additional experiments or analyses?
