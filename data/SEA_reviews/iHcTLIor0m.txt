 **Summary:**
The paper investigates contrastive learning with multiple views per sample, termed poly-view contrastive learning, and proposes new learning objectives based on information maximization and sufficient statistics. It theoretically analyzes the impact of view multiplicity on mutual information and provides empirical evidence that poly-view contrastive models can outperform SimCLR with fewer epochs and a smaller batch size. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings, suggesting that poly-view contrastive learning could lead to more efficient and effective representation learning. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental scope, lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and insufficient discussion on the practical implications of its findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel concept of poly-view contrastive learning, which extends traditional contrastive learning by allowing for more than two views per sample.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust and well-structured, particularly in Section 2.3, which offers a comprehensive understanding of the proposed method.
- The paper presents clear and well-written theoretical results, with a strong connection between the proposed poly-view contrastive loss and sufficient statistics.
- The experimental results demonstrate that poly-view contrastive learning can achieve better performance with fewer epochs and a smaller batch size compared to SimCLR.
- The paper provides a comprehensive view of contrastive learning, including theoretical analysis, empirical evidence, and practical implications, which could be beneficial for future research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the choice of the objective function, which is crucial for understanding the theoretical contributions.
- The experimental setup is limited, with only a few models tested, which does not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a notable absence of comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, which is crucial for evaluating the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the practical implications of the findings, such as the computational cost and scalability of the proposed method.
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the experimental setup and the presentation of results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and scope.

**Questions:**

- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific models used and the training parameters?
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets, such as CIFAR-100, and what are the implications of these results?
- Can you discuss the computational cost and scalability of the proposed method, especially in relation to other methods like SimCLR?
