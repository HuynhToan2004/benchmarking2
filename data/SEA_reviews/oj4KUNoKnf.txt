 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for knowledge distillation from closed-source language models (LLMs) using a Bayesian estimation framework. The method involves a two-stage process: first, an initial probability distribution over the next token is estimated from the corpus generated by the closed-source LLM, and then this distribution is refined using a proxy model. The student model is trained on the soft labels derived from this refined distribution, aiming to distill knowledge effectively from the closed-source model. Experiments on various benchmarks demonstrate that this approach outperforms direct instruction fine-tuning on hard labels. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in explaining the methodology, particularly in the use of Bayesian estimation and the need for multiple stages of distillation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge distillation from closed-source language models, utilizing a Bayesian estimation framework and a two-stage process that leverages a proxy model to refine initial estimates.
- The methodology is supported by comprehensive experiments on various benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed ablation study that helps in understanding the contributions of different components of the method.
- The introduction of a Bayesian estimation framework for knowledge distillation is a significant contribution, as it allows for the estimation of output distributions from closed-source models, which is a challenging problem in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more clarity in explaining the methodology, particularly in how Bayesian estimation is used and the rationale behind the two-stage distillation process.
- The experimental results, while showing improvements over direct instruction fine-tuning, do not convincingly demonstrate superiority over other knowledge distillation methods that do not rely on closed-source models.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of closed-source models and its scalability to larger models.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the work, which could be significant given the potential applications of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how Bayesian estimation is applied in the context of knowledge distillation, specifically in the estimation of the output distribution of closed-source models?
- Why is a two-stage distillation process necessary, and how does it compare to other methods that use a single stage of distillation?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other closed-source models, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in applying it to different types of models?
- Given the importance of the proxy model in the method, how does the choice of the proxy model affect the performance of the distilled model?
- Can you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the work and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
