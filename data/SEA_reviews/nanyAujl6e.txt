 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection using CLIP models, specifically focusing on the use of negative prompts to enhance the model's ability to discern OOD samples. The approach involves learning both positive and negative prompts for each class, which are then used to measure similarity and dissimilarity in feature space. The methodology is supported by extensive experiments on various datasets, demonstrating improvements over existing methods. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to previous works, and the experimental results show only marginal improvements over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive review of related work.
- The proposed method is straightforward and effective, showing promising results on various datasets.
- The use of negative prompts for OOD detection is innovative and provides a new perspective on improving the performance of CLIP models.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, with improvements over existing methods like CLIPN and ZOC.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in OOD detection, which is crucial for the robustness and reliability of AI systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of why the proposed method, which uses negative prompts, is more effective than previous methods that use positive prompts.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as similar methods using negative prompts for OOD detection have been previously explored.
- The experimental results show only marginal improvements over existing methods, which might not justify the additional complexity introduced by the use of negative prompts.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost associated with the use of negative prompts, which could be a significant limitation for practical applications.
- The method's reliance on the CLIP model, which is known to have limitations in handling OOD samples, might limit its general applicability.
- The paper's experimental setup and results presentation could be improved to better support the claims made about the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify why the proposed method using negative prompts is more effective than previous methods using positive prompts?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational cost and practical applicability to existing methods?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including standard deviations and statistical significance tests, to support the claims made about the method's effectiveness?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of OOD samples, such as those from completely unseen classes or from the same class but with different attributes?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
