 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel application of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) for predicting the maximum wind speed (VMAX) in tropical cyclones. By representing cyclone data as a graph, the model can capture both spatial and temporal dependencies, which are critical for understanding cyclone behavior. The paper evaluates the performance of the proposed model against a benchmark dataset and demonstrates that it outperforms conventional physical models. However, the evaluation is limited to the North Indian Ocean, and the model's performance in other regions is not explored. Additionally, the paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the model and does not include comparisons with other deep learning models or traditional physical models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The application of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to predict tropical cyclone maximum wind speed is innovative and provides a new approach to understanding and predicting cyclone behavior.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the experimental setup and results.
- The use of GNNs to model the temporal evolution of cyclones and capture spatial dependencies is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper demonstrates the potential of machine learning methods in improving our comprehension of intricate meteorological processes, offering new avenues for research in atmospheric science.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation is limited to the North Indian Ocean, which may not generalize well to other regions. Expanding the evaluation to other ocean basins could provide a more robust assessment of the model's performance.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the model, including its assumptions, constraints, and potential societal impacts.
- There is a lack of comparison with other deep learning models and traditional physical models, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the model's performance.
- The paper does not discuss the model's scalability, which is crucial for practical applications in operational forecasting systems.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the choice of hyperparameters, which could significantly affect the model's performance.
- The paper lacks a thorough literature review, which could provide a better context for the research and highlight the novelty of the contributions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the North Indian Ocean dataset and discuss the potential for extending the model to other ocean basins?
- How does the model perform in predicting other cyclone attributes, such as precipitation or storm track?
- Can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter selection and its impact on the model's performance?
- How does the model compare to other deep learning models and traditional physical models in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of the model, particularly in terms of disaster risk reduction and climate change adaptation?
- How does the model handle missing data, which is common in real-world datasets?
- Can the authors provide more details on the model's scalability and its potential for operational forecasting applications?
