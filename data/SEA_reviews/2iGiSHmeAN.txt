 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework, BroGNet, which integrates stochastic differential equations (SDEs) with graph neural networks (GNNs) to learn Brownian dynamics directly from trajectory data. This approach enables the model to conserve momentum, enhancing its performance and data efficiency. The framework is validated through experiments on several benchmarked Brownian systems, demonstrating its superiority over existing methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental validation, lack of comparison with more complex physical systems, and potential issues with the Euler-Maruyama method used for integrating the SDE.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by integrating stochastic differential equations (SDEs) with graph neural networks (GNNs) to learn Brownian dynamics directly from trajectory data, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The model is designed to conserve momentum, which is a crucial feature for accurately modeling Brownian dynamics, and the authors have demonstrated that this feature enhances the model's performance.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and easy to understand, and includes a detailed explanation of the proposed methodology, which aids in reproducibility.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms existing baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper addresses the challenge of learning Brownian dynamics directly from trajectory data, which is a significant step forward in understanding and predicting the behavior of complex physical systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with more complex physical systems, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential shortcomings.
- The experimental validation is limited to simple systems, and the paper does not explore how the method performs on more complex or realistic systems, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The paper's reliance on the Euler-Maruyama method for integrating the SDE raises concerns about the accuracy of the model's predictions, especially for longer time horizons.
- The paper's claims about the model's ability to conserve momentum and its impact on performance are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the proposed method compared to other existing methods, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform on more complex physical systems, such as those involving more particles or different types of interactions?
- Can the authors provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including its applicability, assumptions, and potential shortcomings?
- How does the model handle the integration of the SDE using the Euler-Maruyama method, and what are the implications for the accuracy of the predictions?
- Can the authors provide empirical evidence to support the claim that the model's ability to conserve momentum directly leads to better performance?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational cost to other existing methods, and what are the implications for practical applications?
- Can the authors provide more detailed information on the training process, including the number of epochs and the learning rate, to help replicate the results?
