 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the Best Arm Identification (BAI) problem in the context of stochastic rising bandits (SRBs), where the expected reward of each arm increases over time. Two algorithms are proposed: R-UCBE, which utilizes an optimistic estimator, and R-SR, which employs a successive reject procedure. Both algorithms are designed to identify the arm with the highest expected reward within a fixed budget, and theoretical analyses are provided to derive error probability bounds. The paper also discusses the necessity of a sufficiently large budget and presents numerical simulations to validate the algorithms' performance. Despite its theoretical contributions, the paper faces criticism for its lack of novelty, as it appears to be a combination of existing methods without significant advancement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the algorithms and theoretical results, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed algorithms are simple and easy to follow, with a focus on the rising bandit setting which is not commonly explored in previous works.
- The paper provides theoretical analysis for the algorithms, including error probability bounds and lower bounds, which are supported by numerical simulations.
- The problem setting is interesting and relevant, particularly in scenarios where the reward of each arm increases over time, which is not typically addressed in standard multi-armed bandit (MAB) settings.
- The paper is structured well, with clear explanations of the assumptions and results, and the proofs are provided in the appendix.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as it appears to be a combination of existing methods without significant advancement.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples of the rising bandit setting, especially in the introduction and early sections.
- The theoretical results are based on assumptions that are not realistic or practical, such as the knowledge of the time budget T, which is not typically known in real-world applications.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithms, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions may not hold, such as when the reward function is not concave or when the time budget is unknown.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing works, especially those that also address the rising bandit setting, to highlight the contributions and advantages of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of their work compared to existing methods, particularly in terms of the algorithmic contributions?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the rising bandit setting, and what specific advantages does it offer over standard MAB settings?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or scenarios where the rising bandit setting is applicable?
- Is there a plan to relax the assumption of known time budget T in future work, and how might this affect the theoretical results?
- How do the proposed algorithms perform in scenarios where the reward function is not concave or when the time budget is unknown?
- Can the authors discuss the computational complexity of their algorithms, particularly in comparison to existing methods?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with related works, especially those that also address the rising bandit setting, to highlight the contributions and advantages of their methods?
