 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Prompt-OIRL, a method leveraging offline inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) to optimize prompt selection for large language models (LLMs) in arithmetic reasoning tasks. It addresses the challenges of inference-time evaluation and online prompt evaluation by utilizing a reward model trained on offline data to evaluate prompt effectiveness without direct LLM interaction. This approach aims to improve prompt selection efficiency and reduce computational costs. The method is validated across various LLMs and arithmetic reasoning datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency. However, concerns about the generalizability of the method beyond arithmetic reasoning and its reliance on specific LLMs and datasets are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-structured presentation of the proposed method.
- The introduction of the concept of query-dependent prompt optimization is novel and provides a new perspective on prompting in large language models (LLMs).
- The use of offline inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) to learn a reward model from offline data is innovative and effective in evaluating prompts without direct LLM interaction.
- The method is shown to be efficient in terms of time and cost, making it a promising approach for practical applications.
- The paper is empirically strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method through extensive experiments across various LLMs and datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's applicability is limited to arithmetic reasoning tasks, and its generalization to other types of reasoning or broader LLM applications is not explored.
- The method's reliance on specific LLMs and datasets, such as TigerBot-13B-Chat and GPT-3.5-turbo, may limit its broader applicability and scalability.
- The use of a reward model trained on offline data raises concerns about its ability to generalize to unseen data, especially in real-world scenarios where new prompts or queries may arise.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to other types of reasoning tasks and its scalability to larger datasets.
- The method's effectiveness is not compared against other existing methods, such as those using differentiable prompt embeddings or LLM-based self-critic methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the scalability of the method and its potential impact on the community, particularly in terms of its generalizability and broader applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the method be adapted to other types of reasoning tasks beyond arithmetic reasoning? If so, how would the method need to be modified to accommodate these tasks?
- How does the method perform when using different LLMs, such as GPT-4 or Llama-2, which may have different prompting preferences?
- What are the specific prompts used in the experiments, and how do they influence the results?
- How does the method handle the generalization of the reward model to unseen data?
- Could you provide more details on the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of its ability to handle larger datasets or more complex reasoning tasks?
- How does the method compare against other existing methods in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Can you discuss the potential impact of the method on the broader community, particularly in terms of its generalizability and broader applicability?
