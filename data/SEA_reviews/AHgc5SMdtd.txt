 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel zero-shot anomaly classification and segmentation method for industrial vision, named MuSC, which utilizes unlabeled test images to determine anomalies. The method leverages the abundant normal and abnormal cues in these images to assign anomaly scores to each other, employing a Mutual Scoring Mechanism (MSM) and a Re-scoring with Constrained Image-level Neighborhood (RsCIN) to optimize image-level anomaly classification. MuSC has been tested on the MVTec AD and VisA datasets, showing significant improvements over existing methods. The method's key innovation is its ability to operate without training or prompts, making it suitable for scenarios where labeled data is scarce.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel zero-shot anomaly classification and segmentation method, MuSC, which utilizes unlabeled test images to determine anomalies, a concept that is both innovative and practical.
- The methodology is clearly presented, making it easy to follow and understand, which is crucial for reproducibility and adoption by the community.
- The experimental results are robust, showing significant improvements over existing methods, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, which enhances its readability and accessibility.
- The potential applications of the MuSC method are significant, particularly in scenarios where labeled data is scarce, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as similar approaches using unlabeled data for anomaly detection have been previously explored.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of its relative performance.
- The computational cost and inference speed of the MuSC method are not discussed, which are important factors for practical deployment.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed ablation studies and comparisons to better understand the contributions of different components of the MuSC method.
- There are some presentation issues, such as inconsistencies in terminology and formatting, which could detract from the paper's professionalism and clarity.
