 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to embodied AI by integrating large language models (LLMs) with reinforcement learning (RL) to create a vision-language policy for embodied tasks. The method, termed LLaRP, utilizes a pre-trained LLM to encode textual instructions and visual observations, generating actions based on these inputs. The approach is evaluated on a new benchmark, Language Rearrangement, which includes 150,000 training and 1,000 testing tasks, demonstrating the model's ability to generalize to unseen tasks and outperform other baselines. The paper also discusses the potential of LLMs in embodied tasks and provides a new benchmark to facilitate further research in this area.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The integration of LLMs with RL in an embodied AI setting is novel and provides a new perspective on using LLMs for embodied tasks.
- The introduction of a new benchmark, Language Rearrangement, with 150,000 training and 1,000 testing tasks, is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of LLMs in embodied tasks, showing that LLMs can be used to encode textual instructions and visual observations, which is a promising direction for future research.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed method, LLaRP, outperforms other baselines on various tasks, including Atari games.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as similar methods have been explored in previous works, such as those mentioned in references [1] and [2].
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process of the LLMs, particularly how they are adapted to the specific tasks, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The experimental setup and the choice of baselines are not sufficiently justified, raising questions about the fairness and robustness of the comparisons.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for training and the potential impact on the performance of the LLMs.
- The paper's claims about the efficiency and effectiveness of the LLM-based approach are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which could be more convincing with a broader range of tasks and more detailed comparisons.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the LLMs are adapted to the specific tasks and what specific training procedures were used?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational resources and scalability?
- Can you clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of baselines and the conditions under which the LLM-based approach was tested?
- How does the performance of the LLMs change when they are trained on specific tasks, and what are the implications of this for the generalizability of the results?
- Could you discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its applicability in real-world scenarios?
- Can you provide more details on the training process for the LLMs, including the training data and the training algorithms used?
