 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to auxiliary learning by employing an asymmetric network architecture that enables the removal of auxiliary connections during inference, thereby maintaining single-task inference costs. The proposed method leverages gradients and features from auxiliary tasks during training, with a focus on neural architecture search (NAS) for efficient integration. The paper presents two methods: Aux-G and Aux-FG-NAS, which differ in the use of auxiliary features and gradients. The experimental results, conducted on various datasets and backbones, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods in enhancing primary task performance while maintaining inference efficiency. However, concerns about the novelty of the approach and the complexity of the NAS search process are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach to auxiliary learning, focusing on the use of asymmetric network architectures that facilitate the removal of auxiliary connections during inference, thereby maintaining single-task inference costs.
- The proposed methods are general-applicable and can be integrated with existing optimization-based auxiliary learning approaches, demonstrating strong performance across various datasets and backbones.
- The paper provides a comprehensive taxonomy of the proposed methods within the areas of multi-task learning and auxiliary learning, clearly illustrating the differences and advantages of the proposed methods.
- The experimental results show that the proposed methods outperform existing baselines on multiple datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed ablation study that supports the claims made.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the novelty of the proposed methods, particularly in relation to existing works in the field of auxiliary learning.
- The complexity of the NAS search process, which includes bi-directional connections at every layer between the primary and auxiliary tasks, is not adequately addressed in terms of computational efficiency and training time.
- There is a lack of comparative analysis with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and inference time, which is crucial for evaluating the practical applicability of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed methods to more complex or diverse datasets, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of the proposed methods compared to existing works in the field of auxiliary learning, particularly in terms of novelty and performance improvements?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and inference time with other state-of-the-art methods, especially when considering the complexity of the NAS search process?
- Is there a plan to release the code for the proposed methods to facilitate reproducibility and further research by the community?
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed methods to more complex or diverse datasets, and discuss any potential challenges or limitations in applying the methods to such datasets?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of negative transfer, particularly in scenarios where the auxiliary tasks are not directly related to the primary task?
