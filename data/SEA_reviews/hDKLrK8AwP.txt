 **Summary:**
The paper explores the generation of readable Scalable Vector Graphics (SVG) by introducing a novel loss function aimed at enhancing the structural consistency, appropriate use of elements, and redundancy reduction in SVGs. The authors propose a set of metrics to evaluate the readability of SVGs and demonstrate their effectiveness through various experiments, including a study using GPT-3.5 and an ablation study. The paper also discusses the limitations of the proposed metrics and the challenges in measuring readability directly, instead focusing on proxies. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation, lack of real-world applications, and potential over-simplification of SVGs which could affect their visual fidelity.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and under-explored problem of generating readable SVGs, which is crucial for applications in text-to-vector models.
- The authors have introduced a novel loss function that encourages the generation of simpler and more readable SVGs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed metrics for evaluating SVG readability are innovative and could be useful for future research in this area.
- The experiments, including the GPT-3.5 study and the ablation study, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in generating more readable SVGs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed metrics, particularly in terms of their correlation with human perception of readability and their applicability to real-world scenarios.
- The readability-oriented loss functions and their impact on the visual quality of the SVGs are not thoroughly discussed, raising concerns about potential loss of visual fidelity.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of its advantages and limitations.
- The paper's focus on simplifying SVGs to improve readability might lead to over-simplification, which could affect the visual accuracy and complexity of the generated SVGs.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the trade-offs between visual quality and readability, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How do the proposed metrics correlate with human perception of readability, and can they be validated through user studies?
- Can the proposed method be applied to real-world scenarios, such as generating SVGs for logos or other complex vector graphics?
- How does the paper address the trade-off between visual quality and readability, and what are the practical implications of this trade-off in different applications?
- Could the paper benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation, including a broader set of metrics and more comparisons with existing methods?
- How does the paper ensure that the simplification of SVGs does not lead to significant loss of visual information or complexity?

**Paper Decision:**
- Decision: Reject
- Reasons: The paper, while innovative in its approach to SVG readability, suffers from significant limitations in its evaluation and practical applicability. The metrics proposed for evaluating readability are seen as proxies rather than direct measures, and the evaluation is limited to synthetic datasets. The paper also lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods and a detailed analysis of the trade-offs between visual quality and readability. These issues, combined with concerns about the practical applicability and potential over-simplification of SVGs, lead to the decision to reject the paper. The reviewers encourage the authors to address these issues and consider resubmitting the work to a future venue.
