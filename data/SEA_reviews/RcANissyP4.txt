 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SelfEval, a method that leverages diffusion models to evaluate the text-image understanding capabilities of text-to-image diffusion models. By estimating the likelihood of real images given text prompts, the method can assess various aspects of model performance, including attribute binding, color recognition, counting, shape recognition, spatial understanding, and text corruption. The authors demonstrate that this approach can align well with human evaluations and outperform traditional methods like CLIP scores. The methodology is applicable across different model architectures and datasets, showing potential for broader application in evaluating generative models. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach and the need for a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to evaluating text-image understanding capabilities of diffusion models.
- The proposed method, SelfEval, is innovative and effective, demonstrating strong correlations with human evaluations, which is a significant advancement in automated evaluation of text-to-image diffusion models.
- The methodology is simple yet robust, making it a valuable tool for evaluating text-to-image diffusion models, particularly in terms of text-image alignment.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across various datasets, tasks, and model architectures, showcasing the method's versatility and applicability.
- The experimental results are convincing, with clear visualizations and explanations that support the claims made.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as similar approaches have been explored in previous works, particularly those using ELBO as a proxy for likelihood.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential biases introduced by the method, which could affect the reliability and generalizability of the results.
- There is a need for more extensive comparisons with existing methods, especially those that use similar approaches, to establish the superiority or uniqueness of the proposed method.
- The methodology section is somewhat unclear, particularly in the explanation of how the method is applied to different datasets and tasks, which could lead to confusion about its applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from more diverse examples and a broader range of tasks to better demonstrate the method's capabilities and limitations.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational costs and scalability of the method, which could be critical for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Could you provide more details on how the method is applied to different datasets and tasks, particularly in terms of data preprocessing and task-specific adaptations?
- How does the method perform on more diverse and complex tasks, such as those involving more abstract or nuanced visual-textual relationships?
- Can you elaborate on the method's limitations and potential biases, and discuss how these might affect the reliability and generalizability of the results?
- How does the method handle variations in the quality and diversity of the datasets used for evaluation?
- Could you provide more details on the human evaluation process, including the number of raters, the criteria for selection, and the inter-rater reliability?
- How does the method perform on different types of generative models, such as those based on GANs or VAEs, and could it be adapted to evaluate these models effectively?
