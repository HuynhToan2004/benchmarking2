 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel text-guided target speaker extraction (TSE) model, named LLM-TSE, which utilizes large language models (LLMs) to extract semantic cues from user-provided text inputs. These cues serve as additional extraction cues, task selectors, or complements to traditional pre-registered cues, enhancing the feasibility, controllability, and performance of existing TSE models. The proposed model integrates text-based cues with pre-registered cues, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance in some scenarios. However, the paper's experimental setup and methodology have been critiqued for their simplicity and lack of real-world applicability. Concerns about the model's generalization to real-world conditions and the need for more comprehensive evaluations have been raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to target speaker extraction (TSE) by integrating natural language descriptions as additional cues, which can serve as independent extraction cues, task selectors, or complements to pre-registered cues.
- The proposed model demonstrates significant improvements in performance over existing models, as shown in Table 1.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the model's architecture and training procedures.
- The model's flexibility allows for the integration of context-dependent information from typed descriptions with pre-registered cues, which can significantly enhance the effectiveness of TSE in practical scenarios.
- The system's control mechanism through natural language descriptions facilitates the speaker extraction process, enabling selective retention or removal of sources based on semantic concepts expressed in the text.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed model, particularly in terms of its generalization to real-world conditions and its performance under varying conditions such as different room acoustics and speaker distances.
- The experimental setup is limited, focusing primarily on simulated data and not adequately testing the model's performance in more realistic settings like noisy environments or with multiple speakers.
- There is a noticeable absence of comparisons with other recent works in the field, which could have provided a clearer picture of the model's relative performance and contributions.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and inference time of the model, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The methodology and the choice of parameters, such as the number of speakers in the mixture and the distance of the sound source from the microphone, are not sufficiently justified or explained.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the model generalizes to real-world conditions, especially considering the simplicity of the experimental setup?
- How does the model perform under varying conditions such as different room acoustics and speaker distances?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and inference time of the model, and how these times scale with the number of speakers in the mixture?
- How does the model handle scenarios where the target speaker is not present in the mixture?
- Could the authors elaborate on the methodology and the choice of parameters used in the model, particularly the number of speakers in the mixture and the distance of the sound source from the microphone?
- How does the model handle different types of text prompts, such as those describing the target speaker's language, gender, or other attributes?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other recent works in the field, particularly those that also utilize LLMs for TSE?
