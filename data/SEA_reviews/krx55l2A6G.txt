 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel attack framework named SEER, designed to bypass client-side detection in federated learning (FL) by stealing data from gradients. SEER uses a secret decoder jointly trained with the shared model to disaggregate data in a hidden space, making it harder to detect. The authors demonstrate that existing malicious server attacks are detectable, proposing a new gradient space metric (D-SNR) to measure data vulnerability. The paper also discusses the limitations of existing attacks and presents SEER as a more effective and harder-to-detect attack method. Extensive experiments are conducted to validate the effectiveness of SEER across various datasets and network architectures, showing its ability to recover private client data from large batches.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the limitations of existing malicious server attacks, which is crucial for understanding the current state of the field.
- It introduces a new gradient space metric (D-SNR) to measure data vulnerability, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed SEER attack is novel, effective, and harder to detect compared to existing attacks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are extensive, covering various datasets and network architectures.
- The authors have made the code open-source, which will be a valuable resource for future research.
- The paper is the first to study client-side detectability of malicious server attacks in federated learning, a significant gap in the current research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not discuss the potential limitations of the SEER attack, which could be a significant drawback.
- The evaluation of the attack's effectiveness is limited to one dataset (CIFAR10), which might not generalize well to other datasets.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of batch size mismatch on the attack's effectiveness.
- There is a lack of discussion on the scalability of the attack to other datasets and architectures, which could limit its applicability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational cost of the attack, which is a critical factor in practical deployments.
- The paper does not compare the proposed attack with other state-of-the-art attacks, which could help in understanding its relative effectiveness.
- The paper does not discuss the potential impact of the attack on fairness in federated learning, which is a significant concern.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the SEER attack and how these could be addressed?
- How does the SEER attack perform on other datasets beyond CIFAR10, and what are the expected outcomes?
- Can the authors discuss the impact of batch size mismatch on the attack's effectiveness and provide insights into how this could be mitigated?
- How does the computational cost of the SEER attack compare to other attacks, and what are the implications for practical deployments?
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability of the attack to other architectures and datasets, and what are the expected outcomes?
- How does the proposed attack compare with other state-of-the-art attacks in terms of effectiveness and computational cost?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of the attack on fairness in federated learning and propose mitigation strategies?
