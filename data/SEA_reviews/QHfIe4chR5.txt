 **Summary:**
The paper investigates targeted poisoning attacks in graph neural networks (GNNs) where the attacker injects nodes outside the target node's k-hop neighborhood to manipulate the graph structure. It proposes two methods: a meta-learning based approach (MetaLDT) and a heuristic-based approach (MimicLDT). The former is computationally expensive but effective, while the latter is faster but less accurate. The paper evaluates these methods against various GNN models and defenses, showing that long-distance attacks can be effective and challenging to detect. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the attack scenarios, the clarity of the paper's presentation, and the strength of the evaluation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel type of attack, long-distance poisoning, which is a significant contribution to the field of GNN security.
- The proposed optimization-based methods, MetaLDT and MimicLDT, are effective and can be used to manipulate GNNs in a targeted manner, which is a practical application in real-world scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments are thorough, comparing the proposed methods with various GNN models and defenses, providing a comprehensive evaluation of the attack's effectiveness.
- The authors demonstrate the feasibility of long-distance poisoning attacks, showing that these attacks can be successful even when the attacker is not directly connected to the target node.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claim that long-distance attacks are more difficult to detect than short-distance attacks is not convincingly supported by the evidence provided.
- The practicality of the attack scenarios is questionable, as it is unclear why an attacker would choose to inject nodes far from the target node rather than directly modifying the target node or its neighbors.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and readability. The use of subscripts and the organization of the paper could be better optimized for understanding.
- The evaluation of the attacks is limited to small datasets and does not sufficiently address the scalability of the attacks on larger, more complex graphs.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of their applicability to other types of GNNs or in different settings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the societal implications of the research, which could be a significant concern for the deployment of these attacks in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify why an attacker would choose to inject nodes far from the target node rather than directly modifying the target node or its neighbors?
- How do the proposed methods perform on larger, more complex graphs, and what are the implications for their scalability and effectiveness?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their proposed methods and how they might be adapted or improved to address these limitations?
- In light of the concerns about the practicality of the attack scenarios, how do the authors justify the choice of long-distance attacks over more direct methods?
- How does the paper address the issue of scalability in the evaluation, particularly in terms of the size and complexity of the graphs used?
- Can the authors provide more details on the design and evaluation of the end-to-end attack, including the specifics of the decoder used and the results of the attack on different GNN models and defenses?
