 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel neural network architecture, the Granger Causality Mixer (GC-Mixer), designed to infer Granger causality in multivariate time series. The architecture utilizes a time-sliding window, Mixer Blocks, and a Causality Inference Block to predict future time series values based on past observations. The model employs hierarchical group lasso penalties to automatically select optimal time lags and extends to time-varying Granger causality inference through a multi-level fine-tuning algorithm. The paper presents experimental results demonstrating the model's effectiveness, particularly in scenarios with increased sparsity and nonlinearity. However, concerns are raised about the model's novelty, the clarity of its contributions, and the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive experimental section that includes extensive ablation studies.
- The proposed method shows good performance, and the authors have conducted thorough experiments to demonstrate its effectiveness.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with the potential to be applied to various fields.
- The paper addresses the important problem of time-varying Granger causality, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it appears to be a simple combination of existing techniques such as the Mixer block and the use of hierarchical group lasso for automatic time lag selection.
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the contributions of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its advantages over existing methods like cMLP and cLSTM.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over other models, especially in scenarios where the model's performance is not optimal.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential avenues for future research.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods, which is a significant oversight given the importance of the problem addressed.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their method and explain how it differs from existing approaches like cMLP and cLSTM?
- Why was the choice made to not compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods, and how does this affect the perceived validity of the results?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with different levels of sparsity and nonlinearity, and what are the implications of these differences on the model's effectiveness?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the contributions of the proposed method and how it addresses the specific challenges of time-varying Granger causality inference?
