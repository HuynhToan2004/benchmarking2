 **Summary:**
The paper introduces QUIC-FL, a novel Distributed Mean Estimation (DME) technique aimed at improving communication efficiency in federated learning by optimizing encoding and decoding times. It proposes a method that allows clients to send compressed gradients using unbiased quantization, which is optimized for the specific distribution of gradients. The method involves a combination of random rotations and quantization to minimize quantization error and reduce computational overhead. The paper provides theoretical and empirical evidence of QUIC-FL's effectiveness, demonstrating its ability to achieve optimal NMSE with reduced computational and communication costs compared to existing methods. The method's practicality is validated through extensive experiments on various datasets and federated learning tasks, showing that it outperforms state-of-the-art techniques in terms of both accuracy and efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in federated learning by proposing a novel method for distributed mean estimation (DME) that optimizes encoding and decoding times while maintaining optimal NMSE.
- The method is well-motivated, with clear and concise writing that effectively communicates the proposed techniques and their implementation.
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis and empirical validation, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The optimization problem presented in the paper is novel and could be of independent interest, contributing to the ongoing research in this field.
- The methodology is clearly presented, with a detailed algorithmic description and experimental setup that supports the claims made.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure that facilitates understanding of the complex concepts and methodologies involved.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claims of being the first to achieve optimal NMSE with O(d log d) encode and O(n d + d log d) decode times are not entirely accurate, as similar methods have been previously proposed, such as those using the Kashin representation.
- The paper's experimental setup is limited in scope, focusing primarily on synthetic data and a simple federated learning task, which may not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness in more complex or realistic scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach, particularly concerning the scalability and generalizability of the method to different types of data and federated learning settings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the societal impact of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in federated learning applications.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and memory requirements of the proposed method, which could be crucial for practical implementation.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the privacy implications of the method, which is a critical aspect of federated learning.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific advantages of their method over existing techniques, such as those using the Kashin representation, in terms of computational efficiency and NMSE performance?
- How does the method perform in more complex or realistic federated learning scenarios, such as those involving non-IID data or large-scale deployments?
- What are the computational and memory requirements of the proposed method, and how do these compare to existing methods in terms of practical implementation?
- How does the method handle privacy concerns, especially in federated learning settings where data is distributed across multiple clients?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the scalability and generalizability of their method across different types of data and federated learning scenarios?
- How does the method perform in terms of convergence speed and accuracy compared to other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in practical federated learning applications?
