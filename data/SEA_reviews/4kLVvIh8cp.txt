 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel algorithm, Pessimistic Nonlinear Least-Square Value Iteration (PNLSVI), for offline reinforcement learning (RL) with non-linear function approximation. The algorithm is designed to achieve instance-dependent regret bounds, which are dependent on the complexity of the function class and the size of the dataset. It builds upon the pessimistic value iteration approach, integrating a variance-based weighted regression scheme and a subroutine for variance estimation. The theoretical analysis provided includes a regret bound that is dependent on the complexity of the function class and achieves minimax optimal instance-dependent regret when specialized to linear function approximation. However, the paper's practical applicability is questioned due to its reliance on a strong uniform data coverage assumption, which might not hold in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel algorithm, PNLSVI, which is designed for offline RL with non-linear function approximation, marking a significant step towards extending the applicability of pessimism-based algorithms to non-linear function approximation.
- The algorithm is shown to achieve near-optimal regret bounds for non-linear function approximation, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm, including a regret bound that depends on the complexity of the function class and achieves minimax optimal instance-dependent regret when specialized to linear function approximation.
- The authors extend the previous instance-dependent results within simpler function classes to a more general framework, which is a notable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper heavily relies on a strong uniform data coverage assumption, which might not hold in real-world scenarios, potentially limiting the practical applicability of the proposed algorithm.
- The algorithm's complexity and the practicality of its implementation, especially the computational cost associated with the variance-based weighted regression scheme and the subroutine for variance estimation, are not thoroughly discussed.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the algorithm performs in scenarios where the uniform data coverage assumption does not hold, which could be crucial for understanding its robustness.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved by including more detailed discussions on the practical implications of the algorithm and providing a more comprehensive comparison with existing works.

**Questions:**
- How does the algorithm perform in scenarios where the uniform data coverage assumption does not hold?
- Can you provide a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the algorithm, particularly in terms of its computational cost and the assumptions it makes?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare with existing works in terms of computational efficiency and the strength of assumptions made?
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of the function class in the context of the algorithm, especially in relation to the uniform data coverage assumption?
- Is there a possibility to weaken the uniform data coverage assumption to a weaker coverage assumption, and how might this affect the performance and applicability of the algorithm?
