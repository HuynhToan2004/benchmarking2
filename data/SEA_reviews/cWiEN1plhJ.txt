 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to detecting text generated by large language models (LLMs) by leveraging style representations trained on human-authored text. This methodology, which does not require access to the LLMs at training time, aims to distinguish between machine-generated and human-authored texts, even in few-shot scenarios. The approach involves using a variety of style representations, including those from the UAR model, to assess the similarity between texts and identify the LLM that generated them. The methodology is evaluated across several datasets and shows promising results, although concerns about the generalizability and scalability of the approach are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem of detecting LLM-generated text, which is crucial for maintaining the integrity of textual data in various applications.
- The approach proposed is novel, leveraging style representations trained on human-authored text, which is a promising direction for this task.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering various settings and datasets, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of style representations to distinguish between machine-generated and human-authored texts is a significant contribution to the field, particularly in the context of LLM-generated text.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the specifics of the style representations used, particularly how they are extracted and how they differ from other models like SBERT.
- The methodology's generalizability and scalability are concerns, as the effectiveness of the approach might be limited to specific domains or types of texts.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the approach to different languages or the ability to distinguish between different versions of the same LLM.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential limitations and ethical implications of the proposed method, which could be crucial for understanding its real-world applicability and impact.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing watermarking techniques, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its advantages and disadvantages.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specifics of the style representations used, particularly how they are extracted and how they differ from other models like SBERT?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different languages or in multilingual settings?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training data used for the style representations, including its diversity and how it affects the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method handle different versions of the same LLM, and what are the implications of this for the model's ability to distinguish between different instances of the same model?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations and ethical implications of their approach, and how these might impact its real-world applicability?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing watermarking techniques, and what are the advantages and disadvantages of each approach in practical scenarios?
