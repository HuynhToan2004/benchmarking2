 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the generalization of modular neural networks (MNNs) compared to non-modular networks, focusing on the sample complexity in high-dimensional, modular tasks. It proposes a novel learning rule to optimize MNNs and empirically demonstrates their improved generalization. Theoretically, the paper shows that MNNs can generalize effectively even with limited training data, which is a significant advantage over non-modular networks. The paper also introduces a new theoretical framework for understanding the scaling laws of modular networks and provides empirical evidence to support these claims. However, the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis are quite strong, and the paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of these assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel theoretical framework for understanding the scaling laws of modular networks, which is the first of its kind.
- It empirically demonstrates that modular neural networks (MNNs) can generalize effectively with limited training data, which is a significant advantage over non-modular networks.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, even for readers not deeply familiar with the field.
- The proposed learning rule for modular networks is novel and has been empirically validated to improve generalization, both in and out of distribution.
- The paper is the first to provide explicit, non-asymptotic expressions for generalization error in modular architectures, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper makes strong assumptions in its theoretical analysis, such as the linearity of the model and the feature matrix, which may not hold in practical scenarios.
- The empirical validation of the theoretical model shows some discrepancies, particularly in the interpolation threshold and the test loss for low training data.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly demonstrated to show that the observed improvements are due to the modular structure of the network and not other factors.
- The paper does not adequately compare its results with existing scaling laws for monolithic networks, which could help in understanding the practical implications of the theoretical findings.

**Questions:**
- How does the paper's theoretical analysis compare with existing scaling laws for monolithic networks, particularly in terms of the practical implications of the theoretical findings?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations for the discrepancies observed in the empirical validation of the theoretical model, particularly in the interpolation threshold and the test loss for low training data?
- How do the authors justify the strong assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, and what are the potential implications if these assumptions do not hold in practical scenarios?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results to better demonstrate that the observed improvements are due to the modular structure of the network and not other factors?
- Is there a possibility to extend the theoretical analysis to more general modular architectures, such as those that use non-linear module projections?
