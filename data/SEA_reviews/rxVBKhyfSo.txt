 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SelMix, a selective mixup-based fine-tuning technique for optimizing non-decomposable performance objectives in pre-trained models. It utilizes a sampling distribution to perform mixups between features from different classes, aiming to improve performance on practical metrics such as worst-case recall and fairness constraints. The approach is validated through experiments on various datasets including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and STL-10, showing improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the experimental setup, and the lack of comprehensive theoretical justification.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of machine learning by focusing on optimizing non-decomposable objectives, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The proposed SelMix method is novel and well-explained, with a clear mathematical formulation and a solid theoretical foundation.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, which is essential for a broad audience.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to validate the effectiveness of SelMix, demonstrating its ability to improve performance on practical metrics.
- The methodology is flexible and can be applied to various datasets and settings, showcasing its adaptability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how SelMix optimizes non-decomposable objectives, especially in terms of the practical implications and the specifics of the optimization process.
- The theoretical justification for SelMix is not robust, particularly in the convergence analysis section, which is not convincingly explained.
- The experimental setup and results are not clearly presented, with important details relegated to supplementary materials, which could confuse readers and undermine the reproducibility of the results.
- There is a lack of novelty in the SelMix method as it closely resembles existing mixup techniques, and the improvements over existing methods are not clearly demonstrated.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of SelMix, such as its computational cost and scalability, which could impact its practical applicability.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods and a more thorough discussion of related work to better situate its contributions within the field.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how SelMix optimizes non-decomposable objectives and provide a more detailed explanation of the optimization process?
- How does SelMix compare to other mixup-based methods, and what are the specific advantages of using SelMix over these methods?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the convergence analysis and the computational cost of SelMix?
- Is there a possibility to extend the SelMix method to other types of non-decomposable objectives, such as those related to fairness or other performance metrics?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations and ethical considerations of SelMix, particularly in terms of its impact on fairness and other performance metrics?
