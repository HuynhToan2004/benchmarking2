 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of TRAK, a data attribution method, to diffusion models, specifically focusing on DDPMs. The authors introduce a new method, D-TRAK, which replaces the weighting terms in TRAK with identity matrices and utilizes different loss functions to compute the attribution scores. This modification, while theoretically unjustified, empirically outperforms TRAK across various datasets and metrics, including linear data modeling score and counterfactual evaluations. The paper also discusses the counterintuitive nature of this observation and its implications for data attribution in generative models. However, the theoretical underpinnings of D-TRAK are not thoroughly explored, and the generalizability of the findings to other diffusion models is questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise sections, making it easy to follow.
- The empirical results are strong, demonstrating that D-TRAK outperforms TRAK in terms of LDS scores and counterfactual evaluations.
- The observation that different loss functions can lead to different attribution results is counterintuitive and adds depth to the understanding of data attribution in diffusion models.
- The paper is reproducible, with clear instructions provided for reproducing the results, and the code is submitted to ensure reproducibility.
- The authors provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of their work, which is commendable for its transparency and honesty.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear theoretical explanation for why D-TRAK performs better than TRAK, which could be a significant limitation in understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned, as the proposed method seems to be a simple modification of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The generalizability of the findings to other diffusion models is not explored, which could limit the applicability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational costs associated with the proposed method, which could be a concern for practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for any new technology.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a theoretical explanation for why D-TRAK performs better than TRAK?
- How does the proposed method perform on other diffusion models, such as Stable Diffusion, and what are the expected outcomes?
- Could the authors elaborate on the computational costs associated with the proposed method and discuss potential strategies for reducing these costs?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between computational scalability and effectiveness in practical scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their proposed method and suggest ways to mitigate these impacts?
