 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Flood and Echo Net, a novel execution framework for Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) inspired by distributed algorithms. This framework is designed to facilitate information exchange throughout entire graphs, improving efficiency by reducing the number of messages exchanged. Theoretical analysis and empirical evidence demonstrate the framework's expressiveness and efficiency, showing that it can generalize to larger graph sizes and solve tasks that other GNNs cannot. The paper also explores the potential for extrapolation to larger graph instances, aligning the framework with algorithm design principles from distributed computing.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel execution framework for GNNs, Flood and Echo Net, which is inspired by distributed algorithms and can be used in combination with any existing message-based graph convolution.
- Theoretical analysis and empirical evidence are provided to demonstrate the expressiveness and efficiency of the proposed framework.
- The framework is shown to generalize to different graph sizes and can be used for algorithm learning, particularly for tasks that require the exchange of information throughout the entire graph.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a clear presentation of the main ideas.
- The Flood and Echo Net framework is shown to be more efficient in terms of message complexity compared to other GNNs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for why the Flood and Echo Net framework is designed in the specific way it is, and how it aligns with the design principles of distributed algorithms.
- There is a lack of clarity in the experimental setup, particularly in the extrapolation experiments, where the models are trained on graphs of size 10 and tested on graphs of size 100, raising concerns about the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed framework, such as its scalability and applicability to larger, more complex graphs.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other GNNs, particularly in terms of performance and scalability.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the Flood and Echo Net framework aligns with the design principles of distributed algorithms and why it is designed in the specific way it is?
- How does the Flood and Echo Net framework compare in terms of performance and scalability with other GNNs, especially in larger, more complex graphs?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly in the extrapolation experiments, to ensure the results are generalizable?
- How does the Flood and Echo Net framework handle graphs with different connectivity patterns, and what are the implications for its performance and scalability?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed framework and how these might affect its practical applicability?
