 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for enhancing the performance of large language models (LLMs) in specialized domains by integrating domain-specific markers and functional tokens. These tokens are learned embeddings added to the LLM's embedding matrix, allowing for the retention of pre-trained weights and the model's original capabilities. The approach involves a three-stage training protocol that progressively refines the tokens from general to task-specific, enabling the model to handle various tasks effectively. The methodology was tested across multiple datasets, showing promising results in domains such as protein and molecular property prediction, and drug-drug interaction prediction. The paper also discusses the potential for zero-shot generalization to unseen tasks and the modular nature of the proposed tokens.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and innovative method for adapting large language models (LLMs) to specialized domains, which is a significant advancement in the field of natural language processing.
- The use of markup-style language extensions, termed "ability tokens," is a novel approach that enhances the adaptability of LLMs to specialized tasks.
- The proposed framework is versatile, enabling the model to perform effectively across various domains and tasks, including molecular property prediction and drug-drug interaction prediction.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of different components of the framework.
- The methodology is clearly explained, making it accessible to readers and facilitating reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential challenges.
- There is a lack of comparison with other prompt tuning methods, which could provide a clearer picture of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The paper does not include a discussion on how the method would perform on larger language models, which could be a significant limitation given the increasing popularity and effectiveness of these models.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a few datasets, which may not fully demonstrate its generalizability across different domains and tasks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity or training time associated with the proposed method, which could be crucial for practical deployment in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the scalability of the method with respect to the size of the pre-trained model.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential challenges that could hinder its widespread adoption?
- How does the proposed method compare with other prompt tuning methods in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method would perform on larger language models, and discuss the potential challenges associated with scaling up the method?
- How does the computational complexity and training time of the proposed method vary with respect to the size of the pre-trained model?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method would perform on other datasets, particularly those outside the biomedical domain, to demonstrate its generalizability?
- How do the authors ensure that the domain markers do not introduce biases or inaccuracies, especially in domains where data availability is limited?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of the proposed method on the interpretability of LLMs, particularly in specialized domains where understanding the model's decision-making process is crucial?
