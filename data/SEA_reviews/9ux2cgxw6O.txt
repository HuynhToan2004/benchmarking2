 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for training-free long video editing using a pre-trained stable diffusion model. It employs a cross-window attention mechanism to enhance consistency across video frames and a latent fusion strategy to preserve the original video's structure. The methodology also includes a video frame interpolation module to address flickering issues in the edited videos. The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation using both quantitative and qualitative metrics, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in maintaining fidelity and temporal consistency. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned due to its reliance on existing techniques like cross-window attention and latent fusion.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with a focus on training-free long video editing, which is a significant practical application.
- The method's components, such as cross-window attention and latent fusion, are well-motivated and clearly explained, enhancing the paper's readability and comprehensibility.
- The evaluation is comprehensive, including both quantitative and qualitative metrics, which provides a robust assessment of the method's performance.
- The paper includes a detailed ablation study, which helps in understanding the individual contributions of each component of the method.
- The use of a video frame interpolation module to address flickering issues is a novel approach that adds value to the methodology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of video editing metrics such as CLIP-Text, CLIP-Temp, and CLIP-SE.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it primarily combines existing techniques like cross-window attention and latent fusion without significant innovation.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the cross-window attention mechanism, which is crucial for reproducibility and understanding the method's effectiveness.
- The evaluation is limited to a small dataset of 30 videos, which may not adequately represent the method's performance across a broader range of video types and styles.
- The paper's reliance on a pre-trained stable diffusion model may limit its adaptability to different video editing tasks and styles.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method in detail, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the cross-window attention mechanism, particularly how it is applied and its impact on the method's performance?
- How does the method perform when compared to state-of-the-art methods in terms of video editing metrics such as CLIP-Text, CLIP-Temp, and CLIP-SE?
- Can the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and the baselines, especially in terms of the use of DDIM inversion and the cross-attention maps?
- How does the method handle different video styles and content, especially in scenarios where the source video contains significant content changes?
- Can the authors provide more qualitative results to demonstrate the method's effectiveness in maintaining temporal consistency and visual quality, particularly in long videos?
