 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel self-supervised learning method named DoRA, designed to learn from long, continuous, and uninterrupted videos. The method leverages the cross-attention mechanism to track objects over time, enhancing the recognition of multiple objects in a video. It also proposes a new dataset, Walking Tours, consisting of 10 long, high-resolution videos, which are used to evaluate the proposed method against existing datasets like ImageNet. The paper claims that DoRA achieves competitive performance on various downstream tasks such as semantic segmentation, object detection, and image classification, demonstrating its effectiveness. However, the novelty of the method and the necessity of the new dataset are questioned, as similar approaches have been previously explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel self-supervised learning method, DoRA, which effectively leverages the cross-attention mechanism to track objects over time, enhancing the recognition of multiple objects in a video.
- The proposed method achieves strong performance on various downstream tasks such as semantic segmentation, object detection, and image classification, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The introduction of a new dataset, Walking Tours, provides a valuable resource for the research community, enhancing the credibility and practical application of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method can achieve better performance than previous methods, particularly when trained on the new dataset.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as similar approaches have been previously explored, such as using cross-attention to track objects over time.
- The necessity and utility of the new dataset, Walking Tours, are not convincingly justified. The paper does not sufficiently demonstrate how this dataset is superior or more relevant than existing datasets like ImageNet.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing datasets and methods, particularly in terms of dataset size and diversity, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The methodology and its technical details are not clearly explained, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the contributions and implications of the research.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could hinder the understanding of its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method differ from existing methods in terms of its technical contributions and novelty?
- Can the authors clarify the necessity and utility of the new dataset, Walking Tours, and how it enhances the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of dataset size and diversity with other existing datasets, such as ImageNet?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the methodology and its technical components to enhance the reader's understanding?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of occlusion, especially in scenarios where objects are partially or fully occluded?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their method and how these might affect its practical application?
