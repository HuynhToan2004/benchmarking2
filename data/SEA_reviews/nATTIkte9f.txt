 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework, LMO-DP, for fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) with differential privacy (DP). It leverages the Renyi Differential Privacy (RDP) moment accountant to optimize the DP noise added to the gradients, aiming to enhance utility under strict privacy constraints. The framework is evaluated on various tasks, including sentence classification and table-to-text generation, showing promising results. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the presentation, the practicality of the proposed methods, and the fairness of the comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using the Renyi Differential Privacy (RDP) moment accountant to optimize the DP noise added to the gradients, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed LMO-DP framework is shown to be effective in improving the accuracy and convergence speed of fine-tuning LLMs under strict privacy constraints.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in improving the accuracy and convergence speed of fine-tuning LLMs.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed method, including a rigorous proof of the RDP guarantee, which is crucial for the reliability and reproducibility of the results.
- The authors have released the code, which is a valuable resource for the community and allows for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the presentation of the LMO-DP framework and the optimization problem, which could hinder understanding and replication of the results.
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the notation and definitions used throughout the paper, which could confuse readers and affect the interpretation of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and memory requirements, which could limit their applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The comparison with existing methods is not fair or comprehensive, as the paper does not consider the most recent or relevant methods, which could undermine the validity of the results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and does not provide a thorough analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed methods with larger models or datasets, which could limit their applicability in more complex or realistic scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the optimization problem in Equation (2) and provide a more detailed explanation of the LMO-DP framework?
- How does the proposed method compare with the most recent and relevant methods, particularly in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency?
- What are the specific advantages of using the LMO-DP framework over other existing methods, such as those based on Gaussian noise or other non-Gaussian mechanisms?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger models or datasets, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in such scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter settings and their impact on the performance of the proposed methods?
- How does the proposed method handle the practical challenges of fine-tuning LLMs under strict privacy constraints, and what are the implications for the scalability and applicability of the method?
