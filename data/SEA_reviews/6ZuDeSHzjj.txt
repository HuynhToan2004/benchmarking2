 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the memorization behavior of diffusion models, focusing on the differences in memorization rates between inliers, outliers, and near duplicates in the training dataset. The authors introduce a novel methodology using a 2:1 ratio to measure memorization, which is tested on a toy dataset with two features. The study reveals that outliers are less likely to be memorized early in the training process, suggesting potential applications in anomaly detection and hyperparameter tuning. However, the paper's conclusions are limited by its reliance on a single dataset and a lack of rigorous definition and experimental validation of key terms like "memorization" and "outliers."

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and introduces an interesting phenomenon related to the memorization of outliers in diffusion models.
- The concept of "memorization" is introduced as a novel approach to understanding how diffusion models handle training data, potentially applicable to various domains.
- The paper provides a clear and intuitive explanation of the memorization phenomenon, which could be beneficial for further research in the field.
- The authors have made a significant effort to define key terms such as "memorization," "inliers," "outliers," and "near duplicates," which are crucial for understanding the paper's contributions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's conclusions are based on a single dataset, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The definition of "memorization" is not rigorously defined, which could lead to confusion or misinterpretation of the results.
- The experimental setup is limited to a toy dataset, which may not adequately represent real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a clear definition of "outliers," which is crucial for understanding the experimental results and their implications.
- There is a lack of quantitative analysis or theoretical explanation to support the observed phenomena, which could undermine the paper's claims.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant barrier to practical application.
