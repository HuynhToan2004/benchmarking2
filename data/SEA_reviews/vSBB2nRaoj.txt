 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel bi-directional goal-conditioning framework for reinforcement learning, which involves training an agent to navigate from a start state to a goal state and back, using a combination of forward and backward trajectories. This approach, termed the Scrambler-Resolver-Explorer (SRE) algorithm, involves sampling goal states and using them to define both forward and reverse tasks. The algorithm uses a Universal Value Function Approximator (UVFA) and a DQN-like agent to learn from these samples, incorporating hindsight relabeling and "fore-sight" for the backward task. The paper presents experiments in simplified gridworld environments, demonstrating that the proposed method can be effective in state space search problems. However, concerns are raised about the clarity and depth of the experimental results, the practical applicability of the method in more complex environments, and the novelty of the contributions compared to existing literature.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the presentation is clear and well-organized.
- The idea of using bi-directional goal-conditioning to solve state space search problems is innovative and well-motivated, especially in the context of goal-conditioned reinforcement learning (GCRL).
- The paper provides a comprehensive description of the proposed method, including a detailed explanation of the SRE algorithm and its implementation.
- The experiments conducted are extensive, covering various environments, and the results show that the proposed method outperforms baseline methods in some cases.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to bi-directional goal-conditioning, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of key concepts such as "state space search problems," "binary reward system," and "forward task" and "reverse task," which could confuse readers.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some figures (e.g., Figure 3) showing significant performance gaps that are not adequately explained.
- The paper does not adequately compare its results with existing literature, particularly in terms of performance metrics and methodological approaches.
- The paper's contribution is seen as incremental rather than novel, as similar methods have been previously explored in other works.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup is considered too simplistic, which might not adequately demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in more complex or realistic environments.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definitions and explanations of "state space search problems," "binary reward system," and "forward task" and "reverse task"?
- How does the proposed method compare to other bi-directional goal-conditioning methods, especially those that use different learning objectives or methodologies?
- In the experimental results, why does the performance of SRE-DQN decrease significantly for the 15x15 gridworld environment?
- Can you provide more detailed explanations for the performance gaps observed in Figure 3 and the overall experimental results?
- How does the proposed method handle the practical issues of goal state sampling and the computational demands of generating new tasks?
- Can you elaborate on the potential applications and limitations of the proposed method in real-world scenarios, especially in domains where goal states are not explicitly defined?
