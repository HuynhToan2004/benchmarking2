 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MultiHyperGNN, a hypernetwork-based graph neural network designed for domain generalization in graph transformation. MultiHyperGNN employs an encoder-decoder architecture to handle both input and output modes, leveraging a semi-supervised link prediction mechanism to enhance the transformation task. The model is evaluated on three datasets, showing superior performance compared to other models in prediction and domain generalization tasks. However, the paper suffers from issues such as unclear definitions, poor writing, and lack of novelty, with some reviewers expressing concerns about the method's complexity and the clarity of its contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-motivated, addressing the significant problem of graph transformation, particularly in scenarios where the testing and training data do not preserve the same distribution.
- The proposed MultiHyperGNN model is innovative, utilizing a hypernetwork-based architecture to tackle the domain generalization problem in graph transformation, which is a novel approach.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a good overview of the problem setting.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the superior performance of MultiHyperGNN compared to other models, highlighting its effectiveness in handling graph transformation tasks.
- The paper introduces a novel hypernetwork-based graph neural network, which is a promising direction for further research in graph neural networks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from poor writing, with some sections being difficult to understand, and the overall presentation could be improved for better clarity.
- There are numerous typos and grammatical errors throughout the manuscript, which detract from its professional quality.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The complexity of the method, particularly the use of multiple hypernetworks and the semi-supervised link prediction mechanism, raises concerns about its practical applicability and the scalability of the model.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The related work section is inadequately discussed, missing out on important references that could provide a better context for the research.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the model, which could be a significant concern for its practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition of "mode" in the context used in the paper?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of using multiple hypernetworks, and how does this approach compare to simpler alternatives?
- How does the model handle the complexity introduced by the semi-supervised link prediction mechanism, and what are the potential impacts on the model's performance and scalability?
- Can you provide more details on the datasets used in the experiments, particularly the source and availability of these datasets?
- How does the model handle the generalization to unseen domains, and what are the specific mechanisms in place for this generalization?
- Could you elaborate on the computational complexity of the model and discuss potential strategies for reducing this complexity in practical applications?
- In the context of the experiments, how do the results compare when different hypernetwork architectures are used, and what are the implications of these choices on the model's performance?
