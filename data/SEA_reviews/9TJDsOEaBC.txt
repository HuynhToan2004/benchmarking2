 **Summary:**
The paper introduces VOGP, a novel Bayesian optimization algorithm designed for vector optimization problems, leveraging Gaussian processes to model the objective function. VOGP employs a cone-based Pareto set identification method and provides theoretical guarantees for its performance. It introduces an acquisition function that utilizes mutual information between the posterior and the set of function evaluations, aiming to maximize the information gain. The algorithm is tested against existing methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its efficiency and effectiveness. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the paper, and the fairness of the experimental comparisons.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel acquisition function for vector optimization problems, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The algorithm is well-articulated, with clear descriptions of the various subroutines and their functions, making it accessible to readers.
- The theoretical analysis provided is comprehensive, including proofs of the algorithm's effectiveness and convergence guarantees.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear introduction and detailed methodology, which aids in understanding the complex mathematical concepts presented.
- The experimental results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing ones, showing the practical applicability of the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the mathematical notations and definitions, particularly those related to the cone and its properties.
- There are several instances of unclear or confusing statements and definitions, such as the meaning of certain variables and the relationships between different parts of the algorithm.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as it seems to be a combination of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The experimental comparisons are potentially unfair, as the proposed method is tested against a baseline that may not be directly comparable due to differences in sampling strategies.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and potential areas for future research, which could enhance its applicability and relevance.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of readability and clarity, to better engage readers and facilitate understanding.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the meaning and usage of the variable 'u' in the context of the paper?
- What is the role and significance of the 'pessimistic Pareto set' in the algorithm, and how is it computed?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness when applied to continuous design spaces?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the baseline method was adapted to ensure a fair comparison?
- How does the method handle different types of ordering cones, and what are the implications of using different cones in practical applications?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of parameters such as βt and how they affect the performance of the algorithm?
- In the context of the experiments, how does the method perform when compared to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of Pareto statistics and success rates?
