 **Summary:**
The paper investigates various hyperparameters and techniques to enhance the transferability of adversarial attacks, focusing on gradient-based, input transformation-based, and ensemble strategies. The authors propose a set of tricks aimed at improving the transferability of adversarial examples across different models, including the use of spectral-based input transformations and dual examples. Extensive experiments are conducted to validate the effectiveness of these tricks, with results showing significant improvements in transferability on ImageNet. The paper also discusses the impact of different initialization methods, step sizes, and momentum decay factors on the transferability of adversarial examples.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a comprehensive study on the transferability of adversarial attacks, exploring various hyperparameters and techniques to enhance their performance.
- It provides a detailed analysis of the effects of different initialization methods, step sizes, and momentum decay factors on the transferability of adversarial examples.
- The paper introduces several novel tricks to improve the transferability of adversarial examples, such as spectral-based input transformations and dual examples, which are supported by experimental results.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed methods and their impact on adversarial transferability.
- The experiments are extensive and cover a wide range of attacks and defense methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed tricks across different settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on improving the transferability of adversarial attacks without considering the robustness of the defense models, which could limit the practical applicability of the findings.
- The paper's claims about the novelty of the proposed tricks are not convincingly supported by existing literature, as many of the techniques have been previously explored.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to state-of-the-art methods, and the improvements are sometimes marginal or not statistically significant.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and the potential negative societal impacts of using adversarial attacks.
- There are concerns about the fairness of the comparisons made in the paper, particularly regarding the use of different model architectures and the impact of these differences on the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of the proposed tricks compared to existing literature, particularly in terms of the specific contributions and improvements over previous work?
- How do the authors address the potential negative societal impacts of using adversarial attacks, especially in scenarios where these attacks could be used maliciously?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specific model architectures used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How do the authors ensure the fairness and reliability of the comparisons made in the paper, particularly regarding the use of different model architectures and the impact of these differences on the results?
- Can the authors provide more statistical evidence to support the claims made in the paper, particularly concerning the improvements in transferability achieved by the proposed tricks?
