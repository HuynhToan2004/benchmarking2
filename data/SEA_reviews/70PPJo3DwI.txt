 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for federated learning (FL) that addresses the out-of-federation (OOF) generalization challenge by leveraging client topology. The approach involves two steps: first, inferring the client topology based on model similarity, and second, using this topology to identify influential clients and build a robust model. The methodology is supported by a series of experiments on various datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in enhancing OOF robustness and communication efficiency. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the method in real-world scenarios due to its reliance on model similarity for client topology inference, which could be infeasible in FL settings where clients maintain privacy. Additionally, the paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods and a detailed analysis of the method's limitations and potential negative societal impacts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in federated learning (FL) by focusing on out-of-federation (OOF) generalization, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The proposed method is novel and innovative, leveraging client topology to improve OOF generalization, which is a novel approach in FL.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive empirical evaluation that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets.
- The methodology is clearly presented, with a logical structure that guides the reader through the problem, methodology, and results.
- The paper includes a thorough ablation study, which helps in understanding the impact of different components of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The reliance on model similarity for client topology inference is not feasible in FL settings where clients maintain privacy, raising concerns about the practicality of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, especially in large-scale federated learning settings, which could limit its applicability.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for ensuring ethical and responsible AI development.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed or validated, with some results (e.g., Figure 3) being unclear or potentially misleading.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical analysis or intuitive insights into the proposed method, which could enhance understanding and credibility.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the model similarity does not accurately reflect the true relationships between clients?
- Can you provide more details on how the method handles clients with heterogeneous data distributions?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle multiple rounds of OOF generalization, and how would this affect the computational efficiency and scalability?
- How does the method perform when the number of clients is large, and what are the computational costs associated with this?
- Could you clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly in Figure 3, to ensure the clarity and accuracy of the presented data?
- How does the method ensure that the topology inference does not leak sensitive information about the clients, especially in terms of data distribution?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how do you address these in your research?
