 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the identifiability of Switching Dynamical Systems (SDS) by extending the analysis of Identifiable Latent Variable Models (ILVM) to the temporal setting. It establishes conditions for the identifiability of latent variables and non-linear mappings in SDS up to affine transformations. The authors demonstrate the practical application of these models through experiments on causal discovery and time-series segmentation. The paper also introduces estimation algorithms for identifiable SDS and discusses their application in various domains, including climate data and video segmentation. Despite its theoretical and practical contributions, the paper is criticized for its limited discussion on the practical implications of identifiability and the lack of a broader empirical validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper extends the analysis of Identifiable Latent Variable Models (ILVM) to the temporal setting, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the results are presented clearly.
- The authors demonstrate the practical application of identifiable SDS through experiments on causal discovery and time-series segmentation, showing the utility of these models in real-world scenarios.
- The paper establishes the identifiability of latent variables and non-linear mappings in Switching Dynamical Systems up to affine transformations, which is a significant theoretical contribution.
- The paper discusses the estimation algorithms for identifiable SDS, which is crucial for understanding the practical implementation of these models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the practical implications of identifiability, which could be a significant limitation for some readers.
- The paper does not include a broad empirical validation, which could be seen as a weakness in demonstrating the general applicability of the proposed methods.
- The paper's contributions are seen as incremental, as it builds on previous works such as Kivva et al. (2022), with limited novel theoretical results.
- The paper's theoretical results are not clearly explained, particularly in the context of their practical implications, which could make it difficult for readers to fully grasp the contributions.
- The paper's experimental validation is limited to specific domains, which may not fully demonstrate the general applicability of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**

- How do the authors address the issue of state collapse in variational learning for sequential LVMs, which is a known challenge in the field?

- Could the authors elaborate on the practical utility of the identifiability results, particularly in terms of their impact on the performance or effectiveness of the proposed methods?
- The paper's experimental validation is limited to specific domains. Could the authors discuss the potential for broader empirical validation to demonstrate the general applicability of the proposed methods?
