 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ForgetNet, a novel neural algorithmic reasoning model that challenges the use of historical embeddings, which are inherently non-Markovian. This is achieved by removing historical embeddings and introducing a gating mechanism in G-ForgetNet to selectively integrate historical information. The model is evaluated on the CLRS-30 benchmark and shows improved performance over baseline models, particularly in out-of-distribution settings. The authors argue that this approach aligns better with the Markovian nature of algorithmic reasoning tasks, improving generalization capabilities. Despite these strengths, the paper faces criticisms for its limited novelty and insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical flow that effectively communicates the authors' ideas.
- The introduction of ForgetNet and G-ForgetNet is a significant contribution to the field of algorithmic reasoning, challenging the traditional use of historical embeddings.
- The experiments conducted are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly in out-of-distribution settings.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the gating mechanism in G-ForgetNet, highlighting its alignment with intuitions and effectiveness for robust performance.
- The methodology is novel and interesting, with a focus on the Markov property of algorithmic reasoning tasks, which is a significant aspect of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks sufficient novelty as the proposed methods are seen as minor modifications of existing models, such as the Triplet-GMPNN model.
- The comparison with state-of-the-art models is insufficient, which limits the evaluation of the proposed methods against the most advanced models in the field.
- The paper's contribution is viewed as limited due to the focus on a specific benchmark (CLRS-30) and the lack of broader experimental validation across different datasets.
- The methodology, particularly the use of historical embeddings and the gating mechanism, is not sufficiently justified or explained, raising questions about its effectiveness and the rationale behind its design.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations or negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role of historical embeddings in the Triplet-GMPNN model and explain how the proposed methods differ from this model?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to state-of-the-art models in terms of generalization capability?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results and comparisons with other models to better demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed methods?
- How does the paper address the limitations discussed, particularly the focus on the CLRS-30 benchmark and the lack of broader experimental validation?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed justification for the use of historical embeddings and the gating mechanism, including empirical evidence or theoretical support for their effectiveness?
