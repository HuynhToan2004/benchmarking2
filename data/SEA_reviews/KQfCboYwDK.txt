 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Adiabatic Replay (AR), for continual learning that addresses the challenge of catastrophic forgetting by selectively replaying and updating previous knowledge. AR utilizes Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) to represent each task's data distribution, which facilitates the selective retrieval of relevant data for replay. This method aims to maintain a constant time complexity by replaying a fixed number of samples per task, thereby avoiding the linear growth of replayed samples with the number of tasks. The paper provides a detailed experimental evaluation, comparing AR with various baselines and demonstrating its effectiveness in preventing forgetting and maintaining performance over time.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear structure that aids in understanding the content.
- The methodology, particularly the use of GMMs and the concept of selective replay, is innovative and well-explained, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.
- The paper includes a comprehensive experimental evaluation that covers various datasets and benchmarks, providing a robust validation of the proposed method.
- The use of GMMs for continual learning is novel and shows promising results in reducing computational costs and improving performance.
- The paper is well-positioned within the existing literature, providing a clear context for the reader and highlighting the unique contributions of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other relevant works, such as the GEM method, which could have provided a more comprehensive view of the method's performance and positioning in the field.
- The experimental setup and results could be more clearly presented and analyzed, particularly in terms of the time complexity and the impact of different parameters on the performance.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method, which could provide a more balanced view of its strengths and weaknesses.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made by the method and how these assumptions might affect its applicability in different scenarios.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed visualizations and explanations of the experimental results, particularly in figures where the data is dense or complex.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that also use GMMs, such as those mentioned in the literature?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the time complexity of the method, particularly how it scales with the number of tasks and the size of the datasets?
- How sensitive is the method to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the number of components in the GMM and the annealing radius?
- Could the authors elaborate on the assumptions made by the method and discuss how these assumptions might limit the applicability of the method in different scenarios?
- In the experimental results, why does the method perform worse on certain datasets compared to others, and what might explain these differences?
