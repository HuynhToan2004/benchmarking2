 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the noise scheduling in diffusion models, proposing a novel metric called weighted signal-to-noise ratio (WSNR) to quantify the noise levels in both RGB and latent spaces. It introduces a data-driven sampling noise schedule based on the probability flow ODE framework, aiming to optimize the integration interval of the ODE. The authors also explore the relationship between the number of evaluation points and the generation quality, proposing a dynamic scheme for choosing numerical methods within computational constraints. The proposed method refines the FID of pre-trained CIFAR-10 and FFHQ-64 models, showing potential improvements in generation quality and efficiency. However, the paper's experimental scope is limited to a few datasets, and the theoretical underpinnings are not thoroughly explored, which could limit the generalizability and applicability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel metric, WSNR, which consistently quantifies the noise level of the training data in both RGB space and latent space.
- It proposes a WSNR-Equivalent training noise schedule to improve the performance of diffusion models in the RGB space and latent space.
- The authors analyze the relationship between the number of evaluation points and the generation quality, leading to the development of a Number of Function Evaluations (NFE)-guided sampling noise schedule.
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed methods.
- The proposed method achieves good performance gains in terms of FID, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed metrics and methods.
- The paper is original in its approach, using frequency analysis to understand noise in diffusion models, which is a novel perspective.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental scope is limited, focusing on only a few datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed methods.
- The theoretical underpinnings of the paper are not thoroughly explored, particularly the derivation of equations and the explanation of the method's effectiveness.
- The paper lacks detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the specific advantages of the proposed methods over existing ones.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the explanation of certain equations and the method's implementation details.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed methods are not adequately supported by empirical evidence, particularly in terms of quantitative results.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of datasets, such as text-to-image models or video datasets?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed methods and how they compare to existing methods in terms of efficiency?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed methods over existing methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of computational complexity, particularly in high-resolution models?
- Can the authors clarify the derivation of certain equations, such as Eq. 2 and the explanation of the ideal denoiser?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other metrics like SNR, and what are the theoretical justifications for the choice of WSNR over SNR?
- Could the authors provide more quantitative results to support the claims of improved performance and efficiency?
