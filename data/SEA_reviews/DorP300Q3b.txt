 **Summary:**
The paper introduces P3DTrack, a novel method for 2D Multiple Object Tracking (MOT) that leverages 3D object representation learned from video and supervised by 2D tracking labels. This method aims to improve data association in MOT by utilizing a pseudo 3D object label generation technique and a joint learning framework that integrates 3D object representation and object association. The proposed method is evaluated on large-scale ego-centric datasets such as KITTI and Waymo Open Dataset (WOD), demonstrating state-of-the-art performance. However, the paper is criticized for its reliance on 3D labels derived from 2D data, which may not be as accurate as using ground truth 3D labels, and for not sufficiently addressing the issue of occlusion in 2D MOT.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method, P3DTrack, achieves state-of-the-art performance on large-scale ego-centric datasets such as KITTI and Waymo Open Dataset (WOD), demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The methodology is clearly explained, and the authors provide a comprehensive ablation study that effectively demonstrates the contributions of different components of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses the data association problem in 2D Multiple Object Tracking (MOT) by utilizing 3D object representation, which is a novel approach that could potentially enhance the accuracy of MOT in various applications.
- The proposed method is innovative in its use of pseudo 3D object labels and a joint learning framework, which could inspire further research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's main contribution, the pseudo 3D object label generation, is questioned for its accuracy and practicality. The method relies on the assumption that moving objects can be reconstructed from videos, which may not always be true, especially in dynamic scenes.
- The paper's reliance on 2D tracking labels for learning 3D object representation might limit the method's effectiveness, as it does not directly utilize ground truth 3D object labels which are typically more accurate.
- The method's performance in scenarios with severe occlusions or complex scenes is not adequately addressed, which is a significant limitation given the challenges posed by such scenarios in MOT.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that use 3D object representation or other advanced techniques for MOT.
- The paper's technical contribution is questioned, as the use of 3D object representation and pseudo 3D object labels are seen as incremental improvements rather than significant innovations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the pseudo 3D object labels are generated and ensure their accuracy, especially in scenarios where moving objects are not effectively reconstructed?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with severe occlusions or complex scenes, and what measures are taken to address these challenges?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that use 3D object representation or other advanced techniques for MOT?
- The paper mentions the use of a 3D Kalman Filter for motion modeling. Could the authors explain why a 2D Kalman Filter was not used, and what specific advantages does the 3D Kalman Filter provide in this context?
- Given the reliance on 2D tracking labels for learning 3D object representation, how does the proposed method ensure that the learned 3D object representation accurately reflects the true 3D positions of the objects in the scene?
