 **Summary:**
The paper investigates feature learning in a two-layer linear neural network under a high-dimensional setting, focusing on the generalization properties of the first layer's parameters. It introduces a criterion for selecting the first layer's parameters, which are shown to achieve near-optimal Bayes risk under certain conditions. The authors compare this approach with ridge regression and demonstrate that their method can generalize better in specific scenarios. The theoretical analysis is supported by numerical experiments, although the paper could benefit from a clearer presentation and a more rigorous discussion on the assumptions and limitations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel theoretical analysis of the generalization properties of the first layer's parameters in a two-layer linear neural network, which is a significant contribution to understanding feature learning in deep neural networks.
- The proposed criterion for selecting the first layer's parameters is a useful theoretical tool for analyzing the generalization performance of two-layer linear neural networks, and it is supported by both theoretical and numerical evidence.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations and detailed proofs.
- The theoretical results are supported by numerical experiments, which demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed criterion.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of deep learning, specifically the benign overfitting phenomenon in linear models, which is a significant area of interest in the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the second layer's parameters are already known, which might not be realistic in practical scenarios.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to linear neural networks, which might not fully capture the complexity of non-linear networks commonly used in practice.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed criterion, which is a significant omission given its potential practical applications.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the sub-Gaussianity of the input random variable and the covariance matrix of the target signals, are not adequately justified, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and structure, to better guide the reader through the complex theoretical arguments.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a critical aspect of responsible AI research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly regarding the sub-Gaussianity of the input random variable and the covariance matrix of the target signals?
- How does the proposed criterion perform in practical scenarios where the second layer's parameters are not known in advance?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed criterion and discuss its scalability to larger datasets?
- How does the proposed criterion compare to other methods for selecting the first layer's parameters in linear neural networks, such as those based on implicit regularization?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their method, particularly in terms of fairness and bias in machine learning applications?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of noise, such as label noise or feature noise, and what are the implications for the generalization performance?
