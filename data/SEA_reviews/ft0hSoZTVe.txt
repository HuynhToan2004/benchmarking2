 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multi-task learning approach that integrates object detection, instance segmentation, semantic segmentation, and image captioning using the YOLOR backbone and ELAN. The method leverages a shared semantic representation and a unified training strategy to enhance performance across tasks. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces significant criticisms regarding its experimental validation, particularly the lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art models and the absence of essential baseline comparisons. The methodology, while promising, lacks sufficient evidence to substantiate its claims of superiority or novelty over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed method is innovative, combining object detection, instance segmentation, semantic segmentation, and image captioning using a unified backbone and training strategy, which is a significant advancement in multi-task learning.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method can achieve competitive performance on all tasks while maintaining a low parameter count and without pre-training.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments and ablation studies to validate their claims.
- The paper presents a novel approach to data augmentation, which is crucial for improving model performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of model size, training time, and performance metrics.
- There is a notable absence of essential baseline comparisons, such as the YOLOv7 baseline and Pix2Seq v2, which are crucial for evaluating the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the training time and computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for assessing its practical applicability.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the impact of data augmentation techniques on the performance of the model, which is a significant aspect of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the related works, particularly those that have explored similar multi-task learning approaches.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art models, including the YOLOv7 baseline and Pix2Seq v2?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of model size, training time, and performance metrics with other multi-task learning models?
- Could the authors elaborate on the data augmentation techniques used and their impact on the performance of the model?
- What are the specific limitations of the proposed method, and how do these affect its practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other multi-task learning models in terms of computational efficiency and training time?
- Can the authors discuss the potential of extending the proposed method to include other tasks or datasets?
- Could the authors provide more detailed visualizations and examples to support the claims made in the paper?
