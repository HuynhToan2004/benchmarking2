 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CoPreFL, a meta-learning-based pre-training approach for federated learning (FL) that addresses the challenges of initializing models with data distributed among clients and the server. This method aims to strike a balance between average performance and fairness, ensuring robust initialization for any unseen downstream FL tasks. The paper presents two scenarios for pre-training and demonstrates that CoPreFL outperforms existing methods in terms of average performance and fairness. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned due to its similarity to existing meta-learning-based FL methods. The paper is well-written and provides extensive experimental results, but lacks a detailed comparison with existing works and a discussion on the computational efficiency of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed method, CoPreFL, which is a meta-learning-based pre-training approach for federated learning (FL).
- The method is evaluated across multiple datasets and scenarios, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving average performance and fairness.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the method, including extensive experimental results and ablation studies, which support the claims made.
- The problem of federated pre-training is well-motivated, and the paper addresses the challenges of initializing FL with data distributed among clients and the server.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to federated learning by combining meta-learning with federated learning, which is a promising direction for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it is similar to existing meta-learning-based FL methods, such as FedMeta, which also use a similar approach.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing works, particularly in terms of the computational efficiency and fairness of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not convincing, particularly in scenarios where the proposed method does not show significant improvements over existing methods.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical implementation in federated learning environments.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of fairness in federated learning, which is a significant challenge in real-world applications.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts, which are important considerations for any technological innovation.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency with existing meta-learning-based FL methods?
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison with existing works, particularly in terms of fairness and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data distribution is more heterogeneous or where the data is more non-IID?
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the fairness metrics used in the paper and how they compare to other existing fairness metrics in federated learning?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of fairness in federated learning, particularly in scenarios where the data distribution is highly imbalanced?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, including the computational cost and the scalability of the method in large-scale federated learning environments?
