 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LogicBench, a novel dataset designed to evaluate the logical reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) across propositional, first-order, and non-monotonic logics. The dataset, which includes 25 distinct inference rules and reasoning patterns, was constructed using a three-stage method involving GPT-3 for sentence generation and template-based conversion to logical rules. The authors conducted experiments using various LLMs, demonstrating that these models struggle with logical reasoning, particularly with complex rules and negations. The dataset is intended to be a valuable resource for evaluating and enhancing the logical reasoning capabilities of LLMs, although concerns were raised about its linguistic diversity and the methodology used for data generation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel dataset, LogicBench, which covers a broad spectrum of logical reasoning types, including propositional, first-order, and non-monotonic logics, and includes 25 distinct inference rules and reasoning patterns.
- The dataset is well-structured and clearly presented, with a three-stage method used to construct it, which includes GPT-3 for sentence generation and template-based conversion to logical rules.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of various LLMs, demonstrating their struggles with logical reasoning, particularly with complex rules and negations.
- The dataset is intended to be a valuable resource for evaluating and enhancing the logical reasoning capabilities of LLMs, and it includes both a high-quality, cost-effective, and reliable dataset for evaluation and a synthetically augmented version for training.
- The authors have conducted experiments using different LLMs and have analyzed the results, providing insights into the challenges faced by these models in logical reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the methodology used for data generation, particularly the process of converting natural language sentences into logical rules and the quality control measures employed.
- The linguistic diversity of the context presented in the dataset is not adequately addressed, and the dataset could benefit from a more diverse range of linguistic styles to better reflect real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential biases in the dataset, which could influence the results and the conclusions drawn from the experiments.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the specific inference rules used in the experiments, and the paper does not provide sufficient details on how the experiments were conducted, such as the number of models used and the variation in results.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the dataset, such as its applicability to other types of reasoning tasks and its generalizability to other domains.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the methodology used for data generation, particularly the process of converting natural language sentences into logical rules and the quality control measures employed?
- How does the dataset address the linguistic diversity of context, and what steps have been taken to ensure that the context is representative of real-world scenarios?
- What specific inference rules were used in the experiments, and how were these rules chosen?
- How were the experiments conducted, and what were the specifics of the experimental setup? For example, how many models were used, and what was the variation in results?
- How does the dataset address the potential biases that could influence the results and the conclusions drawn from the experiments?
- Could you provide more details on the evaluation metrics used and how they were chosen?
- How does the dataset address the limitations mentioned, such as its applicability to other types of reasoning tasks and its generalizability to other domains?
