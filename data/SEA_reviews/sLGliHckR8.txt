 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GEAM, a novel fragment-based drug discovery framework designed to enhance molecular diversity and improve drug efficacy. GEAM comprises three modules: FGIB for goal-aware fragment extraction, SAC for fragment assembly, and GA for fragment modification. The framework employs a dynamic vocabulary update mechanism, enabling the exploration of unseen fragments during the generation process. GEAM's performance is validated through extensive experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness in discovering drug candidates with high binding affinity and drug-likeness. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper's presentation and the necessity of using the GEAM framework over simpler, more direct methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to fragment-based drug discovery by using a dynamic vocabulary update mechanism, which allows for the exploration of unseen fragments during the generation process.
- The proposed method, GEAM, demonstrates superior performance in molecular optimization tasks compared to existing methods, as shown in extensive experiments.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed descriptions of the proposed method and experimental setup.
- The motivation behind the research is clear, and the paper is easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The methodology is technically sound, and the results are convincing, showing that the proposed method is effective in discovering drug candidates with high binding affinity and drug-likeness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the methodology, particularly in how the different modules of GEAM are integrated and how they contribute to the overall performance.
- The performance of the proposed method, while impressive, is not significantly better than existing methods, and the improvements are sometimes marginal.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper's presentation is criticized for being difficult to follow, with some sections containing unclear or incomplete descriptions.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods, and the novelty of the proposed method is questioned.
- There is a lack of clarity in the experimental setup, particularly in the definition of terms and the details of the experimental conditions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the integration of the different modules of GEAM and how they contribute to the overall performance?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of overfitting, and what measures have been taken to ensure the generalizability of the results?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the definitions and conditions used in the experiments?
- How does the proposed method compare to simpler, more direct methods for fragment-based drug discovery, and what are the advantages of using GEAM over these methods?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training data and the selection criteria for the top-K fragments used in the experiments?
- How does the proposed method ensure the diversity and novelty of the generated molecules, particularly in the context of the dynamic vocabulary update mechanism?
