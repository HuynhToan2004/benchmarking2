 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an ensemble of low-rank adapters (LoRA) for fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) to address issues like overconfidence and poor calibration in predictions. This method involves training multiple LoRA adapters on the same pre-trained model with different random initializations, which are then combined to improve predictive accuracy and uncertainty quantification. The authors demonstrate that LoRA ensembles outperform regular LoRA and other methods in multiple-choice question-answering tasks, showing consistent improvements in accuracy and calibration. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its limited evaluation scope, lack of comparative analysis with more advanced methods, and insufficient theoretical backing.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to improving uncertainty quantification in language models.
- The use of LoRA ensembles is innovative and provides a practical solution for handling large ensembles of LLMs, which is a significant challenge in the field.
- The experimental results show consistent improvements in predictive accuracy and uncertainty quantification, supported by extensive ablation studies and comparisons with multiple baselines.
- The method is simple yet effective, demonstrating significant improvements in accuracy and calibration error, as shown in figures and tables.
- The paper is well-structured and provides a comprehensive review of related work, highlighting the authors' deep understanding of the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation is limited to multiple-choice question-answering tasks, which might not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in other types of tasks, such as open-ended generation.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other ensemble methods, particularly those that use different regularization techniques, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The theoretical analysis is insufficient, and there is a lack of discussion on how the LoRA ensemble improves uncertainty quantification and how it compares to other ensemble methods.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse tasks and comparing with a broader range of methods, including other ensemble methods and different regularization techniques.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the LoRA ensemble and its impact on uncertainty quantification.

**Questions:**
- How does the LoRA ensemble compare to other ensemble methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and predictive accuracy?
- Can the LoRA ensemble be adapted to other types of tasks, such as open-ended generation? What would be the expected outcomes in such scenarios?
- How does the LoRA ensemble handle the diversity of the ensemble members? What are the implications of using different regularization techniques on the ensemble's performance?
- Could the authors provide more detailed theoretical analysis on how the LoRA ensemble improves uncertainty quantification and how it compares to other ensemble methods?
- In the experimental setup, could the authors consider including a broader range of tasks and more diverse methods to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method?
