 **Summary:**
The paper introduces fMRI-PTE, a novel auto-encoder model designed for pre-training on fMRI data, aimed at enhancing brain activity decoding. The model converts fMRI signals into 2D representations, which are then used to train a transformer-based auto-encoder. This model is evaluated using the NSD dataset, demonstrating its ability to reconstruct fMRI data and perform cross-subject decoding. The paper also discusses the challenges of pre-training on fMRI data and the potential for improving brain decoding tasks. However, concerns were raised regarding the clarity of the methodology, the lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art models, and the limited scope of the evaluation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach for fMRI data pre-training, focusing on the unique challenges posed by fMRI data, such as the need for specialized data transformations and learning strategies.
- The proposed method, fMRI-PTE, is innovative in its use of a transformer-based auto-encoder to handle fMRI data, which is different from typical natural image-based models.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the methodology, including the data processing steps and the implementation of the transformer architecture, which aids in understanding the proposed method.
- The results section shows that fMRI-PTE can outperform existing models in various decoding tasks, indicating the potential effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes detailed information about the implementation and experimental setup, which is beneficial for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the choice of using a transformer-based auto-encoder for fMRI data, especially considering the potential limitations in handling high-frequency signals and spatial interactions.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, focusing primarily on the NSD dataset and not comparing the proposed method with a broader range of datasets or state-of-the-art models, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the number of subjects used for training and testing, which could affect the robustness of the results.
- The methodology section is not clearly written, with some terms and concepts not defined, which could confuse readers unfamiliar with the specifics of fMRI data processing.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights into potential improvements or future directions for research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the choice of using a transformer-based auto-encoder for fMRI data and discuss how it addresses the unique challenges posed by this type of data?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, particularly in terms of the number of subjects used for training and testing?
- Could the authors provide more details on the data processing steps, such as the conversion of fMRI signals into 2D representations and the use of ROIs for training?
- What are the specific contributions of the proposed method compared to existing methods like LEA and LR, particularly in terms of the use of transformers and the handling of high-frequency signals?
- How does the method perform when applied to other datasets, such as those from the UKB, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the findings?
