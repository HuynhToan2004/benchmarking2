 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach using pre-trained large language models (LLMs) as visual encoder layers in vision tasks, specifically in ViT architectures. The authors argue that LLMs, when frozen and integrated into ViTs, can enhance performance across various tasks including image and point cloud classification, action recognition, and non-semantic tasks. The methodology involves inserting a frozen LLM block into the ViT architecture, followed by trainable linear layers to align feature dimensions. The paper also proposes an "information filtering" hypothesis to explain the effectiveness of LLMs in visual encoding. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of comprehensive experimental validation, particularly in terms of the breadth of tasks tested and the depth of analysis on the impact of LLM layers on performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using frozen pre-trained large language models (LLMs) as visual encoder layers in vision tasks, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The methodology is simple and effective, showing consistent performance improvements across a range of tasks, including image classification, point cloud classification, action recognition, and motion forecasting.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the experimental results and the proposed information filtering hypothesis.
- The proposed method is flexible and can be applied to various tasks, including vision-language tasks, demonstrating its versatility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive experimental validation, particularly in terms of the breadth of tasks tested. It would be beneficial to include more diverse tasks such as object detection and segmentation to validate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis on why the LLM layers improve performance, nor does it explore the impact of different LLM layers on performance.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the computational cost of using LLMs in vision tasks and the potential for overfitting due to the complexity of the LLM architecture.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a more robust evaluation of its effectiveness.
- The paper does not include code, which could limit the reproducibility of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when compared with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- Can the proposed method be applied to other types of tasks, such as object detection and segmentation? If so, how does it perform in these tasks?
- What are the specific contributions of the LLM layers in improving performance, and how do these contributions vary across different tasks?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational cost of using LLMs in vision tasks, and what are the potential limitations in terms of scalability and complexity?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specific LLM used and the training parameters, to help replicate the results?
