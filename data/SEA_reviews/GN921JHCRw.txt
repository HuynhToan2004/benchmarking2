 **Summary:**
The paper introduces RAPTOR, a novel retrieval-augmented language model (RALM) that utilizes a recursive summarization method to enhance the retrieval of information from long documents. This method involves clustering text into a tree structure, with each cluster represented by a summary, which is then used to augment the context of a large language model (LLM) for question-answering tasks. The paper demonstrates that RAPTOR outperforms existing retrieval models and baselines on several benchmarks, including NarrativeQA, QASPER, and QuALITY, using GPT-4 as the reader. The approach leverages a hierarchical clustering method to create a tree-structured index of summaries, which is then used to retrieve and summarize relevant information from large text corpora.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive related work section that effectively positions the research within the existing literature.
- The proposed method is novel and effective, demonstrating good results on several datasets, including NarrativeQA, QASPER, and QuALITY.
- The use of a tree-structured index of summaries is a novel approach that enhances retrieval efficiency and the quality of retrieved information.
- The paper includes a detailed description of the clustering algorithm and the querying process, which aids in understanding the methodology and its implementation.
- The experiments are well-designed, with controlled baselines and state-of-the-art comparisons, providing a robust evaluation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The use of GMM for clustering and the choice of parameters (e.g., the number of clusters) are not sufficiently justified or ablated, which could affect the reliability and reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not include a detailed analysis of the computational complexity or the scalability of the method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The evaluation primarily focuses on question-answering tasks and does not explore other applications of the retrieved information, such as text classification or summarization, which could provide a more comprehensive view of the method's capabilities.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the current evaluation, such as the choice of datasets and the generalizability of the findings to other types of retrieval tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its applicability in different scenarios?
- How does the choice of parameters, such as the number of clusters in the GMM, affect the performance and reliability of the clustering process?
- What are the computational requirements of the proposed method, and how does it scale with the size of the retrieval corpus and the number of queries?
- Have the authors considered using other clustering methods, such as k-means or hierarchical clustering, and if so, what were the results?
- How does the proposed method perform on other types of retrieval tasks, such as those involving text classification or summarization?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the method, especially in relation to the size of the retrieval corpus and the number of queries?
