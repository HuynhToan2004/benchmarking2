 **Summary:**
The paper introduces KNIFE, a method for transferring reasoning knowledge from a teacher LM to a student LM using free-text rationales (FTRs) as a knowledge source. The teacher LM is trained to predict task outputs based on task inputs and FTRs, while the student LM is trained to align its hidden states with the teacher's, enabling it to perform reasoning tasks without FTRs. The method is evaluated on two QA datasets, showing that KNIFE outperforms various baselines, including standard finetuning, prompting, and pipeline rationalization. The paper also includes extensive ablation studies and failure analyses to support its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and well-explained methodology.
- The proposed method, KNIFE, is simple and effective, outperforming various baselines in experiments.
- The paper provides extensive ablation studies and failure analyses, which are crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness and limitations.
- The methodology is well-explained, and the paper includes detailed experimental setups and results, which are supported by extensive ablation studies and failure analyses.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of natural language processing, specifically the use of free-text rationales (FTRs) to improve the performance of language models (LMs) on reasoning tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comparison with more recent and powerful models like GPT-3.5 and GPT-4, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The evaluation is limited to two QA datasets, which may not fully represent the generalizability of the method across different domains or tasks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the failure cases or the specific reasons for the observed performance improvements, which could enhance the understanding of the method's effectiveness.
- The method's reliance on FTRs, which are known to be noisy and unreliable, could limit its applicability and robustness.
- The paper does not address the scalability of the method to larger datasets or more complex tasks, which could limit its practical utility.
- The method's effectiveness in handling different types of FTRs, such as those generated by different models or in different domains, is not thoroughly explored.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify why the student LM is not initialized with the teacher's parameters, especially when both models use the same encoder-decoder architecture?
- How does the paper ensure that the FTRs used for training are of high quality and not noisy or misleading?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the method's performance on specific types of questions or tasks?
- How does the method handle different types of FTRs, such as those generated by different models or in different domains?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of the method's reliance on FTRs on its scalability and applicability to larger or more complex tasks?
- How does the method compare to other knowledge distillation methods that use different types of knowledge or different distillation techniques?
