 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Annealing Self-Distillation Rectification (ADR) aimed at enhancing the robustness of adversarial training by rectifying labels. This method uses a momentum encoder to generate soft labels, which are then interpolated with ground-truth labels to improve model robustness. The ADR method is designed to address the issue of robust overfitting by smoothing the label space, thereby reducing the gap between adversarial and non-adversarial data. The paper provides empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of ADR, showing improvements in robustness and generalization performance. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of novelty, as similar approaches have been previously explored, and for not providing sufficient theoretical backing for its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear and well-motivated analysis of the characteristics of robust models, specifically focusing on the calibration ability of robust models and their consistency in outputting similar probabilities for clean and adversarial data.
- The proposed method of using annealing self-distillation rectification (ADR) to rectify labels is novel and effective, as demonstrated by the empirical results which show improvements in robust accuracy and standard accuracy.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides detailed empirical results that support the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The methodology of ADR is simple yet effective, and the paper provides a clear explanation of the intuition behind the method.
- The paper is well-organized and includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as similar approaches have been previously explored, such as using annealing schedules for interpolation parameters and self-distillation techniques.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis to support the claims made in the paper, particularly regarding the consistency of robust models in outputting similar probabilities for clean and adversarial data.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the hyperparameters used in the experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to certain datasets and model architectures, and the paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method to larger datasets or more complex models.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and robustness against different types of adversarial attacks.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims made about the consistency of robust models in outputting similar probabilities for clean and adversarial data.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameters used in the experiments, particularly how these parameters were chosen and their impact on the results?
- How does the proposed method perform against different types of adversarial attacks, and what are the computational costs associated with using this method?
- Could the authors provide more evidence to support the claim that robust models maintain output consistency on benign data and its adversarial counterpart?
- In terms of the consistency of robust models, how does the paper define and measure the consistency, and what are the implications of this consistency for the robustness of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of its effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and methodology, particularly in the appendices where additional information is provided?
