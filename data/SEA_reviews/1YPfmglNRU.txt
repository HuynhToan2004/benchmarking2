 **Summary:**
The paper explores the concept of expertise in treatment effect estimation, specifically focusing on the types of expertise that decision-makers can exhibit: predictive and prognostic. It introduces measures to quantify these types of expertise and proposes a method for estimating treatment effects based on the type of expertise present in a dataset. The authors argue that different types of expertise can lead to different performance outcomes in treatment effect estimation, and propose a method for selecting appropriate estimation methods based on the type of expertise. The paper is supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, including a simulation study that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited applicability to real-world datasets, its reliance on assumptions that may not hold in practical scenarios, and the lack of a rigorous comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear definitions and discussions of the two types of expertise.
- The concept of expertise is clearly defined and used to inform model selection, which is a novel approach.
- The paper includes both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support its claims, including a simulation study that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important problem in the field of treatment effect estimation.
- The proposed method for selecting appropriate estimation methods based on the type of expertise present in a dataset is a useful contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions, such as the assumption of no hidden confounding and the assumption of a static treatment effect, may not hold in real-world scenarios, limiting the applicability of the proposed method.
- The simulation study is limited in scope and does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed approach.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not address how the method performs in scenarios where the assumptions do not hold.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the relationship between the two types of expertise and does not compare the proposed method with existing methods in terms of computational efficiency or scalability.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the proposed method can be applied to real-world datasets, which is a significant limitation for practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the simulation study and discuss how these assumptions affect the validity of the results?
- How does the proposed method perform when the assumptions do not hold, and what are the implications of this for the practical applicability of the method?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the type of expertise is not clearly defined or where there is a mix of different types of expertise?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the relationship between the two types of expertise and how they might interact in real-world scenarios?
- How can the proposed method be applied to real-world datasets, and what are the challenges associated with this application?
