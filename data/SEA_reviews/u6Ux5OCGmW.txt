 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Riemannian Sharpness-Aware Minimization (RSAM), which extends the Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) framework to Riemannian manifolds. The authors propose a new notion of sharpness on manifolds, supported by a theoretical analysis that establishes a tighter bound on the generalization gap compared to existing bounds. The RSAM method is evaluated on various tasks and datasets, demonstrating its ability to enhance generalization capabilities. Despite its theoretical contributions, the paper has been critiqued for its limited empirical evaluation and lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to generalize the SAM optimizer to Riemannian manifolds.
- The proposed method, RSAM, is supported by a theoretical analysis that characterizes generalization capabilities with respect to manifold sharpness, providing a tighter bound on the generalization gap.
- The paper includes a comprehensive experimental evaluation that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method across various settings, including supervised and self-supervised learning, and different datasets and models.
- The introduction of a new notion of sharpness on Riemannian manifolds, backed by a theoretical analysis, is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The empirical evaluation of the proposed method is limited, with only a few datasets used, and the comparisons with existing methods are not comprehensive.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts.
- The practical applicability of the method is questioned, as it requires the knowledge of the manifold structure and retraction operations, which might not be available or practical for all types of manifolds.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples, particularly in the sections where the mathematical notations are complex.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to existing methods like SAM.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed discussions on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts?
- Could the authors provide more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, including more datasets and different types of neural networks?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and practical applicability to existing methods?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations and examples to help clarify the complex mathematical notations used in the paper?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of neural networks beyond CNNs, and what are the implications for the generalization gap?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to existing methods like SAM?
