 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Hebbian learning-based orthogonal projection (HLOP) method for continual learning in spiking neural networks (SNNs). This approach leverages Hebbian learning to extract the principal subspace of neural activities, facilitating orthogonal projection of activity traces into an orthogonal subspace, thus preventing synaptic weight updates from interfering with old tasks. The method is demonstrated to be effective in continual learning scenarios, showing significant improvements over baseline methods across various datasets and network architectures. The authors provide a detailed explanation of the methodology and its implementation, along with a thorough evaluation that highlights the method's efficacy in reducing forgetting in SNNs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly explained with sufficient details.
- The proposed method, Hebbian learning based orthogonal projection (HLOP), is novel and shows significant improvements in continual learning scenarios, particularly in reducing forgetting in spiking neural networks (SNNs).
- The paper includes a thorough evaluation of HLOP across various datasets and network architectures, demonstrating its effectiveness in continual learning.
- The method is well-motivated, with clear explanations of the rationale behind the method and its application in neuromorphic computing systems.
- The paper provides a detailed explanation of the methodology, including a clear description of the Hebbian learning-based orthogonal projection and its implementation using skew-symmetric synaptic weights.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other continual learning methods, particularly those specifically designed for SNNs, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The method's effectiveness might be limited by the complexity of the tasks and the size of the datasets used in the experiments. The paper could benefit from evaluations on more complex tasks and larger datasets to demonstrate its scalability.
- The paper does not discuss the biological plausibility of the method, which could be a significant limitation for some readers.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential drawbacks of the proposed method, which would provide a more balanced view of its strengths and weaknesses.
- The paper could be improved by providing more detailed experimental results and a more comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods and different network architectures.
