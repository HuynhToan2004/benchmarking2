 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel decentralized machine learning algorithm named Malcom-PSGD, which integrates gradient compression, model sparsification, and dithering-based quantization to enhance communication efficiency in decentralized learning. The algorithm leverages proximal stochastic gradient descent to manage the non-smoothness induced by L1 regularization in model sparsification. Theoretical analysis and numerical experiments demonstrate the algorithm's ability to reduce communication costs by approximately 75% compared to state-of-the-art methods. However, concerns are raised about the clarity and depth of the theoretical analysis, the practical implications of the assumptions, and the empirical evidence supporting the claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel decentralized machine learning algorithm that integrates gradient compression, model sparsification, and dithering-based quantization, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, providing a comprehensive understanding of the algorithm's behavior and performance.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed experimental setup that supports the theoretical claims.
- The proposed algorithm is innovative, combining various techniques to address the challenges of decentralized learning, particularly in terms of communication efficiency.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and good illustrations that aid in understanding the complex concepts presented.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the communication costs and the impact of different network topologies on these costs.
- There is a lack of clarity in the theoretical analysis, particularly in the convergence analysis and the proofs, which could benefit from additional explanations or proofs in the main text.
- The paper assumes that the model parameters are bounded, which may not hold in practical scenarios, and the implications of this assumption are not adequately discussed.
- The paper does not adequately compare its results with existing methods, particularly in terms of communication efficiency and convergence rates.
- The experimental setup is not sufficiently detailed, and the results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithm over existing methods.
- There is a lack of discussion on the practical implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the boundedness of the model parameters and the convergence rates.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the communication costs and how they vary with different network topologies?
- How does the proposed algorithm perform in scenarios where the model parameters are not bounded?
- Can you provide a more detailed comparison of your results with existing methods, particularly in terms of communication efficiency and convergence rates?
- How do the theoretical results compare with practical scenarios, and what are the implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis?
- Could you elaborate on the proofs and assumptions used in the convergence analysis, particularly the proof of Theorem 2 and the assumptions regarding the boundedness of the model parameters?
- How do the experimental results support the claims made about the superiority of the proposed algorithm over existing methods?
