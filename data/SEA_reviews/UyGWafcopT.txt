 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for extracting meaning representations from autoregressive language models by considering the distribution of all possible trajectories extending an input text. This approach, which is prompt-free and does not require fine-tuning, utilizes a distributional perspective on semantics and connects it to standard constructions in automata theory. The methodology is applied to various benchmarks such as the STS-B dataset, demonstrating competitive performance against zero-shot and prompt-free baselines. The paper also discusses the limitations of the approach, including computational cost and the need for more sophisticated prompts to improve performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to extracting meaning representations from autoregressive language models, which is prompt-free and does not require fine-tuning.
- The method is well-motivated and supported by theoretical analysis, providing a new perspective on the use of autoregressive models for semantic similarity tasks.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and experimental results.
- The method shows promising results in various benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness in extracting meaning representations from text.
- The paper is original in its application of the distributional hypothesis to autoregressive models, which is a significant advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the computational cost and the need for more sophisticated prompts to improve performance.
- The method's performance on semantic search tasks is not discussed, which is a significant limitation given the potential applications of the method in this area.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other methods, especially those that use similar approaches but differ in their implementation or results.
- The method's reliance on a single model (Falcon) for all experiments raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.
- The paper could be improved by providing more detailed experimental results, such as the performance of the method on different models and datasets, and the computational cost of the method.
