 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of the number of demonstrations on In-Context Learning (ICL) performance, challenging the common belief that more demonstrations lead to better performance. The authors propose a novel method, D2Controller, which dynamically adjusts the number of demonstrations based on the dataset and model, improving performance by up to 5.4%. The method is validated across various LLMs and datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and applicability to different ICL models. The paper also introduces a new metric, IICScore, to guide the selection of evaluation examples, enhancing the method's practicality and efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper challenges the common belief that more demonstrations lead to better performance in In-Context Learning (ICL), providing a fresh perspective on the topic.
- The proposed D2Controller method is simple, efficient, and easy to implement, making it accessible and practical for various applications.
- The method is evaluated across multiple large language models (LLMs) and datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with a clear presentation of results and methodology.
- The introduction of the IICScore metric is innovative and helpful in guiding the selection of evaluation examples, enhancing the method's accuracy and reliability.
- The paper's findings are significant as they challenge the conventional wisdom in ICL, potentially leading to new research directions and improvements in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the computational cost and time efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The motivation behind the proposed method is not clearly articulated, making it difficult to understand why the method was developed and how it addresses specific issues in ICL.
- The method's performance improvements are not consistently strong across all datasets and LLMs, with some showing only marginal improvements or even decreases in performance.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical or empirical evidence to support the claims made about the relationship between the number of demonstrations and model performance.
- There is a lack of comparison with other existing methods, such as those that use different metrics or approaches for demonstration selection and ordering.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed information on the computational cost and time efficiency of the proposed method?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of ICL models, such as those designed for question answering or natural language generation?
- Can the authors elaborate on the motivation behind the proposed method and how it addresses specific issues in ICL?
- How does the method compare to other existing methods for demonstration selection and ordering in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more insights into the factors that influence the performance of the method, such as the size and complexity of the datasets and LLMs used?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the optimal number of demonstrations varies significantly across different datasets and models?
