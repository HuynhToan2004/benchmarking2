 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for representing complex human-scene interaction data using sparse mask representation, which aims to enhance the efficiency of neural network processing by reducing redundant information. This approach is applied to various downstream tasks such as contact prediction and scene synthesis, demonstrating improvements in both accuracy and inference speed. The method involves defining a sparse mask to select important information from the input, which is then integrated into the deep backbone using sparse operations. The paper also discusses the challenges of balancing accuracy and inference speed, and provides a user study to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, the clarity of the experimental setup, and the potential impact of the method on broader applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for representing complex human-scene interaction data using sparse mask representation, which effectively reduces redundant information and improves the efficiency of neural network processing.
- The method is demonstrated to be effective across various downstream tasks, including contact prediction and scene synthesis, showing improvements in both accuracy and inference speed.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, providing a clear explanation of the methodology and experimental results.
- The use of sparse masks to select important information from the input data is a simple yet effective method for representing high-dimensional human-scene interaction data.
- The paper includes a user study to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method, which adds a practical dimension to the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the sparse mask representation is applied to different network layers during training and testing, which could affect the reproducibility and understanding of the method.
- The novelty of the method is limited as it primarily focuses on using sparse masks to select important information from the input data, which has been previously explored in other works.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, particularly the challenges in balancing accuracy and inference speed and the potential impact of the method on broader applications.
- The experimental setup and results are not clearly presented, with some results being unclear or confusing, and the paper lacks a detailed discussion on the training and testing processes.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation of the method's effectiveness on different datasets and tasks to validate its generalizability and robustness.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for any new technology.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the sparse mask representation is applied to different network layers during training and testing?
- How does the method handle the selection of sparse masks during inference, and what criteria are used to determine which masks to discard?
- In the user study, how were the participants selected and what were the criteria for evaluating the naturalness and non-collision of the generated objects?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the training and testing processes, to improve the reproducibility and understanding of the results?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods in terms of accuracy and inference speed, especially in more complex or varied scenarios?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations and negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how these might be addressed or mitigated?
