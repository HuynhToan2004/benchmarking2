 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the sample complexity of learning in partially observable Markov decision processes (POMDPs) with partial online state information (OSI), where the agent can actively query a subset of state features. The authors establish a lower bound for POMDPs without full OSI, showing that achieving an epsilon-optimal policy requires exponential sample complexity. They also identify tractable classes of POMDPs with partial OSI, where they design near-optimal algorithms with polynomial regret. The paper provides theoretical insights into the hardness of POMDPs without full OSI and proposes algorithms for two tractable classes of POMDPs with partial OSI.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly explains the problem setup, making it accessible to readers.
- The motivation behind the study is clear, and the authors provide a novel lower bound for POMDPs without full OSI, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper introduces two novel tractable classes of POMDPs with partial OSI, which are supported by near-optimal algorithms with polynomial regret.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, and the paper provides a clear structure and logical flow, making it easy to follow.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of POMDPs, which is of significant interest to the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithms, which is crucial for understanding their applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The proof of Theorem 1 is not self-contained, and the assumptions made in the proof are not adequately justified, which could lead to confusion and misunderstanding of the results.
- The paper does not adequately compare its results with existing works, such as those by Chen et al. (2022a), which could help in understanding the novelty and significance of the contributions.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the algorithms, which is a critical aspect for practical implementation.
- There are several minor typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the proof of Theorem 1 and provide a more detailed explanation of how these assumptions affect the results?
- How do the proposed algorithms compare in terms of computational complexity and practical applicability with existing methods, such as those by Chen et al. (2022a)?
- Can the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed algorithms and discuss potential scenarios where they may not perform optimally?
- How does the paper address the computational complexity of the algorithms, especially in scenarios with large state spaces?
- Could the authors elaborate on the differences between their approach and existing works, such as those by Liu et al. (2022), and discuss the implications of these differences for the practical applicability of the results?
