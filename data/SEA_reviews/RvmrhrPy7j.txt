 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to causal inference using Large Language Models (LLMs) to infer causal orders, which are then utilized to construct backdoor adjustment sets. The method leverages LLMs to determine causal orders based on domain expert knowledge, aiming to improve the accuracy of causal effect estimation. The approach is evaluated using benchmark datasets, showing that it outperforms existing methods in terms of causal ordering accuracy. However, concerns were raised about the method's generalizability to larger datasets and its reliance on the accuracy of LLMs in providing causal orders.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel prompting strategy based on triplets, which allows for more accurate answers and avoids cycles when querying relationships between variables.
- It combines LLM-generated causal orders with existing causal discovery algorithms, demonstrating the utility of LLMs in enhancing the performance of these algorithms.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical flow that makes it accessible to readers.
- The use of LLMs to infer causal orders is a significant contribution to the field of causal inference, as it provides a novel approach to utilizing LLMs for this purpose.
- The paper includes comprehensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, showing that LLM-based methods can outperform existing methods in certain scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's methodology relies heavily on the accuracy of LLMs in providing causal orders, which may not always be reliable, especially for larger, more complex datasets.
- The generalizability of the method to larger datasets is unclear, as the experiments were primarily conducted on small datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential limitations or ethical considerations of using LLMs for causal inference, such as the potential for biased or inaccurate results.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to small datasets, which may not adequately represent the performance of the method on larger, more complex datasets.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and potential future research directions.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform on larger, more complex datasets?
- How accurate are LLMs in providing causal orders, and what are the implications of using these orders for causal inference?
- Could you provide more details on how the LLMs are trained, particularly if they are trained on the same datasets used for evaluation?
- How do the authors address potential biases or inaccuracies in the LLM-generated causal orders, and what measures are in place to ensure the reliability of these orders?
- Could you discuss the potential ethical considerations of using LLMs for causal inference, such as the potential for biased or inaccurate results?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
