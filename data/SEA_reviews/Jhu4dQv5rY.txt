 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for contextual biasing in Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) systems using the Knuth-Morris-Pratt (KMP) algorithm for pattern matching. This approach aims to enhance the recognition of rare entities by biasing the search towards specific phrases without modifying the underlying model. The method is designed to be efficient and can be integrated into existing ASR systems, including those using Tensor Processing Units (TPUs), without requiring additional model parameters. The paper provides empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness through experiments on various datasets, demonstrating improvements in Word Error Rate (WER) reduction.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for contextual biasing in Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) systems using the KMP algorithm, which is a simple yet effective approach.
- The method is well-motivated and well-written, with clear explanations of the KMP algorithm and its application to ASR.
- The paper includes comprehensive empirical evaluations, demonstrating significant improvements in Word Error Rate (WER) reduction across various datasets.
- The method is efficient, with a time complexity of O(γB), which makes it suitable for real-time applications.
- The method is simple to implement and can be easily integrated into existing ASR systems, making it a practical solution for improving ASR performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to other sequence transduction tasks and its performance on out-of-domain data.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, which makes it difficult to assess the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the method, which is crucial for understanding its efficiency in practical scenarios.
- The method's reliance on specific training data and the need for extensive preprocessing of biasing phrases could limit its generalizability and applicability to other domains or datasets.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in certain applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be adapted to other sequence transduction tasks, such as machine translation or speech recognition in other languages?
- How does the method perform on out-of-domain data, and what are the implications of using different training datasets?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the method, including a comparison with other methods in terms of time and memory efficiency?
- How does the method handle the recognition of prefixes, and what are the implications of using different prefixes in different contexts?
- Can the method be integrated with other biasing methods, such as those based on language models or attention mechanisms, to further enhance its performance?
- How does the method handle the recognition of rare or unseen entities that are not part of the training data, and what are the implications of using different biasing phrases or different datasets for training and testing?
