 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TEDDY, a novel one-shot edge sparsification method for graph neural networks (GNNs) that utilizes edge-degree information to prune edges and parameters. TEDDY leverages the importance of low-degree edges and the spectral stability of the graph to optimize sparsity and performance. The method is tested on various datasets and shows significant improvements in generalization performance compared to iterative approaches. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings and practical implications of TEDDY, including its ability to maintain graph structure and node features.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel methodology for edge pruning in GNNs that leverages the importance of low-degree edges and the spectral stability of the graph.
- The proposed TEDDY method achieves state-of-the-art performance in terms of generalization performance compared to iterative approaches.
- The paper is well-written, making the proposed method clear and easy to follow.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that TEDDY can maintain graph structure and node features.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis that supports the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the time complexity of the proposed method, which could affect its scalability to larger datasets or more complex models.
- There is a lack of comparison with other existing methods that also use low-degree edges for pruning, which could provide a better understanding of TEDDY's advantages or disadvantages.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential areas for future research.
- The paper could be improved by providing more detailed experimental results, such as the average degrees of pruned edges and the performance of the proposed method on other types of datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the time complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the dataset or the complexity of the model?
- How does TEDDY compare to other methods that use low-degree edges for pruning in terms of performance and computational cost?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, such as the average degrees of pruned edges and the performance of the proposed method on other types of datasets?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of over-smoothing in GNNs, and what are the implications of this for the performance and generalization of the model?
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential areas for future research. Can the authors provide more insights into these areas?
