 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a model-free algorithm for learning an epsilon-optimal Nash equilibrium in two-player zero-sum Markov games. It employs a min-gap based reference-advantage decomposition to achieve sample complexity that matches the lower bound, which is a significant improvement over previous model-free algorithms. The algorithm leverages a variance reduction technique and a CCE oracle to update optimistic and pessimistic value functions, ensuring the monotonicity property is preserved. The theoretical analysis demonstrates the algorithm's effectiveness and optimality in terms of sample complexity. Despite its theoretical strength, the paper lacks empirical validation, which could help in understanding the practical implications and limitations of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the algorithm and its design, making it accessible to readers.
- The algorithm is novel, utilizing a min-gap based reference-advantage decomposition technique which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis is solid, demonstrating the optimality of the proposed algorithm in terms of sample complexity, which matches the lower bound.
- The paper provides a detailed proof sketch and a schematic algorithm, which aid in understanding the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method.
- The algorithm's design and analysis are novel, particularly the use of min-gap based reference-advantage decomposition, which is a new approach in the context of multi-agent reinforcement learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical validation, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications and limitations of the proposed method.
- There is a potential issue with the horizon dependence of the sample complexity, which could be improved to match the lower bound more closely.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing works, particularly in terms of the algorithm's novelty and its contributions to the field.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its approach, which could provide a more balanced view of the proposed method's capabilities and challenges.
- The notation used in the paper is sometimes confusing, which could hinder understanding for readers unfamiliar with the specific terminology used.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the empirical validation of the proposed method? How does it perform in practical scenarios?
- How does the algorithm compare to other existing methods in terms of sample complexity and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors elaborate on the choice of the min-gap based reference-advantage decomposition and discuss its implications for the overall performance of the algorithm?
- What are the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how might these affect its applicability in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors clarify the notation used in the paper, particularly in the proofs and the algorithm descriptions, to make the content more accessible to a broader audience?
