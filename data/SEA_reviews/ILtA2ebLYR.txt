 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for interactive preference learning in multi-objective optimization problems, leveraging an active dueling bandit algorithm to facilitate direct preference elicitation from users. This framework, termed RUCB-AL, integrates active learning with dueling bandits to manage the sampling and consultation budgets effectively. The methodology is evaluated through a series of experiments, including traditional benchmark problems and real-world applications like protein structure prediction. The paper claims that RUCB-AL outperforms traditional methods by reducing the need for explicit fitness function calculations and providing a more direct user preference-based approach. However, the paper suffers from several issues including unclear writing, lack of novelty, and insufficient experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework for interactive preference-based multi-objective optimization, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The integration of active learning with dueling bandits is innovative and provides a new approach to managing sampling and consultation budgets.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over traditional methods in various benchmark problems.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is well-motivated and theoretically supported, with a clear structure that makes it easy to understand.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from unclear writing and lacks detailed explanations in some sections, making it difficult to follow.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive literature review and comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have strengthened its position in the field.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, with a limited number of benchmark problems and a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant methods.
- There are several typographical errors and inconsistencies in the paper that need to be corrected.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed method over traditional methods are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the Copeland score in the proposed method, especially in scenarios where it might not be applicable?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational complexity, particularly in scenarios with a large number of solutions or high-dimensional objective spaces?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other active learning methods and discuss how their approach differs or improves upon existing methods?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over traditional fitness-based methods in practical applications, especially in scenarios where the fitness function is not readily available?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with a small number of solutions or in high-dimensional objective spaces, where traditional methods might struggle?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of benchmark problems and the rationale behind their selection?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential inconsistencies or noisy feedback from users in the consultation module?
