 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Federated Orthogonal Training (FOT) to address the Global Catastrophic Forgetting (GCF) in Continual Federated Learning (CFL). FOT modifies the aggregated updates of new tasks to be orthogonal to the global principal subspace of old tasks, thereby mitigating interference between tasks. The method is evaluated across multiple datasets, showing superior performance over existing methods in terms of accuracy and forgetting rate. Despite its effectiveness, the paper is critiqued for its assumptions, such as the availability of task boundaries and the privacy implications of the Global Principal Subspace Extraction (GPSE) process. The paper also discusses the practical implications of the method in terms of communication and computational costs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and practical problem of Global Catastrophic Forgetting (GCF) in Continual Federated Learning (CFL), which is crucial for the application of federated learning in real-world scenarios.
- The proposed method, Federated Orthogonal Training (FOT), is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated, showing superior performance over existing methods in terms of accuracy and forgetting rate.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear problem formulation and a detailed explanation of the proposed method.
- The paper includes extensive experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method and providing insights into its limitations.
- The paper addresses privacy concerns by using secure aggregation and differential privacy, ensuring that the server learns nothing about individual clients' data.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that task boundaries are known in advance, which might not be practical in real-world scenarios where tasks can arrive dynamically.
- The method's dependence on the availability of task boundaries and the need for communication rounds to extract global principal subspace information could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not compare its method with some relevant works, such as those using generative models to prevent forgetting, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the privacy implications of the Global Principal Subspace Extraction (GPSE) process, which might leak information about the data distribution.
- The paper's claims about the method's resistance to data heterogeneity are not adequately supported, and the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate superior performance across different data distributions.
- The paper does not discuss the computational and communication costs of the proposed method, which could be a significant drawback in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the privacy implications of the Global Principal Subspace Extraction (GPSE) process?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods that use generative models to prevent forgetting in terms of computational and communication costs?
- Could the authors provide more insights into the practical applicability of the method in scenarios where task boundaries are not known in advance?
- How does the method handle the trade-off between forgetting and the number of tasks to learn, especially in scenarios where the threshold value needs to be optimized?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational and communication costs of the proposed method, especially in comparison to other methods that might have similar or better performance?
