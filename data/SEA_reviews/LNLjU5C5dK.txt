 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FIGA, a novel approach to aligning large language models (LLMs) with human preferences using a dataset of initial responses and their revised versions. This method leverages a reward model to identify instances where the LLM's initial response needs improvement, then uses these instances to fine-tune the model, focusing on specific tokens in the revised responses. The paper also proposes a new loss function that utilizes these fine-grained quality signals to guide the learning process. Experimental results demonstrate that FIGA outperforms several baselines in terms of reward scores and human alignment benchmarks. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, the clarity of its positioning within existing methods, and the potential for overfitting due to the small size of the training dataset.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method, FIGA, is innovative and shows strong performance in aligning LLMs with human preferences.
- The dataset construction and the use of a reward model to filter instances are well-motivated and contribute to the effectiveness of the method.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the superiority of FIGA over several baselines in terms of reward scores and human alignment benchmarks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including an ablation study on different components and hyper-parameters.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that use similar techniques such as the DeBERTa reward model and the filtering based on reward scores.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experiments, including a broader range of benchmarks and a comparison with other recent alignment methods.
- There are concerns about the potential for overfitting due to the small size of the training dataset.
- The paper could provide more detailed explanations and examples of the revised responses and the filtering process to enhance clarity and understanding.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the construction of the revised responses and the filtering process, including examples and a detailed description in the main text?
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent alignment methods, particularly those that use similar techniques such as the DeBERTa reward model and the filtering based on reward scores?
- Could the authors clarify the positioning of FIGA within existing methods and discuss how it differs from or improves upon these methods?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting due to the small size of the training dataset?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, such as the number of instances used in the training and testing phases, and the specifics of the reward model used?
