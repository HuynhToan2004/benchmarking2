 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SIRO, a sensitivity-informed regularizer designed to enhance the performance of offline optimizers by regulating the sensitivity of surrogate models. SIRO is model-agnostic and applicable to various existing offline optimizers, leveraging a sensitivity measurement to optimize model performance. The methodology involves a regularizer that encourages the surrogate model to be less sensitive to perturbations, which is crucial for reliable predictions in out-of-distribution scenarios. The paper presents a novel approach by integrating a sensitivity-informed regularizer into existing offline optimizers, aiming to improve performance across diverse benchmarks. Despite its innovative approach, concerns are raised regarding the computational cost, the robustness of the sensitivity measure, and the clarity of the presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The proposed method is novel and has potential for broad application across different offline optimization algorithms.
- The paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments, including a thorough ablation study, and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of a sensitivity-informed regularizer is a novel approach that could be beneficial for offline optimization, particularly in scenarios where surrogate models are used to predict outputs beyond their training data.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear problem setting and a logical flow of ideas, making it accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational cost of the proposed method, which could be a significant drawback for practical applications.
- The sensitivity measure used in the paper is not well-defined and may not accurately reflect the sensitivity of the surrogate model, especially in scenarios where the model's sensitivity is not directly related to the perturbation of its parameters.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the robustness of the sensitivity measure against different types of perturbations, such as noise in the data or variations in the model architecture.
- There is a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could help in understanding the relative performance of the proposed method.
- The paper's presentation could be improved to better highlight the novelty and contributions of the work.
- The method's effectiveness in scenarios where the surrogate model is not well-calibrated or when the training data is limited is not discussed, which could limit the practical applicability of the method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the computational cost of the proposed method and how it compares to other offline optimization methods?
- How does the sensitivity measure used in the paper relate to the actual sensitivity of the surrogate model, and can the authors provide more intuitive examples to illustrate its application?
- What are the implications of using a sensitivity measure that is not directly related to the perturbation of the model's parameters?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the surrogate model is not well-calibrated or when the training data is limited?
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the proposed method and existing methods, such as those that use differentiable neural networks to model the posterior distribution of Bayesian optimization?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, and how does it compare in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
