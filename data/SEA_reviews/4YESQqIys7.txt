 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel neural network architecture, NfgTransformer, designed to represent normal-form games (NFGs) and solve various game-theoretic tasks such as equilibrium-solving, deviation gain estimation, and ranking. The architecture is equivariant to permutations of players and actions, enabling it to handle different game sizes effectively. The NfgTransformer is evaluated against existing methods on both synthetic and real-world games, demonstrating its superior performance. The authors claim that their approach offers a flexible and interpretable framework for learning representations of NFGs, which could potentially be applied in real-world scenarios. However, concerns are raised about the scalability of the model to larger games and the generalizability of the results to more complex or diverse game scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel neural network architecture, NfgTransformer, specifically designed for normal-form games (NFGs), which can be used for various game-theoretic tasks such as equilibrium-solving, deviation gain estimation, and ranking.
- The architecture is equivariant to permutations of players and actions, making it versatile and applicable to different game sizes.
- The model is parameter-efficient and can be integrated into deep learning systems for real-world applications.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with extensive experiments and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The methodology is innovative, using self-attention to model the interaction between actions in a game, which is a novel approach in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the equivariance of the NfgTransformer and how it is achieved, which could confuse readers.
- The scalability of the model to larger games is questionable, as the performance on larger games (e.g., 16x16x16) is not as good as on smaller games.
- The generalizability of the results is limited, as the experiments were conducted on only one instance of each game type, which might not adequately represent the performance across different instances.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of games and the scalability to larger games.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the related work and a clearer exposition of the methodology, especially the role of the action-to-action self-attention in the model.

**Questions:**
- How does the NfgTransformer ensure equivariance to permutations of players and actions? Can you provide a clearer explanation of this equivariance?
- What is the role of the action-to-action self-attention in the model, and how does it influence the performance of the model?
- How does the model perform on different instances of the same game type, and what are the implications of this variability in performance?
- Can you discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and generalizability to different types of games?
- How does the model handle the learning of different strategies, and what are the implications of this learning for the model's performance and interpretability?
