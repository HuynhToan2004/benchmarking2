 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel evolutionary algorithm, SA-SHADE-tri-ensin, designed to optimize the weights of pre-trained neural networks (NNs) using backpropagation (BP) and evolutionary algorithms (EAs). This algorithm combines SADE and SHADE variants of DE with trigonometric and sinusoidal mutation strategies to enhance the performance of pre-trained models on large-scale datasets like ImageNet. The methodology involves initializing the population with pre-trained models and iteratively evolving the weights using BP and EA, demonstrating potential improvements over traditional methods like weight averaging and ensembling. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of novelty, insufficient comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, and inadequate experimental validation, particularly concerning the generalizability of the proposed method across different network architectures and datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by combining backpropagation (BP) and evolutionary algorithms (EAs) to optimize pre-trained neural networks, which is a promising direction for improving neural network training.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with a clear description of the experimental setup, including the use of a large-scale dataset (ImageNet) and a comprehensive ablation study that supports the design choices.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method's performance, including the evolution of population diversity and the impact of hyperparameters, which adds depth to the understanding of the proposed method.
- The integration of pre-trained models with evolutionary algorithms is an innovative approach that could potentially improve the training of deep neural networks.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a technical paper.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency. This makes it difficult to gauge the true effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable as it primarily combines existing evolutionary algorithms without significant modifications or innovations.
- The paper suffers from poor writing and presentation, with unclear descriptions and a lack of clarity in the methodology, which could confuse readers and detract from the paper's impact.
- The paper's experimental setup is limited, focusing only on ResNet variants and not exploring other architectures or datasets, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed method over traditional methods like SGD are not convincingly supported, and the results presented do not consistently outperform other methods.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the hyperparameters and their impact on the performance, which is crucial for reproducibility and understanding the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the population initialization tricks, particularly in comparison to the pre-trained models provided by PyTorch?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency with state-of-the-art methods, especially when trained on different architectures and datasets?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the hyperparameters used and their impact on the performance of the proposed method?
- Given the limited scope of the experimental setup, how do the authors plan to expand the testing to include other architectures and datasets to validate the generalizability of their findings?
- Could the authors elaborate on the method's ability to handle different types of neural networks and datasets beyond the ResNet variants and ImageNet, and discuss any potential challenges or limitations?
