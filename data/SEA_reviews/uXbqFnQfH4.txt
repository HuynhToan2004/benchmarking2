 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multi-objective optimization framework, Multi-objective multi-solution Transport (MosT), which addresses the challenge of optimizing multiple objectives with limited solutions. MosT formulates the problem as a bi-level optimization of weighted objectives, using optimal transport between objectives and solutions to ensure diverse trade-offs. The framework is designed to handle scenarios where the number of objectives outnumbers the number of solutions, providing a method for balancing objectives and achieving Pareto optimality. MosT is evaluated on various benchmarks and real-world applications, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling complex, high-dimensional Pareto frontiers. The paper also includes theoretical analysis and empirical results, highlighting the framework's ability to outperform existing methods in terms of solution diversity and effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The MosT algorithm is novel and addresses the challenge of balancing multiple objectives when the number of objectives outnumbers the number of solutions.
- The paper includes a theoretical analysis of the MosT algorithm's convergence and provides empirical results demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The methodology is applicable to a wide range of machine learning problems, and the paper includes a variety of experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing the algorithm's effectiveness in diverse scenarios.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons with existing methods and a convergence analysis, which strengthens the credibility of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing multi-objective optimization methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the MosT algorithm's effectiveness.
- The theoretical analysis is limited, particularly in terms of the convergence rate and the specifics of the MosT algorithm's performance.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the MosT algorithm, which is crucial for understanding its scalability and applicability in large-scale problems.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the MosT algorithm over existing methods, particularly in scenarios where the number of objectives is limited.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical challenges of optimizing a large number of objectives, such as the computational complexity and the potential for suboptimal solutions.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the algorithm's implementation, especially in the experimental sections, to enhance reproducibility and understanding.
