 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the effectiveness of domain generalization (DG) algorithms compared to empirical risk minimization (ERM) in the presence of label noise. It provides a theoretical analysis of the conditions under which ERM fails to generalize due to spurious correlations and label noise, while DG algorithms show robustness. The study includes experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets, showing that DG algorithms can outperform ERM in certain scenarios but not consistently across all datasets. The paper discusses the implications of these findings for the practical application of DG algorithms and suggests that further research is needed to fully understand the conditions under which DG algorithms are beneficial.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely question regarding the effectiveness of domain generalization (DG) algorithms compared to empirical risk minimization (ERM) in the presence of label noise, which is a significant issue in practical applications.
- The theoretical analysis provided is insightful and offers a novel perspective on the relationship between spurious correlations and label noise in DG algorithms.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive and well-designed, including both synthetic and real-world datasets, which helps in validating the theoretical claims and providing practical implications.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations and visualizations that aid in understanding the complex concepts discussed.
- The inclusion of additional baselines and the comparison of different DG algorithms with ERM adds depth to the analysis and provides a more complete picture of the performance of these algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to linear models, which may not fully capture the complexities of real-world scenarios, especially in non-linear settings.
- The experimental results are not consistent across all datasets, with some showing no clear advantage of DG algorithms over ERM, which contradicts the theoretical expectations.
- The paper's focus on label noise as the primary source of failure in DG algorithms may not be the most critical factor, as other forms of noise or biases could also impact the performance.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the linear separability of invariant features and the strong correlation between spurious features and labels, may not hold in practical scenarios, limiting the applicability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of how the findings can be applied to improve the design of DG algorithms in real-world applications.
- The experimental setup and methodology are not clearly described, which could limit the reproducibility and transparency of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the paper's analysis extend to non-linear models, and what are the implications of this extension for the practical application of DG algorithms?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly regarding the linear separability of invariant features and the strong correlation between spurious features and labels?
- How do the experimental results compare when using different hyperparameters or training strategies for the DG algorithms, and what are the implications of these variations for the generalizability of the findings?
- In the context of the experimental results, can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the discrepancies observed between the theoretical expectations and the empirical results, especially in terms of the robustness of DG algorithms to label noise?
- How do the authors address the issue of spurious correlations in real-world scenarios, and what practical steps can be taken to mitigate their impact on the performance of DG algorithms?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical findings, particularly in terms of how the insights gained from this study can be applied to improve the design of DG algorithms in real-world applications?
