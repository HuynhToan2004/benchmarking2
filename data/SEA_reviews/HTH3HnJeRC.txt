 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the DER-Solomon benchmark, an extended dataset derived from the Solomon benchmark using a backward derivation method. This method aims to generate a large number of instances with similar characteristics to the Solomon benchmark, enhancing the dataset's size and utility for training and testing deep reinforcement learning (DRL) algorithms. The benchmark's distribution is analyzed to generate new instances, which are then used to train DRL algorithms and compare their performance with traditional methods. The paper claims that the DER-Solomon benchmark can significantly reduce computational time while maintaining solution quality, thus improving the efficiency of DRL algorithms. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the backward derivation method and the limited scope of the dataset, which might not adequately represent real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem by extending the Solomon benchmark, which is crucial for the field of combinatorial optimization.
- The proposed method for generating new instances is innovative and provides a novel approach to solving the CVRPTW problem.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and includes a comprehensive literature review, making it accessible and informative.
- The results demonstrate that the DER-Solomon benchmark can reduce computational time while maintaining solution quality, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper includes code and data, which enhances reproducibility and allows for further research and validation of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the backward derivation method is limited, as it is a common approach in generating synthetic datasets for machine learning.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and solution quality.
- The dataset's size and diversity are limited, which might not adequately represent real-world scenarios and could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of scalability, which is crucial for practical applications of the proposed methods.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and the potential negative societal impacts, which are essential for a complete evaluation of the proposed solutions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of the backward derivation method and how it specifically contributes to the generation of the DER-Solomon benchmark?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and solution quality with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in scenarios with larger and more complex datasets?
- Is there a plan to expand the dataset to include more diverse scenarios and to address the scalability issues?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and the potential negative societal impacts?
- In the context of the DER-Solomon benchmark, how do the authors ensure that the generated instances are not too similar to existing ones, which could limit the diversity and effectiveness of the dataset?
