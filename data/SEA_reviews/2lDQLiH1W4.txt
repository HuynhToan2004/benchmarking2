 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Instant3D, a method that generates 3D assets from text prompts using a two-stage approach. In the first stage, a sparse set of images is generated from text using a fine-tuned diffusion model. In the second stage, these images are used to reconstruct a NeRF using a transformer-based model. This approach allows for faster generation times (around 20 seconds) compared to traditional optimization-based methods, which can take hours. The method is evaluated on the Objaverse dataset and demonstrates improved performance over existing methods, although it has limitations in handling complex scenes and does not compare against all relevant baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear methodology.
- The proposed method achieves better performance than previous optimization-based methods and is faster than diffusion-based methods.
- The method is evaluated on the Objaverse dataset, showing improved performance over previous methods.
- The paper includes an ablation study that shows the importance of different components of the method.
- The method is simple and effective, with a novel approach to fine-tuning the diffusion model using a simple scorer and Gaussian blob initialization.
- The method is well-motivated and well-presented, with a clear explanation of the challenges addressed in 3D generation and the benefits of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method relies heavily on the quality and diversity of the training data, which may limit its applicability to more complex or varied scenes.
- The paper lacks comparisons with some relevant baselines, such as Shap-E and SparseNeUs, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The method's performance is limited by the quality and diversity of the training data, which may not generalize well to unseen objects or complex scenes.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method, such as the potential for inconsistencies in the generated images and the impact of the quality of the training data on the results.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the inference time with other methods, which could affect the perceived speed advantage of the proposed method.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the ethical and legal implications of the method, which could be important for understanding the broader impact of the technology.

**Questions:**
- How does the method handle complex scenes with more than four views, and what are the implications for the quality and consistency of the generated images?
- Can the method be adapted to handle more than four views in the first stage, and how would this affect the performance and inference time?
- How does the method compare in terms of inference time with other methods, especially when the number of diffusion steps is varied?
- How does the method handle the Janus problem, and what steps have been taken to address this issue?
- Can the method be adapted to handle different types of prompts, such as those that describe objects in different styles or with different attributes?
- What are the implications of using a pretrained ViT for image encoding, and how does this affect the performance and generalizability of the method?
- Can the method be adapted to handle other types of 3D representations, such as point clouds or volumetric representations?
