 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Fili, an unsupervised learning method for automatic program repair, focusing on fixing syntax errors. Fili utilizes a single model to learn from both correct and incorrect program examples, contrasting the BIFI method which employs two models (breaker and fixer). Fili's approach involves a curriculum learning strategy, where the complexity of errors is gradually increased during training. The methodology is evaluated on the Github-Python dataset, showing improved performance over BIFI. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty, as it primarily builds on existing methods, and for its lack of comprehensive experimental validation, particularly in terms of program correctness and real-world applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations and a logical flow that facilitates understanding.
- The proposed method, Fili, shows significant improvements over the state-of-the-art BIFI method in terms of performance and efficiency, particularly in its ability to handle a broader range of errors and its simplicity in implementation.
- The use of curriculum learning to fine-tune the fixer model is innovative and effective, demonstrating a novel approach to handling errors in a structured manner.
- The method's simplicity and the absence of complex architectures make it accessible and potentially easier to implement in practical scenarios.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear problem formulation and method description, and includes a comprehensive evaluation and ablation study.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough evaluation of the method's effectiveness in fixing real-world syntax errors, as the evaluation primarily focuses on parse rates and edit distances without considering the actual correctness of the repaired programs.
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as it primarily builds on existing methods like BIFI and does not introduce significant new technical contributions.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare Fili with other state-of-the-art methods, such as those using large language models (LLMs), which could provide a more comprehensive view of Fili's performance and relevance.
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the paper, such as incorrect grammar and formatting, which detract from its professional quality.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, particularly its applicability to more complex programming languages or larger datasets, which could impact its practical utility.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide examples or case studies of real-world syntax errors that Fili has successfully repaired? This would help validate the method's effectiveness in practical scenarios.
- How does Fili compare with other methods, particularly those using LLMs, in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can you clarify the methodology used in the evaluation, particularly the choice of metrics and the methodology for determining the edit-distance threshold?
- Is there a possibility to extend Fili to other programming languages or to larger datasets? What are the potential challenges and limitations in doing so?
- Could you elaborate on the method's performance when applied to more complex programming languages, such as Java or C++?
- How does Fili handle cases where the initial fixer model is trained on a different programming language or a different dataset? What are the implications of such differences in training data?
