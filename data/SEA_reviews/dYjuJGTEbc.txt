 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for graph partitioning using the Gromov-Wasserstein barycenter, aiming to address the limitations of existing methods like GWL and SpecGWL. The approach introduces a new optimization problem that avoids the need for predefined target distributions and structures, offering a more flexible and adaptable solution. The paper also presents a new algorithm that uses successive upper-bound minimization and regularization techniques to solve the non-convex problem, showing theoretical guarantees for convergence. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing favorably against existing methods. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of novelty, unclear theoretical contributions, and insufficient experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new optimization problem that does not require predefined target distributions and structures, offering a more flexible and adaptable solution.
- The proposed algorithm is based on successive upper-bound minimization and regularization techniques, which are theoretically guaranteed to converge to the extremal points of the objective function.
- The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the convergence of the proposed algorithm, which is a significant contribution.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a good balance between theoretical analysis and experimental results.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets, showing that it can outperform existing methods in graph partitioning tasks.
- The methodology is clearly explained, and the paper includes a thorough literature review, highlighting the connections between the proposed method and existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the intuition behind the proposed optimization problem, which could be confusing for readers.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the computational cost and the size of the datasets that can be handled.
- There is a lack of novelty in the method, as it builds upon existing methods like srGW and SpecGWL, and the theoretical contributions are not clearly articulated.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have strengthened the paper's claims.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could be critical for understanding its applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed or convincing, particularly in terms of the choice of parameters and the clarity of the experimental setup.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the intuition behind the proposed optimization problem and how it addresses the limitations of existing methods?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, particularly in terms of computational cost and dataset size?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, including a discussion on the method's advantages and disadvantages compared to these methods?
- How does the proposed method perform when the number of clusters is not predefined or when the data is noisy?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
- In the experimental results, how were the hyperparameters chosen, and how do these choices affect the performance of the method?
