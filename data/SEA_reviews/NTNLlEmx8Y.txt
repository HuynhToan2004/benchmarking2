 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for learning input-dependent levels of symmetries from data without labels, focusing on the continuous group of rotations SO(2). It proposes a self-supervised learning approach that infers the distribution of symmetries in data and generates pseudo-labels to learn the levels of symmetry of each input. The method aims to improve the generalization and robustness of non-equivariant models by predicting the symmetry levels of unseen objects and detecting out-of-distribution symmetries. It is evaluated on both real and synthetic datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in various scenarios. However, the method's applicability to more complex symmetry distributions and real-world datasets remains a concern.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method to learn input-dependent levels of symmetries from data without labels, which is a significant advancement in the field of symmetry detection and equivariant learning.
- The method is well-motivated, with a clear and well-written paper that effectively communicates the key ideas and methodology.
- The proposed method is evaluated on both real and synthetic datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in various scenarios and its potential to improve the performance of non-equivariant models.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow and understand, and includes detailed explanations of the methodology and experimental results.
- The theoretical contributions, particularly the derivation of sufficient and necessary conditions to learn the distribution of symmetries in the data, are significant and well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability more clearly.
- The method's reliance on the uniformity of symmetries assumption could limit its applicability to more complex symmetry distributions.
- The experiments are limited to simple datasets such as MNIST and its variants, which might not fully demonstrate the method's capabilities in more complex or real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing methods for symmetry detection and equivariant learning, which could help in evaluating its advantages and disadvantages more clearly.
- The paper's assumptions about the distribution of symmetries and the use of pseudo-labels for training could be seen as strong and might not generalize well to more complex or real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex scenarios, such as those involving more than one rotation angle or different types of symmetries?
- Can the method be adapted to handle more complex symmetry distributions, such as those found in real-world datasets like CIFAR10?
- How does the method compare with existing methods for symmetry detection and equivariant learning in terms of accuracy, efficiency, and generalization to different types of symmetries?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the number of neighbors used for pseudo-labeling and the architecture of the boundary prediction network?
- How does the method handle the assumption of uniformity of symmetries, and what are the implications if this assumption does not hold in real-world scenarios?
