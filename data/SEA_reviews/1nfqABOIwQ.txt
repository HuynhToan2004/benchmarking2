 **Summary:**
The paper introduces RIME, a robust algorithm for preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL) designed to handle noisy human preferences. RIME incorporates a warm-start method to bridge the performance gap during the transition from pre-training to online training and a denoising discriminator to filter out noisy samples, enhancing the robustness of the reward model. The method is evaluated across various tasks in the Meta-World and DeepMind Control Suite environments, demonstrating significant improvements over existing baselines in noisy conditions. The paper also includes an ablation study to analyze the contributions of individual components of RIME, such as the warm-start and the denoising discriminator.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to address the robustness of preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL) in the presence of noisy human feedback, which is a significant and relevant issue in the field.
- The proposed method, RIME, is well-motivated, well-designed, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the algorithm and its components.
- The experimental results show significant improvements over existing baselines, particularly in noisy conditions, and the ablation studies provide valuable insights into the contributions of individual components of RIME.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and intuitive illustrations, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The methodology is well-grounded in existing literature, particularly in the context of robust training for learning from noisy labels, and extends this to the PbRL setting.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be crucial for understanding the broader implications of the work.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The experimental setup and results could be improved by including more complex tasks and a broader range of environments, such as those involving continuous control actions.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the method, particularly in noisy environments.
- There is a lack of comparison with other robust training methods for PbRL, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of noisy labels and its sensitivity to hyperparameters.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when the noise model is different from the mistake model, particularly in terms of its ability to handle other types of noisy labels?
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, including the time and space requirements?
- How does the performance of the method vary with different hyperparameters, particularly in noisy environments?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the experimental results, particularly the results for SAC in Figure 3, and the results for clean preference data in Figure 6?
- How does the proposed method compare to other robust training methods for PbRL, such as those mentioned in the literature review?
- In the context of the Meta-World tasks, why were only three tasks chosen for evaluation, and what are the specific reasons for this choice?
