 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel High-Resolution ODE (HR-ODE) framework aimed at unifying various existing first-order optimization algorithms, including those based on Polyak's damped oscillator equation. This framework leverages integral quadratic constraints from robust control theory to enhance convergence analysis, potentially improving convergence rates for methods like the Triple Momentum Method (TMM) and Quasi Hyperbolic Momentum (QHM) algorithm. The authors also provide a detailed comparison with previous works, highlighting the improvements in convergence rates. However, the paper's contributions are seen as incremental, with some reviewers questioning the practical significance of the improvements and the necessity of the proposed framework.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel High-Resolution ODE framework that unifies existing first-order optimization algorithms, offering a general framework for analyzing convergence rates.
- The proposed GM2-ODE is shown to recover many existing algorithms, demonstrating a significant theoretical contribution.
- The authors provide a detailed comparison with previous works, highlighting the improvements in convergence rates and providing a comprehensive analysis of the convergence properties of various algorithms.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the theoretical results and their implications.
- The use of integral quadratic constraints from robust control theory in the convergence analysis adds a novel approach to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is seen as incremental, as it mainly extends existing works by adding a gradient correction term, which might not be perceived as a significant advancement.
- The practical significance of the improved convergence rates is questionable, as the theoretical improvements might not translate into substantial real-world benefits.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, which is crucial for understanding their practical applicability.
- The theoretical results are not sufficiently supported by numerical experiments, which could have validated the theoretical claims and demonstrated the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper's reliance on the SIE discretization to recover existing algorithms raises concerns about the novelty and depth of the contributions.
- The paper's focus on continuous-time analysis is seen as a limitation, as many practical applications involve discrete-time dynamics.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, especially in comparison to existing methods?
- How does the proposed GM2-ODE compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability to other existing methods?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical significance of the improved convergence rates and discuss potential real-world applications where these improvements would be most beneficial?
- Is there a possibility to extend the analysis to more general cases, such as non-strongly convex functions, and what would be the implications of such an extension?
- How does the paper address the limitations discussed, particularly the focus on strongly convex functions and deterministic methods?
