 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called PAIC (Peer Adaptation with In-Context policy) aimed at enabling agents to adapt quickly to new peers in multi-agent settings. PAIC employs an in-context policy that leverages contextual information from previous interactions to balance exploration and exploitation, enhancing the agent's ability to adapt to various peer behaviors. This approach is evaluated in two environments, Kuhn Poker and Overcooked, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods. The methodology involves learning an auxiliary task for peer identification, which serves as an intrinsic reward to encourage exploration. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental scope and the need for more diverse and complex environments to validate its effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, PAIC, which effectively balances exploration and exploitation in unknown peer environments, outperforming existing methods in both cooperative and competitive settings.
- The approach is well-motivated and well-explained, with a clear description of the methodology and its application in various environments.
- The use of an auxiliary task for peer identification and the incorporation of an intrinsic exploration reward are innovative and well-executed, contributing to the effectiveness of the PAIC method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are comprehensive, including ablation studies that help clarify the contributions of different components of the PAIC method.
- The method's ability to adapt to different peer policies and its potential for real-world applications in various domains, such as autonomous driving and service robots, is highlighted.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental scope is limited, focusing on only two environments, which may not fully validate the effectiveness of the PAIC method across a broader range of scenarios.
- The method's reliance on the diversity of the peer pool for effective performance raises concerns about its applicability in environments with limited or repetitive peer behaviors.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the PAIC method, particularly in scenarios where the method may not perform optimally or where it could be improved.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant works, such as those using graph neural networks and value decomposition for generating latent types, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the PAIC method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the PAIC method handles non-stationary peers and how it might be adapted for such scenarios.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in the clarity of figures and the organization of the content, to enhance readability and understanding.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on how the PAIC method might be adapted for scenarios where the peer agent is also adapting?
- How does the PAIC method handle non-stationary peers, and what adjustments might be necessary to ensure effective adaptation in such scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the PAIC method to include a more diverse range of environments, such as those involving more complex mixed-motive settings?
- How does the PAIC method compare to other recent and relevant works, such as those using graph neural networks and value decomposition for generating latent types?
- Could the authors clarify the methodology used in the t-SNE visualization in Figure 6 and provide a more detailed explanation of the process used to generate these visualizations?
- How does the PAIC method ensure that it does not overfit to the training peer pool, and what steps are taken to prevent this?
