 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the challenge of segmenting thin CT scans using annotations from thicker slices, a task that is typically more difficult due to the higher resolution and diagnostic precision of thin slices. The authors propose a novel dataset, CQ500-Thin, which includes expertly labeled thin annotations for evaluation, alongside a benchmark and evaluation metrics. They also introduce the Thin-Thick Adapter (TTA), a module designed to bridge the domain gap between thin and thick scans, which significantly enhances segmentation performance. The paper demonstrates that TTA outperforms baseline methods by a substantial margin, showing a 10.18% increase in mDSC and a 12.34% increase in mIoU. The TTA is validated on a newly created dataset, CQ500-Thin, which is a subset of the publicly available CQ500 dataset.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant clinical need by proposing a novel task of segmenting thin scans using annotations from thicker slices, which is crucial for improving the diagnostic precision of thin slices in medical imaging.
- The introduction of the CQ500-Thin dataset, a subset of the publicly available CQ500 dataset, provides a valuable resource for the community, enhancing the research potential in the field of medical imaging segmentation.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed Thin-Thick Adapter (TTA) is a simple yet highly effective module that bridges the domain gap between thin and thick scans, showing significant improvements in segmentation performance.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed TTA, demonstrating its effectiveness and potential for further research in medical imaging segmentation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the impact of slice thickness on image quality and the necessity of using thicker slices for annotation, which could be crucial for understanding the rationale behind the proposed method.
- There is a lack of comparative analysis with existing methods specifically designed for thin-slice segmentation, which could provide a clearer picture of the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the dataset, such as the number of annotated slices, the distribution of annotations across different classes, and the methodology used for annotation.
- The paper does not address the potential limitations or challenges of the proposed method, which could be valuable for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be an important consideration for medical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the methodology used for annotation in the CQ500-Thin dataset? How were the annotations verified for accuracy and consistency?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other medical imaging modalities, such as MRI or PET?
- Can the authors provide more details on the slice thickness used in the experiments and how it affects the segmentation performance?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods specifically designed for thin-slice segmentation?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations or challenges of the proposed method and how these might impact its practical application in clinical settings?
- How does the proposed method handle variations in slice thickness or resolution, which could affect the segmentation accuracy?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the number of annotated slices, the distribution of annotations across different classes, and the methodology used for data augmentation?
