 **Summary:**
The paper explores the theoretical underpinnings of the "more is better" phenomenon in deep learning, specifically focusing on random feature regression and kernel ridge regression. It demonstrates that under certain conditions, such as optimal ridge parameter and powerlaw eigenstructure, overfitting is necessary for achieving optimal test error. The authors present a novel eigenframework to derive closed-form estimates for test risk, which is validated through experiments on real datasets. The paper argues that these findings could potentially shift the narrative from overparameterization being harmful to it being beneficial, provided the inductive bias matches the task. However, the paper's claims are seen as somewhat limited by their reliance on specific model architectures and the assumptions made, which may not generalize well to more complex or diverse scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely question about the "more is better" phenomenon in deep learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- It provides a clear and accessible exposition of the results, making complex concepts understandable to a broad audience.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported by empirical evidence, which enhances the credibility of the findings.
- The paper introduces a novel eigenframework that unifies and generalizes previous results on random feature regression, offering a new perspective on the problem.
- The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate strong agreement with the theoretical predictions, providing empirical validation for the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claims about the generalizability of the findings to more complex or diverse scenarios are not convincingly supported, which limits the practical applicability of the results.
- The paper's focus on specific model architectures and assumptions (e.g., Gaussian universality ansatz) may limit the applicability of the findings to other types of models or datasets.
- The presentation of some sections, particularly Section 3, could be improved for clarity and accessibility.
- The paper's reliance on certain assumptions, such as the Gaussian universality ansatz, may not hold in all practical scenarios, which could limit the robustness of the results.
- There are some inconsistencies and potential errors in the mathematical formulations and proofs, which need to be addressed for the paper to be more convincing.

**Questions:**
- How do the authors justify the applicability of the findings to more complex or diverse scenarios, especially given the limitations of the assumptions made?
- Could the authors elaborate on the implications of the findings for more general models or datasets that do not fit the specific assumptions made in the paper?
- How do the authors address the potential issues with the Gaussian universality ansatz, and what are the implications of these issues for the validity of the results?
- Can the authors clarify the technical issues mentioned in the review, particularly the proofs and the assumptions made in the derivations?
- In light of the paper's claims about the "more is better" phenomenon, how do the authors respond to criticisms that this phenomenon is not universal or generalizable across different models or datasets?
