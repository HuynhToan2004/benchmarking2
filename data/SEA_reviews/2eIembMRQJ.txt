 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Hidden Utility Bandit (HUB) framework to address the challenge of learning from multiple human teachers, each with distinct rationality and expertise levels. The framework models this as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP), where the agent must decide which teacher to query and when to pull an arm, maximizing the expected discounted sum of utilities. The Active Teacher Selection (ATS) algorithm is proposed to optimize this process, and its effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments in paper recommendation systems and COVID-19 vaccine testing. The paper also discusses the limitations and potential extensions of the HUB framework, suggesting that it could be applied to various real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel problem called the Hidden Utility Bandit (HUB) problem, which is well-motivated and relevant to real-world applications, particularly in the context of reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF).
- The proposed Active Teacher Selection (ATS) algorithm is innovative, providing a method for active teacher selection, which is crucial in scenarios where multiple teachers are available.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
- The problem of learning from multiple teachers with different rationality and expertise levels is well-motivated and addresses a significant limitation in current RLHF systems.
- The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation and empirical validation, showing that the HUB framework and ATS algorithm can be applied to real-world domains such as paper recommendation systems and COVID-19 vaccine testing.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential extensions of the HUB framework, which could enhance its applicability and impact.
- The experiments are limited in scope, focusing on paper recommendation systems and COVID-19 vaccine testing, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed methods.
- The assumptions made in the HUB framework, such as the assumption that all teachers share the same utility function, may not hold in real-world scenarios, which could limit the practical applicability of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, which could be a significant limitation in large-scale or complex applications.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods for active teacher selection, which could help in evaluating the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the HUB framework could be applied to other real-world domains and how it could be extended to handle more complex scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms and how this might affect their applicability in large-scale or complex scenarios?
- How do the authors envision the HUB framework being extended to handle scenarios where the utility function is not shared among all teachers?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of the HUB framework on the field of RLHF and how it could be integrated into existing systems?
- How does the ATS algorithm compare to other existing methods for active teacher selection, and what are the key differences in terms of effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the HUB framework could be applied to other real-world domains beyond paper recommendation systems and COVID-19 vaccine testing?
