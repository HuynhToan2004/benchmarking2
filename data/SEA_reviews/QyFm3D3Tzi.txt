 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel pre-training framework, GPDiff, aimed at addressing the challenges of spatio-temporal graph (STG) transfer learning in smart city applications. It leverages a diffusion model with a transformer-based denoising network to generate tailored model parameters based on city-specific characteristics and data distributions. The methodology involves optimizing model parameters using data from source cities and adapting these parameters to target cities through a generative hypernetwork. The framework's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive experiments on real-world datasets, showing improvements over state-of-the-art baselines. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the experimental setup, the choice of baseline models, and the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to STG transfer learning by leveraging a diffusion model with a transformer-based denoising network, which is model-agnostic and can be integrated with powerful STG models.
- The proposed method shows significant improvements in terms of MAE and RMSE on real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling spatio-temporal data from diverse cities and regions.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and comprehensive explanations of the methodology, including the use of a diffusion model with a transformer-based denoising network, and the optimization of model parameters using data from source cities.
- The proposed GPDiff method is a pioneering practice in handling urban data-scarce scenarios with pretrained models, and it demonstrates the ability to adaptively generate distinctive parameters in response to diverse data distributions, addressing the challenges arising from data gaps across cities or regions.
- The paper includes extensive experiments that show the effectiveness of the proposed method in handling spatio-temporal data from diverse cities and regions, and it provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method against state-of-the-art baselines.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the experimental setup, particularly in the division of datasets into source and target cities, which raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
- The choice of baseline models appears somewhat outdated, and there is a need for a more recent and comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art models.
- The paper's claims about the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method are somewhat overstated, and the paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the challenges and limitations of the proposed method.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by including more visualizations and detailed explanations of the experimental setup, particularly in the appendix where space is more limited.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and applicability of the proposed method to larger, more complex datasets, which could limit its practical utility in real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its broader implications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly how datasets are divided into source and target cities, and how this division affects the generalizability of the findings?
- How does the proposed method compare to more recent and comprehensive baselines, and what are the implications of this comparison for the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the experimental setup, particularly in the appendix where space is more limited?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability and applicability to larger, more complex datasets, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in applying the method to such datasets?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how these impacts might be mitigated or addressed in future work?
