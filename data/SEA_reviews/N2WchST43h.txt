 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel adversarial training algorithm for a two-layer neural network with a shifted ReLU activation function. The algorithm leverages a half-space reporting data structure to efficiently identify sparsely activated neurons, thereby reducing the time complexity of each training iteration from O(mn) to O(m). This reduction is achieved by showing that only a sublinear number of neurons are activated for each input data per iteration. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the convergence of adversarial training and the approximation of the target function by the neural network, which is supported by rigorous proofs. Despite its theoretical contributions, the paper lacks empirical validation and does not address the generalizability of its findings to other network architectures or real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to adversarial training by focusing on the time complexity of each training iteration, which is significantly reduced from O(mn) to O(m) using a half-space reporting data structure.
- The theoretical proofs provided in the paper are rigorous and well-structured, offering a clear explanation of the algorithm's operation and its implications.
- The paper introduces a new algorithm for adversarial training that could potentially be applied to other types of neural networks, expanding the scope of its impact.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the theoretical results and the methodology used.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of adversarial training by providing a new algorithm that can potentially be used in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical validation of the algorithm, which makes it difficult to assess its practical effectiveness and efficiency.
- The algorithm's applicability is limited to two-layer neural networks with shifted ReLU activations, which may not generalize well to other network architectures or activation functions.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing methods, which makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's performance relative to state-of-the-art techniques.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its approach, which could include the scalability of the algorithm to larger networks or the practical feasibility of its implementation in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's title and some of its claims (e.g., about the time complexity of adversarial training) are misleading or overly optimistic, which could confuse readers or mislead them about the paper's contributions.

**Questions:**

- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of their approach and how these might affect the scalability or practical feasibility of the algorithm?
