 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of clean generalization and robust overfitting (CGRO) in adversarial training, focusing on a one-layer CNN model trained on a patch-structured dataset. It introduces a theoretical framework that explains how adversarial training leads to the memorization of both true and spurious features, which contributes to the CGRO phenomenon. The study is supported by empirical evidence, including experiments on MNIST and CIFAR10 datasets, and theoretical analyses that show how the model's feature learning process influences its robustness and generalization capabilities. Despite the novelty of the approach, the paper's theoretical contributions are seen as somewhat limited by the simplicity of the model and the dataset, and the practical implications of the findings are not clearly demonstrated.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel theoretical framework to explain the phenomenon of clean generalization and robust overfitting (CGRO) in adversarial training, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- It provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the CGRO phenomenon, including the use of feature learning theory and representation complexity to understand the underlying mechanisms.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the theoretical concepts and empirical results, making it accessible to readers.
- The experiments conducted on MNIST and CIFAR10 datasets support the theoretical findings, providing empirical evidence for the proposed theories.
- The paper offers a novel perspective on adversarial training by focusing on the learning dynamics of adversarial training, which is an interesting and unexplored area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical framework and assumptions used in the paper are somewhat simplistic and may not generalize well to more complex models or datasets.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the theoretical framework and the assumptions made, which could affect the robustness and applicability of the findings.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly linked to the theoretical claims, and the practical implications of the findings are not clearly demonstrated.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the work, particularly the gap between the theoretical analysis and real-world applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work, especially in the area of adversarial training and feature learning.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of the "spurious feature" in the context of the theoretical framework?
- How does the paper address the potential limitations and assumptions of the theoretical framework, and what are the implications of these limitations for the practical applicability of the findings?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the adversarial attack used and the training parameters?
- How does the paper's theoretical analysis relate to the empirical results, particularly in demonstrating the CGRO phenomenon in real-world datasets?
- What are the specific contributions of the paper to the understanding of adversarial training, and how do these contributions differ from or build upon existing research in the field?
