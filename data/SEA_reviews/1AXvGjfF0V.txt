 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HalluQA, a Chinese benchmark designed to evaluate the hallucination phenomenon in Chinese large language models (LLMs). The dataset comprises 450 adversarial questions, categorized into misleading and knowledge domains, and includes both open-source and closed-source models. The authors use GPT-4 for automated evaluation, focusing on the non-hallucination rate and conducting extensive experiments. The paper discusses the challenges posed by HalluQA and provides insights into the types of hallucinations exhibited by different models. However, concerns were raised about the dataset's size, the variety of models tested, and the novelty of the evaluation method compared to existing benchmarks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear introduction and methodology.
- The dataset is well-constructed, with a clear motivation and detailed explanation of the evaluation method, including the use of GPT-4 for automated evaluation.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of various models, including open-source and closed-source models, and includes a detailed analysis of the results.
- The inclusion of both open-source and closed-source models, and the use of GPT-4 for automated evaluation, adds depth to the study.
- The paper is the first to focus on evaluating hallucinations in Chinese LLMs, which is a significant and timely topic given the growing interest in Chinese LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The dataset size (450 questions) is considered small, and it remains unclear how the questions were selected and whether they are representative of real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately compare its evaluation method to existing benchmarks, such as TruthfulQA, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The variety of models tested is limited, particularly in terms of closed-source models, which could have provided a more comprehensive view of the model landscape.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its study, which could have provided a more balanced view of the findings.
- The novelty of the proposed evaluation method is questionable, as it appears similar to existing methods without significant innovation.

**Questions:**
- How were the questions selected for inclusion in the dataset, and what criteria were used to ensure their representativeness and quality?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the questions were constructed, particularly the process of generating adversarial questions and ensuring their diversity and quality?
- How does the proposed evaluation method compare to existing benchmarks, and what are the key differences in terms of methodology and results?
- Given the focus on Chinese LLMs, how does the paper address the potential cultural or linguistic biases that might affect the evaluation results?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their study and how these might impact the generalizability and applicability of the findings?
