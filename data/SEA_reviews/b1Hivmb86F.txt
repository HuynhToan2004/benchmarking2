 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MetaTST, a transformer-based architecture designed for time series analysis, which replaces the traditional attention mechanism with pooling operations. The model's components include instance normalization, seasonal-trend decomposition, and patching, aiming to improve the performance in forecasting, classification, imputation, and anomaly detection tasks. The authors claim that MetaTST achieves comparable or better results than existing methods, although the novelty of the approach is questioned due to its similarity to existing models like Metaformer. The paper's experimental validation is extensive, covering various datasets and tasks, but the results are not consistently superior to the state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across multiple tasks, including forecasting, classification, imputation, and anomaly detection, which demonstrates the versatility and applicability of the proposed MetaTST architecture.
- The experiments are well-designed, covering a broad range of datasets and tasks, which supports the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the proposed method is clearly articulated, which enhances the readability and comprehensibility of the paper.
- The idea of replacing the attention mechanism with a pooling operation is innovative and could potentially simplify the model design, making it more accessible to practitioners.
- The paper effectively highlights the importance of architecture design in time series analysis, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the proposed method, particularly in explaining why pooling operations are more effective than attention mechanisms, which is a critical aspect of the study.
- The experimental results are not consistently superior to existing methods, such as PatchTST, which raises questions about the practical superiority of the proposed method.
- The paper's contributions are not clearly distinguished from existing works, particularly from the Metaformer, which raises concerns about the novelty of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of ablation studies to support the effectiveness of each component, such as pooling, decomposition, and patching, which could strengthen the paper's claims.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and a more comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art models to establish its advantages more convincingly.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed motivation for why pooling operations are more effective than attention mechanisms in the context of time series analysis?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art models in terms of computational efficiency and model size?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the experimental results, particularly where MetaTST outperforms other methods and where it does not?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of time series data, such as those with varying frequencies or non-stationary dynamics?
- Could the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the pooling operation in the context of the MetaTST architecture?
- Is there any potential for the proposed method to be adapted to other domains, such as computer vision or natural language processing?
