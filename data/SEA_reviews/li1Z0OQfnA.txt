 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new solution concept for non-concave games, specifically a local correlated equilibrium, which is a generalization of local Nash equilibrium. This concept is designed to capture the convergence guarantees of online gradient descent and no-regret learning in non-concave games. The authors propose two instantiations of this equilibrium concept, which are shown to be computationally tractable and converge to the equilibrium under specific conditions. The paper also discusses the computational complexity and the relationship between different notions of regret, providing both upper and lower bounds for the regret. The proposed solution concept is shown to be a significant advancement in the field, offering a new approach to handling non-concave games, which are prevalent in machine learning applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel solution concept for non-concave games, which is a significant advancement in the field and addresses a long-standing open problem in game theory.
- The proposed solution concept is well-motivated, well-defined, and well-analyzed, providing both upper and lower bounds for the regret.
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions, intuitive explanations, and good examples that aid in understanding the complex concepts.
- The results are technically sound and non-trivial, offering a fresh perspective on the convergence of non-concave games.
- The paper provides a comprehensive discussion on the computational complexity and the relationship between different notions of regret, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed solution concept.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes a strong smoothness assumption (Assumption 2) which might limit the applicability of the results to general smooth games.
- The proofs and theoretical results are complex and may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the field, suggesting that the paper could benefit from additional intuitive explanations and simpler examples.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed solution concept, particularly in the global regime where the parameter δ is large.
- The paper's assumptions and the scope of applicability are not clearly defined, which could limit its practical utility in certain scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the societal impact of its findings, which is a significant omission in the discussion section.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed solution concept handle games where the utility functions are not differentiable, and what are the implications for the existence and computation of the equilibrium?
- In the proofs and theoretical results, could the authors provide more intuitive explanations or simpler examples to help readers better understand the complex concepts?
- Can the authors discuss the societal impact of their findings, particularly how the proposed solution concept might be applied in real-world scenarios to improve outcomes in non-concave games?
