 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for learning hierarchical world models, specifically focusing on the Dreamer framework. It proposes a new loss function that encourages sparse changes in the latent state, which is used to segment trajectories into meaningful units. This segmentation is then utilized to train a high-level world model that predicts the next context change, which is fed back into the low-level world model to predict the next context state. The method is evaluated on various tasks, including MiniHack, VisualPinPad, and MultiWorld, demonstrating its effectiveness in enhancing model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) and model-predictive control (MPC) performance. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticisms regarding its clarity in explaining the method's contributions and its reliance on Dreamer-specific hyperparameters, which could limit its general applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning hierarchical world models, which is a significant contribution to the field of model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL).
- The method is well-motivated and clearly presented, with a clear exposition of the methodology and its application in various scenarios.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in MBRL and MPC performance.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive set of experiments that validate the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The method's ability to learn sparse latent dynamics and its application in learning high-level policies for model-based planning is innovative and could be beneficial for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how the high-level world model is trained, particularly the role of the context in the training process.
- The method's dependence on the Dreamer framework and its specific hyperparameters (e.g., beta sparse) could limit its general applicability to other model-based planning methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient comparisons with other hierarchical world model learning methods, which could help in understanding the method's advantages and limitations.
- The paper's reliance on the Dreamer framework raises questions about the method's effectiveness when applied to other world model learning methods, such as those based on transformers.
- The paper's evaluation could be strengthened by including more complex environments and comparing the method's performance against a broader range of baselines.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by including more detailed explanations and figures that better illustrate the method's operation and its contributions to the field.

**Questions:**
- How does the high-level world model learn to predict the next context change, and what role does the context play in this learning process?
- Could the method be adapted to other model-based planning methods, such as those based on transformers, and how would this affect its performance?
- How does the method handle the segmentation of trajectories into meaningful units, and what criteria are used to determine these segments?
- Could the method be extended to learn hierarchical world models with more than two levels, and how might this affect its performance and applicability?
- How does the method compare to other hierarchical world model learning methods, and what are the specific advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method compared to these approaches?
- In the experiments, how does the method perform when applied to more complex environments, and what are the implications of these results for the method's general applicability and effectiveness?
