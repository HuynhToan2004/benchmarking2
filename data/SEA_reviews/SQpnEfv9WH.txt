 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Social-Transmotion, a transformer-based model for human trajectory prediction, leveraging various visual cues such as 2D and 3D poses and bounding boxes. The model integrates these cues through a dual-transformer architecture, where one transformer processes individual features and another captures social interactions. The model's robustness is demonstrated through experiments on multiple datasets, showing improvements over state-of-the-art methods. However, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty, as the methodology and components are seen as incremental adaptations from existing works. The experimental results are also questioned for their generalizability and the clarity of the method's contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear descriptions of the proposed method.
- The experiments are comprehensive and include multiple datasets, which helps in validating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The inclusion of visual cues such as bounding boxes and 2D/3D poses in the model enhances the accuracy of trajectory prediction.
- The model is robust against imperfect inputs and noisy data, making it suitable for real-world applications.
- The ablation studies are thorough, providing insights into the model's performance under different conditions.
- The paper demonstrates a novel approach by integrating visual cues into trajectory prediction, which is a promising direction for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as the methodology and components are similar to existing works, such as those by Kress et al. and Bouhsain et al.
- The performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods are not significant, and the paper lacks a detailed comparison with these methods.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the model handles the integration of different visual cues, particularly how it manages the potential differences in scale and resolution between these cues.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the model, including its performance in scenarios where visual cues are missing or inaccurate.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting due to the availability of large amounts of data, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The methodology section could be clearer, particularly in explaining the role and integration of different components such as the CMT and ST transformers.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the model handles the integration of different visual cues, especially in terms of scale and resolution differences?
- How does the model ensure robustness against missing or inaccurate visual cues, such as bounding boxes or poses?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training strategy and the role of masking in the model's performance?
- Is there a possibility to include qualitative results to better understand the model's performance in specific scenarios?
- How does the model compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of incorporating more diverse visual cues, such as gaze direction or actions, to enhance the model's predictive capabilities?
