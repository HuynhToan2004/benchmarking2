 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GATE, an extension of the Graph Attention Network (GAT), designed to address the over-smoothing issue in GATs by allowing for the selective aggregation of node features based on their relevance. GATE achieves this by introducing a new attention mechanism that differentiates between node and neighborhood features, enabling better control over the neighborhood aggregation process. Theoretical insights and empirical evidence are provided to support the effectiveness of GATE in handling heterophilic datasets and in scenarios where node features are crucial. However, the paper's experimental scope is limited, and the improvements over existing methods like GAT are marginal, which raises concerns about its practical utility.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the limitations of GAT, specifically its inability to effectively switch off neighborhood aggregation, which is a significant drawback of GATs.
- The authors introduce GATE, a novel extension of GAT that addresses these limitations by allowing for selective neighborhood aggregation, which is a crucial aspect of graph neural networks.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, and the theoretical insights are clearly explained, enhancing the understanding of the proposed method.
- The experiments conducted on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of GATE in handling heterophilic datasets and in scenarios where node features are crucial.
- The paper presents a clear motivation and a simple yet effective method for addressing the over-smoothing problem in GATs, which is a significant issue in graph neural networks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other methods designed specifically for handling heterophilic datasets, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of GATE's performance.
- The improvements in performance over existing methods like GAT are marginal, which questions the practical utility of GATE.
- The experimental setup is limited, focusing only on a few datasets, and the results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage of GATE over GAT.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of GATE, especially in large-scale datasets, which is a critical concern in graph neural networks.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational efficiency of GATE compared to GAT, which is essential for understanding the practical implications of using GATE.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are not sufficiently novel, as similar insights have been previously discussed in other works.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of GATE with other methods designed for handling heterophilic datasets?
- How does GATE perform in terms of computational efficiency compared to GAT, especially in large-scale datasets?
- Is there a significant difference in the computational complexity between GATE and GAT?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of scalability, particularly in large-scale datasets?
- Can the authors clarify the differences between GATE and GAT in terms of theoretical contributions and practical implications?
- Why does GATE perform better than GAT in some scenarios but not in others, as shown in the experimental results?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the scalability and computational efficiency of GATE in comparison to GAT?
