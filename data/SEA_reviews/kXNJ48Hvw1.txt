 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel sampling method for Restricted Boltzmann Machines (RBMs) using a stacked tempering approach. This method involves training a sequence of RBMs, each with a smaller number of hidden units, to perform coarse-graining of the energy landscape. The method is supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results on datasets such as MNIST, Lattice Proteins, and the 2D Ising model. The authors demonstrate that this approach improves sampling efficiency over traditional methods like Alternating Gibbs Sampling and Parallel Tempering. The paper also discusses the potential for using this method in more complex scenarios, though its applicability and scalability are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed stacked tempering method is novel and supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results.
- The empirical results are convincing, showing that the stacked tempering method is more effective than Alternating Gibbs Sampling and Parallel Tempering.
- The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the mechanism of the stacked tempering method and its relationship with the data.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear presentation of the problem and the proposed method, which is supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the limitations and potential applications of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and applicability to more complex scenarios.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with existing methods, such as the Deep Tempering method, to establish the advantages and disadvantages of the stacked tempering approach.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational complexity of the stacked tempering method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- The theoretical analysis, while interesting, is limited to a simplified setting that may not fully capture the complexity of real-world applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implementation of the stacked tempering method, including the selection of hyperparameters and the training of each RBM in the stack.

**Questions:**
- Can the stacked tempering method be extended to more complex scenarios, such as high-dimensional data or more complex energy landscapes?
- How does the stacked tempering method compare with other existing methods, such as the Deep Tempering method?
- What are the specific advantages of the stacked tempering method over other methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- How does the stacked tempering method perform in scenarios where the data distribution is complex or multimodal?
- Could the authors provide more details on the practical implementation of the stacked tempering method, including the selection of hyperparameters and the training of each RBM in the stack?
- Is there a possibility of extending the theoretical analysis to more complex settings, such as those involving a larger number of data points?
