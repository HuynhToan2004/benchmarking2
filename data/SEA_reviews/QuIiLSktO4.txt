 **Summary:**
The paper investigates caching and Metrical Task Systems (MTS) using parsimonious predictions, specifically focusing on action predictions that describe the state of the optimal algorithm at a given time. The authors propose an algorithm for caching that is 1-consistent, robust, and smooth, and another for general MTS that scales linearly with the decreasing number of predictions. The paper provides theoretical and empirical evidence, demonstrating that the algorithms perform well with a reduced number of predictions, which is beneficial for scenarios where computational resources are limited. The paper also discusses the implications of these algorithms in terms of consistency, robustness, and smoothness, and provides a comparison with existing results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly explains the problem setting and the results, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The problem studied is interesting and relevant, with a focus on parsimonious predictions which is a significant aspect of online learning and prediction in the online setting.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed algorithms, including both theoretical and empirical evidence, which supports the claims made about the consistency, robustness, and smoothness of the algorithms.
- The results are solid and well-supported by empirical evidence, which is crucial for validating the theoretical claims.
- The paper is a significant contribution to the field, as it addresses a fundamental problem in the context of learning-augmented algorithms and provides a novel approach to the problem of caching with predictions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithms, which is crucial for understanding the practical applicability and scope of the results.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the results, particularly in the figures and tables, which could benefit from better labeling and clearer presentation to enhance readability and comprehension.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the lower bounds for the smoothness and robustness of the algorithms, which would provide a more complete understanding of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the availability of action predictions for the optimal algorithm, which may not be feasible in real-world scenarios.
- There is a need for a more detailed discussion on the practical implementation of the algorithms, particularly in terms of how they would perform in different settings and with different types of predictors.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the lower bounds for the smoothness and robustness of the algorithms?
- How does the paper address the practical implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly in terms of the availability and accuracy of action predictions?
- Could the authors clarify the practical implementation of the algorithms, particularly in terms of how they would perform in different settings and with different types of predictors?
- How do the results compare with other existing algorithms, particularly in terms of the number of predictions required and the consistency and robustness of the algorithms?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the Belady algorithm as the benchmark for the caching problem, and how it impacts the results and the practical applicability of the algorithms?
