 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MIRAGE, a novel graph distillation algorithm designed to address the challenges of model-specificity and high computational demands in graph neural networks (GNNs). MIRAGE leverages a tree decomposition approach to distill graph datasets, focusing on the frequency distribution of computation trees. This method is claimed to be robust across various GNN architectures and datasets, showing significant improvements in performance, compression, and efficiency. The paper presents extensive experiments, including a comparative analysis with existing methods and ablation studies, to validate the effectiveness of MIRAGE. However, concerns were raised about the scalability of the method and its generalizability, particularly in heterophilous datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to graph distillation by focusing on the frequency distribution of computation trees, which is a significant shift from traditional methods that rely on gradient trajectories.
- The method is model-agnostic, which enhances its applicability across various GNN architectures and datasets, making it a versatile tool for graph classification tasks.
- Extensive experiments are conducted, including comparisons with existing methods and ablation studies, which provide a robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly explained, which aids in understanding the proposed approach.
- The use of CPU-bound operations instead of GPUs for distillation makes the method more energy-efficient, which is a significant advantage in resource-constrained environments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of how it performs on larger, more complex datasets.
- The method's generalizability is limited, especially in heterophilous datasets where the assumption of a power-law distribution of computation trees may not hold.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as its dependency on the assumption of a power-law distribution of computation trees.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the scalability of the method, including a comparison of running times and dataset sizes.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as similar ideas have been explored in previous works, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from these existing methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational resources used for the experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform on larger, more complex datasets? Are there any scalability issues that need to be addressed?
- Can the method be adapted to other types of graphs, such as temporal networks?
- How does the method perform on heterophilous datasets, where the assumption of a power-law distribution of computation trees may not hold?
- Could you provide more details on the computational resources used for the experiments, including the hardware specifications and software configurations?
- How does the method compare in terms of efficiency and effectiveness with other existing methods, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions of the method may not hold?
- Is there any potential for the method to be adapted to other graph-related tasks, such as node classification or link prediction?
