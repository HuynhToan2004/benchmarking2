 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the equivalence between robust MDPs and risk-sensitive MDPs, specifically focusing on the soft robust MDP and risk-sensitive MDP formulations. It establishes a connection between these two frameworks, demonstrating that the value functions and Q-functions are identical under certain conditions. The paper further introduces a sample-based offline learning algorithm, the robust fitted-Z iteration (RFZI), for a specific soft RMDP problem with a KL-divergence regularization term. This algorithm is shown to have sample complexity guarantees and is compared against existing methods in the literature. The paper also discusses the policy gradient theorem and its implications for both the soft RMDP and risk-sensitive MDP problems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper establishes a novel connection between robust MDPs and risk-sensitive MDPs, which is both interesting and significant for the field.
- The proposed offline learning algorithm, RFZI, is a notable contribution, showing sample complexity guarantees and potentially improving upon existing methods.
- The paper provides a clear and well-structured presentation, making the complex theoretical concepts accessible and understandable.
- The authors have effectively leveraged the equivalence between robust MDPs and risk-sensitive MDPs to derive policy gradient theorems and prove global convergence of exact policy gradient methods, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-organized and includes a comprehensive literature review, which is beneficial for readers unfamiliar with the subject.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions and theorems, particularly Theorem 2 and the assumptions leading to it, are not clearly explained or justified, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the implications and assumptions behind the results.
- The paper's focus on the KL-divergence regularization term limits the general applicability of the results to other types of regularization terms, which could be a significant drawback for broader application in different scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its results with existing literature, particularly with recent works that have addressed similar problems, which could undermine the perceived novelty and significance of the contributions.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are somewhat limited by the lack of practical applications or experimental validations, which could strengthen the paper's claims and impact.
- The paper's presentation could be improved in terms of clarity and organization, particularly in the main body of the paper where the theoretical results are presented.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions and theorems, particularly Theorem 2 and the assumptions leading to it, to make the paper more accessible and understandable?
- How does the paper's result compare with existing works in the literature, particularly in terms of novelty and significance?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other methods, such as those mentioned in the literature review, to highlight the advantages and limitations of their approach?
- How does the paper's approach handle different types of regularization terms beyond the KL-divergence, and what are the implications for the results?
- Could the authors discuss the practical applications of their theoretical results, perhaps through examples or case studies, to illustrate the potential impact of their findings?
- How does the paper address the computational challenges associated with the robust fitted-Z iteration (RFZI) algorithm, particularly in large or continuous state and action spaces?
