 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called "visual evidence prompting" to mitigate hallucinations in large vision-language models (LVLMs). This approach involves using outputs from small visual models, such as object detectors and scene graph generators, to provide precise and factual information that can correct inaccuracies in LVLM responses. The method is evaluated on three large language models and shows significant improvements in object and relation hallucination benchmarks. The paper also introduces a new benchmark for relation hallucinations, which is evaluated against existing datasets. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental scope, lack of comparison with existing methods, and potential limitations in the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, "visual evidence prompting," which effectively mitigates hallucinations in large vision-language models (LVLMs) by leveraging the outputs of small visual models.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, with a straightforward approach that makes it easy to understand and implement.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation of the method across various models and datasets, demonstrating its robustness and general applicability.
- The introduction of a new benchmark for relation hallucinations provides a valuable resource for the community, contributing to a more rigorous evaluation of LVLM performance.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations that make it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The proposed method shows improvements in both object and relation hallucination benchmarks, highlighting its effectiveness in addressing a significant issue in LVLM performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that address hallucinations in LVLMs, which could help establish the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The experimental scope is limited, focusing on a few models and datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the method across different models and domains.
- The paper does not discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for applications involving human subjects.
- The results section lacks detailed analysis and discussion, particularly on the performance of the method on out-of-domain images and the impact of different visual evidence formats.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the computational costs of the proposed method against other methods, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive evaluations on a broader range of models and datasets to establish the method's effectiveness more convincingly.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform on a broader range of models and datasets, especially those that are not specifically optimized for object detection or scene graph generation tasks?
- Could the authors provide more detailed analysis and discussion on the results, particularly the performance on out-of-domain images and the impact of different visual evidence formats?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, which is a known issue in fine-tuning models?
- Could the authors clarify the potential societal impacts of the proposed method and discuss how these impacts are mitigated or addressed?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational costs and practical applicability against other methods for mitigating hallucinations in LVLMs?
