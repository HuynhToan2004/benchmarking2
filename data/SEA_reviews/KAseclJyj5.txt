 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel offline RL algorithm for unsupervised skill discovery, leveraging a mutual information objective and KL-divergence state occupancy constraints to maximize diversity in learned skills while ensuring they imitate expert demonstrations. The approach is evaluated on both standard D4RL benchmarks and custom datasets from a 12-DoF quadruped robot, demonstrating the transferability of policies from simulation to real-world systems. The methodology employs Fenchel duality and reinforcement learning to optimize these constraints, and the paper provides a theoretical analysis of the proposed approach. The experiments show that the algorithm can effectively discover diverse skills, although there are concerns regarding the clarity of the writing, the practicality of the assumptions, and the robustness of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to unsupervised skill discovery in offline RL, leveraging Fenchel duality and reinforcement learning to optimize mutual information and KL-divergence state occupancy constraints.
- The method is evaluated on both standard D4RL benchmarks and custom datasets from a 12-DoF quadruped robot, demonstrating the transferability of policies from simulation to real-world systems.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to support the proposed approach, and the paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in discovering diverse skills.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and a logical structure that aids in understanding the complex mathematical derivations.
- The paper includes a detailed appendix that provides additional insights into the methodology and the experimental setup, enhancing the reproducibility and transparency of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from better organization and clarity, particularly in the presentation of the methodology and the experimental results.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the uniform sampling of latent skills and the existence of a solution satisfying the constraints, are not well justified or explained.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing offline RL algorithms, which could help in understanding the relative advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.
- The experimental results are not robust enough, with significant variance in the performance metrics, which raises concerns about the practical applicability of the method.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters and the potential for overfitting in the model training.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected for better readability and professional presentation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the assumptions regarding the uniform sampling of latent skills and the existence of a solution satisfying the constraints?
- How does the proposed method compare with other offline RL algorithms in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the experimental results, particularly the significant variance observed in the performance metrics?
- How sensitive is the method to hyperparameters, and what steps can be taken to mitigate the risk of overfitting in the model training?
- Could the authors provide more detailed descriptions of the experimental setups, including the specifics of the datasets used and the training procedures for the baseline models?
- How does the paper address the issue of imitation constraints in the methodology, and what are the implications of these constraints on the practical applicability of the method?
