 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to offline reinforcement learning (RL) by proposing a Decision ConvFormer (DC) model, which replaces the attention mechanism in the Decision Transformer (DT) with local convolution filtering. The DC model is designed to capture local dependencies in trajectories, which is a significant improvement over the DT's inability to effectively model local associations. The paper presents extensive experiments across various benchmarks, demonstrating the DC's superior performance and generalization capabilities compared to existing models. The authors also discuss the model's architecture and its implications for the RL community, suggesting a new direction in modeling local dependencies in RL datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to modeling local dependencies in offline RL, which is a significant improvement over existing models like the Decision Transformer (DT).
- The proposed model, Decision ConvFormer (DC), shows superior performance and generalization capabilities across various benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness in capturing local associations in RL datasets.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experiments that support the claims made about the DC's performance and generalization capabilities.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the attention scores in DT and demonstrate that the DC's local convolution filtering can effectively capture local associations in RL datasets, which is crucial for decision-making in RL.
- The paper includes a thorough discussion on the mechanisms that allow DC to excel in decision-making and considers the aspects of understanding local associations and model complexity.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the DC model and potential directions for future research.
- There is a lack of comparison with other models that also use convolution filters, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the DC's performance and advantages.
- The paper does not discuss the scalability of the DC model to more complex environments or the potential impact of different filter lengths on the model's performance.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the DC's training and inference times with other models, which could help assess the practicality of using the DC in real-world applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the DC handles credit assignment across long horizons, which is a critical aspect of RL decision-making.
- The paper does not address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed model, which could be a significant concern for some readers.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the DC model and potential directions for future research?
- How does the DC model compare with other models that also use convolution filters? What are the key differences in terms of performance and generalization capabilities?
- How does the DC model perform in more complex environments? Does it scale well to handle more complex decision-making scenarios?
- What is the impact of different filter lengths on the DC's performance? How does the model adapt to environments where the Markov property is weak?
- How does the DC model handle credit assignment across long horizons? What mechanisms does it use to ensure that rewards are correctly attributed to the actions that led to them?
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the DC's training and inference times with other models? How does the DC's computational efficiency compare to other models in terms of practical applicability?
