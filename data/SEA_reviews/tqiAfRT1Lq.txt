 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework named GATE (Generative Active Task Elicitation) that leverages language models (LMs) to elicit user preferences by generating questions or examples. This approach aims to enhance the accuracy and efficiency of user preference elicitation compared to traditional methods like supervised learning or active learning. The framework is evaluated across three domains: email validation, content recommendation, and moral reasoning, showing promising results in terms of user effort and model performance. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in the definition of "preferences" and the experimental setup, which might not adequately demonstrate the framework's effectiveness in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, GATE, which effectively utilizes pretrained language models (LMs) to elicit user preferences, demonstrating a significant advancement in the field.
- The research is well-organized, clearly written, and provides a comprehensive evaluation of GATE across three domains, showing promising results.
- The authors have made a substantial effort to ensure the reproducibility of their work by providing detailed information about their methods, including the code used and the experimental setup.
- The paper presents a novel approach to using language models to elicit user preferences, which is a significant step forward in the field of human-AI collaboration.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate that GATE can elicit user preferences more effectively than traditional methods, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining what constitutes a "preference," which is crucial for understanding the framework's goals and effectiveness.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, focusing on simple domains such as email validation and content recommendation, which might not adequately demonstrate the framework's capabilities in more complex or realistic scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently explore the limitations of the GATE framework, which could provide valuable insights for future research and application.
- There is a concern about the generalizability of the findings, as the experiments were conducted with a closed-source model (GPT-4) which might not be accessible to all researchers.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the area of active learning, to better contextualize the contributions of the GATE framework.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition of "preferences" and how they are elicited within the GATE framework?
- How does the GATE framework handle the potential for model-induced preferences, and what steps are taken to mitigate this effect?
- In terms of practical application, how does the GATE framework perform in real-world scenarios, particularly in domains where user preferences are more complex or varied?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the model's predictions are calibrated and the impact of this calibration on the model's performance?
- How does the GATE framework compare to other methods in terms of user effort and model performance, particularly in more complex or realistic scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal implications of using language models to elicit user preferences, particularly in terms of privacy and automation bias?
