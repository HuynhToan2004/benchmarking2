 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Occupancy-Matching Policy Optimization (OMPO) for reinforcement learning (RL) under various policy and dynamics shifts. OMPO addresses these shifts by estimating the transition occupancy distribution, which is used to define a new objective function that minimizes the KL divergence between the estimated and true transition occupancy distributions. The method is formulated as a min-max optimization problem and is tested across multiple benchmark environments, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing baselines. The approach is designed to handle both stationary and non-stationary environments, and the paper includes extensive empirical evaluations to validate its effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by leveraging the concept of transition occupancy distribution to address policy and dynamics shifts in reinforcement learning (RL), providing a unified framework for handling various types of shifts.
- The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and effectively handles complex scenarios involving both policy and dynamics shifts.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology, theoretical underpinnings, and empirical evaluations, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The empirical results are robust and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in various benchmark environments, showing superior performance compared to existing baselines.
- The paper includes extensive ablation studies and theoretical analyses, which provide a deeper understanding of the proposed method and its components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods that address policy and dynamics shifts in RL, which could help in understanding the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including its applicability in different scenarios and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental setup and the choice of hyperparameters are not thoroughly discussed, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially in more complex or larger-scale environments.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
