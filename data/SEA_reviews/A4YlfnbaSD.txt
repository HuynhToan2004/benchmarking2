 **Summary:**
The paper explores the stability gap in continual learning (CL), a phenomenon where performance on previously learned data deteriorates upon learning new data, which is often mitigated by retraining. The authors propose a method called SGM, which includes techniques such as soft-targets, LoRA, and output layer freezing to address the stability gap. The method is evaluated across various datasets and settings, demonstrating improvements in mitigating the stability gap and enhancing learning efficiency. The paper introduces new metrics to measure the stability gap and presents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, as many of the proposed techniques have been previously explored, and the paper's presentation could benefit from clearer explanations and a more rigorous comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant and timely issue of the stability gap in continual learning, which is a critical challenge in real-world applications.
- It introduces novel metrics to measure the stability gap, which can be useful for evaluating the performance of continual learning methods.
- The proposed method, SGM, is simple, effective, and applicable to various continual learning settings, including non-rehearsal methods.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides comprehensive experimental evaluations, demonstrating the effectiveness of SGM in reducing the stability gap.
- The authors conduct a thorough analysis of the stability gap and propose a comprehensive solution, which includes a novel method for continual learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claims about being the first to study the stability gap are not entirely accurate, as similar studies have been previously published.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in explaining the hypotheses and the motivation behind the proposed solutions.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, such as those that address the stability gap using different techniques.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it primarily combines existing techniques like LoRA and output layer freezing without significant innovation.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, especially in large-scale or long-term continual learning scenarios.
- The paper's focus on a specific dataset (ImageNet-1K) and a limited number of classes (1365) may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method across different datasets and scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how their method addresses the stability gap differently from existing methods, especially in terms of novelty and effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability with other methods that address the stability gap in continual learning?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other methods that address the stability gap, particularly in terms of experimental results and theoretical contributions?
- How does the proposed method perform in different continual learning settings, such as online and incremental learning, and what are the implications for these settings?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the hypotheses presented in the paper and how they are supported by the experimental results?
- How does the proposed method handle the stability gap in scenarios where the number of classes is significantly larger, and what are the implications for the method's performance and applicability?
