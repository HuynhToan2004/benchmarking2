 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for tracking invisible objects based on Qualitative-Quantitative Spatial-Temporal Reasoning (QQ-STR), inspired by the concept of object permanence. The framework consists of three modules: a visual perception module, a qualitative spatial relation reasoner (SRR), and a quantitative relation-conditioned spatial-temporal relation analyst (SRA). The SRR module infers qualitative relationships between objects and the target object based on historical observations, while the SRA module predicts quantitative locations of the target object based on these relationships and a diffusion model that captures object motion. The paper evaluates the framework on a synthetic dataset (LA-CATER) and a new real-world RGB-D video dataset (iVOT), demonstrating its generalization ability to novel scenes and viewpoints. However, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity, presentation, and the novelty of its contributions, particularly in terms of its reliance on existing methodologies and its lack of comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new dataset, iVOT, which is a significant contribution to the field of tracking invisible objects.
- The framework proposed in the paper is well-designed and effectively demonstrates the ability to learn object permanence and infer spatial relations.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes extensive experiments that are well-documented.
- The qualitative and quantitative spatial-temporal reasoning framework is a novel approach to tracking invisible objects, which is a challenging problem in computer vision.
- The paper includes an ablation study that supports the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, particularly in the explanation of key concepts such as the "Visual Perception" module and the "Qualitative Relation Reasoner" module.
- The paper does not adequately compare its methodology with existing state-of-the-art methods, which limits the ability to assess the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The performance of the proposed method on the iVOT dataset is not significantly better than existing methods, and the paper does not provide a detailed analysis of why the proposed method performs better or worse than other methods.
- The paper's methodology and the rationale behind certain design choices, such as the use of diffusion models for trajectory prediction, are not well-explained.
- The paper's contribution is seen as limited due to its reliance on existing methodologies and the lack of a clear differentiation from prior works.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a clearer explanation of the "Visual Perception" module and the "Qualitative Relation Reasoner" module, including a detailed description of the input and output of these modules?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where objects are occluded or contained in a complex manner?
- What is the rationale behind using a diffusion model for trajectory prediction, and how does this approach compare to other methods such as those mentioned in the paper?
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of their method with other state-of-the-art methods, including a discussion of the results and their implications for the effectiveness of the proposed approach?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where there are multiple objects that are occluded or contained, and how does it handle scenarios where the object disappears for extended periods?
