 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HERON, a novel framework for learning reward functions from human preferences, specifically through a hierarchical decision tree that ranks the importance of reward factors. This method allows for the efficient training of reinforcement learning agents without requiring extensive human feedback. The framework, tested across various domains including robotics and code generation, demonstrates competitive performance against hand-designed reward functions and reward engineering techniques. It also shows potential for improving sample efficiency and robustness. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the methodology, particularly in the reward learning section, and the generalizability of the approach to more complex or less structured reward functions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations that make it easy to follow.
- The proposed HERON framework is novel and provides a method for learning reward functions from human preferences, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The framework is validated through experiments on various domains, including robotics and code generation, demonstrating its effectiveness and potential for broader applications.
- The methodology is flexible and can be adapted to different environments and reward hierarchies, showing robustness to changes in the training environment.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear introduction and methodology, and includes a comprehensive set of experiments that validate the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The methodology, particularly the reward learning section, is not clearly explained, making it difficult to understand how the reward model is trained and how it is used to assign rewards to trajectories.
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how the decision tree is constructed and how the reward factors are ranked, which are crucial aspects of the proposed method.
- There is a concern about the generalizability of the approach, particularly in environments where the reward function is not well-defined or where the reward factors are not easily ranked.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential biases in the reward model and the scalability of the approach to more complex environments.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed descriptions of the experiments, such as the environments used and the specifics of the reward functions and policies.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify how the reward model is trained and how it is used to assign rewards to trajectories?
- How is the decision tree constructed, and what are the specifics of the reward factors used in each environment?
- How does the HERON framework handle cases where the reward function is not well-defined or where the reward factors are not easily ranked?
- Can you discuss the potential biases in the reward model and how they might affect the performance of the reinforcement learning agent?
- How does the HERON framework compare to other methods for learning reward functions from human preferences, such as inverse reinforcement learning?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the environments and the policies used?
