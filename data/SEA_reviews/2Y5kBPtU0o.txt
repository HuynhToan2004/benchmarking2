 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MEND, a method for distilling demonstrations into vectors, aiming to enhance in-context learning (ICL) efficiency without compromising performance. The approach employs a two-stage training process, including meta-distillation pretraining and fine-tuning, to learn the distillation process without retraining for a new task. The methodology is evaluated across seven ICL task partitions using both decoder-only (GPT-2) and encoder-decoder (T5) models, demonstrating improvements in efficiency and performance. The paper also explores the impact of different hyperparameters on the distillation process and its performance, providing a comprehensive evaluation of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to distill demonstrations into vectors, enhancing in-context learning (ICL) efficiency without compromising performance.
- The methodology is evaluated across seven ICL task partitions using both decoder-only (GPT-2) and encoder-decoder (T5) models, demonstrating improvements in efficiency and performance.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the method, including a detailed analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters on the distillation process and its performance.
- The writing is clear and the figures are well-presented, making the content accessible and understandable.
- The approach of distilling demonstrations into vectors is innovative and could be beneficial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of how it would perform with larger demonstrations or more complex models.
- The methodology is somewhat limited in its applicability to different model families, as it is primarily tested with GPT-2 and T5 models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and potential directions for future research.
- The comparison with other methods, such as prompt tuning and hypernetworks, could be more thorough and convincing, especially in terms of computational efficiency and performance.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the distillation process, which could be crucial for practical implementation.
- The paper lacks a broader evaluation on different models and datasets, which could strengthen the claims of the method's effectiveness and generalizability.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform with larger demonstrations or more complex models?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the distillation process, particularly in terms of time and memory requirements?
- How does the method compare with other distillation methods, especially in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the performance of the distillation process with different hyperparameter settings?
- How does the method handle different model families, and could it be adapted to other models beyond GPT-2 and T5?
- What are the potential limitations of the method, and how might these affect its practical application?
- Could the authors discuss potential directions for future research to address these limitations and enhance the method's effectiveness?
