 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel algorithm for offline Preference-based Reinforcement Learning (PbRL) that leverages maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) with general function approximation from offline data to estimate implicit rewards. It then solves a distributionally robust planning problem using a confidence set around the MLE. The algorithm is designed to learn any target policy with a polynomial number of samples, provided the target policy is covered by the offline data. The paper also introduces a new single-policy concentrability coefficient and extends the analysis to settings where the transition kernel is unknown. Theoretical guarantees are provided, and the paper is well-organized, making it accessible and clear. However, the paper lacks empirical validation, which is crucial for demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and the writing is clear and accessible.
- The theoretical results are sound and well-presented, with a clear discussion of the limitations of the work.
- The paper is the first to provide theoretical guarantees for offline PbRL with general function approximation, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors have provided a novel theoretical analysis of the PAC learning problem in the context of offline PbRL, which is an important and timely problem.
- The paper introduces a new single-policy concentrability coefficient, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper extends the analysis to settings where the transition kernel is unknown, which is a practical and relevant consideration in many real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical validation, which is crucial for demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, which could help in understanding the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, which is a significant concern for practical implementation.
- The paper does not discuss the scalability of the proposed algorithm, which is essential for understanding its applicability in large-scale real-world problems.
- The paper assumes that the target policy is covered by the offline data, which might not always be the case in practical scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on how the proposed algorithm can be implemented in practice, which could be a barrier for replication and further research.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed algorithm compare with existing methods in terms of computational complexity and scalability?
- Can the authors discuss the practical implications of the assumption that the target policy is covered by the offline data?
- How can the proposed algorithm be implemented in practice, and what are the potential challenges and limitations in doing so?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their work and how these might affect the practical applicability of their findings?
