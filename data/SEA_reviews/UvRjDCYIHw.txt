 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for doubly inductive link prediction in knowledge graphs, focusing on the equivariance of both node and relation types. It proposes two methods, ISDEA+ and DEq-InGram, which leverage double-equivariant representations to predict new nodes and relations without requiring extra context. The authors construct new datasets to evaluate their models against state-of-the-art baselines, showing improvements in zero-shot performance. The paper also discusses the theoretical foundations of double-equivariant models and their practical implications, including the ability to generalize across different domains.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the problem and the proposed methods, with a clear distinction from previous works.
- It introduces a novel framework for doubly inductive link prediction, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical foundation for double-equivariant models, which is a notable contribution to the field.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, showing improvements over existing baselines.
- The introduction of new datasets for evaluating the proposed methods is a valuable contribution to the community.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with a clear distinction between the proposed methods and previous works.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which could provide a more balanced view of their capabilities.
- The experimental setup and results are not thoroughly validated, with concerns about the choice of baselines and the generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key concepts like double equivariance and the new datasets.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with existing methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of scalability, particularly in the context of large-scale knowledge graphs.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related works, particularly those that address similar issues in knowledge graph learning.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the new datasets were constructed and how they differ from existing datasets in terms of structure and content?
- How do the authors ensure that the models trained on different datasets do not leak information between them, especially when evaluating zero-shot capabilities?
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of double equivariance in the context of knowledge graph learning?
- How do the proposed methods compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability with existing methods, especially in the context of large-scale knowledge graphs?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of the specific datasets and metrics used?
- How do the authors address the issue of scalability in their proposed methods, and what are the potential limitations in terms of handling very large or complex knowledge graphs?
