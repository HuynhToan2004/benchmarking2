 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel metric learning approach to detect whether a given text was generated by a large language model (LLM) or by a human. The method involves training a metric model using triplet loss to maximize the distance between human and LLM responses while minimizing the distance between LLM responses generated from the same context. The proposed method is evaluated on four datasets, demonstrating its ability to maintain F1 scores between 0.87 to 0.95 across various scenarios, including paraphrasing and out-of-corpus settings. The paper also provides new datasets for benchmarking, which are valuable for the research community. Despite the method's potential, concerns are raised about the experimental evaluation, which is limited to a single model (GPT-3.5 Turbo) and does not include comparisons with other state-of-the-art LLMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel metric learning approach for detecting LLM-generated texts, which is a significant and timely research topic.
- The method is described clearly, making it easy to understand and replicate, and the paper provides new datasets which are valuable for the community.
- The approach is simple, efficient, and effective, as demonstrated by the experiments which show that the method maintains F1 scores between 0.87 to 0.95 across various scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, and the method does not require access to LLMs' internal computations, which is a significant advantage.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its generalizability to different LLMs and its robustness against paraphrasing and out-of-corpus texts.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to a single model (GPT-3.5 Turbo) and does not include comparisons with other state-of-the-art LLMs, which could provide a more robust validation of the method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its potential negative societal impacts.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the choice of the MPNet model for text embedding and does not provide a detailed analysis of the impact of hyperparameters on the model's performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related works, especially in the area of LLM text detection, and a more detailed comparison with existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the method performs on other LLMs, especially those with different generation styles or capabilities?
- How does the method handle paraphrasing and out-of-corpus texts, which are known to be challenging for LLM detection?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their work and how they plan to mitigate these risks?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- How does the choice of the MPNet model for text embedding affect the performance of the method, and have other models been considered?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of hyperparameters on the model's performance, and discuss the potential benefits of using a more complex model architecture?
