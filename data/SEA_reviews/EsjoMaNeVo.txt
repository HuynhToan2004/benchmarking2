 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the problem of steering no-regret learning agents to desirable equilibria in extensive-form games, focusing on the payment mechanism to guide agent behaviors. It presents algorithms for both full-feedback and bandit feedback settings, demonstrating that vanishing average payments are sufficient for steering agents to desired equilibria. The paper also discusses the theoretical implications and experimental results, highlighting the challenges and potential solutions in different game settings. Despite the innovative approach and the theoretical contributions, the paper faces criticism for its experimental results and the clarity of its assumptions and conclusions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel problem of steering agents to desirable equilibria in extensive-form games, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The algorithms presented are well-structured and provide a clear theoretical framework for understanding the problem.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive discussion of the problem, its implications, and potential solutions.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the context of steering agents to desirable equilibria in extensive-form games, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The algorithms are presented in a clear and structured manner, which aids in understanding the problem and the proposed solutions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed experimental results and comparisons with existing algorithms, which limits the ability to assess the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the computational power of the mediator and the behavior of the agents, are not clearly stated or justified, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and the conditions under which they might not be applicable.
- The experimental results presented are not convincing, and the paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing algorithms, which is crucial for evaluating the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper's claims about the steering problem being impossible in certain settings are not adequately supported by evidence, which could weaken the paper's arguments.
