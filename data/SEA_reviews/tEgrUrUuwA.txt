 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel message-passing framework, Partitioning Message Passing (PMP), aimed at addressing the challenges of label imbalance and heterophily in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) for Graph Fraud Detection (GFD). PMP distinguishes neighbors based on their labels, utilizing distinct node-specific aggregation functions to adaptively adjust information aggregation from heterophilic and homophilic neighbors. This approach helps prevent the model from being dominated by benign nodes and ensures a balanced handling of information from different classes. Theoretical analysis and extensive experiments on public datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of PMP in enhancing GFD performance. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, as it seems to be a straightforward adaptation of GraphSAGE, and the theoretical claims lack detailed proof.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel message-passing framework, PMP, specifically designed for Graph Fraud Detection (GFD), which effectively addresses the challenges of label imbalance and heterophily.
- The method is well-motivated and theoretically grounded, with a clear presentation of the problem and its solution.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PMP in enhancing GFD performance, and the methodology is straightforward and easy to understand.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and logical presentation of the proposed method and its experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it appears to be a straightforward adaptation of GraphSAGE, with only minor modifications such as distinct weight matrices for labeled neighbors.
- The theoretical analysis lacks depth, particularly in the proof of Theorem 1, which is critical for understanding the claimed properties of the model.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in large-scale graphs, where the computational complexity might be a concern.
- The experimental setup and results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of PMP over existing methods like GraphSAGE, and there is a lack of detailed discussion on the experimental results and their implications.
- There are concerns about the fairness of the comparisons made in the paper, particularly in the semi-supervised setting where PMP's performance might be influenced by the use of additional labels.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their method compared to existing approaches, particularly in terms of the theoretical contributions and practical implications?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues in large-scale graphs, and what are the potential computational complexities involved?
- Could the authors provide more detailed proof or clarification on the theoretical claims, especially regarding the properties of the model as claimed in Theorem 1?
- In light of the experimental results, how do the authors justify the superiority of PMP over GraphSAGE, especially in the semi-supervised setting where PMP has an unfair advantage?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of homophily in graphs, which is not explicitly addressed in the paper?
- Can the authors provide more detailed discussions on the experimental results, including potential confounding factors and the implications of the results for practical applications?
