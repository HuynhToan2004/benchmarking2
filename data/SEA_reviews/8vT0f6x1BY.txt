 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the transferability of adversarial examples, specifically focusing on the role of early stopping in enhancing transferability. The authors challenge the conventional wisdom that early stopping improves transferability by proposing a new hypothesis: the sharpness of the loss landscape. They argue that early stopping benefits transferability by reducing sharpness, which in turn increases the transferability of non-robust features. The paper supports this hypothesis with empirical evidence, demonstrating that minimizing sharpness can be an effective strategy for improving transferability. The study also explores the use of SAM (Sharpness-Aware Minimization) and its variants to minimize sharpness and enhance transferability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the transferability of adversarial examples, a topic of significant interest in the field of machine learning.
- The authors challenge the widely accepted hypothesis that early stopping improves transferability and propose a new hypothesis, which is supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with figures that effectively illustrate the main ideas.
- The experiments are thorough, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method and providing a detailed analysis of the results.
- The paper is innovative in its approach to understanding transferability, particularly in its focus on the sharpness of the loss landscape and its impact on transferability.
- The paper provides a novel perspective on the role of early stopping in transferability, which could lead to new insights and methodologies in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of adversarial examples and models.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, including a discussion on how the proposed method compares in terms of computational efficiency and transferability performance.
- There is a need for more experimental results to support the claims made in the paper, particularly regarding the effectiveness of the proposed method across different models and adversarial examples.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method, which could limit its applicability to larger, more complex models.
- The paper's reliance on early stopping as a method for improving transferability is not novel, and the proposed method's contribution to the field is not clearly articulated.
- The paper's conclusions are somewhat limited by the scope of the experiments, which primarily focus on vision models and do not extend to other types of models or datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method could be applied to different types of adversarial examples, such as those generated by different attack methods?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and transferability performance with other existing methods?
- Could the authors provide more experimental results to support the claims made in the paper, particularly regarding the effectiveness of the proposed method across different models and adversarial examples?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of models, such as transformer-based models?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their method and how these might impact its applicability in real-world scenarios?
- Is there a possibility to extend the experiments to include a broader range of models and datasets to validate the generalizability of the findings?
