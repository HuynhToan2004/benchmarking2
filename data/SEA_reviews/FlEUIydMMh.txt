 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to factor analysis, termed Neuro-Causal Factor Analysis (NCFA), which integrates latent causal discovery with a variational autoencoder (VAE) to model the data-generating process. The methodology involves learning a latent variable graph from the data, which is then used to constrain the VAE, ensuring that the latent variables adhere to the Markov factorization of the distribution. The NCFA model is evaluated on both synthetic and real data, showing competitive performance compared to standard VAEs and the ability to recover causal structures. However, concerns were raised about the method's sensitivity to hyperparameter choices and the complexity of the identifiability assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and accessible, with a logical flow that effectively connects the various components of the proposed method.
- The integration of latent causal discovery with a variational autoencoder (VAE) is a novel approach that combines the strengths of both methodologies.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear definitions and a clear presentation of the algorithm and its components.
- The paper provides a good balance between theoretical guarantees and practical applications, which is particularly useful in causal discovery and factor analysis.
- The empirical evaluation shows that the NCFA model can recover causal structures in synthetic data and improve performance on real data compared to standard VAEs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the identifiability of the latent variable graph, which is crucial for the validity of the causal interpretations.
- The method's sensitivity to hyperparameter choices, such as the number of latent variables and the threshold for independence tests, is not adequately addressed.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other factor analysis methods or other causal discovery methods, which could help contextualize the contributions of the NCFA model.
- The empirical evaluation is limited, with only a few datasets used, which may not adequately demonstrate the method's scalability or robustness.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the method, which could be a significant concern for larger datasets or more complex models.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method, which could help in understanding the scenarios where the NCFA model may not be effective.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on how the method handles scenarios where the causal structure is misspecified? How does this affect the identifiability and interpretability of the latent variables?
- How does the NCFA model compare to other causal discovery methods, particularly in terms of scalability and computational complexity?
- Could you provide more details on the identifiability of the latent variable graph, especially in scenarios where the assumptions of the method may not hold?
- How does the NCFA model perform in high-dimensional settings, where the number of latent variables required may be large?
- Could you discuss the potential for extending the method to handle non-Gaussian distributions for the latent variables?
- How does the NCFA model handle the complexity of the identifiability assumptions, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions may not hold?
