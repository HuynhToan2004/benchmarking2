 **Summary:**
The paper introduces STREAM, a novel evaluation metric designed to assess the spatial and temporal qualities of video generative models. It critiques the limitations of existing metrics like FVD and proposes a new method that separately evaluates spatial and temporal aspects using skewness of power law distribution and precision/recall metrics. The metric's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real video datasets, showing its ability to provide more granular insights into video quality compared to traditional metrics. However, concerns were raised about the practical applicability of the metric in real-world scenarios and the need for further validation against human judgement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the need for a new evaluation metric for video generation.
- The proposed metric, STREAM, is novel and provides a more detailed analysis of video generation quality by separating spatial and temporal aspects.
- The experiments are extensive, demonstrating the metric's effectiveness in various scenarios, and the paper includes code for reproducibility.
- The methodology is sound, with a thorough analysis of existing metrics and their limitations, which leads to the development of STREAM.
- The paper provides a detailed explanation of the new metric, which is both intuitive and easy to understand, and includes a comparison with existing metrics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the practical implications of the new metric, particularly how it can be used in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity of the new metric compared to existing ones.
- The evaluation of the metric's effectiveness is limited to a few models and datasets, and there is a need for more comprehensive evaluations across different models and datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could include issues with the metric's applicability in certain scenarios or its sensitivity to specific types of noise.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of parameters such as k in the precision/recall metrics.
- The paper's experimental results, particularly those in Table 1, do not align well with the qualitative results, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the practical implications of using the new metric in real-world scenarios?
- How does the computational complexity of the new metric compare to existing metrics?
- Can you clarify the discrepancy between the qualitative results and the quantitative results in Table 1?
- How does the choice of k in the precision/recall metrics affect the results, and could you provide more details on this parameter?
- Could you discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the metric might not be effective?
- How do the authors address the issue of the metric's sensitivity to specific types of noise, and what steps are taken to mitigate this?
