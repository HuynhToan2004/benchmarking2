 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph neural network architecture, DAG-MLP, designed to address the issues of oversquashing and redundancy in message-passing neural networks. By leveraging neighborhood trees and a neural tree canonization technique, the proposed method aims to improve expressivity and avoid redundant computations. The paper provides theoretical analysis and experimental results, showing that DAG-MLP outperforms existing methods in terms of expressiveness and computational efficiency. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, unclear writing, and lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in graph neural networks, focusing on the reduction of redundancy and oversquashing in message-passing networks.
- The proposed method is theoretically sound and provides a novel approach to handling redundancy in graph neural networks, which is a significant issue in the field.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving expressivity and reducing computational complexity.
- The paper provides a comprehensive discussion of related work, which helps in positioning the research within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing works, particularly with RFGNN, which has similar ideas and a similar approach.
- The writing and presentation of the paper are criticized for being unclear and difficult to understand, especially in sections where the methodology and results are discussed.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, which could limit its applicability to larger and more complex graphs.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive experimental evaluation, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is a critical aspect for understanding its practical implications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between their method and RFGNN, particularly in terms of the approach to handling redundancy and the expressive power of the networks?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and runtime with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in scenarios where the graph size is large?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the performance of the method on larger and more complex graphs, and the impact of varying parameters such as k and l on the performance?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of oversquashing, particularly in scenarios where the number of layers is increased?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of their method and discuss potential scenarios where the proposed method may not be effective or may lead to suboptimal results?
