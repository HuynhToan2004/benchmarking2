 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for estimating causal effects using deep generative models, particularly focusing on high-dimensional variables and unobserved confounders. The authors propose a modular adversarial training algorithm, WhatIfGAN, which can learn a deep causal generative model (DCM) by training each component in a specific order based on the causal graph. This method is demonstrated to be effective in handling both low-dimensional and high-dimensional variables, and the experimental results show its superiority over existing methods. However, the paper's novelty and the clarity of its presentation have been questioned, with concerns about the complexity of the algorithm and its assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear and compelling motivation.
- The proposed method is novel and innovative, particularly in its application to high-dimensional variables, which is a significant challenge in the field of causal inference.
- The paper is theoretically grounded, with a clear theoretical framework and a novel approach to training a deep causal generative model (DCM) that can handle high-dimensional variables.
- The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in comparison to existing methods.
- The paper is significant in its potential to advance the field of causal inference, particularly in the application of deep learning to high-dimensional variables.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is somewhat limited as it builds on existing work, particularly the modular training of deep generative models, which has been previously explored.
- The paper's presentation is complex and could benefit from clearer explanations and more intuitive examples to aid understanding.
- The assumptions made by the method are strong and may not hold in practical scenarios, such as the availability of a causal graph and the absence of unobserved confounders.
- The paper's experimental setup is somewhat limited, with only a few datasets used, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the method.
- The paper's contribution to the field is not clearly articulated, particularly in terms of its novelty and the extent to which it advances the state of the art.
- There are several minor issues with the paper's presentation, such as typos and unclear definitions, which could detract from its clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made by the method and discuss how these assumptions could be relaxed to make the method more applicable in practical scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for handling high-dimensional variables in causal inference, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the modular training process and the construction of the H-graph, possibly with additional examples or figures to aid understanding?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of confounding, particularly those that are not latent?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their method and suggest potential directions for future research to address these limitations?
