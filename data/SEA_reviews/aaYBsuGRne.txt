 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Pelican Soup Hypothesis, a novel perspective on in-context learning (ICL) in large language models (LLMs). This hypothesis suggests that ICL can be understood as a generalization of modeling linguistic phenomena under distribution shifts. The authors support this hypothesis through empirical evidence, including a synthetic dataset (Calcutec) and experiments using a digit addition task. They argue that ICL is facilitated by the commonsense knowledge base, and the absence of reasoning steps and structure mismatch can affect ICL performance. The paper also explores the role of in-context learning in language modeling and proposes a formalism for NLP classification tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel hypothesis, the Pelican Soup Hypothesis, which provides a new perspective on in-context learning (ICL) in large language models (LLMs).
- The hypothesis is supported by empirical evidence, including a synthetic dataset (Calcutec) and experiments using a digit addition task, which are novel and contribute to the understanding of ICL.
- The paper provides a formalism for NLP classification tasks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, with clear explanations and examples that aid in understanding complex concepts.
- The hypothesis is supported by a variety of experiments, which help to validate the claims made.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the formalism for NLP classification tasks and the explanation of the Pelican Soup Hypothesis.
- The connection between the Pelican Soup Hypothesis and the experiments conducted is not clearly established, making it difficult to see how the hypothesis is supported by the empirical evidence.
- The paper's assumptions and the assumptions underlying the Calcutec dataset are not clearly stated, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work and a clearer explanation of how the Pelican Soup Hypothesis relates to existing theories and empirical evidence.
- The paper's claims about the generalizability of the findings to real-world scenarios are not convincingly supported, and the synthetic nature of the data used raises questions about the applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the connection between the Pelican Soup Hypothesis and the experiments conducted, particularly how the hypothesis is supported by the empirical evidence?
- How does the Pelican Soup Hypothesis relate to existing theories and empirical evidence on in-context learning and language modeling?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the assumptions underlying the Calcutec dataset and how they relate to the real-world scenarios where in-context learning is applied?
- How do the authors address the concerns about the generalizability of the findings from the synthetic data used in the experiments to real-world scenarios?
- Could the paper benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work and a clearer explanation of the assumptions and implications of the formalism for NLP classification tasks?
