 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the privacy risks associated with curriculum learning (CL), a technique that orders training data based on difficulty scores to enhance model performance. It explores two types of privacy attacks: membership inference attacks (MIAs) and attribute inference attacks (AIAs), using various datasets and models. The study reveals that models trained using CL are more susceptible to MIA, particularly for samples with high difficulty scores. To mitigate this risk, a new MIA method called Diff-Cali is proposed, which utilizes difficulty scores to calibrate membership scores and enhance attack performance. The paper also evaluates existing defense methods against these attacks, showing that while they offer some protection, they also impact model accuracy. The research contributes to the understanding of privacy risks in CL and proposes a new method to address these risks, although it has been critiqued for its limited novelty and the need for more comprehensive experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic of privacy in machine learning, specifically focusing on curriculum learning (CL), which is a significant and relevant area of study.
- The authors conduct a comprehensive empirical evaluation of the privacy risks associated with CL, employing a wide range of datasets, models, and attack methods, which provides a thorough understanding of the privacy risks in CL.
- The paper proposes a novel MIA method called Diff-Cali, which leverages difficulty scores to calibrate membership scores, potentially improving the attack performance and offering a new approach to privacy attacks.
- The findings of the paper could be beneficial for the community, particularly in understanding the privacy implications of CL and developing new attack benchmarks and defense solutions.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method, Diff-Cali, is questioned as it appears to be a minor variation of existing methods, such as calibrated MIA, which might not significantly advance the field.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing works, particularly in terms of the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method against other state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method across various datasets and models, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis and deeper insights into the privacy risks introduced by CL, which could enhance the understanding and applicability of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the study, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the privacy risks associated with CL, particularly in terms of the specific types of data and models that might be more vulnerable to these risks.
