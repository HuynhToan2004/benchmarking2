 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for estimating the contribution of individual agents in multi-agent systems using Exchange Values (EVs), which are akin to Shapley Values but adapted to handle different group sizes. EVs are used to compute agent contributions to a Desired Value Function (DVF) and are estimated from incomplete datasets using EV-Clustering. The method, EV2BC, imitates only agents with high EVs, enhancing the alignment of agent behavior with the DVF. The paper presents experiments across various environments, demonstrating the effectiveness of EV2BC in outperforming baseline methods. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the writing, the necessity of the clustering step, and the generalizability of the method to more complex, real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the problem and the proposed solution, with a focus on the use of Shapley Values and their adaptation to multi-agent systems.
- The proposed method, EV2BC, is novel and innovative, addressing the issue of credit assignment in multi-agent systems, which is crucial for aligning agent behavior with desirable characteristics.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis and empirical evidence demonstrating the effectiveness of EV2BC in outperforming baseline methods, showing its potential for practical applications in real-world scenarios.
- The methodology is simple and easy to follow, which is beneficial for understanding and implementing the proposed solution.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of certain terms and concepts, which could confuse readers.
- The necessity and effectiveness of the clustering step are not clearly justified, and the impact of the clustering on the estimation of EVs is not thoroughly analyzed.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, especially in more complex and larger-scale environments, which could limit its applicability.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly demonstrated, with concerns about the realism of the simulated environments and the generalizability of the results to more complex, real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the term "desirability score" and its implications for the behavior of agents in the system?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the desirability score is not clearly defined, and how does it ensure that the behavior of the imitated agents remains desirable?
- Can you provide more details on how the Exchange Values are estimated in environments where the number of agents and group sizes vary?
- How does the EV2BC method compare to other methods that use different value functions or different clustering strategies?
- In the experiments, how are the thresholds for EVs determined, and how do they affect the performance of the method?
- How does the method ensure that the imitated agents do not exhibit undesirable behaviors, especially when the desirability score is not clearly defined?
