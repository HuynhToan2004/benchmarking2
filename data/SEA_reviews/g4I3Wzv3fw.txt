 **Summary:**
The paper explores robust reinforcement learning (RL) in the context of static transition models, focusing on the equivalence between static and dynamic models of uncertainty under certain conditions. It introduces a novel method, Incremental Worst-Case Search (IWOCS), which iteratively refines a set of transition functions to identify the worst-case model and optimize policies accordingly. The method is tested across various MuJoCo environments, demonstrating competitive performance against state-of-the-art algorithms. The paper also discusses the theoretical foundations and empirical evaluations of IWOCS, highlighting its potential to improve robustness in RL systems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive overview of the theoretical foundations of robust RL, including the equivalence between static and dynamic models of uncertainty, and the no-duality gap property.
- It introduces a novel method, IWOCS, which offers a new perspective on solving robust MDPs by iteratively refining a set of transition functions.
- The method is well-motivated, with a clear presentation of the problem statement and the proposed method.
- The empirical evaluation is extensive, covering a variety of MuJoCo environments and demonstrating the competitive performance of IWOCS against state-of-the-art algorithms.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in robust RL, which is crucial for real-world applications, and provides a new approach that could potentially improve robustness in RL systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the description of the algorithm and its theoretical foundations.
- There is a noticeable absence of discussion on the computational complexity and convergence of the IWOCS method.
- The empirical evaluation does not include a broader range of environments or comparisons with other robust RL algorithms, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the assumptions made and the potential for suboptimal solutions.
- There is a lack of discussion on the scalability of the method to larger state and action spaces, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other robust RL algorithms and a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the IWOCS method.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition and role of the function $V^T_i$ in Algorithm 1?
- How does the IWOCS method handle the computational complexity and convergence issues, especially in large state and action spaces?
- Can you provide more details on how the IWOCS method compares to other robust RL algorithms, especially in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the IWOCS method handle the assumption of stationary policies, and what are the implications if this assumption is not met?
- Could you discuss the potential for extending the IWOCS method to handle non-stationary policies or more complex environments?
- What are the specific advantages of the IWOCS method over other robust RL algorithms, and how does it address the limitations of existing methods?
