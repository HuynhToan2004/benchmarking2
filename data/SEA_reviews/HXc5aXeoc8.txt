 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the issue of divergence artifacts in diffusion models, particularly when using higher-order numerical methods for sampling. The authors propose two methods: Heavy Ball (HB) and Generalized Heavy Ball (GHB) momentum, aimed at enhancing the stability of the diffusion process and reducing artifacts. The HB method is a simple modification to existing diffusion solvers, while GHB offers a more complex approach that interpolates between different-order solvers. The paper provides theoretical and empirical evidence, including extensive experiments, to support the effectiveness of these methods. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned, as similar methods have been previously explored, and the paper does not sufficiently compare its methods with existing works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of diffusion models, specifically the issue of divergence artifacts, which is crucial for improving the quality of generated images.
- The theoretical analysis and experimental results provided are comprehensive and robust, offering a clear understanding of the problem and the proposed solutions.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the visualizations are effective in illustrating the issues and improvements.
- The methodology is straightforward and easy to implement, with the potential to be integrated into existing diffusion models with minimal modifications.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison of the proposed methods with existing ones, including extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed techniques.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed methods is limited as similar approaches using momentum methods to address divergence artifacts have been previously explored.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.
- The comparison with existing works, particularly in terms of methodology and results, is insufficiently detailed, making it difficult to assess the true advancements of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical backing for the claims made about the convergence of the proposed methods, which could be crucial for their acceptance and implementation.
- The paper's reliance on specific diffusion models for testing, such as Stable Diffusion, might limit the generalizability of the findings to other models.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of their proposed methods compared to existing works, particularly in terms of novelty and effectiveness?
- How do the proposed methods perform when applied to other diffusion models, such as those based on SDEs?
- Could the authors provide more detailed theoretical analysis or proofs to support the claims made about the convergence of the proposed methods?
- What are the computational complexities of the proposed methods, and how do they compare to existing methods in terms of efficiency and practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of diffusion models, such as pixel-based and latent-based models?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations and negative societal impacts of their proposed methods, and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
