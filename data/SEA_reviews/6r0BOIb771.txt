 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for meta-continual learning (MCL) that integrates neural networks with simple statistical models to address the challenges of catastrophic forgetting in continual learning. The approach employs a sequential Bayesian update rule to manage forgetting in the embedding space, while meta-learned neural networks bridge the gap between raw data and the embedding space. The paper proposes a general framework that can be applied to various problems and integrated with existing model architectures. It also presents a variational bound for meta-continual learning and discusses its application in different settings, including supervised and unsupervised learning. The paper's experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in several benchmarks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework for meta-continual learning that integrates neural networks with simple statistical models, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- It provides a general framework that can be applied to a wide range of problems and easily integrated with existing model architectures, enhancing its applicability and relevance.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a logical structure that makes it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is domain-agnostic and model-agnostic, which makes it applicable to various domains and models, and can be integrated with existing model architectures with minimal modifications.
- The paper demonstrates significant improvements in performance and scalability compared to SGD-based MCL methods, showcasing its practical utility.
- The paper is original in its approach to meta-continual learning, combining the strengths of neural networks and statistical models to address the challenges of forgetting in continual learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential drawbacks of the proposed framework, which could provide a more balanced perspective on its applicability and effectiveness.
- There is a lack of comparative analysis with other state-of-the-art methods in meta-continual learning, which could help in understanding the relative performance and advantages of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the size of the meta-training set and the number of meta-training iterations, which could affect the reproducibility and reliability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical applications where computational resources are limited.
- The paper's focus on specific datasets and benchmarks might limit its generalizability to other domains or tasks, which could affect its broader applicability.
- The paper's claims regarding the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.
