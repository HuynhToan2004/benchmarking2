 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph-based supply prediction model that leverages graph neural networks (GNNs) to predict supplies, inventory, and imbalances in complex supply chain networks. The model, named GSP, integrates historical data, demand forecasting, and original supply plan inputs to enhance prediction accuracy. The GSP model is evaluated using historical data from a global consumer goods company, demonstrating significant improvements in supply and inventory prediction accuracy. The paper also introduces a new prediction error metric called sMACE to assess prediction inaccuracies caused by unpredictable supply variability. Despite its technical soundness and practical application, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty, insufficient experimental validation, and lack of comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of supply chain optimization, focusing on the prediction of supplies, inventory, and imbalances, which is crucial for successful supply planning.
- The proposed model, GSP, is technically sound and well-presented, providing a clear and concise explanation of the methodology and its application.
- The paper introduces a novel graph-based approach to supply prediction, which is innovative and could potentially outperform traditional methods.
- The use of a probabilistic approach in the model allows for more nuanced predictions, taking into account various factors that influence supply and inventory levels.
- The paper includes a new prediction error metric called sMACE, which is a significant contribution to the field and could be useful for evaluating the effectiveness of other predictive models.
- The experimental results show that the GSP model can improve supply and inventory prediction accuracy, demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in the area of supply chain forecasting and optimization. This makes it difficult to assess the true novelty and effectiveness of the proposed model.
- The experimental validation is limited, with only one dataset used, which raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the GSP model with other GNN-based models, which could have highlighted the unique advantages of the proposed method.
- The paper's presentation could be improved to make it more accessible to a broader audience, including more detailed explanations of key concepts and figures.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed model, which are important considerations for practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the GSP model compares with other GNN-based models in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the GSP model perform in scenarios with different levels of data availability or data quality?
- Can the authors elaborate on the selection of the GSP model parameters and how these parameters affect the model's performance?
- How does the GSP model handle different types of supply chain networks, such as those with varying levels of complexity or those with different demand patterns?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the size and characteristics of the dataset used, and the specifics of the training and validation processes?
- How does the GSP model handle scenarios where the planned shipment events are not feasible for execution?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and how these could be addressed in future research?
