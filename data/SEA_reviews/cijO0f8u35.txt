 **Summary:**
The paper explores the relationship between pre-training loss, supervised data amount, and augmented data amount in the context of mathematical reasoning abilities of large language models (LLMs). It introduces a novel approach called Rejection Sampling Fine-Tuning (RFT) to augment training data, focusing on improving mathematical reasoning performance. The study reveals that pre-training loss is a better indicator of performance than model parameter count, and there is a log-linear relationship between data amount and model performance. The paper also demonstrates that RFT, which uses supervised models to generate correct reasoning paths, can significantly enhance mathematical reasoning performance. The findings suggest that pre-training loss is a significant factor in determining the model's performance, and that the relationship between data amount and model performance is log-linear.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel analysis of the relationship between pre-training loss, supervised data amount, and augmented data amount in mathematical reasoning tasks, which is crucial for understanding the scaling behavior of large language models (LLMs).
- The introduction of Rejection Sampling Fine-Tuning (RFT) is a significant contribution to the field, offering a practical method for augmenting training data and potentially improving mathematical reasoning performance.
- The paper is well-written and clearly presents the findings, making it accessible and easy to understand.
- The experimental results are solid, and the paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the scaling relationship between LLMs and mathematical reasoning ability.
- The observation that pre-training loss is a better indicator of the model's performance than the model's parameter count is an interesting and important finding.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on mathematical reasoning tasks, which may limit the generalizability of the findings to other types of reasoning tasks or other domains.
- The experiments are primarily conducted on a single dataset (GSM8K), which might not be sufficient to draw robust conclusions. More experiments on different datasets could strengthen the findings.
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the diversity and quality of the augmented data generated by RFT, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the method.
- The comparison of RFT with other methods such as CoRE and PCS is insufficient, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's advantages or disadvantages.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its findings, particularly the generalizability of the results beyond mathematical reasoning tasks.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of computational resources and the generation of biased or inaccurate reasoning paths?
- How does the diversity and quality of the augmented data generated by RFT vary with different model sizes and training data sizes?
- Can you provide more detailed results and analysis on the performance of RFT on other datasets beyond GSM8K?
- How does the RFT method compare in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness with other data augmentation techniques like CoRE and PCS?
- Could you discuss the potential implications of the findings for other types of reasoning tasks beyond mathematical reasoning?
- What are the specific configurations used in the RFT experiments, and how do these configurations affect the performance and diversity of the generated reasoning paths?
