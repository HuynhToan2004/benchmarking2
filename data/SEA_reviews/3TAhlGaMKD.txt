 **Summary:**
The paper examines the security and privacy vulnerabilities of three techniques for adapting Large Language Models (LLMs) with private data: Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), Soft Prompt Tuning (SPT), and In-Context Learning (ICL). It evaluates these methods against various attacks such as membership inference, backdoor, and model stealing. The authors find that while ICL is vulnerable to membership inference attacks, LoRA and SPT are susceptible to backdoor attacks. The paper also discusses the trade-offs between model utility and privacy, suggesting that the choice of method depends on the specific threat model and the amount of private data available.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of the security and privacy properties of LLM adaptation techniques, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The study is novel in its approach to evaluating the privacy and security properties of LLM adaptation techniques, a topic that has not been extensively explored.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the vulnerabilities of different LLM adaptation techniques, which is useful for practitioners and researchers in the field.
- The experimental setup is well-designed, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the trade-offs between model utility and privacy, which is crucial for practitioners and policymakers.
- The experiments are limited to a few datasets, which may not fully represent the range of LLM applications.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which is essential for a complete understanding of the findings.
- There is a lack of comparison with other recent studies that evaluate similar aspects of LLM adaptation techniques, which could provide a more robust evaluation.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the attacks, such as the number of samples used in the membership inference attack, which could affect the reliability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the membership inference attack was implemented, particularly the number of samples used and the methodology for selecting members and non-members?
- How does the choice of trigger word in the backdoor attack affect the results, and what are the implications of using different triggers?
- Could the authors clarify the differences in results between SPT and LoRA in the membership inference attack, especially in relation to the number of samples used in training?
- What are the specific attacks used in the model stealing scenario, and how do these attacks impact the performance of the models?
- Could the authors discuss the implications of the findings for the choice of LLM adaptation techniques in different scenarios, particularly in terms of privacy and security concerns?
