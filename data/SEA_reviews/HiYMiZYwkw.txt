 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Self-guided Masked Autoencoders (SMA), a domain-agnostic self-supervised learning method that leverages masked modeling without relying on domain-specific assumptions. SMA learns masks to sample from attention maps of the first encoding layer during masked prediction training, thereby avoiding the need for domain-specific tokenizers. The method is evaluated across various scientific domains, including protein biology, chemical property prediction, and particle physics, showing state-of-the-art performance. Despite its innovative approach, concerns about the novelty of the method and its generalization to other domains are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel domain-agnostic self-supervised learning method that does not rely on domain-specific assumptions, which is a significant departure from existing methods that heavily rely on domain-specific knowledge.
- The proposed method, Self-guided Masked Autoencoders (SMA), is evaluated on three diverse data modalities, demonstrating its effectiveness and generalization across different domains.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and the experiments are comprehensive, covering a wide range of domains and tasks.
- The proposed method shows promising results, particularly in domains where there is a lack of strong prior knowledge, such as protein biology and chemical property prediction.
- The method's simplicity and the intuitive nature of the masking strategy make it appealing and potentially applicable to various domains.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other domain-agnostic self-supervised learning methods, which could provide a clearer understanding of the method's advantages and limitations.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The method's effectiveness in domains with well-studied priors, such as computer vision and natural language processing, is not explored, which could limit the understanding of its generalizability.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its method from existing domain-agnostic learning methods, particularly those that also leverage attention maps for masking.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently convincing, especially in domains where the method's performance is not as strong as expected, such as in image classification tasks.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study, which could help in understanding the impact of different components of the method on its performance.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method differs from existing domain-agnostic learning methods, particularly in terms of its approach to masking and its effectiveness in different domains?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the generalization of the method to other domains, especially those where it has not been tested?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially when applied to large datasets or complex models?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the method and provide insights into how these could be addressed in future work?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other domain-agnostic self-supervised learning methods, including a discussion on the method's advantages and disadvantages compared to these approaches?
