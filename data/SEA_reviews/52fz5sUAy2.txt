 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the neighborhood effect in recommender systems, proposing a novel framework that integrates causal inference to address selection bias in the presence of neighborhood effects. It introduces a new ideal loss function and two estimators, N-IPS and N-DR, to evaluate this loss. Theoretical analysis is provided to justify the proposed methods, and experimental results on semi-synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The paper also discusses the limitations of existing methods and provides a new perspective on handling selection bias in recommender systems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel perspective by formulating the neighborhood effect as an interference problem from the perspective of causal inference, which is a significant contribution to the field of recommender systems.
- It proposes a new ideal loss that can be used to deal with selection bias in the presence of neighborhood effects, along with two new estimators for estimating the proposed ideal loss.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to justify the proposed methods, and extensive semi-synthetic and real-world experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the theoretical analysis is thorough.
- The paper provides a new perspective on handling selection bias in recommender systems, which is an important issue in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which is essential for practical implementation.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and their implications in practical scenarios.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed or explained, particularly the results in Table 1 and the choice of parameters such as the bandwidth in the kernel density estimation.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the impact of the neighborhood effect on the performance of the proposed methods, which is a critical aspect of the study.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and their implications?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed methods compare to existing methods, and what are the practical implications of this complexity?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and their implications in practical scenarios?
- How does the paper's theoretical analysis account for the potential biases in the estimation of propensity scores, and what impact does this have on the validity of the results?
