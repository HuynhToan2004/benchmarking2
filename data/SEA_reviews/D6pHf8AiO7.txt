 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the FishLeg Surgeon (FLS), a novel second-order pruning method for neural networks that utilizes the Fisher-Legendre optimizer to estimate the importance of weights and compensate for their deletion. FLS employs a meta-learning approach to amortize the inverse Fisher information matrix (FIM), enhancing computational and memory efficiency while maintaining accuracy. This method is shown to outperform other second-order pruning techniques such as M-FAC and oBERT on the ResNet50 model, particularly in high sparsity regimes. The paper also discusses the scalability issues of second-order pruning methods and the advantages of directly estimating the inverse FIM for less sensitivity to stochasticity during inversion.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using the FishLeg optimizer for pruning neural networks, which is a significant advancement in the field of neural network pruning.
- The proposed method, FishLeg Surgeon (FLS), demonstrates impressive results, showing improvements over existing methods such as M-FAC and oBERT, particularly in high sparsity regimes.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The use of the Fisher information matrix (FIM) for pruning is a novel approach that shows promise in improving the accuracy of pruned models.
- The method is scalable and can be applied to both dense and convolutional layers, making it a versatile tool for pruning different types of neural networks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability issues of second-order pruning methods, which is a significant limitation given the focus on this aspect.
- The experiments are limited to small-scale models such as MNIST autoencoder and ResNet50, which may not adequately demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on larger, more complex models.
- The paper does not include comparisons with other state-of-the-art pruning methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The method's performance on larger, more complex models like transformers is not discussed, which is a significant gap in the evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not address the limitations of the method, such as the assumptions made about the inverse Fisher information matrix and the potential inaccuracies in the pruning process due to these assumptions.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed discussion on the scalability issues of second-order pruning methods and how your proposed method addresses these challenges?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art pruning methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- Can you elaborate on the method's performance on larger, more complex models like transformers?
- What are the specific advantages of using the Fisher-Legendre optimizer over other optimization methods for neural network pruning?
- How does the method handle the assumption that the inverse Fisher information matrix can be well approximated by a specific form of positive definite matrix Q(λ)?
- Can you provide more details on the method's initialization and the choice of parameters, particularly how these affect the performance of the pruning process?
