 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the convergence of stochastic first-order methods in the context of composite and distributed optimization, focusing on heavy-tailed noise. It proposes new algorithms that clip the difference between stochastic gradients and shifts, which are updated on the fly, to achieve accelerated convergence rates. The paper also discusses high-probability convergence guarantees for these methods, which are crucial for handling the stochastic nature of the gradients. The authors extend the analysis to distributed settings and provide convergence rates that improve with the number of clients, demonstrating the effectiveness of parallelization. The paper also addresses the challenges posed by heavy-tailed noise and the non-convexity of the objective function, providing a comprehensive theoretical framework for understanding the behavior of stochastic optimization methods under various conditions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of stochastic optimization, focusing on high-probability convergence analysis which is crucial for handling stochastic gradients.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the technical challenges involved in the analysis.
- The proposed algorithms, such as Prox-clipped-SGD-shift and DProx-clipped-SGD-shift, are novel and provide new insights into the convergence behavior of stochastic optimization methods under heavy-tailed noise.
- The paper provides a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of existing methods and proposes new algorithms that address these limitations, supported by rigorous theoretical analysis.
- The convergence rates are shown to improve with the number of clients, demonstrating the effectiveness of parallelization in distributed settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithms, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions may not hold or where the convergence rates are not optimal.
- The paper does not include experimental validation, which could help to demonstrate the practical applicability and effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The assumptions made, such as the bounded central α-th moment and the bounded domain assumption, are quite strong and may not hold in all practical scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the technical challenges involved in the analysis, particularly the use of Bernstein's inequality and the proof techniques used.
- The paper could provide more intuition and insight into the theoretical results, especially in terms of the practical implications and the choice of parameters in the algorithms.
