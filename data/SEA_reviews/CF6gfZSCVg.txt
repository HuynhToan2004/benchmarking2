 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel asynchronous federated bilevel optimization algorithm (AFBO) aimed at addressing the challenges of asynchronous participation, heterogeneous local iteration numbers, and non-IID data in federated bilevel problems. The AFBO algorithm is designed to allow clients to participate in any outer or inner rounds, participate asynchronously, and participate with any number of local iterations, thereby enhancing flexibility and efficiency. Theoretical analysis is provided to support the convergence of the AFBO algorithm, demonstrating a convergence rate of O(1/T) and a linear convergence speedup. The paper also includes numerical experiments to verify the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of asynchronous federated bilevel optimization, which is crucial for improving the efficiency and flexibility of federated learning systems.
- The proposed AFBO algorithm is novel and innovative, allowing clients to participate in any outer or inner rounds, participate asynchronously, and participate with any number of local iterations, which is a significant advancement over existing methods.
- The theoretical analysis provided supports the convergence of the AFBO algorithm, demonstrating a convergence rate of O(1/T) and a linear convergence speedup, which matches that of existing benchmarks.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology and its implications.
- The numerical experiments conducted verify the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm, providing empirical evidence of its utility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of convergence rate and practical implementation.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to a specific setting, which may not be applicable in more general or practical scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of stragglers in the asynchronous setting, which is a significant challenge in federated learning systems.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis are strong and may not hold in practical scenarios, which could limit the applicability of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its practical implications and potential shortcomings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis apply in practical scenarios and discuss the implications of these assumptions on the applicability of the results?
- How does the AFBO algorithm handle the issue of stragglers in the asynchronous setting, and what are the potential impacts on the performance and efficiency of the algorithm?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of convergence rate and practical implementation, to better understand the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method?
- How does the AFBO algorithm perform when the number of clients is very large, and what are the potential impacts on the performance and scalability of the algorithm?
- Could the authors discuss the potential applications of the AFBO algorithm beyond federated learning, such as in other machine learning and communication applications?
