 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to feature selection and explanation using a multilinear game model, support vector machine (SVM), and dynamic programming algorithms. It extends the Shapley value to account for interactions among features, which is typically ignored in traditional additive models. The authors propose a dual SVM solution to compute Shapley values, which are shown to be computationally efficient and scalable. The method is demonstrated to be effective in feature selection tasks, outperforming several baselines in both synthetic and real-world datasets. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its reliance on existing methodologies like the multilinear extension of cooperative games and dual SVM solutions for Shapley value computation. The paper's experimental evaluation is also criticized for its limited scope and lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to feature selection and explanation by extending the Shapley value to account for interactions among features, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method is computationally efficient and scalable, making it suitable for handling large datasets and complex models.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and well-defined problem formulation.
- The use of dual SVM solutions for Shapley value computation is innovative and could potentially improve the accuracy and reliability of feature importance scores.
- The paper provides a comprehensive literature review and positions its work well within the existing body of research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is questioned as it heavily relies on existing methodologies like the multilinear extension of cooperative games and dual SVM solutions for Shapley value computation.
- The experimental evaluation is limited and lacks comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the validity of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its inability to account for higher moments of features and its compatibility with L2 regularization in SVMs.
- The paper's claims regarding the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- The paper's discussion on the related work is insufficient, and it does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods.
- The paper's experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with some results showing marginal improvements or even worse performance compared to baseline methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of their approach and how it differs from existing methods, particularly in terms of the computational methodology and the use of dual SVM solutions for Shapley value computation?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially in handling high-dimensional datasets?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their method and how these limitations affect the applicability and effectiveness of the proposed approach?
- Could the authors provide a more comprehensive experimental evaluation that includes comparisons with a broader range of state-of-the-art methods and addresses the limitations mentioned in the review?
- How does the proposed method handle feature interactions beyond pairwise interactions, and what are the implications of this approach for the computational complexity and the accuracy of the Shapley values?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the number of features and the size of the dataset?
