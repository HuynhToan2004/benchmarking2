 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach called Autonomous Tree-search Ability (ATS) for Large Language Models (LLMs), which allows LLMs to generate search trajectories for solving problems without the need for external programs. ATS is demonstrated to be more efficient and flexible than previous methods, such as the Chain of Thought (CoT) and Tree of Thoughts (ToT), through experiments on four puzzles. The method involves a fixed system prompt for GPT-4 and fine-tuning for smaller models, showing improvements in accuracy and efficiency. The paper also discusses the potential of ATS in enhancing the search capabilities of LLMs, which could be beneficial for complex reasoning tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a good presentation of the proposed approach and experiments.
- The introduction of the autonomous tree-search ability (ATS) is a novel and promising direction in enhancing the reasoning capabilities of LLMs, offering a more flexible and efficient approach compared to existing methods like CoT and ToT.
- The experiments are well-designed, showing significant improvements in accuracy and efficiency, particularly with the fine-tuning of LLaMA models.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison of ATS with other methods, highlighting its advantages in terms of flexibility, efficiency, and capability.
- The dataset collected using the ATS prompt method is valuable for future research, and the approach could be useful in reducing the number of queries to LLMs for complex tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation of ATS is limited to a small set of puzzles, which may not fully demonstrate its generalizability or effectiveness in more complex, real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the limitations of ATS, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of ATS, which could be a significant concern if the technology is adopted widely.
- The experiments are primarily focused on small models, and it is unclear how ATS would perform on larger models like GPT-4, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not discuss how ATS might perform in scenarios where the search space is vast and complex, which could be a significant challenge for the method.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other recent methods, such as Algorithm of Thoughts, which could provide a more robust evaluation of ATS.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how ATS might perform in scenarios where the search space is vast and complex, and how it compares to other methods like CoT and ToT in such scenarios?
- How does ATS handle cases where the search space is infinite or where the optimal solution is not unique?
- Can you elaborate on the design choices for the ATS prompt and how it was tested to ensure its effectiveness?
- How does ATS compare to other methods like Algorithm of Thoughts, especially in terms of efficiency and effectiveness?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of ATS, and how can these be mitigated?
- How does ATS perform on larger models like GPT-4, and what are the implications of this for its practical applicability?
