 **Summary:**
The paper investigates associative memory mechanisms in transformer language models, focusing on the scaling laws with respect to sample size and parameter size. It introduces a simple model that stores associations through outer products of embeddings, comparing different memory storage schemes and their statistical efficiency. Theoretical analyses are provided to understand the statistical efficiency of different estimators and the role of various design choices. The paper also discusses the impact of different optimization algorithms and their effects on memorization in transformers. It validates the theoretical results with extensive numerical experiments, offering a detailed analysis of the statistical efficiency of different estimators and the role of various design choices.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to understanding the scaling laws in associative memory mechanisms, focusing on the inner layers of transformer language models.
- Theoretical analyses are provided to understand the statistical efficiency of different estimators and the role of various design choices, which are supported by extensive numerical experiments.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations and visualizations of the stored memory associations.
- The authors provide precise statistical rates for outer-product memories with random embeddings, which are compared to the weights learned by various optimization algorithms used in practice.
- The paper offers a detailed analysis of the statistical efficiency of different estimators and the role of various design choices, which is supported by extensive numerical experiments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for why understanding the scaling laws of associative memory mechanisms is important, especially in the context of large language models (LLMs).
- The experimental setup is limited to simple models, which may not adequately represent the complexity of real-world LLMs.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the practical implications of the findings, such as how the results can be applied to improve the design of LLMs.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the results can be extended to more complex models that include attention and feed-forward layers, which are crucial components in modern LLMs.
- The paper does not address the limitations of its approach, particularly how the findings might not generalize to more complex models or real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the motivation behind studying the scaling laws of associative memory mechanisms in LLMs?
- How can the findings of this paper be applied to improve the design of LLMs?
- How do the results of this paper compare to other works on scaling laws in LLMs, such as those by Kaplan et al. (2020) and Hoffmann et al. (2022)?
- Could you discuss the potential limitations of your approach and how the findings might not generalize to more complex models or real-world scenarios?
- In the context of the experimental setup, how does the simplicity of the models used affect the validity of the results?
- How do the findings of this paper relate to other recent works on scaling laws in LLMs, such as those by Bahri et al. (2021), Maloney et al. (2022), Sorscher et al. (2022), and Sukhbaatar et al. (2019)?
