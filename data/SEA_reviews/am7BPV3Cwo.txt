 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the issue of out-of-distribution (OOD) detection in scenarios where data is imbalanced, specifically focusing on the disparate decision spaces between tail and head classes. It introduces a statistical framework, ImOOD, which aims to calibrate OOD detectors by adjusting the class-aware bias item between balanced and imbalanced OOD detection. The framework employs both post-hoc normalization and training-time regularization techniques to enhance the performance of OOD detectors on imbalanced datasets. The paper presents empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods on benchmarks like CIFAR10-LT, CIFAR100-LT, and ImageNet-LT. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, unclear theoretical justification, and insufficient experimental validation on larger, more varied datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a good explanation of the main idea.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and easy to implement, with good empirical performance on three representative benchmarks.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the imbalanced OOD detection problem and present a unified perspective of post-hoc normalization and training-time regularization techniques.
- The paper introduces a statistical framework, ImOOD, to formulate and explain the imbalanced OOD detection issue, which could potentially be applied to various OOD detection methods.
- The proposed method achieves superior OOD detection performance on three representative benchmarks, outperforming the state-of-the-art method by a significant margin.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as similar methods have been previously proposed, such as adjusting the class-aware bias item and using post-hoc normalization and training-time regularization techniques.
- The theoretical justification of the proposed method is weak, with the theoretical analysis not clearly explaining why the proposed method works effectively.
- The paper lacks sufficient experimental validation, particularly on larger, more varied datasets, and does not include comparisons with other recent OOD detection methods that also address the imbalanced OOD detection problem.
- The performance improvement over existing methods is not significant, and the paper does not provide sufficient analysis or explanation for the observed performance improvements.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect the practical applicability and robustness of the findings.
- The method requires access to the full training set, which may not be feasible in real-world scenarios, and does not discuss potential solutions to this limitation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the theoretical justification of the proposed method, particularly why it works effectively?
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent OOD detection methods that also address the imbalanced OOD detection problem?
- Can the authors provide more experimental results on larger, more varied datasets to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method on a broader scale?
- Why does the proposed method not significantly improve the performance over existing methods, and what are the specific reasons for the observed performance improvements?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and potential solutions to address these limitations in practical applications?
