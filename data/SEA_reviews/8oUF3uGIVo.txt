 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to enhancing graph transformers by incorporating a high-order message-passing strategy within the Transformer architecture. This strategy involves the creation of virtual nodes to capture high-order community information and facilitate long-range information passing. The method is evaluated across various datasets and tasks, showing competitive performance and effectiveness in node classification tasks. The paper also explores the theoretical foundations of the approach, discussing its expressiveness and its relationship to other graph transformers. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its incremental nature and lack of comprehensive comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to enhance the expressiveness of graph transformers by incorporating a high-order message-passing strategy, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method is theoretically grounded, providing a strong foundation for its practical application.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The empirical results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and tasks.
- The use of virtual nodes to capture high-order community information and facilitate long-range information passing is a novel and effective approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's methodology is seen as incremental, building on existing works such as Graph Transformers and Virtual Nodes, which limits its novelty.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which is crucial for establishing the superiority of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for a balanced view of its strengths and weaknesses.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and performance on larger datasets.
- The paper's theoretical analysis and proofs are not rigorously presented, which could affect the credibility of the claims made.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for its practical application in large-scale graph learning tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of their method in relation to existing works, particularly in terms of the use of virtual nodes and community-based message passing?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other hypergraph-based methods to establish the superiority of their approach?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, especially in terms of capturing high-order correlations in complex graphs?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the hyperparameters used and the datasets' characteristics, to better understand the results?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of over-smoothing, which is a common problem in graph neural networks?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their method and suggest areas for future research to address these limitations?
