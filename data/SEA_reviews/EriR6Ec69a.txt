 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of low-rank, sparse recurrent connectivity in closed-loop control systems to enhance robustness against distribution shifts. It introduces a parameterization of recurrent weights using a singular value decomposition (SVD) and a binary mask to control sparsity. Theoretical and empirical analyses are provided to support the effectiveness of this parameterization in reducing spectral radius and spectral norm, which are indicative of robustness and generalization. The study is supported by experiments across various architectures, including RNNs, LSTMs, and GRUs, in different environments, showing that low-rank, low-sparsity CfCs perform best under distribution shifts. However, the paper's contribution is limited by its focus on specific architectures and its lack of comparison with other methods, such as antisymmetric RNNs or Lipschitz RNNs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined problem.
- The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experiments are extensive, covering various architectures and environments, including MuJoCo and Atari.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the spectral properties of the recurrent weights, which is interesting and relevant to the field.
- The use of low-rank and sparse recurrent neural networks (RNNs) is innovative and could potentially reduce the number of parameters needed for robustness in closed-loop settings.
- The paper includes a detailed appendix that provides additional insights and justifications for the methodology used.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader comparison with other methods, such as antisymmetric RNNs or Lipschitz RNNs, which could have strengthened the argument for the superiority of the proposed method.
- The focus on closed-loop control and the specific task of playing games in Atari environments might limit the generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks or environments.
- The paper's theoretical claims, particularly regarding the spectral properties of the recurrent weights, are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence, especially in the context of the full singular value spectrum.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for overfitting or the impact of different initialization methods on the results.
- The paper's title and some of its claims might be overly ambitious, potentially misleading readers about the scope and applicability of the research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition of "in-distribution" and "out-of-distribution" in the context of the experiments?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods, such as antisymmetric RNNs or Lipschitz RNNs, in terms of robustness and generalization?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, especially for the Alien and HalfCheetah environments, to support the claims made about the robustness of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of distribution shifts, such as those that might affect the input or the transition dynamics?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the SVD parameterization over other methods, such as pruning weights or using antisymmetric or Lipschitz weights?
- In light of the theoretical claims about the spectral properties of the recurrent weights, can the authors provide more empirical evidence to support these claims, especially in the context of the full singular value spectrum?
