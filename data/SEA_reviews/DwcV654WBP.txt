 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training strategy for pre-training video foundation models, focusing on the use of ASR transcripts and a partially frozen text encoder to address the issue of performance degradation in existing video models. The strategy involves freezing the shallow layers of the text encoder while allowing deeper layers to adapt, aiming to preserve the generalization ability of the text encoder. This methodology is validated through extensive experiments, demonstrating improved performance in various video tasks such as text-to-video retrieval and action recognition. The paper also explores the scalability of the training method using masked video encoders, which reduces computational overhead. Despite its strengths, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty and the need for a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the performance degradation of existing video models and proposes a novel training strategy to address this issue.
- The proposed training strategy, which includes a partially frozen text encoder, is simple, effective, and can be easily integrated into existing video pre-training pipelines.
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the research methodology and results, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.
- The experiments are thorough, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various video tasks, including zero-shot and linear probe evaluations.
- The use of ASR transcripts as supervision signals is highlighted as a significant advantage, providing a valuable temporal dependency that facilitates temporal reasoning.
- The paper offers a detailed analysis of the impact of different training strategies and model architectures on the performance of video models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and training efficiency, which could undermine the claims of its novelty and effectiveness.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of generalization ability in video models, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The use of ASR transcripts as supervision signals introduces noise, which could affect the performance of the model, and the paper does not adequately address this issue.
- The paper does not explore the application of the proposed method to other video tasks, such as action recognition and anomaly detection, which could limit its practical utility.
- The paper's focus on improving the performance of video models without addressing broader societal impacts could limit its relevance and applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed training strategy compare in terms of performance and training efficiency with existing methods, particularly in large-scale training scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process, such as the specific hyperparameters used and the computational resources required?
- How does the proposed method handle the noise introduced by ASR transcripts, and what steps are taken to mitigate this issue?
- Can the proposed method be applied to other video tasks beyond text-to-video retrieval and action recognition, and if so, what are the expected outcomes?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of generalization ability in video models, and what steps are taken to ensure that the model can adapt to new, unseen data?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the scalability of the training method, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for training larger models?
