 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the use of video data for pre-training representations in reinforcement learning (RL) settings, focusing on the Block MDP and Ex-Block MDP frameworks. It theoretically explores the learning of latent state representations from video data, comparing it to trajectory data. The study includes empirical evaluations in two visual domains, showing that the proposed methods can learn latent states efficiently in the absence of exogenous noise but struggle in its presence. The paper provides theoretical upper and lower bounds for learning latent states from video data, highlighting the challenges of learning from video data compared to trajectory data. However, the paper's theoretical contributions are seen as incremental and the empirical evaluations are criticized for not sufficiently demonstrating the advantages of the proposed methods over existing approaches.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive literature review and a clear exposition of the problem and the proposed solution.
- The theoretical framework is well-structured, and the assumptions are well-justified, which supports the theoretical claims.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the limitations of learning from video data compared to trajectory data, which is an important contribution to the field.
- The empirical results are well-presented and support the theoretical findings, showing that the proposed methods can learn latent states efficiently in the absence of exogenous noise.
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem in the field of RL, focusing on the use of video data for pre-training representations, which is a promising direction for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical contributions are seen as incremental and do not significantly advance the current understanding of the problem.
- The empirical evaluations are criticized for not adequately demonstrating the advantages of the proposed methods over existing approaches, such as ACRO.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly Assumption 3, are considered too strong and not well-justified, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's reliance on specific types of video data (e.g., video from agents navigating in the world) may limit its applicability to other types of video data, such as those from different domains or different types of agents.
- The paper's focus on a specific type of video data could limit its broader applicability and relevance to other areas of RL research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly Assumption 3, and provide more detailed justification or examples where these assumptions hold?
- How do the authors justify the use of specific types of video data in their experiments, and can they discuss the implications of using different types of video data for their proposed methods?
- Can the authors provide more details on the empirical evaluations, particularly how the proposed methods compare to existing approaches in terms of sample efficiency and other performance metrics?
- How do the authors address the concerns about the incremental nature of their theoretical contributions, and can they discuss potential directions for future research to advance the field?
