 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for machine unlearning in large language models (LLMs) through selective pruning of neurons based on their importance to specific capabilities. This approach is evaluated across various models, including OPT, Galactica, Pythia, RoBERTa, and ViT, and across different tasks such as coding, Python, and image classification. The authors demonstrate that pruning neurons based on their relative importance to different datasets can effectively forget specific skills while retaining others. The method is shown to be effective in maintaining performance on the retain dataset while significantly reducing performance on the forget dataset. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticisms for its limited scope in terms of the number of datasets and models evaluated, and the lack of a broader impact analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for machine unlearning in large language models (LLMs) that is both task-agnostic and can be applied to any dataset and task.
- The method is straightforward, well-explained, and supported by extensive experiments across different models and tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in forgetting specific skills while retaining others.
- The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of their method, comparing it to existing methods and demonstrating its superior performance in maintaining performance on the retain dataset while significantly reducing performance on the forget dataset.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a detailed analysis of the method, including its limitations and future directions.
- The experimental setup is solid, with thorough evaluations across different models and tasks, demonstrating the robustness of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader impact analysis, particularly in terms of the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to scenarios where the capability to be forgotten is neatly captured by a dataset, which may not be applicable in many practical scenarios.
- The paper does not evaluate the method on the retrainability metric, which is a popular evaluation metric for machine unlearning methods.
- The method's generalizability is limited as it has only been tested on a small set of models and datasets, raising concerns about its applicability to other models and tasks.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of why the method works, which could limit its understanding and adoption by the community.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and does not provide sufficient theoretical or empirical evidence to support its claims.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a clearer explanation of why the method works and how it can be generalized to other models and tasks?
- How does the method perform when the capability to be forgotten is not neatly captured by a dataset?
- Could the authors evaluate the method on the retrainability metric to provide a more comprehensive evaluation?
- How does the method compare to other methods in terms of efficiency and effectiveness, especially in scenarios where the capability to be forgotten is not neatly captured by a dataset?
- Is there a theoretical explanation for why the method works, or is it purely empirical?
- How does the method perform when applied to different models and tasks, and what are the implications of these differences in performance?
