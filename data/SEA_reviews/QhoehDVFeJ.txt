 **Summary:**
The paper introduces EMA (Efficient Meshy Neural Fields), a method for creating animatable human avatars from video sequences using a rasterization-based differentiable renderer. EMA employs a combination of neural fields to optimize canonical shapes, materials, and motion dynamics, achieving real-time rendering and training efficiency. The method is evaluated against existing approaches, showing improvements in training time and rendering quality. However, the paper is criticized for its incremental nature and lack of significant novelty in its technical contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly explained, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method achieves faster training speed and real-time inference speed, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper presents a novel approach by combining canonical geometry, materials, lights, and motions, which is a significant advancement in the field of human avatar modeling.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the method, demonstrating its effectiveness through various experiments and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.
- The methodology is supported by thorough ablation studies and comparisons, which validate the proposed method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of training and inference speed, which is crucial for assessing the method's efficiency.
- The novelty of the technical contributions is questionable, as many components are derived from existing methods, which may not justify the paper's contribution to the field.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, which could hinder its adoption by the community.
- The paper's methodology is not sufficiently detailed, particularly in the implementation of the rasterization-based differentiable renderer, which is crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness.
- The paper's focus on training and inference speed does not adequately address the quality of the avatars generated, which could be a significant concern for applications requiring high-fidelity avatars.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the training and inference speeds of their method with existing methods, including a breakdown of the time spent on different components of the pipeline?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of avatar quality, especially in terms of geometric accuracy and texture detail, with other methods that focus on high-fidelity avatar generation?
- Could the authors clarify the methodology used in the rasterization-based differentiable renderer, particularly how the mesh is rasterized and how the gradients are computed?
- How does the method handle different lighting conditions, especially in scenarios where the lighting is not constant or varies significantly across different frames?
- Could the authors provide more details on the initialization and optimization of the SDF field, especially how it affects the quality of the generated avatars?
- How does the method handle the optimization of the SDF field, and what are the implications for the quality of the generated avatars if this optimization is not performed correctly?
