 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel adversarial defense method called Implicit Representation-driven Image Resampling (IRAD), which leverages implicit representations to enhance the robustness of deep models against adversarial attacks. The IRAD method involves reconstructing continuous representations from discrete images using bilinear or nearest interpolation, followed by a sampling process that shifts pixel coordinates to generate a new image. This approach is evaluated on various datasets such as CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and ImageNet, showing significant improvements in robust accuracy while maintaining high accuracy on clean images. The method is compared against existing defenses and demonstrates superior performance in both oblivious and adaptive adversary scenarios. However, concerns were raised regarding the clarity of the method's presentation, the novelty of the approach, and the empirical evidence supporting its effectiveness across different datasets and attack scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise language, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed method, IRAD, is simple, effective, and well-motivated, with a novel approach to image resampling for adversarial defense.
- The paper includes comprehensive experiments and ablation studies, which are well-presented and provide valuable insights into the performance of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and logical flow, which enhances understanding of the proposed method and its applications.
- The method demonstrates effectiveness in both oblivious and adaptive adversary scenarios, showing significant improvements in robust accuracy while maintaining high accuracy on clean images.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the motivation behind using implicit representations for adversarial defense, which could be better articulated in the introduction.
- The empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed method is limited to specific datasets and attack scenarios. More comprehensive evaluations on different datasets and against a broader range of attacks are needed to substantiate the claims.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing state-of-the-art methods, such as DiffPure, which could have provided a better understanding of its relative performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of adversarial attacks and its generalization across different datasets.
- There are some issues with the clarity and presentation of the method, including unclear formulations and potential errors in the mathematical representations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a clearer explanation of how the implicit representation is used to remove adversarial perturbations and how this relates to the overall defense mechanism?
- How does the proposed method perform against different types of adversarial attacks, such as transfer attacks and adaptive attacks?
- Can the authors clarify the training strategy for the SampleNet and explain how it is trained using adversarial denoising tasks?
- How does the method perform in terms of computational efficiency, especially in real-time applications?
- In the context of the method's generalization, can the authors provide more detailed results on how the method performs when trained on one dataset and tested on another?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, such as DiffPure, to better understand the relative performance of their proposed method?
