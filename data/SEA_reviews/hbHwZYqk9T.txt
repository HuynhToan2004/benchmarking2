 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedP3, a federated learning framework that addresses model heterogeneity across clients by employing pruning techniques. This approach allows each client to train a subset of the model, thereby reducing communication costs and improving privacy. The framework includes both global and local pruning strategies, which are adaptable to each client's computational capabilities. Theoretical analyses are provided to support the convergence and communication efficiency of the proposed method. However, the experiments are limited to small datasets and simple neural networks, which may not adequately demonstrate the framework's effectiveness in more complex or varied scenarios. The paper also lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods and a detailed discussion on the practical implications of the proposed techniques.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant issue of model heterogeneity in federated learning, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The proposed method is simple, easy to understand, and theoretically justified, providing a clear explanation of the convergence and communication efficiency of the proposed FedP3.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The framework is versatile and adaptable, allowing for customization based on each client's unique constraints, which enhances its practical applicability.
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the convergence and communication costs of the proposed method, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experiments are limited to small datasets and simple neural networks, which may not adequately demonstrate the framework's effectiveness in more complex or varied scenarios.
- There is a lack of comprehensive comparison with existing methods, which is necessary to establish the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implications of the proposed techniques, particularly in terms of their scalability and applicability to larger or more complex models.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the privacy implications of the proposed method, which is a significant concern given the focus on privacy-preserving federated learning.
- The theoretical analysis and experimental results do not convincingly support the claimed improvements in communication efficiency and model performance.
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation from existing methods, such as FedAvg and FedProx, which may lead to confusion about the novel contributions of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the neural networks used and the data splits employed?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods in terms of communication efficiency and model performance?
- Can the authors clarify the privacy implications of the proposed method and discuss how it addresses or mitigates the privacy concerns in federated learning?
- How does the proposed method handle the heterogeneity in data distributions across clients, and what are the potential impacts on the model's performance and generalization?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of the convergence rates and communication costs?
- How does the proposed method scale with larger or more complex models, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in such scenarios?
