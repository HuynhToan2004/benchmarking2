 **Summary:**
This paper investigates the in-context learning (ICL) mechanism in large language models (LLMs) by employing neuroscience-inspired techniques such as representational similarity analysis (RSA) and parameterized probing to measure the attention ratio between relevant and irrelevant information. The authors explore how ICL influences LLM behavior and performance on tasks like reading comprehension, linear regression, and adversarial prompt injection. They propose a novel method for computing the attention ratio and conduct experiments on two open-source LLMs, Vicuna-1.3 and Llama-2, to understand the neural mechanisms underlying ICL. The paper provides insights into how ICL affects embeddings and attention representations, contributing to a better understanding of how LLMs process information and learn from context.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a solid experimental design.
- The analysis of attention ratio is novel and provides a new perspective on how LLMs process information, contributing to a better understanding of the mechanisms behind in-context learning (ICL).
- The paper proposes a parameter-free method for computing the attention ratio, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The use of representational similarity analysis (RSA) and parameterized probing is well-executed, offering valuable insights into how in-context learning (ICL) affects large language models (LLMs).
- The experiments are well-designed, with a focus on tasks with a priori relationships among their conditions, which allows for a more controlled analysis of ICL.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the neuroscience-inspired techniques used, such as representational similarity analysis (RSA) and parameterized probing, to make the content more accessible to readers unfamiliar with these concepts.
- The novelty of the proposed attention ratio method is questionable, as it seems similar to existing methods in the field, and the paper lacks a detailed comparison with these methods.
- The experimental design is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on tasks with a priori relationships among their conditions, which might not fully capture the complexity and variability of real-world applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and a deeper exploration of how the findings might apply to other types of tasks or models.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other methods for measuring attention, which could provide a more robust validation of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**

- How do the findings of this study apply to other types of tasks or models, and what are the potential limitations of the proposed methods?
- Could you provide more details on the neuroscience-inspired techniques used, such as RSA and parameterized probing, to make the content more accessible to readers unfamiliar with these concepts?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of the experimental design, such as the focus on tasks with a priori relationships among their conditions, and what steps were taken to ensure the generalizability of the findings?
