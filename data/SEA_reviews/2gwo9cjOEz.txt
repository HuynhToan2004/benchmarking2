 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the alignment between the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) and the output of a neural network in the context of graph neural networks (GNNs), focusing on the graph shift operator (GSO) derived from the cross-covariance between input and output data. It proposes that this approach optimizes the alignment, potentially improving training and generalization performance. The theoretical analysis is supported by experiments on the HCP-YA dataset, showing that GNNs with cross-covariance as the graph shift operator outperform those that operate on the covariance matrix from only the input data. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the design of GNNs and provides a detailed theoretical analysis of the NTK and GSO in GNNs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the theoretical analysis and experimental results.
- The theoretical analysis is solid and provides a detailed characterization of the NTK for GNNs, particularly focusing on the two-layer GNN.
- The paper introduces the concept of alignment and its optimization, which is a novel approach in the context of GNNs.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that GNNs with cross-covariance as the graph shift operator outperform those that operate on the covariance matrix from only the input data.
- The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of the NTK and GSO in GNNs, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's motivation and the connection between the theoretical analysis and practical applications are not clearly articulated.
- The experiments are limited to a single dataset and do not include comparisons with other methods or baselines, which could undermine the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper primarily focuses on two-layer GNNs, which may not be representative of more complex or practical GNN architectures.
- The optimization of the GSO in the experiments is not well-explained, and the practical implications of the theoretical results are not clearly demonstrated.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach, such as the potential negative effects of using cross-covariance in GNNs, which could include increased computational complexity or the need for large amounts of data.

**Questions:**

- How does the paper address the potential negative effects of using cross-covariance in GNNs, such as increased computational complexity or the need for large amounts of data?
