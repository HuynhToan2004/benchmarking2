 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel parameter-free, model-agnostic fine-tuning approach called Collaborative Adaptation with Gradient Learning (CoLA) that decouples the computation of the gradient of hidden representations and parameters. This method is designed to reduce computational costs in Fine-Tuning as a Service (FTaaS) by offloading the computation of the gradient to low-cost devices. Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate that CoLA can perform on par or better than existing Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods on various benchmarks. The approach is also shown to be scalable and effective in collaborative learning settings, where multiple users contribute their local models for aggregation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to parameter-free, model-agnostic fine-tuning, which is both scalable and effective in collaborative learning settings.
- The method is supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results that demonstrate its effectiveness in reducing computational costs and improving performance.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are extensive, covering various benchmarks and settings.
- The proposed method is original and offers a new perspective on fine-tuning, which could lead to significant advancements in the field.
- The experimental results are impressive, showing that the proposed method can perform on par or better than existing PEFT methods on various benchmarks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and applicability to different model architectures.
- There is a need for more comprehensive ablation studies to understand the impact of various hyperparameters on the performance of the method.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the experimental setups, including the specifics of the datasets and the experimental conditions.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing PEFT methods in terms of computational efficiency and cost-effectiveness, particularly in large-scale collaborative learning settings.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is not well-integrated with the experimental results, and there is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the theoretical contributions.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation of the method's performance on larger-scale datasets and more complex models to validate its scalability and effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method can be applied to different model architectures and what are the limitations in terms of scalability?
- How does the method perform in large-scale collaborative learning settings, and what are the computational costs associated with such settings?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the experimental setups, including the specifics of the datasets and the experimental conditions?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing PEFT methods in terms of computational efficiency and cost-effectiveness?
- Can the authors provide more examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications of the proposed method in real-world scenarios?
- How does the method handle the issue of gradient offloading, and what are the implications for the accuracy and efficiency of the training process?
