 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel defense mechanism, Adversarial Feature Map Pruning for Backdoor (FMP), aimed at mitigating backdoor attacks in deep neural networks (DNNs). FMP operates by pruning backdoor feature maps, which are trained to extract backdoor information from inputs. This approach allows for the fine-tuning of models using a secure subset of training data, thereby enhancing model robustness against backdoor attacks. The paper presents extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of FMP in reducing attack success rates and maintaining high robust accuracy across multiple datasets. However, the paper's scope is limited to vision-based tasks, and its novelty is questioned due to similarities with existing methods like Fine-Pruning and Neural Cleanse.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the presentation is clear and concise.
- The proposed method, Adversarial Feature Map Pruning for Backdoor (FMP), is novel and effective, as demonstrated by extensive experiments.
- FMP is straightforward and can be easily implemented, with the code provided in the supplementary material.
- The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in the field of backdoor attacks, focusing on the mitigation of backdoor attacks in deep neural networks (DNNs).
- The experiments conducted are thorough, covering multiple datasets and attack scenarios, and the results consistently show that FMP outperforms existing state-of-the-art defense methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights for future research.
- The novelty of the paper is questionable as it resembles existing methods like Fine-Pruning and Neural Cleanse, particularly in the use of feature map pruning.
- The methodology's dependence on the feature map's characteristics raises concerns about its effectiveness in scenarios where backdoor triggers are not strongly correlated with specific features.
- The paper's scope is limited to vision-based tasks, and it does not explore the application of the method to other types of tasks such as NLP or speech recognition.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed or convincing, particularly in terms of the robustness of the model against different types of attacks and the impact of different pruning strategies.
- There are several grammatical and formatting errors that need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- How does FMP perform against other types of attacks, such as those that target different layers or use different triggers?
- Can the authors provide more details on the impact of different pruning strategies on the model's performance and robustness?
- How does FMP compare to other defense methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical deployment in real-world scenarios?
- What are the potential limitations of FMP, and how might these affect its effectiveness in different scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the method's effectiveness in scenarios where backdoor triggers are not strongly correlated with specific features?
- How does the paper address the issue of overfitting, particularly in the context of feature map pruning?
- The paper mentions that the method is effective in scenarios where the poisoning rate is low. Can the authors provide more details on how the method performs under different poisoning conditions?
