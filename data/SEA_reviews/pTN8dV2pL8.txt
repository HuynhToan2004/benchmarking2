 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for reconstructing reflective objects using a Gaussian-based representation of normals in Signed Distance Functions (SDFs). This method leverages polarization priors to guide the learning of geometry behind specular reflection, addressing the challenges of entangled specular radiance and complicated geometry in reflective scenes. The authors propose a reweighting strategy to mitigate noise issues in polarization priors and introduce a new dataset to evaluate the proposed method's effectiveness. The method is validated through qualitative and quantitative comparisons, demonstrating improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised regarding the method's generalization to more complex, real-world scenes and the clarity of some experimental setups.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for reconstructing reflective objects using a Gaussian-based representation of normals in Signed Distance Functions (SDFs), which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is well-motivated and supported by thorough experimental validation, demonstrating its effectiveness in reconstructing reflective objects with high accuracy.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear illustrations and detailed explanations of the proposed method and its components.
- The use of polarization priors to guide the learning of geometry behind specular reflection is innovative and addresses a critical challenge in the reconstruction of reflective objects.
- The introduction of a reweighting strategy to mitigate noise issues in polarization priors is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The paper includes a new dataset that is specifically designed for evaluating the proposed method, enhancing the credibility of the results and the utility of the research for the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the normal estimation process, which is crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness and limitations.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the dataset used, such as the number of images, their resolution, and the field of view, which are necessary for evaluating the method's robustness and generalization.
- The method's applicability to more complex, real-world scenes with varying illumination conditions and complex materials is not thoroughly addressed, raising concerns about its practicality and scalability.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its scope and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, including those that use different types of polarization priors or other supervision signals.
- The paper does not address how the method would perform in scenarios with more complex, real-world lighting conditions, which could significantly impact the reconstruction quality.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by addressing some typographical and formatting errors, such as missing citations and unclear equations.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the normal estimation process, including the methodology and the accuracy of the normals derived?
- How does the proposed method handle more complex, real-world scenes with varying illumination conditions and complex materials?
- Could the authors elaborate on the method's scalability and applicability to larger, more complex scenes?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods that use different types of polarization priors or other supervision signals?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of the method's limitations on its practical application and future research directions?
- How does the method perform in scenarios with more complex, real-world lighting conditions?
- Could the authors clarify the meaning and usage of certain terms and equations, such as the "Reweighted AoP Priors" and the "DoP reweighing strategy"?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the dataset's characteristics and the specifics of the evaluation metrics used?
