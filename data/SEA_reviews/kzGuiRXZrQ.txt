 **Summary:**
The paper introduces EQGAT-diff, a novel equivariant diffusion model for de novo 3D molecular design, which integrates a time-dependent loss weighting mechanism and leverages E(3)-equivariant graph attention. The model demonstrates improved performance over existing models like EDM and MiDi on benchmarks such as QM9 and GEOM-Drugs, achieving state-of-the-art results. EQGAT-diff also effectively utilizes additional features like hybridization states to enhance the validity of generated molecules. The paper explores the design space of diffusion models, comparing various parameterizations and loss weightings, and provides a thorough ablation study across popular benchmark sets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear introduction of the problem and the proposed method.
- The proposed EQGAT-diff model achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) results in terms of performance and training time compared to previous architectures.
- The paper conducts an extensive ablation study on the design choices of 3D molecular diffusion models, providing insights into the factors that contribute to the model's effectiveness.
- The methodology is technically sound and presents a novel approach to molecular design, particularly in the area of 3D molecular diffusion models.
- The paper provides a thorough exploration of the design space of 3D molecular diffusion models, including various parameterizations, loss weightings, data, and input feature modalities.
- The results are promising, showing that the proposed model outperforms existing models in terms of performance and training time.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed model.
- The novelty of the proposed model is questioned, as it seems to be a simple combination of existing methods such as EQGAT and time-dependent loss weighting.
- There is a lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of performance metrics like training time and model parameters.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the transferability of the model to other datasets, which is crucial for evaluating its generalizability.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the model to larger datasets or more complex molecular structures, which could limit its practical applicability.
- There are concerns about the reproducibility of the results due to the absence of a code release.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role of the "x0" parameterization in your model, as it appears to be a minor modification over the "x" parameterization?
- How does the model perform on larger datasets or more complex molecular structures, such as those found in the Enamine REAL Space?
- Can you provide more details on the training and inference times for EQGAT-diff, particularly in comparison to other models like EDM and MiDi?
- How does the model handle the transferability to other datasets, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
- Could you elaborate on the decision to use a composite loss function in your model, and how this affects the model's performance and interpretability?
- How do you address the potential issues of overfitting or underfitting in your model, and what steps have been taken to mitigate these risks?
