 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework, SCEIKG, designed to enhance the accuracy of traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) recommendations by integrating patient state dynamics and domain knowledge. The framework leverages a sequential prescription-making problem approach, incorporating an interaction knowledge graph to model the relationships between symptoms and herbs. The experimental results on a real-world dataset demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing TCM recommendation methods. However, the paper lacks a detailed explanation of the model's components and the experimental setup, which could hinder the understanding and reproducibility of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, SCEIKG, which integrates patient state dynamics and domain knowledge to enhance the accuracy of TCM recommendations.
- The proposed method is technically sound, with a well-designed model architecture and a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing TCM recommendation methods.
- The paper provides a detailed explanation of the framework, including the model architecture and the training process, which is supported by a thorough evaluation and analysis of the results.
- The integration of domain knowledge from TCM into the model is highlighted as a significant contribution, enhancing the understanding and application of TCM principles in the recommendation system.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in TCM by considering the dynamics of patient conditions across multiple diagnoses, which is a novel approach in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the model's components, such as the horizontal patient representation and the transition condition module, which could hinder the understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The experimental setup and dataset used are not clearly described, making it difficult to assess the generalizability and applicability of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts, which are crucial for understanding the broader implications of the work.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the model, which is essential for assessing its scalability and efficiency.
- The paper does not adequately address the interpretability of the model, which is crucial for understanding how the model makes recommendations and for building trust in the system.
- The experimental results are limited to a single dataset, which raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the model's components, particularly the horizontal patient representation and the transition condition module?
- How does the proposed method handle the variability in patient conditions, especially in scenarios where the same prescription may not have the same effect on different patients?
- What is the specific role and contribution of the IKG in the model, and how does it enhance the recommendation accuracy?
- How does the model handle the integration of domain knowledge from TCM, and what are the specific TCM principles that are incorporated?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts?
- How does the model handle the computational complexity, and what are the scalability and efficiency implications of the model?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the dataset used and the specifics of the experimental design?
- How does the model ensure the interpretability of its recommendations, and what steps are taken to build trust in the system?
