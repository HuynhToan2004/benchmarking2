 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for causal effect estimation, specifically focusing on non-linear conditional instrumental variable (CIV) regression with Confounding Balancing Representation Learning (CBRL.CIV). This method aims to balance the distribution of confounders between different groups and reduce the bias caused by unobserved confounders. The paper presents a three-stage methodology that includes a CIV regression for balancing distributions, a treatment regression for balancing distributions between treatment and outcome, and an outcome regression using a learned balancing representation. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper suffers from several issues such as unclear presentation, lack of clarity in assumptions, and potential errors in proofs and experimental setups.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in causal inference by considering non-linear relationships and the effect of unobserved confounders.
- The proposed method, CBRL.CIV, is novel and theoretically sound, providing a practical solution for balancing distributions and reducing bias caused by unobserved confounders.
- The paper is well-structured and provides a clear motivation for the research, with extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method is applicable in various fields where causal effect estimation is crucial, and it is the first work to address the challenging problem of balancing distributions in CIV regression.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the proposed method is straightforward, which enhances its practical applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the assumptions, particularly the assumptions of positivity and independent compliance type, which are crucial for the validity of the results.
- The proof of Theorem 1 contains errors, specifically in the identification of the counterfactual prediction function, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as the requirement for a valid CIV and the assumption of independent compliance type, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with issues such as inconsistencies in the presentation of results and the lack of detailed comparisons with baseline methods.
- The paper suffers from poor presentation and lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the method and the assumptions.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide a detailed explanation of how the method handles the unobserved confounders and ensure that the CIV is valid?
- How does the method handle the non-linear relationship between variables, especially in the context of causal inference?
- Could you clarify the assumptions made in the method, particularly the assumptions of positivity and independent compliance type?
- In the proof of Theorem 1, could you correct the identification of the counterfactual prediction function to ensure the validity of the results?
- How does the method perform when the assumptions are not met, and what are the implications for the validity of the results?
- Could you provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially in large-scale applications?
- Can you compare the performance of the proposed method with other baseline methods, such as CFR and CBIV, in more detail to better establish its advantages?
