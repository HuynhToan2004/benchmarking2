 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MaskComp, a novel method for object completion that employs an iterative mask denoising (IMD) process to refine object masks from partial objects. This method integrates image generation and segmentation to enhance the accuracy and completeness of object masks, which in turn improves the quality of the generated objects. The IMD process involves a sequence of generation and segmentation steps, where the segmentation model is used to refine the object mask based on the generated images. The paper also explores the use of ControlNet for mask-conditioned image generation, which is a key component of the IMD process. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of MaskComp over existing methods, showcasing its ability to handle heavy occlusions and generate realistic and detailed objects.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to object completion by integrating image generation and segmentation, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed iterative mask denoising (IMD) process is innovative and effective, as demonstrated by the experimental results.
- The use of ControlNet for mask-conditioned image generation is a novel application that enhances the quality of the generated images.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear figures and detailed explanations of the methodology.
- The experimental results are strong, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in terms of both quantitative and qualitative metrics.
- The method's ability to handle heavy occlusions and generate realistic and detailed objects is a notable strength, as shown in the figures and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and failure cases of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights for future research.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of inference time and computational efficiency.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the impact of different segmentation models on the performance of the proposed method, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- The paper does not explore the generalization of the method to different datasets or scenarios, which could limit its applicability.
- The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the number of iterations and the impact of different segmentation models.
- There are some inconsistencies and potential errors in the paper, such as incorrect citations and unclear descriptions in figures and tables.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method, particularly in terms of inference time and computational resources required?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of occlusions, and what are the implications for the quality and accuracy of the segmentation results?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of different segmentation models on the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of inference time and computational efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods for object completion?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other methods, particularly in terms of the experimental setup and the metrics used to evaluate the performance?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the complete object mask is not available, and what are the implications for the quality and accuracy of the segmentation results?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the design choices of the IMD process, particularly the number of iterations and the impact of different segmentation models on the performance?
