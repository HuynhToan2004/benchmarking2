 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Symphony, an autoregressive model for generating 3D molecular structures using E(3)-equivariant features. It builds on previous work by utilizing spherical harmonic projections to parameterize the distribution of new atom locations, enabling predictions based on features from a single focus atom. The model is evaluated on the QM9 dataset, showing competitive performance against existing methods. The paper also discusses the challenges of ensuring the validity and uniqueness of generated structures, which are critical for molecular modeling applications. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in certain sections and the need for more comprehensive comparisons with other models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and presentation of the proposed method.
- The method is innovative, utilizing spherical harmonic projections to parameterize the distribution of new atom locations, which is a novel approach in molecular modeling.
- The paper includes a thorough evaluation of the method, with experiments conducted on the QM9 dataset, demonstrating the method's performance against existing models.
- The use of spherical harmonics for molecular generation is a significant advancement over previous methods, which relied on lower-degree representations.
- The paper addresses the importance of validity and uniqueness in molecular modeling, which is crucial for the practical application of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the model ensures the validity and uniqueness of generated structures, particularly in the context of the "closest atom pair" selection and the impact of the focus node.
- The comparison with other models is not comprehensive enough, particularly missing comparisons with other autoregressive models like G-SphereNet and G-SchNet.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of certain sections, such as the implementation of E(3) equivariance and the training details.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the method, such as the potential for overfitting due to the use of high-degree spherical harmonics and the computational cost of the model.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed experimental results, such as the performance of the model on larger datasets like QM128 and QM9Mv2.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the model ensures the validity and uniqueness of generated structures, particularly in relation to the focus node and the selection of the closest atom pair?
- How does the model handle the potential for overfitting due to the use of high-degree spherical harmonics?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the model and how it compares to other methods in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the model perform on larger datasets like QM128 and QM9Mv2, and what are the implications of these results for the scalability and applicability of the method?
- Could the authors elaborate on the training details, particularly how the model is trained and the impact of different training strategies on the model's performance?
