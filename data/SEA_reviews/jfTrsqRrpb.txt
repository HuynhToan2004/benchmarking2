 **Summary:**
The paper introduces UDOS, a method for open-world instance segmentation that combines bottom-up and top-down approaches. UDOS utilizes a bottom-up method to generate class-agnostic segmentation masks, which are then refined using a top-down method that merges these masks into whole objects. The methodology involves a clustering algorithm to group similar masks, followed by a refinement stage to enhance the quality of the segmentation. The paper presents a novel approach to handling unseen classes in instance segmentation, although it relies on a pre-trained Mask R-CNN and requires additional preprocessing steps. The experiments demonstrate that UDOS outperforms existing methods on various datasets, although concerns about the novelty and computational efficiency are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to open-world instance segmentation, combining bottom-up and top-down methods, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The use of unsupervised segmentation algorithms to generate class-agnostic masks for training is a novel and effective strategy.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- Extensive experiments are conducted, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and scenarios.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including the use of pairwise affinity and clustering algorithms, which are well-explained and supported by empirical results.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art results on several benchmarks, indicating its effectiveness and practical applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, such as LDET and GGN, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational efficiency or inference speed of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for real-world applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential improvements.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the clustering algorithm and its impact on the final segmentation results.
- The paper's reliance on a pre-trained Mask R-CNN and the need for additional preprocessing steps might limit its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, such as LDET and GGN, to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with densely clustered objects of similar appearance?
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the computational efficiency and inference speed of the proposed method, especially in comparison to other methods?
- How does the clustering algorithm affect the final segmentation results, and can the authors provide more details on this process?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the refinement module in the segmentation process and its impact on the final results?
- What are the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how might these impact its practical applicability?
