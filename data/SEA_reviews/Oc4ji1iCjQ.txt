 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ShadowCatcher, a novel method designed to generate shadow variables from observational data to address collider bias in causal inference. ShadowCatcher utilizes a conditional independence test to ensure the validity of generated shadow variables, which are then used to estimate treatment effects. The paper presents a theoretical framework and empirical evidence demonstrating the effectiveness of ShadowCatcher in various settings, including synthetic and real-world data. The methodology involves a two-step process: first, ShadowCatcher generates shadow variables, and second, these variables are used by the ShadowEstimator to estimate treatment effects. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is critiqued for its lack of clarity in explaining certain methodological details and assumptions, and the experimental design is questioned for its robustness and generalizability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly presents the problem and its solutions, with a clear motivation for the research.
- The proposed method, ShadowCatcher, is innovative and addresses a significant challenge in causal inference by generating shadow variables from observational data.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical framework and empirical evidence, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The methodology is simple yet effective, with a clear and straightforward presentation of the proposed method and its components.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The paper addresses a significant and important problem in causal inference, focusing on the identification of treatment effects under collider bias.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining certain methodological details, particularly the process of generating shadow variables and the role of the shadow estimator.
- The experimental design is limited, focusing only on a few datasets and not adequately addressing the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the assumption of collider bias, which is crucial for the validity of the results.
- There are concerns about the practicality of the method, particularly in terms of the computational cost and the complexity of the algorithm.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation, including a broader range of datasets and a more detailed analysis of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the assumptions required for its applicability and the potential for overfitting in the shadow variable generation process.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the process of generating shadow variables and the role of the shadow estimator in the overall methodology?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting in the shadow variable generation process, and what measures are in place to prevent this?
- What are the assumptions required for the proposed method to be applicable, and how do these assumptions impact the validity of the results?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the assumptions of collider bias are not met?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the performance of the method on different datasets and under varying conditions?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational cost and practical applicability?
