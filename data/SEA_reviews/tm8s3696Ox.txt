 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Co-Boosting, a novel one-shot federated learning (OFL) algorithm that enhances the quality of synthesized data and the ensemble model through a mutually beneficial relationship. Co-Boosting leverages the current ensemble model to synthesize higher-quality samples in an adversarial attack manner, which are then used to adjust the ensembling weights for each client model. This approach aims to achieve high-quality data and ensemble models, which are critical for the success of OFL. The paper includes extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of Co-Boosting in outperforming existing baselines. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel one-shot federated learning algorithm, Co-Boosting, which addresses the challenge of improving the performance of one-shot federated learning by enhancing both the quality of the synthesized data and the ensemble model.
- The proposed method is simple, easy to implement, and does not require alterations to the client's local training, making it practical for contemporary model market scenarios.
- Extensive experiments are conducted across various datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in enhancing the performance of the server model.
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the methodology and the results, and includes a comprehensive evaluation that considers multiple factors such as data distribution and model architecture.
- The idea of synthesizing data and improving the ensemble model is innovative and could have significant implications for the field of one-shot federated learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is questioned due to the straightforward combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it primarily involves combining existing techniques without significant advancement.
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the adversarial attack used in the method, particularly how it contributes to the improvement of the server model.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, as they are based on a limited number of client models and do not consider a broader range of model architectures or datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the privacy implications of the proposed method, particularly the potential for privacy leakage due to the use of a generator trained locally.
- There is a lack of discussion on the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of the number of clients and the computational complexity.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the adversarial attack used in the method and how it contributes to the improvement of the server model?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to a larger number of clients, and what are the computational costs associated with this?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the scalability and computational complexity of the proposed method?
- How does the method ensure privacy protection, especially in the context of the generator trained locally?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the adversarial loss in the method and how it affects the performance of the server model?
