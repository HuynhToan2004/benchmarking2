 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a hierarchical approach to explain the behavior of poisoned AI models by analyzing the utilization of computation units, subgraphs, and graph representations. The methodology is based on the TrojAI Challenge dataset, focusing on the utilization of tensor-state values across different neural network layers. The paper proposes a novel metric for measuring AI model utilization and demonstrates its effectiveness in detecting poisoned samples. However, the clarity of the paper is a significant concern, with reviewers highlighting issues in the writing, the organization of the content, and the lack of a clear motivation and problem formulation. The paper's methodology and experimental results are also criticized for being insufficiently detailed and not convincingly demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to detecting poisoned samples in AI models by analyzing the utilization of computation units, subgraphs, and graph representations, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The concept of measuring AI model utilization and the proposed metric are well-defined and could potentially be beneficial for future research in explaining the behavior of AI models.
- The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic, focusing on the explainability of AI models, which is a critical issue in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its writing and organization, making it difficult to follow and understand the main contributions and the methodology employed.
- The motivation and problem formulation are not clearly articulated, making it unclear what the paper aims to achieve and how the proposed methods contribute to the field.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with insufficient details on the dataset used, the methodology employed, and the results obtained.
- The paper's reliance on the TrojAI Challenge dataset raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings to other datasets or real-world applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach, such as the potential impact of different types of backdoor attacks on the detection of poisoned samples.
- The paper suffers from several typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the motivation and problem formulation of the paper to better understand the contributions and the relevance of the proposed methods?
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets or real-world applications beyond the TrojAI Challenge?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the dataset used, the methodology employed, and the results obtained?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of backdoor attacks, such as those that do not involve specific triggers like sticky notes or Instagram filters?
- Can the authors provide a clearer explanation of the utilization metrics and how they are computed, possibly with a step-by-step algorithm or a more detailed description in the appendix?
- How does the paper address the limitations of its approach, particularly in terms of the potential impact of different types of backdoor attacks on the detection of poisoned samples?
