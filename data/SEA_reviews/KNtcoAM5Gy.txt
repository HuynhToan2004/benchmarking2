 **Summary:**
The paper introduces BaFTA, a novel test-time adaptation method for vision-language models, specifically designed for CLIP. BaFTA employs a backpropagation-free approach using online clustering to estimate class centroids within a projected embedding space, which aligns text and visual embeddings. This method aims to refine class embeddings without the need for validation data or learning rates, as seen in traditional test-time prompt tuning methods. BaFTA also incorporates Renyi entropy to dynamically aggregate predictions from both estimated and original class embeddings, enhancing its performance in zero-shot image classification. Despite its innovative approach, the paper's experimental results are not significantly superior to existing methods like TPT, raising concerns about its practical utility.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel backpropagation-free test-time adaptation method, BaFTA, which directly estimates class centroids using online clustering within a projected embedding space that aligns text and visual embeddings.
- The methodology is clearly explained, and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow.
- BaFTA demonstrates significant performance improvements over TPT in zero-shot image classification tasks, particularly in datasets with a large number of categories.
- The paper includes comprehensive experiments and ablation studies that validate the effectiveness of BaFTA's components.
- BaFTA does not require learning rates, which is a significant advantage over traditional test-time prompt tuning methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of BaFTA compared to TPT, which could affect its practical utility.
- The experimental results do not show a significant advantage of BaFTA over TPT, raising questions about the method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of BaFTA, particularly in scenarios where it may not perform as well as TPT.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the methodology, with some sections (e.g., Section 3.1 and 3.2) being difficult to understand without additional explanations.
- The paper's contribution is seen as limited as it primarily builds upon existing methods like TPT and Hu et al. (2023), without introducing significant novelty.
- The paper does not address the societal impact of the proposed method, which is a critical consideration for many applications of machine learning.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of BaFTA compared to TPT?
- Why does BaFTA perform worse than TPT in some datasets, and what are the specific scenarios where BaFTA outperforms TPT?
- How does BaFTA handle the issue of class imbalance in datasets, and what are the implications for its performance?
- Can you clarify the methodology and the definitions used in the paper, particularly in Section 3.1 and 3.2?
- How does BaFTA ensure the reliability of its predictions, especially in scenarios where there is a significant class imbalance?
- What are the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, and how do you address these concerns?
