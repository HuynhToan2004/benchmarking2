 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach using pre-trained Vision-Language Models (VLMs) as reward models in reinforcement learning (RL) tasks, specifically focusing on CLIP models. This method allows for the learning of complex tasks by providing a single sentence text prompt describing the desired task, thereby avoiding the need for manually specified reward functions. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach through various experiments, including simple tasks like CartPole and MountainCar, and more complex tasks such as controlling a humanoid robot. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation scope, focusing primarily on CLIP models, and its reliance on textual descriptions which may not accurately capture the complexity of visual tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for using Vision-Language Models (VLMs) as reward models in reinforcement learning (RL).
- The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various tasks, including the ability to learn complex tasks from a single sentence text prompt.
- The use of VLMs as reward models is a novel and interesting approach, potentially reducing the need for manual reward specification and improving sample efficiency in RL tasks.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of how the performance of VLM reward models scales with the size of the VLM, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of this method.
- The use of CLIP models as a reward model is highlighted as a promising direction for RL research, showing potential for improving performance with larger VLM models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily uses CLIP models as the VLM, which limits the generalizability of the findings to other VLMs.
- The evaluation of the VLM reward model is limited, focusing on simple tasks like CartPole and MountainCar, which may not adequately demonstrate the capabilities of the method in more complex tasks.
- The reliance on textual descriptions for defining tasks may not accurately capture the complexity of visual tasks, which could limit the effectiveness of the VLM reward model in such scenarios.
- The paper lacks a broader evaluation of different VLMs and their performance as reward models, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the effectiveness of this approach.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the method, particularly the potential for misspecification of the reward model if the text description does not contain enough information about what the human intends or if the VLM generalizes poorly.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of using VLM-RMs in RL applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other VLMs in terms of performance as a reward model?
- What are the specific advantages of using CLIP models over other VLMs for this task?
- Can you provide examples of how the method could be adapted to handle more complex tasks beyond the simple examples shown in the paper?
- How does the performance of the VLM reward model scale with the complexity of the task and the size of the VLM?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of using VLM-RMs in RL applications, and how might these be mitigated?
- Could you discuss the limitations of the method in more detail, particularly the potential for misspecification of the reward model and the generalizability of the findings to other VLMs?
