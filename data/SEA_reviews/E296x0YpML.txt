 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AdvFooler, a novel defense mechanism against adversarial attacks in NLP models, specifically targeting adversarial word substitution. AdvFooler operates by randomizing the latent representations of input at inference time, making it difficult for adversaries to identify and replace critical words with synonyms. This approach does not require additional training or access to model architecture or parameters, and it is shown to be effective against various adversarial attacks. The methodology is supported by theoretical and empirical analyses, demonstrating its robustness and efficiency. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its limited scope of application and potential impact on clean accuracy.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and easy to implement, making it accessible to practitioners.
- Theoretical analysis supports the method's effectiveness, and empirical results demonstrate its robustness against various adversarial attacks.
- The method does not require additional training, making it practical for deployment in real-world scenarios.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the impact of randomizing latent representations on adversarial attacks and clean accuracy.
- The proposed method can be combined with other defenses, enhancing its versatility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's application is limited to text classification tasks and may not be as effective in other types of NLP tasks like question answering or machine translation.
- The paper's focus on randomizing latent representations might not be the most effective approach for all adversarial attacks, as it could potentially reduce the model's performance on clean data.
- The method's effectiveness against different types of adversarial attacks, such as black-box attacks, is not thoroughly explored.
- The paper's claims about being attack-agnostic and pluggable are not convincingly supported, particularly in terms of its compatibility with other defenses.
- The method's reliance on randomizing latent representations might not be robust against all types of adversarial attacks, especially those that can adapt to the randomization.
- The paper's experimental setup and results could be improved, particularly in terms of the variety of datasets and models used, and the clarity of the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the meaning of "synonym set for word substitution" and how it relates to the method's effectiveness against different types of adversarial attacks?
- How does the proposed method compare to other randomization defenses in terms of its impact on clean accuracy and its effectiveness against different types of adversarial attacks?
- What is the impact of the method on the clean accuracy, and how does this compare to the performance of the base model?
- How does the method perform against different types of adversarial attacks, particularly those that are not word-level substitution attacks?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the adversarial attacks used and the methodology for calculating the number of queries required to locate important words?
- How does the method perform when combined with other defenses, and what are the potential drawbacks or limitations of this combination?
