 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for video tracking and 3D map creation using motion vectors computed by standard video encoders, such as H.264/5. The approach involves clustering motion vectors to form a coreset, which is then used for tracking and map creation. This method is computationally efficient and can be implemented on low-end hardware, demonstrating real-time performance. The theoretical underpinnings of the method include a coreset construction and a novel algorithm for clustering motion vectors. The paper also includes empirical results showing the method's effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world video data. However, the paper suffers from poor presentation, lack of detailed methodological explanation, and insufficient comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to video tracking and 3D map creation using motion vectors computed by standard video encoders, which is both efficient and applicable to low-end hardware.
- The proposed method is theoretically sound, with a clear presentation of the theoretical results and algorithms, including the construction of a coreset and its application to video tracking.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method, showing real-time performance on a Raspberry Pi.
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions and a clear structure that makes it easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed methodological explanations, particularly in the empirical evaluation section, which could benefit from a more comprehensive description of the tracking method used.
- The comparison with existing methods is insufficient, and the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work.
- The presentation of the paper is poor, with small figures and inadequate explanations of the experimental setup and results.
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in certain sections, such as the discussion of the coreset construction and its application to video tracking.
- The paper's contribution is not clearly articulated, and it is unclear how the proposed method differs from existing approaches.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the tracking method used in the empirical evaluation, particularly how it relates to the proposed coreset construction?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of motion vectors, such as those generated by different video encoders?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specific hardware used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the coreset construction and its application to video tracking?
- The paper mentions a "coreset for convex shapes and hyper-plane fitting." Could the authors elaborate on this concept and its relevance to the proposed method?
