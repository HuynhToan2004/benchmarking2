 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a Curriculum Dynamic Graph Invariant Learning (CDGIL) model, which leverages curriculum learning to address distribution shifts in dynamic graphs. CDGIL employs a dual disentangled dynamic attention network to capture invariant and variant patterns, a self-paced intervention mechanism for adversarial sample creation, and a sample importance reweighting strategy. The model is tested on both synthetic and real-world dynamic graph datasets, demonstrating superior performance over existing baselines. The authors also address the challenges of handling distribution shifts in dynamic graphs by proposing a novel curriculum learning strategy that guides the learning of invariant and variant patterns.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in dynamic graph invariant learning, proposing a novel curriculum learning method for dynamic graph generalization.
- The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated, with a clear presentation that enhances understanding.
- Extensive experiments and ablation studies are conducted to verify the effectiveness of the proposed method, demonstrating superior performance over existing baselines.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear introduction, methodology, and experimental results, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The novelty of the paper lies in its application of curriculum learning to dynamic graph invariant learning, which is a first in this field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the self-paced curriculum intervention method and the dynamic curriculum importance reweighting method, particularly the intuition behind these methods.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, such as the formalization of dynamic graphs and the definition of key concepts like invariant and variant patterns.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential improvements.
- The complexity of the proposed method is not thoroughly analyzed, which could affect the practical deployment of the model in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's related work section is inadequately described, missing out on relevant literature that could contextualize the research.
- There is a need for a more rigorous comparison with existing methods, especially in terms of computational complexity and experimental setup.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the self-paced curriculum intervention method and the dynamic curriculum importance reweighting method, including the intuition behind these methods?
- How does the proposed method handle the complexity of dynamic graph invariant learning, and what are the implications for practical deployment?
- Could you clarify the formalization of dynamic graphs and the definition of key concepts such as invariant and variant patterns?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and experimental setup with existing methods?
- Could you discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and suggest areas for future research?
- In the experimental results, could you provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
