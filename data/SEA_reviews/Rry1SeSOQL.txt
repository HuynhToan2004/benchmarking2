 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to reference-free machine translation evaluation by formulating it as a pairwise ranking problem. This approach leverages synthetic data and indirect supervision from natural language inference to train a model that predicts which translation is better without direct human annotation. The model is trained in multiple stages, using XNLI for indirect supervision, human translations for supervised training, and synthetic data for weak supervision. The methodology is evaluated against various benchmarks, showing state-of-the-art performance on several datasets. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty and the lack of a broader comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to machine translation evaluation by formulating it as a pairwise ranking problem, which is a creative and innovative solution to the challenge of reference-free evaluation.
- The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of their proposed method across multiple datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness against various evaluation metrics.
- The use of synthetic data for training and the three-stage training approach are highlighted as effective strategies for improving the model's performance.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the work and a well-structured presentation of the results.
- The methodology is novel, using a simple and effective approach that does not require direct supervision from human annotations, which is a significant advantage in the field of machine translation evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the use of synthetic data and the three-stage training approach. The absence of such comparisons makes it difficult to assess the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The methodology is limited to evaluating two translations at a time, which may not adequately capture the quality of translations in real-world scenarios where multiple translations are available.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method in detail, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the proposed method performs on more challenging datasets such as MQM22 and ACES, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for any technological innovation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the synthetic data is generated and how it contributes to the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use synthetic data and the three-stage training approach?
- Can the authors provide more insights into how the model handles multiple translations and how it compares to other methods that evaluate multiple translations?
- How does the model perform on more challenging datasets such as MQM22 and ACES, and what are the implications of these results for the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated?
- How does the model handle the absence of reference translations in real-world scenarios, and what are the implications of this for the practical applicability of the method?
