 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Multi-Exit Token Reduction (METR), a novel approach to token reduction in Vision Transformers (ViTs) by integrating multi-exit architecture with token reduction. METR aims to enhance the reliability of token scoring by applying early task pressure, thereby improving the effectiveness of token reduction methods. This is achieved by using the attention score from the CLS token as a metric for token importance, which is refined through self-distillation. The method is evaluated across various models and datasets, demonstrating improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty and the clarity of the method's contribution, as well as its computational efficiency and the robustness of its results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-designed methodology.
- The method is simple and effective, with extensive experiments that demonstrate the method's effectiveness in improving token reduction performance.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, with a clear motivation and a well-designed methodology.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the effectiveness of the proposed method, including detailed ablation studies and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.
- The method is technically sound and shows significant improvements in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on related works, particularly on the topic of multi-exit networks, which are crucial for understanding the context and the novelty of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing methods like DiffRate and EViT, which could have strengthened the argument for the superiority of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned as the combination of multi-exit architecture and token reduction is seen as a straightforward approach.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impact of the proposed method, which is a significant omission in ethical considerations.
- The computational efficiency of the method is not clearly demonstrated, with some results showing inconsistencies or insufficient evidence of efficiency improvements.
- The paper does not adequately address the robustness of the results, particularly in terms of the variability in performance across different datasets and model configurations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the related works, particularly on the topic of multi-exit networks and their relevance to the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare with DiffRate and EViT in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of the proposed method, especially in relation to existing works that have explored similar approaches?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how are these addressed or mitigated?
- Could the authors provide more detailed evidence or clarification on the computational efficiency claims, particularly in terms of the inference speed and the efficiency of the model under different conditions?
- How robust is the proposed method to variations in the dataset and model configurations, and what steps are taken to ensure the reliability and reproducibility of the results?
