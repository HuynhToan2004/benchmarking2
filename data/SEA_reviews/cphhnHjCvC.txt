 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to image-based visual navigation, specifically focusing on the ImageNav and Instance-ImageNav tasks. It leverages a dual-encoder architecture, integrating a pre-trained binocular ViT model for relative pose estimation and visibility prediction, alongside a monocular encoder for general navigation tasks. The methodology involves a pre-training phase with the CroCo dataset and a second phase with a custom RPEV dataset, followed by fine-tuning on the ImageNav task. The paper demonstrates significant improvements in performance, achieving state-of-the-art results on both benchmarks. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its incremental nature, lack of novelty in its pre-training tasks, and potential issues with real-world applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly presented, making it accessible to readers.
- The experimental results are robust, showing strong performance in image navigation tasks, particularly in the ImageNav and Instance-ImageNav benchmarks.
- The use of a dual encoder architecture, including a binocular ViT model for relative pose estimation and visibility prediction, is innovative and effective.
- The pre-training phase with the CroCo dataset and the RPEV dataset is well-justified, enhancing the model's performance.
- The paper demonstrates a strong improvement over previous state-of-the-art methods, showing a significant advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as it primarily builds upon existing pre-training tasks without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The real-world applicability of the method is questionable, as the experiments were conducted in simulation environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the choice of pre-training tasks and the potential impact of different pre-training strategies.
- The experimental setup and results, particularly in the ImageNav task, are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with some results being misleading or not directly comparable.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the work, which could be significant given the application of the proposed method in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific contributions of the proposed method beyond the existing pre-training tasks?
- How does the proposed method handle different camera intrinsics and heights in the Instance-ImageNav task, and what are the implications for real-world applications?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, especially in terms of performance metrics and experimental setups?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how are these addressed or mitigated?
- How does the paper handle the issue of data collection for the RPEV dataset, particularly concerning the balance of difficulty across different stages of the dataset?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the pre-training to pairs with different camera intrinsics and backgrounds, and how this might affect the performance in real-world scenarios?
