 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the capacity of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) in capturing node interactions across various graph learning tasks, highlighting a phenomenon termed the "representation bottleneck." This bottleneck arises from the inductive bias introduced by existing graph construction mechanisms, which limit GNNs' ability to learn interactions of appropriate complexity. The authors propose a novel graph rewiring approach based on interaction patterns learned by GNNs to adjust the receptive fields of each node dynamically. This approach is tested on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in alleviating the representation bottleneck and enhancing GNN performance. The paper also discusses the limitations of GNNs compared to other DNNs and provides a comprehensive review of related works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the problem and the proposed solution.
- The authors provide a comprehensive review of related works, which helps in understanding the context and the contributions of the study.
- The experiments are well-designed and well-executed, with a thorough comparison to several baseline methods.
- The proposed method is novel and shows strong performance in the experiments.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of graph neural networks (GNNs), focusing on the representation bottleneck and the inductive bias introduced by existing graph construction mechanisms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to other types of GNNs and its scalability to larger datasets.
- There is a lack of clarity on the relationship between the proposed method and other existing works that address similar issues, such as the use of multi-order interactions for GNNs.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the mathematical notations and definitions used, particularly in the early sections.
- The paper could be improved by including more recent and diverse datasets in the experiments to demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its potential to worsen over-smoothing issues or its effectiveness on larger datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the proposed method and other existing works that address similar issues, such as the use of multi-order interactions for GNNs?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent rewiring methods like SDRF, especially in terms of effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the dataset?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for increased over-smoothing in GNNs, and what steps have been taken to mitigate this issue?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of datasets and the rationale behind using specific datasets for different tasks?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in scaling up the method?
