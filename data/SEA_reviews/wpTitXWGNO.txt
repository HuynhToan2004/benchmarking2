 **Summary:**
The paper introduces xCodeEval, a multilingual, multitask benchmark for evaluating large language models (LLMs) in code generation and understanding tasks. This benchmark includes seven tasks and 25 million document-level coding examples across 17 programming languages. It utilizes a novel data splitting and selection schema based on geometric mean and graph-theoretic principles to ensure fairness and effectiveness in model evaluation. The benchmark includes a multilingual code execution engine, ExecEval, which supports unit test-based execution in all languages. The paper also discusses the challenges of evaluating LLMs in a multilingual and multitask setting and provides insights into the effectiveness of different models across various programming languages.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new benchmark, xCodeEval, which is a large-scale, multilingual, multitask dataset designed to evaluate the performance of large language models (LLMs) in code generation and understanding tasks.
- The dataset is comprehensive, covering 17 programming languages and including 25 million document-level coding examples, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The inclusion of a multilingual code execution engine, ExecEval, adds a valuable tool for evaluating LLMs in a practical setting.
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic, as the evaluation of LLMs in code generation and understanding tasks is a critical area of research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the benchmark, which could be crucial for understanding its applicability and scope.
- The evaluation of the benchmark is limited to a single model (ChatGPT), and it would be beneficial to include a broader range of models to validate the benchmark's effectiveness.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential biases in the dataset, which could affect the fairness and effectiveness of the model evaluations.
- The data splitting and selection schema, while innovative, are not sufficiently justified or explained, which could limit the understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of the retrieval task and the metrics used, which could lead to confusion about the evaluation process.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential risks and ethical considerations associated with the use of the dataset.

**Questions:**
- How does the benchmark handle the potential biases in the dataset, and what steps are taken to ensure fair and unbiased evaluations?
- Could you provide more details on the data splitting and selection schema, and how it ensures a balanced and fair representation of different programming languages and tasks?
- How does the paper address the potential risks and ethical considerations associated with the use of the dataset, particularly in terms of data privacy and security?
- What are the specific challenges and limitations of evaluating LLMs in a multilingual and multitask setting, and how does the benchmark address these challenges?
- Can you clarify the retrieval task and the metrics used, and how do these metrics reflect the effectiveness of the LLMs in generating or translating code?
- How does the paper ensure the quality and reliability of the executable code generated by the LLMs, particularly in terms of security and functionality?
