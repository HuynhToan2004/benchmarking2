 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TEST, a novel approach for integrating time series data with large language models (LLMs) by encoding time series data into LLM-friendly representations. This is achieved through a two-stage process: first, the time series data is tokenized and encoded using a TS encoder, which includes instance-wise, feature-wise, and text-prototype-aligned contrastive learning. Next, the encoder outputs are used to create soft prompts, which are then utilized to guide the LLM's processing of the time series data. The paper claims that TEST can achieve comparable or better results than state-of-the-art methods across various tasks, including classification, forecasting, and representation. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the methodology, the lack of detailed experimental results, and the potential limitations of the approach, particularly in handling complex time series data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-motivated, clearly explaining the challenges and potential of using large language models (LLMs) for time series analysis, and providing a comprehensive overview of existing approaches.
- The proposed TEST method is innovative, integrating contrastive learning with LLMs to enhance time series data representation, and is supported by thorough experiments across various tasks and datasets.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed discussion on the design choices and potential limitations of the proposed method.
- The experimental results show that TEST can achieve comparable or better results than state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper provides a good literature review, which helps in understanding the current state of research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the methodology, particularly the details of how the time series data is tokenized and the specifics of the TS encoder's design.
- There is a lack of detailed experimental results, such as the training and test sets used, the specific configurations of the LLM and TS encoder, and the training details like learning rate and batch size.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to complex time series data with varying frequencies or multiple variables.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with some sections being difficult to understand due to unclear figures and insufficient explanation of certain concepts.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of the TEST method over other approaches, such as supervised fine-tuning or specialized models, are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the time series data is tokenized and the specifics of the TS encoder's design, including the number of layers, heads, and dimensions?
- How were the training and test sets chosen for the experiments, and what were the specific configurations of the LLM and TS encoder used?
- What is the rationale behind using the TCN architecture for the TS encoder, and how does it compare to other potential architectures like RNNs or Transformers?
- Can you clarify the role and selection of text prototypes in the TEST method, and how they are chosen to align with the time series data?
- How does the TEST method handle multivariate time series data, and what are the implications for its performance in such scenarios?
- Could you provide more detailed results on the computational efficiency and generalization capabilities of the TEST method, especially in comparison to other approaches like supervised fine-tuning or specialized models?
