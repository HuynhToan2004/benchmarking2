 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel paradigm for detecting graph anomalies by leveraging a forward diffusion process, which distinguishes anomalous nodes from normal ones based on their evolving patterns. The authors propose a diffusion-based model, DiffAD, which generates additional training samples to enhance the detection performance. The model is tested on various datasets and shows promising results, although it faces criticism for its limited novelty and insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods. The paper also proposes a generative model for graph anomaly detection, which is evaluated against several existing methods. However, the experimental results are not convincing, and the paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with recent works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel paradigm for detecting graph anomalies based on the forward diffusion process, which is both innovative and theoretically grounded.
- The proposed methods, including the forward sequence classification (FSC) and trajectory representation-based classification (TRC), are clearly explained and supported by detailed mathematical formulations and experimental results.
- The paper presents a comprehensive literature review and effectively positions its work within the existing research landscape.
- The use of denoising diffusion models to generate training samples is an interesting approach that could enhance the performance of existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for academic papers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the term "anomalous nodes," which is used interchangeably with "anomalies" and "anomalous graphs."
- The novelty of the proposed methods is questioned, as similar approaches have been previously explored in other works.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental results, such as the time complexity of the proposed methods and the impact of different hyperparameters on performance.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with recent works on graph anomaly detection, which limits the evaluation of its contribution to the field.
- The paper's experimental results are not convincing, with marginal improvements over existing methods and insufficient statistical significance testing.
- The paper's writing could be improved for clarity and consistency, particularly in the use of terms and notations.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and usage of the term "anomalous nodes" throughout the paper?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and scalability?
- How does the paper address the issue of class imbalance in the data, and what strategies are used to mitigate its effects on the results?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the impact of different hyperparameters on performance and the statistical significance of the observed improvements?
- How does the paper ensure the reproducibility of its results, and what measures are in place to validate the effectiveness of the generated training samples?
