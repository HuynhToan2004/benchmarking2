 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Tabular data Pre-Training via Meta-representation (TabPTM), a method aimed at pre-training a model on heterogeneous tabular datasets and applying it to unseen datasets without further training. This approach represents instances by their distances to prototypes, which are used to standardize datasets and enable training-free generalization. The method involves a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) or transformer to learn class-specific confidence scores based on these meta-representations. Experiments are conducted on 22 datasets, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in few-shot scenarios. However, the paper is criticized for its limited evaluation, lack of comparison with more recent methods, and insufficient discussion on the method's novelty and effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for pre-training on heterogeneous tabular datasets, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The use of meta-representations to pre-train a model on heterogeneous datasets and apply it to unseen datasets without additional training is a novel and interesting approach.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The method's simplicity and the use of meta-representations to pre-train a model on heterogeneous datasets and apply it to unseen datasets without additional training are highlighted as strengths.
- The paper includes a good set of experiments, showing that the proposed method outperforms several baselines in few-shot scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the meta-representation, which is a crucial component of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent methods, such as TabCaps and DANets, which limits the evaluation of the proposed method against current state-of-the-art techniques.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be a simple application of meta-learning to tabular data, which has been explored in other areas like few-shot learning.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the effectiveness of the method, particularly in terms of how it performs compared to other methods in different scenarios.
- The method's scalability and applicability to larger datasets or more complex tasks are not addressed, which could limit its practical utility.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the meta-representation is defined and calculated, and how it contributes to the overall methodology?
- How does the proposed method compare to more recent methods like TabCaps and DANets, particularly in terms of performance and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and testing datasets used in the experiments, including their characteristics and potential biases?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the number of training instances per class is small, and what are the implications for its generalization ability?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
- Is there a plan to release the code for the proposed method to facilitate reproducibility and further research?
