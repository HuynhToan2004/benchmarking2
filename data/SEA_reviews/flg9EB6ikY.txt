 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for selective prediction based on the training dynamics of deep neural networks. It utilizes the prediction instability of test points over intermediate model states during training to measure the reliability of predictions. The method, applicable to both classification and regression tasks, is evaluated across various datasets and shown to outperform existing baselines. The approach is simple, efficient, and can be applied to existing models, making it a promising tool for real-world applications. The paper also discusses the potential societal implications of the technology, including fairness and privacy concerns.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and logical structure that makes the content accessible and understandable.
- The proposed method is simple, efficient, and effective, with the potential to be applied to any existing model, making it a versatile tool for real-world applications.
- The empirical results are strong, demonstrating the method's ability to outperform existing baselines across various datasets and tasks, including classification, regression, and time series forecasting.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation, including a detailed discussion of the method's performance across different datasets and tasks, and a comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
- The method's ability to leverage training dynamics to improve selective prediction is a novel approach that has not been extensively explored in previous works.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The method's sensitivity to hyperparameters, such as the weighting parameter k and the checkpoint selection strategy, is not adequately addressed.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational cost of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for real-world applications.
- The paper does not include a detailed comparison with other selective prediction methods, such as those based on statistical inference or distribution modeling, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the method's performance on datasets with complex label structures, such as those with many classes or imbalanced labels.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential societal implications of the technology, including fairness and privacy concerns.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other selective prediction methods, particularly those based on statistical inference or distribution modeling?
- Can the authors provide a detailed discussion on the computational cost of the proposed method, including the time and memory requirements for training and inference?
- How sensitive is the method to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the weighting parameter k and the checkpoint selection strategy?
- How does the method perform on datasets with complex label structures, such as those with many classes or imbalanced labels?
- Could the authors provide more details on the method's performance on time series forecasting tasks, including the specific datasets and metrics used?
- How does the proposed method address fairness and privacy concerns, particularly in applications where selective prediction could potentially discriminate against certain groups or individuals?
