 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TRACE, a novel benchmark designed to evaluate continual learning in large language models (LLMs), addressing the challenges of catastrophic forgetting and the limitations of existing benchmarks. TRACE comprises eight datasets tailored for continual learning, covering domain-specific tasks, multilingual capabilities, code generation, and mathematical reasoning. The benchmark is evaluated using six different LLMs, ranging from 7B to 70B parameters, demonstrating significant declines in general and instruction-following abilities after training. The authors propose a new training strategy called Reasoning-augmented Continual Learning (RCL) to mitigate these issues, which involves integrating task-specific cues with meta-rationales to enhance model performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a timely and important issue of continual learning in large language models (LLMs), which is crucial for enhancing the capabilities of these models in real-world applications.
- The introduction of TRACE, a comprehensive benchmark, is a significant contribution to the field, providing a structured framework for evaluating the performance of LLMs under continual learning conditions.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments conducted on TRACE using six different LLMs ranging from 7B to 70B parameters demonstrate significant declines in general and instruction-following abilities after training, highlighting the challenges of continual learning in LLMs.
- The paper proposes a novel training strategy called Reasoning-augmented Continual Learning (RCL) to address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, which is a significant limitation in current LLM training methodologies.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the performance of LLMs under continual learning conditions, which is a valuable contribution to the ongoing research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing benchmarks, particularly in terms of the novelty and complexity of the tasks included in TRACE.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed RCL approach, especially in terms of its applicability to larger models or more complex tasks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational requirements of the proposed benchmark, which could be a significant barrier for adoption by the research community.
- The paper's focus on specific datasets and metrics may limit its applicability to other types of continual learning scenarios, and the generalizability of the findings is not adequately discussed.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed benchmark, such as the potential biases in the datasets used and the impact of these biases on the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed RCL approach, including a broader range of models and tasks to validate its effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison of TRACE with existing benchmarks, particularly in terms of the novelty and complexity of the tasks included?
- How does the RCL approach perform on larger models or more complex tasks?
- What are the specific computational requirements of the proposed benchmark, and how do these compare to existing benchmarks?
- Could you discuss the potential biases in the datasets used in TRACE and how these might impact the results?
- How does the proposed RCL approach compare to other continual learning methods in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Could you provide more details on the manual annotation process used in the RCL approach, particularly how this was performed and what quality control measures were implemented?
- How does the RCL approach perform in scenarios where the reasoning paths provided by GPT-4 are incorrect or misleading?
- Could you clarify the definition and calculation of the "Safety Delta" metric, particularly how it relates to the overall safety of the model's responses?
