 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for training low-rank neural networks, termed Maestro. This method leverages a generalized variant of Ordered Dropout to progressively learn the low-rank structure of the network during training. Theoretical analysis is provided to demonstrate the equivalence of the proposed method to SVD in specific scenarios. The paper includes experiments across various datasets and network architectures, showing that Maestro can achieve better performance than existing methods like Pufferfish and Cuttlefish. The methodology is also evaluated on a larger dataset, MetaNet, and compared to other low-rank methods, pruning methods, and quantization methods. However, the paper has been criticized for its clarity in presentation, particularly in the explanation of the methodology and the experimental setup, and for the limited novelty of the approach compared to existing methods like FjORD.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The proposed method is simple and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for its use in low-rank approximation.
- The experiments are extensive, covering various datasets and network architectures, including CNN, Transformer, and FC layers.
- The method demonstrates good performance on CIFAR10, showing improvements over existing methods like Pufferfish and Cuttlefish.
- The paper provides theoretical analysis that is well-explained and supported by empirical results, showing the equivalence of the proposed method to SVD in specific scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the methodology and the experimental setup.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it is seen as an extension of FjORD, with similarities in formulation and approach.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with marginal improvements over existing methods and inconsistencies in the reported results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational overhead and the impact of the proposed method on training time.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of model size and performance.
- The theoretical guarantees provided are not sufficiently strong, and the assumptions made are not clearly stated or justified.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of hyperparameters and the impact of these choices on the results?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of model size, training time, and performance?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other low-rank methods, including the impact of different initialization methods and hyperparameters?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets, such as ImageNet, and what are the expected improvements or challenges?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the computational overhead and the impact of the proposed method on training time?
- How does the proposed method handle the initialization of the low-rank factors, and what are the implications of different initialization methods?
