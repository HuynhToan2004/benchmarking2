 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FrAug, a frequency-domain data augmentation technique designed for long-term time series forecasting. FrAug includes two methods: frequency masking and frequency mixing, which operate in the frequency domain to preserve semantic consistency. The authors demonstrate that FrAug enhances the performance of deep learning models on eight benchmark datasets, particularly when training data is scarce. The methodology is evaluated across various scenarios, including long-term forecasting, cold-start forecasting, and test-time training, showing improvements over existing augmentation techniques. Despite its effectiveness, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the method across different model architectures and the lack of comparisons with state-of-the-art models in long-term forecasting.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces FrAug, a novel frequency-domain data augmentation technique that enhances the performance of deep learning models in long-term time series forecasting.
- The proposed FrAug is simple yet effective, preserving semantic consistency and improving forecasting accuracy in scenarios with limited training data.
- The paper provides a clear motivation for the use of frequency domain augmentation, which is well-supported by experimental results.
- The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of FrAug across various scenarios, including long-term forecasting, cold-start forecasting, and test-time training, showing improvements over existing augmentation techniques.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experiments and ablation studies, enhancing its clarity and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the potential overfitting of models due to the augmentation process, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The generalizability of the proposed method is limited as it is evaluated on only one model architecture (Transformer-based), and does not consider other architectures like CNN or RNN.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art models in long-term forecasting, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of detailed explanation on how the augmented data is utilized during test-time training, which could affect the understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of augmentation on the training efficiency and the computational cost, which are crucial for practical applications.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with some inconsistencies and contradictions noted in the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when combined with other state-of-the-art models in long-term forecasting?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the augmented data is utilized during test-time training, especially in terms of data selection and processing?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of overfitting, and what measures are in place to prevent it?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of augmentation on the training efficiency and computational cost, and provide recommendations for practical applications?
- Is there a possibility to extend the proposed method to other architectures like CNN or RNN, and what are the expected outcomes?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other augmentation methods, including those that operate in the frequency domain, to better understand the advantages of FrAug?
