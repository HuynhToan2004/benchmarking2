 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach, BTBS-LNS, which combines machine learning with traditional Large Neighborhood Search (LNS) to tackle mixed-integer programming (MIP) problems. The methodology includes a "binarized tightening" technique that converts general integer variables into binary variables, enabling the application of existing LNS policies. Additionally, a branching network is introduced to optimize fixed variables, and a tripartite graph is utilized to encode the MIP problem. The approach is evaluated against various baselines and commercial solvers, demonstrating competitive performance in some cases. However, the paper suffers from several issues, including unclear explanations of key components, insufficient experimental validation, and a lack of comparison with relevant literature.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to MIP solving by combining machine learning with traditional LNS methods, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The "binarized tightening" technique is a novel contribution that effectively addresses the challenges of dealing with general integer variables in MIP problems.
- The use of a tripartite graph to encode the MIP problem is a novel approach that could enhance the understanding and modeling of MIPs.
- The paper is well-written and provides a clear overview of the proposed method, making it accessible and understandable.
- The inclusion of an extra branching network to optimize fixed variables is a thoughtful addition that could potentially improve the performance of the LNS policy.
- The paper presents a clear and concise methodology, which is easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the "binarized tightening" technique, particularly how it transforms general integer variables into binary variables and how this affects the problem-solving process.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and implementation of the branching policy, which is a crucial component of the proposed method.
- The comparison with existing literature is insufficient, and the paper does not adequately position its contributions within the current state of research.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing solvers, particularly in scenarios where the proposed method underperforms compared to commercial solvers.
- The paper suffers from several grammatical errors and could benefit from a thorough proofreading to ensure clarity and professionalism.
- The paper's claims about the effectiveness of the "binarized tightening" technique and the "tripartite graph" are not adequately supported by the experimental results, which raises concerns about the validity of these claims.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the "binarized tightening" technique works in detail, particularly how it transforms general integer variables into binary variables and how this affects the problem-solving process?
- How is the branching policy trained and implemented? What specific data and methods are used to train this policy?
- Why does the proposed method underperform compared to commercial solvers in some scenarios, and how does this compare to the performance of other state-of-the-art methods?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific instances used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent learning-based approaches for MIP solving, such as those mentioned in the literature review?
- Can the authors elaborate on the claims made about the "tripartite graph" and its role in encoding the MIP problem?
- What are the implications of using a single seed for the experiments, and how does this affect the reliability and reproducibility of the results?
