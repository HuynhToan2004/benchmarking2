 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the "Snowflake Hypothesis," proposing that each node in a graph neural network (GNN) should have a unique receptive field, akin to the distinct patterns of snowflakes. This hypothesis is tested through two methods, SnoHv1 and SnoHv2, which involve pruning the adjacency matrix based on gradient magnitude or cosine distance. The methods aim to improve the performance of GNNs by reducing over-smoothing and overfitting, particularly in deep GNNs. Experiments are conducted on various datasets and backbones, including small and large-scale graphs, to validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. Despite some concerns about the clarity and depth of the experimental results and the novelty of the approach, the paper presents a well-articulated hypothesis and a methodological approach that could contribute to the field if further refined.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel hypothesis, the Snowflake Hypothesis, which challenges conventional GNN training methods and provides a unique perspective on node receptive fields.
- The proposed methods are simple, intuitive, and can be integrated with existing GNN architectures, making them practical for real-world applications.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, which facilitates understanding and reproducibility of the research.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering a variety of datasets and backbones, and include both small and large-scale graphs, demonstrating the scalability of the proposed methods.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of GNNs, focusing on the issue of over-smoothing and overfitting in deep GNNs, which is a significant challenge in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the proposed methods address the over-smoothing problem in GNNs, particularly in terms of the role of the cosine distance in measuring similarity between node representations.
- There is a lack of detailed comparison with existing methods, such as DropEdge, which also addresses the issue of over-smoothing in GNNs.
- The paper does not provide sufficient empirical evidence to support the claim that the proposed methods improve interpretability, and the experiments do not convincingly demonstrate this improvement.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of scalability and generalizability to other types of GNNs.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer explanation of the theoretical underpinnings of the Snowflake Hypothesis.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, especially in the experimental results and methodology sections.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the cosine distance is used to measure the similarity between node representations in the context of the proposed methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods like DropEdge, particularly in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Can you provide more detailed empirical evidence to support the claim that the proposed methods improve interpretability?
- What are the specific limitations of the proposed methods, and how do these limitations affect the scalability and generalizability of the approach?
- How do the proposed methods perform in terms of computational efficiency, especially in large-scale GNNs?
- Could you provide more detailed experimental results, including standard deviations and error bars, to better understand the variability and reproducibility of the results?
- How do the proposed methods perform on other types of GNNs, such as those based on attention mechanisms or graph transformers?
