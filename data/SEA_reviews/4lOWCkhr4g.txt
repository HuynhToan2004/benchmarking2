 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for training an unsupervised Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) system using pseudo-labels from a source language and a target language model. This approach, termed "cross-lingual pseudo-labeling," is evaluated across multiple languages and shown to outperform character-based wav2vec-U 2.0 on LJSpeech with limited labeled data. The method leverages the similarities in language structures across different languages to generate pseudo-labels, which are then used to train a target language model. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as it builds on existing pseudo-labeling techniques and lacks comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of unsupervised Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR), focusing on the transfer of knowledge from a source language to a target language using a target language model.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it easy to understand and follow, and the experiments are conducted across various languages, demonstrating the applicability of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to read, with a clear motivation and a focus on the practical application of the proposed method.
- The results are impressive, showing that the proposed method can outperform other methods like wav2vec-U 2.0 on LJSpeech, indicating the effectiveness of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the similarities and differences between the proposed method and existing unsupervised ASR methods, which could help in better understanding the novelty and contributions of the proposed approach.
- The experimental setup and results are limited, particularly in terms of the number of languages evaluated and the diversity of the data used. This limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of data availability, which is crucial for the application of the proposed method in real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that leverage multilingual models, which could provide a better understanding of the performance of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training procedures and hyperparameters used, which could affect the reproducibility and reliability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant in certain applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the similarities and differences between their proposed method and other unsupervised ASR methods, particularly those that use adversarial training or phoneme-based lexicons?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and efficiency with other unsupervised ASR methods, especially those that use different training procedures or hyperparameters?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training procedures and hyperparameters used in their experiments, including the specifics of the data augmentation techniques used?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to languages with different alphabets, and what modifications are necessary for such applications?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their proposed method and how these could be mitigated?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that leverage multilingual models or other advanced techniques?
