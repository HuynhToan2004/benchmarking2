 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel asymmetric zero-shot composed image retrieval (ZSCIR) method that leverages a lightweight model for query processing and a large vision-language model for database retrieval. The method involves converting image features into a sequence of tokens using a lightweight visual encoder and an adaptive token learner, which are then matched with text features in the embedding space. The proposed method shows improved retrieval performance compared to symmetric approaches and is particularly effective in resource-constrained environments. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as it primarily extends existing methods to the asymmetric setting, and for its reliance on a strong foundation model, which may limit its applicability in scenarios where such models are not available.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel asymmetric approach to composed image retrieval (CIR), which is more flexible and potentially more efficient than symmetric methods.
- The method is well-motivated, with a clear description of the proposed approach, including the use of an adaptive token learner to convert image features into textual tokens.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are well-designed, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of an asymmetric framework allows for more flexible deployment in real-world scenarios, which is a significant advantage over symmetric methods.
- The method demonstrates better retrieval performance compared to symmetric approaches, which is a notable achievement.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the use of different models for query and database processing.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it primarily extends existing methods to the asymmetric setting, with limited technical innovation.
- The reliance on a strong foundation model for image-text retrieval may limit the applicability of the method in scenarios where such models are not available or practical.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough evaluation on different datasets and a more detailed analysis of the impact of different components of the method.
- The description of the adaptive token learner and its role in the method could be clearer, and more visual aids or detailed explanations could help in understanding its operation.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the method and potential areas for future research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the use of different models for query and database processing?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to symmetric approaches in terms of computational efficiency and retrieval performance?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of different components of the method, such as the adaptive token learner and the use of different models for query and database processing?
- How does the method perform when using different models for query and database processing, and what are the implications of this approach in terms of computational efficiency and retrieval performance?
- Could the authors provide more visual aids or detailed explanations to help understand the operation of the adaptive token learner and its role in the method?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how might these impact its practical application in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss potential areas for future research to address these limitations and improve the method's applicability and effectiveness?
