 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for language-conditioned grasping, leveraging SE(2) symmetries to enhance sample efficiency and performance in grasping tasks. It proposes a Language-conditioned Equivariant Grasp (LEG) network that maps language instructions to steerable kernels with rotational symmetry, which is then cross-correlated with visual features to determine the grasp distribution. The method is evaluated on a new benchmark consisting of 10 language-conditioned grasping tasks and is demonstrated to be effective in both simulated and real-world robot experiments. The paper also presents a new benchmark for evaluating language-conditioned grasping and discusses the implications of symmetry in grasping tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for language-conditioned grasping that leverages SE(2) symmetries to enhance sample efficiency and performance, providing a new benchmark for evaluating language-conditioned grasping tasks.
- The method is evaluated on a new benchmark consisting of 10 language-conditioned grasping tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in both simulated and real-world robot experiments.
- The proposed method achieves a high success rate in language-conditioned grasp, as shown in the real-world experiment, and demonstrates a strong inductive bias on sampling efficiency.
- The paper provides a detailed explanation of the proposed method, including the design of the network architecture and the use of steerable kernels, which are critical for the effectiveness of the method.
- The introduction of a new benchmark for evaluating language-conditioned grasping tasks is a significant contribution, as there is a lack of benchmarks specifically designed for this task.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the proposed method compares to existing methods, particularly in terms of its novelty and performance improvements.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the real-world experiments, such as the number of grasps attempted and the criteria for considering a grasp successful, which makes it difficult to evaluate the robustness and reliability of the method in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the work.
- The method's reliance on a large number of demonstrations (500) might limit its practical applicability in real-world scenarios where such data may not be readily available.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for ethical AI research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the proposed method compares to existing methods in terms of performance and novelty?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex, cluttered environments where the target object might be occluded or surrounded by other objects?
- What are the specific criteria used to consider a grasp successful in the real-world experiments, and how does this compare to the criteria used in simulated environments?
- How does the proposed method handle variations in lighting and sensor noise in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the language embedding on the performance of the method, particularly in scenarios where the language instruction might not be directly related to the grasping task?
- How does the proposed method perform when the language instruction is ambiguous or unclear, and what measures are in place to handle such scenarios?
