 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework named joint-cluster supervised learning for graph-structured data, aimed at improving node classification in graph neural networks (GNNs). This framework challenges the traditional independent cross-entropy loss by modeling the joint distribution of node and cluster labels, conditioned on their representations. The authors argue that this approach enhances the generalization of GNNs and makes them more robust against adversarial attacks. The proposed method is tested across 12 benchmark datasets and 7 backbone models, demonstrating improved performance in various scenarios. However, the paper is criticized for its incremental contribution, unclear motivation, and potential issues with the experimental setup and the robustness of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and well-organized presentation.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, with potential for broad application in various fields.
- The paper provides extensive experimental results, including on large-scale datasets and different types of datasets, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper introduces a novel approach by modeling the joint distribution of nodes and their corresponding clusters, which is different from traditional methods that treat node labels as independent.
- The paper discusses the limitations of the proposed method, which is a commendable effort towards transparency and honesty in scientific research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- The motivation behind the proposed method is not clearly articulated, and the paper does not provide sufficient theoretical or empirical evidence to support its claims.
- The experimental setup and results are not robust, with significant inconsistencies and potential biases in the data used.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, and the potential negative societal impacts are not addressed.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the robustness of the results to different hyperparameters, which could affect the reproducibility and reliability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the motivation behind the proposed method and provide more detailed theoretical or empirical evidence to support its effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability with state-of-the-art methods?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the data was collected and processed, and how the results were validated?
- How does the proposed method perform under different hyperparameters, and what are the potential implications of these variations on the results?
- Can you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
