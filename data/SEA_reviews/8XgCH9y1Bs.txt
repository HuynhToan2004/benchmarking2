 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel 3D object representation learning method that integrates a template mesh with feature vectors at each vertex, aiming to enhance robustness in image classification and 3D pose estimation. This approach uses a contrastive learning framework to learn these representations from class labels and annotated 3D object poses. The methodology is evaluated on multiple datasets, demonstrating competitive performance and faster inference times compared to some baselines. However, the paper's claims of being the first to employ a 3D representation for image classification are disputed, as similar methods have been previously explored. The paper is praised for its clarity and presentation, but criticized for its limited novelty and the lack of detailed comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel 3D object representation learning method, which is the first to employ a 3D representation for image classification.
- The method uses a template mesh with feature vectors at each vertex, which enhances the robustness of classification and 3D pose estimation.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and intuitive figures that aid in understanding the proposed method.
- The method is evaluated on multiple datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness in various scenarios.
- The paper provides a detailed ablation study that helps in understanding the impact of different components of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claim of being the first to employ a 3D representation for image classification is disputed, as similar methods have been previously explored.
- The method's novelty is limited as it is an extension of existing works, particularly the NeMo model, which also uses a 3D representation for 3D pose estimation.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and parameter count.
- The method's performance in certain scenarios, such as corrupted data, is not as strong as claimed, and the results are not consistently superior to existing methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach, such as the potential for overfitting and the need for a more detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough evaluation on larger, more diverse datasets to better demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and parameter count with existing methods, particularly those that use similar 3D representations?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with the NeMo model, particularly in terms of the training and inference processes, to clarify the differences and improvements over the existing method?
- How does the method handle different levels of occlusion, especially in scenarios where objects are partially occluded or occluded by other objects?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the concentration parameter and the momentum coefficient, and their impact on the performance of the method?
- How does the method perform when the number of categories in the dataset increases? Are there any limitations to the scalability of the method?
