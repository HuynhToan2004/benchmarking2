 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LMDX, a methodology for document information extraction using large language models (LLMs) without training. It utilizes a prompting strategy that includes OCR results, task descriptions, and schema representations to guide the LLM's extraction of hierarchical entities with precise localization. The methodology is evaluated on the VRDU and CORD benchmarks, showing promising results in zero-shot and low-data regimes. However, concerns about the method's reliance on high-quality OCR and its potential hallucination issues persist. The paper also discusses the importance of prompt engineering and the need for more extensive evaluations on different document types and LLMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to document information extraction using large language models (LLMs) without training, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear descriptions of each stage, including OCR, chunking, prompt generation, completion targets, LLM inference, and decoding.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the methodology, including comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of different components.
- The methodology is data-efficient, making it a practical solution for real-world applications where labeled data are scarce.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, which is crucial for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The methodology's reliance on high-quality OCR output could limit its applicability in scenarios where OCR quality is poor, which is a common issue in document image processing.
- There is a potential for hallucination in the LLM's responses, which could lead to incorrect extractions, especially when the OCR results are noisy or incorrect.
- The methodology's performance heavily depends on the quality of the OCR output, which may not be consistently high across different document types and OCR engines.
- The paper does not adequately address how the methodology handles scenarios where the OCR results are incorrect or incomplete, which could lead to incorrect extractions.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methodology, which is crucial for ensuring ethical and responsible use.
- The paper's evaluation could be more comprehensive by including a wider range of document types and LLMs to assess the methodology's robustness and generalizability.

**Questions:**
- How does the methodology handle scenarios where the OCR results are incorrect or incomplete? What measures are in place to ensure the accuracy of the extracted information?
- Could you provide more details on the specific OCR engine used in your experiments and how the methodology performs with different OCR engines?
- How does the methodology handle scenarios where the OCR results are noisy or contain errors? What strategies are used to mitigate the impact of these errors on the extraction process?
- Could you provide examples or case studies that demonstrate the methodology's effectiveness in real-world scenarios?
- How does the methodology compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of efficiency and accuracy, especially in scenarios with limited training data?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methodology, and how are these addressed in the paper?
- Could you discuss the potential for the methodology to be adapted to other document types or domains beyond those evaluated in the paper?
