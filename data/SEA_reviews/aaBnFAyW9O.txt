 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Soft Mixture Denoising (SMD) to address the expressive bottleneck in diffusion models, which is primarily caused by the Gaussian assumption in the posterior probability. The authors argue that this assumption limits the expressiveness of diffusion models, particularly in approximating multimodal data distributions. The proposed SMD method integrates a mixture of Gaussian distributions to enhance the model's expressiveness, thus improving the approximation of complex data distributions. The paper includes theoretical analysis and experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of SMD in various diffusion models, particularly in scenarios with few backward iterations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in diffusion models by proposing a novel solution to the expressive bottleneck issue, which is a crucial limitation in current diffusion models.
- The proposed Soft Mixture Denoising (SMD) method is a straightforward yet effective approach that integrates a mixture of Gaussian distributions to enhance the model's expressiveness.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex theoretical concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in different types of diffusion models, especially in scenarios with few backward iterations.
- The paper provides a new theoretical analysis of the limitations of diffusion models, which is both interesting and insightful, offering a new perspective on the challenges and potential of diffusion models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that address similar issues such as multimodal data distribution approximation and expressive bottleneck in diffusion models.
- The experimental results do not consistently demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods, especially in high-resolution image datasets, which raises questions about the generalizability and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, especially considering its application in multimodal data distributions.
- The computational cost and training time of the proposed method are not discussed, which is crucial for understanding the practical feasibility of the method.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is not thoroughly supported by empirical evidence, which could weaken the claims made about the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that address similar issues in diffusion models?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with a large number of Gaussian components, and what are the computational implications of this approach?
- Can the authors provide more experimental results, including visual examples, to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across different datasets and scenarios?
- How does the proposed method handle multimodal data distributions, and what are the potential negative societal impacts of this approach?
- Could the authors discuss the computational cost and training time of the proposed method, and how these compare to existing methods?
- How does the proposed method perform when the number of mixture components is not known, and how does it compare to other methods that address this issue?
