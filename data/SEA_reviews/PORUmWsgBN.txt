 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for generating 3D facial animations from speech using a diffusion-based approach, which integrates a style encoder for personalized speaking styles. The method leverages a dataset of 2D audio-visual data to train a diffusion model that can predict 3D facial expressions and head poses. The model is trained on a newly collected dataset, TFHP, which includes 342 subjects, and is evaluated against existing methods such as FaceFormer and CodeTalker. The paper claims that the proposed method outperforms these methods in terms of lip synchronization, head pose alignment, and speaking style. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the lack of comparison with more recent methods, and the insufficient quantitative evaluation metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed appendix that provides additional insights.
- The methodology is well-motivated, with a focus on generating personalized speaking styles from a style encoder trained on a diverse dataset.
- The use of a diffusion-based method to model the many-to-many mapping between speech and facial expressions is innovative and well-explained.
- The paper introduces a novel dataset, TFHP, which is publicly available and will be beneficial for future research in this area.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms existing methods, showing improvements in lip synchronization, head pose alignment, and speaking style.
- The methodology includes a speaking style encoder that captures personalized speaking styles from a short reference video, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the technical contributions of the proposed method, particularly in relation to existing methods such as CodeTalker.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant methods, such as those using VAEs for generating 3D facial animations.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of training and inference times, which could be crucial for evaluating the computational efficiency of the proposed method.
- The paper does not include a user study to evaluate the naturalness and diversity of the generated animations, which could be a significant limitation in assessing the practical applicability of the method.
- The dataset used for training is limited to 3DMM parameters, which might not capture the full richness of the 2D data, potentially affecting the quality of the generated animations.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights into its applicability and potential improvements.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with CodeTalker, particularly in terms of the technical contributions and experimental results?
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent methods that use VAEs for generating 3D facial animations?
- What are the training and inference times for the proposed method, and how do they compare with those of other methods?
- How is the speaking style encoder trained, and what are the details of the training process?
- Can you clarify the use of the term "style" in the context of the speaking style encoder? Is it referring to a style of speaking or a style of appearance?
- How does the method handle different speakers with similar styles, and what are the implications for the diversity of the generated animations?
- Could you provide more details on the training and evaluation of the speaking style encoder, including the number of positive and negative samples used?
- How does the method handle different speech speeds and accents, and what are the implications for the quality and diversity of the generated animations?
