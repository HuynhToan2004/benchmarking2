 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel meta-Stackelberg game framework for robust federated learning, focusing on adaptive attacks that are difficult to predict. The authors model the federated learning process as a Bayesian Stackelberg Markov game, with a meta-Stackelberg equilibrium that allows for strategic adaptation to different types of attacks. This framework is supported by a two-timescale policy gradient algorithm, which converges to a near-optimal solution. The paper also provides theoretical analysis of the convergence rate and empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method against various attacks. The approach is designed to address the limitations of existing defenses that rely on pre-defined attack types and can adapt to new, unknown attacks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework for federated learning security using meta-learning and Stackelberg equilibrium, which is both innovative and relevant to current challenges in the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, with a comprehensive proof of convergence to a near-optimal solution, enhancing the credibility of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making complex concepts accessible and understandable, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method against various attacks, and the paper includes a detailed analysis of the convergence rate, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in federated learning by proposing a novel defense mechanism that can adapt to new, unknown attacks, which is a critical advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential drawbacks and scenarios where the method might not be effective.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse datasets and varying the attack ratios to better reflect real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the meta-SG framework and the role of the attacker in the game.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with existing methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the strictly competitive nature of the game, might not hold true in all real-world scenarios, which could limit the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the attack ratio is low, such as 1%?
- Could you elaborate on the role of the attacker in the game and how their actions influence the outcome of the game?
- What are the specific advantages of the meta-SG framework over existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- How does the method handle different types of attacks, especially those that are not explicitly modeled in the training phase?
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity and resource requirements of the meta-SL algorithm, especially in comparison to other meta-learning algorithms?
- In the experimental setup, how does the method perform under different levels of malicious client participation, and what are the implications for the effectiveness of the defense?
