 **Summary:**
The paper explores the feature learning capabilities of neural networks under anisotropic data, focusing on the k-sparse parity problem. It extends previous work by showing that anisotropic data can improve statistical and computational complexity, allowing for a better understanding of feature learning in non-isotropic settings. The authors introduce a coordinate transform based on the gradient covariance matrix to enhance the alignment between feature directions and the target function, thereby reducing the computational complexity. The paper also includes a kernel lower bound for anisotropic parity, which highlights the advantages of gradient-based feature learning over kernel methods. Despite the theoretical advancements, the paper's practical applicability and the clarity of its experimental results are questioned, suggesting that further empirical validation and clearer presentation could enhance its impact.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the results and making the contributions clear.
- The analysis of the anisotropic setting is novel and provides a new perspective on feature learning in neural networks.
- The paper demonstrates that anisotropic data can improve both statistical and computational complexity, showing that this is a general phenomenon that can be exploited in various settings.
- The inclusion of a kernel lower bound for anisotropic parity is a valuable contribution to the field, providing a comprehensive understanding of the problem.
- The paper is self-contained, with clear proofs and a well-organized structure, making it accessible and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on the k-sparse parity problem, which may not fully capture the benefits of anisotropic data in more general settings.
- The practical applicability of the findings is questionable, as the paper does not provide clear guidance on how to apply the proposed techniques in real-world scenarios.
- The experimental results are not convincingly presented, with figures that are difficult to interpret and lack detailed explanations.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly the trade-offs between statistical and computational complexity.
- There are several minor issues with the paper, such as missing citations and typographical errors, which could be corrected to improve the overall quality of the manuscript.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in Theorem 1 and how they affect the generalizability of the results?
- Is there a possibility to extend the results to more general target functions beyond k-sparse parity?
- How does the paper's coordinate transform based on the gradient covariance matrix compare to other methods like PCA, and what are the practical implications of using this approach?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples of how the anisotropic setting can be applied in practice, particularly in scenarios where the data is not naturally anisotropic?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their methods and how these might affect the practical applicability of their findings?
