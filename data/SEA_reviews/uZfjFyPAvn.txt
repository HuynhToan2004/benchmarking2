 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of wavelets as activation functions in implicit neural representations (INRs), focusing on their expressivity and potential advantages over sinusoidal functions. It introduces a novel split architecture that leverages wavelets for both low-pass and high-pass approximations, demonstrating improved performance on both 1D and 2D signals. The theoretical analysis includes a characterization of the function class represented by INRs and a detailed study on the expressivity of wavelet-based INRs. Empirical results showcase the effectiveness of this approach, particularly in initializing INRs based on wavelet modulus maxima. The paper also discusses the implications of wavelet-based INRs for signal processing and machine learning applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the ideas and results, with a clear structure and detailed explanations.
- The authors have demonstrated a novel approach to using wavelets as activation functions in INRs, showing that this method can be advantageous in terms of expressivity and performance.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, providing a comprehensive understanding of the function class represented by INRs and the expressivity of wavelet-based INRs.
- The empirical results are convincing, showing the effectiveness of wavelet-based INRs in both 1D and 2D signals, and the paper's experiments are well-designed to support the claims.
- The paper offers valuable insights into the use of wavelet-based INRs for signal processing and machine learning applications, particularly in handling singularities in signals.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other activation functions, such as sinusoidal or Gaussian functions, which could have provided a more comprehensive understanding of the advantages of wavelet-based INRs.
- The paper does not discuss the scalability of the proposed methods to higher dimensions (e.g., images), which is crucial for practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational efficiency of the proposed methods, which is important for practical deployment.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which could help in understanding the scenarios where wavelet-based INRs might not be the best choice.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the potential societal impacts of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison of wavelet-based INRs with other activation functions, particularly in terms of expressivity and computational efficiency?
- How do the authors plan to scale the proposed methods to higher dimensions, such as images, and what are the expected challenges and benefits in doing so?
- Could you discuss the computational efficiency of the proposed methods and how it compares to other INR architectures?
- What are the potential limitations of using wavelet-based INRs, and in what scenarios might other INR architectures be more suitable?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and inference times of the proposed methods, and how these compare to other INR architectures?
- How does the proposed split architecture compare to other architectures in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- What are the implications of the wavelet-based INRs for the handling of singularities in signals, and how does this compare to other methods for handling singularities?
