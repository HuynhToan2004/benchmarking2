 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of 8-bit floating-point (FP8) formats for training and inference of large language models (LLMs), focusing on the selection of scaling factors to prevent underflow and overflow issues. The authors propose two methods: FP8-AMAX and FP8-CSCALE, which dynamically adjust scaling biases for different tensors. The paper provides empirical evidence on the effectiveness of these methods across various LLM sizes and tasks, including fine-tuning and inference. Despite the paper's clarity and the novelty of its approach, concerns are raised about the originality of the methods and the depth of the empirical results, particularly the lack of comprehensive comparisons with other quantization methods and hardware platforms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the proposed methods and their implementation.
- The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed methods can converge to the same validation accuracy as FP16 with different LLM sizes, showing that FP8 can be used for training and inference.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the FP8 methodology, including a detailed comparison of the FP8-AMAX and FP8-CSCALE methods, and discusses the impact of scaling biases on model performance.
- The paper addresses the significant issue of training and inference efficiency in large language models (LLMs), which is crucial for practical deployment.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other quantization methods such as INT8 and FP16, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the advantages and limitations of FP8.
- The experimental results are limited to a few tasks and LLM sizes, which might not adequately represent the generalizability of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the computational efficiency or throughput of FP8 with other quantization methods, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of using FP8.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of FP8 on hardware platforms beyond those used in the experiments, which could limit the applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of using FP8 or other quantization methods, which could be a significant concern for the ethical implications of AI deployments.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational efficiency and throughput of FP8 with other quantization methods, particularly on different hardware platforms?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods for selecting scaling factors, such as those based on percentiles or mean square error?
- What are the implications of using FP8 for different types of layers beyond linear layers, such as attention layers?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of using FP8 or other quantization methods, and how these impacts could be mitigated?
- Could the authors provide more details on the hardware and software used in the experiments, including the specific FP8 formats and the hardware platforms used?
- How do the scaling biases affect the performance of the models, and could the authors provide more detailed analysis or visualizations to better understand their impact?
