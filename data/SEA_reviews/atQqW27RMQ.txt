 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GENIU, a novel framework for class unlearning in imbalanced data settings with restricted data access. GENIU employs a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) to generate proxy samples that represent each class, eliminating the need for original training data. The framework also incorporates an "in-batch tuning" strategy to mitigate performance degradation. Experiments demonstrate that GENIU outperforms existing methods, particularly in scenarios with class imbalance. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation, and for its experimental setup, which is considered too simplistic and lacks comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is practical and applicable in real-world scenarios, addressing the challenge of class unlearning with restricted data access and class imbalance.
- The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing superior performance over existing methods in scenarios with class imbalance.
- The paper is the first to explore the challenges of data access restricted class unlearning within an imbalanced data setting, providing a novel approach to the problem.
- The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of the challenges and potential solutions, making the paper a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks significant technical novelty as it primarily combines existing techniques without introducing substantial new ideas.
- The experimental setup is limited, focusing on a small number of datasets and not comparing the proposed method with state-of-the-art unlearning methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of data privacy, which is crucial in unlearning scenarios.
- The paper's claim of being the first non-retrain-based unlearning framework for imbalanced data is questionable, as similar frameworks have been previously proposed.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and effectiveness in various scenarios.
- The paper's evaluation is not comprehensive, particularly in terms of the number of datasets used and the variety of unlearning methods compared.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the forgetting data does not consist predominantly of the majority class?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the noise samples are generated and how they are used in the unlearning process?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other state-of-the-art unlearning methods, particularly those that do not rely on data access restrictions?
- Could the authors clarify the claim of being the first non-retrain-based unlearning framework for imbalanced data, especially in light of previous work on similar frameworks?
- How does the paper address the issue of data privacy, particularly in the context of unlearning?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other unlearning methods, including a discussion on the computational costs and the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the proposed method?
