 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the convergence properties of prefix and causal language models in the context of in-context learning (ICL). It provides theoretical proofs and empirical evidence to support the claim that prefixLM models converge to the optimal solution of linear regression, while causalLM models converge to the weights of an online learning system, which may not converge to the optimal solution. The study is based on a simplified linear self-attention model and is supported by experiments on synthetic and real tasks using various transformers. The paper aims to explain the empirical observation that prefixLM models often outperform causalLM models in ICL tasks, though it is limited to linear self-attention and does not consider more complex architectures like ResNet.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear and detailed theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of prefix and causal language models in in-context learning (ICL), which is an important and under-explored area.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments to support their claims, including on large language models like PaLM2, which adds practical relevance to the findings.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the theoretical proofs are well-explained, contributing to the understanding of the ICL problem.
- The experiments are designed to verify the theoretical claims, providing a robust validation of the proposed theories.
- The paper offers a novel approach to studying the convergence of prefix and causal language models in ICL, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to linear self-attention, which might not generalize well to more complex architectures like ResNet, raising concerns about the universality of the findings.
- The experimental setup is somewhat simplistic, using synthetic data and a limited set of transformer architectures, which might not fully reflect the complexity of real-world applications.
- The paper's claims about the sub-optimality of causalLM in ICL are based on a specific construction of the linear self-attention model, which might not be representative of all causalLM models.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the practical implications of the theoretical findings, which could limit its impact on the development of more effective ICL models.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the work, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of their findings for the development of more effective ICL models?
- How do the authors address the limitations of their theoretical analysis, particularly the assumption of linear self-attention?
- In light of the simplified experimental setup, how do the authors justify the conclusions drawn from the experiments?
- Could the authors provide more details on the construction of the linear self-attention model used in the theoretical analysis, particularly how it relates to real-world causalLM models?
- How do the authors envision the theoretical framework being applied to more complex architectures, such as those found in large language models like PaLM2?
