 **Summary:**
The paper presents a comprehensive benchmarking study of 12 structural inference methods applied to synthetic dynamical systems. The authors evaluate these methods across various interaction graph typologies and dynamical system simulations, aiming to identify their strengths, weaknesses, and performance under different conditions such as noise and graph properties. The study uses a novel dataset of trajectories generated by dynamical systems and provides insights into the scalability and robustness of each method. Despite the extensive evaluation, the paper's contribution is questioned due to its limited applicability to real-world datasets and its potential over-reliance on synthetic data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined research question.
- The authors have compiled a comprehensive set of methods from diverse fields, providing a valuable resource for researchers in the field of structural inference.
- The study is significant as it provides a benchmarking framework for evaluating the performance of various methods across different types of graphs and datasets, which is crucial for advancing the field.
- The paper includes a novel dataset of trajectories generated by dynamical systems, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The results are robust, showing a positive correlation between method performance and the average shortest path length of the graph, and revealing insights into the performance of different methods under varying conditions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily uses synthetic data, which may not adequately represent real-world datasets, raising concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
- The evaluation is limited to 11 interaction graph typologies, which may not be sufficient to capture the full range of interactions that could be present in real-world systems.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the methods, particularly in terms of computational resources and data efficiency.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the methods and the assumptions made in the study.
- The paper's focus on a specific subset of methods may limit the breadth of its impact and the depth of its insights.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation and tuning of the methods, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the choice of the number of time steps affect the results, particularly in terms of data efficiency?
- Could the authors clarify the differences in performance between different methods, especially those that perform similarly?
- What are the implications of using synthetic data for benchmarking methods in real-world applications?
- How do the authors address the scalability concerns, particularly in terms of computational resources and data efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the implementation and tuning of the methods to enhance the reproducibility and comparability of the results?
- How do the authors plan to address the limitations of the study, such as the use of static graphs and the limited number of interaction graph typologies?
