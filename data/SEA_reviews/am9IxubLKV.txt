 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of tensor networks (TNs) to represent convolutional layers in neural networks, aiming to enhance the computational efficiency and theoretical understanding of convolutions. The authors introduce a novel approach by representing convolutions as tensor networks, which allows for a more straightforward analysis and implementation. This representation is used to derive various operations like autodiff and curvature approximations, and to optimize the contraction order of tensors. The paper also discusses the practical implications of these theoretical insights, such as the potential for faster computation and improved memory management. However, the paper's presentation is criticized for being dense and difficult to follow, and the practical benefits of the proposed methods are not convincingly demonstrated.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to represent convolutions using tensor networks, which provides a new perspective on the computational aspects of convolutions.
- The authors demonstrate the expressive power of tensor networks by deriving diagrams for various autodiff operations and second-order information, showing the potential for more efficient computations.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed methods show promise in improving computational efficiency and reducing memory requirements, which are crucial for large-scale neural network training.
- The paper includes a thorough literature review and discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, providing a comprehensive understanding of the current state of the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is quite dense and difficult to follow, particularly for readers not familiar with tensor networks or the specific mathematical notations used.
- The practical benefits of the proposed methods are not convincingly demonstrated, with some results showing only marginal improvements or no significant differences compared to existing methods.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage, which could undermine the claimed advantages of the proposed methods.

- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, including a broader range of neural network architectures and a more detailed analysis of the computational and memory requirements of the proposed methods.
