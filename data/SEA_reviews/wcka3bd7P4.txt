 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph neural network (GNN) framework named FROND, which integrates fractional calculus into graph neural ordinary differential equations (ODEs). The authors explore the use of fractional derivatives to model graph neural ODEs, aiming to capture long-term dependencies and mitigate over-smoothing. Theoretical analysis and experimental results demonstrate that FROND can enhance the performance of existing graph neural ODE models across various datasets. The paper also discusses the relationship between fractional derivatives and fractal geometry, suggesting potential applications in graph learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and concise introduction that effectively motivates the use of fractional derivatives in graph neural ODE models.
- The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the FROND framework, including a detailed discussion on how fractional derivatives can capture long-term dependencies and mitigate over-smoothing in graph neural networks.
- The experimental results are robust and convincing, demonstrating that the FROND framework can enhance the performance of existing graph neural ODE models across various datasets.
- The paper is original in its approach, introducing a novel graph neural network framework that integrates fractional calculus, which is a significant departure from traditional graph neural networks.
- The authors provide a comprehensive literature review, effectively positioning their work within the existing body of research on graph neural networks and fractional calculus.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the FROND framework, which could be a significant concern for large-scale graph learning applications.
- There is an absence of experimental results on graph generation tasks, which could have provided valuable insights into the application of the FROND framework in generating new graphs.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of over-smoothing, which is a critical challenge in graph neural networks. The proposed solution, while innovative, may not be robust enough to address this issue in all scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the FROND framework, including its applicability to different types of graphs and the potential computational challenges associated with fractional derivatives.
- The experimental setup and results are somewhat limited, with a need for more comprehensive experiments across a broader range of datasets and graph neural network architectures to fully validate the claims made.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the computational complexity of the FROND framework and discuss potential strategies for reducing computational costs in large-scale graph learning applications?
- How does the FROND framework perform in generating new graphs, particularly in tasks such as molecular design and social network analysis?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the over-smoothing issue and discuss the robustness of the proposed solution across different graph neural network architectures?
- In the experimental results, why was GRAND-I not included in all datasets, and what were the specific reasons for its exclusion?
- Could the authors clarify the definition of the "Fractional dimension" and its relevance to the FROND framework?
- How does the FROND framework compare to other graph neural network architectures that use fractional derivatives, such as those mentioned in related works?
