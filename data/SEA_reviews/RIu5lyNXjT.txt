 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the sensitivity of large language models (LLMs) to subtle changes in prompt formatting, particularly in few-shot settings. It introduces FormatSpread, a method that evaluates a set of plausible prompt formats for a given task, providing an interval of expected performance without requiring access to model weights. The study reveals that widely used LLMs exhibit significant sensitivity to prompt formatting, even when factors like model size, number of few-shot examples, or instruction tuning are considered. The findings suggest that reporting a range of performance across plausible formats could enhance the reliability and comparability of LLM evaluations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach, FormatSpread, which evaluates a set of plausible prompt formats for a given task, providing an interval of expected performance without accessing model weights.
- The analysis on the sensitivity of prompt formatting in LLMs is insightful, highlighting the significant impact of seemingly innocuous features on model performance.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive set of analyses that characterize the nature of sensitivity to prompt formatting.
- The findings are significant, as they demonstrate that choices in formatting few-shot examples during in-context learning introduce spurious biases, which can lead to significantly different conclusions in model performance.
- The paper presents a novel tool, FormatSpread, which facilitates systematic analysis of variance across a wide set of semantically equivalent prompt formats within a user-specified computational budget.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how the grammar is constructed and how it is used to define semantic equivalence among formats.
- The methodology for measuring prompt sensitivity is not clearly explained, and it is unclear how the plausible prompt formats are defined and sampled.
- The paper's focus on classification tasks may limit the generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the results might be influenced by the specific choice of models or tasks used in the study.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on how the results might be influenced by the specific choice of models or tasks used in the study.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how it might be influenced by the specific choice of models or tasks used in the study.

**Questions:**

- How do the results generalize to other types of tasks, such as generation tasks?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the grammar is constructed and how it is used to define semantic equivalence among formats?
- How do the results vary with different model sizes and different numbers of few-shot examples?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the plausible prompt formats are defined and sampled?
- How does the proposed method ensure that the selected formats are not biased towards specific types of tasks or models?
