 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel hierarchical clustering algorithm, SHC, designed to address the issue of sensitivity to perturbations in hierarchical clustering. The algorithm is based on the concept of average sensitivity, which measures the stability of the clustering results under data deletions. The authors propose a new definition of average sensitivity for hierarchical clustering and demonstrate that their algorithm, SHC-SR, is less sensitive to perturbations than existing methods. The paper also discusses the theoretical properties and experimental results of SHC-SR, showing its superiority over existing methods in terms of stability and clustering quality.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new hierarchical clustering algorithm, SHC, which addresses the issue of sensitivity to perturbations in hierarchical clustering, a significant problem in data analysis.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a strong theoretical foundation.
- The proposed algorithm, SHC, is novel and provides a theoretical guarantee for its performance, with an approximate algorithm for minimizing sparsest cut.
- The paper provides a new definition of average sensitivity for hierarchical clustering, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experimental results demonstrate that SHC-SR outperforms existing methods in terms of stability and clustering quality, showing that it is a promising approach for robust hierarchical clustering.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the related work, particularly in the area of robust hierarchical clustering, which is crucial for understanding the context and the novelty of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with limited datasets and a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the credibility of the results.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and the scalability of the proposed algorithm, which are important factors for practical implementation.
- The definition of average sensitivity and its relation to existing definitions, such as those in Varma and Yoshida (2021), is not clearly explained, which could lead to confusion about the novelty and the theoretical contributions of the paper.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the choice of parameters and their impact on the performance of the algorithm, as well as a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between sensitivity and clustering quality.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational complexity and scalability, particularly in large-scale applications?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the choice of parameters and their impact on the performance of the algorithm?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-offs between sensitivity and clustering quality, especially in real-world applications where data perturbations are common?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the choice of datasets, the rationale behind the experimental design, and the results for other metrics such as dendrogram purity and cophenetic correlation?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where data perturbations are not due to deletions but rather due to other types of noise or errors?
- Can the authors clarify the definitions and relationships between different types of average sensitivity, particularly in relation to the definitions in Varma and Yoshida (2021)?
