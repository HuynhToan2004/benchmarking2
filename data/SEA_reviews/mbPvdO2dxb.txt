 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Meta-Guided Diffusion Models (MGDM), for solving inverse problems in medical imaging using diffusion models. The method leverages a bi-level guidance strategy to optimize data consistency and approximates measurement-conditioned posterior means. It includes a refinement phase to reduce discrepancies between outputs of different levels, thereby improving data consistency in recovered samples. The proposed MGDM model is evaluated against several state-of-the-art methods and demonstrates superior performance in terms of data fidelity and visual quality. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty and the lack of a comprehensive comparison with more recent methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive literature review and detailed theoretical analysis.
- The proposed method, Meta-Guided Diffusion Models (MGDM), is novel and technically sound, providing a significant contribution to the field of medical imaging.
- The paper includes thorough experimental evaluations and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method over existing approaches.
- The methodology is clearly presented, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to solving inverse problems using diffusion models.
- The paper includes both qualitative and quantitative results, showing that the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comparison with more recent methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it builds on existing methods like DDNM, with only minor modifications.
- The paper does not provide sufficient implementation details, such as the specifics of the diffusion model used and the training parameters.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and training time of the proposed method, which are crucial for practical deployment.
- The paper does not include results on more challenging datasets or in more complex imaging scenarios, which could demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and training time with other state-of-the-art methods?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the number of training samples and the specifics of the diffusion model used?
- Why was the comparison limited to only two methods, and could the authors consider including more recent and relevant methods for a more comprehensive evaluation?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results, including visual examples and additional metrics, to better demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in more challenging scenarios, such as with more complex imaging modalities or in the presence of more severe noise or artifacts?
