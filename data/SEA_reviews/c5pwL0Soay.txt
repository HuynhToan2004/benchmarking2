 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel unsupervised reinforcement learning (RL) method called METRA, which leverages a metric-aware abstraction to learn diverse behaviors from high-dimensional, complex environments. The core idea is to learn a mapping from the state space to a compact latent space, where the metric is defined by the temporal distance between states. This method aims to cover a significant portion of the state space, which is crucial for scalability in complex environments. The paper provides extensive experiments and comparisons with existing methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of METRA in various scenarios. However, concerns about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the experimental setup, and the lack of detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the method and its intuition, and includes extensive experiments that are well-presented.
- The introduction of the temporal distance metric for measuring the distance between two states is a novel and potentially impactful contribution to the field of unsupervised RL.
- The paper effectively covers a significant portion of the state space, which is crucial for scalability in complex environments.
- The method is evaluated in a wide variety of environments, including both state-based and pixel-based tasks, demonstrating its versatility and applicability.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the method's performance in different environments, comparing it with a large number of existing methods, which helps in understanding its strengths and weaknesses.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the experimental setup, particularly in how the method is implemented in different environments and how the results are validated.
- The method's performance is not consistently superior across all environments, and the paper does not provide a clear explanation for why METRA sometimes performs worse than other methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the method, which could be critical for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There are concerns about the novelty of the approach, as similar methods have been previously explored, such as using temporal distance as a metric and learning a mapping from the state space to a latent space.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the method compares with state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the method, which could be a significant concern for scalability in more complex environments.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the method is implemented in different environments and how the results are validated?
- How does the method compare with state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency, especially in more complex environments?
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies in performance between METRA and other methods, especially in environments where METRA performs worse?
- How does the method handle the potential issue of asymmetric temporal distances, which are currently embedded into a symmetric Euclidean distance in the latent space?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the method and how these might impact its applicability in different scenarios?
- How does the method compare with other unsupervised RL methods that also use similar techniques, such as the metric-aware abstraction used in METRA?
