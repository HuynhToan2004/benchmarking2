 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the correctness of automatic differentiation (AD) for neural networks with non-differentiable activations like ReLU and max pooling, focusing on the Clarke subdifferential. It presents theoretical results on when AD correctly computes the Clarke subdifferential, contrasting with previous works that only considered the Bouligand subdifferential. The paper also discusses practical implications, such as the use of distinct bias parameters and different proxy derivatives, which can ensure AD's correctness even in more complex network configurations. Experimental validations are provided to support these theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of automatic differentiation (AD) in neural networks, focusing on the Clarke subdifferential which is more widely used than the Bouligand subdifferential in previous works.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of concepts and results, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The theoretical results are novel and provide a deeper understanding of AD in practical neural network configurations, including the use of distinct bias parameters and different proxy derivatives.
- The paper includes experimental validations that support the theoretical findings, enhancing the credibility of the results.
- The discussion on the limitations of AD in practical scenarios is insightful, providing a balanced view of the technology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contributions appear somewhat limited, as it primarily extends previous results to the Clarke subdifferential without significant new insights or applications.
- The paper's focus on specific conditions, such as distinct bias parameters and minibatch sizes, may limit its applicability to more general neural network configurations.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of how these findings can be applied to improve AD in real-world scenarios.
- There are some issues with the clarity and organization of the paper, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key terms and concepts.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough literature review, especially in relation to existing works on AD and generalized derivatives.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly how these findings can be applied to improve AD in real-world neural network configurations?
- How does the paper's analysis of AD for neural networks compare to existing works, such as those by Bolte and Pauwels (2020a,b) and Davis et al. (2020)?
- What are the specific practical scenarios where the results of this paper are most relevant, and how do these scenarios differ from those addressed in previous works?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications of the theoretical results?
- In the context of the paper's focus on distinct bias parameters, could the authors elaborate on how this condition impacts the practical applicability of the results?
