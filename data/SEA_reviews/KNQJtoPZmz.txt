 **Summary:**
The paper explores the phenomenon of "simplicity bias" in overparameterized machine learning models, focusing on the generalization abilities of deep networks and neural networks. It argues that the probability space over models is biased towards simple functions, which is crucial for understanding generalization in overparameterized learning models. The authors present theoretical analyses and examples, including Boolean functions, infinitely wide neural networks, and infinitely deep non-wide neural networks, to support their claims. They propose that simplicity bias is a key factor in the effectiveness of these models, and that this bias is not dependent on the optimizer used. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the training of deep neural networks and the role of simplicity bias in generalization performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel perspective on the generalization of overparameterized machine learning models by focusing on the concept of "simplicity bias," which is a significant and under-explored aspect of model generalization.
- The authors provide rigorous theoretical analyses and examples, such as Boolean functions, infinitely wide neural networks, and infinitely deep non-wide neural networks, to support their claims.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the results and their implications.
- The authors propose a new approach to understanding the generalization ability of deep networks, which is both insightful and innovative.
- The paper challenges conventional wisdom by showing that simplicity bias is a critical factor in the generalization of deep networks, regardless of the optimizer used.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and concise summary of its main contributions, making it difficult for readers to grasp the key takeaways.
- There are several typographical and notation errors throughout the paper, which could confuse readers and detract from the paper's credibility.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its work, such as the assumptions made and the potential negative societal impacts of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive review of existing literature, which could have strengthened the argument by situating the work within the broader context of the field.
- The paper's claims about the universality of simplicity bias and its optimizer-independence are not convincingly supported by the examples and theoretical analyses provided.
- The paper's examples and analyses are somewhat contrived and do not convincingly demonstrate the general applicability of the simplicity bias phenomenon across different types of models and training scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the term "naive algorithm" used in the paper and explain how it contributes to the understanding of simplicity bias?
- How does the paper's analysis of simplicity bias apply to other types of models beyond neural networks, such as decision trees or support vector machines?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or experiments to support the claim that simplicity bias is optimizer-independent?
- How does the paper's analysis of simplicity bias relate to other existing theories, such as the Shannon effect or the Kolmogorov complexity reasoning?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of their findings for the training of deep neural networks, and discuss how simplicity bias might be leveraged to improve training outcomes?
- In light of the paper's claims about the universality of simplicity bias, how do the authors address potential counter-examples or alternative explanations for the generalization abilities of deep networks?
