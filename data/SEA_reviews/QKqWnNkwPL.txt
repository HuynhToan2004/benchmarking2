 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel self-distillation technique for diffusion models, aimed at reducing the number of sampling steps required for high-quality generation. By training a subnetwork to mimic the output of a larger network, the method aims to achieve faster convergence and comparable image sample quality. The approach involves using a small network to replicate the output of a larger network, which is trained using a loss function that encourages the student network to replicate the teacher's behavior. The methodology is tested across various models and datasets, demonstrating improvements in performance and efficiency. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, as it closely resembles existing self-distillation methods, and the paper's clarity in explaining the method and its experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel self-distillation technique for diffusion models, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is clearly presented, making it easy to understand and replicate.
- The paper demonstrates the application of self-distillation to diffusion models, which is a promising area for future research.
- The experiments conducted show improvements over previous methods, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, which is crucial for disseminating knowledge in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it closely resembles existing self-distillation methods, which might not be sufficiently differentiated from previous works.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art distillation techniques, which could have provided a clearer benchmark for its effectiveness.
- The method's complexity and the computational resources required are not thoroughly discussed, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive experimental evaluation, particularly in terms of the number of models tested and the statistical significance of the results.
- The presentation of the paper needs improvement, particularly in the clarity of the methodology and the results, which could be enhanced with better figures and clearer explanations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the distinction between their proposed self-distillation method and existing methods, especially in terms of novelty and effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method compare with other distillation techniques in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including statistical significance and broader model testing?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of diffusion models, such as those trained from scratch or using different architectures?
- Could the authors elaborate on the computational resources required for training and inference, and discuss the potential for reducing these resources?
- How does the proposed method impact the quality of the generated images, particularly in terms of FID scores and visual quality?
