 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for detecting disparities in feature importance across different subgroups, termed Feature Importance Disparity (FID). This method aims to identify subgroups with significant differences in feature importance, which could indicate biases in machine learning models. The authors propose an algorithm that optimizes the FID by solving a constrained optimization problem, which is shown to be NP-hard in the worst-case scenario. The approach is evaluated on various datasets and demonstrates its effectiveness in detecting subgroups with large FID values. However, the paper's assumptions and methodological choices, such as the assumption of separable feature importance notions and the use of an oracle-efficient algorithm, have been critiqued for their practicality and theoretical soundness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in machine learning, specifically the detection of disparities in feature importance across subgroups, which is crucial for understanding and mitigating biases in models.
- The proposed algorithm is theoretically sound, with a rigorous proof of its optimality, and it is empirically evaluated on multiple datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in identifying subgroups with large feature importance disparities.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, and includes extensive empirical results that support the theoretical claims.
- The idea of using feature importance disparities as a diagnostic tool for fairness is innovative and could be a valuable addition to the fairness literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper makes a strong assumption of separable feature importance notions, which might not hold in all practical scenarios, limiting the general applicability of the proposed method.
- The computational complexity of the proposed algorithm is high, which could be a barrier to its practical implementation, especially in large-scale datasets.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on related works, particularly those that address similar issues in feature importance and fairness, which could have enriched the context and comparison of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly robust, with some results showing large variance and potential biases in the datasets used.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the ethical implications of the proposed method, particularly in terms of how it might be misused or misinterpreted.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when the assumption of separable feature importance notions does not hold? Can the algorithm still be effective in identifying subgroups with large FID values?
- Is there a way to modify the algorithm to handle non-separable feature importance notions?
- Could you provide more details on the computational complexity of the algorithm and how it scales with the size of the dataset?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for detecting biases in machine learning models?
- Can you clarify the definitions and assumptions used in the paper, particularly the definitions of rich subgroups and the assumptions made about the feature importance notions?
- How do the authors address the potential biases in the datasets used, and what steps have been taken to ensure the robustness and reliability of the results?
