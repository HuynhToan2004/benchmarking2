 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel diffusion model-inspired random feature model (DRFM), which leverages random feature models to enhance the interpretability of diffusion models. The model is designed to be computationally efficient, using a fixed number of trainable parameters, and is theoretically justified through the derivation of generalization bounds. The DRFM is shown to perform comparably to fully connected neural networks and U-Net architectures on the Fashion MNIST and instrumental audio datasets. However, the experimental evaluation is limited, and the paper's claims about the model's performance and interpretability are not convincingly supported by the results presented. The paper's contribution to the field is questioned due to its limited novelty and the lack of a compelling experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise sections that effectively convey the key ideas and findings.
- The proposed model, DRFM, is a novel approach to enhancing the interpretability of diffusion models, which is a significant issue in the field.
- The paper provides a theoretical justification for the DRFM through the derivation of generalization bounds, which helps to establish the model's reliability and accuracy.
- The model is shown to perform comparably to fully connected neural networks and U-Net architectures on certain datasets, indicating its potential effectiveness.
- The use of random feature models in the context of diffusion models is a novel and interesting approach, which could lead to new insights and applications in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the model's performance, with only limited experiments conducted on small and specific datasets such as Fashion MNIST and instrumental audio.
- The experimental results presented do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority or even equivalence of the DRFM over existing models like U-Net and fully connected neural networks.
- The paper's claims about the model's interpretability are not convincingly supported by the results presented, and there is a lack of discussion on how the model's parameters translate into interpretable features.
- The paper's contributions are not clearly distinguished from existing works, particularly from Kawaguchi et al. (2018), which raises questions about the novelty of the proposed model.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the model.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the model's parameters translate into interpretable features, and how these features can be used to enhance the model's interpretability?
- How does the model perform on other datasets beyond Fashion MNIST and instrumental audio? What are the implications of using different datasets for evaluation?
- Could the authors clarify the model's performance in terms of denoising tasks, as there are inconsistencies in the results presented?
- How does the model's performance compare to other models in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the model, particularly in terms of data privacy and security?
- How does the model's performance vary with different parameter settings, and what are the implications of these variations for the model's effectiveness and interpretability?
