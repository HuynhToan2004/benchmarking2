 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a two-stage method for controllable image generation, where the first stage leverages a pre-trained segmentation model to generate a rough image guided by text and segmentation masks. The second stage refines the image quality using a diffusion model, focusing on improving image details and quality. This method aims to balance controllability and quality in text-to-image generation, showing competitive results compared to state-of-the-art methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the experimental setup, and the fairness of the comparisons made.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in the field of text-to-image generation, focusing on the controllability and quality of generated images.
- The proposed two-stage method is simple yet effective, utilizing a pre-trained segmentation model and a diffusion model to achieve controllability and quality in image generation.
- The method is flexible and compatible with various diffusion models, enhancing its applicability in different scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can produce high-quality images with good controllability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as similar methods combining different models for controllable image generation have been previously explored.
- The paper lacks detailed experimental settings, including the specifics of the pre-trained models used, the hyperparameters involved, and the training details.
- The method's reliance on a pre-trained segmentation model may limit its adaptability to different scenarios, especially when the segmentation model does not generalize well to new classes.
- The comparison with state-of-the-art methods is not fair due to the different training data and models used, which could mislead the evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential improvements.
- The method's performance in generating images from text-only prompts and its ability to handle complex image compositions are not thoroughly evaluated.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific segmentation model used in the first stage and how it was trained?
- How are the hyperparameters determined in the first stage, and what is the impact of different hyperparameters on the segmentation quality?
- What is the impact of the resolution of the input mask on the performance of the first stage?
- How does the proposed method handle cases where the input mask is not perfect, and what are the potential failure cases?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training data and models used for comparison to ensure a fair evaluation of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when generating images from text-only prompts, and what are the limitations in handling complex image compositions?
