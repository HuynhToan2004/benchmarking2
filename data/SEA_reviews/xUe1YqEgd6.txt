 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel unsupervised motion segmentation method using a transformer-based network that operates on a volume of consecutive optical flow fields. The method leverages a B-spline model to capture motion patterns over time and employs a loss function based on the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) framework. The approach aims to segment multiple motions in a video sequence in one go, demonstrating competitive results on various datasets. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods, and the evaluation primarily focuses on binary segmentation, which may not fully capture the method's capabilities in more complex scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel unsupervised motion segmentation method that is the first of its kind to involve long-term temporal consistency and segment multiple motions in a video sequence in one go.
- The method is technically sound, with a well-designed loss function that ensures temporal consistency in motion segmentation, and the use of B-splines to model motion patterns over time is innovative.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The methodology is simple and easy to understand, with a clear explanation of the network architecture and the loss function, which is inferred using the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) framework.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the network architecture, loss function, and training procedure, which is useful for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is limited as it builds upon existing methods, particularly those using B-splines for motion modeling and the ELBO framework for loss function design.
- The method's performance in handling complex motion patterns, such as articulated or non-rigid motions, is not adequately evaluated, which is a significant limitation given the complexity of real-world motion segmentation scenarios.
- The evaluation primarily focuses on binary segmentation, which may not fully capture the method's capabilities in more complex scenarios involving multiple motion segments.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential shortcomings.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with a broader range of state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of its performance.
- There are several typographical errors and unclear descriptions in the paper, which could affect its readability and comprehension.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and contribution of the transformer module in the proposed method, especially in terms of its impact on the model's performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the method perform when handling complex motion patterns, such as articulated or non-rigid motions? Are there specific scenarios or datasets where the method struggles to accurately segment these motions?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that also use unsupervised learning and optical flow fields for motion segmentation?
- What are the specific limitations of the proposed method, and how do these limitations affect its performance and applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the method handle occlusions and disocclusions in the motion segmentation process? Are there any strategies or techniques employed to mitigate the effects of these issues?
- Could the authors provide more detailed visual results, such as video demonstrations or additional frames, to better illustrate the method's capabilities and limitations?
