 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Concise and Organized Perception (COP), a novel approach to deductive reasoning that leverages large language models (LLMs) to efficiently process complex logical problems. COP utilizes LLMs to generate concept maps and mind maps from input rules and facts, which are then used to simplify and organize the reasoning context. The method demonstrates significant improvements in performance over existing methods on benchmarks such as ProofWriter and PrOntoQA, showing a reduction in the number of inference calls and an increase in the efficiency of reasoning. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its limited scope to specific datasets and the lack of a detailed comparison with other methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to deductive reasoning using LLMs, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is well-motivated and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- Extensive experiments are conducted, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across different datasets and LLMs.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the results, including the number of inference calls and the impact of different LLMs on performance.
- The use of concept maps and mind maps to structure the reasoning context is a novel and intuitive approach that enhances the understanding and application of logical rules.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other methods, particularly those that use similar approaches like MindMap and ToT, which could provide a clearer picture of the method's strengths and limitations.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to specific datasets, such as ProofWriter and PrOntoQA, and it's unclear how well it would generalize to other types of logical reasoning tasks or datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the time complexity or the computational resources required by the method, which is crucial for assessing its scalability and practical applicability.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method and how it could be improved or adapted for different types of reasoning tasks.
- The method's dependence on the quality of the initial input and the simplification of rules and facts by LLMs could limit its effectiveness in more complex or nuanced reasoning scenarios.

**Questions:**

- Could you provide more details on the time complexity and computational resources required by the method, and how it scales with the size and complexity of the reasoning problems?
- How does the method handle more complex or nuanced reasoning scenarios where the initial input may not be sufficient or accurate?
