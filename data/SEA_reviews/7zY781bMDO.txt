 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of return-conditioned supervised learning (RCSL) in offline reinforcement learning (RL) settings, focusing on the challenges of Bellman completeness and trajectory stitching. It introduces a model-based RCSL (MBRCSL) algorithm that leverages learned dynamics models to stitch together trajectories, thereby improving performance. Theoretical analyses are provided to show how RCSL can outperform dynamic programming-based methods when Bellman completeness is not satisfied. Experiments are conducted in simulated environments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. However, concerns are raised about the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, the clarity of the presentation, and the limited scope of the empirical evaluations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to offline RL by using RCSL, which is a promising alternative to traditional dynamic programming-based methods.
- Theoretical analyses are provided to show that RCSL can outperform dynamic programming-based methods when Bellman completeness is not satisfied.
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions and a well-structured organization that aids in understanding the complex concepts discussed.
- The empirical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods in simulated environments, showing that MBRCSL outperforms other model-based offline RL algorithms.
- The paper addresses an important problem in offline RL by showing that RCSL does not require Bellman completeness, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper makes strong assumptions about the dataset and the environment, which may not hold in practical scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the results.
- The theoretical analysis relies on specific assumptions about the dataset and the environment, which may not be practical or realistic.
- The empirical evaluation is limited to simple simulated environments and does not include more complex or realistic scenarios, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the definition of key terms and the explanation of certain theoretical concepts.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and how they compare to other existing methods, such as model-free RCSL and other model-based RL methods.
- The paper does not address how the proposed methods can be extended to handle stochastic environments, which is a significant limitation given the practical relevance of such environments.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and discuss how these assumptions affect the practical applicability of the results?
- How do the authors address the limitations of the empirical evaluation, particularly the simplicity of the environments used and the lack of diversity in the tasks?
- Can the authors provide more details on the choice of environments and the rationale behind their selection?
- How does the paper handle the limitations of RCSL, particularly in terms of its inability to stitch trajectories?
- Could the authors discuss the relationship between their work and other existing methods, such as model-free RCSL and other model-based RL methods?
- How do the authors ensure the robustness of their methods in stochastic environments, and what are the potential challenges in scaling up their methods to more complex and large-scale environments?
