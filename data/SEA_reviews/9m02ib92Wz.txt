 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DataInf, a novel influence approximation method aimed at large-scale generative AI models, including LLMs and text-to-image models. DataInf is designed to be computationally and memory efficient, offering a closed-form expression that simplifies computations. It approximates the influence function using a closed-form expression, which is shown to be faster and more efficient than existing methods like LiSSA and Hessian-free. The method is validated through experiments that demonstrate its effectiveness in identifying influential training data points and detecting mislabeled data. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its limited applicability to non-LoRA-based models and its theoretical assumptions that may not hold in practical scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel closed-form expression for computing the influence function, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- DataInf, the proposed method, is computationally and memory efficient, making it practical for large-scale generative AI models.
- The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of the approximation error, which is crucial for understanding the method's limitations and potential improvements.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of DataInf in identifying influential training data points and detecting mislabeled data.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on LoRA-based models, which limits its applicability to other models that use different fine-tuning techniques.
- The method's reliance on a closed-form expression for the inverse of a sum of a rank-one matrix and a diagonal matrix might not hold in practical scenarios where the model's parameters are not fixed.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant oversight.
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the experimental setup and results, such as the absence of error bars in some figures and the lack of comparison with other influence-based methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the model's parameters are not fixed or where the influence function might not be the most effective metric for model evaluation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method can be applied to non-LoRA-based models?
- Is there a theoretical or practical reason why the influence function is the most effective metric for evaluating the utility of model training data?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational complexity when the number of parameters is large?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of parameters and the datasets used?
- Could the authors address the inconsistencies in the experimental results and provide a more comprehensive comparison with other influence-based methods?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy with other influence function-based methods, especially in scenarios where the model's parameters are not fixed?
