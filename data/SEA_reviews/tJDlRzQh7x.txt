 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the use of neural networks to approximate Solomonoff induction (SI), a powerful universal predictor that is typically intractable due to its reliance on unlimited computational resources. The authors explore meta-training neural networks on universal data sources generated by a universal Turing machine (UTM), aiming to achieve convergence to various SI variants under certain assumptions. The study also evaluates the performance of different network architectures, including LSTMs and Transformers, on algorithmic data from variable-order Markov sources and tasks at different levels of the Chomsky hierarchy. The paper claims that scaling network size improves performance and that transformers outperform other models, even achieving optimality on certain tasks. However, the paper's empirical results are not convincing, and the theoretical claims are not well-supported by the experimental evidence.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and well-motivated problem by exploring the use of neural networks to approximate Solomonoff induction (SI), a concept that has been of interest for decades.
- The authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis, which is supported by extensive empirical results across various tasks, including variable-order Markov sources and tasks on different levels of the Chomsky hierarchy.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and accessible, with a comprehensive background section that effectively introduces the key concepts and notations.
- The paper includes a novel approach to generating synthetic data that makes universal approximators converge to SI in the limit, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experimental results are extensive and show that scaling network size improves performance on all tasks, with transformers outperforming other models in certain scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's empirical results are not convincing, with limited tasks and datasets used, which does not sufficiently support the claims of the paper.
- The theoretical claims, such as the convergence of neural networks to SI, are not well-supported by the experimental evidence, and the practical implications of the theoretical results are not clearly demonstrated.
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the limitations and assumptions of the proposed methods, particularly in relation to the practical applicability and the computational resources required.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical challenges of generating sufficient training data for the neural networks to converge to SI, nor does it discuss the computational efficiency of the proposed methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and assumptions of the proposed methods, particularly in relation to the practical applicability and the computational resources required.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed justification for the choice of tasks and datasets used in the empirical evaluation?
- How do the authors address the practical challenges of generating sufficient training data for the neural networks to converge to SI?
- Can the authors clarify the theoretical assumptions and limitations, particularly in relation to the practical applicability of the proposed methods?
- How do the authors justify the use of transformers for solving tasks on different levels of the Chomsky hierarchy, especially given the different memory structures required?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational resources required for the proposed methods and discuss the practical implications of these requirements?
