 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the theoretical lower bounds for the model size required to achieve a desired accuracy in machine learning-based database operations such as indexing, cardinality estimation, and range-sum estimation. It provides theoretical insights into the model size needed to ensure error bounds, both in worst-case and average-case scenarios. The study is based on information-theoretic arguments, using packing and metric entropy to bound the size of the model. The paper also presents empirical results that support the theoretical findings, comparing the model size with the error observed in experiments. Despite the theoretical contributions, the paper has been critiqued for its practical applicability, the lack of experimental validation, and the limited scope of its results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the problem and the main results.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the lower bounds on the required model size to achieve a desired accuracy, which is an important aspect of learned database operations.
- The paper includes both theoretical and empirical results, which support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical implications of the theoretical results.
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem of understanding the size of learned models in database operations, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper provides lower bounds on the required model size in terms of data size, dimensionality, tolerable error, and domain size, which are useful for practitioners and researchers in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly how the model size impacts the error and the performance of the learned database operations.
- The empirical results do not directly validate the theoretical findings, as they do not compare the model size with the error as predicted by the theory.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the theoretical results, which could affect the applicability of the findings in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's contribution is seen as limited as it only provides lower bounds on the model size without proposing new algorithms or methods to achieve these bounds.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the discretization of the data domain and the use of specific error metrics, may not be applicable in all practical scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of different data distributions on the model size and error, which could be a significant factor in practical applications.
