 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MADiff, a novel multi-agent offline reinforcement learning (MARL) framework that leverages diffusion models to model the complex coordination among agents. MADiff utilizes an attention-based diffusion model to handle multi-agent trajectory planning and incorporates a centralized training and decentralized execution (CTDE) framework. The framework is designed to address the challenges of offline MARL by learning from offline datasets, which is crucial for multi-agent scenarios. The paper presents extensive experiments across various environments, including SMAC, MPE, and NBA, demonstrating the effectiveness of MADiff compared to existing methods. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in explaining the centralized training and decentralized execution, insufficient baseline comparisons, and the need for more rigorous evaluation metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel application of diffusion models in multi-agent offline reinforcement learning (MARL), which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed MADiff framework effectively models complex interactions and coordination among agents, offering a promising solution for offline MARL.
- Extensive experiments are conducted across various environments, including SMAC, MPE, and NBA, demonstrating the effectiveness of MADiff compared to existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear descriptions of the proposed methods and results.
- The inclusion of an ablation study provides valuable insights into the effectiveness of the attention module in MADiff.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the centralized training and decentralized execution, which are crucial aspects of the proposed framework.
- The choice of baselines for comparison is insufficient, particularly missing recent works in the field of MARL.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational cost and scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The evaluation metrics used in the paper, such as the episodic return, may not be the most suitable for offline MARL, as it does not account for the variability in dataset quality.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability in environments with a large number of agents.
- There is a need for more robust experimental validation, including additional datasets and more rigorous comparisons with existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the centralized training and decentralized execution are implemented in the MADiff framework?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, particularly in environments with a large number of agents?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost and efficiency of the MADiff framework?
- How does the attention mechanism in MADiff handle the potential issues of self-attention in multi-agent scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of evaluation metrics and consider using more robust metrics that account for the variability in dataset quality?
- Is there a plan to include more recent works in the field of MARL as baselines for comparison?
- Can the authors provide more details on the implementation and training of the inverse dynamics model, particularly how it handles the potential instability in training?
