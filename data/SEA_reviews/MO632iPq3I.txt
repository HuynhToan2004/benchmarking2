 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel differentiable Euler Characteristic Transform (ECT) for shape classification, aiming to enhance the computational efficiency and applicability of the ECT in various data modalities such as point clouds, meshes, and graphs. The authors propose a DECT method that leverages a sigmoid function to approximate the ECT, making it differentiable and suitable for integration into deep learning models. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on datasets like MNIST-superpixel, ModelNet40, and several graph datasets, showing competitive performance compared to other topological descriptors. The paper also explores the potential of DECT in optimizing point cloud coordinates and learning the directions of ECT, providing a scalable and efficient alternative to traditional ECT methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The authors provide a comprehensive background on ECT and its variants, which is helpful for readers unfamiliar with the topic.
- The introduction of a differentiable ECT is a significant contribution, as it allows for the integration of ECT into deep learning models, enhancing its applicability and scalability.
- The experiments conducted are thorough and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various settings, including classification and optimization of point clouds.
- The paper is original in its approach, providing a novel method for ECT that is not only differentiable but also computationally efficient, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The potential applications of the proposed method are numerous, including its use in learning tasks such as shape classification and point cloud optimization.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other topological descriptors like persistent homology, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The choice of hyperparameters such as the number of directions and the hyperparameter λ in the sigmoid function is not thoroughly explored, which could affect the performance and generalization of the model.
- The experimental setup is limited to simpler datasets like MNIST-superpixel and ModelNet40, which might not adequately represent real-world scenarios with more complex shapes and larger datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the DECT, especially how it approximates the ECT and the implications of this approximation.
- There is a lack of discussion on the scalability and computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other existing methods in terms of performance and efficiency, which could help in better positioning the proposed method within the current landscape of topological descriptors.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a detailed comparison of DECT with other topological descriptors like persistent homology, especially in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the number of directions used in DECT affect its performance, and what are the implications of using a different number of directions for different datasets?
- How sensitive is the DECT to the choice of hyperparameters such as λ in the sigmoid function, and what are the recommended settings for different datasets?
- Could you elaborate on the theoretical justification for using a sigmoid function to approximate the ECT, and how this approximation affects the overall performance and accuracy of the model?
- How does DECT compare in terms of computational complexity and scalability with other existing methods for topological data analysis?
- Can DECT be adapted to handle more complex shapes or datasets, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in scaling up the method?
- How does DECT perform on more challenging datasets, such as those with higher dimensionality or more complex topological structures?
