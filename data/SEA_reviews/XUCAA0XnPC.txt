 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Ensembler, a framework designed to enhance security in collaborative inference by obfuscating intermediate representations, thereby hindering model inversion attacks. Ensembler operates by training multiple server models with distinct weights, allowing the client to selectively combine outputs to maintain model accuracy. This approach is evaluated against several baselines, demonstrating improved resistance to model inversion attacks. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, unclear writing, and lack of comprehensive experimental validation, particularly concerning the privacy protection and the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations and a well-structured presentation that makes it easy to follow.
- The concept of using ensembling to protect against model inversion attacks is innovative and interesting, offering a novel approach to privacy protection in collaborative inference.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of model inversion attacks and their impact on privacy, highlighting the importance of model splitting strategies in enhancing security.
- The proposed method of using a selector to combine outputs from multiple server models is a simple yet effective solution for privacy protection.
- The paper includes a comprehensive review of related works, providing a solid foundation for the discussion on privacy protection in collaborative inference.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining key terms and concepts, such as "regularization term" and "shadow network," which could confuse readers unfamiliar with the specific terminology used.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it appears similar to existing works, particularly those by He et al. and Zhou et al., which could lead to questions about its originality.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with insufficient evidence to support the claims of privacy protection and the effectiveness of the proposed method against various types of attacks.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational costs and communication overheads associated with the proposed method, which could be significant in real-world applications.
- The paper's assumptions about the adversary's capabilities and the model's vulnerabilities are not clearly defined, which could limit the generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and definition of the "regularization term" mentioned in the paper?
- How does the proposed method ensure privacy protection against different types of attacks, such as model extraction attacks?
- Can you provide more details on the computational costs and communication overheads associated with the proposed method, especially in comparison to other existing methods?
- How does the paper address the potential limitations of the method, such as its applicability to different model architectures and the scalability of the results across different datasets?
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing works, particularly those by He et al. and Zhou et al., to clarify the differences and the contributions of your approach?
- How does the paper address the assumption that the server has a good estimate of the DNN used for inference, and what are the implications of this assumption on the security of the system?
