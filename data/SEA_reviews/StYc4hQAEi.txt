 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for reducing the variance of Monte Carlo estimation of the sliced Wasserstein (SW) distance using control variates. The authors propose two novel control variates that serve as lower and upper bounds for the Wasserstein distance between two Gaussian approximations of two measures. These control variates are designed to improve the estimation of the SW distance, particularly in high-dimensional settings where the original SW distance suffers from the curse of dimensionality. The paper provides theoretical justification and empirical evidence demonstrating the effectiveness of these control variates in reducing variance and improving performance in various applications, such as point-cloud gradient flows and generative models. The methodology involves the use of Gaussian approximations of projected one-dimensional measures, which are then used to design control variates using the closed-form solution of the Wasserstein distance between two Gaussian distributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to reducing the variance of Monte Carlo estimation of the sliced Wasserstein (SW) distance using control variates, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors provide a theoretical justification for their method and empirical evidence demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing variance and improving performance in various applications.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear definitions and explanations of the proposed methods.
- The proposed method is computationally efficient and does not require additional computational complexity compared to the conventional estimator of the SW distance.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the variance of the SW distance and its relationship to the variance of the original Wasserstein distance, which is a useful insight for understanding the behavior of the SW distance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and variance reduction.
- The method's reliance on the assumption that the projections are Gaussian may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where the data is not Gaussian.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including more diverse datasets and comparing the proposed method with other variance reduction techniques.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the computational efficiency of the proposed method compared to existing methods, which could be a critical factor in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity of their method with existing methods, particularly in terms of the number of projections required to achieve a certain level of accuracy?
- How does the proposed method perform when the data is not Gaussian, and what are the implications of this for the accuracy and applicability of the method?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their method and suggest areas for future research to address these limitations?
- In terms of the experimental evaluation, could the authors consider using more diverse datasets and comparing the proposed method with other variance reduction techniques to provide a more comprehensive assessment of its performance?
