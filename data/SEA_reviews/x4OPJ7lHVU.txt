 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of differential privacy (DP) in in-context learning (ICL) to address privacy concerns in large language models (LLMs). It introduces a framework for differentially private in-context learning (DP-ICL) that utilizes a sample-and-aggregate paradigm to privatize ICL. The framework is evaluated across various tasks such as text classification, document question answering, and dialog summarization. The evaluation demonstrates that DP-ICL can achieve comparable performance to non-private ICL while maintaining privacy, although it comes at the cost of increased API calls. The paper also discusses the trade-offs between privacy and utility, suggesting that the method is practical for real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The topic of privacy in in-context learning is both relevant and important, addressing a significant concern in the deployment of large language models (LLMs).
- The proposed framework, DP-ICL, is novel and effectively integrates differential privacy into in-context learning, providing a practical approach to maintaining privacy while utilizing LLMs.
- The evaluation of DP-ICL is thorough, covering various tasks such as text classification, document question answering, and dialog summarization, which demonstrates the versatility of the method.
- The paper provides a comprehensive privacy analysis, including a detailed discussion on the privacy amplification through subsampling, which is crucial for understanding the privacy implications of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the trade-offs between privacy and utility, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed method.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a small number of queries, which might not adequately reflect the performance in real-world scenarios where a large number of queries could be used.
- The method relies on the choice of a privacy budget parameter, which could significantly impact the utility of the results. The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on how to choose this parameter, which could limit the practical applicability of the method.
- The paper does not discuss the potential impact of the proposed method on the performance of in-context learning, which is a critical concern given the known issues with in-context learning performance.
- There is a lack of comparison with other methods for private aggregation, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the privacy budget parameter and how it affects the utility of the results?
- How does the method perform when using a larger number of queries?
- Can you provide a more detailed comparison with other methods for private aggregation, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the method handle the potential degradation in performance of in-context learning, and what steps are taken to mitigate this issue?
- Could you provide a more detailed discussion on the trade-offs between privacy and utility, particularly in terms of the practical implications for real-world applications?
