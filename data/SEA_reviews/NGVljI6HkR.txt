 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of programmatic spaces for reinforcement learning (RL) in comparison to latent spaces, focusing on their effectiveness in program synthesis. The authors evaluate the performance of various search algorithms, including hill climbing, cross-entropy method, and beam search, in both programmatic and latent spaces. The experiments are conducted using the Karel domain, showing that the programmatic space outperforms the latent space in certain scenarios. The paper argues that the programmatic space is more conducive to local search algorithms, suggesting that it might be a more effective approach for program synthesis in RL.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear focus on the research question.
- The experimental setup is thorough and the results are well-presented, showing that the programmatic space outperforms the latent space in the Karel domain.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison between programmatic and latent spaces, highlighting the advantages of programmatic spaces for local search algorithms.
- The evaluation of the paper is comprehensive, with multiple metrics used to demonstrate the effectiveness of the programmatic space.
- The paper is significant in its exploration of the use of programmatic spaces in reinforcement learning, which is a relatively unexplored area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader evaluation across different domains and a more detailed analysis of the programmatic space, which could strengthen the claims made about its effectiveness.
- The choice of the Karel domain is questionable as it is a toy problem that might not generalize well to more complex environments.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the programmatic space, such as its scalability and the complexity of the grammar, which could impact its practical applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, especially those that use differentiable programming languages or other search algorithms.
- The paper's reliance on a single domain for evaluation limits the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the choice of the Karel domain and discuss its relevance and generalizability to more complex environments?
- How does the programmatic space compare in terms of scalability and complexity with other methods like differentiable programming languages or other search algorithms?
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the programmatic space and discuss potential strategies for addressing these limitations?
- How does the programmatic space perform in more complex environments, and what are the implications of its performance in such scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of metrics used in the evaluation and discuss their relevance and effectiveness in demonstrating the advantages of the programmatic space?
