 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for skill discovery in sparse-reward reinforcement learning (RL) environments using a technique inspired by tokenization in natural language processing (NLP). The method involves discretizing the action space into a vocabulary of skills through k-means clustering and then merging them using a proxy for the distance traveled in the observation space. This method is evaluated on several sparse-reward tasks from the D4RL benchmark and is shown to outperform several baselines, including SAC and skill-based methods. The paper also discusses the limitations of the method, such as the assumption of a common action space between demonstrations and downstream tasks, and the potential for the method to fail in environments with fast movements or high-dimensional visual inputs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear and concise presentation of the method and its implementation.
- The method is novel and innovative, leveraging tokenization techniques from natural language processing (NLP) to create a vocabulary of skills for sparse-reward RL, which is a promising approach to address the challenge of sparse reward environments.
- The method is shown to outperform several baselines, including SAC and skill-based methods, and demonstrates faster skill generation compared to neural network-based methods.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that validate the effectiveness of the method across various domains, including visual domains like CoinRun, and provides a detailed analysis of the method's performance and limitations.
- The method is efficient in terms of computational resources, requiring significantly less time for skill generation compared to neural network-based methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of the assumptions it makes and the environments where it may not perform well.
- The method's generalizability is a concern, as it assumes a common action space between demonstrations and downstream tasks, which may not hold in all real-world applications.
- The method's reliance on demonstrations for skill discovery may limit its applicability in scenarios where such data is not readily available.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the hyperparameters used in the experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The method's assumption that the action space of the downstream task is the same as the demonstrations may not hold in all cases, which could limit its applicability.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method with increasing degrees of freedom (DoF) in the action space, which could affect its performance in more complex environments.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform in environments with fast movements, such as locomotion environments, where the discretization of the action space could lead to suboptimal actions?
- How does the method compare to other skill discovery methods that do not rely on demonstrations, such as those that learn skills from scratch or from a set of predefined primitives?
- Can the method be adapted to handle continuous action spaces, and how would this affect the performance and the skill discovery process?
- How does the method handle the issue of redundant skills in the vocabulary, and what are the implications of pruning these skills on the overall performance of the method?
- Could the method be adapted to handle more complex environments with higher degrees of freedom in the action space, such as manipulation tasks?
- How does the method handle the issue of credit assignment, particularly in environments where the reward signal is sparse or delayed?
- In the experiments involving CoinRun, why was the resolution of the images reduced, and how does this affect the performance of the method?
