 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GraSP, a novel graph similarity prediction method that leverages a gating mechanism and residual connections within a graph neural network backbone to enhance node features. GraSP employs a multi-scale pooling technique to generate meaningful representations, which are used to predict graph edit distance and maximum common subgraph metrics. The method is evaluated on four datasets, showing superior performance over existing methods in terms of both effectiveness and efficiency. GraSP's approach includes positional encoding and multi-scale pooling, which are designed to improve the expressiveness of the model and pass the 1-WL test. The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the methodology and results, although it has been critiqued for its limited novelty and insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, GraSP, is simple yet effective, with a focus on enhancing node features through positional encoding and multi-scale pooling.
- GraSP achieves superior performance on both effectiveness and efficiency metrics, as demonstrated by extensive experiments on real-world datasets.
- The method is well-motivated, with a clear focus on improving the expressiveness of graph neural networks (GNNs) by passing the 1-WL test.
- The paper includes a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, comparing it against state-of-the-art methods and demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on related works, particularly in the area of graph similarity prediction, which could provide a richer context for the research.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods, such as those using attention mechanisms or other advanced techniques.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the methodology and results sections, which could benefit from further explanation or clarification.
- The paper's experimental setup and results could be improved by including more diverse datasets and a more rigorous evaluation of the method's scalability and robustness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the related works in the field of graph similarity prediction?
- How does the proposed method compare with state-of-the-art methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and the quality of the predicted graph edit distances?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the size and characteristics of the datasets used, and the specifics of the training and evaluation procedures?
- How does the method handle different types of graph data, and what are the implications for its performance and applicability in various domains?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the design choices made in the method, particularly the decision to use positional encoding and multi-scale pooling?
- How does the method perform under different conditions, such as with very large or very small graphs, and what are the implications for its scalability and applicability in these scenarios?
