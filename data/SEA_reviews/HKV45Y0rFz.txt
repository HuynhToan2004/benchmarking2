 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Data-Driven Confidence Minimization (DCM) framework, which aims to improve model robustness by minimizing confidence on an uncertainty dataset. This dataset is used to differentiate between known and unknown inputs, enhancing the model's ability to abstain from making predictions on out-of-distribution (OOD) data. The DCM framework is evaluated across various datasets and shows superior performance compared to existing methods. It is noted that the framework's effectiveness is contingent on the availability and quality of the uncertainty dataset, which could be a limitation in practical applications. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the DCM framework, providing insights into its effectiveness in scenarios where OOD data is prevalent.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, DCM, which effectively minimizes confidence on an uncertainty dataset, enhancing the model's ability to distinguish between known and unknown inputs.
- Theoretical analysis provided in the paper is robust, supporting the practical applicability of the DCM framework in scenarios where OOD data is prevalent.
- Extensive experiments conducted on various datasets demonstrate the superior performance of the DCM framework compared to existing methods, highlighting its potential in real-world applications.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors have provided a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, showing that DCM consistently outperforms them.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, making it a valuable contribution to the field of OOD detection.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practicality of the DCM framework in scenarios where the uncertainty dataset is not readily available or is of low quality, which could significantly impact the framework's effectiveness.
- Theoretical results, particularly Proposition 4.2, are not clearly explained, which could hinder understanding of the framework's theoretical underpinnings.
- The paper's reliance on a specific dataset (CIFAR-10) for most of its experiments limits the generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational overhead of the DCM framework compared to existing methods, which could be a significant concern in resource-constrained environments.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of label noise in the validation set, which could affect the accuracy of the model's predictions.

**Questions:**
- How does the DCM framework handle scenarios where the uncertainty dataset is not readily available or is of low quality?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the practical implications of the theoretical results presented in the paper?
- How does the DCM framework compare in terms of computational overhead to existing methods?
- How sensitive is the DCM framework to the quality of the uncertainty dataset?
- How does the DCM framework perform in scenarios where the validation set contains label noise?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and how these might impact its practical application?
- Is there a possibility to extend the DCM framework to other domains beyond image classification?
