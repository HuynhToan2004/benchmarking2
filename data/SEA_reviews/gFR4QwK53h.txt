 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel causal dropout model to address the issue of dropouts in single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data. The model proposes a theoretical framework that allows for the recovery of conditional independence relationships in the presence of dropouts by ignoring samples with zero values for conditioned variables. This approach is validated through extensive experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness compared to existing methods. The paper also discusses the assumptions and limitations of the model, including the potential impact of dropout rates on the performance of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is theoretically sound and has been validated through extensive experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper provides a novel perspective on handling dropouts in scRNA-seq data, which is a significant issue in the field.
- The method is flexible and can be integrated with existing structure learning approaches, making it applicable in various settings.
- The theoretical results presented are interesting and contribute to the understanding of conditional independence relationships in the presence of dropouts.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and assumptions of the proposed method, particularly how these assumptions might affect the performance of the method in real-world scenarios.
- The method's reliance on the assumption that dropout is only influenced by the expression level of the gene being measured could limit its applicability in more complex scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the potential impact of dropout rates on the performance of the method, which is a critical factor in scRNA-seq data analysis.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more datasets and varying dropout rates to better understand the method's performance under different conditions.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the method, which could be a concern for larger datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the implications of the method's assumptions and the potential limitations of the experimental design.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the assumption of a single parent for the dropout variable might not hold?
- Can the method be adapted to handle different types of dropouts, such as those caused by technical issues like library size or sequencing depth?
- How does the method perform when the dropout rate is high, and what are the implications of this on the inferred regulatory network?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the method, particularly in relation to the size of the dataset?
- How does the method compare to other existing methods for handling dropouts in scRNA-seq data, and what are the advantages of the proposed approach?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions and implications of the method, particularly in relation to the assumptions about the causal sufficiency and Markov condition?
