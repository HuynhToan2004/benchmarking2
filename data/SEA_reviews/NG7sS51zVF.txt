 **Summary:**
The paper introduces StreamingLLM, a novel approach to handling long contexts in language models by addressing the attention sink problem, where the model tends to allocate significant attention to the first few tokens, even if they are not semantically important. This issue is addressed by introducing a learnable sink token at the beginning of each training sample, which is not used for prediction but serves as a placeholder to absorb the excess attention. This approach, termed "attention sink," allows for efficient handling of long sequences without the need for fine-tuning. The method is evaluated across various models, including Llama-2, MPT, Falcon, and Pythia, and shows promising results in terms of perplexity and efficiency. The paper also proposes a new benchmark, StreamEval, to evaluate the performance of streaming LLMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and practical issue in language models, specifically the attention sink problem, which is crucial for handling long sequences.
- The proposed solution, using a learnable sink token, is simple yet effective and can be applied to existing models without significant modifications.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments are comprehensive, including evaluations on various models and a new benchmark, StreamEval, which is useful for assessing the performance of streaming LLMs.
- The paper presents a novel concept of "attention sinks" and demonstrates their impact on model performance, which is both interesting and insightful.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the effectiveness of the sink token across different layers and models, which could provide deeper insights into its impact.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational efficiency of the sink token approach, particularly in terms of the additional computational overhead it might introduce.
- The paper's claims about the attention sink phenomenon and the necessity of multiple initial tokens as sinks are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- There is a lack of comparison with other methods that could potentially address the attention sink problem, such as the use of larger context windows or different attention mechanisms.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the sink token, which could affect the reproducibility and applicability of the findings.
- The evaluation of the sink token approach is limited to perplexity metrics, which may not fully capture the performance improvements in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the sink token affect the model's performance across different layers, and is there a layer-wise analysis available?
- What are the computational costs associated with using a sink token, and how does it compare to other methods like increasing the context window size?
- Can the sink token approach be combined with other methods like RoPE to potentially improve performance?
- How does the sink token affect the model's performance on benchmarks that are not directly related to language modeling, such as those used in the NLP benchmarks mentioned?
- What is the rationale behind using a learnable sink token rather than a fixed token, and how does this choice impact the model's performance?
- Could you provide more details on the implementation of the sink token, including how it is initialized and managed during training?
