 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PolyNet, a novel learning-based approach that utilizes a single-decoder model to generate diverse solutions for combinatorial optimization problems. PolyNet employs a unique training strategy that updates the model based on the best solution found, thereby encouraging diverse solution strategies. This method is evaluated against state-of-the-art techniques across various optimization problems, including TSP, CVRP, and CVRPTW, demonstrating significant improvements in both solution quality and diversity. Despite its innovative approach, concerns were raised about the lack of comparison with some recent and relevant works, the limited scope of problem instances, and the clarity of some experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of combinatorial optimization, specifically focusing on the generation of diverse solutions.
- The proposed method is innovative, utilizing a single-decoder model that learns complementary solution strategies, which is a departure from traditional methods that rely on multiple decoders.
- The methodology is well-explained, making the paper accessible and understandable to readers.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear sections and a logical flow that facilitates comprehension.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method over state-of-the-art techniques.
- The paper includes a novel approach to diversity in neural CO, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with recent and relevant works, particularly those that address similar issues in neural combinatorial optimization.
- The experimental setup is limited to relatively small problem instances, which may not adequately demonstrate the scalability of the proposed method.
- There is a noticeable absence of a detailed discussion on the limitations and societal implications of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation on how the method would perform with larger problem instances, such as those with over 1000 nodes.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational efficiency of the method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the method to other combinatorial optimization problems beyond the ones tested.
- The experimental results are not always consistent, which raises questions about the robustness and reliability of the method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent and relevant works in the field of neural combinatorial optimization?
- Can the method be scaled to handle larger problem instances, such as those with over 1000 nodes?
- What are the computational requirements of the method, and how do they compare to other state-of-the-art methods?
- How does the method ensure that the diversity in solutions is beneficial, and not just random or suboptimal?
- Can the method be adapted to other combinatorial optimization problems beyond those tested in the paper?
- How does the method handle the training process, particularly the selection of the best solution and the update of the model weights?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to clarify the experimental results and their implications?
