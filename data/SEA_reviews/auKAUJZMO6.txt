 **Summary:**
The paper investigates how Large Language Models (LLMs) handle conflicting knowledge, focusing on their receptivity to external evidence that contradicts their parametric memory. It introduces a novel framework for constructing counter-memory to evaluate LLMs' behavior in such scenarios. The study reveals that LLMs can be influenced by external evidence, showing both receptivity and confirmation bias, depending on the coherence and relevance of the evidence. The experiments were conducted across various LLMs, including both closed-source and open-source models, using datasets like PopQA and StrategyQA. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the deployment of LLMs in real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely question regarding the behavior of LLMs when faced with conflicting knowledge, which is crucial for understanding and improving the performance of LLMs.
- The proposed framework for constructing counter-memory is novel and well-executed, providing a systematic approach to evaluating LLMs' responses to external evidence.
- The study is comprehensive, covering a wide range of LLMs and datasets, which enhances the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The results are insightful, highlighting the complex interactions between LLMs and external evidence, which are not always intuitive.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the study, particularly concerning the generalizability of the findings to more diverse or complex scenarios.
- The experiments primarily focus on factual knowledge, which may not fully capture the complexity of LLMs' behavior in more nuanced or ambiguous scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a clear definition or operationalization of "coherence" in the context of counter-memory, which could lead to ambiguity in the interpretation of the results.
- There is a potential bias in the selection of LLMs for testing, as the study primarily uses models known for their strong parametric memory, which may not reflect the behavior of weaker models.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough exploration of the ethical implications of LLMs' susceptibility to misinformation, particularly in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Can you elaborate on the potential limitations of the study and how the findings might generalize to more diverse or complex scenarios?
- How does the paper address the potential bias in the selection of LLMs for testing? Could the results be more robust if a broader range of models were included?
- Could you provide a clearer definition or operationalization of "coherence" in the context of counter-memory? How does this concept influence the interpretation of the results?
- How do the findings of this study relate to the broader ethical implications of LLMs' susceptibility to misinformation? What steps can be taken to mitigate these risks in real-world applications?
- In light of the potential for LLMs to generate misinformation, how can we ensure that the knowledge they elicit is accurate and reliable?
