 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Delayed EfficientZero, a model-based reinforcement learning algorithm designed to address stochastic execution delays in MDPs. This algorithm builds on the EfficientZero framework by incorporating a Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) mechanism to predict future states based on current actions and delays. The authors demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness through experiments on the Atari 100k benchmark, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper's contribution is seen as limited by its reliance on a learned forward model, which may not be practical in all real-world scenarios where delays are unpredictable.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to address stochastic delays in reinforcement learning (RL) by focusing on the class of Markov policies, which is more computationally efficient than history-dependent policies.
- The proposed Delayed EfficientZero algorithm is innovative and effectively leverages Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) to infer future states from action queues, thereby handling delayed execution while maintaining sample efficiency.
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of ideas and thorough empirical analysis that demonstrates the algorithm's superior performance in Atari games compared to existing methods.
- The theoretical results are well-explained, and the proofs are rigorous, providing a solid foundation for the claims made.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is seen as limited due to its reliance on a learned forward model, which may not be practical in all real-world scenarios where delays are unpredictable.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it primarily builds on the EfficientZero framework, with minor modifications for handling delays.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the independence of the delay process from the MDP environment, may not hold true in all real-world applications, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse environments and varying the delay parameters to better validate the algorithm's performance in different scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to continuous action spaces and its scalability to more complex environments.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed algorithm perform when the delay is not independent of the MDP environment?
- Can you provide more details on how the algorithm handles stochastic delays in continuous action spaces?
- How does the algorithm's performance compare to other state-of-the-art algorithms in terms of sample efficiency and scalability?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the Atari 100k benchmark and consider testing the algorithm on more diverse environments to validate its generalizability?
- How does the algorithm's performance vary with different delay parameters, and what are the implications for its practical applicability in real-world scenarios?
