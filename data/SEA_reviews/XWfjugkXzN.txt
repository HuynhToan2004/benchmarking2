 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the sampling of states from imperfect information game information sets to optimize learning. It explores the trade-off between the number of samples and the accuracy of the evaluation, using experiments on MNIST with corrupted labels, Texas Hold'em Poker, and Reconnaissance Blind Chess. The study demonstrates that sampling multiple evaluations per information set can enhance the model's performance, although the optimal number of samples varies across different tasks. The paper also discusses the computational efficiency and the impact of sampling on the learning process, suggesting that a fixed number of samples per information set can be beneficial for learning in imperfect information games.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem, focusing on the use of limited resources for learning in imperfect information games, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear problem statement and a well-defined objective.
- The experiments are well-designed, with results showing that sampling multiple evaluations per information set is beneficial, and the paper provides a detailed analysis of the results.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to sampling information sets to learn from imperfect information, which could be beneficial for learning in imperfect information games.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the impact of sampling on the learning process, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of different sampling strategies.

**Weaknesses:**

- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
