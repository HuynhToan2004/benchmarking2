 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) based attention mechanism called Expert Projection Attention (EPA), which aims to reduce the number of attention matrices required for computation and storage, thereby minimizing computational and memory requirements in Transformers. This method, which is compatible with MoE MLP layers, allows for the creation of a "Fast Transformer" that achieves performance comparable to dense counterparts while reducing resource usage. The EPA method is designed to handle both source and destination projections, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments on various language modeling datasets. The paper also provides a detailed analysis of the attention maps, showing that the maximum of attention maps taken over all heads are qualitatively similar to those of dense baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by applying Mixture of Experts (MoE) to the attention mechanism, which is an interesting and novel idea.
- The proposed method, Expert Projection Attention (EPA), is well-articulated and effectively addresses the challenge of reducing computational and memory requirements in Transformers.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a good presentation that aids in understanding the proposed methodologies and results.
- The experiments conducted on various language modeling datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the EPA method, showing that it can achieve performance comparable to dense counterparts while using fewer resources.
- The analysis of attention maps provides valuable insights into the behavior of the EPA mechanism, helping to understand its impact on the model's performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other attention-based methods like Sparse Attention and Linear Attention, which are also designed to reduce computational and memory requirements.
- The paper primarily focuses on the Transformer XL model, which might limit the generalizability of the findings to other Transformer architectures.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The results presented in the paper are not always clear, and some tables and figures (e.g., Table 2 and Figure 2) are difficult to interpret, which could hinder understanding of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of the impact of the number of experts on performance and computational efficiency.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with other models like RoPE and the use of more recent datasets to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancy in the performance of EPA with different numbers of experts, as shown in Table 2?
- How does the proposed method compare with other attention-based methods like Sparse Attention and Linear Attention in terms of computational and memory efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the hardware and software configurations used in their experiments to ensure reproducibility?
- How does the proposed method perform on more recent and larger datasets, such as those used in recent large language model studies?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with the MoA method, particularly in terms of computational and memory efficiency?
- How does the EPA method perform when combined with other acceleration techniques, such as those proposed by Dao et al. (2022)?
