 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to Out-of-Distribution (OOD) detection using activation histograms (HAct) of neural network layers. The method involves computing activation histograms from the training dataset, averaging them per class, and using these as a reference for detecting OOD samples. The authors propose a simple yet effective method for OOD detection, which is computationally efficient and achieves state-of-the-art performance on various benchmarks. The method is validated through extensive experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, as it resembles existing methods like those by Lacombe et al. (2021), and the experimental results are somewhat limited to specific datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, with a focus on computational efficiency.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on various benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The ablation study is thorough, providing insights into the method's performance and design choices.
- The method is described as a significant improvement over existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations and detailed experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as it resembles existing methods by Lacombe et al. (2021), particularly in the use of activation histograms.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to specific datasets and may not generalize well to others.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational efficiency of the method, which is crucial for practical deployment.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, especially when compared to the top-performing methods like LINE.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of hyperparameter selection, which is critical for the method's performance.
- The method's reliance on training data for the preparation step may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different architectures and its sensitivity to hyperparameter choices.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to existing methods, particularly those by Lacombe et al. (2021)?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency with other methods, especially those that do not require access to training data?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations on how the hyperparameters are selected and their impact on the method's performance?
- How does the method perform on other datasets, especially those not used in the experiments?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the method's sensitivity to different hyperparameters and how these choices affect the method's performance?
- How does the method perform when applied to different architectures, such as transformers?
