 **Summary:**
The paper explores the universal approximation property of dynamical systems using a finite set of flow maps, each corresponding to a unique composition of these maps. The authors demonstrate that this finite set of maps, which includes affine maps and generalized ReLU functions, can approximate any orientation-preserving diffeomorphism on a compact domain with arbitrary precision. This result is significant as it provides a theoretical basis for the expressive power of certain classes of neural networks, such as those with a finite set of learnable functions. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the design of efficient neural network architectures and the understanding of the universal approximation property in dynamical systems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to the universal approximation problem using dynamical systems, which is both interesting and relevant to the field.
- The proofs provided are constructive, meaning they offer practical guidelines for approximating any orientation-preserving diffeomorphism on a compact domain using a finite set of flow maps.
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions, notations, and a logical flow that aids in understanding the complex mathematical concepts.
- The results are significant, as they provide a new perspective on the universal approximation property of dynamical systems, which could inspire further research in this area.
- The paper is well-positioned within the existing literature, highlighting the novelty and relevance of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implications of the results, particularly how they might be applied to design more efficient neural network architectures.
- The set of flow maps used in the proofs is not optimal, and it is unclear whether there exists a smaller set of maps that could achieve the same results.
- The paper does not address the limitations of its approach, such as the computational complexity of approximating functions using the proposed set of maps.
- There are some minor issues with the clarity of the presentation, such as the need for more detailed explanations in certain sections and the use of potentially confusing notation.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its assumptions, such as the compact domain and the orientation-preserving property of the diffeomorphisms.

**Questions:**
- Can the results of this paper be used to design more efficient neural network architectures, and if so, how?
- Is it possible to approximate any continuous function using the proposed set of flow maps, or are there limitations to the class of functions that can be approximated?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of their results, particularly in terms of computational complexity and the efficiency of approximating functions using the proposed set of maps?
- Is there a connection between the results of this paper and the expressivity of neural networks, especially in terms of the finite set of learnable functions?
- How does the paper's approach compare to other methods for universal approximation, such as those based on dynamical systems or neural ODEs?
