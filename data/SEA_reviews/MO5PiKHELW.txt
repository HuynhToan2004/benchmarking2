 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the evolution of Syntactic Attention Structure (SAS) in masked language models (MLMs) during pretraining, focusing on a critical phase transition where SAS is significantly enhanced, leading to improved grammatical capabilities. The authors propose a regularizer to enhance or suppress SAS, demonstrating that its suppression early in training can accelerate the acquisition of SAS and potentially improve model performance. The study also explores the relationship between SAS and model complexity, suggesting that SAS might act as a simplifying bias that aids in model training. Despite these contributions, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper's presentation and the depth of its experimental validation, particularly in terms of its generalizability and the robustness of its findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise sections that make it easy to follow.
- The study of phase transitions and the developmental analysis of language models is a novel and intriguing topic, providing new insights into the training dynamics of masked language models.
- The use of a regularizer to enhance or suppress Syntactic Attention Structure (SAS) is a creative approach that could be beneficial for understanding and manipulating model behavior.
- The findings on the relationship between SAS and model complexity, particularly the simplifying bias, are interesting and could lead to new research directions.
- The paper is well-motivated, with clear explanations and insights into the phase transition phenomenon, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the generalizability of the findings, particularly whether the phase transition and SAS are specific to the BERT model or can be observed in other models.
- There is a need for more detailed explanations and examples to clarify the computational details of the proposed regularizer, which is crucial for reproducibility and understanding.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly robust, with inconsistencies in the reported metrics and a lack of statistical significance in some results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the area of causal interpretability, which is highly relevant to the topic.
- The paper's claims about the necessity of SAS for grammatical reasoning are not convincingly supported by the evidence presented, which could lead to confusion about the role and impact of SAS in model performance.
- The paper's focus on BERTBase limits the generalizability of the findings, and it would be beneficial to see how these phenomena manifest in larger models or different model architectures.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify whether the phase transition and SAS are specific to the BERT model or if they can be observed in other models?
- How does the proposed regularizer function computationally, and can the authors provide more detailed examples or explanations to aid in understanding?
- Could the authors elaborate on the relationship between SAS and model complexity, particularly how this simplifying bias influences model performance?
- How do the authors explain the discrepancies in the reported metrics, such as the UAS and BLiMP scores, and the inconsistencies in the experimental results?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the role of SAS in grammatical reasoning, particularly in light of the mixed evidence presented in the paper?
- How does the proposed regularizer affect the model's performance in terms of other aspects of language understanding, such as semantic or pragmatic capabilities?
