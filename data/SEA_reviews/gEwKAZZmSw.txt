 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel variance-controlled adaptive sampling (VCAS) method aimed at reducing computational costs in neural network training by selectively dropping data and tokens during backpropagation. This method is designed to maintain the original training loss trajectory and validation accuracy while significantly reducing FLOPs. The VCAS method is evaluated across various fine-tuning and pre-training tasks in both vision and natural language domains, demonstrating up to 73.87% FLOPs reduction in backpropagation and 49.58% FLOPs reduction in the whole training process. Despite its effectiveness in reducing computational costs, concerns are raised about the method's novelty and the fairness of the experimental comparisons.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a simple yet effective method for reducing computational costs in neural network training by dropping data and tokens during backpropagation, which is a novel approach compared to previous methods that only drop samples.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it easy to understand, and the paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various tasks and datasets.
- The VCAS method is shown to be effective in reducing computational costs significantly, with up to 73.87% FLOPs reduction in backpropagation and 49.58% FLOPs reduction in the whole training process, while maintaining the original training loss trajectory and validation accuracy.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with good figures and clear explanations of the methodology and experiments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques, with the main contribution being the variance-controlled adaptive sampling strategy.
- The experimental comparisons may not be fair as the VCAS method requires additional hyperparameters for variance control, which were not optimized for the baseline methods.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of wall-clock time reduction, which is crucial for evaluating the practical benefits of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the computational overhead of the proposed method and how it compares to other efficient training methods.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive ablation studies to better understand the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the VCAS method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how they affect its applicability and performance?
- How does the VCAS method compare to other efficient training methods in terms of computational overhead and practical benefits?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the optimization of hyperparameters for baseline methods?
- How does the VCAS method perform in terms of wall-clock time reduction, and could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of this metric across different tasks and datasets?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational overhead of the VCAS method and how it scales with different model sizes and training datasets?
- Could the authors provide more details on the variance control hyperparameters and how they were set for the baseline methods?
