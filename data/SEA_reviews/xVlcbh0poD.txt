 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AutoRT, a system designed to collect diverse data for training robots in various real-world environments using large language models (LLMs) and vision language models (VLMs). AutoRT employs multiple LLMs and VLMs to generate tasks, filter tasks, and execute tasks, aiming to collect a large dataset of robot demonstrations. The system is evaluated through a 7-month deployment across multiple buildings and 20 robots, showing improvements in task diversity and generalization over existing methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is critiqued for its lack of clarity in explaining certain components and its reliance on human supervision, which may limit its scalability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in the field of robot learning by proposing a method for collecting diverse data in real-world environments, which is crucial for training robots.
- The use of LLMs and VLMs to generate tasks and filter tasks is innovative and could potentially reduce the need for extensive human intervention.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the AutoRT system and its components.
- The evaluation of AutoRT is comprehensive, including both quantitative and qualitative assessments, and demonstrates improvements in task diversity and generalization over existing methods.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the system's components and their effectiveness, including a comparison with existing methods and a discussion on the system's limitations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining certain components of the system, such as the role of the robot constitution and the filtering process, which are crucial for understanding the system's operation.
- There is a lack of detailed information on the implementation and evaluation of the system, including the specifics of the LLMs and VLMs used, the training data for these models, and the evaluation metrics for task diversity and generalization.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the safety mechanisms and human supervision required for the system, which are critical for ensuring the safety and reliability of the data collected.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the system, particularly in terms of the number of robots and the complexity of the environments it can handle.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods in terms of task diversity and generalization, which could help to better position the AutoRT system within the current research landscape.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the implementation of the robot constitution and how it influences the system's operation?
- How does the filtering process work, and what criteria are used to determine which tasks are accepted or rejected?
- Could you clarify the role of the human supervisor in the system and how their input is incorporated into the task generation and filtering processes?
- How does the system handle scenarios where the LLMs and VLMs fail to generate or filter tasks effectively?
- What specific metrics were used to evaluate task diversity and generalization, and how do these metrics compare to those used in existing methods?
- How does the system ensure safety and reliability in real-world deployments, particularly in environments where human supervision may not be available?
- Could you provide more details on the evaluation of the system, including the specific environments and tasks used, and the criteria for evaluating task diversity and generalization?
