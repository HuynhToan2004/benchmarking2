 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework, SCoRe, for representation learning in class-imbalanced settings, employing submodular combinatorial loss functions. This framework addresses the challenges of large inter-class bias and intra-class variance in deep learning models by modeling diversity and cooperation among feature clusters. It demonstrates its effectiveness through experiments on benchmarks such as CIFAR-10, MedMNIST, IDD, and LVIS, showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art metric learners. The paper also discusses the generalization of existing contrastive learning approaches to submodular counterparts, enhancing the understanding and application of submodular functions in representation learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- It introduces a novel framework, SCoRe, which addresses the challenges of large inter-class bias and intra-class variance in deep learning models, particularly in class-imbalanced settings.
- The paper presents a comprehensive review of related work and effectively demonstrates the generalization of existing contrastive learning approaches to submodular counterparts.
- The experiments conducted on benchmarks such as CIFAR-10, MedMNIST, IDD, and LVIS show that the proposed loss functions consistently outperform state-of-the-art approaches in metric learning for class-imbalanced classification tasks.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the SCoRe framework and the submodular combinatorial loss functions, enhancing the understanding of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed loss functions, which is crucial for understanding their practical applicability, especially in large-scale datasets.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets and do not include a broader range of datasets to validate the generalizability of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive ablation study on the hyperparameters of the proposed loss functions, which could provide insights into their sensitivity and optimal settings.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, which could help in understanding their scope and applicability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the impact of different submodular functions on the performance of the proposed methods, which could help in understanding the trade-offs between different loss functions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed loss functions and how it scales with the size of the dataset?
- How do the proposed methods perform on other datasets such as ImageNet-LT and Places-LT, which are widely used in long-tail learning?
- Can the authors provide a detailed ablation study on the hyperparameters of the proposed loss functions and their impact on the performance?
- How do the proposed methods compare in terms of training time and computational resources required compared to other state-of-the-art methods?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed methods and provide insights into when they might not be suitable or effective?
- How do the proposed methods perform in scenarios where the data distribution is not class-imbalanced but has other forms of imbalance, such as temporal or spatial imbalance?
