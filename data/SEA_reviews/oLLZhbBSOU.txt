 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to reinforcement learning (RL) called RLIF (Reinforcement Learning via Intervention Feedback), which utilizes intervention signals from suboptimal human experts as rewards. This method leverages the assumption that experts intervene when they perceive suboptimal behavior from the RL agent, thereby providing negative rewards that guide the RL agent to learn optimal policies. The approach is evaluated on both simulated and real-world robotic tasks, showing promising results compared to traditional methods like DAgger. Theoretical analysis is provided to justify the method's effectiveness, and empirical results demonstrate its superiority over DAgger in various settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed RLIF algorithm is novel and innovative, offering a new perspective on reinforcement learning that utilizes intervention signals from suboptimal human experts as rewards.
- The theoretical analysis provided is rigorous and supports the claims made about the algorithm's effectiveness.
- The empirical results are convincing, showing the superiority of RLIF over DAgger in various settings, including real-world applications.
- The method is flexible and can be used with any off-policy RL algorithm, making it applicable to a wide range of problems.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments, including both simulated and real-world scenarios, which demonstrate the robustness and practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the RLIF algorithm, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The experimental results are not comprehensive enough, with insufficient comparisons to other RL algorithms that do not require reward signals.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the assumptions made, particularly Assumption 6.1, which is crucial for the validity of the theoretical results.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed explanations of the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the robot arm used in the real-world experiments.
- There is a need for a more rigorous evaluation of the method's performance with different types of suboptimal experts, as well as a comparison to other methods that use different types of rewards or intervention strategies.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in Theorem 6.3 and Corollary 6.4, particularly regarding the knowledge of $V^{\pi^{ref}}$?
- How does the RLIF algorithm perform when used with different types of RL algorithms, such as those based on deep deterministic policy gradient (DDPG)?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the robot arm used in the real-world experiments and the methodology for evaluating the success rate of the robot in inserting the peg?
- How does the RLIF algorithm compare to other methods that use different types of rewards or intervention strategies, such as those that use human demonstrations as rewards?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of different intervention strategies on the performance of the RLIF algorithm, and provide a more detailed analysis of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis?
