 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel neural network architecture, termed GConvNets, which utilizes tensor neurons instead of scalar neurons in traditional convolutional neural networks (ConvNets). This modification allows for the handling of structured feature maps, which are claimed to enhance performance and reduce model size. The paper presents a generalized convolution operation and discusses its application across various datasets including CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and Tiny ImageNet. Despite the innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its lack of clarity in definitions and mathematical explanations, insufficient experimental validation, and unclear advantages over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by replacing scalar neurons with tensor neurons, which is a significant departure from traditional neural network architectures.
- The proposed GConvNets demonstrate superior efficiency in terms of parameter usage, as shown through experimental results.
- The paper provides a comprehensive explanation of the GConvNets architecture, including its structure and components, and offers a detailed analysis of its effectiveness.
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of the advantages of GConvNets over existing methods, such as CapsNets and Transformers, and have demonstrated the potential of GConvNets in various applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining key terms such as "structured neurons" and "structured hidden representations," which are crucial for understanding the proposed method.
- There is a notable absence of mathematical proofs or rigorous theoretical analysis to support the claims made about the advantages of GConvNets.
- The paper does not adequately compare GConvNets with state-of-the-art methods, nor does it provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity or training time of GConvNets.
- The experimental results presented in the paper are not sufficiently convincing, with the accuracy of GConvNets not surpassing that of existing methods like ResNets and EfficientNets.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the tensor product operation within GConvNets contributes to the network's performance or how it differs from traditional convolutional operations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the tensor product operation and its role within GConvNets?
- How does the proposed GConvNets architecture compare to other recent methods, such as those mentioned in the paper, in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of "structured neurons" and "structured hidden representations" within the context of GConvNets?
- How does the GConvNets architecture handle the computational complexity and training time, especially in comparison to traditional ConvNets?
- Could the authors elaborate on the advantages of GConvNets over other methods like CapsNets and Transformers, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
