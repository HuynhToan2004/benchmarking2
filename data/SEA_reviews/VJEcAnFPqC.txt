 **Summary:**
The paper investigates stepwise inference in transformers through a novel synthetic task, which is a simplified version of a directed acyclic graph (DAG) navigation problem. The authors explore how the model's performance varies under different conditions, such as zero-shot, few-shot, and scratchpad settings, to understand the mechanisms of stepwise inference. Key findings include the effectiveness of stepwise inference over direct inference, the influence of in-context exemplars on model navigation, and the impact of sampling temperature on accuracy and diversity. The paper also introduces a DAG generation process and a synthetic dataset to study these phenomena.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a synthetic task that is simple, interpretable, and controllable, providing a new perspective on stepwise inference in transformers.
- The use of a synthetic graph navigation model to study the mechanisms of stepwise inference is innovative and offers a novel approach to understanding the behavior of transformers.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and includes detailed explanations of the experiments, making it accessible and understandable.
- The authors present a comprehensive analysis of the stepwise inference gap, the diversity-accuracy tradeoff, and the bias towards shorter paths, which are significant contributions to the field.
- The experimental results are robust and well-presented, showing that the model can generalize to unseen orders of motif and that in-context exemplars can steer the navigation path chosen by the model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples of the synthetic graph navigation model, particularly in the introduction section, to better illustrate its utility.
- The paper's focus on the performance of transformers in solving DAG navigation tasks might limit its applicability to other types of transformers, such as those used in natural language processing.
- The synthetic graph navigation model's relevance to real-world applications is questionable, as it might not accurately represent the behavior of transformers in more complex, natural language scenarios.
- The paper's findings, while interesting, are not particularly surprising and may not offer new insights beyond what is already known in the field.
- The paper lacks a broader discussion on the limitations of the model and the potential societal impacts of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the relevance of the DAG generation process to the main findings of the paper?
- How do the authors plan to extend the model to handle more complex, real-world scenarios, such as those involving natural language processing?
- In Figure 4, why does the model's accuracy increase with temperature at low temperatures, and what does this imply about the model's behavior at higher temperatures?
- Can the authors explain the discrepancy between the model's performance in the single graph and multi-graph scenarios, particularly in terms of the path diversity observed?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of their model, such as its applicability to real-world scenarios and its generalizability to other types of transformers?
