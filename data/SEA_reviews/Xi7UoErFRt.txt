 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedGP, a method designed to address catastrophic forgetting in continual federated learning (CFL) by leveraging local buffer samples and aggregated buffer gradients. This method aims to improve performance across various continual learning and CFL techniques, as demonstrated through experiments on standard benchmarks. The approach is compatible with existing methods and has been shown to enhance accuracy and reduce forgetting in diverse scenarios. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of its presentation, and the robustness of its experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of continual federated learning, focusing on mitigating catastrophic forgetting, which is crucial for improving model performance over time.
- The proposed method, FedGP, is novel and innovative, utilizing local buffer samples and aggregated buffer gradients to preserve knowledge across multiple clients, which is a unique approach in the field.
- The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and provides a comprehensive literature review, enhancing its accessibility and relevance to the research community.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that FedGP can improve the performance of existing CL and CFL methods across various benchmark datasets.
- The method is compatible with existing FL+CL and CFL techniques, which makes it versatile and applicable in a variety of scenarios.
- The paper includes a thorough discussion of related work and presents a clear and well-organized structure, which facilitates understanding and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the number of tasks and clients, which could limit its applicability in more complex or large-scale settings.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations or potential drawbacks of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the methodology and results, which could make it difficult for readers to fully grasp the implications and contributions of the research.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust or convincing, with some results showing marginal improvements or inconsistencies across different settings.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational and communication costs associated with the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation of the method's performance in different scenarios, such as asynchronous task boundaries or varying data distributions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the number of tasks and clients it can handle?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational and communication costs?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the reference gradient in the proposed method, and how it is chosen or updated?
- In the experimental results, what do the numbers in brackets represent, and how do they affect the interpretation of the results?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex or realistic settings, such as asynchronous task boundaries or varying data distributions?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations or potential drawbacks of the proposed method, and how these might affect its practical applicability?
