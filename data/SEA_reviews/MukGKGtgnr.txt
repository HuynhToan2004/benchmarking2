 **Summary:**
The paper explores the identification of causal structures in linear latent variable models, focusing on scenarios where traditional assumptions such as non-Gaussianity, purity, and two-pure-children are not necessary. It introduces new assumptions and proposes algorithms to recover the causal structure from observational data, showing that latent variables can be identified under certain conditions. The paper also discusses the identification of causal structures in cases where these assumptions are relaxed, providing theoretical proofs and experimental validations. The main contributions include the identification of latent variables and the recovery of causal relations between them and observed variables, which is crucial for understanding complex systems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of causal discovery, focusing on the identification of latent variables and the recovery of causal relations between them and observed variables.
- The proposed algorithms are theoretically sound and computationally efficient, with proofs of identification under milder assumptions than traditional methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and the assumptions and methodologies are clearly articulated.
- The introduction of new assumptions and the demonstration of their effectiveness in identifying latent variables and recovering causal structures is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The experimental results show the effectiveness of the proposed methods, providing empirical evidence for their utility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies on strong assumptions that may not hold in real-world scenarios, such as the linearity and faithfulness of the causal model, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The experimental setup is limited, with only synthetic data used, which may not adequately represent real-world complexities.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed assumptions and the potential challenges in identifying them in practical scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the algorithms, which could be a significant concern in large-scale applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related works and a clearer delineation of its contributions compared to existing literature.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the linearity and faithfulness of the causal model, and discuss their practical applicability?
- How does the paper handle non-linear relationships and cyclic dependencies, which are common in real-world systems?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the algorithms and how they scale with the size of the data?
- How do the proposed assumptions compare to those in existing literature, and what are the specific advantages of the new assumptions introduced in this paper?
- In the context of the experiments, how do the results compare when using different types of data, such as real-world data, and how do they perform in scenarios with a larger number of variables?
