 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Multi-Method Self-Training (MMST), which leverages different methods within a large language model (LLM) to enhance its performance on various tasks. This approach involves training one method on the outputs of another, thereby improving both less performant and more performant methods. The methodology is tested using a 176B parameter model, showing improvements in performance across different tasks such as math problem solving and out-of-domain reasoning. The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's effectiveness and its potential for future research, although it has been critiqued for its limited scope and the need for more comprehensive evaluations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method, Multi-Method Self-Training (MMST).
- The method is simple, intuitive, and effective, as demonstrated through experiments that show improvements in performance.
- The analysis of why MMST works is insightful, providing a deeper understanding of the method's effectiveness.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to training LLMs, which could potentially enhance the performance of various models.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments and ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of MMST, demonstrating its potential as a valuable tool for enhancing model performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a single model (BLOOM), which might not generalize well to other models or tasks.
- The paper lacks a broader comparison with other methods, which could help in understanding the method's relative effectiveness.
- The method's application is currently limited to text and code generation, which may not be applicable to other types of tasks.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis on how different methods perform in different scenarios, which could provide valuable insights into the method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of using MMST, which is a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experiments, including those that test the method's effectiveness on larger datasets and more diverse tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on how the method could be adapted for other types of tasks beyond text and code generation?
- How does the method perform when applied to different models, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost of using MMST, especially in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the method perform when applied to larger datasets, and what are the implications for its scalability?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending the method to include more than two methods, and how this might affect its effectiveness?
- Is there a possibility of using the method in a zero-shot or few-shot setting, and how might this affect its performance?
