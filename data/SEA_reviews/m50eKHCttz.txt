 **Summary:**
The paper explores the complementary knowledge between different pre-trained models, proposing a method to transfer this knowledge from one model to another without significant performance degradation. The approach is based on data partitioning, where samples are classified into two sets: one for knowledge transfer and another for retaining original knowledge. This methodology is evaluated across various models and datasets, showing promising results in terms of transfer success rates. However, the paper has been criticized for its lack of clarity in methodological details, insufficient comparisons with existing methods, and potential issues with the experimental setup and the interpretation of results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer between pretrained models, focusing on the complementary knowledge between different models, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, with a focus on data partitioning, which is a new perspective in knowledge transfer.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, demonstrating its effectiveness across various datasets and models.
- The experimental results showcase the method's ability to transfer knowledge from weaker models to stronger ones, highlighting its practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the methodological details, particularly in the explanation of how the method is applied and the rationale behind certain design choices.
- There is a significant lack of comparison with existing methods, which makes it difficult to assess the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with some results showing inconsistencies or insufficient detail, which undermines the credibility of the findings.
- The paper's claims about the existence and utility of complementary knowledge between models are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence, which could lead to skepticism about the validity of the findings.
- The paper's reliance on a single dataset (ImageNet) for evaluation limits the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a clearer explanation of how the method is applied, including the specifics of data partitioning and the rationale behind the design choices?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including standard deviations and a broader range of datasets to validate the findings?
- How does the paper address the issue of overfitting in the data partitioning process, and what measures are in place to ensure that the student model does not overfit to the data?
- Could the authors clarify the meaning and implications of the term "complementary knowledge" and its role in the proposed method?
- In light of the concerns about the experimental setup and results, how do the authors ensure the reliability and validity of their findings?
