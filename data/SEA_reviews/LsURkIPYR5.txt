 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LaneSegNet, a novel end-to-end mapping network designed to predict lane segments for autonomous driving applications. This approach integrates both geometry and topology information, which is a departure from traditional methods that either focus on geometry-based lane detection or topology-based centerline perception. LaneSegNet utilizes a lane attention module to capture critical details within long-range feature spaces and employs an identical initialization strategy for reference points to enhance learning of positional priors. The model is evaluated on the OpenLane-V2 dataset, showing significant improvements in map element detection, centerline perception, and the newly defined lane segment perception. Despite its innovative approach and the release of code, the paper faces criticism for its incremental technical novelty and the limited scope of its experiments, which are primarily focused on the OpenLane-V2 dataset.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to lane segment prediction, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that focus on either geometry-based lane detection or topology-based centerline perception.
- The methodology is supported by a comprehensive set of experiments, including an ablation study, which helps to validate the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of a new metric for lane segment perception is a valuable contribution to the field, providing a more nuanced way to evaluate the performance of autonomous driving systems.
- The paper includes the release of code, which facilitates reproducibility and further research in the field.
- The proposed lane attention module, which utilizes a heads-to-regions mechanism, is innovative and effectively captures long-range attention while preserving local details.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's technical novelty is questioned, with some reviewers suggesting that the approach is a combination of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to the OpenLane-V2 dataset, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed approach across different datasets and real-world scenarios.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed approach over existing methods are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which are sometimes misleading or inaccurately presented.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed approach and the potential negative societal impacts of its deployment.
- The paper's focus on a single dataset raises concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method, especially given the variability in lane marking styles across different regions.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the potential applications of the proposed method in real-world scenarios and how it might be adapted to different driving conditions.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the technical novelty of your approach and how it differs from existing methods in the field?
- How does the proposed method perform when evaluated on other datasets, particularly those that differ significantly from the OpenLane-V2 dataset in terms of lane marking styles and driving conditions?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed approach over existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and resource utilization?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the lane markings are not clearly visible or where there are multiple lanes with overlapping markings?
- Could you provide more details on the training data and the data processing approach used in your experiments?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of deploying your proposed method in real-world scenarios, and how are these addressed in your research?
- How does the proposed method perform in different driving conditions, such as heavy rain or extreme temperatures, which could affect the visibility and accuracy of lane markings?
