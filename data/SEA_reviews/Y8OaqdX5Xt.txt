 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Planning with Theory of Mind (PToM), a novel algorithm for multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) in sequential social dilemmas (SSDs). PToM employs a hierarchical approach, integrating a high-level opponent modeling module that infers opponents' goals and behaviors and a low-level planning module that utilizes Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for action selection. The algorithm demonstrates superior performance in few-shot adaptation scenarios compared to existing methods, showing potential in complex multi-agent environments. However, concerns are raised regarding the clarity of the methodology, the complexity of the algorithm, and the need for more rigorous comparisons with other recent methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the experimental setup and results.
- The proposed method, Planning with Theory of Mind (PToM), is novel and shows good performance in self-play and few-shot adaptation scenarios.
- The integration of opponent modeling and Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for action selection is a significant contribution to the field of multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL).
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across three different social dilemma paradigms, demonstrating the versatility of the proposed method.
- The inclusion of an ablation study and additional results in the appendix offers a deeper understanding of the method's components and their impact.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the methodology, particularly in the opponent modeling module and the update process for beliefs about opponents' goals.
- The complexity of the algorithm, with multiple nested loops and multiple updates per timestep, raises concerns about its computational efficiency and scalability.
- The comparison with other methods is limited, and the paper does not sufficiently discuss or compare with other recent relevant works in the field of MARL.
- The paper's claims about the emergence of social intelligence and the effectiveness of the opponent modeling module are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- The goal-conditioned policy learning and the update process of beliefs about opponents' goals are not sufficiently detailed, which could hinder reproducibility and understanding of the method.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify how the opponent modeling module infers opponents' goals and how these goals influence the policy learning process?
- How does the paper ensure that the opponent modeling module is effective in scenarios where opponents' goals are not clearly defined or are dynamic?
- Could you provide more details on the update process for beliefs about opponents' goals and how these updates impact the overall performance of the algorithm?
- How does the algorithm handle scenarios where the number of opponents or the complexity of the environment changes?
- Could you discuss the computational complexity and scalability of the algorithm, especially in more complex environments?
- Why was the decision made to use Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for action selection, and how does this choice impact the overall performance and efficiency of the algorithm?
