 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PromptMSP, a novel method for multimer structure prediction using a pre-training and prompt tuning framework. This approach leverages protein-protein interaction (PPI) knowledge to predict the docking path of multimers, which is a significant challenge in the field. The method is designed to handle the complexity of multimer structures by pre-training on small-scale multimers and fine-tuning on larger, more complex structures. The paper presents a detailed experimental setup and demonstrates that PromptMSP outperforms existing methods in both accuracy and efficiency. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, the clarity of the presentation, and the depth of the experimental validation, particularly in terms of generalization to unseen multimers and the impact of different pre-training methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, PromptMSP, which effectively predicts the docking path of multimers, a challenging task in protein structure prediction.
- The experimental setup is detailed and thorough, with the use of a comprehensive dataset and a variety of baselines, including both traditional software methods and deep learning models.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental results.
- The methodology is innovative, utilizing a pre-training and prompt tuning framework that leverages protein-protein interaction (PPI) knowledge to generalize to multimers of different scales.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the gaps in C-PPI knowledge for multimers of different scales, which is crucial for understanding the challenges in multimer structure prediction.
- The results are impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in both accuracy and efficiency compared to existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the novelty of the method, particularly in relation to existing methods such as those mentioned in the literature.
- The clarity of the paper could be improved, especially in sections where the methodology and results are presented. There are several grammatical errors and unclear explanations that need addressing.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly validated, particularly in terms of the generalization of the method to unseen multimers and the impact of different pre-training methods.
- The method's reliance on pre-training on small-scale multimers raises concerns about its scalability and applicability to larger, more complex structures.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability and potential areas for improvement.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the novelty of the method, particularly in relation to existing methods mentioned in the literature?
- How does the method perform when applied to multimers with chain numbers not used in the pre-training phase?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the prompt in the method, particularly in terms of its effectiveness and the rationale behind its design?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, such as those mentioned in the literature?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the method's performance on unseen multimers and the impact of different pre-training methods on the results?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting, especially given the reliance on pre-training on small-scale multimers?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the method's limitations and potential areas for improvement?
