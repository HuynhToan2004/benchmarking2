 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Q-switch Mixture of Policies (QMP), a novel method for multi-task reinforcement learning (MTRL) that utilizes a Q-switch mechanism to evaluate the shareability of behaviors between tasks. This method selectively incorporates behaviors from other task policies during data collection, enhancing sample efficiency and performance across various MTRL tasks. The approach is demonstrated to be complementary to parameter sharing, showing superior performance in environments with conflicting behaviors. The methodology is supported by extensive experiments across diverse tasks, including manipulation, navigation, and locomotion, and is validated against multiple baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to multi-task reinforcement learning (MTRL) by focusing on sharing behaviors between tasks, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that primarily focus on parameter or data sharing.
- The proposed method, Q-switch Mixture of Policies (QMP), is well-articulated and supported by extensive experiments across diverse tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving sample efficiency and task performance.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive set of experiments and ablations, which are detailed in the appendix.
- The methodology is simple, intuitive, and complementary to parameter sharing, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the proposed method, including a novel approach to identifying shareable behaviors and incorporating them into the training process.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the method might not perform as well or where it could be improved.
- The paper's reliance on a fixed number of timesteps for behavior sharing could limit its adaptability to different environments and tasks.
- The paper's experiments are primarily conducted in discrete action spaces, which might not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness in continuous action spaces.
- There is a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a clearer picture of the method's performance relative to the current state of the art.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the convergence properties of the method and how it compares to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and training time.
- The paper's empirical evaluation could be strengthened by including more diverse environments and a broader range of tasks to better demonstrate the method's generalizability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition of "behavior" in the context of their method and how it differs from the traditional concept of "behavior policy"?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the convergence properties of the method, particularly how it performs in environments with varying degrees of task similarity?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the optimal behaviors between tasks are not necessarily the same, and what are the implications of this for the method's effectiveness?
- Can the authors discuss the potential for extending the method to continuous action spaces and how this might affect its performance and applicability?
- How does the method perform in environments with a large number of tasks, and what are the implications of this for the scalability and generalizability of the method?
