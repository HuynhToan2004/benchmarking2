 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Div-Se and IDiv-Se, two methods designed to enhance the reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) by diversifying prompts. These methods aim to improve performance on complex reasoning tasks by varying the input prompts, thereby increasing the diversity of thought processes. The experiments conducted on GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 models demonstrate that these methods can outperform existing techniques like Chain-of-Thought (CoT) and Self-Consistency (SC) on various benchmarks. The paper also discusses the trade-offs between inference costs and accuracy, showing that the proposed methods can achieve a better balance. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope in terms of the datasets used and the lack of comprehensive evaluation across different models and tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The proposed methods are novel, intuitive, and practical, offering a cost-effective alternative to traditional methods like CoT and SC.
- The experimental results show that the proposed methods can outperform CoT and SC, demonstrating their effectiveness.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of LLMs by focusing on the diversity of thought and its impact on reasoning abilities.
- The method is simple, flexible, and can be applied to various models, including open-source models, which enhances its applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation, particularly in terms of the datasets used and the variety of tasks tested.
- There is a noticeable absence of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed methods with other existing methods, such as those that use different aggregation strategies or that do not require multiple inference calls.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods over existing techniques, especially in scenarios where the gains are marginal.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of error propagation, which could significantly impact the reliability of the results.
- The paper's focus on specific models (GPT-3.5 and GPT-4) limits the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed methods with other existing methods, particularly those that use different aggregation strategies or do not require multiple inference calls?
- How does the error propagation affect the performance of the proposed methods, and what measures can be taken to mitigate this issue?
- In scenarios where the proposed methods do not significantly outperform existing techniques, what are the main reasons for this?
- Could you expand the evaluation to include a broader range of datasets and tasks to validate the generalizability of the findings?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements with other methods, especially in real-world applications?
