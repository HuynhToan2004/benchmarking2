 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the problem of class-conditional conformal prediction (CCP) in imbalanced data settings, focusing on reducing the size of prediction sets while maintaining class-conditional coverage guarantees. The proposed method, k-CCP, leverages the concept of top-k class labels to estimate class-specific non-conformity score thresholds, thereby reducing the prediction set size. The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, including experiments on benchmark datasets, to support the efficacy of the proposed method. The authors also address the limitations of marginal CCP and provide a theoretical analysis of the failure of marginal CCP.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of conformal prediction, focusing on the reduction of prediction set size in imbalanced data scenarios.
- The theoretical analysis provided is thorough, including proofs and detailed explanations of the proposed methodologies.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the methodologies and their implications.
- The empirical results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in real-world scenarios.
- The paper provides a novel approach to class-conditional conformal prediction by leveraging the concept of top-k class labels, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the choice of hyperparameters, such as the value of k, which is crucial for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The experiments are limited to small-scale datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on larger, more complex datasets.
- There is a lack of comparison with other methods that also aim to reduce prediction set size, such as those using balanced loss functions.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and applicability to larger, more complex datasets.
- The theoretical analysis and empirical results are not sufficiently integrated, which could lead to a disconnect between the theoretical claims and the practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential areas for future research.

**Questions:**
- How does the choice of hyperparameters, such as the value of k, affect the performance of the proposed method?
- Can the proposed method be applied to larger, more complex datasets, and what are the implications of scaling up the method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that also aim to reduce prediction set size, such as those using balanced loss functions?
- What are the implications of the theoretical results in practical scenarios, and how do they translate into real-world applications?
- How does the proposed method address the practical challenges of imbalanced data, particularly in terms of its applicability to real-world datasets?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and the choice of hyperparameters used in the experiments?
