 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MERT, a large-scale self-supervised learning model for music representation, which leverages a combination of acoustic and musical teacher models to provide pseudo labels for masked language modeling in music. The model, trained on a large-scale dataset of 160k hours of music recordings, achieves state-of-the-art performance on various music information retrieval (MIR) tasks. It employs a multi-task learning approach to balance acoustic and musical representation learning, with a focus on improving the stability of acoustic model pre-training. The authors also provide detailed ablation studies to analyze the effectiveness of different components of the model.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by integrating both acoustic and musical information for pre-training, which is a significant improvement over previous models that primarily focused on acoustic information.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on a wide range of MIR tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.
- The authors provide a comprehensive ablation study, which helps in understanding the effectiveness of different components of the model.
- The paper introduces a new dataset, which is a valuable contribution to the community, and the proposed model is scalable and computationally efficient, making it accessible for broader applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the description of the model architecture and the details of the training process.
- The paper does not adequately compare its results with existing models, such as JukeMIR, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a single dataset, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method across different datasets and music genres.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide enough details on the training data and the process of collecting it, which could be crucial for reproducibility and validation of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancy between the claimed model size (330M) and the actual size reported in the paper?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other datasets, especially those with different music genres or characteristics?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training data, such as its diversity and the process of collecting and cleaning it?
- How does the model handle different music genres or styles, and what are the implications of this for the generalizability of the findings?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential areas for future research?
