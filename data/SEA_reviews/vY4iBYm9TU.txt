 **Summary:**
The paper explores the effects of transfer learning on adversarial robustness, specifically focusing on the use of pre-trained models to enhance robustness in fine-tuning scenarios. It examines both supervised and self-supervised pre-training methods and their impact on empirical and certified robustness across various downstream tasks. The study is based on experiments conducted using ResNet-50 models trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned on smaller datasets, demonstrating that pre-training can improve robustness in fine-tuning phases. The paper also investigates the necessity of robust pre-training and fine-tuning, concluding that robust fine-tuning is crucial for maintaining adversarial robustness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the experiments and conclusions drawn.
- It addresses an important and relevant topic in the field of adversarial robustness, particularly the use of transfer learning to improve robustness.
- The experimental setup is comprehensive, covering a wide range of downstream tasks, and the results are clearly presented.
- The paper introduces a novel approach by combining transfer learning with adversarial robustness, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a thorough discussion on the importance of fine-tuning for adversarial robustness, which is crucial for maintaining robustness during model training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed approach and does not provide a clear explanation for the observed phenomena, such as the correlation between standard accuracy (SA) and robust accuracy (RA) in different datasets.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more robust baselines and different model architectures to enhance the generalizability of the results.
- There is a significant gap between the theoretical understanding and the empirical results, which could be bridged by more rigorous theoretical analysis or additional experiments.
- The paper's focus on empirical robustness and its comparison to certified robustness is not well-articulated, which could lead to confusion about the practical implications of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a broader range of datasets and model architectures to validate the generalizability of the findings across different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the correlation observed between SA and RA across different datasets, and provide a theoretical explanation for this phenomenon?
- How does the proposed approach compare to other robust pre-training methods, such as those based on auto-augmentation or other adversarial training techniques?
- Could the authors discuss the potential implications of using different model architectures, such as Vision Transformers (ViTs), in the context of adversarial robustness?
- How does the paper address the potential issues of overfitting or underfitting in the fine-tuning phase, especially considering the limited data available in many practical scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of adversarial attack methods and the robustness metrics used in the study, particularly in light of the potential limitations of these methods in real-world applications?
