 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multimodal Variational Autoencoder (VAE) model named CMVAE, which extends the traditional VAE by integrating a Gaussian mixture prior in the latent space to enforce clustering. This approach allows the model to learn data clusters across multiple modalities, enhancing its ability to generate high-quality images and perform weakly-supervised clustering. The model's architecture includes a mixture of experts for the shared latent space and separate encoders for each modality. The authors also propose a post-hoc procedure to automatically determine the number of clusters in the data, which is a significant improvement over existing methods. The paper presents a detailed experimental setup, comparing the proposed model against various baselines, and demonstrates its effectiveness in clustering and generation tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by integrating a Gaussian mixture prior in the latent space of a multimodal VAE, which effectively enforces clustering.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the proposed model and its components, including the use of a mixture of experts for the shared latent space and separate encoders for each modality.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in both clustering and generation tasks.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and visualizations that aid in understanding the proposed method and its results.
- The integration of diffusion models into the VAE framework is a significant contribution, enhancing the model's ability to generate high-quality images.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, particularly concerning the computational cost of training and inference.
- There is a need for more comprehensive ablation studies to understand the impact of different components of the model, such as the use of a Gaussian mixture prior and the post-hoc cluster selection procedure.
- The paper could benefit from a broader comparison with more recent and relevant baselines, including recent advancements in multimodal VAEs and diffusion models.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are somewhat limited, focusing primarily on a few datasets and not adequately demonstrating the generalizability of the proposed method across different modalities and datasets.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning its applicability to real-world scenarios with a large number of modalities.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, particularly concerning the computational cost of training and inference?
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent advancements in multimodal VAEs and diffusion models, such as those mentioned in the review?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly concerning the datasets used and the selection criteria for the number of clusters?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to datasets with a large number of modalities, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in such scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the Gaussian mixture prior and the post-hoc cluster selection procedure, possibly including additional ablation studies to better understand their impact on the model's performance?
