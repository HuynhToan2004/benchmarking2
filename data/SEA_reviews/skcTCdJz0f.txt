 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel probabilistic self-supervised learning framework named ProSMin, which leverages scoring rules to address the issue of dimensional collapse in self-supervised learning. The framework involves two neural networks: an online network and a target network, which collaborate through probabilistic knowledge distillation to learn diverse representations. The proposed method is evaluated across various downstream tasks such as in-distribution generalization, out-of-distribution detection, dataset corruption, low-shot learning, and transfer learning, demonstrating superior performance. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper, the lack of detailed theoretical justification, and the need for more comprehensive empirical evaluations, including comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and different architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of ideas and methodology.
- The proposed method is novel, leveraging scoring rules in self-supervised learning, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is supported by theoretical analysis, particularly the proof of the strict propriety of the proposed objective function, which adds credibility to the approach.
- The paper provides extensive empirical evaluations, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various tasks and datasets.
- The experiments are well-designed, with detailed ablation studies that help in understanding the contributions of different components of the method.
- The paper is significant as it addresses the issue of dimensional collapse in self-supervised learning, which is a critical challenge in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the introduction and methodology, which could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples.
- There is a need for more detailed theoretical justification, especially regarding the role of batch normalization in preventing representation collapse and the necessity of the predictor network.
- The empirical evaluations could be improved by including comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and different architectures to better establish the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the generalizability of the results across different architectures and datasets.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need correction to enhance the professional quality of the paper.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role of batch normalization in preventing representation collapse and why the predictor network is necessary?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational efficiency compared to other methods?
- Can you provide more details on how the proposed method handles the dimensional collapse issue, particularly in terms of the theoretical justification?
- How does the method perform when applied to other architectures, such as CNNs, and what are the implications for the results?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the method?
- In terms of the empirical evaluations, can you include comparisons with state-of-the-art methods to better establish the effectiveness of the proposed method?
