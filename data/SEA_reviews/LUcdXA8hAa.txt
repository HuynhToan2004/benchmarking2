 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the identifiability of relevance scores in unbiased learning to rank (ULTR) models, focusing on the examination hypothesis. It introduces the concept of an identifiability graph (IG) to determine whether the relevance can be recovered from click data, and proposes two methods to enhance IG connectivity: node intervention and node merging. These methods aim to address the issue of unidentifiability in ULTR models by ensuring the identifiability of relevance scores. The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical validation, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods. However, it has been noted that the paper lacks a clear discussion on the implications of its findings and the practical applicability of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear structure that effectively guides the reader through the content.
- The research addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of unbiased learning to rank (ULTR), which is crucial for improving the performance of ranking models.
- The paper introduces a novel approach by defining identifiability in terms of graph connectivity, which is a novel perspective in the context of ULTR.
- The proposed methods, such as node intervention and node merging, are innovative and practical, providing a theoretical guarantee and empirical validation of their effectiveness.
- The paper is well-organized, with a comprehensive literature review and detailed proofs of theorems, which contribute to the paper's credibility and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the implications of its findings and the practical applicability of the proposed methods.
- The assumption of a connected graph in the examination hypothesis is not clearly justified, and the paper does not adequately address the limitations of this assumption.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its proposed methods with existing literature, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed methods.
- The paper's experimental results are not convincing, particularly the results of node merging, which do not show a significant improvement over the baseline in some cases.
- The paper's theoretical analysis and empirical results do not fully support the effectiveness of node merging, which is a critical component of the proposed methodology.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by better integrating the proofs and discussions into the main text, and by providing clearer explanations of the assumptions and methodologies used.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions and implications of the examination hypothesis, particularly the assumption of a connected graph?
- How does the paper address the issue of practical applicability of the proposed methods in real-world scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing literature to validate the effectiveness of their proposed methods?
- How do the authors ensure the scalability and applicability of their methods to larger and more complex datasets?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical and empirical results supporting the effectiveness of node merging, particularly in scenarios where it does not show a significant improvement over the baseline?
