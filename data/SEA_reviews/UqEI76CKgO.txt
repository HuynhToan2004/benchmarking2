 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Amphibian, a gradient-based meta-learner designed for online continual learning, which updates model parameters based on current batch data to achieve a balance between fast learning and continual learning. Amphibian learns a diagonal scale matrix in each layer, accumulating the history of gradient alignments among data samples, and updates the model online in the directions with positive cumulative gradient alignments. The method has been evaluated on various continual learning benchmarks, showing state-of-the-art accuracy and fast learning with less data. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the method, the lack of comprehensive experimental validation, and the absence of comparisons with some relevant baseline methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, Amphibian, is novel and well-motivated, offering a simple yet effective approach to online continual learning.
- The method is evaluated on various continual learning benchmarks, demonstrating state-of-the-art accuracy and fast learning with less data.
- The paper provides a detailed derivation of the proposed method, enhancing its credibility and reproducibility.
- The use of visualization results to support the effectiveness of the method is a valuable addition to the paper.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the method achieves fast learning with less data, which is a significant claim.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it resembles existing works like SGP and NCL, with similar meta-objectives and gradient scaling.
- The experimental results are not convincing, particularly in terms of the statistical significance of the results and the lack of comparisons with relevant baselines.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, which is a critical aspect of continual learning.
- The methodology section is not clearly explained, particularly the role and impact of the scale matrix in the model's performance.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which is essential for validating the claims of superior performance.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the method achieves fast learning with less data and provide empirical evidence to support this claim?
- How does the proposed method compare to other meta-learning methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and learning outcomes?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the continual learning benchmarks used and the data splits?
- How does the method perform when evaluated on different datasets or in different continual learning scenarios?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the experimental results, including statistical significance tests and comparisons with relevant baselines?
- How does the scale matrix affect the performance of the model, and can the authors provide more insights into its role in the model's operation?
