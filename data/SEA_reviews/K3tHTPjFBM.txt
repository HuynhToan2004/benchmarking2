 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multi-task learning framework for protein structure-based tasks using a heterogeneous multichannel equivariant network (HeMeNet). The framework is designed to handle multiple tasks such as protein-ligand binding affinity, protein-protein interaction, and enzyme commission number prediction, leveraging a new dataset called PROMPT. HeMeNet is an E(3)-equivariant network that utilizes a task-aware readout mechanism to predict various properties from 3D protein structures. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated through comprehensive experiments on the PROMPT dataset, showcasing improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of presentation, and the adequacy of the ablation studies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel multi-task learning framework for protein structure-based tasks, which is the first of its kind.
- The framework, HeMeNet, is designed to handle multiple tasks jointly, which is a significant advancement in protein modeling.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of HeMeNet on the PROMPT dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.
- The proposed dataset, PROMPT, is a valuable contribution to the community, offering a new benchmark for protein multi-task learning.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments are comprehensive, including ablation studies that provide insights into the model's components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited, as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the PROMPT dataset and the methodology used.
- There is a noticeable absence of ablation studies on key components such as the task-aware readout mechanism and the multi-channel equivariant message passing.
- The performance improvements of HeMeNet over existing methods are not substantial, and the paper does not adequately address the issue of sparse data in protein datasets.
- The paper's focus on protein structure-based tasks might limit its applicability to other types of biological data.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the construction of the PROMPT dataset, especially how the labels for different tasks are assigned?
- How does HeMeNet handle the sparsity of data in protein datasets, and what measures are in place to mitigate this issue?
- Could the authors provide more details on the task-aware readout mechanism and the multi-channel equivariant message passing, possibly through additional ablation studies?
- How does HeMeNet compare to other multi-task learning methods for protein structure-based tasks, and what are the specific advantages of using HeMeNet?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending HeMeNet to other types of biological data beyond protein structures?
