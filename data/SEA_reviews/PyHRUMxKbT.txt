 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel feed-forward network architecture, InfoNet, designed to estimate mutual information (MI) and maximal correlation coefficients (MCC) between random variables. It leverages a dual formulation of MI and MCC, employing a lookup table to achieve real-time computations. The network is trained on Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and tested on various distributions, showing significant improvements in computational efficiency and accuracy compared to existing methods. The proposed method is particularly beneficial for real-time applications, such as in robotics, where the efficiency of mutual information estimation is crucial. The paper also discusses the limitations and potential extensions of the method, including its applicability to higher-dimensional data and real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel feed-forward network architecture, InfoNet, for estimating mutual information and maximal correlation coefficients, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is evaluated on various families of distributions and real-world data, demonstrating its effectiveness and generalization capabilities.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The approach is efficient and fast, which is crucial for real-time applications.
- The use of a lookup table for MI estimation is a novel approach that has not been explored extensively in previous works.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, comparing it to existing methods and showing improvements in efficiency and accuracy.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- There is a concern about the scalability of the method to higher-dimensional data and real-world scenarios, which are not thoroughly addressed.
- The method's dependence on the quality of the training data is not adequately discussed, which could impact the reliability of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process, such as the number of training steps and the choice of hyperparameters, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to specific types of data (Gaussian mixture models), which may not fully demonstrate its capabilities in more complex or real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method in terms of its applicability to different types of data and its generalization to higher-dimensional or more complex scenarios.
