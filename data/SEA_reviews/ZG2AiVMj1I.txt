 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Imprecise Bayesian Continual Learning (IBCL), a novel approach to continual learning that enables the generation of models tailored to user preferences without requiring additional training. IBCL employs a convex hull of model parameter distributions to maintain a knowledge base, which is updated with each new task. This method ensures a sublinear buffer growth and probabilistic Pareto-optimality, allowing for the selection of models that balance performance across different tasks. The paper also discusses the limitations of existing methods, such as the inability to guarantee Pareto-optimality and the high computational cost associated with training models for each user preference. Experiments demonstrate the method's efficiency and effectiveness, showing improvements over baseline methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Imprecise Bayesian Continual Learning (IBCL), which enables the generation of models tailored to user preferences without additional training, maintaining a constant training overhead per task.
- The method guarantees Pareto-optimality, which is a significant advantage over existing methods that do not ensure this property.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the efficiency and effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over baseline methods.
- The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the method, which are crucial for its validation and acceptance in the academic community.
- The method's novelty lies in its ability to generate models for different user preferences without requiring additional training, which is a significant departure from existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods that allow for user preferences, such as those discussed in Mahapatra & Rajan (2020, 2021).
- The paper does not discuss the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the method, which could be crucial for practical implementation.
- There is a lack of clarity in the paper regarding the method's assumptions, such as the assumption of task similarity, which is critical for the method's applicability.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, particularly concerning the scalability and practicality of the assumptions made.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational complexity of the method, which is crucial for understanding its scalability and efficiency.
- The paper's claims about the method's efficiency and effectiveness are not sufficiently supported by experimental evidence, particularly in terms of the method's ability to maintain sublinear buffer growth and its impact on computational resources.

**Questions:**

- How does the method perform when there are a large number of tasks, and what are the computational and memory requirements in such scenarios?
- How does the method ensure that the generated models are not only Pareto-optimal but also perform well in practical applications?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the hyperparameters used and the computational resources required?
- How does the method compare to other methods that allow for user preferences in continual learning, such as those discussed in Mahapatra & Rajan (2020, 2021)?
- Could you elaborate on the method's scalability and its practical applicability in real-world scenarios?
