 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PriViT, a novel approach for training Vision Transformers (ViTs) to enhance their compatibility with secure multi-party computation (MPC) protocols. PriViT utilizes a gradient-based algorithm to selectively replace nonlinearities in ViTs with Taylorized versions, aiming to reduce latency while maintaining accuracy. This method is designed to make ViTs more efficient for private inference, addressing the computational challenges posed by non-polynomial operations in MPC. The paper presents experiments demonstrating PriViT's effectiveness in reducing latency and improving accuracy on various image classification tasks. However, concerns were raised about the incremental nature of the contributions, the clarity of the writing, and the limited scope of the experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for training Vision Transformers (ViTs) that selectively Taylorizes nonlinearities in ViTs, making them more compatible with secure multi-party computation (MPC) protocols.
- The proposed method is conceptually simple, easy to implement, and achieves improved performance over existing approaches for designing MPC-friendly transformer architectures.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in reducing latency while maintaining accuracy on various image classification tasks.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the proposed method and its implementation.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the impact of different non-linearities on the performance of ViTs, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of these models under different conditions.
- The paper presents a new dataset (TinyImageNet) that is specifically designed for training ViTs, which could be useful for further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing methods, particularly the SNL method, which also uses gradient descent for linearizing ReLUs and softmaxes.
- The writing of the paper could be improved to make it clearer and more accessible to readers.
- The experiments are limited in scope, focusing only on the CIFAR dataset, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the descriptions of the Delphi protocol and the training process of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the hardware used for the experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include potential issues with model robustness or scalability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and the SNL method, particularly in terms of the training process and the use of gradient descent?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets such as ImageNet, and what are the expected latency improvements?
- Can the authors provide more details on the hardware used for the experiments, including the specifications of the GPUs and the operating system?
- How does the proposed method compare to other MPC-friendly models, such as those based on BERT or other architectures?
- Could the authors explain the rationale behind the specific choices made in the training process, such as the use of knowledge distillation and the binarization of certain variables?
- How does the proposed method perform under different MPC protocols, and what are the implications for the latency and accuracy trade-offs?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process, particularly the use of different loss functions and the impact of these choices on the model's performance?
