 **Summary:**
The paper introduces VarNMF, a probabilistic extension of Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF), which models sources as distributions to account for variations in source signals between samples. VarNMF employs an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to optimize the likelihood of the observed data given the model parameters. The method is demonstrated to be effective in handling real-world datasets, particularly in genomic applications such as cell-free ChIP-seq, where it can extract cancer-associated source distributions from mixed samples. However, the paper is criticized for its limited evaluation scope, lack of comparison with existing methods, and insufficient explanation of the EM algorithm's convergence and computational complexity.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its implementation.
- The introduction of a probabilistic framework for NMF is innovative and addresses a significant gap in the literature, particularly in modeling source distributions.
- The method is demonstrated to be effective in real-world datasets, showing practical utility in genomic applications.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the method's performance on real-world data, including detailed explanations of the results and their implications.
- The authors have made the code and data publicly available, enhancing the reproducibility and transparency of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that also model source distributions, which limits the understanding of VarNMF's advantages and disadvantages.
- The evaluation scope is limited, focusing only on one real-world dataset, which may not generalize well to other datasets or applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and convergence of the EM algorithm, which is crucial for understanding the scalability and reliability of the method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential overfitting due to the increased number of parameters in the model, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the method, including its assumptions and potential biases.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison with other methods that model source distributions, discussing the advantages and disadvantages of VarNMF in this context?
- How does the method perform on other datasets or applications beyond genomic data? Could the authors provide examples or additional evaluations to demonstrate the generalizability of the method?
- Could the authors discuss the computational complexity of the EM algorithm and provide insights into how it scales with the size of the dataset or the number of sources?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting due to the increased number of parameters? Are there strategies or modifications that could be employed to mitigate this risk?
- Can the authors provide more details on the convergence of the EM algorithm and the criteria used to determine convergence?
- In the real data experiments, how does the method perform when the number of sources is varied? Are there specific values of K that are more effective, and what are the implications of these choices?
