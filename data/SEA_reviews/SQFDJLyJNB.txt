 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for continual category discovery (CCD) using a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) as a prompting method. This approach, termed the Gaussian Mixture Prompt Module (GMP), dynamically updates a prompt pool to guide the embedding of data representations and prevent catastrophic forgetting. The GMP is designed to estimate the number of categories in the unlabelled stream without prior knowledge, which is a significant advantage over existing methods. The method is evaluated on several datasets and shows promising results, although there are concerns about the novelty of the method and the depth of the experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is novel and simple, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a clear explanation of the method.
- The method introduces a new prompt learning technique for continual category discovery (CCD), which is innovative and could be a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a detailed explanation of the method and its components, including the Gaussian Mixture Prompt Module (GMP) and the optimization objectives for different learning stages.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing significant improvements over existing methods in various datasets.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the proposed method, including ablation studies that show the effectiveness of different components of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the method handles the estimation of the number of categories in the unlabelled stream, which is a crucial aspect of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it appears to be a combination of existing methods like GMM and prompt learning, without sufficient differentiation from these methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, which is a significant challenge in continual learning.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other methods in terms of computational cost and efficiency, which is crucial for evaluating the practical applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and the challenges that may arise in its practical implementation.
- The paper does not discuss the potential impact of the method on other tasks, such as fine-tuning on downstream tasks, which is a common practice in continual learning.

**Questions:**
- How does the method handle the estimation of the number of categories in the unlabelled stream? What are the assumptions made about the data distribution, and how does the method ensure that it accurately estimates the number of categories?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost and efficiency of the proposed method compared to other methods?
- How does the method compare in terms of performance and efficiency with other methods, particularly in scenarios where the number of categories is unknown?
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with other methods in terms of its ability to handle different types of data, such as image and text data?
- How does the method handle the issue of catastrophic forgetting, and what measures are in place to prevent it?
- What are the potential limitations of the method, and how do these impact its practical applicability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and the datasets used, particularly in terms of the data splits and the evaluation metrics used?
