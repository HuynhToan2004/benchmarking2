 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for backdooring Large Language Models (LLMs) by directly modifying a subset of the model's parameters, a departure from traditional methods that rely on extensive tuning data. This approach, termed BadEdit, is designed to be practical, efficient, and robust, even after subsequent fine-tuning or instruction tuning. The method is evaluated against existing backdoor injection techniques, showing superior performance in terms of practicality, efficiency, and robustness. The paper also discusses the limitations of the method and its potential impacts on the security of LLMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and the experimental results are solid, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The idea of backdooring LLMs by directly editing the model's parameters is innovative and potentially more efficient than existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the content.
- The method is evaluated against existing backdoor injection techniques, showing superior performance in terms of practicality, efficiency, and robustness.
- The paper is the first to formulate backdoor injection as a lightweight knowledge editing problem, which is a novel approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the ethical implications of backdooring LLMs, which is a significant concern given the potential misuse of such capabilities.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly demonstrated to be robust against various attacks and defenses, which is a critical aspect of backdoor research.
- The paper's claims of efficiency and robustness are not adequately supported by experimental evidence, especially in scenarios where the attacker has access to the entire training data.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a small number of models and datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method and how these limitations affect the practical application of the method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of efficiency and robustness to existing methods, especially when the attacker has access to the entire training data?
- Can the authors provide more details on the ethical implications of backdooring LLMs and how these implications are addressed in the paper?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the attacker has limited access to the training data, and what are the implications of this on the method's effectiveness?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the model's performance is evaluated in different scenarios, and what metrics are used to assess the method's effectiveness?
