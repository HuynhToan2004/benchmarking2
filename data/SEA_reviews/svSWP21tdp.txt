 **Summary:**
The paper explores model-induced distribution shifts (MIDS) in machine learning models, focusing on how these shifts impact fairness and performance across different generations of models. It introduces a novel taxonomy for MIDS, highlighting their impacts on underrepresented groups. The paper also proposes a methodology for algorithmic reparation (AR) to mitigate these issues, using synthetic data to address historical biases. The empirical results demonstrate that MIDS can lead to biased models over time, and AR can help mitigate these biases. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in defining MIDS and the limited scope of its empirical evaluations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem of fairness in machine learning models, particularly focusing on the impact of model-induced distribution shifts (MIDS) on fairness.
- It introduces a novel taxonomy for MIDS, which helps in understanding the complex interactions between models and data distributions.
- The empirical results are robust and demonstrate the negative effects of MIDS on model performance and fairness, particularly for underrepresented groups.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and visualizations that aid in understanding the complex concepts discussed.
- The proposed method of algorithmic reparation (AR) is innovative and shows promise in mitigating the negative impacts of MIDS.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining MIDS and its relationship with other concepts like model collapse and performative prediction.
- The empirical evaluations are limited in scope, focusing primarily on synthetic data and not adequately addressing the impact of MIDS on real-world datasets.
- The paper's discussion on the limitations of the proposed methodology is insufficient, and there is a need for more detailed analysis of the costs and trade-offs associated with AR.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough literature review and a clearer distinction from existing works, particularly those that discuss similar issues in fairness and machine learning.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly robust, with concerns about the generalizability of the findings to real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and scope of MIDS, particularly in relation to other concepts like model collapse and performative prediction?
- How does the proposed method of AR compare to other existing methods for mitigating biases in machine learning models?
- In the experimental setup, how were the oracle models (A_L and A_S) trained, and what impact does this have on the results?
- How do the authors address the concerns about the generalizability of their findings to real-world datasets, and what steps are being taken to ensure the robustness of the proposed methodology?
- Could the authors provide more detailed analysis on the costs and trade-offs associated with the use of AR, particularly in terms of computational resources and the potential for overfitting?
