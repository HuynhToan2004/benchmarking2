 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Self-Motivated Imitation Learning (SMILE), a novel method for learning policies from noisy demonstrations by leveraging diffusion models. SMILE employs a policy-wise diffusion framework to model the degradation of expertise in policies, using a noise approximator to guide the generation of actions. This approach allows for the automatic identification and filtering of noisy demonstrations, enhancing the robustness of the learned policy. The method is evaluated against several baselines on MuJoCo tasks, showing promising results in handling noisy demonstrations. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of presentation, and the limited scope of experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The methodology is well-motivated and the proposed framework, SMILE, is innovative, leveraging diffusion models for imitation learning, which is a novel approach.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the proposed method, which strengthens its credibility and applicability.
- The experimental results show that SMILE can handle noisy demonstrations effectively, outperforming baseline methods in certain scenarios.
- The paper addresses an important problem in imitation learning, namely the handling of noisy demonstrations, which is a significant challenge in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods such as ILEED and VILD, which also address the issue of noisy demonstrations.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to MuJoCo tasks, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential negative societal impacts and ethical considerations.
- The clarity of presentation could be improved, particularly in sections where the methodology and experimental setup are described.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method to larger or more complex datasets, which is a significant limitation for its practical applicability.
- There are concerns about the novelty of the approach, as it appears to be a combination of existing methods without sufficient differentiation or innovation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with ILEED and VILD, highlighting the key differences and advantages of SMILE over these methods?
- How does the proposed method perform on more complex tasks beyond MuJoCo, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in scaling up the method?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential negative societal impacts and ethical considerations?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of noise in demonstrations, particularly in scenarios where the noise level is high or the demonstrations are highly varied?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the noise approximator in the filtering process, and how it contributes to the overall effectiveness of the method?
- In terms of practical implementation, how does the method perform in real-world scenarios where the quality and consistency of demonstrations may vary significantly?
