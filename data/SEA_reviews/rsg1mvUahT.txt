 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel algorithm, FedWaD, designed to compute the Wasserstein distance between two probability distributions in a federated learning (FL) environment. This algorithm leverages the triangle inequality and the properties of Wasserstein geodesics to iteratively approximate the Wasserstein distance without direct access to the data on the clients. The paper also explores the application of FedWaD in various scenarios such as computing Wasserstein coresets and enhancing federated learning algorithms. Theoretical analysis and empirical results are provided to support the effectiveness and convergence of the proposed method. However, the paper has been critiqued for its reliance on the triangle inequality, which may not be the most efficient approach for approximating the Wasserstein distance, and for its limited experimental validation on larger datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel algorithm, FedWaD, for computing the Wasserstein distance between two probability distributions in a federated learning (FL) environment, which is an interesting and relevant problem.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the convergence of the proposed algorithm, which enhances the credibility of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and intuitive descriptions of the proposed algorithm and its applications.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm in various scenarios, including Wasserstein coresets and federated learning, showing promising results.
- The paper introduces a novel application of the proposed algorithm to federated learning, which has the potential to significantly impact the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies heavily on the triangle inequality to approximate the Wasserstein distance, which may not be the most efficient approach as it requires multiple iterations to converge.
- The experiments are conducted primarily on small datasets such as MNIST and CIFAR-10, which may not adequately demonstrate the scalability or effectiveness of the proposed algorithm on larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning its applicability to different types of data and its scalability to larger datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other existing methods for computing the Wasserstein distance in a federated learning setting, which could have strengthened the argument for the superiority of the proposed method.
- There are some unclear notations and definitions in the paper, which could confuse readers and detract from the clarity of the presentation.
