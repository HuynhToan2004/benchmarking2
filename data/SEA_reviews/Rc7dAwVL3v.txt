 **Summary:**
The paper introduces NaturalSpeech 2, a text-to-speech (TTS) system that leverages a neural audio codec with residual vector quantizers to generate quantized latent vectors and uses a diffusion model to generate these latent vectors conditioned on text input. This system aims to address challenges in previous large-scale TTS systems by using a diffusion model to enhance zero-shot capability, ensuring diverse speech synthesis. The paper claims that NaturalSpeech 2 outperforms previous TTS systems in terms of prosody/timbre similarity, robustness, and voice quality in a zero-shot setting. It also introduces a speech prompting mechanism to facilitate in-context learning in the diffusion model and the duration/pitch predictor. The system was scaled to a large dataset and evaluated for its voice quality on unseen speakers.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed system is designed to be simple yet effective, leveraging a neural audio codec with residual vector quantizers to generate quantized latent vectors and using a diffusion model to generate these latent vectors conditioned on text input.
- The paper introduces a speech prompting mechanism to facilitate in-context learning in the diffusion model and the duration/pitch predictor, which is a novel approach to zero-shot capability.
- The system was scaled to a large dataset and evaluated for its voice quality on unseen speakers, demonstrating its effectiveness in real-world applications.
- The paper includes a comprehensive ablation study and provides a detailed comparison with previous works, which helps in understanding the contributions and improvements of the proposed system.
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem of text-to-speech synthesis, which is of significant interest and relevance to the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the neural audio codec and the diffusion model, which are crucial components of the proposed system. More details on the architecture and training of these models are needed.
- There is a lack of clarity on the specific role and effectiveness of the speech prompting mechanism in the diffusion model and the duration/pitch predictor. More details and possibly an ablation study are needed to clarify its impact.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the training and inference complexity of the proposed system, which is crucial for understanding its practical deployment.
- The paper does not include a subjective evaluation of the generated audio quality, which is essential for assessing the system's effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not compare the proposed system with the latest TTS models, which limits the assessment of its performance against the state-of-the-art.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed system, which is crucial for understanding its broader implications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the neural audio codec and the diffusion model, including their architecture and training procedures?
- How does the speech prompting mechanism specifically contribute to the effectiveness of the diffusion model and the duration/pitch predictor? Could an ablation study be conducted to clarify its impact?
- What are the specific training and inference complexities of the proposed system, and how do these compare to other TTS systems?
- How does the proposed system handle zero-shot singing synthesis, and what are the specific challenges and solutions in this domain?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with the latest TTS models to assess the performance of their system against the state-of-the-art?
- How does the proposed system ensure that the generated speech maintains the speaker's identity, and what measures are in place to prevent misuse of the model?
