 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to anomaly detection in tabular data using a masked cell modeling framework (MCM). This method extends masked modeling techniques from image and language domains to tabular data, employing a learnable masking strategy and diversity loss to capture intrinsic correlations within the data. The proposed MCM is evaluated against various datasets and reportedly outperforms existing methods, demonstrating its effectiveness in anomaly detection. The paper also discusses the interpretability of the model, highlighting the correlations between features and the anomaly score of each individual feature. However, concerns are raised regarding the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the methodology, and the robustness of the results, particularly in the context of different types of anomalies and the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to anomaly detection in tabular data using masked cell modeling, which extends existing successes in masked image and language modeling to this domain.
- The methodology is well-explained, with a clear description of the proposed MCM framework and its components, including the mask generator, ensemble learning, and diversity loss.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with a comprehensive evaluation across 20 datasets and thorough ablation studies.
- The paper provides a detailed discussion on the interpretability of the model, which is crucial for understanding the anomaly detection process and the correlations between features.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in anomaly detection, and the paper includes an ablation study to validate the contributions of different components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as the idea of masking data for anomaly detection is not new, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its method from existing approaches.
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the description of the masking strategy and the methodology for generating masks.
- The robustness of the method to different types of anomalies is not adequately addressed, with concerns about its performance on local and global anomalies compared to other methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods specifically designed for anomaly detection in tabular data, which could affect the perceived effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with some results showing significant discrepancies between the proposed method and existing methods, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the masking strategy is implemented, particularly how the masks are generated and what criteria are used for their selection?
- How does the proposed method perform on different types of anomalies, especially local and global anomalies, compared to other methods?
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies in the results presented in the paper, especially the significant differences in performance between the proposed method and existing methods on certain datasets?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods specifically designed for anomaly detection in tabular data?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect the generalizability and applicability of the findings?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of anomalies, and what are the implications of this for the effectiveness of the method in real-world scenarios?
