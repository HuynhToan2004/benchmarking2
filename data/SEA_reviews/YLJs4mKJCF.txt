 **Summary:**
The paper explores the vulnerability of fair representation learning (FRL) methods to data poisoning attacks, proposing a novel attack framework that maximizes mutual information between representations and sensitive features. This attack method is designed to manipulate the training data by injecting poisoned samples, which can lead to unfair representations that contain as much demographic information as possible. The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed attack, showing that it outperforms existing methods in terms of poisoning samples required. The study also discusses the implications of these attacks on fairness and proposes a theoretical analysis on the minimal number of poisoning samples needed for the attack to succeed.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel attack method for fair representation learning (FRL), which is the first of its kind and is well-motivated by the need to understand the vulnerability of FRL methods to data poisoning attacks.
- The proposed attack method is principled and well-explained, with a clear connection to existing fairness notions such as demographic parity.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis on the minimal number of poisoning samples required for the attack to succeed, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed attack method, showing that it outperforms existing methods in terms of poisoning samples required.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a good balance of theoretical analysis and empirical results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions, particularly in Theorem 3.4, are considered too strong and not well-supported, which may limit the practical applicability of the results.
- The attack method is designed to maximize mutual information between representations and sensitive features, but it is unclear how this relates to the fairness goal of FRL, which is often defined as minimizing the mutual information between sensitive features and representations.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed attack method, which could be significant given the sensitive nature of the data involved.
- There are some presentation issues, such as unclear figures and a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, which could make it difficult for readers to fully understand the methodology and results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of fairness notions and the potential for the attack to be detected.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the connection between the attack method and the fairness goal of FRL, particularly in terms of how maximizing mutual information between representations and sensitive features relates to the fairness goal?
- How does the proposed attack method compare in terms of effectiveness and practicality to other attack methods that might not be as constrained by the assumptions used in Theorem 3.4?
- Could the authors provide more details on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed attack method and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
- In terms of the experimental setup, could the authors clarify how the BCE loss is computed and whether it is a valid measure of fairness in the context of the attack?
- How does the proposed attack method perform when applied to different types of fairness notions, and what are the implications of this for the practical applicability of the attack?
