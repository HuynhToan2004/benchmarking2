 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel probabilistic framework for image editing using stochastic differential equations (SDEs) and ordinary differential equations (ODEs). The authors demonstrate that SDEs can outperform ODEs in terms of KL divergence between marginal distributions, and introduce a new method called SDE-Drag for point-based content dragging. The paper also includes a comprehensive evaluation of SDE-Drag against various baselines and presents a new benchmark for evaluating image editing methods. Theoretical analyses and empirical results support the superiority of SDE-Drag over traditional methods, although concerns about the clarity of theoretical explanations and the practical implications of the method's assumptions persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive evaluation that covers various tasks such as inpainting, image-to-image translation, and dragging.
- The proposed SDE-Drag method is simple and effective, with strong results demonstrated through a user study on a new benchmark dataset.
- The paper provides a novel theoretical analysis on the convergence rate of SDEs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear figures and a detailed explanation of the proposed method, which makes it accessible and reproducible.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of SDE-Drag, including comparisons with other methods and a detailed analysis of the method's performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of practical implementation and computational efficiency.
- The theoretical analysis and proofs in the paper are not always clear or intuitive, and some assumptions and notations are not well-explained or justified.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the number of parameters and computational resources used.
- There are some inconsistencies and potential errors in the presentation of results and methodology, such as missing references and unclear figures.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly concerning the choice of parameters and the assumptions about the data distribution.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly regarding the choice of parameters and the implications for the data distribution?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and parameter usage with other state-of-the-art methods?
- Could you provide more details on the practical implementation of the method, including any potential limitations or challenges in real-world applications?
- In the proof of Theorem 3.1, could you explain why the inequality holds and whether it is a strict inequality?
- How do the authors address the potential issues of model approximation error and discretization error in their theoretical analysis?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of parameters and their impact on the performance of the method, particularly in scenarios where the target point is far from the source point?
