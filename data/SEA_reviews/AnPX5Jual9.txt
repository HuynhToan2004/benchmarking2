 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for modeling feature interactions in machine learning tasks using a Rotative Factorization Machine (RFM). This model represents each feature as a polar angle in the complex plane and models feature interactions as rotations, which allows for the learning of arbitrarily large order interactions. The RFM incorporates a self-attentive rotation function to adaptively learn the interaction orders from different contexts, and a modulus amplification network to learn the modulus of the complex features, enhancing the representation. The method is evaluated on several datasets and shows promising results, outperforming several baselines. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the method's presentation, the novelty of the approach, and the empirical evaluation, suggesting that the paper could benefit from further refinement and validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly explained.
- The proposed method is innovative, using a novel approach to feature interaction learning by representing each feature as a polar angle in the complex plane.
- The paper includes thorough experiments and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method is shown to outperform several baselines in terms of prediction performance.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the proposed method, making it accessible and understandable.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the content.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with related works, particularly in the area of feature interaction learning.
- The presentation of the method could be improved, with some equations and notations being confusing or incorrect.
- The empirical evaluation of the method is not convincing, with limited datasets used and a lack of detailed experimental results.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in relation to existing methods.
- There are some issues with the clarity and organization of the paper, including missing references and unclear explanations of certain components of the method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of datasets and the potential for overfitting due to the complexity of the model.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with related works, particularly in the area of feature interaction learning?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability and computational complexity, especially in comparison to existing methods?
- Can the authors clarify the definitions and notations used in the paper, particularly in equations and figures that are currently confusing?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets, and what are the implications for its scalability and applicability in different domains?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the training time and the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting, and what measures have been taken to prevent this?
