 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Seesaw, a novel neural architecture search method designed for privacy-preserving machine learning (PPML) tasks, specifically focusing on image classification. Seesaw leverages linear computations and nonlinear result reuse to compensate for accuracy loss caused by nonlinear reduction. The method employs a one-shot NAS technique to search for optimized neural network architectures that balance accuracy and execution latency. The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation on datasets like CIFAR100 and ImageNet, showing that Seesaw achieves significant improvements in accuracy and latency reduction compared to state-of-the-art methods. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and the need for a broader range of experiments to validate its claims more robustly.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem in the field of privacy-preserving machine learning (PPML), focusing on reducing latency in nonlinear computations.
- The proposed method, Seesaw, is innovative, combining pruning and NAS techniques to efficiently handle the larger design space, and it is supported by comprehensive evaluations and ablation studies.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear structure and logical flow, making it accessible to readers.
- Seesaw's approach of using ADD operators instead of CONCAT and incorporating learnable weight parameters for linear operators is novel and potentially more effective in reducing latency.
- The paper presents a thorough evaluation, including a comparison with state-of-the-art methods and a detailed analysis of the impact of different components on accuracy and latency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is somewhat limited as it primarily focuses on the DELPHI framework and does not explore the applicability of Seesaw across other PPML frameworks or different types of models like transformers.
- The methodology section lacks detailed explanations on the NAS techniques used, particularly how the branch weights are updated and how the NAS modules are sampled.
- The paper could benefit from a broader range of experiments, including evaluations on other datasets like MNIST and different PPML frameworks to validate the effectiveness of Seesaw more robustly.
- There are some inconsistencies and unclear explanations in the paper, such as the discrepancies in performance between Seesaw and other methods, and the rationale behind certain design choices.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its potential impact on model size and performance on different hardware platforms.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies in performance between Seesaw and other methods, particularly in terms of the number of ReLU operators and the accuracy achieved?
- How does Seesaw compare in terms of performance and efficiency when applied to other PPML frameworks or different types of models like transformers?
- Could the authors provide more details on the NAS techniques used, including how the branch weights are updated and how the NAS modules are sampled?
- How does Seesaw handle the trade-off between accuracy and latency, especially when the ReLU budget is tight?
- What are the specific benefits of using ADD operators over CONCAT, and how does this affect the overall performance and efficiency of the model?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the design choices made in Seesaw, particularly the decision to use more linear computations and nonlinear result reuse?
