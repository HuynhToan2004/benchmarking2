 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DISTA, a novel Test Time Adaptation (TTA) method that leverages auxiliary tasks to accelerate adaptation to distribution shifts. The method involves distilling the predictions of a pretrained model on clean data during adaptation, which is shown to improve performance by up to 6% in federated settings. The authors also explore the integration of federated learning with TTA, enhancing adaptation across different models and data distributions. The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations and detailed experimental results, although it lacks in novelty and comprehensive comparative analysis with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its implementation.
- The proposed method, DISTA, is simple yet effective, as demonstrated by empirical results on various benchmarks.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's performance under different settings, including episodic, continual, and federated evaluations.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear motivation for the research and a comprehensive discussion of related works.
- The experiments are thorough, covering different settings and architectures, and the results show that DISTA outperforms previous methods in terms of performance and efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The method's novelty is limited as it builds upon existing works like EATA and distillation techniques, which might not be sufficiently distinguished.
- The paper does not provide sufficient comparative analysis with other state-of-the-art methods, such as those using labeled data or different data selection schemes.
- The computational cost of DISTA is higher than that of other methods, which might limit its practical application.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the method's performance in scenarios with more severe distribution shifts or in settings where labeled data is available.
- There are some typographical errors and unclear explanations in the paper, which could be improved for better clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the method performs in scenarios with more severe distribution shifts, such as those found in the ImageNet-C and ImageNet-3DCC datasets?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods that use labeled data or different data selection schemes?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost and memory requirements of DISTA compared to other methods?
- How does the method handle the potential issue of overfitting, especially in federated learning settings where models are aggregated?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their method, especially in applications where model accuracy might be critical?
