 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of spurious correlations in self-supervised learning (SSL) and proposes a novel method, LateTVG, to mitigate these issues. The study begins by demonstrating how common augmentations in SSL can introduce spurious correlations, affecting the learning of core features. It then introduces LateTVG, which prunes weights in later layers of the encoder to remove spurious information, improving performance on minority subgroups. The paper also discusses the limitations of existing methods in addressing spurious correlations and shows that LateTVG can enhance the robustness of SSL models to such correlations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive literature review that effectively situates the research within the existing body of knowledge.
- The experimental setup is well-designed, with detailed reporting of results and methodologies, including the use of both SimSiam and SimCLR models.
- The paper provides a novel approach to addressing spurious correlations in SSL through the use of LateTVG, which is an innovative method that has been empirically shown to be effective.
- The analysis of the toy model and the empirical analysis of spurious connectivity are insightful and contribute to a better understanding of the problem.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations and figures that aid in understanding the proposed methods and their implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications for the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly regarding the assumptions of equal numbers of samples per group and the probabilities of attribute and label changes.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, LateTVG, and does not compare it with other potential baselines, which could provide a more robust evaluation of its effectiveness.
- There is a need for more experimental validation on larger and more complex datasets, such as CIFAR-100 or ImageNet, to assess the scalability and generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential impact of the proposed method on the performance of SSL models on majority groups, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need correction to enhance the professional quality of the manuscript.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly regarding the equal numbers of samples per group and the probabilities of attribute and label changes?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger and more complex datasets, such as CIFAR-100 or ImageNet?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of LateTVG on the performance of SSL models on majority groups?
- Is there a possibility to extend the analysis to include more complex datasets or different types of spurious correlations?
- How does the proposed method compare with other potential baselines, and what are the specific advantages of LateTVG over these methods?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the results obtained with LateTVG against those from supervised learning, especially in terms of the performance metrics used?
