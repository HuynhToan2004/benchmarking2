 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for parameter-efficient multi-task learning (MTL) in language models, focusing on the transfer of knowledge from source tasks to target tasks using minimal parameters. The proposed method, ScaLearn, scales the output representations of source adapters and combines them using a simple weighted sum operation. This approach is shown to outperform existing methods like AdapterFusion, demonstrating effectiveness and efficiency in transfer learning scenarios. The method's simplicity and parameter efficiency are highlighted, making it a promising alternative to traditional MTL methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to multi-task learning (MTL) by scaling the output representations of source adapters, which is a simple yet effective method for transferring knowledge from source tasks to target tasks.
- The proposed method is parameter-efficient, using only 0.00% of the parameters of AdapterFusion, making it a significant improvement in terms of model efficiency.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a thorough analysis of the effects of scaling output representations.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various tasks and datasets, showing that it outperforms baseline methods in terms of both performance and parameter efficiency.
- The paper includes a detailed comparison with existing methods, highlighting the advantages of the proposed approach in terms of transfer learning performance and parameter efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on the GLUE benchmark, which may limit the generalizability of the findings to other domains or tasks.
- The methodology of the paper is not entirely novel, as similar approaches have been explored in previous works, such as the use of scaling vectors for task learning and domain adaptation.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other parameter-efficient methods like LoRA and BitFit, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method's advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of task interference, which is a known problem in MTL, and does not discuss how the proposed method might address this issue.
- The paper's claims about the novelty of the method are somewhat overstated, as similar methods have been previously proposed, which might undermine the perceived originality of the work.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the specific role of the "Task" parameters mentioned in the paper? Are these parameters only used for training the source tasks or do they also play a role in the transfer learning phase?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the target task is not present in the source tasks? Can you provide examples or additional experiments to demonstrate this?
- In light of the findings in your experiments, how do you explain the observed performance differences between the proposed method and other baseline methods, especially in terms of the number of parameters used?
- Could you elaborate on the method's ability to address the task interference problem, which is a known issue in MTL? How does the proposed method mitigate this issue compared to other methods?
- The paper mentions that the method is effective in few-shot settings. Could you provide more details on how the method performs in such settings, perhaps by including additional experiments or results?
