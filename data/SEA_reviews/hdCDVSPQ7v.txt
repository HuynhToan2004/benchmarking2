 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Jorge, a second-order optimizer designed for GPU platforms that aims to eliminate the computational bottleneck of matrix inversions in second-order optimizers. Jorge approximates the preconditioner computation in Shampoo, allowing for rapid convergence benefits and high computational efficiency. The authors demonstrate that Jorge can be hyperparameterized from a well-tuned SGD baseline, potentially improving generalization and training efficiency. The paper presents empirical evaluations showing Jorge's superiority over SGD, AdamW, and Shampoo across multiple deep learning models, both in terms of sample efficiency and wall-clock time. However, the paper's contributions are seen as incremental and the methodology is criticized for being overly complex and not sufficiently novel.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant computational cost of matrix inversions in second-order optimizers, which is a well-known bottleneck.
- The proposed method, Jorge, is shown to be efficient on GPUs, with a focus on reducing the computational cost of matrix inversions.
- The paper provides a clear and well-structured presentation, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The empirical results demonstrate the superiority of Jorge over existing methods such as SGD, AdamW, and Shampoo in terms of sample efficiency and wall-clock time.
- The methodology of Jorge is simple and effective, with a focus on reducing the computational cost of matrix inversions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is seen as incremental, as it primarily builds on existing methods like Shampoo and the binomial series expansion for inverse computation.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other second-order optimizers such as K-FAC and Hessian-Free Optimization, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The methodology of Jorge is overly complex, with numerous hyperparameters that require careful tuning, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how Jorge's hyperparameters are derived from SGD, which could affect the reproducibility and applicability of the results.
- The paper's claims of novelty are questionable, as the use of binomial series expansion for inverse computation is not new.
- The experimental results are not convincing, particularly in terms of the generalization of Jorge, which is inconsistent with the claims made in the paper.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of Jorge with other second-order optimizers such as K-FAC and Hessian-Free Optimization?
- How does Jorge perform when compared to other adaptive second-order optimizers like Adagrad?
- Could the authors clarify how Jorge's hyperparameters are derived from SGD and provide a clearer explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process?
- What are the specific advantages of Jorge over other second-order optimizers, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and convergence properties?
- How does Jorge handle the issue of overfitting, which is a known problem with second-order optimizers?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the hardware and software configurations used, to ensure reproducibility?
