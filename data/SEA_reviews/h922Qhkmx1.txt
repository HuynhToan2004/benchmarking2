 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel diffusion-based generative model, termed Multi-Source Diffusion Model (MSDM), capable of handling both music synthesis and source separation tasks. This model leverages the joint probability density of sources sharing a context, allowing for both generation and separation tasks. It also introduces a new inference method based on Dirac likelihood functions for source separation. The model is trained on the Slakh2100 dataset and shows competitive results in source separation tasks. The paper also explores the partial generation task of source imputation, where a subset of sources is generated given the others, which is a new task in the audio domain. However, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity and depth in explaining certain methodologies and results, and for the limited evaluation on only one dataset.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is the first to train a diffusion model on the Slakh2100 dataset, which is significant for the field.
- The proposed model is the first to handle both generation and separation tasks in music, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of a new inference method for source separation based on Dirac likelihood functions is innovative and could be beneficial for future research.
- The model's ability to perform both generation and separation tasks using a single model trained on a single dataset is a notable achievement.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and logical flow, which aids in understanding the content.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the inference method, particularly the role of the Dirac delta function and the sampling procedure.
- The paper does not provide enough details on the training process, such as the number of training steps and the specifics of the diffusion model used.
- The evaluation is limited to one dataset, which may not adequately represent the model's performance across different datasets.
- The paper does not compare its method with other existing models, such as the NCSN-BASIS algorithm, which could provide a better understanding of the model's strengths and limitations.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implications of the model, such as its computational efficiency and scalability to larger datasets or more complex mixtures.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the model's architecture and the role of different components, such as the score network and the sampler.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and implementation of the Dirac delta function in your model?
- How does the sampling procedure work in your model, particularly in the context of source separation?
- Can you provide more details on the training process, such as the number of training steps and the specifics of the diffusion model used?
- How does the model perform when applied to different datasets, and what are the implications of using different datasets?
- Could you elaborate on the practical implications of your model, such as its computational efficiency and scalability?
- How does the model handle different types of music or mixtures, and what are the limitations in terms of complexity and diversity of sources?
- Can you provide more examples or demonstrations of the model's performance, particularly in terms of source separation and generation quality?
