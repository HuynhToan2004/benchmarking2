 **Summary:**
The paper introduces BCNAS-SNN, a novel framework designed to optimize the placement of backward connections (BCs) in Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) to enhance their performance. By employing an evolutionary algorithm to search for the most effective BCs, the authors demonstrate significant improvements in classification accuracy across various datasets, including CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and Tiny-ImageNet. The approach involves a two-step search strategy that first utilizes zero-shot methods to select architectures and then employs an evolutionary algorithm to refine the selection. Despite these achievements, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the findings to other network architectures and the efficiency of the search process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to enhancing SNNs by introducing backward connections, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The use of an evolutionary algorithm to search for optimal backward connections is innovative and effectively demonstrated through extensive experiments.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making the complex methodology accessible to readers.
- The results show a significant improvement in classification accuracy on various datasets, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The introduction of zero-shot methods for initial population selection and the use of one-shot weight-sharing methods in the evolutionary search process are highlighted as effective strategies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with the evolutionary algorithm used, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The focus on ResNet architecture limits the generalizability of the findings to other network architectures, which could be a major drawback for broader applicability.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the research, which is a critical omission in ethical considerations.
- The search process for the optimal BCs is time-consuming and may not be practical for large-scale applications, which could limit the practical utility of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to other network architectures or its scalability to larger datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the evolutionary algorithm used in the search for optimal backward connections?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other network architectures, such as VGG or Transformer, which are not tested in the study?
- Is there a possibility to extend the search process to include more layers in the network to enhance the performance of SNNs further?
- How do the authors address the issue of potential overfitting in the search process, especially considering the limited dataset sizes used in the experiments?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the research, particularly in the context of SNNs and their applications?
