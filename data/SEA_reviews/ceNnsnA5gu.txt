 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel algorithmic model for Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) named WL-trees, which are derived from Breadth-First-Search (BFS) on input graphs. These trees are equivalent to the node colors computed by the 1-WL algorithm, providing a new perspective on the expressiveness of GNNs. The authors demonstrate that WL-trees can be used to analyze the expressiveness of GNNs and propose methods to recover anchored subgraphs from WL-trees. The paper also discusses how WL-trees can be applied to enhance the understanding of GNNs and potentially lead to the development of more expressive GNNs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow explanations that facilitate understanding.
- The introduction of WL-trees provides a novel perspective on the analysis of graph neural networks (GNNs), potentially leading to new insights and methodologies.
- The theoretical results, such as the bijection between WL-trees and 1-WL colors, are robust and well-explained, contributing to a deeper understanding of GNNs.
- The paper is original in its approach, introducing a new algorithmic model (WL-trees) that is both theoretically sound and potentially useful for analyzing and improving GNNs.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive and support the claims made about the expressiveness of GNNs, providing empirical evidence for the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the introduction of WL-trees and does not convincingly demonstrate their practical utility beyond theoretical analysis.
- The theoretical results, while interesting, are not sufficiently grounded in practical applications or real-world scenarios, which could limit their relevance and impact.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are limited, focusing on small-scale datasets and not sufficiently demonstrating the practical implications of the theoretical findings.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, such as the 1-WL algorithm and other GNN models, which could help to contextualize the contributions of WL-trees.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation and comparison with state-of-the-art methods to better establish the effectiveness and superiority of WL-trees.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations on how WL-trees can be used to enhance the expressiveness of GNNs, especially in practical applications?
- How do WL-trees compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability to other GNN models, particularly in large-scale datasets or complex graph structures?
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of "anchored subgraphs" in the context of GNNs and their relevance to the expressiveness of GNNs?
- How does the paper address the issue of over-smoothing and over-squashing, which are mentioned but not thoroughly discussed?
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods. Could the authors elaborate on these aspects?
