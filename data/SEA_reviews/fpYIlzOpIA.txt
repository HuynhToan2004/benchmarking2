 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel benchmarking dataset, NLPBench, designed to evaluate the problem-solving capabilities of large language models (LLMs) in natural language processing (NLP) contexts. The dataset, sourced from university final exams, includes 378 questions covering various NLP topics and is used to assess the performance of models like GPT-3.5/4, PaLM-2, and LLAMA-2 using advanced prompting strategies such as chain-of-thought (CoT) and tree-of-thought (ToT). The study reveals inconsistent effectiveness of these strategies and highlights the need for enhancing LLMs' problem-solving skills, particularly in logical decomposition and reasoning. Despite its innovative approach and comprehensive evaluation, the paper faces criticism for its limited scope in terms of model diversity and the generalizability of its findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the presentation is clear, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The dataset, NLPBench, is novel and comprehensive, covering a wide range of NLP topics and including questions with context, which enhances the evaluation of LLMs' problem-solving capabilities.
- The experimental design is robust, including a variety of prompting strategies and models, which provides a thorough analysis of LLMs' performance.
- The paper addresses a significant and under-explored problem in the field of LLMs, focusing on their ability to solve NLP problems, which is crucial for advancing the technology.
- The dataset is diverse, including questions with context and multiple-turn communication, which adds complexity to the evaluation and reflects real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the rationale behind using specific models (e.g., GPT-3.5/4, PaLM-2, LLAMA-2) and the choice of these models for the study.
- The evaluation is limited to a few models, which may not adequately represent the performance of LLMs across the board.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the generalizability of the findings, which is a critical aspect of any benchmarking study.
- The dataset's size and complexity are not adequately justified, and the paper does not convincingly argue for the necessity of including a wide range of models for a comprehensive evaluation.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on how the dataset was collected and processed, which could affect the reliability and validity of the results.
- The evaluation metrics used are not clearly defined, which could lead to confusion about the results and their interpretation.

**Questions:**
- How were the specific models (GPT-3.5/4, PaLM-2, LLAMA-2) chosen for the study, and what was the rationale behind their selection?
- Can the authors provide more details on the data collection process, including how the questions were sourced and verified for accuracy and relevance?
- How were the questions categorized into different NLP topics, and what criteria were used for this classification?
- Could the authors clarify the evaluation metrics used in the study and provide more detailed explanations of how these metrics were chosen and applied?
- Given the limited model diversity in the study, how do the authors plan to expand the evaluation to include a broader range of models in future work?
- How do the authors address the concerns about the generalizability of the findings, and what steps are being taken to ensure that the benchmark is applicable across different LLM architectures and training paradigms?
