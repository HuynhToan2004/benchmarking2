 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of differentially private principal component analysis (DP-PCA) in a federated learning (FL) setting where data is vertically partitioned among clients. It introduces a novel algorithm, SPCA, which leverages the BGW protocol to enable each client to perturb the covariance matrix with Skellam noise, ensuring both server-observed and client-observed privacy. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that SPCA can achieve privacy-utility trade-offs comparable to those in the centralized setting. Experiments on real-world datasets validate these theoretical claims. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty and potential performance degradation compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel algorithm for DP-PCA in the vertical federated learning (FL) setting, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust and supports the algorithm's efficacy in achieving privacy-utility trade-offs comparable to centralized settings.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The use of the BGW protocol to enable each client to perturb the covariance matrix with Skellam noise is an innovative approach that ensures both server-observed and client-observed privacy.
- The experiments conducted on real-world datasets validate the theoretical claims, demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is limited as it primarily adapts existing algorithms like SPCA and BGW to the vertical FL setting without significant innovation.
- The performance of the algorithm, SPCA, is inferior to existing methods like CPCA, which could limit its practical utility.
- The discretization of data to ensure privacy might lead to a loss of information, which could affect the accuracy of the PCA results.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and communication overhead introduced by the discretization and noise addition steps.
- The assumption of error-free and secure channels between clients is unrealistic and not practical in many FL settings.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of privacy guarantees and computational efficiency.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it seems to be a straightforward extension of existing works, particularly those by Dwork et al. (2014).

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition of the σ parameter in Algorithm 1 and its role in the privacy guarantees?
- How does the proposed algorithm ensure that the noise added by different clients does not accumulate to a point where it significantly degrades the utility of the PCA results?
- Is there a possibility to use a noise distribution other than Skellam to potentially improve the utility of the PCA results?
- Can you provide a detailed comparison of the communication overhead and computational complexity introduced by the discretization and noise addition steps with the original SPCA algorithm?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare in terms of privacy guarantees and computational efficiency with existing methods like CPCA and the centralized SPCA?
- Could you elaborate on the novelty of your approach and how it extends or improves upon the existing works by Dwork et al. (2014)?
