 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel self-supervised learning method for 3D molecular structures, named LEGO, which treats tetrahedrons as fundamental building blocks. This approach aims to address the atom-bond inconsistency problem by modeling molecular graphs as sets of nodes and edges, explicitly generating edges during pre-training. The methodology involves perturbing a portion of tetrahedrons and reconstructing them, which is further divided into spatial orientation prediction and internal arrangement generation. Extensive experiments on molecular quantum and biochemical property prediction tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach. However, the paper lacks detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and does not provide a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to self-supervised learning on 3D molecular structures, which treats tetrahedrons as fundamental building blocks, addressing the atom-bond inconsistency problem by modeling molecular graphs as sets of nodes and edges.
- The methodology is well-explained, and the paper is generally well-written, making it easy to follow.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in certain molecular property prediction tasks.
- The paper addresses the atom-bond inconsistency problem, which is a significant issue in molecular modeling, and proposes a novel solution by modeling the molecular graph as a set of nodes and edges.
- The proposed method is simple and easy to implement, with clear descriptions of the algorithms used.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and the effectiveness of the proposed method in handling different types of molecules, especially those with complex structures.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of the research.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the scalability of the proposed method to larger datasets or more complex molecular structures, which could affect its practical applicability.
- The methodology section could be clearer, particularly in explaining the segmentation of 3D molecular structures into building blocks and the perturbation of local structures.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training data, such as the size and diversity of the dataset used for pre-training, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed visualizations and explanations of the molecular structures used in the experiments to better illustrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform on more complex molecular structures, such as those with multiple rings or large molecular weight?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the proposed method compared to other state-of-the-art methods?
- How does the proposed method handle molecules with different conformations, and what are the implications of this for the effectiveness of the method?
- Could the authors clarify the segmentation process of 3D molecular structures into building blocks and the perturbation of local structures?
- What are the specific training data used for pre-training the model, and how does the diversity of the dataset affect the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare to other pre-training methods in terms of the quality of the pre-trained model, and what are the implications of this for downstream tasks?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of baselines and the experimental conditions used for comparison?
