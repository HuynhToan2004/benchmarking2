 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for class-incremental learning called Prediction Error-based Classification (PEC), which utilizes a random shallow neural network as a teacher to mimic the outputs of a frozen random network on data from each class. This approach is contrasted with traditional generative classification methods and shown to be effective in single-pass-through data scenarios, outperforming other rehearsal-free baselines in all cases and rehearsal-based methods with moderate replay buffer sizes in most cases. The method is supported by theoretical justifications and extensive experimental validations across multiple datasets. However, concerns are raised about the scalability of the method and its performance in more complex, high-dimensional datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The experimental results are comprehensive, including comparisons with multiple baselines and ablation studies that analyze the impact of different factors on the performance of the proposed method.
- The proposed method, PEC, is novel and innovative, offering a new approach to class-incremental learning that is simple, efficient, and effective.
- The theoretical justification of PEC is well-supported, providing a strong foundation for the method's application in class-incremental learning.
- The paper includes extensive experiments that validate the effectiveness of PEC across multiple datasets and task splits, demonstrating its robustness and versatility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced perspective and guide future research.
- The scalability of the method is a concern, particularly in high-dimensional datasets such as mini-ImageNet, where the method's performance is not as strong as in lower-dimensional datasets like MNIST and SVHN.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the theoretical justifications provided, particularly the connection between the Gaussian Process and the PEC method.
- The method's reliance on a random shallow neural network as a teacher raises questions about its scalability and effectiveness in more complex, high-dimensional datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of dataset imbalance, which could significantly impact the performance of the method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage, which are critical factors in continual learning.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the scalability of the PEC method, particularly in high-dimensional datasets like mini-ImageNet?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex, high-dimensional datasets, such as those used in large-scale visual recognition tasks?
- Can the authors provide more details on the theoretical justification of the connection between the Gaussian Process and the PEC method?
- How does the method handle dataset imbalance, and what strategies could be employed to mitigate its effects?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of using different types of teacher functions, such as pre-trained neural networks or perceptual hashes, to improve the performance of the PEC method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage with other state-of-the-art methods in continual learning?
