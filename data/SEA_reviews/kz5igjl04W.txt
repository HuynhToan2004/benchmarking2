 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel methodology named "causal disentanglement with extreme values" (CDEV) aimed at interpreting the latent space of deep generative models. This methodology is demonstrated through its application to the communication system of sperm whales, where it successfully identifies meaningful properties encoded in the latent space. The approach involves manipulating individual latent variables to extreme values and applying causal inference methods to test for meaningful properties of unknown data. The paper argues that this method can provide insights into complex data systems and potentially be applied to other domains. However, the method's applicability to different datasets and its reliance on specific model architectures are questioned, as are the assumptions made about the causal structure of the data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined problem.
- The authors have successfully demonstrated the application of their method to a novel and interesting dataset, providing insights into the communication system of sperm whales.
- The methodology is innovative, combining latent space exploration with causal inference to gain insights into the communication system of sperm whales, which is an area of significant interest.
- The paper includes a comprehensive literature review, effectively situating the research within the current state of knowledge in the field.
- The proposed methodology is general and could potentially be applied to other domains, showing promise in advancing our understanding of complex data systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's applicability is limited to specific model architectures and datasets, which may not generalize well to other domains or datasets.
- The paper's reliance on the assumption that the data follows a specific causal structure could limit its applicability to other datasets where this assumption may not hold.
- The method's effectiveness in disentangling factors of variation in the latent space is questionable, as it appears to only disentangle a few factors and not comprehensively address all factors of variation.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational costs associated with the method, which could be a significant barrier for practical implementation.
- The paper's claims about the method's ability to "disentangle" factors of variation in the latent space are overstated, as the method does not comprehensively address all factors of variation.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly demonstrated to be generalizable to other datasets or domains, which could limit the method's practical utility.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific model architecture used in their experiments and discuss its generalizability to other datasets or domains?
- How does the method perform when applied to datasets where the causal structure is different from what is assumed in the paper?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational costs associated with their method and discuss potential strategies for reducing these costs?
- How does the method handle the identification of all factors of variation in the latent space, and what are the implications of not identifying all factors?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the "click detector" used in their experiments and discuss its effectiveness in identifying all clicks in the dataset?
- How does the method handle the identification of factors of variation that are not explicitly tested, and what are the implications of not identifying these factors?
