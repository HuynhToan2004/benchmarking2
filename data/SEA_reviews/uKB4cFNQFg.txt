 **Summary:**
The paper introduces BEND, a benchmark for evaluating DNA language models (LMs) on various biological tasks using the human genome. The benchmark comprises seven tasks, including gene finding, enhancer annotation, chromatin accessibility, histone modification, CpG methylation, and noncoding variant effects. The authors compare several DNA LMs, including AWD-LSTM, Dilated ResNet, and various transformer-based models, against supervised baselines and expert methods. The results suggest that while DNA LMs can approach expert performance on some tasks, they struggle with others, particularly in long-range feature modeling. The paper also highlights the need for more comprehensive evaluation frameworks and larger datasets to advance the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmark for evaluating DNA language models, focusing on biological tasks, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- It provides a comprehensive evaluation of several DNA LMs, comparing their performance across various tasks, which is a valuable contribution to the literature.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The inclusion of a variety of DNA LMs from different sources and the use of different metrics to evaluate their performance adds depth to the study.
- The benchmark tasks are chosen to be relevant to real-world genome annotation, which is a commendable approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition of what constitutes a "long-range" feature in the context of DNA language models, which could lead to confusion and inconsistency in the evaluation of models.
- The benchmark tasks are limited to the human genome, which might not adequately represent the diversity of genomic data across different species.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of label imbalance in gene finding tasks, which could bias the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the choice of supervised baselines and their performance, which could affect the interpretation of the results.
- The inclusion of only a few models in the benchmark might not provide a comprehensive view of the performance landscape of DNA LMs.
- The paper does not explore the potential of using more advanced models or techniques, such as transformer-based models, which could enhance the evaluation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify what they consider to be a "long-range" feature in the context of DNA language models?
- How were the supervised baselines chosen, and what are their performance metrics across different tasks?
- Why were only a few models included in the benchmark, and how does this choice affect the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of using more advanced models or techniques, such as transformer-based models, to enhance the evaluation?
- How does the label imbalance in gene finding tasks affect the results, and could this be addressed in future studies?
- Can the authors provide more details on the choice of the 128bp sequence length for enhancer annotation tasks and its impact on the results?
