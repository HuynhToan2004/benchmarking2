 **Summary:**
The paper introduces L-MBOP-E, a model-based offline planning algorithm that utilizes a low-dimensional latent state space for dynamics modeling and employs Thompson sampling for exploration. This approach aims to reduce the effects of compounding errors in dynamics modeling and improve exploration by leveraging an extrinsic policy. The method is evaluated against existing model-based offline planning algorithms on benchmarks like MuJoCo D4RL and Deepmind Control tasks, showing significant improvements. However, concerns are raised regarding the clarity of the method's advantages over existing techniques, the choice of hyperparameters, and the robustness of the results to these choices.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, L-MBOP-E, is a novel combination of existing techniques such as latent dynamics modeling, Thompson sampling, and extrinsic policies, which are well-motivated and well-explained.
- The empirical results demonstrate that L-MBOP-E outperforms existing model-based offline planning algorithms on benchmarks like MuJoCo D4RL and Deepmind Control tasks, indicating its effectiveness.
- The use of a low-dimensional latent state space for dynamics modeling and Thompson sampling for exploration are innovative approaches that enhance the method's performance and robustness.
- The method's ability to adapt to new tasks without retraining and its performance on random datasets showcases its general applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with existing methods like TD-MPC and DreamerV3, which also use similar approaches, and does not adequately discuss the advantages of its method over these.
- The choice of hyperparameters, such as the latent dimension and the number of rollouts, is not well-justified, and the sensitivity of the results to these choices is not explored.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its method, particularly in terms of computational cost and the practicality of using an extrinsic policy in real-world applications.
- The method's reliance on the extrinsic policy for exploration might limit its applicability in scenarios where such policies are not available or practical.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training of the extrinsic policy and the dynamics model, which could affect the reproducibility and understanding of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods like TD-MPC and DreamerV3, highlighting the specific advantages of their approach?
- How sensitive is the performance of L-MBOP-E to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the latent dimension and the number of rollouts? Could the authors provide a sensitivity analysis or a range of hyperparameters that work well across different datasets?
- What is the computational cost of training L-MBOP-E compared to other methods, and how does it scale with the size of the dataset?
- How does the method perform when the extrinsic policy is not available or when it is trained on a different dataset?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training of the extrinsic policy and the dynamics model, including the architecture and training procedures?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of hyperparameters and the rationale behind their selection?
- How does the method handle the potential for compounding errors in the dynamics model, especially when the latent dimension is too small?
