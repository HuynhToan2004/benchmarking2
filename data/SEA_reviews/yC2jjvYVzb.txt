 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CosMoS, a method for model selection in scenarios where multiple classifiers, each trained on a source dataset, need to be adapted to target distributions. CosMoS employs clustering of test data to identify similarities and assigns the most confident model for each cluster. The approach aims to enhance performance across diverse subpopulations by leveraging the strengths of different models. The paper also explores the use of CosMoS in hyperparameter tuning and other applications, demonstrating its potential in various settings. However, concerns about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of its advantages over existing methods, and the robustness of the clustering algorithm are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible.
- The proposed method, CosMoS, is simple yet effective, particularly in scenarios where multiple models are available, and it does not require access to target data.
- The method's application is broad, including hyperparameter tuning and other use cases, which demonstrates its versatility.
- The paper provides a novel approach to handling spurious correlations by focusing on the strengths of different models across subpopulations, rather than attempting to mitigate these correlations.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering a wide range of datasets and configurations, which supports the robustness of the findings.
- The paper includes a detailed discussion of related work, which helps contextualize the research and highlights the unique contributions of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear justification for the choice of clustering as the method for identifying similarities between data points. Other methods like k-nearest neighbors or graph-based clustering might provide more reliable results.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as the use of model confidence and clustering for model selection has been explored in previous works.
- The paper does not adequately address the robustness of the clustering algorithm, which is crucial for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant methods in the field, which limits the evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly concerning the assumptions made about the data and the potential societal impacts of the work.
- The experimental results, while comprehensive, do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods, especially in terms of the average regret metric.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods like k-nearest neighbors or graph-based clustering in terms of reliability and effectiveness?
- Can you provide more detailed results on the robustness of the clustering algorithm, particularly under different conditions or with different datasets?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to more recent and relevant datasets, such as those from the MetaShift and DomainBed benchmarks?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the clustering method and how it was validated to ensure its effectiveness in the proposed method?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the clustering results are not as expected, and what are the potential implications for the overall performance of the model selection process?
- What are the potential societal impacts of the work, particularly concerning the use of spurious correlations and the implications for fairness and bias in machine learning models?
