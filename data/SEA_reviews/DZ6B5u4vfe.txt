 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the alignment of large language models (LLMs) with human brain activity, focusing on the effects of instruction tuning. It employs a variety of models and datasets to assess the brain alignment of LLMs, comparing instruction-tuned models with vanilla ones. The study reveals that instruction tuning enhances brain alignment, although it does not significantly improve behavioral alignment. The authors also explore the correlation between brain alignment and model properties like world knowledge and model size. However, the paper's findings are limited by the small sample size of brain datasets and the generalizability of the results across different model architectures and training methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the results and conclusions, with a strong motivation for the research.
- The experiments are extensive, involving a variety of models and datasets, which provides robust evidence for the claims made.
- The study is significant as it investigates the alignment of LLMs with the human brain, a topic of growing interest in the field.
- The paper includes a comprehensive analysis of the factors contributing to brain alignment, such as model size and world knowledge.
- The results are supported by multiple datasets and models, which enhances the reliability of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's main claim that instruction tuning improves brain alignment is not convincingly supported by the data, especially concerning the behavioral alignment which shows no improvement.
- The correlation between brain alignment and model properties, such as world knowledge and model size, is not clearly explained, and the causality of these correlations is not established.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison of the alignment between instruction-tuned models and vanilla models, which could have provided more insights into the effectiveness of instruction tuning.
- The paper's reliance on a single dataset for behavioral alignment limits the generalizability of the results.
- There is a noticeable gap in the alignment between the brain and behavioral data, which is not adequately addressed.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the study, including the small sample size of the brain datasets and the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancy between the brain alignment and behavioral alignment results, especially in light of the strong correlation between brain alignment and model size?
- How do the authors explain the lack of improvement in behavioral alignment despite the enhanced brain alignment?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the alignment between instruction-tuned models and vanilla models, perhaps through a direct comparison of brain activity and behavioral data?
- Given the limitations of the behavioral alignment dataset, how do the authors plan to address the generalizability of their findings to other datasets or model architectures?
- How do the authors address the potential confounding variables in the brain alignment measurements, such as the noise ceiling and the influence of different model architectures on brain activity?
