 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for off-policy selection (OPS) in human-centric systems, specifically addressing the challenges of participant heterogeneity. The framework, termed Universal Off-Policy Selection (UOPS), divides participants into sub-groups based on behavioral similarity and tailors OPS criteria for each group. This approach is evaluated through real-world applications in intelligent tutor systems and healthcare, demonstrating significant improvements in student learning and patient outcomes. The paper also includes theoretical analysis and empirical results, showcasing the framework's effectiveness and practical applicability. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of presentation, the necessity of certain assumptions, and the lack of detailed experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem of personalized off-policy selection in human-centric systems, which is a significant challenge in real-world applications.
- The proposed UOPS framework is novel and well-motivated, providing a comprehensive solution to the problem of off-policy selection in human-centric systems.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The evaluation of the UOPS framework is thorough, including real-world applications and a comprehensive set of baselines, which demonstrates the framework's effectiveness.
- The paper includes a theoretical analysis and proofs, which provide a solid foundation for the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in several sections, particularly in the problem formulation and the methodology used, which could confuse readers and hinder understanding.
- There are inconsistencies and unclear definitions in the mathematical notations used, which could lead to confusion and misinterpretation.
- The paper assumes that there is a unique correspondence between each trajectory and the participant, which may not hold in real-world scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of heterogeneity among participants, which is a critical aspect of the study.
- The experimental results and their presentation are not convincing, with some figures and tables lacking clarity and detail.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and potential negative societal impacts, which are essential for a complete assessment of the technology.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the discrepancy between the problem formulation in the abstract and the actual problem formulation in the paper?
- How does the UOPS framework ensure that the sub-groups are meaningful and distinct, and what are the implications if the sub-groups overlap or are too similar?
- What are the specific criteria used to determine the number of sub-groups and how does this affect the performance of the framework?
- How does the UOPS framework handle scenarios where the number of sub-groups is not predefined or when new sub-groups need to be created?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the data collection and preprocessing steps, to ensure reproducibility and transparency?
- How does the UOPS framework ensure that the policies selected are fair and equitable across different sub-groups, particularly in scenarios where the sub-groups may have inherent biases?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the UOPS framework, and how do you address these concerns?
