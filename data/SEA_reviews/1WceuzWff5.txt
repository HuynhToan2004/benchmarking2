 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the transfer of skills across diverse reinforcement learning environments using a multitask learning approach. It introduces the SEAL policy, which leverages a shared, environment-agnostic latent policy architecture to facilitate skill transfer. The authors evaluate the effectiveness of this approach using the MetaWorld and Franka Kitchen environments, comparing it to traditional multi-task RL methods. The paper claims that the SEAL policy enables learning with fewer gradient update steps, though the results are not consistently superior to existing methods. The experiments are limited to state-based control environments, and the paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the method could be applied to more complex, pixel-based tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the important problem of transferring skills across diverse environments in reinforcement learning, which is a significant challenge in the field.
- The proposed SEAL policy is well-motivated and clearly described, with a focus on reducing the number of gradient update steps required for learning.
- The experiments are well-designed, with a focus on understanding the design decisions and training protocols necessary for skill transfer.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their methodology and results.
- The study is significant as it explores the transferability of skills across different environments, which is crucial for the advancement of reinforcement learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the SEAL policy could be extended to handle pixel-based tasks, which are more complex and diverse.
- The experiments are limited to state-based control environments, which may not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the SEAL policy in more complex settings.
- The results presented in the paper do not consistently outperform the baseline methods, raising questions about the practical advantages of the SEAL policy.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in certain applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the SEAL policy handle the transfer of skills across environments with different action spaces?
- Can you provide more details on how the SEAL policy could be adapted to handle pixel-based tasks, and what challenges might be encountered in such an adaptation?
- How does the SEAL policy compare in terms of sample efficiency and learning speed to other state-of-the-art methods in more complex, pixel-based tasks?
- In the experiments where the SEAL policy is shown to be less effective than the baseline, what are the specific reasons for this performance gap?
- How does the SEAL policy handle the transfer of skills across environments with very different state and action spaces, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the learned policies?
- Could you provide more details on the computational resources required to implement the SEAL policy, and how this might impact the scalability of the method?
