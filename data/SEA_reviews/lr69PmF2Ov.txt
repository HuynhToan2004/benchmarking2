 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Discriminatively Matched Part Tokens (DMPT) for pointly supervised instance segmentation, extending the capabilities of self-attention mechanisms to handle object deformation and appearance variations. DMPT involves a series of steps including part token allocation, token-classifier matching, and part-based guidance generation, aiming to enhance the localization and segmentation capabilities of the self-attention mechanism. The approach is evaluated on the PASCAL VOC and MS-COCO datasets, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper is criticized for its complexity, lack of clarity in presentation, and limited novelty in its approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides clear explanations of the proposed methods, enhancing understanding for readers.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on the PASCAL VOC dataset and significantly improves the performance of the Segment Anything Model (SAM).
- The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed components, providing valuable insights into the method's functionality.
- The method's simplicity and the potential for integration with SAM make it a promising approach for enhancing instance segmentation performance.
- The paper provides a clear motivation for the research and presents a novel approach to instance segmentation, which is well-explained and supported by experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and training costs, which could affect the practicality of the proposed approach.
- The presentation of the paper is confusing, with unclear explanations and a lack of detailed implementation details, making it difficult to replicate the results.
- The paper's experimental results are limited to a few datasets, and there is a lack of comparison with a broader range of datasets and methods, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it seems to resemble existing methods like Point2Mask, with similarities in the use of part tokens and part-level guidance.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impact of the proposed methods, which could be significant in applications involving object recognition and segmentation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a clearer comparison with existing methods, including computational efficiency and training costs?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods like Point2Mask in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the implementation details and provide more detailed explanations to improve the reproducibility of the results?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of objects, particularly those with complex shapes or varying deformations?
- Could the authors provide more visual examples and comparisons to better illustrate the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential societal impacts, particularly in applications involving object recognition and segmentation?
