 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to simulating the time-evolving Schrödinger equation using a stochastic mechanics formulation, which leverages neural networks to approximate the gradient of the logarithm of the wave function's norm and the gradient of the wave function's phase. This method aims to avoid the curse of dimensionality by focusing on the low-dimensional structure of the wave function. The proposed approach is evaluated against the Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINN) method, showing potential advantages in computational efficiency and accuracy, especially in higher-dimensional systems. However, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity, lack of detailed theoretical justification, and potential limitations in its applicability to more complex quantum systems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and structured presentation that is easy to follow.
- The introduction of a novel approach using stochastic mechanics to simulate quantum systems is both innovative and relevant, particularly in addressing the curse of dimensionality in quantum simulations.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence, demonstrating its effectiveness and potential for future research.
- The paper is original in its application of stochastic mechanics to quantum simulations, offering a fresh perspective on the problem.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the derivation of the equations and the explanation of the neural network architecture used.
- There is a need for more detailed theoretical justification, especially regarding the convergence of the method and the implications of the assumptions made.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on simple quantum systems, which may not fully demonstrate the method's capabilities in more complex scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other existing methods, such as quantum Monte Carlo methods, to better establish the advantages of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach, particularly in terms of its applicability to more complex quantum systems and the potential impact of its assumptions on the accuracy of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific neural network architecture used in the simulations and how it was chosen?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy with other existing methods, such as quantum Monte Carlo methods?
- In the experimental setup, how were the hyperparameters chosen, and what impact do they have on the results?
- Could the authors provide more detailed theoretical justification for the convergence of the method and the implications of the assumptions made?
- How does the method handle more complex quantum systems, such as those involving multiple particles or more intricate potentials?
- Is there a plan to extend the method to include more complex quantum systems, and if so, what are the anticipated challenges and potential solutions?
