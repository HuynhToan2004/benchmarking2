 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the effectiveness of activation shaping for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection, specifically focusing on the ASH method. It proposes a new method called SCALE, which scales activations to enhance OOD detection without compromising in-distribution accuracy. Additionally, the paper introduces ISH, a training-time enhancement method that shapes intermediate tensors to improve OOD detection. The paper presents a thorough analysis of activation shaping and its impact on OOD detection, showing that scaling activations can be more effective than pruning. The experiments demonstrate that SCALE and ISH can achieve state-of-the-art OOD detection performance with minimal computational overhead.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the ASH method and its impact on OOD detection, which is both interesting and relevant.
- The introduction of SCALE and ISH offers a novel approach to OOD detection, showing potential for significant improvements in OOD detection performance.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and logical arguments, making it accessible and understandable.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods in OOD detection, and the paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across various datasets.
- The methodology is innovative, particularly the application of activation scaling and intermediate tensor shaping, which are novel contributions to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the intermediate tensor shaping (ISH) method and its impact on computational resources.
- The assumption of Gaussian distribution for activations might not hold true for all datasets, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed methods with existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and resource utilization.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, with some sections being unclear or overly detailed, which could confuse readers.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and how they might perform under different conditions or with different types of data.
- The paper's experimental setup and methodology could be more rigorous, with a broader range of datasets and a more detailed comparison to existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the computational efficiency of the ISH method and how it compares to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of resource utilization?
- How does the paper ensure that the assumptions made about the Gaussian distribution of activations hold true across different datasets?
- Can you provide more details on the methodology used for the ISH method, particularly how it shapes intermediate tensors and how this affects the OOD detection performance?
- How does the paper address the potential limitations of the proposed methods, especially in scenarios where the assumptions about the activations might not hold true?
- Could you elaborate on the comparison with other existing methods, particularly in terms of the experimental setup and the selection of datasets?
- How does the paper ensure that the OOD detection performance is not compromised by the modifications made to the activation scaling and intermediate tensor shaping?
