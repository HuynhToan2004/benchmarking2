 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two novel methods, AW-HMM and Bi-HMM, to address label bias in non-autoregressive machine translation (NAT) using directed acyclic transformers (DAT). The proposed methods aim to balance the number of outgoing transitions across different positions and employ bi-directional HMMs to counteract label bias. The experiments conducted on WMT14 and WMT17 datasets demonstrate that these methods can improve translation quality, although the improvements are marginal and not consistently better than existing methods like DAT. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of these methods, including their relationship to Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) and their impact on the label bias problem.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the proposed methods and their theoretical underpinnings.
- The proposed methods, AW-HMM and Bi-HMM, are innovative and well-motivated, addressing the label bias problem in NAT effectively.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods in reducing label bias and improving translation quality.
- The paper includes a comprehensive analysis of the results, including a detailed comparison with existing methods and a discussion on the impact of different parameters.
- The authors have made the code available, which facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the relationship between the proposed methods and the label bias problem, particularly how the methods address the root cause of label bias.
- The improvements in translation quality, as measured by BLEU scores, are marginal and not consistently better than existing methods like DAT.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, such as the potential for increased training and inference times due to the bi-directional HMMs.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently robust, with some results showing inconsistencies or lack of statistical significance.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the impact of different parameters and their optimal values, as well as a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed methods address the root cause of label bias, particularly in terms of the transitions between different positions in the translation process?
- How do the authors justify the choice of parameters, such as the window size and the L2R/R2L weight, and what are the optimal values for these parameters?
- In the experimental results, why does the combination of AW-HMM and Bi-HMM not show improved performance over either method alone?
- How do the authors address the potential for increased training and inference times due to the bi-directional HMMs?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of translation quality and inference speed?
