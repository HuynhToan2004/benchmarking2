 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel test-time adaptation (TTA) method named DART, which addresses the challenge of class distribution shifts during testing. DART trains a distribution shift-aware module during the intermediate time using diverse class distributions from the training dataset. This module is then utilized to refine predictions made by the classifiers, focusing on class-wise confusion patterns. The method has been tested across various datasets including CIFAR, PACS, ImageNet, and digit benchmarks, demonstrating significant performance improvements. The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis and empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of DART in mitigating the impact of test-time distribution shifts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and important problem in the field of test-time adaptation (TTA) by focusing on the issue of class distribution shifts, which is often overlooked in current methods.
- The proposed method, DART, is well-motivated, clearly described, and easy to follow, with a clear and detailed explanation of the experimental setup and methodology.
- The paper provides extensive ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing consistent improvements across various datasets.
- The methodology of DART is simple yet effective, with a novel approach to addressing the problem of class distribution shifts, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the results and methodology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods, such as LSA, which also uses a similar method of training a label shift adapter during intermediate time.
- There is a lack of clarity and detail in some sections of the paper, particularly in the description of the training of the distribution shift-aware module and the experimental results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not consistently superior to existing methods, and the improvements are sometimes marginal or not statistically significant.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the scalability of the method to larger datasets or the computational cost of training the distribution shift-aware module.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to specific types of class distribution shifts, and it does not address other types of distribution shifts such as input distribution shifts.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between their method and existing methods like LSA, particularly in how the training of the distribution shift-aware module is handled?
- How does the proposed method perform when the test data distribution significantly differs from the training distribution, such as in scenarios with significant input distribution shifts?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training of the distribution shift-aware module, including the specifics of the training dataset and the training process?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting during the intermediate training phase, and what measures are in place to prevent this?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their method, particularly in terms of fairness and bias in classification outcomes?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost and scalability of their method, especially in comparison to other existing TTA methods?
