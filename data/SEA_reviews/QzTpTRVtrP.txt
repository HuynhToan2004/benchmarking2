 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Large Brain Model (LaBraM), a neural network model designed to process EEG data using a transformer architecture. The model is pre-trained on over 2,500 hours of diverse EEG data from various public and private datasets, leveraging a vector-quantized neural spectrum prediction for semantic tokenization. The authors demonstrate that LaBraM can outperform state-of-the-art methods in several EEG-related tasks such as abnormal detection, event type classification, emotion recognition, and gait prediction. Despite its innovative approach, concerns were raised about the novelty of the methodology and the comparative analysis with existing models. The paper is criticized for lacking a comprehensive discussion on related works and not sufficiently demonstrating the generalizability of the model across different EEG configurations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the model architecture and training methodology.
- The model shows strong performance across various tasks, demonstrating its versatility and effectiveness.
- The idea of using a large-scale pre-training model for EEG data is novel and innovative, particularly in the context of brain-computer interfaces (BCIs).
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the model on multiple downstream tasks, demonstrating its robustness and applicability.
- The use of a transformer architecture for processing EEG data is innovative, allowing for more efficient and effective processing of complex neural signals.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the methodology is questionable, as the paper does not sufficiently differentiate from existing models like BIOT, particularly in the use of vector-quantized neural spectrum prediction.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on related works, which could have provided a richer context for the research.
- The generalizability of the model to different EEG configurations, such as different numbers of channels or different data preprocessing methods, is not adequately demonstrated.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the challenges posed by the low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and the presence of noise in EEG data.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other existing models, particularly in terms of performance metrics and model configurations.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their methodology compared to existing models like BIOT, particularly in the use of vector-quantized neural spectrum prediction?
- How does the model perform when applied to different EEG configurations, such as those with fewer or more channels?
- What are the specific challenges posed by the low SNR and noise in EEG data, and how does the model address these issues?
- How does the model compare in terms of performance and computational efficiency with other state-of-the-art models in EEG data processing?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their model and potential directions for future research?
