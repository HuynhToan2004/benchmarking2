 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel adversarial training framework using Bregman divergence to enhance the robustness of neural networks against image corruptions. The authors propose a self-supervised learning algorithm to train an input convex neural network (ICNN) to learn a Bregman divergence that can capture the corruption type, such as contrast and fog. This divergence is then used to define a robustness threat model, which is optimized using mirror descent to improve the robustness of the model. The approach is evaluated on the CIFAR-10-C dataset, showing improvements in robustness against specific corruptions. However, the paper is criticized for its limited evaluation on a single dataset and the lack of a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to adversarial training by using Bregman divergence, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The paper is well-written and clearly presents the methodology, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on the CIFAR-10-C dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the proposed method, which adds depth to the understanding of the approach.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, including comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, and the results are convincing.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (CIFAR-10-C), which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the method across different datasets and corruption types.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other recent works that have shown significant improvements in robustness against image corruptions, which could provide a better context for the results.
- The methodology section is overly detailed, which may obscure the main contributions of the paper and make it less accessible to readers not familiar with the specific mathematical concepts used.
- The paper does not provide code, which makes it difficult to replicate the results and verify the claims made.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of its practical applicability to larger, more complex models.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be adapted to other types of corruptions, such as brightness or saturation, or is it limited to the specific corruptions evaluated in the paper?
- How does the method perform on other datasets, such as ImageNet, and how does it compare to other state-of-the-art methods on these datasets?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these limitations might affect its practical applicability?
- What are the specific advantages of using Bregman divergence over other similarity measures, such as LPIPS, and how does the learned Bregman divergence compare in terms of computational complexity and scalability?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the hyperparameters in the proposed method, particularly how they are chosen and how they affect the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of robustness against different types of attacks, such as white-box attacks or other types of corruptions not evaluated in the paper?
