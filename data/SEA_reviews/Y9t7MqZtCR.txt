 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Sparse Weight Averaging with Multiple Particles (SWAMP), a novel method for iterative pruning that leverages multiple particles trained with different SGD noise to enhance the generalization performance of pruned networks. The methodology builds on the Iterative Magnitude Pruning (IMP) framework, which is known for its effectiveness in achieving state-of-the-art pruning results. SWAMP averages the weights of multiple particles trained with different random seeds to achieve better generalization, demonstrating improved performance over existing baselines. The paper provides empirical evidence and theoretical analysis to support its claims, showing that SWAMP outperforms IMP and other pruning methods across various datasets and network architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Sparse Weight Averaging with Multiple Particles (SWAMP), which is a simple yet effective extension of the Iterative Magnitude Pruning (IMP) method.
- SWAMP demonstrates superior performance compared to IMP and other pruning methods across various datasets and network architectures, as shown in extensive experiments.
- The methodology is supported by both empirical evidence and theoretical analysis, providing a strong foundation for the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the loss landscape, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the rationale behind using multiple particles in SWAMP, and the impact of different particle weights on performance is not thoroughly analyzed.
- There is a lack of comparison with other pruning methods such as RigL, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of SWAMP, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the method.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods like IMP, and the improvements are marginal in some cases.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the parallelization strategy and its impact on the training cost of SWAMP.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of SWAMP to larger datasets and more complex network architectures, which could limit its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind using multiple particles in SWAMP and how the weights of these particles are determined?
- How does SWAMP compare to other pruning methods, particularly RigL, in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of SWAMP and how these might impact its practical application?
- How does SWAMP scale to larger datasets and more complex network architectures, and what are the potential challenges in implementing SWAMP in these scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the training time and memory usage of SWAMP compared to other pruning methods?
