 **Summary:**
The paper explores the challenges of causal discovery in temporally aggregated data, focusing on the linear and non-linear cases. It demonstrates that aggregation can lead to significant errors in causal inference, particularly when the aggregation factor is large. The paper provides theoretical analysis and simulation experiments to support its findings, showing that the results from aggregated data may not align with the true causal relationships. The study highlights the need for caution in interpreting causal discovery outcomes from aggregated data and suggests that more advanced algorithms may be necessary to handle the complexities introduced by aggregation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of causal discovery, focusing on the challenges posed by temporally aggregated data.
- The theoretical analysis provided is solid and well-supported by simulation experiments, enhancing the credibility of the findings.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for academic publications.
- The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of the theoretical conditions necessary for accurate causal discovery from temporally aggregated data, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper addresses a critical issue that is often overlooked in the literature, highlighting the potential for misleading results when using aggregated data for causal discovery.

**Weaknesses:**
