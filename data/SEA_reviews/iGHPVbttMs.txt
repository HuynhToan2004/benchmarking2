 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to finding Nash equilibria in non-cooperative games by focusing on the equivalence between a complete set of cyclical strategies and the support set of a Mixed Strategy Nash Equilibrium (MSNE). The authors propose a new algorithm, Graph-based Nash Equilibrium Search (GraphNES), which leverages this equivalence to find Nash equilibria more efficiently than existing methods. The paper presents theoretical proofs and empirical validations, showing that GraphNES can discover Nash equilibria in games like Connect4 and Naruto Mobile more effectively than previous methods. However, concerns were raised about the clarity and completeness of the proofs, the novelty of the approach, and the empirical evaluation's scope.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in game theory and artificial intelligence, focusing on the efficient discovery of Nash equilibria in non-cooperative games.
- The authors provide a clear and comprehensive literature review, effectively contextualizing their work within existing research.
- The paper introduces a novel approach that equates a complete set of cyclical strategies with the support set of a Mixed Strategy Nash Equilibrium (MSNE), which is both theoretically interesting and practically useful.
- The proposed algorithm, Graph-based Nash Equilibrium Search (GraphNES), shows promising results in empirical evaluations, demonstrating its effectiveness in finding Nash equilibria in games like Connect4 and Naruto Mobile.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The proof of Theorem 4.1 is not clear and lacks formal definitions for key concepts such as "complete set" and "support set," which are crucial for understanding the theorem's implications.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the novelty of the approach, particularly how it differs from existing works such as those by Akin (1980) and Biggar & Shames (2023), which also explore the relationship between cyclical strategies and Nash equilibria.
- The empirical evaluation is limited to a few games, which does not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method across different types of games.
- The paper suffers from several typographical and formatting errors, which detract from its professional presentation.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in more complex environments like StarCraft.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and assumptions used in the proof of Theorem 4.1, particularly the concept of a "complete set" and its implications for the theorem's conclusions?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing works that have also explored the relationship between cyclical strategies and Nash equilibria, such as those by Akin (1980) and Biggar & Shames (2023)?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications and effectiveness of the Graph-based Nash Equilibrium Search (GraphNES) algorithm in different types of games?
- How does the proposed method handle games where the Nash equilibrium is not unique, and what are the implications of this for the algorithm's performance and the stability of the equilibrium solutions it identifies?
- Could the authors address the concerns regarding the clarity and completeness of the proofs, and consider revising the paper to improve its presentation and formal rigor?
