 **Summary:**
The paper presents a novel approach to learning stochastic planning models, focusing on the distribution of effects for each action, which is a generalization of prior work. The method employs a polynomial time and sample complexity algorithm to learn these models from example trajectories, ensuring safety by only permitting actions in states where the model's accuracy is guaranteed. The paper also introduces a method for learning preconditions and effects using a method of moments, aiming to ensure the accuracy and completeness of the learned models. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, unclear presentation, and lack of empirical evaluation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of safe learning of planning models in stochastic environments.
- The approach is novel, utilizing tensor decomposition to ensure the uniqueness of the learned model, which is a significant departure from previous methods.
- The theoretical results are robust, providing strong guarantees of safety and approximate completeness for the learned models.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The methodology is innovative, particularly in the use of tensor decomposition to ensure the uniqueness of the learned model, which is a significant departure from previous methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation from prior work, particularly in the novelty of the method and the theoretical results presented.
- The presentation of the paper is difficult to follow, with excessive use of mathematical notation and unclear explanations in some sections.
- There is a lack of empirical evaluation, which is crucial for validating the claims made about the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the learned models, such as their generalizability to new domains or their scalability to larger, more complex problems.
- The paper suffers from several typographical and formatting errors, which could detract from its professional quality.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of this work compared to prior studies, particularly in terms of novelty and theoretical advancements?
- How does the paper address the issue of scalability, especially in terms of the number of fluents and actions?
- What are the practical implications of the learned models, and how do they compare to existing methods in terms of generalizability and scalability?
- Could the authors provide a clearer explanation of the algorithm and its components, perhaps with additional examples or illustrations to aid understanding?
- How does the paper ensure that the learned models are not overly conservative or overly permissive, and what are the potential consequences of these biases in practical applications?
