 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel variant of the SEIR epidemiological model named SEPAI3R3O, which incorporates additional compartments to model pre-symptomatic and asymptomatic individuals, and uses genetic algorithms to optimize the model's parameters. The model is applied to real-world data from Recife, Brazil, to estimate key epidemiological parameters and predict disease dynamics. The paper claims that this approach enhances the accuracy of disease spread predictions and provides valuable insights for public health decision-making. However, the novelty of the model and the methodology is questioned, with concerns about the lack of comparison with existing models and the clarity of the methodological contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides clear explanations of the model and its application.
- The inclusion of additional compartments in the SEIR model to better capture disease dynamics is a significant contribution.
- The application of the model to real-world data from Recife, Brazil, demonstrates its practical utility in public health decision-making.
- The use of genetic algorithms to optimize model parameters is a novel approach, enhancing the accuracy of disease spread predictions.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions and explanations of the SEPAI3R3O model and the application of genetic algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the model is limited, as it is a simple extension of the SEIR model with additional compartments, which have been previously used in other models.
- The methodological contributions are not clearly articulated, particularly the role and effectiveness of the genetic algorithm in optimizing model parameters.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other epidemiological models, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the model's performance.
- There is a lack of detailed justification for the choice of parameters and their impact on model outcomes, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the model, such as the assumptions of homogeneous mixing and well-mixed populations, which may not reflect real-world conditions.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the computational complexity or the sensitivity of the model to different initial conditions and parameter values.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of the SEPAI3R3O model and how it differs from existing models in terms of its methodological contributions and epidemiological insights?
- How were the initial conditions and parameter values for the genetic algorithm chosen, and what impact do these choices have on the model outcomes?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other epidemiological models, particularly in terms of model performance and the ability to capture disease dynamics accurately?
- How sensitive is the model to different initial conditions and parameter values, and what are the implications of these sensitivities for model predictions and public health decision-making?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the model, such as the assumptions of homogeneous mixing and well-mixed populations, and how these limitations might affect the model's applicability and accuracy in different settings?
