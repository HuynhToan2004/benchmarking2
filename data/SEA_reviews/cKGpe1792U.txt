 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel gradient leakage attack named RGLA, which leverages a generative feature inversion model to recover high-resolution data from gradients, addressing the challenges of duplicate labels in federated learning. RGLA optimizes the model output logits to obtain feature maps and then inverts these to reconstruct the original data. The methodology is validated against existing attacks and defenses, demonstrating its effectiveness in various scenarios. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty in the core idea, the absence of a comprehensive evaluation against more recent attacks and defenses, and the limited discussion on potential societal impacts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the critical issue of data privacy in federated learning (FL) by proposing a novel gradient leakage attack (RGLA) that effectively recovers high-resolution data from gradients, which is applicable in realistic FL scenarios.
- The methodology of RGLA is innovative, utilizing a generative feature inversion model to invert feature maps, which is a significant advancement over existing methods.
- The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and provides a detailed explanation of the methodology, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- Extensive experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of RGLA in reconstructing images with high resolution and large batch sizes, while considering duplicate labels.
- The paper includes an ablation study that explores the functionality of different optimization objectives, providing insights into the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of RGLA, particularly in scenarios where the batch size is larger than the number of classes plus one.
- There is a significant omission of recent attacks and defenses in the experimental evaluation, which could have provided a more comprehensive comparison and validation of RGLA.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed attack, which is a critical aspect of security research.
- The paper's contribution is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods such as FGLA, with the main difference being the use of the Moore-Penrose inverse method for inverting matrices.
- The paper's experimental setup and results lack clarity and detail, particularly in the explanation of the dataset used and the specifics of the experimental conditions.
- The paper's motivation and the necessity of the proposed attack are not convincingly justified, particularly in scenarios where privacy-preserving mechanisms are already in place.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancy in the experimental results, specifically the significant drop in performance when the proportion of duplicate label data in the batch increases?
- How does the proposed attack perform when the batch size is larger than the number of classes plus one?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of RGLA with existing attacks and defenses, including the most recent methodologies?
- How does RGLA handle the potential negative societal impacts, particularly in terms of data privacy and security?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup and results, including the specifics of the dataset used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does RGLA compare to other attacks that do not rely on the ground-truth label, such as those that use synthetic labels or other methods for label recovery?
