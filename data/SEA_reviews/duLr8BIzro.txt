 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GECO, a novel graph attention replacement that leverages global convolution to capture both local and long-range dependencies in large graphs. This approach aims to improve efficiency by reducing the quadratic complexity associated with traditional graph transformers, which is a significant barrier to scalability. GECO is designed to operate with quasilinear time complexity, making it suitable for handling large-scale graphs. The paper presents extensive experimental results demonstrating GECO's effectiveness in terms of accuracy and scalability, particularly on large-scale datasets. Despite its strengths, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, as it heavily relies on existing models like Hyena, and the paper's contribution is seen as primarily an application rather than a significant innovation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-understand content, and the motivation behind the research is well-articulated.
- The proposed GECO model is simple, intuitive, and effective, with extensive experiments conducted to validate its performance.
- The GECO model is efficient and scalable, with a quasilinear time complexity that makes it suitable for large-scale graph applications.
- The model's design, including the use of positional encodings, local propagation, and global convolution, is well-explained and supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the GECO model, comparing it with a range of existing models and demonstrating its effectiveness across various benchmarks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the GECO model, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for large-scale graph applications.
- The novelty of the GECO model is questionable, as it heavily relies on existing models like Hyena, with only minor modifications for graph applications.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis on the expressive power of the GECO model, which could help in understanding its limitations and potential.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the GECO model, such as its sensitivity to permutation and the potential for overfitting in certain scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the design choices of the GECO model, particularly the decision to use a convolution-based approach over other possible methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational resources required for large-scale graph applications using the GECO model?
- How does the GECO model compare in terms of expressive power to other graph attention models like Graphormer and GraphGPS?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for combining the GECO model with other graph attention models to potentially enhance its performance?
- In the ablation studies, why was the decision made to use a convolution-based approach instead of other possible methods?
- How does the GECO model perform when applied to very large graphs, such as those with billions of nodes?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the GECO model, particularly in terms of its sensitivity to permutation and potential for overfitting?
