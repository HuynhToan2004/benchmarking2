 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Prompting Language-Informed Distribution (PLID), for compositional zero-shot learning (CZSL) using a large language model (LLM) to generate class-specific distributions. This method enhances the diversity and informativeness of the prompts, addressing the limitations of existing methods such as CSP and ProDA. The PLID model uses these distributions to prompt the CLIP model, enhancing the model's ability to recognize unseen compositional concepts. The paper also proposes a visual-language primitive decomposition (VLPD) module and a stochastic logit mixup strategy to fuse decisions from compositional and primitive logit spaces. The method is evaluated on three CZSL datasets and shows superior performance compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, PLID, which effectively leverages large language models (LLMs) to generate class-specific distributions, enhancing the diversity and informativeness of prompts.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the proposed techniques, such as the VLPD module and stochastic logit mixup strategy.
- The paper is well-written, with detailed explanations of the methodology and experimental results, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing significant improvements over existing methods in closed-world and open-world settings.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the proposed method, including ablation studies and qualitative results, which help in understanding the contributions of different components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the number of parameters and training costs, which could affect the fairness of the comparisons.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The use of LLMs to generate class descriptions might introduce additional computational costs and require careful handling of noisy or incorrect class descriptions.
- The paper does not thoroughly discuss the limitations of the CLIP model, which is a critical component of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of bias and fairness.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and impact of the "a photo of" prompt in the PLID method?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for noisy or incorrect class descriptions generated by the LLM?
- In terms of computational efficiency, how does the proposed method compare to existing methods, particularly in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for bias in the LLM-generated class descriptions?
- Can you provide more details on the training and inference times for the proposed method compared to existing methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that leverage LLMs for prompting, such as those mentioned in the review?
