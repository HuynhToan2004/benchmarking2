 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LPTM, a transformer-based model designed for pre-training on various time-series datasets, aiming to address the challenges of segmenting time-series data for different domains. The model uses a segmentation module to adapt to different datasets, and two self-supervised learning tasks are proposed to enhance model performance. The authors claim that LPTM outperforms state-of-the-art models in terms of accuracy and efficiency, requiring fewer training data and steps. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the methodology, and the generalizability of the results across different datasets and domains.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant challenge of adapting to different datasets with varying segmentation needs, which is crucial for time-series analysis tasks.
- The proposed method, LPTM, is well-motivated and addresses the issue of segmenting time-series data effectively, which is a critical aspect of time-series analysis.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of a transformer-based architecture for time-series data pre-training is highlighted as an innovative approach, which could potentially enhance the understanding and prediction of time-series data.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental results, which are supported by a comprehensive evaluation and ablation studies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and justification of the segmentation problem, which is crucial for understanding the motivation behind the proposed method.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it seems to heavily rely on existing techniques such as random masking and last token masking, which have been previously used in other models.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodology section, particularly in the explanation of how the segmentation module operates and how it is trained.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process, such as the specifics of the training data and the pre-training and fine-tuning stages.
- The generalizability of the results is limited due to the use of a small number of datasets, which may not adequately represent the diversity of time-series data.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation, including a broader range of datasets and more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art models.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the segmentation problem and provide a clearer definition of the segmentation module?
- How does the segmentation module ensure that it adapts to different datasets effectively?
- How is the segmentation module trained, and what are the specifics of this training process?
- Why was the GRU chosen over other models like LSTM or RNN for the segmentation module?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art models in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training data and the specifics of the pre-training and fine-tuning stages?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of segmentation in different domains, particularly in domains where segmentation might be more complex or varied?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of datasets and the rationale behind their selection for the experiments?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to datasets with varying segmentation needs, and what are the implications of this for the generalizability of the results?
