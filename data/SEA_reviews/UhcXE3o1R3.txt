 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Apollo, a modular framework designed to leverage the expertise of multiple pre-trained models across different modalities (vision, audio, and language) to perform complex multi-modal tasks without relying on prompt engineering or multi-modal training. This approach enables decentralized command execution, allowing each model to contribute and benefit from the expertise of others. The framework is demonstrated to outperform semi-supervised state-of-the-art models in tasks such as stylized image captioning and audio-aware image captioning, showcasing its effectiveness in zero-shot settings. The paper also introduces a novel task, audio-aware image captioning, where an image and audio are given and the task is to generate text that describes the image within the context of the provided audio.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel modular framework that leverages the expertise of different foundation models over different modalities and domains, enabling decentralized command execution.
- The framework allows each model to contribute and benefit from the expertise of other models, which is a significant advancement in the field of multimodal tasks.
- The paper demonstrates the framework's effectiveness in zero-shot settings, which is a notable achievement as it avoids costly training, data collection, and prompt engineering.
- The approach is applicable to a variety of foundation models, including audio and vision, and is not limited to language models, which enhances its versatility.
- The paper includes a comprehensive experimental evaluation, showcasing the framework's performance in tasks such as stylized image captioning and audio-aware image captioning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could help in understanding the framework's performance relative to existing solutions.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process, such as the number of training steps or the specifics of the loss function used.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the selection and role of different expert models in the framework, which could impact the effectiveness of the approach.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed approach, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities and challenges.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other methods in terms of computational resources and inference speed, which are crucial factors in practical applications.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the framework's performance across a broader range of tasks and datasets, which could strengthen the claim of its general applicability and effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison of the proposed framework with other state-of-the-art methods, including a discussion of the framework's advantages and disadvantages compared to these methods?
- How does the framework handle the selection and role of different expert models? Is there a systematic approach to choosing the most appropriate models for a given task?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process, such as the number of training steps and the specifics of the loss function used?
- How does the framework perform in terms of computational resources and inference speed?
- Could the authors expand the evaluation of the framework to include a broader range of tasks and datasets, and discuss the implications of these results for the general applicability and effectiveness of the approach?
