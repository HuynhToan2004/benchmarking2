 **Summary:**
The paper explores the challenges in fine-tuning large-scale text-to-image models, specifically focusing on the issues of text-image alignment and alignment drift. It proposes a novel method using contrastive learning to optimize text feature representations, aiming to enhance text-image alignment and reduce alignment drift. The approach is tested on specialized datasets, demonstrating improvements in fine-tuning performance. Despite these strengths, the paper is critiqued for its limited scope in terms of dataset diversity and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with other fine-tuning methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of text-to-image generation, focusing on the alignment between text and image features, which is crucial for enhancing the quality of generated images.
- The introduction of contrastive learning for feature distribution optimization is a novel approach that effectively optimizes textual feature representations during generation, improving text-to-image alignment and reducing alignment drift.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its implications.
- The use of CLIP for evaluation is a sensible choice, as it provides a robust and comprehensive assessment of the text-image alignment.
- The method is described as plug-and-play, which makes it easily integrable into existing controllable generation methods, enhancing its applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claims regarding the limitations of direct fine-tuning and the inability to effectively improve text-image alignment and reduce alignment drift are not convincingly supported by empirical evidence.
- The experimental results, particularly the FID scores, do not convincingly demonstrate superiority over existing methods like Dreambooth, which also addresses the alignment between text and image features.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other fine-tuning methods, particularly those that have been shown to improve alignment in text-to-image generation.
- The use of CLIP for evaluation is criticized for being potentially biased due to its similarity to the Stable-Diffusion model, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The paper's focus on specialized datasets raises concerns about its generalizability to other datasets and its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning its applicability to datasets with minimal inter-class variances and its computational efficiency.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancy between the FID scores reported in the paper and those reported in other studies, particularly those involving Dreambooth?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency with other fine-tuning methods, especially in scenarios where computational resources are limited?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, such as the number of epochs and learning rates used, to better understand the method's impact on the model's performance?
- Given the focus on specialized datasets, how does the proposed method perform on more general datasets, and what implications does this have for its practical applicability?
- Could the authors discuss the potential biases introduced by using CLIP for evaluation and consider using alternative evaluation methods to ensure the reliability of the results?
