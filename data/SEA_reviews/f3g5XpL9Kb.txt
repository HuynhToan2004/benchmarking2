 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel metric, LiDAR, for evaluating the quality of representations in Joint Embedding (JE) architectures, specifically designed to measure the effectiveness of self-supervised learning (SSL) models. LiDAR builds upon the RankMe metric by incorporating a linear discriminant analysis (LDA) matrix, which is claimed to better capture the information content required for solving the SSL task. The metric is evaluated across various SSL methods, including contrastive, regularized, and masking-based approaches, and is shown to correlate well with downstream performance, particularly in hyperparameter selection. The paper also discusses the limitations of LiDAR and suggests potential improvements, such as its integration as a loss signal during pretraining.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and well-defined contributions.
- The proposed LiDAR metric is simple, intuitive, and shows significant improvements over RankMe in evaluating the quality of learned representations.
- The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of LiDAR in identifying optimal hyperparameters for SSL models, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper provides a theoretical justification for the use of LDA in evaluating SSL models, which adds a strong theoretical foundation to the proposed method.
- The proposed LiDAR metric is shown to be effective in various SSL methods, including contrastive, regularized, and masking-based approaches, indicating its broad applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of LiDAR, particularly in scenarios where it may not correlate well with downstream performance, such as in VICReg with high dimensional embeddings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational overhead associated with calculating the LiDAR metric, which might be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper primarily focuses on linear probing and does not explore the use of LiDAR as a loss signal during pretraining, which could be a valuable direction for future research.
- The experiments are somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on ImageNet-1K and not exploring other datasets or different SSL methods, which could strengthen the claims of the paper.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the LiDAR metric, particularly in high-dimensional embedding spaces, which could limit its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of LiDAR and how these limitations might affect its practical applicability?
- How does the computational overhead of calculating the LiDAR metric compare to other metrics, and what are the practical implications of this overhead?
- Is there a possibility to integrate LiDAR as a loss signal during pretraining, and what would be the expected outcomes of such an approach?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of datasets and SSL methods used in the experiments, and consider expanding the scope to include a broader range of datasets and methods?
- How does the LiDAR metric perform in high-dimensional embedding spaces, and what steps could be taken to address the scalability issues?
