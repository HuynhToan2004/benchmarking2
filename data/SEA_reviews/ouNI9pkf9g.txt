 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the privacy risks associated with sharing generative models that are trained on private data, specifically focusing on the potential for re-identification of training samples. It introduces a method for estimating the upper bound of the likelihood of reproducing identifiable training images during the sampling process, using a synthetic anatomic fingerprint (SAF) to enhance the model's sensitivity to privacy concerns. The methodology involves training a classifier to detect SAFs and a second classifier to identify samples from the training set. Experiments are conducted using various datasets, including MedMNIST and CelebA-HQ, to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited scope in terms of dataset diversity and model complexity, and for not thoroughly addressing the practical implications of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a timely and important issue regarding the potential risks associated with sharing generative models trained on private data, which is of significant relevance given the increasing popularity of these models.
- The introduction of a synthetic anatomic fingerprint (SAF) as a tool for enhancing the model's sensitivity to privacy concerns is a novel and interesting approach, adding a new dimension to the analysis of generative models.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations that aid in understanding complex concepts, and the use of figures and equations effectively illustrates the methodology.
- The authors have provided a clear and detailed explanation of their methodology, which is supported by a thorough experimental evaluation, including a comprehensive set of experiments across various datasets and models.
- The paper addresses a critical issue in the field of generative models, which is the potential for privacy breaches through the sharing of models trained on private data, and provides a valuable contribution to the ongoing discourse on data privacy in machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method across a diverse range of datasets, particularly in terms of the complexity and variability of the data. This could lead to overestimation of the method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The use of a synthetic SAF may not accurately reflect the privacy risks associated with real-world scenarios, where the SAFs could be more complex and varied.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implications of the findings, particularly in terms of how the proposed method could be used to mitigate privacy risks in real-world applications.
- The methodology and experimental setup are limited in scope, with a focus on specific datasets and models that may not be representative of the broader landscape of generative models.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous theoretical analysis and a broader discussion of related work to position the research more effectively within the existing literature.
- There are concerns regarding the generalizability of the results due to the limited diversity in datasets and models used in the experiments.
