 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multitask fine-tuning framework, UniBoost, which leverages unsupervised pre-trained unimodal models to enhance zero-shot performance in various vision-language tasks. UniBoost aligns or fuses vision and language space on a variety of tasks with supervision, allowing for multimodal alignment and domain/task adaptation. The framework is evaluated on several benchmarks, including PASCAL-5i, COCO-20i, and VQA, demonstrating improvements over existing methods. The approach utilizes separate pre-trained vision and language models, which can be adapted to specific downstream tasks, making it a promising alternative to jointly pre-trained models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its applications.
- The proposed multitask fine-tuning framework, UniBoost, is novel and effective, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods in various vision-language tasks.
- The framework leverages unsupervised pre-trained unimodal models, which can be adapted to specific downstream tasks, providing a flexible and efficient solution.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments across different vision-language tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of unsupervised pre-training for vision-language tasks is a promising approach that can potentially reduce the dependency on labeled data.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the number of parameters and computational efficiency.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a few benchmarks and datasets, which may not fully demonstrate its generalization ability.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of domain gaps between pre-training datasets and downstream tasks, which could affect the performance of the model.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
