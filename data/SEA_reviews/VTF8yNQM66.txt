 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SWE-bench, a novel evaluation framework designed to assess the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) in software engineering tasks. SWE-bench utilizes a dataset of 2,294 real-world GitHub issues and corresponding pull requests across 12 popular Python repositories, which are used to evaluate the performance of LLMs in code editing. The framework includes a benchmark dataset, SWE-benchmark, and two fine-tuned models, SWE-Llama 7b and 13b, based on the CodeLlama model. The evaluation reveals that even the most advanced LLMs can only solve a small percentage of the tasks, highlighting the need for further research and development in this area.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a timely and significant problem by evaluating the performance of large language models (LLMs) in software engineering tasks, specifically focusing on the limitations of current models in handling complex, real-world software engineering problems.
- The dataset and benchmark introduced in the paper are robust, diverse, and representative, providing a comprehensive evaluation of LLMs in software engineering scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a detailed analysis of the limitations of current LLMs and potential avenues for future research.
- The evaluation of LLMs on real-world software engineering tasks is crucial for understanding their practical applicability and for guiding future research and development.
- The paper provides a realistic setting for evaluating LLMs, including a large codebase and a detailed description of the issue, which reflects the challenges of real-world software engineering problems.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the methodology and the results, and includes a detailed appendix that provides additional insights and analyses.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the specific software engineering tasks evaluated by the benchmark, which could help in understanding the scope and complexity of the tasks.
- The evaluation metrics used in the paper are not clearly defined, which could affect the interpretation of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of using LLMs for software engineering tasks, which could be a significant concern for the ethical implications of such applications.
