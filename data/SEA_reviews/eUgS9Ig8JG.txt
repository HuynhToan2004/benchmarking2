 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a scalable simplicial-aware neural network (SaNN) for higher-order graph representation learning. SaNN precomputes features of simplices by aggregating features of their respective upper, lower, boundary, and coboundary adjacent simplices, which significantly reduces computational time and memory requirements. Theoretical proofs demonstrate that SaNN is more expressive than the Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL) graph isomorphism test and as expressive as the simplicial Weisfeiler-Lehman (SWL) test. The paper also presents empirical evidence of SaNN's effectiveness in various applications, such as trajectory prediction, simplicial closure prediction, and graph classification. Despite its scalability, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing models, and the theoretical proofs are criticized for not adequately addressing the computational complexities involved.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the scalability issue of simplicial neural networks (SNNs) by proposing a scalable simplicial-aware neural network (SaNN) that precomputes features of simplices, which significantly reduces computational time and memory requirements.
- Theoretical proofs demonstrate that SaNN is more expressive than the Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL) graph isomorphism test and as expressive as the simplicial Weisfeiler-Lehman (SWL) test.
- The paper provides empirical evidence of SaNN's effectiveness in various applications, including trajectory prediction, simplicial closure prediction, and graph classification.
- The proposed method is scalable and efficient, with a constant run-time and memory requirements independent of the size of the simplicial complex and the density of interactions.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing models such as SIGN, which also precomputes features of simplices.
- The proofs in the paper are criticized for not adequately addressing the computational complexities involved, particularly in the proofs of Theorems 4.1 and 4.2.
- The paper's contribution is seen as limited, as the proposed method primarily relies on precomputing features of simplices, which may not be novel or groundbreaking.
- The empirical evidence provided is not convincing, with the paper not demonstrating a significant advantage over existing methods in terms of performance or computational efficiency.
- The paper's theoretical claims are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence, and the proofs do not adequately address the computational complexities involved.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to existing models like SIGN?
- How does the proof of Theorem 4.1 address the computational complexities involved, especially in the context of the SWL test?
- In the proof of Theorem 4.1, can the authors provide more details on how the SWL test is used to distinguish non-isomorphic graphs with a complex-clique lifting?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations on how the proposed method precomputes features of simplices and how this impacts the computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and expressive power to other simplicial neural networks, particularly those that do not rely on precomputing features?
