 **Summary:**
The paper explores the problem of congestion in online marketplaces where consumers make decisions based on limited information about items. It proposes a differentiable proxy for welfare that allows for end-to-end training on consumer choice data, aiming to optimize representations that reduce congestion and improve social welfare. The authors argue that this approach can help manage congestion by controlling the information presented to users, which is crucial in settings where prices are not controlled. The paper presents theoretical conditions for when decongestion promotes welfare and validates the approach through extensive experiments on both synthetic and real data. However, the paper's assumptions and the practical applicability of its findings have been critiqued, with concerns about the generalizability of the results and the clarity of the methodology.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a timely and relevant issue of congestion in online marketplaces, where users make decisions based on limited information about items.
- The authors propose a novel differentiable proxy for welfare, which can be trained end-to-end on consumer choice data, potentially improving social welfare.
- The paper provides theoretical conditions for when decongestion promotes welfare, which is an interesting and useful contribution to the field.
- The experiments conducted are extensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can handle high-dimensional data and achieve high welfare.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and illustrative figures that aid in understanding the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper makes several assumptions that may not hold in real-world scenarios, such as the assumption that users are rational and make choices based on the best information available, which is not always the case.
- The methodology used in the paper, particularly the use of a differentiable proxy for welfare, is not clearly explained, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the approach.
- The paper's experimental setup is limited to small-scale datasets, which may not generalize well to larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of soft congestion, which is more prevalent in digital marketplaces and could have significant impacts on social welfare.
- There is a lack of discussion on the practical implications of the findings, particularly how the proposed method could be applied in real-world marketplaces.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to a specific setting and does not explore how the results might apply in more general settings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the differentiable proxy for welfare is computed and how it is used to optimize the representation?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of soft congestion, which is more prevalent in digital marketplaces?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate how the method could be applied in real-world marketplaces?
- How do the authors justify the assumptions made in their theoretical analysis, particularly the assumption of rational decision-making by users?
- Could the authors discuss the implications of their findings for other types of marketplaces, such as those dealing with digital goods?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for managing congestion in online marketplaces?
