 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedJETs, a novel federated learning method that utilizes a mixture of experts (MoE) framework to personalize models for clients without requiring labeled data. This approach allows the selection of the most relevant expert for each client based on their data distribution, thus enhancing the model's performance on both seen and unseen clients. The methodology includes a pretrained common expert model that aids in the routing of input to the most relevant expert, and a gating function that dynamically selects the most suitable experts for each client. The paper presents experiments on CIFAR10 and CIFAR100 datasets, demonstrating significant improvements in accuracy and zero-shot performance compared to existing methods. However, the paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts, and the experimental setup and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods are not thoroughly discussed.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to personalizing models in federated learning (FL) settings, which is both innovative and well-explained.
- The methodology is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the challenges and potential solutions, making the paper accessible and understandable.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in terms of accuracy and zero-shot performance.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the proposed methodologies and their impacts.
- The use of anchor clients and the gating function are highlighted as significant contributions, with potential for broader applications in the field of federated learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of the research.
- The experimental setup and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods are not thoroughly discussed, which could lead to questions about the fairness and validity of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of model drift, which is a significant challenge in federated learning.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definitions and roles of different components, such as the common expert and the anchor client, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing methods in terms of computational and communication costs, which are critical factors in federated learning.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the privacy implications of the proposed method, which is a significant concern in federated learning.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and impact of the anchor clients in the proposed method? How do they influence the selection and specialization of experts?
- How does the proposed method handle model drift, especially in scenarios where clients have varying data distributions?
- What are the computational and communication costs associated with the proposed method, and how do they compare with existing methods?
- How does the proposed method ensure privacy in federated learning settings, particularly in terms of data protection and communication security?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the number of clients and the specifics of the data partitioning?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods in terms of end-to-end performance, especially in scenarios where the number of clients and data partitions varies?
- Could you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
