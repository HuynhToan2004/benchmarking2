 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Matrix Function Neural Networks (MFNs), a novel architecture designed to model non-local interactions in graphs. MFNs utilize matrix functions to capture long-range dependencies, leveraging a resolvent expansion for computational efficiency. The architecture is evaluated on various datasets, including cumulenes and ZINC, demonstrating superior performance over existing models. The authors argue that MFNs address limitations of existing models by allowing for non-local interactions and potentially linear scaling with system size. However, concerns are raised about the scalability and complexity of the model, particularly in terms of parameter count and computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to modeling non-local interactions in graphs using Matrix Function Neural Networks (MFNs), which can potentially scale linearly with the number of nodes in the graph.
- The MFN architecture is designed to capture long-range dependencies, which is a significant advancement over traditional message-passing neural networks (MPNNs) that typically suffer from oversmoothing and oversquashing.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a solid theoretical foundation.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing models on various datasets.
- The use of resolvent expansion for parameterizing matrix functions is innovative and could be a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other non-local methods, which is crucial for understanding the unique advantages of the MFN architecture.
- The scalability of the MFN architecture is questionable, as it requires learning a matrix for every edge in the graph, which could lead to high computational and parameter costs, especially for large graphs.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive analysis of the computational complexity and memory requirements of the MFN architecture, which is essential for understanding its practical applicability.
- There are concerns about the reproducibility of the results due to the lack of availability of the code and datasets used in the experiments.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential drawbacks of the MFN architecture, such as its potential for oversmoothing and its scalability issues.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the computational complexity and memory requirements of the MFN architecture, particularly how these scale with the size of the graph?
- How does the MFN architecture compare in terms of parameter count and computational efficiency with other non-local methods, such as global attention MPNNs?
- Can you provide a more detailed comparison with other models like SpookyNet and MACE, particularly in terms of the number of parameters and computational efficiency?
- How does the MFN architecture handle the issue of oversmoothing, which is a common problem in message-passing neural networks?
- Could you clarify the notation and definitions used in the paper, particularly in Section 4.1 and the equations involving matrix functions and the group action?
- How does the MFN architecture ensure the equivariance of the matrix functions with respect to the group action, as claimed in the paper?
