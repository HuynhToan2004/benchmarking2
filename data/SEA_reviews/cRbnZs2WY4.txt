 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a self-supervised data cleaning method named SelfClean, aimed at enhancing the quality of datasets by addressing issues like irrelevant samples, near duplicates, and label errors. The approach utilizes latent space learned through self-supervised learning to detect and remove these issues, thereby improving the dataset's reliability for training models. The method is evaluated on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in identifying and removing noise. Despite its strengths in methodology and experimental evaluation, concerns about the clarity of the method's application and the potential for overfitting due to the self-supervised nature of the data cleaning process are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel self-supervised data cleaning method, which is both innovative and well-executed.
- The methodology is clearly articulated, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The paper is well-structured, with a logical flow of ideas and a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The authors have provided a thorough evaluation of the method, demonstrating its effectiveness in identifying and removing noise from datasets.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and concise presentation of the content, which aids in understanding the complex concepts presented.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how the self-supervised learning model is trained, which could lead to confusion about the method's implementation.
- There is a concern that the method might overfit the data due to its self-supervised nature, which could limit its applicability to different datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of datasets and its scalability.
- The paper could be improved by providing more detailed visualizations and examples to better illustrate the method's effectiveness and the types of noise it can detect.
- The paper's focus on specific types of noise (irrelevant samples, near duplicates, and label errors) may limit its applicability to other types of noise that could be present in datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the self-supervised learning model is trained, including the specifics of the training process and the types of data used?
- How does the method handle different types of noise beyond the specific types mentioned in the paper?
- Can the authors provide more visualizations and examples to better illustrate the method's effectiveness and the types of noise it can detect?
- What are the specific limitations of the method, and how do these limitations affect its applicability to different types of datasets?
- How does the method scale with larger datasets, and what are the potential challenges in implementing it on a larger scale?
- Can the authors discuss the potential implications of the method's focus on specific types of noise, and how this might affect its broader applicability?
