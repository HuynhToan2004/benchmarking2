 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of non-uniform patch sampling for brain MRI super-resolution, focusing on improving the representation of fine-grained details in the cerebellum. The methodology involves altering the proportion of patches extracted from the cerebellum and the rest of the brain, aiming to enhance the model's performance in these regions. The paper employs two existing models, mDCSRN and mDCSRN-WGAN, to validate the approach, showing that it can lead to better reconstruction of the cerebellum. However, the paper's scope is limited by its use of only two models, and it lacks a comprehensive evaluation across a broader range of models and metrics. The method's effectiveness is also questioned due to its reliance on intuitive sampling methods rather than more sophisticated approaches.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of medical imaging, specifically focusing on the representation of fine-grained details in the cerebellum using non-uniform patch sampling.
- The methodology is simple, intuitive, and well-explained, making it accessible and understandable.
- The paper is well-written, with clear descriptions of the methods used and the results presented, which aids in reproducibility.
- The proposed method shows promise in improving the representation of the cerebellum in brain MRI super-resolution, which is a critical aspect for clinical diagnosis and research.
- The paper includes visual results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, which is a valuable addition to the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited by its use of only two models (mDCSRN and mDCSRN-WGAN), which may not be sufficient to validate the proposed method comprehensively.
- The evaluation metrics used are limited to PSNR, SSIM, and NRMSE, which do not adequately capture perceptual quality. More advanced metrics such as LPIPS could provide a more nuanced assessment.
- The method's effectiveness is questioned due to its reliance on intuitive sampling methods rather than more sophisticated approaches, which could potentially lead to suboptimal results.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing state-of-the-art methods, which could help contextualize the proposed method's contributions.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by including more detailed explanations and examples of the method's application, particularly in the methodology section.
- The method's generalizability to other medical imaging modalities or different brain regions is unclear, which limits its applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors consider using more advanced metrics such as LPIPS to evaluate the perceptual quality of the reconstructed images?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or visualizations of the method's application, particularly in the methodology section?
- How does the proposed method perform in other medical imaging modalities, such as CT or PET scans?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential of applying the proposed method to other brain regions or medical imaging tasks?
- Given the limitations in model selection and evaluation metrics, how do the authors plan to expand the validation of their method in future work?
