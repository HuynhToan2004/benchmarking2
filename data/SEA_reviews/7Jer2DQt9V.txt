 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the generalization capabilities of pre-trained graph neural networks (GNNs) in out-of-distribution (OOD) settings, specifically focusing on covariate and concept shifts. It employs a benchmark, PODGenGraph, to evaluate the performance of pre-trained models against various baselines and datasets. The study reveals that pre-trained models, despite their simplicity, can often outperform specialized methods designed for OOD scenarios. The paper also explores factors influencing OOD generalization, such as sample size, learning rates, and in-distribution performance. However, it faces criticism for its lack of novelty, limited evaluation scope, and insufficient comparative analysis with other graph pre-training methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of various graph pre-training strategies and their impact on out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization, which is crucial for advancing the field of graph representation learning.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments across diverse datasets, including molecular and general graph domains, and varying graph sizes, to assess the performance of pre-trained models under different distribution shifts.
- The paper presents a benchmark, PODGenGraph, which could potentially offer valuable insights for future works regarding the selection of pre-trained models and the development of more advanced methodologies for graph OOD.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the presentation of the results is clear and concise, which aids in understanding the findings.
- The study includes a thorough examination of the factors affecting the performance of pre-trained models in OOD settings, providing valuable insights for future research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it primarily focuses on evaluating pre-trained models without proposing new methods or approaches, which could limit its impact in advancing the field.
- The evaluation scope is limited to a few pre-training methods, which may not adequately represent the broader landscape of graph pre-training methods.
- The paper does not include a comparative analysis with other graph pre-training methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The paper's claims about the generalization capabilities of pre-trained models are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, particularly in the general graph domain.
- The paper's methodology and findings may not generalize well to other domains beyond molecular graphs, which could limit the applicability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and the potential negative societal impacts, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.
