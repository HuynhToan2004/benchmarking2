 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel two-stage data imputation framework aimed at enhancing classification performance by utilizing label information to optimize similarity relationships among data. This framework, which includes a kernel matrix imputation and a data imputation stage, is designed to handle high rates of missing data (up to 80%) effectively. The methodology, while innovative in its approach, is somewhat limited by its reliance on kernel methods and its evaluation on small datasets. The paper presents a clear problem statement and provides a detailed algorithmic solution, supported by experimental results that demonstrate the method's effectiveness in certain scenarios. However, concerns are raised about the scalability of the method and its applicability to more diverse datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a clear and well-structured problem statement, effectively communicating the motivation and significance of the research.
- The proposed two-stage data imputation framework is innovative, leveraging label information to optimize similarity relationships among data, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The methodology is supported by a detailed algorithmic solution, which is well-explained and easy to follow.
- Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the data imputation method significantly outperforms other methods.
- The paper provides a detailed algorithmic solution, which is easy to follow and supported by experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is limited in its evaluation, using only four small datasets, which may not adequately demonstrate the scalability or generalizability of the proposed method.
- The method's dependence on kernel methods may limit its applicability to other types of data, such as image data or time-series data.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the method may not perform optimally.
- The experimental results are not convincing, as the differences between the proposed method and other methods are not significant in some cases, and the method's performance is not consistently superior across all datasets.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the method would handle different types of missing data, such as categorical or continuous variables, which could affect the applicability of the method in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other advanced data imputation methods that use deep learning techniques, such as NeuMiss?
- Can the method be extended to handle categorical or continuous variables in addition to numerical data?
- How does the method handle different types of missing data, such as missing values in categorical variables?
- Could the authors provide more detailed information on the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of its ability to handle larger and more complex datasets?
- How does the method perform when the data is missing completely at random (MCAR) versus missing not at random (MNAR)?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method in more detail, particularly in scenarios where the method may not perform optimally?
