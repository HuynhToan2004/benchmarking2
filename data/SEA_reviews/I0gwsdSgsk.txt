 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Constant Memory Attention Block (CMAB) designed to address the memory inefficiencies in neural processes, particularly in meta-learning applications. The CMAB is designed to be permutation invariant, compute its output in constant memory, and perform updates in constant computation. This is achieved by using a combination of self-attention and cross-attention, which allows for efficient handling of large datasets. The authors also propose a variant of this block, CMANP-AND, which is not-diagonal, enabling the prediction of full covariance matrices. The paper presents empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of CMANP-AND in tasks such as image completion and meta-regression, showing significant improvements in memory efficiency over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, CMAB, which addresses the memory inefficiencies in neural processes, specifically in meta-learning applications.
- The proposed method, CMANP-AND, is memory efficient and achieves state-of-the-art results on popular NP benchmarks.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and the results.
- The introduction of CMAB, a novel attention mechanism, is a significant contribution to the field, offering potential for further research and applications.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology, including the use of constant memory and computation, which is a notable improvement over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of CMANP-AND, particularly in terms of the number of context datapoints and the computational efficiency.
- The empirical evaluation of the proposed method is limited, with only two datasets used for evaluation.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the computational efficiency of CMANP-AND with other methods, which could help in understanding the practical implications of using this method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the number of context datapoints and the computational efficiency.
