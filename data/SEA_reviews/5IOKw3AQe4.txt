 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a theoretical framework for dense contrastive learning (DCL), focusing on the patch-level positive-pair graph to analyze the performance of DCL. It establishes a theoretical foundation for DCL by decoupling image-level and patch-level supervision, characterizing the impact of different positive pair selection strategies on DCL performance. The authors propose two unsupervised metrics to guide the selection of positive pairs, validating these insights on both synthetic and real-world datasets. Theoretical analysis is provided to explain the trade-off between the quantity and correctness of positive pairs, and the paper includes experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for dense contrastive learning (DCL), which is the first of its kind to analyze DCL.
- The authors provide a clear and detailed theoretical analysis of DCL, including a characterization of how different positive pair selection strategies affect DCL performance.
- The paper includes empirical validation of the proposed understanding on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the trade-off between the quantity and correctness of positive pairs.
- Two unsupervised metrics are proposed to guide the selection of positive pairs in DCL, which are strongly correlated with model performance.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the theoretical framework and the experimental setup, making it accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of datasets and the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its theoretical framework with existing works, such as Moon et al. (2022), which could have provided a clearer differentiation of the contributions.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of the proposed metrics across different datasets and DCL methods.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the consistency of the dataset and the class of data augmentation, are not well-justified or explained, which could limit the applicability of the results.
- The paper does not include a discussion on how the theoretical framework could be adapted or extended to address the limitations of existing DCL methods, such as PixPro and PixContrast.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific differences between their theoretical framework and that of Moon et al. (2022)?
- How do the authors address the limitations of their theoretical framework, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of datasets and the generalizability of the results?
- In the experimental validation, how do the authors ensure that the proposed metrics are not biased towards specific DCL methods?
- Could the authors provide more details on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the consistency of the dataset and the class of data augmentation?
- How do the authors envision the theoretical framework being applied to address the limitations of existing DCL methods, such as PixPro and PixContrast?
