 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two methods, PERU-FFT and PERU-LoRA, aimed at merging multiple pre-trained models to reduce the need for training a new model from scratch. PERU-FFT involves merging task-specific models and pruning the differences to extract task-specific knowledge, while PERU-LoRA uses singular value decomposition to approximate LoRA matrices, which are then merged. These methods are evaluated on both computer vision and natural language processing tasks, showing that they can achieve performance comparable to single-task models while reducing parameter usage. The experiments demonstrate that these methods outperform existing merging methods and can be integrated with existing multi-task learning algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem by proposing methods to merge multiple pre-trained models, which is beneficial for scenarios where task-specific data is not available.
- The proposed methods are simple, intuitive, and easy to understand, making them accessible to a broader audience.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform existing merging methods, showing a significant improvement in performance.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the proposed methods are flexible and can be integrated with various existing multi-task learning algorithms.
- The use of singular value decomposition to approximate LoRA matrices is innovative and effective, as shown by the experimental results.
- The methodology is simple, requiring only the average of the parameters of all task models, which is straightforward and effective.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of their applicability to more complex tasks or models.
- The experimental results are not comprehensive enough, focusing only on a limited number of tasks and models, which might not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the impact of the rank q in the proposed method and the computational complexity introduced by the additional matrix multiplications.
- The novelty of the proposed methods is questionable as they seem to be a combination of existing techniques such as merging and pruning, which might not be considered a significant advancement.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in the field, which could help in positioning the proposed methods more effectively.
- The experimental results are not convincing, particularly the comparisons with single-task models, which are crucial for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the limitations of the proposed methods and discuss potential future research directions to address these limitations?
- How does the rank q in the proposed method impact the performance, and what are the implications of using a higher rank?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in the field, including a discussion on the computational complexity introduced by the additional matrix multiplications?
- What are the implications of using a higher rank in the proposed method, and how does it affect the performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the experimental results, particularly the comparisons with single-task models, and clarify any discrepancies or inconsistencies in the results?
