 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PolySRL, a novel approach for learning stoichiometric representations of materials that incorporates structural information and accounts for polymorphism. This method leverages a probabilistic approach to model the distribution of stoichiometric representations, which is crucial for capturing the uncertainty associated with different crystal structures. Extensive experiments are conducted across various datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of PolySRL in predicting physical properties of materials. The approach uses a combination of graph neural networks and contrastive learning to encode both stoichiometric and structural information, which is then used to predict properties of materials. The paper also discusses the challenges of integrating structural and stoichiometric information, particularly in the context of polymorphic materials.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the use of probabilistic representation in materials science.
- The approach of learning a probabilistic representation for stoichiometric compositions is innovative and addresses the challenge of polymorphism in materials science.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in predicting physical properties of materials, using both wet-lab and DFT-calculated data.
- The use of Gaussian distributions to model the distribution of stoichiometric representations is a novel approach that allows for the modeling of uncertainty in materials science.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the dataset used, which is crucial for reproducibility and transparency.
- The methodology is sound, with a clear description of the dataset and baseline methods used, which facilitates reproducibility and comparison with existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the probabilistic representation is used in the downstream tasks, particularly in the context of the transfer learning experiments.
- The choice of using Gaussian distributions for the probabilistic representation is not justified, and the paper does not explore alternative distributions such as Dirichlet or multivariate normal.
- The paper does not provide a detailed explanation of how the model handles the uncertainty associated with the probabilistic representation, particularly in terms of its impact on the accuracy of property predictions.
- The experimental results are not always convincing, with some datasets showing lower performance compared to other methods. The reasons for these discrepancies are not adequately explained.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of materials and the potential for overfitting.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational cost of the method, which is crucial for practical applications in materials science.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the probabilistic representation is used in the downstream tasks, particularly in the context of the transfer learning experiments?
- How does the method handle the uncertainty associated with the probabilistic representation, and what are the implications for the accuracy of property predictions?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of materials and the potential for overfitting?
- What are the main differences between the proposed method and other existing methods, particularly in terms of the use of probabilistic representation and the handling of uncertainty?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the computational cost of the method, and how this compares to other methods in terms of practical applicability?
