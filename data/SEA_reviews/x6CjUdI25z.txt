 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Dummy Adversarial Q-learning (DAQ), a novel approach to Q-learning that integrates adversarial behaviors to regulate overestimation biases. DAQ employs multiple Q-estimators and a dummy adversarial player to formulate learning as a two-player zero-sum game. This framework allows for the unification of existing Q-learning variations, including maxmin and minmax Q-learning, and provides a finite-time convergence analysis. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of DAQ through experiments across various benchmark environments, showing improvements over standard Q-learning and other existing methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty and potential overclaiming of its contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to Q-learning by integrating adversarial behaviors, which is both innovative and well-explained.
- The methodology of DAQ is clearly explained, making it accessible and understandable.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear presentation of the proposed method and its applications.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm in improving performance compared to existing methods.
- The paper provides a unified framework that encompasses existing Q-learning variations, enhancing the understanding and application of these methods.
- The finite-time convergence analysis of DAQ is a significant contribution, providing a theoretical foundation for the practical application of the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons and explanations of the differences between DAQ and other related works, such as minmax Q-learning and other adversarial-based Q-learning methods.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some figures showing limited performance improvements or no significant differences between DAQ and other methods.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it primarily introduces a dummy adversarial player without significant modifications to the learning algorithm itself.
- The paper overclaims its contributions, particularly in the area of bias-correction in Q-learning, which is already well-studied.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity introduced by the use of multiple Q-estimators, which could be a significant practical limitation.
- The theoretical contributions, such as the finite-time convergence analysis, are not sufficiently novel or significant given the existing literature on Q-learning and zero-sum games.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between DAQ and other related works, particularly in terms of the adversarial behaviors and the theoretical contributions?
- How does the addition of a constant shift to the reward function affect the optimal policy, and what implications does this have for the practical application of DAQ?
- In the context of the experiments, why do some figures show limited performance improvements or no significant differences between DAQ and other methods?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons of the computational complexity and practical applicability of DAQ compared to other existing methods?
- How does the proposed method address the overestimation bias in Q-learning more effectively than existing methods, particularly in terms of theoretical and practical contributions?
