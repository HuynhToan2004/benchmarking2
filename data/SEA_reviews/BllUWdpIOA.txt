 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method named Continual Momentum Filtering (CMF) for online test-time adaptation (OTTA) in deep neural networks. CMF integrates the Kalman filter with stochastic gradient descent to manage the trade-off between model adaptability and information retention, aiming to mitigate catastrophic forgetting. The approach leverages the Kalman filter to accumulate past observations and denoise the target model parameters, providing a flexible balance between adaptability and robustness. The method is evaluated across various scenarios, demonstrating improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and the lack of detailed experimental analysis and theoretical justification.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, achieving state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on various datasets.
- The use of the Kalman filter (KF) to manage the trade-off between model adaptability and information retention is innovative and well-explained.
- The paper includes a thorough evaluation on a wide range of datasets, including both image classification and speech recognition, demonstrating the robustness of the method across different scenarios.
- The method is computationally efficient, which is crucial for real-time applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the experimental results, particularly in terms of how the method performs in scenarios with varying degrees of distribution shift.
- There is a lack of theoretical justification for the effectiveness of the proposed method, which could strengthen the paper's argument.
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it combines existing methods (Kalman filter and stochastic gradient descent) in a straightforward manner.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need correction.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the experimental results, particularly how the method performs in scenarios with varying degrees of distribution shift?
- How does the method handle different types of distribution shifts, such as concept shifts, where the distribution of the classes changes?
- Could the authors provide more insights into the design choices, particularly the use of the Kalman filter and its impact on the method's performance?
- How does the proposed method compare to other OTTA methods in terms of computational cost and efficiency?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the method and provide suggestions for future research directions?
- In the ablation studies, how does the use of the Kalman filter specifically contribute to the performance improvements observed?
