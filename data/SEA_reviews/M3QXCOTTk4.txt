 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of performance degradation in individual ensemble members when training a diverse ensemble of data-sharing agents in deep reinforcement learning (RL). This issue, termed the "curse of diversity," arises due to the low proportion of self-generated data and the inefficiency of ensemble members in learning from off-policy data. The paper proposes a novel method, Cross-Ensemble Representation Learning (CERL), which addresses this issue by enabling ensemble members to learn each other's value functions as an auxiliary task. Extensive experiments across Atari and MuJoCo environments demonstrate that CERL mitigates the curse of diversity and outperforms single-agent and ensemble-based baselines when combined with policy aggregation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and overlooked problem in ensemble-based exploration, providing a novel perspective on the challenges of learning from off-policy data in ensembles.
- The proposed method, Cross-Ensemble Representation Learning (CERL), is innovative and effectively mitigates the curse of diversity in both discrete and continuous control domains.
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the problem and the proposed solution, with extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of CERL.
- The authors provide a thorough analysis of the causes of the curse of diversity, including the low proportion of self-generated data and the inefficiency of individual ensemble members in learning from off-policy data.
- The paper offers a novel method to mitigate the curse of diversity, which could be beneficial for future research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how CERL affects the diversity of the ensemble and whether it maintains the advantages of using an ensemble.
- The experiments are limited to specific environments and do not include a broader range of environments or different RL algorithms, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the curse of diversity and the effectiveness of CERL.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of CERL, which is a critical factor in practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the curse of diversity and the proposed solutions, which could enhance the understanding and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately compare CERL with other existing ensemble RL methods, which could help in understanding the relative advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential areas for future research.

**Questions:**
- How does CERL affect the diversity of the ensemble, and does it maintain the advantages of using an ensemble?
- Could you provide more details on the computational cost of CERL, especially in terms of memory and training time?
- How does CERL compare with other ensemble RL methods, and what are the specific advantages of CERL over these methods?
- Is there a theoretical explanation for the curse of diversity, and how does CERL address the underlying causes of this phenomenon?
- How does the performance of CERL vary with different ensemble sizes and replay buffer sizes, and what are the optimal settings for these parameters?
- Could you clarify the experimental setup, particularly the role of shared networks in the ensemble SAC and the impact of different network architectures on the performance of CERL?
