 **Summary:**
The paper explores the relationship between the geometric complexity of datasets and the network architecture required for their classification. It introduces a novel approach using polytopes to characterize the geometric properties of datasets and establishes bounds on the widths of ReLU networks needed to approximate the indicator function of these datasets. The paper also proposes a method for discovering a polytope-basis cover for real datasets, which can be approximated by a three-layer ReLU network. Theoretical results are supported by experiments, and the paper discusses the implications of these findings for the design of neural network architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of neural network architecture, specifically focusing on the relationship between the geometry of the dataset and the required network architecture.
- The use of polytopes to characterize the geometric properties of datasets is innovative and provides a novel approach to understanding the relationship between dataset geometry and network architecture.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, and includes detailed proofs and clear explanations of the proposed methods and results.
- Theoretical results are supported by experiments, which enhances the credibility of the findings.
- The paper provides a new perspective on the problem of network architecture design and offers a method for discovering a polytope-basis cover for real datasets, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's reliance on the assumption that the dataset is known to be a polytope or can be approximated by one limits its applicability to real-world datasets, which are typically not known to be polytopes.
- The method for discovering a polytope-basis cover for real datasets is not thoroughly discussed, and it is unclear how this method can be applied to datasets that are not known to be polytopes.
- The paper's theoretical contributions, while interesting, are somewhat limited in their practical applicability due to the assumptions made about the dataset's geometry.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and the assumptions made in the paper, which could affect the generalizability and applicability of the findings.
- The paper's focus on ReLU networks limits its applicability to other types of neural networks, which could be a significant drawback in more general settings.
