 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel algorithm called Federated Dual Extrapolation (FeDualEx) for distributed learning of saddle point problems with composite possibly non-smooth regularization. The algorithm is designed to handle composite optimization challenges and is the first of its kind to encompass both saddle point optimization and composite objectives under the distributed paradigm. The paper provides a convergence rate for FeDualEx under the homogeneous setting and demonstrates its effectiveness through numerical experiments. The theoretical analysis and empirical evaluations are presented to validate the algorithm's performance, although the paper is noted for its complexity and heavy reliance on assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow presentation, and the algorithms are clearly described.
- The proposed method, Federated Dual Extrapolation (FeDualEx), is the first of its kind to encompass both saddle point optimization and composite objectives under the distributed paradigm, and it provides a convergence rate for FeDualEx under the homogeneous setting.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of FeDualEx through numerical experiments, showing that it produces solutions with the desired structure, such as sparsity.
- The paper is technically sound, with a comprehensive theoretical analysis and a clear presentation of the main theorem.
- The authors have provided a detailed comparison with related works, highlighting the advantages of their proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is somewhat difficult to read due to its complexity and heavy reliance on assumptions.
- The experimental setup is limited to a small number of clients (M=100), which may not adequately represent larger-scale distributed systems.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its potential negative societal impacts.
- The convergence analysis of the algorithm is not clearly explained, and some of the proofs are relegated to the appendix, which could make the paper less accessible to some readers.
- The paper does not compare the communication complexity of FeDualEx with other methods, which could be crucial for understanding its practical efficiency.
- The paper does not discuss the potential computational complexity of the proximal operator used in the algorithm, which could be a significant concern for practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proximal operator defined in Definition 4 can be computed efficiently, especially considering the potential computational complexity?
- In the proof of Lemma 5.3, how does the inequality α2(zr,k+1/2)−α2(zr,k)≤α2(zr,k+1/2)−α2(zr,k)−α2(zr,k+1/2)−α2(zr,k) hold, given that α2(zr,k+1/2)−α2(zr,k) could be negative?
- How does the convergence rate of FeDualEx compare with other methods in terms of communication complexity, particularly in large-scale distributed systems?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other methods, such as those mentioned in Table 1, to better highlight the advantages of FeDualEx?
- In the context of the experimental results, can the authors clarify how the sparsity of the solution is measured and what specific values are considered "zero" in the numerical experiments?
