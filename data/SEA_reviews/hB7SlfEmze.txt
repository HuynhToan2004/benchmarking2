 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PhyloGFN, a method for Bayesian and parsimony-based phylogenetic inference using generative flow networks (GFlowNets). PhyloGFN employs a novel tree representation based on Fitch and Felsenstein's algorithms to model rooted trees without additional learnable parameters. The methodology involves a tree construction procedure that explores the entire tree topology space, and it demonstrates competitive performance in marginal likelihood estimation compared to state-of-the-art methods. However, the paper's presentation and clarity need significant improvement, as noted by reviewers.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for Bayesian and parsimony-based phylogenetic inference using GFlowNets, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The use of Fitch and Felsenstein's algorithms to represent rooted trees without additional learnable parameters is innovative and contributes to the method's effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a detailed background on phylogenetic inference and GFlowNets, which is beneficial for readers unfamiliar with these concepts.
- The authors have provided a detailed explanation of the methodology and the experimental setup, which enhances the reproducibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the presentation of the GFlowNet framework and the explanation of the tree construction procedure.
- There are several instances of unclear or confusing notation and terminology, which could hinder understanding for readers not familiar with the field.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to existing methods, which is crucial for assessing its scalability.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, such as PAUP*, and the paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the method and a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to existing methods, and discuss the scalability of PhyloGFN as the number of sequences increases?
- How does the method perform in terms of computational efficiency, and what are the practical implications of using PhyloGFN in real-world applications?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of PhyloGFN with existing methods in terms of model architecture, training strategies, and experimental results?
- What are the specific advantages of using GFlowNets for phylogenetic inference over other methods, such as those based on Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) or variational inference?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest areas for future research to address these limitations?
