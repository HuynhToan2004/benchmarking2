 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Prometheus, an open-source large language model (LLM) designed to evaluate text quality based on customized score rubrics. It leverages GPT-4 to generate a dataset of feedback, which is then used to fine-tune Prometheus. The model is evaluated against both human annotators and other LLMs, demonstrating competitive performance. The dataset, named the Feedback Collection, consists of 1,000 fine-grained score rubrics, 20,000 instructions, and 100,000 responses and language feedback. The paper highlights the potential of using open-source models for evaluation tasks, offering a more affordable and accessible alternative to proprietary models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem by exploring the use of open-source models for evaluation tasks, which is a significant departure from the reliance on proprietary models.
- The proposed dataset, named the Feedback Collection, is a valuable resource for the community, providing a large-scale dataset that can be used for various tasks.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental setup is comprehensive, involving both human evaluations and evaluations by other LLMs, which provides a robust validation of the proposed model.
- The model's performance is competitive with proprietary models like GPT-4, demonstrating the potential of open-source models in evaluation tasks.
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the dataset and the model's performance, which is beneficial for understanding the model's capabilities and limitations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed model, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities.
- The dataset construction process is not sufficiently detailed, which could hinder reproducibility and understanding of the dataset's quality.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the model's performance against other open-source models, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of its capabilities.
- The model's performance is not consistently superior to other models across all benchmarks, which raises questions about its generalizability.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential biases introduced by the dataset, which could affect the model's fairness and accuracy.
- The paper's reliance on GPT-4 for dataset generation raises concerns about the dataset's quality and the model's performance, especially if the dataset is not thoroughly validated.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the dataset construction process, particularly how the dataset was validated to ensure its quality and diversity?
- How does the model perform on other evaluation benchmarks beyond those tested in the paper?
- Can the authors provide more details on the dataset's construction, including the selection criteria for the initial seed rubrics and the methodology used to generate the dataset?
- How does the model handle different types of score rubrics, particularly those that may not be well-suited for evaluation by a model?
- Can the authors clarify the model's performance on different types of tasks, especially those that are not well-represented in the current dataset?
- How does the model handle biases in the dataset, and what steps were taken to mitigate these biases during training?
