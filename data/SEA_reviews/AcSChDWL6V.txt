 **Summary:**
The paper examines the expressive power of Graph Transformers (GTs) and Message-Passing Graph Neural Networks (MPGNNs) with virtual nodes (VNs) in both non-uniform and uniform settings. It demonstrates that GTs and MPGNNs with VNs are universal function approximators in the non-uniform setting, but not in the uniform setting. The authors prove that there are functions that GTs can express but MPGNNs with VNs cannot, and vice versa. The paper also presents experimental results on real-world datasets, showing that MPGNNs with VNs can achieve similar performance to GTs. Despite these findings, the paper is criticized for its limited scope in terms of datasets and model architectures, and for not thoroughly addressing the practical implications of its theoretical results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the theoretical results and the experimental setup.
- It provides a comprehensive overview of the expressivity of both Graph Transformers (GTs) and Message-Passing Graph Neural Networks (MPGNNs), highlighting their differences and similarities.
- The experiments conducted on both synthetic and real-world datasets support the theoretical claims, showing that MPGNNs with virtual nodes can achieve similar performance to GTs.
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of graph learning, focusing on the expressivity of GTs and MPGNNs, which is an important aspect of their design and application.
- The theoretical results are novel and provide new insights into the expressive power of GTs and MPGNNs, particularly in the uniform setting where the model's performance is not limited by graph size.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on GCN-based MPGNNs and GPS-based GTs, which may not fully capture the diversity of models in the field.
- The experimental results are not particularly strong, with some datasets showing only marginal improvements or no significant differences between MPGNNs and GTs.
- The paper's claims about the expressive power of GTs and MPGNNs are not entirely novel, as similar results have been previously discussed in the literature.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are somewhat limited by the focus on simple architectures like GCN and GPS, which may not fully represent the complexity of modern GTs and MPGNNs.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of its theoretical results, particularly how these results could guide the design of more effective GTs and MPGNNs.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the datasets used and the hyperparameters employed?
- How does the paper's theoretical framework apply to more complex architectures like Graph Transformers and MPGNNs?
- Can the authors clarify the practical implications of their theoretical results and discuss how these results could be used to design more effective GTs and MPGNNs?
- Why was the choice made to focus on GCN-based MPGNNs and GPS-based GTs, and how do these choices affect the generalizability of the results?
- Could the authors elaborate on the differences between the results presented in this paper and those in prior works, particularly in terms of the expressive power of GTs and MPGNNs?
