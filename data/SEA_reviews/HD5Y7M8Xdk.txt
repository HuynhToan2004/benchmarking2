 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel variational importance sampling (VIS) approach to enhance the estimation of marginal log-likelihood in latent variable models. By minimizing the forward χ² divergence between the true and proposed distributions, VIS aims to improve the quality of marginal log-likelihood estimation compared to traditional variational inference methods. The approach is tested across various models, including mixture models, VAEs, and POGLMs, demonstrating its potential to outperform existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the method's advantages over traditional methods, the necessity of using VIS in all scenarios, and the practical implications of the method's theoretical advantages.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed method, variational importance sampling (VIS), is a novel approach that utilizes the forward χ² divergence to improve the estimation of marginal log-likelihood, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is tested across various models, including mixture models, VAEs, and POGLMs, demonstrating its applicability and potential effectiveness.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's properties and its relationship to existing methods, which enhances the understanding of its advantages and limitations.
- The experimental results show that VIS can achieve higher marginal log-likelihood and better parameter estimation compared to existing methods, indicating its potential utility in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the advantages of VIS over traditional methods, especially in terms of practical applications and computational efficiency.
- The theoretical analysis, particularly the relationship between the reverse KL divergence and the forward χ² divergence, is not well-explained, which could confuse readers about the method's effectiveness.
- The method's reliance on the number of Monte Carlo samples and the choice of proposal distributions is not adequately discussed, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, especially in scenarios where VIS does not significantly outperform other methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and the scenarios where it might not be the most effective choice.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a clearer explanation of the advantages of VIS over traditional methods, especially in practical scenarios?
- How does the choice of proposal distribution affect the performance of VIS, and what are the practical implications of this choice?
- What is the computational cost of VIS compared to other methods, and how does this affect its scalability to larger datasets?
- How does the method handle the estimation of the marginal log-likelihood in scenarios where the true posterior is not available, such as in real-world applications?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and the methodology used to generate the results?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the model is misspecified, and what are the implications of this for the accuracy of the marginal log-likelihood estimation?
