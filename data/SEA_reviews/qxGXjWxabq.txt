 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Canonpipe, a novel method for efficiently computing Shapley-based data importance over ML pipelines. It employs a data provenance framework to manage data provenance in machine learning pipelines, enabling the computation of Shapley values in a more efficient manner. The methodology is validated through extensive experiments, showing that it can be as effective as existing Monte Carlo baselines and can outperform them in certain scenarios. The paper also discusses the limitations and potential improvements of the method, including its applicability to different types of pipelines and its computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical structure that facilitates understanding.
- The introduction of the notion of a "canonical pipeline" is innovative and provides a new perspective on the computation of Shapley values.
- The paper addresses the important problem of efficiently computing Shapley values for data debugging in ML pipelines, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in reducing computational costs while maintaining performance.
- The paper is well-motivated, with a clear focus on improving the efficiency of Shapley value computation in ML pipelines, which is a critical issue in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of the datasets used and the specifics of the experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, particularly those that use influence functions or other approximations, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in large-scale or complex pipelines, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper's focus on specific types of pipelines (map, fork, and one-to-many join) may limit its applicability to more complex or diverse pipelines, which could be a significant limitation in practical scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its applicability to different types of pipelines?
- How does the computational efficiency of the proposed method compare to existing methods, particularly in terms of computational time and resource requirements?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specific datasets used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex or diverse pipelines, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in such scenarios?
- Could the authors clarify the computational complexity of the proposed method and discuss potential strategies for improving its efficiency in large-scale or complex pipelines?
