 **Summary:**
The paper introduces EXPLEME, a novel method for explaining the decision-making process of a classifier on memes by retrieving tokens from a global vocabulary space. The approach employs a two-stage strategy, first utilizing a multimodal encoder to fuse visual and textual features, followed by a language model to classify and generate explanations. The method is evaluated on the Facebook Hateful Meme dataset, showing superiority over existing input attribution-based methods. The paper also includes theoretical grounding and extensive ablation studies to support the method's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are clearly presented with sufficient details.
- The proposed method is theoretically grounded and offers a novel approach to extracting explanations from a model's decision-making process, which is particularly relevant for meme analysis.
- The paper includes a comprehensive ablation study that demonstrates the effectiveness of each step in the method, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing input attribution-based methods.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the four-step process used to retrieve tokens from a global vocabulary space, which are semantically relevant and faithful to the input meme.
- The paper presents a novel approach to interpretable meme classification, which is a significant contribution to the field of multimodal interpretability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how the method is task-agnostic, which is a crucial claim given the specific focus on meme analysis.
- The evaluation section is insufficient, with only one dataset used, which limits the generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of comparison with other relevant baselines, particularly methods that generate explanations for multimodal models, which could have strengthened the paper's claims.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include issues like the scalability of the method and the potential for overfitting due to the complexity of the model.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the societal impacts of the work, especially considering the potential misuse of the method for harmful purposes.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the method is task-agnostic and whether it can be applied to other tasks beyond meme analysis?
- How does the method perform on other datasets, especially those with different characteristics from the Facebook Hateful Meme dataset?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process for the language model used in the second stage of the method?
- How do the authors ensure that the retrieved tokens are not just semantically similar to the input but also provide new insights that were not present in the input?
- Can the authors explain the discrepancy in the performance metrics between the proposed method and the baselines, particularly in terms of the F1 score?
- How does the method handle cases where the model's decision is influenced by factors that are not directly related to the input, such as biases in the training data?
