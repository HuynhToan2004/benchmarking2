 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel zero-shot NAS method called SiGeo, which leverages a sub-one-shot strategy by training the supernet on a small subset of the training data. This method is designed to bridge the gap between zero-shot and one-shot NAS by utilizing a warm-up phase. The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the geometry of loss landscapes and demonstrate the effectiveness of SiGeo through extensive experiments on NASBench-101, NASBench-201, and NASBench-301, as well as in the recommendation domain. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty, insufficient experimental validation, and unclear writing.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel sub-one-shot NAS strategy that bridges the gap between zero-shot and one-shot NAS, providing a new perspective on NAS.
- It proposes a new proxy called SiGeo, which is proven to be effective when candidate architectures are warmed up, showing consistent performance improvements over state-of-the-art NAS proxies.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the geometry of loss landscapes, connecting the warm-up of the supernet with the effectiveness of the SiGeo proxy.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to assess the effectiveness of SiGeo in both the computer vision and recommendation domains, demonstrating its superior performance compared to other NAS methods.
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of the correlation between the SiGeo proxy and the test accuracy, showing that SiGeo is more effective than other zero-shot NAS proxies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the sub-one-shot setting, and the benefits of this setting are not convincingly demonstrated.
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it builds on existing works like ZiCo and does not sufficiently distinguish itself from these prior studies.
- The paper suffers from unclear writing and lacks a detailed explanation of the main ideas and contributions, making it difficult to follow.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, particularly in demonstrating the effectiveness of the SiGeo proxy in the sub-one-shot setting.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other NAS methods, such as weight-sharing one-shot NAS, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of SiGeo's performance.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in various scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the motivation behind the sub-one-shot setting and how it benefits the NAS process?
- How does the SiGeo proxy perform when the warm-up level is less than 1%?
- Why does the SiGeo proxy perform better than other zero-shot NAS proxies, especially when the warm-up level is low?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the number of epochs used for training the supernet and the selection of architectures?
- How does the SiGeo proxy compare with other NAS methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- Could you elaborate on the methodology and results of the ablation study mentioned in the paper?
- Why is the correlation between the SiGeo proxy and the test accuracy not consistently high across all datasets?
