 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGP) by integrating attention mechanisms and graphical representations to model dependencies among outputs. This method, termed GMOGP, aims to address the computational and storage challenges of traditional MOGPs by allowing each output to depend on a subset of other outputs, which is learned through an attention mechanism. The paper also proposes a distributed learning framework for optimizing kernel hyperparameters and extends the model to handle non-Gaussian data. The approach is evaluated against various synthetic and real-world datasets, showing improved predictive performance and efficiency compared to existing methods. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues, lack of detailed theoretical justification, and insufficient empirical validation, particularly in terms of the scalability and generalizability of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive review of related works.
- The proposed method is novel and offers an innovative approach to modeling dependencies among multiple outputs in multi-output regression tasks.
- The use of attention mechanisms and graphical representations allows for more flexible and efficient modeling of dependencies, which is a significant improvement over traditional MOGPs.
- The method is shown to be effective in both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating improved predictive performance and efficiency compared to existing methods.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear presentation of the method and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed theoretical justification for the attention mechanism and the graphical representation, particularly in terms of how these components contribute to the overall modeling process.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and generalizability of the method, especially in high-dimensional or large-scale datasets.
- There are several typographical and notation errors throughout the paper that need correction.
- The empirical validation of the method is insufficient, with only a few datasets used for evaluation, and the results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of data and its computational efficiency.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and memory requirements of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical justification for the attention mechanism and the graphical representation, particularly how these components contribute to the modeling of dependencies among multiple outputs?
- How does the proposed method handle high-dimensional or large-scale datasets, and what are the computational and memory requirements in such scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability and generalizability of the method, perhaps through additional experiments or theoretical analysis?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent approaches in terms of computational efficiency and memory requirements?
- Can the authors clarify the notations and correct any typographical errors noted in the paper?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches in terms of predictive performance and efficiency, particularly in high-dimensional or large-scale datasets?
- How does the attention mechanism affect the computational complexity and memory requirements of the method?
