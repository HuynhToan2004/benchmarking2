 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DecompOpt, a novel structure-based molecular optimization method that leverages a controllable and decomposed diffusion model to optimize molecular properties while adhering to molecular grammar. This approach allows for both de novo design and controllable generation, which is a significant advancement in the field of drug discovery. The method decomposes ligands into substructures, enabling fine-grained control and local optimization. Experiments demonstrate that DecompOpt outperforms existing methods in various metrics, including binding affinity and molecular properties, and shows potential in tasks like R-group design and scaffold hopping. However, the paper lacks detailed ablation studies and comparisons with more recent baselines, which could strengthen its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to structure-based molecular optimization using a controllable and decomposed diffusion model, which is a significant advancement in the field of drug discovery.
- The methodology is well-explained, and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed DecompOpt method is shown to outperform existing methods in various metrics, demonstrating its effectiveness and potential impact.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including comparisons with recent baselines and a thorough discussion of the results.
- The integration of controllability into the diffusion model is highlighted as a key contribution, enhancing the method's ability to optimize molecular properties while adhering to molecular grammar.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed ablation studies, particularly concerning the impact of different hyperparameters and model configurations on the performance.
- The methodology section could benefit from more clarity and detail, especially in explaining how the diffusion model is integrated with the controllable and decomposed approach.
- There is a need for more comprehensive comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, including those that use similar or more recent datasets and baselines.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include potential biases in the training data, computational costs, or the generalizability of the method across different datasets.
- Some figures and tables (e.g., Figure 2 and Table 3) are not clearly explained or presented, which could confuse readers or limit the understanding of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the diffusion model is integrated with the controllable and decomposed approach, and how this integration impacts the performance of the model?
- How do the authors justify the choice of using a specific diffusion model over others, and what are the implications of this choice for the performance and applicability of the method?
- In the context of the ablation studies, could you provide more detailed results on how different hyperparameters and model configurations affect the performance of DecompOpt?
- Can you elaborate on the computational costs and the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of how it performs with larger or more complex datasets?
- How does the method handle the potential biases in the training data, and what steps have been taken to mitigate these biases?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific conditions under which the ablation studies were conducted and the rationale behind these conditions?
