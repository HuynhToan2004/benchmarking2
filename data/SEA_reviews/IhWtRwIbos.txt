 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Cross-Risk Minimization (XRM), a novel method for environment discovery in domain generalization tasks. XRM trains two identical models on different halves of the training data, encouraging each to mimic the mistakes of the other. This method aims to identify environments that differ in spurious correlations, enhancing the performance of downstream domain generalization algorithms. The paper presents empirical results across various benchmark datasets, showing that XRM can achieve oracle-like performance in worst-group accuracy. However, it lacks theoretical justification and a detailed discussion on the limitations and failure modes of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research.
- The proposed method, Cross-Risk Minimization (XRM), is innovative and simple, requiring no hyper-parameter tuning or early-stopping, which is beneficial for practical applications.
- The empirical results are strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of XRM in achieving oracle-like performance in worst-group accuracy across multiple datasets.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of related work, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of existing methods.
- The methodology is simple and effective, requiring only two identical models trained on different halves of the training data, which simplifies the implementation process.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and failure modes of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and robustness.
- There is a lack of theoretical justification for the method, which could enhance the understanding and credibility of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the method, which is important for practical deployment in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications for certain methodological choices, such as the use of "error when holding-out" and the selection of hyper-parameters.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational resources required for the method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method in terms of its scalability and applicability to different types of data or datasets.
