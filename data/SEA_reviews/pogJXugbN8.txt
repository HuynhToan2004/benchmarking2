 **Summary:**
The paper introduces BAFFLE, a backpropagation-free federated learning (FL) method that utilizes Stein's identity to estimate gradients through multiple forward propagations. This approach aims to address the computational and storage overhead associated with backpropagation, particularly beneficial for edge devices with limited resources. The paper presents a theoretical framework and empirical evidence demonstrating the method's effectiveness in various federated learning scenarios. It also discusses the potential of BAFFLE in enhancing security and privacy in FL systems. However, the paper has been critiqued for its incremental nature, lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and insufficient experimental validation, particularly in more complex and realistic federated learning settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the method and its implications.
- The introduction of BAFFLE as a backpropagation-free federated learning method is innovative, addressing the computational and storage challenges of backpropagation-based FL.
- The method is compatible with inference-only hardware optimization and model quantization or pruning, making it suitable for edge devices with limited resources.
- The paper provides a theoretical framework and empirical evidence to support the method's effectiveness, including experiments on various datasets and models.
- The approach is simple, efficient, and well-motivated, with potential to reduce communication costs and enhance privacy in federated learning systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a better understanding of BAFFLE's relative performance and advantages.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, particularly in more complex and realistic federated learning settings, which could better demonstrate the method's effectiveness and scalability.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of server trustworthiness, which is crucial in federated learning systems.
- The method's reliance on the assumption that the model parameters are perturbed with a Gaussian distribution could limit its applicability to other types of noise.
- The convergence rate of the method is not clearly stated, which could affect the understanding of its practical applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including a discussion on how BAFFLE compares in terms of computational efficiency, memory usage, and communication costs?
- How does BAFFLE perform in more complex and realistic federated learning settings, such as those with larger datasets or more heterogeneous client populations?
- Could the authors clarify the convergence rate of the method and discuss how this affects the practical applicability of BAFFLE?
- How does BAFFLE address the issue of server trustworthiness in federated learning systems?
- Are there any potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how do the authors plan to address these?
