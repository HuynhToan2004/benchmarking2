 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the benefits of multi-task reinforcement learning (MTL) in non-Markovian decision-making problems, specifically focusing on predictive state representations (PSRs) in both upstream and downstream scenarios. It introduces a novel algorithm, UMT-PSR, which leverages the η-bracketing number to measure task similarity and complexity, aiming to improve sample efficiency in MTL. Theoretical analyses are provided to support the advantages of MTL over single-task learning, demonstrating that smaller η-bracketing numbers can lead to more efficient learning. The paper also discusses the practical implications of these findings, though the clarity and depth of the examples and the overall presentation of the paper are noted as areas for improvement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is the first to investigate multi-task learning in non-Markovian decision-making problems, which is a significant and original contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis is sound, and the paper provides a novel algorithm (UMT-PSR) that can identify near-optimal policies for multi-task PSRs, enhancing sample efficiency.
- The introduction of the η-bracketing number as a metric for task similarity and complexity is innovative and useful for quantifying the benefits of multi-task learning.
- The paper is well-written and provides clear examples of multi-task PSRs with small η-bracketing numbers, which are crucial for demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed methods.
- The theoretical results are strong, showing that the proposed algorithm can achieve better sample complexity than single-task learning under certain conditions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical evidence to support the claims made about the superiority of MTL over single-task learning, which could be addressed by including experimental results.
- The examples provided in the paper are too simplistic and do not adequately demonstrate the benefits of MTL in more complex or realistic scenarios.
- The writing and presentation of the paper need improvement, particularly in the clarity of the examples and the overall structure of the paper.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the practical implications of the theoretical results, which could be enhanced by including more detailed examples or case studies.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more complex or realistic examples of multi-task PSRs to better demonstrate the practical benefits of MTL?
- How does the proposed algorithm perform in more general or complex scenarios, and what are the specific conditions under which it is most effective?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the η-bracketing number as a metric for task similarity and complexity, and discuss its advantages over other metrics?
- How does the paper address the practical challenges of implementing MTL in real-world applications, and what are the potential limitations or drawbacks of the proposed methods?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the γ-well-conditioned PSR assumption on the effectiveness of the proposed methods?
