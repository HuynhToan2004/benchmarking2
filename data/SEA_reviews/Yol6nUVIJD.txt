 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ReConcile, a novel multi-agent framework designed to enhance the reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) through a round-table conference approach. This framework facilitates discussions between diverse LLM agents, enabling them to revise their responses based on insights from other agents, aiming to achieve consensus through a confidence-weighted voting mechanism. The methodology is evaluated on various benchmarks, demonstrating improvements over existing single-agent and multi-agent baselines. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the fairness of the comparisons, and the scalability of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible.
- The proposed method, ReConcile, is novel and effective, demonstrating improvements over single-agent and multi-agent baselines on multiple benchmarks.
- The evaluation is comprehensive, covering various tasks and models, and the results are convincing.
- The idea of using a round-table conference among diverse LLM agents to foster diverse thoughts and discussion for improved consensus is innovative and potentially impactful.
- The paper includes ablation studies that help in understanding the effectiveness of different components of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as it builds upon existing techniques such as self-refine and self-consistency, with limited new insights or techniques.
- The fairness of the comparisons is not guaranteed, as different models have varying numbers of parameters and training data, which could affect the outcomes.
- The scalability of the method is unclear, especially in terms of how it would perform with a larger number of agents or more complex tasks.
- The paper lacks detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning its applicability to other tasks or models.
- The methodology, while innovative, is somewhat limited by the need for access to human-written explanations, which may not be readily available for all tasks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach and how it differs from existing methods like self-refine and self-consistency?
- How does the method perform when applied to other tasks or models, and what are the limitations in terms of scalability and applicability?
- Could the authors provide more details on how they ensure the fairness of comparisons between different models, especially when these models have varying numbers of parameters and training data?
- How does the method handle scenarios where consensus is not achieved, and what are the implications for the overall performance and reliability of the model?
- Can the authors elaborate on the methodology used to select convincing samples and how this affects the performance of the model?
