 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DeepEMD, a novel approach to approximating the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) between point clouds using a transformer-based model. The method aims to improve the computational efficiency of EMD estimation, which is typically a computationally expensive process. DeepEMD predicts the bipartite matching matrix from the attention matrix, allowing for faster computation of EMD and its gradient. The model is evaluated against traditional methods like Chamfer distance and the Hungarian algorithm, demonstrating significant speed improvements and competitive performance. The method is also shown to be effective in training generative models and evaluating point cloud generative models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for estimating EMD between point clouds, which is a significant improvement over traditional methods like the Hungarian algorithm.
- The use of a transformer architecture for estimating EMD is innovative and provides a scalable solution for handling large point clouds.
- The method is validated against both traditional methods like Chamfer distance and the Hungarian algorithm, demonstrating its effectiveness in terms of both accuracy and computational efficiency.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and includes a comprehensive evaluation across various datasets and settings.
- The method's scalability and generalization to different point cloud sizes are highlighted, showing its potential for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with existing methods that also aim to approximate EMD, which could help establish the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The evaluation primarily focuses on synthetic datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The computational complexity of the method is not adequately explained, and the scalability of the method to larger point clouds is not convincingly demonstrated.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper that need correction.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in relation to the Hungarian algorithm?
- How does the method perform when applied to real-world datasets, and what are the implications for its practical applicability?
- Can you provide more details on the training and inference times for the method, and how these compare to traditional methods like Chamfer distance?
- How does the method handle the computational demands when scaling to larger point clouds?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle non-uniform point clouds, and if so, how would this affect the computational efficiency and accuracy of the results?
