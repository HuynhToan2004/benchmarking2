 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel diffusion-based framework for solving inverse problems in computer vision, specifically focusing on image restoration tasks like deblurring and inpainting. It models the observation process as a stochastic degradation process, allowing for the recovery of clean images by reversing this process. The framework is designed to maintain consistency with the original measurement throughout the reverse process, offering flexibility in trading off perceptual quality for improved distortion metrics and sampling speedup. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness through experiments on high-resolution datasets and inverse problems, showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art diffusion-based methods in terms of both perceptual and distortion metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is novel, original, and innovative, introducing a new approach to solving inverse problems using diffusion models.
- The method is flexible and allows for trading off perceptual quality for improved distortion metrics and sampling speedup, which is beneficial for practical applications.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, and the paper is supported by a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies that validate the method's effectiveness.
- The paper presents a novel perspective on solving inverse problems, specifically by modeling the observation process as a stochastic degradation process, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and training requirements.
- The method's performance in terms of perceptual quality and distortion metrics is not consistently superior across all datasets and tasks, and there is a need for more comprehensive comparisons to establish its generalizability.
- The paper does not discuss potential negative societal impacts, which could be a limitation for its broader application.
- The method's reliance on training from scratch for each inverse problem could limit its practical applicability, as it may not be feasible for all scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and training requirements of the proposed method compared to existing methods?
- How does the method perform in terms of perceptual quality and distortion metrics across different datasets and tasks, and what are the reasons for any observed inconsistencies?
- Is there a possibility to adapt the method for scenarios where the forward operator is not known?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
- How does the method compare in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements to other state-of-the-art methods?
