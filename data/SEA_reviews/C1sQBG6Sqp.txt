 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Dual Randomized Smoothing (DRS), a novel method aimed at enhancing the robustness of neural networks against adversarial attacks by down-sampling input images into two sub-images and applying randomized smoothing in lower-dimensional spaces. This approach aims to mitigate the curse of dimensionality and improve the certified robustness radius of high-dimensional inputs. Theoretical analysis and empirical evidence demonstrate that DRS provides a tighter certified robustness radius compared to traditional randomized smoothing. The method is tested on datasets like CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, showing improvements in certified accuracy and robustness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant issue of certified robustness in high-dimensional inputs, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The introduction of Dual Randomized Smoothing (DRS) is novel and well-motivated, offering a promising solution to the curse of dimensionality in randomized smoothing.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, providing a tighter certified robustness radius compared to traditional randomized smoothing, which is supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of DRS in enhancing certified accuracy and robustness.
- The methodology is straightforward and intuitive, with a clear focus on improving robustness in high-dimensional spaces.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other methods that address the curse of dimensionality, such as projected randomized smoothing and other approaches mentioned in related works.
- The experimental results, while showing improvements, are not consistently significant across all datasets and noise levels, raising concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
- The implementation of DRS using 2x2 downsampling kernels might not effectively preserve high-dimensional information, potentially affecting the robustness of the model.
- The theoretical analysis and proofs, while sound, are not entirely new or surprising, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the novelty and implications of the theoretical contributions.
- There is a lack of clarity in some parts of the paper, particularly in the definition and usage of certain symbols and equations, which could confuse readers.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the method and potential directions for future research.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide a more detailed comparison of DRS with other methods that address the curse of dimensionality, such as projected randomized smoothing and other approaches mentioned in related works?
- How does the choice of the downsampling kernel in DRS affect the performance and robustness of the model? Could different kernels or sampling strategies be explored to improve the results?
- In the theoretical analysis, could you clarify the assumptions made regarding the symmetry of the distribution and the implications of these assumptions on the results?
- How do the results of DRS compare to other methods when using different noise levels or different datasets? Are there specific conditions under which DRS performs best?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of using the Neyman-Pearson lemma in the proof of Theorem 1 and discuss any potential advantages or disadvantages compared to other proof methods?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of adversarial attacks, such as L_inf attacks?
- In the experiments, could you provide more details on the training strategies and hyperparameters used for DRS and RS to ensure a fair comparison?
- How does the proposed method scale with the size of the input images, and what are the computational costs associated with the DRS approach?
