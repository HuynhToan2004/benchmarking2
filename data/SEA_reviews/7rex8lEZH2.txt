 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to prompt-tuning in reinforcement learning (RL) by integrating a diffusion model to generate high-quality prompts, which are then used to enhance the performance of pre-trained models in few-shot meta-RL tasks. The methodology involves training a diffusion model to generate prompts from random noise, conditioned on the return to go and the timestep token. This approach aims to address the sensitivity of prompt-tuning methods to initialization by avoiding the need for pre-collected expert prompts. The paper is well-structured, with extensive experiments and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, concerns about the clarity of the writing, the novelty of the approach, and the comparative analysis with existing methods have been raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed method and the experiments.
- The experiments are comprehensive, including comparisons with several baselines and ablation studies.
- The proposed method is novel and well-motivated, providing a reasonable solution to the problem of prompt initialization in prompt-tuning methods.
- The paper is organized well, with a clear introduction and a logical flow of ideas.
- The use of diffusion models to generate prompts is a novel approach, and the paper provides a thorough analysis of the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the introduction and the methodology. Some notations are not well-explained, which could confuse readers.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods, such as Prompt-DT, without sufficient differentiation.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly the training of the diffusion model.
- The paper's comparison with baselines and existing methods is not convincing, with some baselines not being directly comparable due to differences in methodology.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the societal impacts of its use.
- There are some inconsistencies and potential errors in the writing, such as inaccurate citations and unclear equations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and Prompt-DT, particularly in terms of methodology and performance?
- How does the proposed method handle the sensitivity to initialization, especially when using random prompts?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in terms of training time and resource requirements?
- How does the proposed method compare to other diffusion-based methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more insights into the societal impacts of their work and discuss any potential limitations or ethical concerns?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of randomness in the generated prompts, and what measures are in place to ensure the quality and consistency of the generated prompts?
