 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the scaling behavior of different fine-tuning methods for large language models (LLMs), specifically focusing on prompt tuning and LoRA, in the context of machine translation and summarization tasks. It proposes a multiplicative scaling law that relates the model size, pre-training data size, fine-tuning data size, and parameter size to performance. The study compares various scaling factors across different model sizes and data sizes, showing that the impact of model size is more significant than pre-training data size. The paper also explores the effects of different fine-tuning data sizes and PET parameter sizes, suggesting that these factors are crucial for performance. Despite its empirical focus, the paper provides valuable insights into the scaling behaviors of LLMs and contributes to the understanding of fine-tuning strategies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of scaling factors for fine-tuning large language models (LLMs), including model size, pre-training data size, fine-tuning data size, and parameter size, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of LLMs in practical applications.
- The empirical results are well-presented, and the paper offers valuable insights into the performance of different fine-tuning methods, such as prompt tuning and LoRA, under varying conditions.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments are conducted across a wide range of model sizes and data sizes, providing a robust foundation for the conclusions drawn.
- The proposed multiplicative joint scaling law is an interesting and novel contribution to the field, potentially useful for guiding the design and optimization of LLM fine-tuning strategies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed scaling law, particularly in terms of its applicability to different model architectures and fine-tuning scenarios.
- The empirical focus of the study may limit the generalizability of the findings to other domains or tasks, as the scaling laws are derived primarily from machine translation and summarization tasks.
- There is a notable absence of theoretical grounding for the proposed scaling law, which could limit the paper's impact and relevance to a broader audience.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the findings, particularly in terms of the scalability and generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational costs associated with fine-tuning different model sizes and data sizes, which could be a significant limitation for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed scaling law perform when applied to different model architectures or fine-tuning scenarios beyond those explored in the study?
- Can the scaling law be generalized to other tasks or domains beyond machine translation and summarization?
- How does the choice of the held-out set affect the scaling laws derived from the study?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the findings, particularly in terms of scalability and generalizability?
- How do the computational costs associated with fine-tuning different model sizes and data sizes impact the practical applicability of the findings?
- In light of the empirical focus of the study, how do the authors address potential biases or limitations introduced by the specific datasets and tasks used in the experiments?
