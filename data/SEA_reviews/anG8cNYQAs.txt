 **Summary:**
The paper introduces INCYDE, a novel dataset comprising over 21,000 cyclone images from the Indian Ocean, annotated with cyclone detection and intensity estimation labels. This dataset, derived from INSAT 3D/3DR satellite imagery, is intended to facilitate research in cyclone detection and intensity estimation, addressing a significant gap in the availability of such datasets. The dataset is presented as a valuable resource for training machine learning models, with potential applications in early warning systems for tropical cyclones. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive experimental validation, and insufficient comparison with existing datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel dataset, INCYDE, which is the largest of its kind, containing over 21,000 images of cyclones from the Indian Ocean.
- The dataset is annotated with both cyclone detection and intensity estimation labels, which are crucial for training machine learning models in this domain.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the dataset creation process, including data collection, cleaning, label interpolation, data augmentation, and data split.
- The dataset's availability in high-resolution format and its potential to serve as a benchmark for future research in the field of cyclone detection and intensity estimation.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the dataset's characteristics and its utility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive experimental validation, with only a few baseline models trained on the dataset, and no comparative analysis with existing datasets.
- There is a significant gap in the novelty of the dataset, as the cyclone detection and intensity estimation tasks are well-studied, and the dataset's contribution does not significantly advance the state-of-the-art.
- The dataset's focus on a single geographical region (Indian Ocean) limits its applicability to a broader range of tropical cyclone research.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the dataset, such as the absence of temporal information, which could be crucial for tracking cyclone intensity over time.
- The paper does not discuss the potential societal impact of the dataset, which could be significant given the potential applications in early warning systems for tropical cyclones.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the dataset's characteristics, such as the distribution of cyclone intensities and the inter-annual variability of cyclone frequency and intensity.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the dataset's characteristics, including the distribution of cyclone intensities and the inter-annual variability of cyclone frequency and intensity?
- How does the dataset's focus on the Indian Ocean region affect its applicability to other tropical cyclone research?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impact of the dataset, particularly in the context of early warning systems for tropical cyclones?
- What are the plans for future work, such as extending the dataset to include temporal information or expanding the geographical coverage?
- How does the dataset's focus on a single geographical region impact the generalizability of the models trained on it?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the dataset and how these might affect the utility of the dataset for training machine learning models?
