 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SEAL, a method that learns a data-driven label hierarchy by integrating latent labels into a model that leverages a 1-Wasserstein metric over the tree metric space. SEAL aims to improve classification performance by aligning observed and latent labels, which can be applied in both supervised and semi-supervised learning scenarios. The methodology is supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results, demonstrating its effectiveness in various datasets. However, the paper has been criticized for its unclear writing, lack of detailed theoretical justification, and insufficient comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method called SEAL that effectively learns a data-driven label hierarchy by augmenting observed labels with latent labels, which is a significant advancement in semi-supervised learning.
- The use of 1-Wasserstein metric over the tree metric space as an objective function is innovative, enabling the simultaneous learning of a data-driven label hierarchy and semi-supervised learning.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis that supports the proposed method.
- The methodology is flexible and can be integrated into various learning schemes, making it applicable to a wide range of scenarios.
- The empirical results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in both supervised and semi-supervised learning, demonstrating its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the definitions and notations used, which could confuse readers and hinder understanding.
- There is a lack of detailed explanation and justification for certain assumptions and methodologies used, such as the choice of α and the optimization of A2.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing approaches, which limits the understanding of its novelty and effectiveness.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which are crucial for practical implementation.
- The experimental setup and results lack sufficient detail, particularly in the description of the datasets used and the comparisons with other methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of its method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and notations used in the paper, particularly those that are not standard or require explanation?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific datasets used and the comparisons made with other methods?
- How does the proposed method handle the optimization of A2, and what are the implications of this optimization for the overall performance of the model?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical justifications for the use of the 1-Wasserstein metric over the tree metric space, and how this choice impacts the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, especially in large-scale datasets or complex hierarchies?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental results, particularly the comparisons with other methods and the implications of these results for the practical application of the proposed method?
