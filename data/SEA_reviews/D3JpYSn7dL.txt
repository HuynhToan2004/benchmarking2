 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multi-teacher knowledge distillation framework for instance-level Multi-tasking Graph Self-Supervised Learning (MGSSL). This framework integrates knowledge from different teachers, each trained with a distinct pretext task, and applies this knowledge to each instance separately, enhancing the performance of the student model. The methodology involves training multiple teachers with different pretext tasks and integrating their knowledge through parameterized knowledge integration schemes, which are then distilled into the student model. Theoretical justifications and extensive experiments are provided to support the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and important problem in the field of graph self-supervised learning, focusing on how to leverage multiple pretext tasks effectively.
- It introduces a novel multi-teacher knowledge distillation framework, which integrates the knowledge of different teachers for each instance separately, and provides a theoretical guideline for how to integrate the knowledge of different teachers.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear mathematical formulations and theoretical justifications, and the experiments are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the methodology and experimental results.
- The proposed method is shown to be effective in combining a few simple but classical pretext tasks to achieve comparable performance to state-of-the-art competitors.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative social impacts of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of comparison with some relevant baselines, such as GraphMAE and CG3, which are not discussed in the paper.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and time efficiency of the proposed method, especially in comparison to other methods.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method sometimes performs worse than baselines on some datasets, and the reasons for this are not adequately explained.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed explanations and discussions on the experimental results, such as the failure cases and the impact of different pretext tasks on performance.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the method and the potential negative social impacts, which are crucial for a comprehensive understanding of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with GraphMAE and CG3, including a discussion on why these baselines were not included in the experiments?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets, and what are the implications for the scalability of the method?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations for the experimental results, especially the cases where the proposed method performs worse than baselines?
- What are the potential negative social impacts of the proposed method, and how can these be mitigated?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential directions for future research to address these limitations?
