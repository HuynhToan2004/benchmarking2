 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for real-time dense mapping using a single RGB camera, leveraging a factorized NeRF mapping framework named FMapping. The framework is designed to optimize the mapping process by predicting depth from RGB images, utilizing a covariance function trained on the ScanNet dataset. The methodology involves a two-stage process: an initialization stage using a noisy pose input and a subsequent on-the-fly stage for real-time performance. The paper claims that this method achieves comparable results to RGB-D mapping while maintaining real-time capabilities, although it lacks comprehensive comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods and does not thoroughly address the scalability and robustness of the depth estimation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The proposed method achieves real-time performance, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The method is designed to work with a single RGB camera, which is more accessible and cost-effective compared to RGB-D cameras.
- The paper introduces a novel approach using a covariance function trained on the ScanNet dataset, which is an interesting and innovative contribution to the field.
- The methodology is supported by a comprehensive evaluation that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that do not use depth supervision, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on a pre-trained covariance function raises concerns about its scalability and robustness, especially in scenarios where the depth distribution deviates significantly from the training data.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of scale ambiguity in monocular depth estimation, which could affect the accuracy of the depth predictions.
- The method's performance is not consistently superior to existing methods, as shown in some comparisons, which could undermine the claimed advantages.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights for future research and development.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specifics of the depth covariance function used in the method, particularly how it is trained and how it influences the depth predictions?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the depth distribution deviates significantly from the training data, and what are the implications for the accuracy of the depth predictions in such cases?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that do not use depth supervision, to better establish the relative performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of scale ambiguity in monocular depth estimation, and what measures are taken to ensure the accuracy of the depth predictions in the presence of scale variations?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might impact its practical applicability and performance in real-world scenarios?
