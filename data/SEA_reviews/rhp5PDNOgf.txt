 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training strategy called "spaced scheduling training" (SST) inspired by the spaced repetition learning method used by humans. SST aims to enhance instruction-tuned large language models (LLMs) by performing data mix selection during training, which is claimed to improve performance on reasoning tasks. The method is evaluated on a benchmark of 13 datasets, showing improvements over random sampling and other pruning and scheduling baselines. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in explaining the experimental setup, the selection of baselines, and the absence of significant performance gains over existing methods. Concerns were also raised about the generalizability of the approach and the potential for overfitting due to the training process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed method, which is a significant strength for a technical document.
- The motivation behind the research is clear and well-articulated, which enhances the reader's engagement and comprehension of the proposed method.
- The method is simple yet effective, with a clear explanation of how it works, which is crucial for reproducibility and further research.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various models and datasets.
- The authors have provided a detailed analysis of the results, which helps in understanding the impact of the method on model performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the experimental setup, particularly the choice of baselines and the rationale behind the experimental design.
- There is a significant overlap with existing works, particularly with the "Repeat before Forgetting" method, which raises questions about the novelty and distinctiveness of the proposed method.
- The performance gains of the proposed method are marginal, which may not justify the additional computational overhead and complexity introduced by the method.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for overfitting due to the training process, which is a critical concern in the context of instruction-tuned LLMs.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the method and potential negative societal impacts, which are essential for a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that detract from the professionalism and readability of the paper.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the rationale behind the choice of baselines and explain how the proposed method differs from existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use similar strategies, such as the "Repeat before Forgetting" method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational overhead introduced by the method and how it affects the training process, especially in terms of training time and resource utilization?
- Is there a risk of overfitting due to the training process, and how does the method address this risk?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the performance of the method across different model sizes and datasets to better understand its generalizability and effectiveness?
- How does the method handle the potential for data leakage, especially in the context of instruction-tuned LLMs?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their method and how they plan to mitigate these risks?
