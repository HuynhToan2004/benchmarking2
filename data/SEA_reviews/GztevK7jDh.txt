 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Learn Subtask representations via Vector Quantization (LSVQ) for cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL). LSVQ leverages a vector quantized-variational autoencoder to learn subtask representations from individual observations of agents, which are then used to reconstruct the global state. This method allows for the integration of subtask representations into various classic MARL frameworks, enhancing decision-making capabilities of agents. The approach is evaluated on two benchmarks, showing improved performance over existing methods. However, concerns about the clarity of the paper's structure and the lack of detailed comparative analysis with existing methods like RODE and LDSA are noted.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the method is clearly presented.
- The proposed method, Learn Subtask representations via Vector Quantization (LSVQ), is simple yet effective, and the experiments demonstrate its superiority over existing methods.
- The paper introduces a novel approach using vector quantization to learn subtask representations, which is a significant contribution to the field of multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL).
- The use of a vector quantized-variational autoencoder to learn subtask representations is a novel approach that enhances the understanding and application of subtasks in MARL.
- The method's effectiveness is supported by extensive experimental results, which are well-documented and provide a clear demonstration of the approach's superiority over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods such as RODE and LDSA, which also use subtask representations. This omission makes it difficult to ascertain the unique advantages of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the subtask representations learned by LSVQ, which could help in understanding the method's effectiveness and limitations.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the relationship between the subtasks learned by LSVQ and the overall task, and how these subtasks contribute to the overall performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the challenges associated with the learning of subtasks in MARL, especially in complex environments.
- The structure of the paper could be improved to make it more coherent, particularly in the organization of the experimental results and the discussion of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the results, including a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential areas for future research.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method differ from existing methods like RODE and LDSA, and what are the unique advantages of LSVQ over these methods?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the subtask representations learned by LSVQ, and how these representations contribute to the overall performance of the agents?
- How does the number of subtasks K affect the performance of LSVQ, and what is the optimal number of subtasks for different scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and inference processes of the subtask learner, including the number of parameters and the size of the subtask dictionary?
- How does the proposed method compare to other subtask-based methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, particularly in complex environments like SMAC?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and suggest areas for future research to address these limitations?
