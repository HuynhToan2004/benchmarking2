 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the rapid improvement in loss during the training of transformer models on multi-step tasks, a phenomenon referred to as "Eureka moments." The authors identify the softmax operation in the self-attention mechanism as a potential cause of this behavior, suggesting that it leads to small gradients for key and value weight matrices, hindering learning. To address this issue, the paper proposes a modified softmax function and other interventions, demonstrating their effectiveness through experiments on synthetic datasets. However, the paper's scope is limited to these datasets, raising concerns about the generalizability of the findings to real-world, more complex tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel phenomenon of Eureka moments in transformers, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments are well-designed and comprehensive, providing a detailed analysis of the problem.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methods and results.
- The proposed method, which involves adjusting the softmax function to mitigate the issue, is simple and effective.
- The study of multi-step learning without intermediate supervision using synthetic data is innovative and provides a new perspective on understanding the behavior of transformers in complex tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to synthetic datasets, which might not adequately represent real-world scenarios, raising concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a broader discussion on the implications of the Eureka phenomenon, especially in terms of its practical applications and the societal impact of the findings.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the proposed method would perform on larger, more complex datasets or real-world tasks, as the experiments are primarily focused on simpler datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, especially concerning its applicability to different types of tasks and its effectiveness in diverse settings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of synthetic datasets and the implications of using these datasets for understanding the behavior of transformers in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform on larger, more complex datasets or real-world tasks?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of synthetic datasets and how they reflect real-world scenarios?
- Is there a possibility that the Eureka phenomenon could be observed in other types of tasks beyond those studied in the paper?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for addressing similar issues in transformer models?
- What are the implications of the Eureka phenomenon for the broader application of transformer models in various domains?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup and the results observed in real-world datasets, especially in terms of their relevance and applicability to the findings presented in the paper?
