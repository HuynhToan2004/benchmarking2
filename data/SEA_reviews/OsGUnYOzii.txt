 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SCRaWI, a novel simplicial complex neural network architecture that integrates random walks and 1D convolutions to enhance the computational efficiency of higher-order topological domains like simplicial complexes. The architecture is designed to address the computational challenges of existing message-passing simplicial neural networks by varying the length and number of random walks. The authors demonstrate that SCRaWI outperforms other simplicial neural networks on real-world datasets, showcasing its practical utility and theoretical contributions. However, concerns about the clarity of the paper's contributions, the novelty of the approach, and the lack of comprehensive empirical evaluations have been raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical structure that makes it accessible to readers.
- The proposed random walk-based learning strategy effectively addresses the limitations of classical WL-test, offering an alternative approach for distinguishing graphs and simplicial complexes.
- The paper includes a comprehensive review of related works, providing a solid foundation for the research.
- The experimental results show that SCRaWI outperforms existing simplicial neural networks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper includes a theoretical analysis of the expressive power of SCRaWI, which is incomparable to message-passing simplicial networks.
- The use of random walks to sample information from simplicial complexes is a novel approach that adds depth to the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the contributions, making it difficult for readers to understand the novelty and significance of the work.
- There is a lack of comparison with other recent random walk-based methods, such as those mentioned in references [1] and [2], which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational efficiency of SCRaWI, particularly in large-scale applications, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation of SCRaWI are not sufficiently detailed, raising questions about the robustness and generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other existing methods, such as those mentioned in references [3] and [4], which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of SCRaWI, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the contributions of the paper and how they advance the field of simplicial complex neural networks?
- How does SCRaWI compare in terms of computational efficiency and performance with other random walk-based methods, particularly those mentioned in references [1] and [2]?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other existing methods, such as those mentioned in references [3] and [4], to better establish the novelty and effectiveness of SCRaWI?
- How does SCRaWI handle the scalability issues in large-scale applications, and what are the potential limitations in terms of computational resources and runtime?
- Could the authors provide more detailed theoretical and empirical analyses to better understand the expressive power and practical applicability of SCRaWI?
