 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework, SUNG, aimed at enhancing offline-to-online reinforcement learning by integrating uncertainty estimation through a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) and optimistic exploration strategies. This framework addresses the challenges of constrained exploratory behavior and state-action distribution shift by guiding exploration towards high-uncertainty actions and ensuring the adaptability of exploitation to different uncertainty levels. The empirical results demonstrate that SUNG outperforms existing methods across various environments, highlighting its potential in improving sample efficiency and handling distribution shifts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the critical challenges of constrained exploratory behavior and state-action distribution shift in offline-to-online RL, which are crucial for practical applications.
- The proposed framework, SUNG, is a generic approach that can be integrated with different offline RL methods, enhancing its applicability and adaptability.
- The use of uncertainty quantification to guide exploration and exploitation is a novel and effective approach, which is supported by empirical results showing improved sample efficiency and performance in various environments.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed methodologies and experimental results.
- The inclusion of an ablation study and hyper-parameter analysis provides a deeper understanding of the framework's components and their impact on performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed methods, particularly in how the VAE-based uncertainty estimation is integrated into the policy learning process.
- The choice of baselines for comparison is limited, which could affect the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The experimental results, while promising, are not consistently superior across all environments and tasks, raising questions about the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the VAE, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with existing methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and the handling of distribution shifts.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed framework handle the computational cost associated with the VAE, particularly in practical applications where computational resources may be limited?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other existing methods, including those that use ensemble techniques for uncertainty estimation?
- How does the VAE-based uncertainty estimation compare to other methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and the ability to handle different types of distribution shifts?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of baselines and the rationale behind their selection?
- Is there a possibility to extend the framework to handle more complex scenarios, such as those involving multiple agents or more complex environments?
