 **Summary:**
The paper introduces UniPredict, a novel method leveraging large language models (LLMs) to predict outcomes in tabular data, addressing the limitations of traditional discriminative modeling methods. The approach involves training a single LLM on a diverse set of tabular datasets and using it to predict target variables based on instructions, which is evaluated against various baselines. The methodology includes a novel prompting technique and a target augmentation strategy, which are shown to enhance the model's performance. The paper claims significant improvements over existing methods, particularly in low-resource settings. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned, as similar methods have been previously explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide detailed descriptions of the dataset and experiments, which enhances the reproducibility of the study.
- The methodology is novel, particularly the use of target augmentation and prompting techniques, which are not commonly used in previous studies.
- The paper demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods, especially in low-resource settings, showing the potential of LLMs for tabular data prediction.
- The experiments are extensive, covering a wide range of datasets and setups, which strengthens the credibility of the results.
- The paper includes an ablation study that helps in understanding the impact of different components of the model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited, as similar methods using LLMs for tabular data prediction have been previously explored.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that use LLMs for tabular data prediction.
- The method's scalability is questionable, as the number of datasets used for training is relatively small compared to the potential of LLMs.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of data leakage, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The method's performance is not consistently superior across all datasets, and there are instances where it underperforms compared to traditional methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the method, which could affect its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to existing methods, particularly in terms of the use of LLMs for tabular data prediction?
- How does the method handle data leakage, and what steps were taken to ensure that the results are not biased by this issue?
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of the number of datasets used for training and the potential for larger-scale experiments?
- How does the method perform in terms of computational efficiency, especially considering the use of LLMs which can be resource-intensive?
- The paper mentions using GPT-2 for training. Could the authors clarify whether this is a typo and if so, why was GPT-2 chosen over GPT-3.5 for this task?
- How does the method compare to other LLMs, particularly those that have been specifically optimized for tabular data prediction?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
