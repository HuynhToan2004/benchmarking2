 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Variational Autoencoder (VAE) model, termed PPGVAE, designed to address the challenges of sparsity and separation in fitness landscapes, particularly in protein design. This model prioritizes high-fitness samples in the latent space, ensuring that these samples are more likely to be generated, which is crucial for optimizing protein sequences. The approach is tested across various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving the performance of model-based optimization (MBO) methods. Despite the innovative approach, concerns are raised regarding the novelty of the method and its comparison to existing techniques.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of protein design, focusing on the optimization of fitness landscapes which are typically sparse and rugged.
- The proposed method, PPGVAE, is innovative and well-articulated, with a clear explanation of its theoretical underpinnings and its application to both discrete and continuous design spaces.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The experimental setup is robust, with comprehensive results demonstrating the effectiveness of PPGVAE across various datasets and scenarios.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the proposed method, which is beneficial for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- There is a noticeable absence of comparisons with existing methods, particularly those that use weighted MLE or other techniques to address the challenges of sparsity and separation in fitness landscapes.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which is a significant concern given the potential for the VAE to learn the training distribution too closely.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse datasets and scenarios to better demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameters used in the experiments, as these can significantly impact the performance of the model.
- There is a lack of discussion on the scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications in high-dimensional spaces.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might impact its practical application?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods, particularly those that use weighted MLE or other techniques to address the challenges of sparsity and separation in fitness landscapes?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and the definition of "separation" in different contexts?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of overfitting, and what measures have been taken to prevent this?
- Could the authors provide more details on the hyperparameters used in the experiments and their impact on the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method perform in high-dimensional spaces, and what are the implications for its scalability?
