 **Summary:**
The paper explores the integration of expert demonstrations in reinforcement learning (RL) through a novel demonstration-regularized RL framework, focusing on behavior cloning and regularized BPI. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the sample complexity of this framework, including in finite and linear MDPs, and discuss its application in RLHF. The paper introduces a new algorithm, UCBVI-Ent+, and discusses its theoretical underpinnings and practical implications. The theoretical analysis is supported by empirical results, although the paper lacks extensive empirical validation and comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the sample complexity of the proposed demonstration-regularized RL framework, which is applicable to both finite and linear MDPs.
- The authors have developed a new algorithm, UCBVI-Ent+, which is a variant of UCBVI-Ent with improved sample complexity.
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of the theoretical results and a detailed description of the algorithms used.
- The paper introduces a novel demonstration-regularized RLHF algorithm, which combines demonstration-regularized RL with maximum likelihood estimation of the reward given preferences dataset.
- The paper is the first to provide sample complexity rates for BPI with demonstrations, offering a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical validation of the proposed algorithm, which is crucial for demonstrating its practical effectiveness.
- There is a significant gap between the theoretical analysis and the practical implementation of the algorithms, particularly in the transition from theoretical results to practical implementations.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed algorithms, such as the assumptions required for their applicability and the potential negative societal impacts.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the algorithms and the scalability of the results to more complex environments.
- The paper's theoretical results are limited to tabular cases, which may not generalize well to more complex environments like those found in modern RL applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the assumptions required for the applicability of the proposed algorithms?
- How do the authors justify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly in relation to the expert policy being close to optimal?
- What are the implications of the results when the assumptions are not met, and how does this affect the practical applicability of the algorithms?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their work, particularly in the context of RLHF applications?
- How do the authors address the computational complexity of their algorithms, and what are the implications for scalability in more complex environments?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and results, particularly in comparison to existing methods?
