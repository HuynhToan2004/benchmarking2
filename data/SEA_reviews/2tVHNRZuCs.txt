 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Implicit Self-Improvement Technique (PIT), a method that enhances the performance of large language models (LLMs) by leveraging preference data to train a reward model. The PIT framework does not require explicit prompting or additional data, and it is demonstrated to be effective in improving the quality of responses through a curriculum-based reinforcement learning process. The methodology is evaluated on multiple datasets, showing that PIT outperforms prompting-based methods like Self-Refine. The paper also discusses the potential of PIT to reduce the reliance on human annotation and improve the efficiency of training processes.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is intuitive, well-motivated, and easy to implement, with a clear and concise writing style.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over prompting-based methods in various scenarios.
- The method's ability to implicitly learn self-improvement from data is a significant advantage, reducing the need for extensive human annotation efforts.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method across multiple datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in different contexts.
- The use of curriculum-based reinforcement learning is innovative, allowing for a more efficient training process that adapts to the model's current capabilities.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and training time.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as similar methods, such as reward-based reinforcement learning, have been previously explored.
- The method's reliance on a reference response for training may limit its applicability in scenarios where such responses are not available.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for reward hacking in the reward model, which could undermine the effectiveness of the method.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to replicate the results or understand the method's performance in different conditions.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work and a clearer explanation of the method's advantages over existing approaches.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the computational efficiency and training time of the proposed method compared to other methods like Self-Refine?
- How does the proposed method compare to other reward-based reinforcement learning approaches, particularly in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- What is the impact of the reward model's accuracy on the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the method handle scenarios where reference responses are not available, and what are the implications for its applicability?
- Can you provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the method's effectiveness and the improvements it brings about?
- How does the method ensure that the improvements it brings about are not due to random fluctuations or biases in the reward model?
