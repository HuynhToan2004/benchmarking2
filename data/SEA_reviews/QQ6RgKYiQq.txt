 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel NeRF-based method for dynamic scene reconstruction, focusing on rigid part discovery from monocular image sequences. It leverages both Eulerian and Lagrangian representations to model scene motion and part trajectories, ensuring consistency between the two. The methodology involves a cycle-consistency loss to maintain consistency and a motion grouping module to segment dynamic objects into rigid parts. The paper demonstrates its effectiveness through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets, showing promising results in terms of both image quality and part segmentation. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces challenges in handling complex motion patterns and long sequence modeling, which may affect the accuracy and efficiency of the segmentation process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the method and the experimental results, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The methodology introduces innovative approaches using both Eulerian and Lagrangian representations to model scene motion and part trajectories, ensuring consistency between the two.
- The paper demonstrates a novel application of Lagrangian and Eulerian views for dynamic scene reconstruction, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art results in both image quality and part segmentation, showing promise in various downstream tasks.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear figures and a comprehensive evaluation that includes both quantitative and qualitative results.
- The method is efficient in terms of training time, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address how it handles complex motion patterns, particularly in long sequences, which might affect the accuracy and efficiency of the segmentation process.
- There is a lack of clarity on the choice of parameters such as the number of groups and the weight of the cycle consistency loss, which could significantly impact the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process, particularly the choice of training data and the optimization strategy, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not compare its results with existing methods that also use Lagrangian and Eulerian views, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the method, such as the handling of non-rigid motions and the impact of different motion patterns on the segmentation results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method handles complex motion patterns, particularly in long sequences?
- How does the method handle the choice of parameters such as the number of groups and the weight of the cycle consistency loss?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the cyclic consistency loss in the optimization process and its impact on the results?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the motion patterns are not rigid or the motion is non-rigid?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training data and the optimization strategy used in the experiments?
- How does the method compare to existing methods that also use Lagrangian and Eulerian views in dynamic scene reconstruction?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the method in more detail, particularly how it handles non-rigid motions and the impact of different motion patterns on the segmentation results?
