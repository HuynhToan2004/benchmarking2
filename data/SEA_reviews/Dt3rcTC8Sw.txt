 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GEMINI, a novel framework designed to enhance the interpretability of graph neural networks (GNNs) by estimating mutual information (MI) between critical subgraphs and the original graph. This is achieved through a variational distribution that approximates the conditional distribution of critical subgraphs given the original graph. The framework also employs a graph-based CLUB (GCLUB) MI upper bound estimator to optimize the variational distribution. Extensive experiments are conducted to evaluate the effectiveness of GEMINI against existing methods, demonstrating its potential in improving interpretability and prediction performance. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the writing, the fairness of experimental comparisons, and the scalability of the method to larger graphs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, GEMINI, which effectively addresses the challenges of estimating mutual information (MI) in graph neural networks (GNNs) by constructing a variational distribution over critical subgraphs.
- The framework is supported by a theoretical foundation, and its practical implementation is validated through comprehensive experiments across eight datasets, showcasing its potential for enhancing both interpretability and prediction performance.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the proposed methodology and its implementation, which aids in understanding the complexities involved in the estimation of mutual information in GNNs.
- The use of a variational distribution to approximate the conditional distribution of critical subgraphs given the original graph is a significant contribution, as it allows for the derivation of a variational upper bound of the mutual information.
- The paper provides a thorough comparison with both self-interpretable GNNs and post-hoc explanation methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in terms of both interpretability and prediction performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from unclear writing and lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the training procedure and the role of different components such as $q_	heta(G_{sub}|G)$.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the method to larger graphs, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The experimental setup and comparisons are not fair or comprehensive, particularly in terms of the backbone models used and the settings for different baselines.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method to larger graphs, which is a significant limitation given the potential applications in large-scale graph datasets.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the experimental setup, such as the specific datasets used and the hyperparameter settings, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights for future research and improvements.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and implementation of $q_	heta(G_{sub}|G)$ in the training procedure?
- How does the method perform when applied to larger graphs, and what are the computational challenges and potential solutions for scaling the method to such datasets?
- In the experimental results, how do the performance metrics of GEMINI compare when using different backbone models, such as GIN and PNA?
- How does the method handle the issue of sparsity in graphs, and what are the implications for the interpretability and prediction performance?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific datasets used, the hyperparameter settings, and the training procedures for the baseline models?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of interpretability and prediction performance, particularly on larger and more complex graph datasets?
