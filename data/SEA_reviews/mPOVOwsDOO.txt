 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge distillation (KD) by proposing a two-way communication process between a teacher model and a student model. This interactive communication involves the teacher and student models training encoder and decoder components, allowing them to exchange messages and interpret each other's outputs. The methodology is evaluated on benchmark datasets from computer vision and recommendation tasks, demonstrating improved performance over existing distillation techniques. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in methodological details, insufficient comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, and unclear motivation behind the interactive communication.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive review of related works, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method of interactive communication between teacher and student models is innovative and provides a novel approach to knowledge distillation.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing distillation techniques.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including a thorough ablation study and an interesting case study on the ImageNet dataset.
- The method is well-motivated, inspired by the human learning process, and could potentially be applied to various domains, including computer vision and recommendation systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in several methodological details, particularly in the descriptions of the encoder and decoder components and the training process.
- The motivation behind the interactive communication between teacher and student models is not convincingly argued, and the benefits of this communication are not clearly demonstrated.
- The experimental setup is limited, with only a few datasets used for evaluation, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the perceived effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper suffers from some inconsistencies and unclear descriptions, particularly in the figures and the explanations of certain components like the "interpreting step."
- The method's practicality and efficiency are questionable, as the interactive communication between models might not be feasible in real-world scenarios due to computational and resource constraints.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific roles and functions of the encoder and decoder components in the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of datasets and models used in the experiments? How do these choices impact the generalizability of the findings?
- How does the proposed method address the challenges of distribution shifts between pre-training data and downstream tasks?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to illustrate the interactive communication process between teacher and student models?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational and resource requirements associated with the interactive communication?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing knowledge distillation techniques, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
