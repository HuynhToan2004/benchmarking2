 **Summary:**
The paper introduces BIT, a novel pre-trained model for molecular representation learning, which integrates Transformer-M with a Mixture-of-Domain-Experts (MoDE) mechanism to handle both small molecules and proteins. The model is trained using a self-supervised denoising task, leveraging both 2D and 3D structural information. BIT demonstrates superior performance on various downstream tasks such as binding affinity prediction, virtual screening, and molecular property prediction. The model's architecture includes a shared self-attention module and two distinct FFNs, one for small molecules and another for proteins, which allows for multimodal pre-training. The paper also includes extensive experimental evaluations, showing the model's effectiveness in various applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to multimodal pre-training, integrating both 2D and 3D structural information, which is a significant advancement in molecular representation learning.
- The proposed model, BIT, demonstrates superior performance across various downstream tasks, showcasing its effectiveness and versatility.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and includes extensive experimental evaluations, which support the claims of the model's effectiveness.
- The integration of Transformer-M with MoDE is innovative, providing a flexible and efficient framework for handling different data formats and domains.
- The use of a unified corrupt-then-recover objective for pre-training allows for scalability and adaptability to different data types.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the effectiveness of different pre-training tasks and the use of different datasets.
- The novelty of the MoDE mechanism is questionable as it resembles similar mechanisms in existing models like Transformer-M.
- The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to support the claims about the effectiveness of MoDE and the different pre-training tasks.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the specific contributions of MoDE and the necessity of its inclusion in the model.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and computational efficiency of the model, especially when applied to larger or more complex datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the model's limitations or potential negative societal impacts.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of MoDE and how it enhances the model's performance compared to existing methods?
- How does the model handle the scalability issues when applied to larger or more complex datasets?
- Can the authors provide more detailed ablation studies to support the claims about the effectiveness of MoDE and the different pre-training tasks?
- How does the model handle the potential for overfitting, especially when trained on smaller or less diverse datasets?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the model, including training and inference times, and the resources required?
- How does the model handle the representation of different molecules and proteins, especially in terms of the diversity and complexity of the data?
