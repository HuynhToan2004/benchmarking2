 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Traffic Forecasting Mixture of Experts (TFMoE), a novel approach to continual learning for traffic forecasting in evolving traffic networks. TFMoE segments traffic data into homogeneous groups and assigns an expert model to each group, utilizing a reconstruction-based gating mechanism to make predictions. The model employs a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) to ensure knowledge consolidation and prevent catastrophic forgetting. Extensive experiments on the PEMSD3-Stream dataset demonstrate TFMoE's effectiveness in addressing catastrophic forgetting and its superior performance compared to existing models. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, insufficient comparison with recent works, and unclear presentation in certain sections.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a detailed explanation of the proposed methodology, which includes the use of a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) for knowledge consolidation and a reconstruction-based gating mechanism to prevent catastrophic forgetting.
- The experiments are comprehensive, involving multiple evaluation metrics and different time horizons, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in addressing catastrophic forgetting.
- The use of graph structure learning and diffusion convolutional layers in the predictor enhances the model's ability to capture spatial dependencies among sensors and temporal dynamics of traffic.
- The paper provides a detailed ablation study that analyzes the contributions of individual components of the proposed method, which helps in understanding the model's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as the idea of using a mixture of experts for traffic forecasting has been previously explored, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods.
- The paper lacks a comparison with recent works on continual learning for traffic forecasting, which could have provided a better context for evaluating the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper suffers from unclear writing in some sections, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key terms and processes, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other continual learning approaches for traffic forecasting, especially those that have been recently proposed?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the training process for the predictor and the reconstruction-based gating mechanism, particularly in how they are trained and their impact on the final predictions?
- How does the paper address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, and what specific measures are taken to prevent it?
- In the context of the PEMSD3-Stream dataset, how does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data is not as diverse or where there are significant variations in traffic patterns over time?
- Can the authors clarify the definitions and roles of certain terms and processes, such as the gating term and the gating weights, to enhance the clarity and understanding of the proposed method?
