 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a theoretical framework aimed at quantifying the reconstruction error in federated learning (FL) data reconstruction attacks. It proposes a method to bound the error between the original data and the reconstructed data, using the Lipschitz constant of the data reconstruction function. The framework is applied to various FL settings and datasets, comparing the performance of different data reconstruction attacks. The authors also discuss the impact of different FL settings on the reconstruction error. Despite the theoretical contributions, the paper has been critiqued for its limited applicability to practical scenarios and its reliance on strong assumptions that may not hold in real-world FL environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel theoretical framework to quantify the reconstruction error in federated learning (FL), which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis is well-structured and easy to follow, providing a clear explanation of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation that supports the theoretical findings, enhancing the credibility of the proposed method.
- The methodology is well-presented, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue in FL, namely the vulnerability to data reconstruction attacks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions, particularly Assumption 5, are considered strong and may not hold in practical FL settings where the reconstruction function is not known a priori.
- The practical applicability of the proposed method is limited, as it does not address non-convex loss functions, which are commonly used in FL.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are somewhat limited, as the analysis primarily focuses on convex loss functions and does not extend to more complex settings.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its applicability and effectiveness.
- There is a significant gap between the theoretical results and the empirical results, which raises concerns about the practical relevance and accuracy of the theoretical analysis.
- The paper's writing and presentation could be improved to better engage the reader and clarify the contributions and implications of the research.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when the reconstruction function is not known a priori?
- Can the proposed method be extended to non-convex loss functions, and if so, how would the analysis need to be modified?
- What are the implications of the strong assumptions made in the paper, particularly Assumption 5, for the practical applicability of the proposed method?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their method and how these might affect its practical utility?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for quantifying reconstruction error in FL, and what are the advantages or disadvantages of each approach?
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between the theoretical results and the empirical results, particularly the observed discrepancies between the two?
