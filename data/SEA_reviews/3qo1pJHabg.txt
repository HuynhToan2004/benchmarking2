 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Language-Driven Resample Continuous Representation (LRR), aimed at enhancing the robustness of object tracking against adversarial attacks. The LRR leverages a spatial-temporal implicit representation (STIR) and a language-driven resample network (LResampleNet) to reconstruct clean frames from perturbed ones, thereby improving tracking accuracy under adversarial conditions. The method is tested on various datasets and demonstrates significant improvements in tracking accuracy under adversarial conditions, with up to 90% relative improvement over state-of-the-art methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its lack of comprehensive baseline comparisons, limited experimental validation, and potential over-reliance on the quality of the object template.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method, LRR, is innovative and effective, demonstrating a significant improvement in tracking accuracy under adversarial conditions, with up to 90% relative improvement.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are well-designed, with comprehensive ablation studies and comparisons against a variety of baselines.
- The method is technically sound, with a clear motivation and detailed implementation, and the results are convincing, showing a clear improvement over existing methods.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and good presentation, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The method's generalization to different trackers and datasets, such as transformer-based trackers and the ToMP-50 tracker, demonstrates its versatility and applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- There is a lack of comprehensive baseline comparisons, particularly with recent and relevant works such as RITAA and DiffPure, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the method's performance.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the time complexity of the proposed method, which could impact its practical deployment in real-time applications.
- The method's reliance on the quality of the object template could limit its effectiveness in scenarios where the template is of poor quality or not representative of the object's appearance in the video.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for ethical implications.
- The method's effectiveness against non-noise-based attacks, such as motion blur, is not evaluated, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the time complexity of the proposed method and how it compares to other defense methods in terms of computational efficiency?
- How does the method perform when the object template is of poor quality or not representative of the object's appearance in the video?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these could be mitigated?
- Can the method be adapted to work with different types of adversarial attacks, such as motion blur attacks?
- How does the method compare in terms of accuracy and efficiency with other defense methods, particularly in scenarios where the tracker is not under attack?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process and the choice of hyperparameters, such as the number of frames used for training and the size of the training dataset?
