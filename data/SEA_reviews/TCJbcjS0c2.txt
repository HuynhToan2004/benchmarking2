 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LASER, a gradient compression scheme designed for distributed training over noisy channels, leveraging the inherent low-rank structure of gradients. The method is evaluated against baselines on various tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing communication costs while maintaining performance. The theoretical analysis supports the method's convergence rate, showing that it is competitive with traditional SGD methods. However, the paper's theoretical contributions are seen as limited, focusing primarily on convergence rates without significant novelty in the proof techniques. Additionally, the practical applicability of the method is questioned due to its reliance on specific channel models and assumptions that may not hold in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear and simple algorithm that is easy to implement.
- The proposed method is effective in reducing communication costs and improving performance, particularly in noisy channels, as demonstrated through both theoretical analysis and empirical results.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to gradient compression that utilizes the low-rank structure of gradients, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided supports the method's convergence rate, which is similar to that of classical SGD, and shows that it is competitive with existing methods.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and a comprehensive experimental setup that includes a variety of benchmarks and baseline methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited in scope, focusing primarily on convergence rates without significant novelty in the proof techniques.
- The method's reliance on the assumption that the channel is additive and slow-fading may limit its practical applicability in real-world scenarios where channels can be more complex.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and communication cost of the proposed method, which are crucial for understanding its efficiency and scalability.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant works in the field, which could provide a better context for the paper's contributions.
- The paper's assumptions and theoretical results are not sufficiently robust, particularly in scenarios where the channel noise is not additive or the SNR is low.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity and communication cost of the proposed method with existing methods, particularly in scenarios where the channel noise is high?
- How does the method perform when the channel noise is not additive, and what are the implications for the convergence rate and performance?
- Could you clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly regarding the channel noise and the SNR?
- How does the method perform when the rank of the gradient matrix is not low, and what are the implications for the convergence rate and performance in such scenarios?
- Could you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in the context of its application in wireless distributed optimization?
