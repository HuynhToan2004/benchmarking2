 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel hybrid classification-regression adaptive loss (HCRA) designed to enhance the performance of object detection by addressing inconsistencies across tasks and focusing on difficult-to-train samples. The proposed loss function, HCRA, is integrated with the Residual of Classification and IoU (RCI) module and the Conditioning Factor (CF) to optimize training by focusing on task inconsistencies and difficult-to-train samples. The paper also introduces the Expanded Adaptive Training Sample Selection (EATSS) strategy to provide additional samples with classification and regression inconsistencies. The methodology is evaluated on the COCO dataset, showing significant improvements over existing methods. However, the paper suffers from issues such as unclear writing, numerous hyperparameters, and a lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-motivated, addressing the challenges of inconsistencies across tasks and focusing on difficult-to-train samples, which is crucial for enhancing the performance of object detection models.
- The proposed loss function, HCRA, is novel and effectively integrates the Residual of Classification and IoU (RCI) module and the Conditioning Factor (CF) to address task inconsistencies and focus on difficult-to-train samples.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear and logical structure, making it easy to follow.
- The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method through extensive evaluations on the COCO dataset.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the proposed methodologies and their impacts.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from unclear writing, particularly in the description of the RCI module and the CF module, which could be improved by adding more detailed explanations and examples.
- There are numerous hyperparameters involved in the HCRA loss function, which could make the method complex and difficult to apply in practice.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the claims of superiority.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed ablation studies to better understand the impact of different components of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations and examples of the RCI module and the CF module to clarify their operations and effects?
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets, such as VOC and Cityscapes, and what are the potential challenges or benefits in these different contexts?
- Could the authors conduct a comparative analysis of the computational complexity of their method against other state-of-the-art methods to assess its practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different backbone models, such as ResNeXt and Hourglass, and what are the potential challenges or benefits in these different configurations?
- Can the authors provide more details on the limitations of their proposed method and discuss potential strategies for addressing these limitations?
