 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel three-stage progressive conditional diffusion model (PCDM) for pose-guided person image synthesis. The model predicts global features of the target image using a prior diffusion model, establishes dense correspondence between source and target images using an inpainting diffusion model, and refines the coarse-grained image using a refining diffusion model. This approach is designed to handle the inconsistency in pose between source and target images, enhancing the quality and consistency of the synthesized images. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive experiments, including comparisons with existing methods and user studies. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the methodology, and the fairness of the comparisons.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the challenging problem of pose-guided person image synthesis, a significant research topic in computer vision.
- The proposed three-stage progressive conditional diffusion model (PCDM) is a novel approach that effectively handles the inconsistency in pose between source and target images.
- Extensive experiments have been conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, including comparisons with existing methods and user studies.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The method is technically sound and includes an ablation study to show the effectiveness of each stage in the PCDMs.
- The application of the method in person re-identification tasks shows potential for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential improvements.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it appears to be a combination of existing methods without significant new technical contributions.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and inference time of the proposed method, which could affect its practical deployment.
- There is a lack of quantitative results for the ablation study, which could have strengthened the argument for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, including more recent and relevant works.
- The writing and presentation of the methodology could be improved for clarity and to avoid potential misinterpretations.
- The paper does not adequately address the fairness of comparisons, particularly in the use of different datasets and evaluation metrics.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the technical novelty of their method compared to existing works?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of training and inference time with other state-of-the-art methods?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and inference time, possibly including the time taken for each stage of the PCDMs?
- What is the impact of the proposed method on the computational resources required for training and inference?
- How does the method perform when the source image and target pose are significantly different, and what are the implications for the quality of the synthesized images?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their method and potential directions for future research?
