 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Heterogeneous Federated Temporal Difference (HFTD) algorithm for federated reinforcement learning (FRL) in a heterogeneous environment, where agents interact with different Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) and have varying computational capabilities. The HFTD algorithm iteratively aggregates agents' local stochastic gradients for TD learning, allowing for different numbers of local iterations to enhance computational capacity. It provides finite-time convergence guarantees under IID sampling and Markovian sampling, demonstrating linear convergence and a sample complexity of O(1/Nε2). The paper also addresses the challenges of data heterogeneity and computation heterogeneity, providing a novel analysis of the convergence error in heterogeneous federated learning scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear problem statements and a logical flow.
- It introduces the first result of asymptotic convergence in federated reinforcement learning (FRL) with heterogeneous environments, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the convergence error in heterogeneous federated learning scenarios, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of the proposed algorithm.
- The authors demonstrate the linear convergence of the HFTD algorithm, which is a notable achievement in the field of reinforcement learning.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with existing works, highlighting the novelty of the proposed algorithm and its theoretical contributions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical validation of the algorithm's performance on real-world datasets, which could have strengthened the claims made about its effectiveness.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the bounded gradient variance and smoothness of the function approximation, are not clearly justified or explained, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithm and its practical implications, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the computational costs and communication requirements of the HFTD algorithm with those of existing algorithms, which could be crucial for understanding its efficiency in practical scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the privacy implications of the federated learning setup, which is a significant concern in practical applications of the algorithm.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the bounded gradient variance and smoothness of the function approximation?
- How does the HFTD algorithm perform in terms of computational costs and communication requirements compared to existing algorithms?
- Could the authors discuss the practical implications of the assumptions made in the paper and how they might affect the performance of the algorithm in real-world scenarios?
- Is there a possibility to include empirical validation of the algorithm's performance on real-world datasets to support the theoretical claims?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the HFTD algorithm with existing algorithms in terms of sample complexity and convergence rate?
- How does the federated learning setup affect the privacy of the agents involved, and what measures are taken to ensure the privacy of the data shared between agents?
