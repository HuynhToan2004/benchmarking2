 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a Quadratic Synchronization Rule (QSR) for distributed deep learning, focusing on dynamically adjusting the synchronization period in local gradient methods. The QSR is proposed to reduce communication overhead and improve test accuracy by setting the synchronization period in proportion to the learning rate squared. This approach is theoretically grounded and empirically validated through experiments on ImageNet, showing that QSR can significantly enhance the generalization performance of local SGD and AdamW, even when the communication frequency is reduced. The paper also discusses the practical implications of QSR in reducing wall-clock time and improving test accuracy in large-scale distributed training scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel Quadratic Synchronization Rule (QSR) for distributed deep learning, which dynamically adjusts the synchronization period based on the learning rate, thereby reducing communication overhead and improving test accuracy.
- Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate that QSR outperforms existing methods like parallel SGD and constant H, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings, making it accessible to readers.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method across various learning rate schedules and model architectures.
- The QSR is a simple yet effective strategy that can be applied to various optimizers and neural network models, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the QSR, particularly its applicability to different learning rate schedules and its effectiveness in scenarios with different model architectures or data types.
- The theoretical analysis of QSR is limited to the SGD optimizer; extending it to other optimizers like Adam could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its applicability.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the QSR is applied in practice, particularly the tuning of the hyperparameters such as H_base and α.
- The paper could benefit from more diverse experimental setups, including different data types and model architectures, to validate the generalizability of the QSR.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a concern in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the QSR is applied in practice, particularly the tuning of the hyperparameters such as H_base and α?
- How does the QSR perform when applied to different learning rate schedules, such as those used in other popular training recipes?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the choice of the growth coefficient α and its impact on the effectiveness of the QSR?
- How does the QSR perform when applied to different model architectures or data types, such as language models or different image datasets?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated in practical applications?
