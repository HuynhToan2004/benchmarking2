 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SECToR, a method designed to enhance the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) by enabling them to learn new skills through a self-education process. SECToR utilizes chain-of-thought reasoning to generate training data, which is then used to fine-tune the model, improving its performance in specific tasks like addition. The method involves a curriculum learning phase followed by a self-training phase, where the model is fine-tuned on its own outputs to mitigate errors and improve its performance. The paper demonstrates that the method can effectively teach models to perform arithmetic tasks, particularly addition, and discusses the potential for broader applications. However, concerns are raised about the scalability of the method beyond simple arithmetic tasks and its generalizability to other types of reasoning or more complex mathematical operations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the methodology and results, making it easy to understand.
- The novelty of the approach lies in the use of chain-of-thought reasoning as a policy improvement operator, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper demonstrates that large language models (LLMs) can learn new skills through a self-education process, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is robust, with a focus on error mitigation through self-consistency checks, which is crucial for ensuring the reliability of the trained models.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of how the method works, including the use of a curriculum learning phase to mitigate errors and the introduction of a "slow" and "fast" training phase to improve the model's performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper focuses solely on arithmetic tasks, which limits the generalizability of the findings to other types of reasoning or more complex mathematical operations.
- The method's scalability is questionable, as it relies heavily on the model's ability to generalize from smaller to larger problems, which may not hold true for more complex tasks.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other self-training methods, which could have provided a better understanding of the method's advantages and disadvantages.
- There is a lack of discussion on the societal impact of the work, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of the research.
- The paper does not adequately address how the method could be applied to other types of reasoning or more complex mathematical operations, which limits the practical applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide examples of how the method could be adapted to teach other types of reasoning or more complex mathematical operations?
- How does the method compare to other self-training methods, particularly in terms of scalability and generalizability?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of the work, particularly in terms of the broader implications of self-educating models?
- How does the method handle the issue of error avalanching in more complex tasks, and what steps are taken to mitigate this effect?
- What are the specific advantages of using chain-of-thought reasoning as a policy improvement operator over other methods, and how does this approach compare to other self-improving LLMs?
