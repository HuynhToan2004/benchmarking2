 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of diffusion models in language modeling, focusing on the scalability and effectiveness of diffusion language models (DLMs) in various tasks. It begins by demonstrating the intrinsic connection between masked language models and discrete diffusion models, which enables the transformation of pretrained masked language models into diffusion language models. The authors then evaluate the performance of these models across different tasks such as translation, summarization, and zero-shot/few-shot learning. The results indicate that DLMs can perform competitively with autoregressive models when scaled appropriately, although they still lag behind in some advanced capabilities like reasoning. The paper also discusses the potential of DLMs in handling causal reasoning and suggests that further research is needed to fully unlock their potential.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors effectively demonstrate the intrinsic connection between masked language models and discrete diffusion models, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, covering a range of tasks and scales, providing valuable insights into the scalability and performance of diffusion language models.
- The paper presents a promising direction for future research in the field of diffusion language models, suggesting that these models could potentially outperform autoregressive models in certain scenarios.
- The paper includes a detailed discussion on the limitations of diffusion language models, which is crucial for understanding the current state of the technology and identifying areas for improvement.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other diffusion models, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the performance and scalability of diffusion language models.
- The evaluation of the models is limited to a few tasks and datasets, which might not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational efficiency of diffusion language models compared to autoregressive models, which is a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the experimental settings and the results, particularly in the sections where the results are less clear or counterintuitive.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the current models, such as the inability to handle complex reasoning tasks, which could undermine the claims of the paper.

**Questions:**
- How does the performance of diffusion language models compare to other diffusion models, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the training and evaluation procedures, to ensure reproducibility and transparency?
- How do the authors address the limitations of diffusion language models, such as the inability to handle complex reasoning tasks? What steps are being taken to address these limitations in future research?
- In the context of the results presented, how do the authors explain the observed performance gaps between different models and tasks? What factors contribute to these gaps, and how can they be addressed?
- Can the authors provide more examples or detailed explanations of the reasoning abilities demonstrated by the diffusion language models, particularly in the context of causal reasoning and backtracking?
