 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the quantification of epistemic and aleatoric uncertainties in hyperspectral image classification (HSIC) using graph convolutional neural networks (GCNs). It introduces two regularization terms, physics-guided unmixing-based regularization and evidence-based total variation regularization, to address the limitations of the uncertainty cross-entropy (UCE) loss function. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of these regularization terms through experiments on three real-world HSIC datasets, showcasing improvements in out-of-distribution (OOD) and misclassification detection. The paper also discusses the theoretical implications of these regularization terms and their impact on uncertainty quantification in HSIC.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces two novel regularization terms, physics-guided unmixing-based regularization and evidence-based total variation regularization, which are effective in improving the performance of uncertainty quantification in HSIC.
- The proposed regularization terms are supported by theoretical analysis, which enhances the credibility of the findings.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methods and their implications.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed regularization terms, highlighting their practical utility in real-world applications.
- The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in HSIC, which has not been extensively explored in previous literature.
- The use of graph convolutional neural networks (GCNs) and evidential graph neural networks (EGCNs) in HSIC is highlighted, providing a new perspective on the application of these models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could include potential biases in the unmixing regularization term and the sensitivity of the results to the mixing matrix and mixing model.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the generalizability of the proposed method to other types of datasets or different architectures, which could limit its applicability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other methods that use different loss functions or regularization terms, which could help in understanding the advantages of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method, which could be a concern for large-scale datasets or complex models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the theoretical assumptions made in the analysis, particularly in relation to the linear mixing model and its applicability to different types of data.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for ethical AI applications.
