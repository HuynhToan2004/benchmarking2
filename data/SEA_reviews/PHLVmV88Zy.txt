 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel unconstrained loss function for Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) and its applications to various CCA-related problems, including stochastic CCA, deep CCA, and self-supervised learning. This loss function is shown to have no spurious local minima, ensuring that local optima are global optima. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through extensive experiments on several benchmarks, including MediaMill, splitMNIST, mfeat, and the UK Biobank dataset. The proposed method is also compared to existing state-of-the-art methods, showing competitive performance and potential for further improvements. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the loss function, including its connection to the Eckhart-Young inequality, and provides a unified framework for CCA and its extensions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel unconstrained loss function for CCA that is applicable to various CCA-related problems, including stochastic CCA, deep CCA, and self-supervised learning.
- The proposed method is shown to have no spurious local minima, ensuring that local optima are global optima, which is a significant advantage in optimization.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a detailed discussion of the proposed method and its applications.
- The methodology is supported by extensive experiments on several benchmarks, including MediaMill, splitMNIST, mfeat, and the UK Biobank dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness and potential for further improvements.
- The paper provides a unified framework for CCA and its extensions, which can facilitate future research in this area.
- The theoretical analysis of the loss function is sound, and the paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and memory requirements.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods, as shown in Figure 1(a) where the proposed method performs worse than the state-of-the-art method in terms of PCC.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical application.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing methods in terms of the number of epochs required to achieve the same performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to very large datasets.
