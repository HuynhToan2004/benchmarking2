 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of clean-priority learning in neural networks under label noise, focusing on the behavior of sample-wise gradients in infinitely wide networks. It demonstrates that in the early stages of training, gradients from clean samples dominate those from noisy samples, leading to a U-shaped test error curve. This behavior is explained through theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, showing that the clean samples are learned first, and the noise is fit later. The paper also discusses the diminishing dominance of clean gradients and the eventual learning of noisy samples. Despite its theoretical and empirical contributions, the paper has been criticized for its lack of novelty and the simplicity of its experimental setups, which may not generalize well to more complex or realistic scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and interesting problem in the field of neural networks, focusing on the phenomenon of clean-priority learning and its implications.
- The theoretical analysis and empirical evidence provided are strong, with clear explanations and intuitive examples that aid in understanding the complex concepts.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers and contributing to the clarity of the presentation.

- The experimental results are convincing, providing empirical evidence to support the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it does not introduce new theoretical or empirical results beyond those already known in the field.
- The experimental setups are limited and may not generalize well to more complex or realistic scenarios, such as those involving larger or more varied datasets.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the work and the assumptions made, which could affect the applicability and reliability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the findings, particularly in practical applications and real-world scenarios.
