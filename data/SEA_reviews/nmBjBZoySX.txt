 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AdaGLT, a novel method for graph lottery ticket (GLT) identification, which addresses the challenges of scalability and adaptability in existing GLT algorithms. AdaGLT utilizes a dynamic and automated framework to adaptively adjust sparsity levels and layer-wise pruning, which is particularly beneficial for deep GNNs. The method is validated through extensive experiments, showing improvements over existing GLT methods across various datasets and GNN architectures. However, the paper is criticized for its presentation clarity, lack of comparison with recent GLT methods, and potential issues with the scalability and practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear and concise presentation of the methodology.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method, demonstrating its superiority over existing GLT methods across various datasets and GNN architectures.
- The proposed method introduces several innovative techniques, such as adaptive layer sparsification, dynamic restoration, and automated pruning scheduling, which enhance the flexibility and robustness of GLT methods.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis that guarantees the effectiveness of the proposed method, adding a solid foundation to the research.
- The methodology is general and can be applied to various GNN architectures, making it a versatile tool for graph learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, with important details and notations not adequately explained, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the methodology and results.
- There is a significant omission of recent GLT methods in the comparison, which limits the evaluation of the proposed method against the state-of-the-art.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The methodology and its implementation details are not clearly explained, which could hinder the reproducibility and adoption of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that the proposed method can scale up to large-scale datasets, which is a significant limitation given the increasing importance of large-scale graph learning.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of over-smoothing, which is a critical challenge in deep GNNs.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the proposed method can be applied to inductive settings, given the focus on transductive settings in the paper?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of over-smoothing in deep GNNs, and can it be adapted to address this issue effectively?
- Could the authors clarify the notations and definitions used in the paper, particularly those related to the graph neural network model and the pruning methodology?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed method compare to existing GLT methods, and what are the practical implications of this complexity in real-world applications?
- Can the authors provide more evidence or experiments to support the claim that the proposed method can scale up to large-scale datasets?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent GLT methods that have been published but not included in the comparison?
