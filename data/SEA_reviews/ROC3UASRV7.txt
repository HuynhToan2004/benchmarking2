 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new derivative-free optimization algorithm, RACE-CARS, which builds upon the RACOS algorithm by incorporating a region-shrinking technique to enhance convergence speed. The authors propose a new parameter, the shattering rate, which is used to upper bound the query complexity of the algorithm. Theoretical analysis and empirical results are presented to demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in presentation, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key terms and algorithms, and the limited scope of its experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by incorporating a region-shrinking technique into the RACOS algorithm, which enhances the understanding of the computational complexity of derivative-free optimization algorithms.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, with the introduction of a new parameter, the shattering rate, which is used to upper bound the query complexity of the algorithm.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in the context of black-box tuning for language-model-as-a-service, showing that RACE-CARS outperforms SRACOS in certain scenarios.
- The paper provides a clear theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm, which is a significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key terms and algorithms. For instance, the definition of the shattering rate is not clear, and the algorithm descriptions are not well-explained, making it difficult for readers to understand the main contributions of the paper.
- The experimental setup is limited, focusing only on a few benchmark test functions and a specific application scenario. This limits the generalizability of the results and the applicability of the findings to a broader range of optimization problems.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its computational efficiency and practical applicability to high-dimensional problems.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the assumptions and limitations of the theoretical analysis, which could affect the reliability and applicability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of the shattering rate in the context of the algorithm? How does this parameter impact the performance and scalability of the algorithm?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art derivative-free optimization algorithms in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- What are the specific challenges and limitations of the proposed method when applied to high-dimensional problems?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the assumptions and limitations of the theoretical analysis? How do these assumptions affect the applicability and reliability of the results?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the objective is not dimensionally local Holder continuous? Are there any specific scenarios where the method might not be effective?
