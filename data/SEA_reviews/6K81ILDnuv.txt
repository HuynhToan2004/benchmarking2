 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) to solve integro-differential equations, focusing on the bias introduced by approximating integrals using Monte Carlo sampling. It proposes three methods: deterministic sampling, double sampling, and delayed target, to address this bias. The paper is structured around these methods, each with varying degrees of practicality and theoretical support. The experimental results show that the delayed target method can achieve comparable performance to methods using larger sample sizes, but it requires careful tuning of hyperparameters. The paper also discusses the challenges of optimizing these methods and the potential for broader application in other areas of machine learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs), specifically focusing on the bias introduced by approximating integrals using Monte Carlo sampling.
- It provides a clear and detailed exposition of the problem, the proposed methods, and the experimental results, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear structure that effectively communicates the key ideas and findings.
- The authors provide a thorough discussion of the limitations and potential future work, showing a thoughtful approach to the research.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods in reducing the bias in PINN training, and the paper includes detailed ablation studies to support the claims.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a formal definition and theoretical analysis of the bias, which could be crucial for understanding the problem and its implications.
- There is a notable absence of comparisons with other methods, such as importance sampling and quasi-Monte Carlo, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly the tuning of hyperparameters and the potential for overfitting.
- The experimental results, while convincing, are limited to a few examples, which may not be sufficient to generalize the findings to broader applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implementation of the methods, including the computational complexity and the impact of different hyperparameters.
- There are some minor typographical and formatting errors that need correction.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition and theoretical analysis of the bias mentioned in the paper?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods, such as importance sampling and quasi-Monte Carlo, in terms of effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- In the experimental results, how were the hyperparameters tuned, and what were the specific values used?
- How does the method perform in terms of computational complexity, especially when the sample size is large?
- Can you provide more details on the practical implementation of the delayed target method, including the computational graph and the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance?
- In the experimental results, how does the method handle the stochastic nature of the target, and what are the implications for the convergence of the training process?
