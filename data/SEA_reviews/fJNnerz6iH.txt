 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel parametrization for hypernetworks, termed Magnitude Invariant Parametrizations (MIP), aimed at addressing the instability in training hypernetworks caused by a proportional relationship between input and output magnitudes. MIP modifies the hypernetwork formulation by introducing an encoding function that maps inputs to a constant-norm vector space and treating hypernetwork predictions as additive changes to the main network parameters. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of MIP through extensive experiments on various hypernetwork tasks, showing improvements in training stability and convergence. The paper also presents a new PyTorch library, HyperLight, which facilitates the development of hypernetwork models. Despite its strengths, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty and the scope of its experiments, which primarily focus on simple hypernetwork architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant issue in hypernetwork training by identifying and characterizing the optimization problem caused by the proportionality between input and output magnitudes.
- The proposed solution, Magnitude Invariant Parametrizations (MIP), is simple yet effective, as demonstrated by extensive experimental results across various hypernetwork tasks.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive experimental setup that includes ablation studies and robustness analysis.
- The authors have developed an open-source PyTorch library, HyperLight, which can be used to turn existing networks into MIP-based hypernetworks, facilitating the adoption of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed parametrization, comparing it with the standard formulation and various normalization strategies, showing that it consistently leads to faster convergence and more stable training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is somewhat limited as the proposed parametrization, while effective, is seen as a straightforward adaptation of existing techniques.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to simple hypernetwork architectures and a narrow set of tasks, which may not fully demonstrate the general applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other hypernetwork initialization methods, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- There is a need for more detailed discussion and analysis of the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the method may not perform optimally or where it introduces additional computational costs.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of the impact of different input encodings and output parametrizations on the performance of hypernetworks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the rationale behind the choice of the specific input encoding function used in the MIP parametrization?
- How does the proposed parametrization perform when applied to more complex hypernetwork architectures, such as those with residual connections or multiple output heads?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational overhead introduced by the MIP parametrization, particularly in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the proposed parametrization compare with other initialization methods, such as those that consider the hypernetwork and primary network architectures?
- Can the authors provide more examples of the hypernetwork input and the corresponding primary network parameters to illustrate the effectiveness of the proposed parametrization?
