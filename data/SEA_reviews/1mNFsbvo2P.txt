 **Summary:**
The paper explores a Bayesian model to predict the risk of disease for both tested and untested individuals, leveraging domain constraints such as prevalence and expertise to enhance parameter inference. The model is applied to a breast cancer risk prediction case study, demonstrating its utility in predicting cancer diagnoses and identifying suboptimalities in test allocation. Theoretical analysis and synthetic data are used to validate the model's efficacy, showing that domain constraints can improve precision and accuracy of parameter estimates. The model is also tested on real-world data from the UK Biobank, validating its applicability in real-world settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel Bayesian model that captures the selective labels setting, which is particularly relevant for healthcare applications where human decisions can censor outcome data.
- The authors propose two domain constraints, prevalence and expertise, which are shown to improve model estimation and are supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the model's effectiveness in real-world applications.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis that shows how domain constraints can improve precision and accuracy of parameter estimates, and this is supported by empirical evidence on synthetic data.
- The model is shown to be applicable to a variety of settings beyond healthcare, such as criminal justice and lending, indicating its broader utility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the model, including the assumptions made and the potential impact of these assumptions on the model's applicability and accuracy.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the context of causal inference and selective labels problems.
- The model's assumptions, such as the linearity of the risk score and the independence of unobservables, may not hold in all real-world scenarios, which could affect the model's performance.
- The paper could be improved by providing more detailed information on the experimental setup, such as the number of features and the size of the dataset used.
- The paper does not provide a formal definition or detailed discussion on the "expertise constraint," which could be confusing for readers.

**Questions:**
- How does the model perform when the assumptions, such as linearity of the risk score and independence of unobservables, do not hold in real-world scenarios?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the number of features and the size of the dataset used?
- How does the model handle the selection bias in the data, especially when the selection bias is not known?
- In the case study, why was the prevalence constraint set to a specific value, and how does this affect the model's performance?
- How does the model handle the potential for unobserved confounding, and what are the implications of this for the model's accuracy?
- Could you clarify the definition and role of the "expertise constraint" in the model?
