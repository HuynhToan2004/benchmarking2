 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the challenge of benchmarking NLP systems when some systems' scores are missing on certain tasks. It introduces a novel approach using a compatible partial ranking method to impute missing data, which is then aggregated using the Borda count method. The methodology is validated through both synthetic and real-world experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling missing data. The paper also introduces an extended benchmark containing over 131 million scores, which is significantly larger than existing benchmarks. Despite its innovative approach and the creation of a substantial benchmark, the paper has been criticized for its clarity in presentation, lack of comprehensive literature review, and potential oversimplification of the problem.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and practical issue in NLP benchmarking: the handling of missing system scores on tasks.
- The authors propose a novel approach using a compatible partial ranking method to impute missing data, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The methodology is validated through both synthetic and real-world experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling missing data.
- The paper introduces an extended benchmark containing over 131 million scores, which is a substantial contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, making it difficult to follow in places.
- The literature review is incomplete, particularly in the area of handling missing data in benchmarking.
- The problem formulation and the proposed methodology are overly simplistic and do not adequately address the complexity of real-world benchmarking scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its approach, such as the potential biases introduced by the method and the assumptions made about the data.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing methods, which could have strengthened the argument for its effectiveness.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of its method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale benchmarking.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and notations used in the paper, particularly in the problem formulation and methodology sections?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the data is biased or where the assumptions made about the data are not met?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their approach and how these limitations might affect the practical applicability of their method?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods for handling missing data in benchmarking, and what are the advantages of the proposed method over these alternatives?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity of their method and how it scales with the size of the benchmark?
- In the experiments, how does the method perform when the proportion of missing data is high, and what are the implications of this for the reliability of the results?
