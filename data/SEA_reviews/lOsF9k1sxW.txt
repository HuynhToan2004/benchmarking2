 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel backdoor purification framework, Smooth Fine-Tuning (SFT), which leverages the knowledge of the Fisher Information Matrix (FIM) to reoptimize models towards smoother minima, thereby purifying them of backdoor attacks. SFT is designed to be computationally efficient and is evaluated across a variety of datasets and models, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance. The methodology involves a regularizer that preserves the clean data distribution and a fast variant (Fast SFT) that reduces computational overhead. The paper also discusses theoretical justifications and empirical evidence supporting the proposed method's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the content.
- The proposed method is novel, particularly the use of the Fisher Information Matrix (FIM) to guide the re-optimization process, which is both intuitive and theoretically grounded.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on various backdoor defense benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness across different tasks, datasets, and model architectures.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including a thorough ablation study that supports the claims made about the method's effectiveness.
- Theoretical justifications are provided for the method's approach, which enhances the credibility of the proposed solution.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and its application, which makes it accessible and understandable to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and scope.
- There is a lack of clarity on the computational efficiency of the proposed method compared to other fine-tuning methods, which could affect its practical deployment.
- The method's effectiveness is not clearly demonstrated in all experimental settings, particularly in scenarios where it fails to achieve optimal performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the method, especially how it relates to existing smoothness-based methods.
- There are some inconsistencies and unclear explanations in the experimental results and methodology, which could confuse the reader about the method's effectiveness and applicability.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in large-scale applications, which could limit its practical utility.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the computational efficiency of the proposed method compared to other fine-tuning methods, particularly in terms of runtime and resource usage?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where it does not achieve optimal performance, and what are the potential reasons for this?
- Could you provide more details on the theoretical justifications for the method's approach, especially how it relates to existing smoothness-based methods?
- How does the method handle different types of backdoor attacks, and what are the implications for its effectiveness in real-world scenarios?
- Can you provide more insights into the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in large-scale applications, and what challenges or limitations might be encountered in such scenarios?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to include other types of regularizers, and if so, what would be the implications for its effectiveness and computational efficiency?
