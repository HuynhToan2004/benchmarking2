 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for learning stochastic subgoal representations in hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) using a Gaussian process (GP) prior. This method, termed HSSR, models the subgoal space as a distribution over latent states, which are then used to guide hierarchical policy learning. The paper claims that this approach enhances exploration and reduces the risk of convergence to local optima, particularly in stochastic environments. The method was tested on various MuJoCo tasks and showed improved performance compared to existing methods. However, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive experimental validation, and potential issues with the scalability and computational efficiency of the GP-based approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and detailed descriptions of the method, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method, HSSR, introduces a novel approach to learning stochastic subgoal representations using a GP prior, which is a significant advancement over deterministic subgoal representations used in previous methods.
- The methodology is evaluated on multiple MuJoCo tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across different scenarios.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including a clear explanation of the learning objective and the use of stochastic subgoals.
- The results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in terms of sample efficiency and robustness against environmental stochasticity.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and a logical flow of information, making it easy for readers to grasp the main ideas and contributions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of scalability and computational efficiency. The experiments are limited to relatively simple environments, and it is unclear how the method would perform in more complex or larger-scale scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the scalability and computational complexity of the GP-based approach.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims of improved performance and robustness.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the related work, particularly the limitations and contributions of existing methods, which could have provided a richer context for the proposed work.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- There are several grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper, which could detract from the professionalism and clarity of the presentation.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method scale to larger, more complex environments, and what are the computational and memory requirements for training and inference?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of scalability, computational efficiency, and performance?
- How does the method handle the transferability of learned subgoals to different tasks, and what are the implications for the generalization of the low-level policies?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method in more detail, including any potential drawbacks or limitations that were not addressed in the paper?
- How does the method perform in environments with different types of stochasticity, such as different types of noise or different levels of environmental variability?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the number of seeds used and the statistical significance of the results?
