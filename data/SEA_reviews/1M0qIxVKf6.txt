 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the decomposition of Transformer hidden states into interpretable components, specifically focusing on the positional and contextual components. It empirically demonstrates that these components exhibit distinct geometric structures, such as low-dimensional and spiral shapes for positional vectors and clustering for contextual vectors. The authors also explore the incoherence between these components, suggesting that they function independently, which could aid in understanding the Transformer's inner workings. The study is supported by extensive experiments across various models and datasets, showcasing the robustness of these findings. Despite some concerns about the generalizability of the results and the clarity of the mathematical formulations, the paper provides valuable insights into the Transformer's internal mechanisms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides extensive empirical evidence supporting the geometric structures of positional and contextual components, which is crucial for understanding the Transformer's internal mechanisms.
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of the Transformer's internal components, which is significant for the community as it helps in interpreting the Transformer's behavior.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating that the findings are consistent across different models and datasets, which adds credibility to the findings.
- The paper introduces novel insights into the behavior of Transformers, such as the geometric structures of positional and contextual components, which could be beneficial for further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the mathematical formulations, which could be improved for better clarity and comprehension.
- The generalizability of the findings is not clearly established, as the study primarily focuses on text-based datasets and does not explore the applicability to other types of data or transformer architectures.
- The paper does not adequately address how the findings could be applied to improve Transformer performance or other aspects of transformer design.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the implications of the findings, particularly how they could be used to enhance the interpretability or performance of Transformers.
- There are some inconsistencies and unclear definitions in the paper, such as the role and definition of the residual component and the relationship between different components of the Transformer's hidden states.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and definition of the residual component in the context of your study? How does it contribute to the overall understanding of the Transformer's behavior?
- How do the geometric structures observed in your study generalize to other types of data or transformer architectures? Have you considered testing your findings on non-textual datasets or other types of transformers?
- In the context of your analysis, what does the term "incoherence" mean, and how does it relate to the orthogonality of the positional and contextual components?
- How does the decomposition of hidden states into positional, contextual, and residual components affect the performance of the Transformer, particularly in terms of interpretability or other metrics?
- Could you provide more details on the methodology used for calculating the geometric structures, such as the choice of parameters and the impact of these choices on the results?
- Have you considered extending your analysis to include the residual component, and if so, what insights did you gain from this extension?
