 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel game-theoretic framework for machine unlearning, formulating it as a Stackelberg game involving both a defender and an attacker. The defender aims to unlearn specific training data, while the attacker attempts to detect traces of this data using membership inference attacks (MIAs). The proposed approach uses gradient-based optimization to solve the game, aiming to limit the attacker's success by reducing the influence of the unlearned data on model outputs. The framework is evaluated on various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in achieving unlearning while maintaining model performance. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the experimental setup, and the robustness of the results against different types of attacks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology.
- The introduction of a Stackelberg game framework for machine unlearning is innovative and provides a novel perspective on the problem.
- The use of implicit differentiation to obtain gradients that limit the attacker's success is an interesting and potentially effective approach.
- The paper presents a comprehensive review of related work, effectively positioning its contributions within the existing literature.
- The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed framework and algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the attacker's utility function, which is crucial for understanding the motivation behind the attacker's actions.
- The experimental setup is not well-described, particularly the details of the attacker's optimization problem and the choice of hyperparameters.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for the attacker to adapt to the defender's strategies, which could undermine the effectiveness of the unlearning process.
- The paper's claims about the novelty of the approach are overstated, as similar formulations and solutions have been previously explored in the literature.
- The empirical results are not robust enough, particularly concerning the test accuracy and the effectiveness of the unlearning process across different types of attacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and assumptions of the proposed framework.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition and role of the attacker's utility function in the context of the game?
- How are the hyperparameters chosen for the different unlearning methods, and what impact do these choices have on the results?
- How does the proposed framework handle scenarios where the attacker's optimization problem is non-convex?
- Can you provide more details on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed framework, especially in comparison to retraining?
- How does the framework perform against different types of membership inference attacks, and what are the implications of these results for the practical applicability of the unlearning process?
- Could you elaborate on the assumptions and limitations of the proposed framework, particularly in relation to the robustness of the unlearning process against adaptive attacks?
