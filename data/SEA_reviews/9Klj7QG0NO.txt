 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ONE-PEACE, a unified multimodal representation learning model capable of integrating visual, audio, and textual data. The model leverages a modular architecture with separate self-attention and FFN layers for each modality, alongside a modality fusion encoder that facilitates multimodal data integration. The authors employ contrastive learning objectives to train the model, achieving state-of-the-art performance across various vision, audio, and vision-language tasks. The model demonstrates strong emergent retrieval capabilities, which are particularly noteworthy given the lack of paired data between modalities during training.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a unified multimodal representation learning model capable of integrating visual, audio, and textual data, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance across various vision, audio, and vision-language tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and the experimental results are convincing.
- The model's emergent retrieval capabilities are noteworthy, especially given the lack of paired data between modalities during training.
- The authors provide comprehensive ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training data used for the audio model, particularly whether it was trained on datasets that contain both text and audio data.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the model performs on different modalities and the reasons for its performance variations.
- The paper does not compare the proposed model with state-of-the-art models like CLIP, which could provide a better understanding of its relative performance.
- The model's training process and the specifics of how it handles different modalities are not adequately explained, which could hinder reproducibility and further research.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed model, which is crucial for understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the training data used for the audio model, particularly whether it was trained on datasets that contain both text and audio data?
- How does the model perform on different modalities, and what are the reasons for its performance variations?
- Could you compare the proposed model with state-of-the-art models like CLIP to provide a better understanding of its relative performance?
- How does the model handle different modalities during training and inference, and what are the specifics of this process?
- Can you discuss the limitations of the proposed model and how they might affect its performance or applicability in different scenarios?
