 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the computational complexity of learning invariant functions using symmetric neural networks under the correlational statistical query (CSQ) model. It provides theoretical lower bounds on the query complexity of learning various symmetric function classes, including Boolean functions, graph neural networks, and frame-averaged networks. The authors demonstrate that the incorporation of symmetries does not necessarily alleviate the fundamental hardness of learning neural networks via gradient descent, which could be a significant limitation for practical applications. Despite the theoretical insights, the paper lacks empirical validation and does not address the limitations of its theoretical framework.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive discussion on the hardness of learning invariant functions, offering a deeper understanding of the limitations and challenges in this area.
- The theoretical results are presented in a clear and accessible manner, making the paper easy to follow and understand.
- The paper introduces new techniques and frameworks for analyzing the hardness of learning invariant functions, which could be beneficial for future research in this field.
- The paper is well-written and the proofs are rigorous, contributing to the credibility and impact of the results.
- The study of hardness in learning symmetric functions is an important and timely topic, as it addresses a critical gap in the current understanding of the theoretical foundations of learning symmetric functions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical validation, which could help to bridge the gap between theory and practical applications.
- The paper does not address the limitations of its theoretical framework, which could affect the generalizability and applicability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of how they might be applied or extended in real-world scenarios.
- There is a noticeable absence of related work and comparative analysis, which could help to contextualize the paper's contributions within the existing literature.
- The paper's focus on worst-case scenarios might not fully capture the complexity of real-world applications, where symmetries can be leveraged effectively to improve learning outcomes.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the practical implications of their theoretical results, particularly in terms of how they might be applied or extended in real-world scenarios?
- How does the paper's theoretical framework compare to other existing approaches for analyzing the hardness of learning symmetric functions?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their theoretical framework and suggest potential directions for future research to address these limitations?
- Is there a possibility to extend the results of this paper to more practical settings, such as learning with limited data or in the presence of noise?
- How do the results of this paper relate to other recent works on the hardness of learning neural networks, such as those by Chen et al. (2022) and Chen and Narayanan (2023)?
