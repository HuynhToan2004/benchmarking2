 **Summary:**
The paper introduces RepCodec, a novel speech representation codec designed for semantic speech tokenization. RepCodec utilizes a vector quantization codebook to reconstruct speech representations from speech encoders like HuBERT or data2vec. This approach is claimed to improve information retention and outperform traditional methods like k-means clustering in both speech understanding and generation tasks. The methodology involves a combination of a codec encoder, a vector quantizer, and a codec decoder, which is trained to reconstruct input speech representations as accurately as possible. The paper presents extensive experiments demonstrating RepCodec's effectiveness across various speech encoders and languages.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to semantic speech tokenization using a vector quantization codebook, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The methodology is well-presented, with clear descriptions of the RepCodec framework and its components, including the codec encoder, vector quantizer, and codec decoder.
- The paper provides extensive experiments demonstrating the effectiveness of RepCodec across various speech encoders and languages, showing improvements over traditional methods like k-means clustering.
- The paper addresses the challenge of information loss in discrete speech tokenization, a critical issue in speech processing.
- The experiments are robust, with multiple baselines and ablations that help in understanding the impact of different components of the model.
- The analysis provided in the paper, such as the comparison of PNMI with WER, is insightful and contributes to a better understanding of the model's performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the explanation of the vector quantization codebook and the training process, particularly how it is trained and updated.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of training and inference times.
- The paper's presentation could be improved to better highlight the novelty and contributions of the proposed method, particularly in differentiating it from existing methods like k-means clustering and other vector quantization techniques.
- There is a need for more comprehensive comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, such as those using adversarial training or other novel quantization methods.
- The paper could benefit from additional ablation studies to better understand the impact of different components of the model, such as the number of clusters and the architecture of the codec encoder and decoder.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on how the vector quantization codebook is trained and updated, specifically how the codebook entries are initialized and how they are updated during training?
- How does the computational efficiency of RepCodec compare to other methods, particularly in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of speech encoders, such as those based on different architectures or training methods?
- Could you clarify the discrepancy in the results between the ideal upper bound for decoder-only ASR and the actual performance of RepCodec? What factors might be contributing to this gap?
- How does the model perform when applied to different languages, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the findings?
- What are the implications of using different architectures for the codec encoder and decoder, and how do these affect the performance and scalability of the model?
