 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two algorithms for reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), focusing on minimizing regret and query complexity. The first algorithm, PR-LSVI, is designed for linear MDPs and employs a model-free approach with randomization. The second algorithm, PbTS, is model-based and uses Thompson sampling. Both algorithms are analyzed for their regret and query complexity, demonstrating a near-optimal trade-off between these metrics. The paper also discusses the computational complexity of these algorithms, although it lacks empirical results to validate the theoretical claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces two novel algorithms, PR-LSVI and PbTS, that address the problem of reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) by balancing regret and query complexity.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, with detailed proofs and discussions on the balance between regret and query complexity.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the algorithms are presented clearly with detailed explanations of their components and their theoretical underpinnings.
- The algorithms are designed to be computationally efficient, which is crucial for practical implementation in real-world scenarios.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of related work, highlighting the novelty and significance of the proposed algorithms in the context of existing research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical validation of the proposed algorithms, which is crucial for demonstrating their practical effectiveness and efficiency.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed algorithms, which could provide a more balanced view of their contributions and potential drawbacks.
- There are several minor typographical errors and unclear definitions that could confuse readers.
- The computational complexity of the algorithms, particularly the first one, is not thoroughly discussed, which could limit the understanding of their practical applicability.
- The paper does not provide a clear comparison with existing algorithms in terms of computational efficiency and the practical implications of their theoretical results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, particularly the first one, and discuss any potential challenges or limitations in practical implementation?
- How does the proposed active learning procedure compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of the link function in the reward model, and discuss how it affects the computational complexity and effectiveness of the algorithms?
- Is there a potential for extending the proposed algorithms to more complex MDPs, such as those with stochastic rewards or non-linear structures?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of how they guide the design and implementation of the algorithms in real-world scenarios?
