 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the performance of deep reinforcement learning algorithms in data-limited versus data-abundant environments, demonstrating that algorithms optimized for high-data settings do not necessarily outperform in low-data scenarios. Theoretical insights are provided through the study of regret bounds and the analysis of sample complexity, supported by extensive experiments in the Arcade Learning Environment. The paper argues that algorithms designed for high-data regimes may not transfer well to low-data settings, challenging the common assumption of monotonic performance improvement with increasing data availability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and concise presentation of the main ideas.
- The authors provide both theoretical and empirical evidence to support their claims, which is crucial for establishing the validity of their findings.
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant issue in the field of deep reinforcement learning, highlighting the need for a more nuanced understanding of algorithm performance across different data regimes.
- The theoretical analysis and empirical results are robust and well-supported, contributing to the paper's credibility and impact.
- The paper effectively challenges the common assumption that high-data regime algorithms automatically perform better in low-data settings, providing valuable insights for future research and algorithm design.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical contributions, particularly the proofs and theorems, are somewhat unclear and difficult to follow, which could limit the paper's accessibility to a broader audience.
- The paper's empirical results, while extensive, are somewhat limited to the ALE environment, which might not generalize well to other environments or scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the implications of the findings for practical applications in reinforcement learning, especially in terms of algorithm design and policy selection.
- The paper's experimental setup and methodology are not always clearly described, which could lead to confusion about the reproducibility and validity of the results.
- There are some typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected for better presentation and readability.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the term "regret" as used in the paper and its relationship to the performance of algorithms in different data regimes?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of their findings when applied to other environments or scenarios beyond the ALE environment used in the study?
- What are the specific implications of the findings for algorithm design and policy selection in reinforcement learning, especially in low-data regime settings?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the choice of environments, hyperparameters, and training conditions, to ensure the reproducibility and validity of the results?
- How do the authors plan to address the issues of clarity and accessibility in the theoretical proofs and theorems to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience?
