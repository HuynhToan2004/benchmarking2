 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection, leveraging optimal transport (OT) to compare empirical distributions of training and test data. It proposes a conditional distribution entropy score function to quantify the uncertainty of test samples being OOD, which is theoretically sound and computationally efficient. The method is validated on multiple datasets, showing superior performance compared to existing methods. However, the paper is critiqued for its lack of clarity in presentation and the limited novelty of its approach, which is seen as a simple extension of existing methods. The method's effectiveness is also questioned in scenarios where the training and test distributions are similar, and the computational efficiency claims are not convincingly supported.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel method for OOD detection using optimal transport (OT), which is theoretically sound and computationally efficient.
- The proposed method shows superior performance compared to existing methods, as demonstrated through extensive experiments and ablation studies.
- The approach is training-agnostic, making it applicable to various scenarios, including supervised and unsupervised settings.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology, making it accessible to readers.
- The method's ability to handle both pairwise and population-wise information is highlighted as a significant advantage.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in presentation, with some sections being difficult to follow and understand.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods using OT for OOD detection.
- The method's effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated in scenarios where the training and test distributions are similar, which is a common setting in OOD detection.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the computational efficiency of the method, particularly in comparison to other methods like KNN.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of "hard OOD detection," where the training and test distributions are similar, which is a significant limitation for the practical application of the method.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the method, which could help in understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the computational efficiency of their method compared to KNN, particularly in terms of time and resource usage?
- How does the method perform when the training and test distributions are similar, and what are the implications for its practical application?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the number of test inputs used and the specifics of the OOD datasets?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the training and test distributions are significantly different, and what are the implications for its effectiveness in such scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their method and provide guidance on when it might not be the most effective approach for OOD detection?
