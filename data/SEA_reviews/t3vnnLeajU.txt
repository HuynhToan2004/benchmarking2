 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DA-CLIP, a novel approach that leverages a pretrained CLIP image encoder to predict high-quality feature embeddings from corrupted images, alongside a degradation feature that matches real-world image degradations. This model is designed to enhance image restoration tasks by integrating a controller that adapts the CLIP image encoder to output high-quality content embeddings. The authors construct a mixed degradation dataset to train DA-CLIP, which achieves state-of-the-art performance on both degradation-specific and unified image restoration tasks. The paper also explores the use of vision-language models for low-level vision tasks, specifically image restoration, and proposes a multi-task framework to improve the transferability of pretrained models to these tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by leveraging pretrained vision-language models (VLMs) to enhance image restoration, specifically by adapting the fixed CLIP image encoder to predict high-quality feature embeddings from corrupted images.
- The authors construct a mixed degradation dataset with synthetic captions for DA-CLIP training, which can be used to train either DA-CLIP or a unified image restoration model.
- The proposed DA-CLIP achieves state-of-the-art performance on both degradation-specific and unified image restoration tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling various types of image degradations.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental results are comprehensive, showing that DA-CLIP outperforms other methods in image restoration tasks.
- The introduction of a prompt learning module to better utilize the degradation context for unified image restoration is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing state-of-the-art methods in image restoration, such as the one by Li et al. (2022a), which could provide a clearer picture of DA-CLIP's advantages and limitations.
- There is a significant increase in model complexity and computational cost, which might limit the practical application of DA-CLIP.
- The paper does not adequately address how the proposed method handles different types of image degradations, such as blurry images, which might affect the overall performance of the model.
- The dataset used for training is not publicly available, which could limit the reproducibility and further validation of the results.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive comparisons with other unified image restoration methods and a broader range of image restoration tasks to solidify the claims of DA-CLIP's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when handling multiple types of degradations in the same image?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost and memory requirements of DA-CLIP compared to other methods?
- How does the performance of DA-CLIP compare when using different backbones for the CLIP image encoder, such as ResNet-50 or ViT?
- Could the authors clarify the necessity of using a separate controller for the CLIP image encoder and whether a simpler approach like adding additional layers or using a learnable parameter could achieve similar results?
- The paper mentions using cross-attention in the bottom blocks of the U-Net for sample efficiency. Could the authors explain why this approach was chosen and how it impacts the performance of the model?
