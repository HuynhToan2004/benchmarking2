 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of category theory to address the challenges of pooling data from multiple sources with varying covariate distributions. The authors propose a method that leverages category theory to ensure invariance and equivariance in the latent space, which is crucial for harmonizing data from different sources. The method is tested on real datasets, showing promising results in terms of accuracy and the ability to handle multiple covariates. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in the introduction and the motivation behind using category theory, which could hinder its accessibility to a broader audience. Additionally, the experimental setup and the choice of baselines are questioned, with concerns about the generalizability of the results and the comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to harmonizing data from different sources using category theory, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The method is well-motivated, clearly described, and the experimental results are robust, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing approaches.
- The use of category theory to address the challenges of pooling data from multiple sources is both innovative and well-executed, providing a new perspective on data harmonization.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a well-structured experimental section that effectively demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The potential impact of this work is significant, as it could inspire further research in the field of data harmonization and representation learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its introduction, particularly in explaining the problem setting and the motivation behind using category theory.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant baselines, such as those that use cycle consistency or other advanced methods.
- The experimental setup is limited, focusing only on brain imaging data, which might not generalize well to other types of data.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the scalability and computational complexity of the approach.
- There is a need for more detailed explanations and justifications for the choices made in the experimental setup and the methodology, particularly in the handling of covariates and the loss functions used.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work and a clearer presentation of the experimental results, including more detailed comparisons and ablation studies.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the specific problem setting that the authors aim to address with this work, and how it differs from other data harmonization tasks?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability and computational complexity, especially when dealing with a large number of covariates?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the choice of datasets and the specifics of the experimental conditions?
- How does the proposed method compare to more recent and relevant baselines, such as those that use cycle consistency or other advanced methods?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of loss functions and the handling of covariates in the experimental setup?
- How do the authors address the issue of fairness in the context of data pooling, especially when dealing with sensitive attributes?
- Could you provide more detailed ablation studies and comparisons to better understand the impact of different components of the proposed method?
