 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the heterogeneity in federated learning (FL) by examining the similarity between layers of neural networks across different clients. It proposes a method called InCo Aggregation, which leverages normalized cross-layer gradients to enhance similarity among deep layers across clients, thereby improving performance in heterogeneous FL settings. The authors conduct extensive experiments to validate their method, showing that it outperforms existing methods in various settings. The paper is well-structured, with a clear presentation of the problem, methodology, and experimental results. However, it lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and comparisons with state-of-the-art approaches, which could undermine its impact.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem in federated learning (FL) by focusing on the heterogeneity of neural networks, which is crucial for improving performance in practical FL scenarios.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the problem, including a detailed case study and a well-designed method, which is supported by extensive experiments and ablation studies.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise presentation of the methodology, experiments, and results, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method, InCo Aggregation, is innovative and practical, requiring no additional communication between clients, which is a significant advantage in FL systems.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through rigorous experiments and provides a theoretical analysis of the convergence of the proposed algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential challenges in real-world scenarios.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods to better position the proposed method within the current research landscape.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the hyperparameters used in the experiments and their impact on the results.
- The paper's claims regarding the superiority of shallow layers in capturing shared features and the role of gradient similarity in performance are not adequately supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the convergence rate and its implications, which could be a critical aspect for practitioners in FL systems.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the number of clients is large, and how does it scale with the number of clients?
- Could you provide more details on the computational overhead introduced by the proposed method, especially in terms of FLOPs and memory requirements?
- How does the method perform in non-convex settings, and what are the implications of this for practical applications?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of the hyperparameters used in the experiments, and how do they impact the results?
- Could you clarify the role of gradient similarity in performance and provide empirical evidence to support this claim?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance, scalability, and computational efficiency?
- What are the potential challenges and limitations of the proposed method, and how might these impact its practical applicability in real-world FL systems?
