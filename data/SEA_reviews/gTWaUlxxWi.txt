 **Summary:**
The paper investigates Federated Ensembles (Fens) in a one-shot federated learning (FL) setting, focusing on the aggregation of local models using various methods. The authors propose a novel approach where clients train their models on their data, and the server aggregates these models using a shallow neural network, demonstrating improved performance under high data heterogeneity. The paper evaluates Fens across several datasets, including CIFAR-10, SVHN, and FLamby, and compares it with existing methods. The primary contributions include the use of a shallow neural network as an aggregator and the exploration of Fens in a one-shot FL setting. However, the paper faces criticism for its lack of novelty, insufficient theoretical analysis, and limited scope in terms of data heterogeneity and privacy concerns.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear and comprehensive writing that makes the content accessible.
- The proposed Fens method is simple yet effective, particularly in scenarios with high data heterogeneity, demonstrating significant performance improvements.
- The evaluation is thorough, including multiple datasets and comparisons with a variety of baselines, which helps in understanding the method's effectiveness across different conditions.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with related works, highlighting the method's strengths and potential contributions to the field.
- The use of a shallow neural network as an aggregator is an innovative approach, which has been validated through experiments on multiple datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for using Fens in a one-shot FL setting, especially when compared to other methods like FedAvg which might be more practical in terms of communication costs.
- The novelty of the paper is limited as similar ideas have been previously explored in other works, such as the use of shallow neural networks as aggregators.
- The theoretical analysis is insufficient, with no formal proofs or guarantees provided for the performance improvements observed in the experiments.
- The paper does not adequately address privacy concerns, particularly in scenarios where clients' models are shared with the server, which could lead to privacy attacks.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets and do not include more diverse or realistic data, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of Fens, such as its potential inefficiencies in homogeneous data settings and its scalability in larger or more complex environments.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the motivation for using Fens in a one-shot FL setting, especially when compared to other methods like FedAvg?
- How does the choice of the neural network aggregator impact the performance of Fens, and could other types of aggregators, such as linear models, be as effective?
- What is the rationale behind using a neural network as the aggregator, and how does this choice affect the computational and communication costs?
- How does the method perform when the number of clients increases, and what are the implications for scalability and performance?
- Can you provide more details on the privacy concerns raised by the sharing of client models with the server, and how these risks are mitigated?
- How does the method perform when the data heterogeneity is low, and what are the potential advantages or disadvantages of Fens in such scenarios?
- Could you elaborate on the experimental setup and results, particularly in the medical datasets where Fens did not perform as well as expected?
