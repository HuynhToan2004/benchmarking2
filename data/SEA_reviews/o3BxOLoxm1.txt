 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for conditional image generation using a training-free approach that leverages pre-trained diffusion models and off-the-shelf neural networks to guide sampling without requiring additional training. This method, termed Manifold Preserving Guided Diffusion (MPGD), operates by updating the diffusion model's output to align with the guidance, thereby preserving the manifold of the data distribution. The approach is validated through experiments on various tasks, showing improvements in both speed and quality compared to existing methods. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the method, including the manifold hypothesis and the use of tangent spaces to guide the sampling process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined problem.
- The proposed method, MPGD, is simple, intuitive, and can be applied to both pixel space and latent diffusion models, showing promising results.
- The method is training-free, which is a significant advantage in terms of computational resources and time efficiency.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the method's effectiveness in terms of both quality and speed, with significant improvements over baseline methods.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the method, which adds depth to the understanding of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential failure cases of the proposed method, which is crucial for a complete understanding of its applicability.
- The assumption of a perfect autoencoder for guidance gradient is strong and not well justified, especially in practical scenarios where the autoencoder may not be perfect.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental evidence to support the claims made about the generalizability of the method across different types of guidance, such as text-to-image generation.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other methods, particularly in terms of computational resources and the impact of different autoencoder architectures on the results.
- The theoretical underpinnings of the method, such as the use of tangent spaces and the manifold hypothesis, are not sufficiently explained or justified, which could limit the understanding of the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the practical limitations and failure cases of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the autoencoder is not perfect, and what are the implications of this for the guidance gradient?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of using the KID metric in the experiments and how it compares to other metrics like FID?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of guidance, such as text-to-image generation, and what are the expected outcomes in such scenarios?
- What are the specific computational resources required for the proposed method, and how do these compare to other methods in terms of efficiency and effectiveness?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and the results, especially in the face ID guidance experiments, to clarify the discrepancies observed in the data?
