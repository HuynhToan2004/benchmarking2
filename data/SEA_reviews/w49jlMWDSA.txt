 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GIST, a method for generating fine-grained text descriptions for images from image-only datasets, leveraging domain-specific prompts to guide large language models (LLMs) in generating detailed descriptions. These descriptions are then used to create image-text pairs, which are utilized to fine-tune vision-language models, specifically CLIP, for improved performance in fine-grained image classification tasks. The methodology involves using GPT-3 to generate text descriptions based on domain-specific prompts, which are then matched to images using CLIP to create aligned image-text pairs. These pairs are used to fine-tune CLIP, demonstrating improvements in accuracy over existing methods. The paper also presents a new dataset, Fitzpatrick40, for fine-grained image classification, which is claimed to be the first to use language priors and image-text foundation models to generate image-specific captions for fine-grained datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The proposed method is innovative, using large language models (LLMs) to generate fine-grained text descriptions, which are then used to generate image-text pairs and fine-tune vision-language models.
- The method is evaluated on four fine-grained classification datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and generalizability.
- The paper introduces a new fine-grained image classification dataset, Fitzpatrick40, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on four fine-grained classification datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including the impact of different caption lengths and the use of different CLIP models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the dataset used, particularly the new dataset Fitzpatrick40, including the number of classes, the number of samples in each class, and the data distribution.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing captioning methods, such as GIT and BLIP, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the reliance on high-quality data and the potential for overfitting due to the use of a large number of captions.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the prompt design for different datasets and the potential impact of using different numbers of captions.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impact of the proposed method, which could be significant given the use of large language models.
- The paper could provide more details on the training and evaluation of the method, including the number of epochs and the choice of hyperparameters.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with existing captioning methods, particularly in terms of accuracy and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the dataset, including the number of classes and samples in each class, and the data distribution?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting due to the use of a large number of captions?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impact of the proposed method, particularly in terms of data privacy and security?
- How does the proposed method perform when using different numbers of captions, and how does this impact the performance of the fine-tuned model?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and evaluation of the method, including the number of epochs and the choice of hyperparameters?
