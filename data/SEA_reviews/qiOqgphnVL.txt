 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a reimplementation of the CDCD model and explores the potential for early exiting in diffusion language models (DLMs) to enhance the efficiency of text generation. It investigates three adaptive early exiting criteria and demonstrates their effectiveness in reducing computational requirements without compromising quality. The authors also release the Democratized Diffusion Language Model, trained using the CDCD framework, to promote further research in this area. The paper's contributions include the evaluation of adaptive early exiting in DLMs and the release of a re-implemented model, although it lacks a broader comparison with other DLMs and a deeper exploration of the early exiting phenomenon.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of adaptive early exiting in diffusion language models (DLMs), which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors have released the Democratized Diffusion Language Model, which is a re-implementation of the CDCD model, enhancing the reproducibility and accessibility of the research.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the adaptive early exiting criteria and their effectiveness.
- The experiments are well-designed, providing valuable insights into the early exiting phenomenon in DLMs.
- The release of the model code and pre-trained weights is a commendable contribution to the research community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader comparison with other DLMs, which could have provided a more comprehensive understanding of the adaptive early exiting phenomenon.
- The novelty of the early exiting method is questioned, as it seems similar to existing methods, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions.
- There is a lack of clarity in the paper, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key concepts such as "score interpolation" and "Democratized Diffusion Language Model."
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the implications of early exiting on the diversity of generated text, which is a critical aspect of text generation.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with some figures and data showing inconsistencies or lack of clarity.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the early exiting method, particularly its potential impact on the quality and diversity of the generated text.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their early exiting method and how it differs from existing methods?
- How does the early exiting method affect the diversity of the generated text, and what steps have been taken to mitigate any negative impacts?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the adaptive early exiting criteria and their implementation?
- Why was the CDCD model chosen for reimplementation, and what specific advantages does it offer over other DLMs?
- How do the authors address the potential issues of overfitting in the early exiting method, and what measures have been taken to ensure that the model does not compromise quality for efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to support the claims made about the effectiveness of the early exiting method?
