 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for estimating survival causal effects using a covariate balancing approach. This method aims to address the instability issues associated with traditional inverse probability weighting methods by directly estimating the inverse using a Riesz representer. The proposed method is shown to be asymptotically efficient and demonstrates competitive performance against existing methods in synthetic and semi-synthetic data. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the presentation, the practicality of the assumptions, and the limited scope of the experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to estimating survival causal effects, focusing on the use of a Riesz representer to estimate the inverse of small probabilities, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with a clear explanation of the problem and the proposed solution, including a detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings and practical implications of the assumptions.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology, assumptions, and results, making it accessible to readers with a solid background in survival analysis and causal inference.
- The authors provide a thorough discussion of the related literature, positioning their work within the existing body of knowledge and highlighting its contributions.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the competitive performance of the proposed method against existing methods, showcasing its potential utility in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the presentation of equations and assumptions, which could hinder understanding for readers unfamiliar with the specific terminology and notations used.
- The paper makes several assumptions that are either not well-explained or seem impractical, such as the assumption that the hazard functions are in a Donsker class, which might not hold in all practical scenarios.
- The experiments are limited in scope, focusing on synthetic and semi-synthetic data, which may not fully represent the performance of the method in real-world settings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for assessing its practical applicability in large-scale datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for a comprehensive understanding of its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**

- How does the method perform in real-world datasets, and what are the potential challenges in applying it to such datasets?
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially in comparison to existing methods?
- How does the method handle the estimation of the Riesz representer, and what are the implications of this estimation for the accuracy and efficiency of the method?
