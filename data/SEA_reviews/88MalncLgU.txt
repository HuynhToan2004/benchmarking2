 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GInX-Eval, a novel evaluation framework for graph neural network (GNN) explanations. It critiques the faithfulness metric, which has been criticized for its inability to discern between informative and non-informative edges, especially when edges are removed. The proposed method, GInX-Eval, measures the informativeness of removed edges by fine-tuning the GNN model on progressively smaller subgraphs, thereby evaluating the explanatory power of the removed edges. This framework is tested across various datasets and shows that many existing explanation methods do not outperform random baselines, challenging the effectiveness of current methods. The paper also introduces the HomophilicRank score to evaluate the ranking of edges by their importance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant issue in the field of graph neural networks (GNNs) by critiquing the faithfulness metric, which has been widely used but lacks clarity in its evaluation of informative edges.
- The proposed GInX-Eval framework is novel and provides a practical solution to the challenges posed by the faithfulness metric, offering a new perspective on evaluating the correctness of GNN explanations.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, covering a variety of datasets and models, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in distinguishing between informative and non-informative edges.
- The paper contributes to the ongoing discussion on the evaluation of GNN explanations, which is crucial for advancing the field and ensuring that models are transparent and interpretable.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on related works, particularly those that have addressed similar issues in the evaluation of GNN explanations.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly robust, with some inconsistencies and potential biases in the data used. For instance, the datasets used in the experiments are mostly small, which may not adequately represent the complexity and diversity of real-world GNN applications.
- The paper lacks a clear definition and justification for the threshold used in the evaluation, which could impact the reliability and generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its proposed method, particularly the computational cost and scalability issues associated with fine-tuning GNN models on progressively smaller subgraphs.
- There is a need for a more rigorous comparison with existing methods, including a detailed discussion on how the proposed method differs from and improves upon these existing approaches.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the threshold used in the evaluation is determined and justify its choice?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of out-of-distribution (OOD) explanations, and what steps are taken to ensure that the explanations remain within the distribution of the training data?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup, particularly how the datasets were selected and how the results were validated against ground-truth explanations?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for evaluating GNN explanations, and what are the advantages and disadvantages of each approach?
- Could the authors discuss the potential implications of their findings for the development of more effective and reliable GNN explanations, and how these insights might be applied in practical settings?
