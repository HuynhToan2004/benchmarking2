 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PerCo, a novel image compression method that leverages a diffusion model as a decoder, conditioned on both vector-quantized latent image representations and textual image descriptions. This approach allows for realistic image reconstruction at ultra-low bitrates, as demonstrated by state-of-the-art FID and KID scores on the MS-COCO 30k dataset. The methodology involves encoding both local and global contexts, with the local context being vector-quantized and the global context being text-based, which is compressed using Lempel-Ziv coding. The paper also explores the use of classifier-free guidance and various ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of different components. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticisms regarding its computational efficiency, the choice of metrics, and the clarity of some experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel method for image compression that utilizes a diffusion model as a decoder, which is a departure from traditional methods using feed-forward decoders.
- The use of text-based global encoding is a significant innovation, enabling the compression of a wide range of images with a single model, unlike previous methods that required different models for different image sizes.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and provides detailed explanations of the methodology, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art FID and KID performance on the MS-COCO 30k dataset, with no significant degradation of FID when reducing the bitrate.
- The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies that validate the effectiveness of different components such as classifier-free guidance and the use of text-based global encoding.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's computational efficiency is a concern, as the diffusion model is computationally intensive and may not be practical for real-time applications.
- The choice of metrics for evaluating image compression quality is questionable, particularly the use of FID and KID metrics which are not designed for compression and may not accurately reflect the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for overfitting due to the use of a large dataset for training the diffusion model.
- The experimental results, particularly in figures and tables, are not always clearly presented or interpreted, which could confuse readers about the actual effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant barrier to its practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational efficiency compared to other state-of-the-art image compression methods?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method?
- How does the method handle different image resolutions, and what are the implications for the effectiveness of the global encoding based on textual descriptions?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental results, particularly in figures and tables, to make the findings more accessible and understandable?
- How does the proposed method compare to other diffusion-based methods in terms of image quality and compression efficiency?
- What are the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, and how does it address ethical considerations related to the use of machine learning models in image compression?
