 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for opponent modeling in offline settings using a Transformer-based model named TAO. TAO is designed to learn from pre-collected offline data to model the opponent's behavior, which is then used to inform the agent's response policy. The method involves three stages: offline policy embedding learning, offline opponent-aware response policy training, and deployment stage for opponent adaptation with in-context learning. Theoretical analysis is provided to support the method's convergence, and empirical results show that TAO outperforms baseline methods in competitive environments with sparse and dense rewards. However, the paper's scope is limited to modeling a single opponent, and the method's generalizability to more complex or cooperative settings is not explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear structure that aids in understanding the complex methodology.
- The problem of offline opponent modeling is well-motivated and the methodology is well-designed, with a novel approach to learning the opponent model offline.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to support the method's convergence, and empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness in competitive environments with sparse and dense rewards.
- The method is flexible and can be applied to various scenarios, as evidenced by the experimental results which show that TAO outperforms baseline methods.
- The paper provides a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is appreciated for its transparency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could have provided a more comprehensive understanding of its applicability and potential pitfalls.
- The method's generalizability to more complex or cooperative settings is not explored, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper's focus on modeling a single opponent is a significant limitation, as it does not address the challenges of modeling multiple opponents or more complex scenarios.
- The theoretical analysis and proofs are not thoroughly explained, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the method's theoretical underpinnings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the challenges of learning the opponent model offline and how the method addresses these challenges.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors elaborate on how the method could be adapted to model multiple opponents or more complex scenarios?
- How does the method perform when the opponent's policy changes during the testing phase?
- Could the authors provide more details on the construction of the offline dataset and how it is used to train the opponent policy encoder?
- Is there a specific reason why the method was not tested in more complex or cooperative settings, and how might it perform in such environments?
- Could the authors clarify the theoretical analysis and proofs, particularly the equivalence between TAO and PSOM, and the convergence of PSOM to the optimal solution?
