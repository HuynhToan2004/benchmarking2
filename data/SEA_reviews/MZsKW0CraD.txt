 **Summary:**
The paper explores the effectiveness of model extraction attacks (MEAs) by challenging the assumption that these attacks are cost-effective for adversaries. It argues that prior knowledge of the victim model's training distribution significantly impacts the attack's success, often making MEAs more expensive than training from scratch. The paper introduces a novel benchmark to evaluate attack policies and proposes a method to reduce the effectiveness of MEAs by manipulating out-of-distribution (OOD) queries. The findings suggest that the attack's success depends more on the adversary's access to in-distribution data rather than the model's labels, which may not be as valuable as previously thought. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the design of defenses against MEAs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant topic in the field of model extraction attacks (MEAs), which is crucial for understanding the practicality and effectiveness of these attacks in real-world scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and provides a thorough analysis of the factors influencing the success of MEAs, including the impact of prior knowledge and the role of in-distribution data.
- The introduction of a benchmark for evaluating attack policies directly is a significant contribution to the field, offering a novel approach to assessing the effectiveness of MEAs.
- The paper challenges common assumptions about the cost-effectiveness of MEAs, providing insights into the practicality of these attacks and the incentives for adversaries to use them.
