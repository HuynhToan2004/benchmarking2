 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to off-policy evaluation (OPE) in offline reinforcement learning (RL) by proposing a policy-conditioned model (PCM) learning method. PCM is designed to address the value gap issue in OPE by adapting to the state-action distribution of the evaluation policy, thereby improving the accuracy of the learned dynamics model. The authors provide theoretical analysis and experimental evidence to support the effectiveness of PCM, demonstrating its superiority over existing methods in the DOPE benchmark. The paper also discusses the implications of PCM in various RL tasks such as offline policy selection and model predictive control.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation and a strong theoretical foundation.
- The proposed PCM learning method is innovative and addresses the significant issue of value gaps in off-policy evaluation.
- The experimental results are robust and demonstrate the effectiveness of PCM over existing methods, showcasing its practical utility in offline RL tasks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of PCM across various tasks, including offline policy selection and model predictive control, demonstrating its versatility and applicability.
- The theoretical analysis is thorough, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method and its application in offline RL.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- There is a need for more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of dataset diversity and its impact on the effectiveness of PCM.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse datasets and a broader range of tasks to better validate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently explore the implications of PCM in other RL tasks, such as policy optimization, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its utility.
- The theoretical analysis, while robust, could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods and a broader range of experimental validations.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the computational efficiency of PCM compared to other methods, particularly in terms of computational resources and runtime?
- How does the performance of PCM vary with different levels of dataset diversity? Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis or additional experiments to explore this further?
- Is there a possibility to extend the use of PCM to other RL tasks beyond OPE, such as policy optimization? If so, what are the potential challenges and benefits?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of PCM with other methods, particularly in terms of model complexity and generalizability?
- In the experimental setup, could the authors include more diverse datasets and tasks to better validate the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of PCM and potential negative societal impacts in more detail?
