 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the orthogonality of weights in neural networks during training, proposing a method that leverages this orthogonality to achieve low-rank decompositions. This approach, termed Orthogonality-Informed Adaptive Low-Rank (OIALR) training, is designed to reduce the number of trainable parameters while maintaining or improving accuracy. The method is tested on various architectures and datasets, including image classification and time series prediction, showing mixed results. The paper's main strength lies in its novel approach to weight stabilization and its potential for reducing computational complexity. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the method due to its computational complexity and the limited improvement in performance over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for weight stabilization in neural networks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method, Orthogonality-Informed Adaptive Low-Rank (OIALR) training, is clearly explained and supported by extensive experiments across various architectures and datasets.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for academic publications.
- The experimental results are robust, showing that the method can achieve similar or better performance with fewer parameters, which is a significant advantage in terms of computational efficiency.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of neural networks, focusing on the orthogonality of weights during training, which is a topic of ongoing interest.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and parameter reduction. This makes it difficult to assess the true impact and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method's practicality is questioned due to its computational complexity, which may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There are inconsistencies in the experimental results, particularly in the improvements shown in different datasets and architectures, which raises concerns about the method's generalizability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the orthogonality of weights in neural networks and how it relates to other low-rank approximations in the field.
