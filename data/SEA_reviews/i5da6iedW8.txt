 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedBiOT, a novel method for federated learning in large language models (LLMs) that addresses data privacy and intellectual property concerns. FedBiOT splits the LLM into an emulator and an adapter, with the emulator trained on a public dataset and the adapter fine-tuned on client-specific datasets. The method employs a bi-level optimization approach to ensure alignment between the compressed and non-compressed emulators. The paper evaluates FedBiOT using LLaMA-7B on various federated learning tasks and demonstrates improvements over existing baselines. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty and insufficient experimental validation, particularly in terms of the number of clients and the diversity of datasets used.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem of fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) in a federated learning setting, focusing on data privacy and intellectual property protection.
- The proposed method of splitting the LLM into an emulator and an adapter, and the use of bi-level optimization to ensure alignment, is innovative and well-explained.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a technical audience.
- The experimental results showcase the effectiveness of the proposed method, with significant improvements over existing baselines.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the challenges and limitations of existing methods, which helps in understanding the motivation behind the proposed solutions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as the approach of splitting the LLM into an emulator and an adapter, and the use of bi-level optimization, are similar to existing methods like FedOT.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of the research.
- The experimental setup is limited, with only a few clients and datasets used, which may not adequately demonstrate the scalability and robustness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational efficiency of the method, particularly in terms of the communication overhead and the computational requirements for training the emulator.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of the hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the emulator, which is a critical component of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to existing methods like FedOT?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential negative societal impacts, particularly in terms of data privacy and intellectual property protection?
- Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the emulator, including the specifics of how it is trained and its computational requirements?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational efficiency issues, particularly in terms of communication overhead and computational requirements?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the performance of the proposed method with different hyperparameters, including the impact of the regularization term and the number of emulator updates?
- How does the proposed method perform when scaled up to a larger number of clients and datasets?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential directions for future research?
