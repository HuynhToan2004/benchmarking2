 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel statistical test to detect test set contamination in black-box language models without requiring access to the pretraining data or model weights. The test is based on the exchangeability of datasets, which assumes that the log probability of a dataset under a model should be the same regardless of its order. The test involves comparing the log probability of a dataset in its canonical order to a shuffled version, and the statistical significance of this difference indicates contamination. The method is demonstrated to be effective in detecting contamination in models like GPT-2, GPT-3, and GPT-4, and is shown to be sensitive to small datasets and low duplication rates. However, the test's effectiveness and the assumptions it makes about the datasets and models are critically evaluated, raising concerns about its applicability and the strength of its conclusions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue of test set contamination in large language models (LLMs), which is crucial for understanding the performance and reliability of these models.
- The proposed test is novel and offers a practical solution to detect test set contamination in black-box models, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is computationally efficient and can be applied to any model that can be queried with log probability, making it broadly applicable.
- The paper is well-written and the methodology is clearly explained, with a focus on the importance of the problem and the potential impact of the proposed solution.
- The paper includes a thorough discussion of related work and limitations, which enhances the credibility and transparency of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions about the datasets and models are not well-justified, particularly the assumption that datasets are exchangeable and that models are trained on the entire pretraining dataset.
- The test's effectiveness is limited by its reliance on the assumption of exchangeability, which may not hold true for all datasets, especially those with sequential or non-randomly ordered elements.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the test, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The methodology's novelty is questioned as similar approaches have been previously used in the literature, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from existing work.
- The paper's conclusions are not convincingly drawn, particularly in cases where the test results are inconclusive or do not support the claims made.
- There is a lack of discussion on the societal impact of the proposed test, which could be significant given the potential for false positives or false negatives in the test results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made about the datasets and models, particularly the exchangeability of datasets and the completeness of model training?
- How does the test perform when applied to models that are not trained on the entire pretraining dataset, or when the dataset is not exchangeable?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the test, especially in terms of the number of permutations and the size of the dataset?
- How does the test handle cases where the results are inconclusive or do not support the claims made, such as the detection of contamination in MMLU?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed test, particularly in terms of false positives or false negatives in the test results?
- Are there any limitations or challenges that the authors foresee in implementing the test in practical scenarios, and how do they plan to address these issues?
