 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Memory-Consistent Neural Networks (MCNNs) for imitation learning, focusing on the compounding error problem in behavior cloning. MCNNs are designed to ensure that neural network outputs remain within specified regions centered around prototypical "memory" training samples, thereby mitigating the risk of policy failure due to compounding errors. The authors provide theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of MCNNs across various tasks, including robotic manipulation and driving, using different backbone models like MLP, Transformer, and Diffusion. The paper also discusses the practical implications of MCNNs, including the potential for hyperparameter tuning and the impact on performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to imitation learning by constraining the function class based on training data memories, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided in the paper is robust, with a detailed explanation of the Memory-Consistent Neural Network (MCNN) and its function class, which is a valuable contribution to the literature.
- The empirical results demonstrate that MCNNs significantly improve the performance of behavior cloning methods across various tasks and datasets, regardless of the underlying architecture.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, with clear explanations of the theoretical concepts and practical applications.
- The inclusion of a comprehensive set of experiments, including the use of different backbone models like MLP, Transformer, and Diffusion, provides a robust validation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The comparison with other baseline methods such as VINN and 1-NN is not sufficiently fair due to differences in the use of hyperparameters.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational efficiency of MCNNs compared to other methods like VINN and 1-NN, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the generalization of MCNNs to other types of imitation learning tasks beyond behavior cloning.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process, particularly the hyperparameter tuning process, which is crucial for the practical application of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the impact of hyperparameters on the performance of MCNNs, including the selection of λ and L.

**Questions:**
- How does the performance of MCNNs compare when using different hyperparameters, particularly λ and L?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process, including the selection of hyperparameters and the computational efficiency of MCNNs compared to other methods?
- How does the MCNN approach perform when applied to other types of imitation learning tasks beyond behavior cloning?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its application in real-world scenarios?
- How does the MCNN approach handle the generalization to unseen states, and what are the implications for the performance of the learned policies in such scenarios?
