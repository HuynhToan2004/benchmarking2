 **Summary:**
The paper introduces COINs (Community-based Inference Networks), a novel method for accelerating inference in knowledge graphs. COINs utilize community detection to partition the knowledge graph into communities, which are then used to optimize the training and evaluation processes. The methodology involves a two-step prediction procedure: first, predicting the community a node belongs to, followed by predicting the node's position within that community. The authors demonstrate that this approach can significantly reduce the number of model evaluations required, thus improving inference efficiency. However, concerns are raised about the method's effectiveness in terms of accuracy and its reliance on the Leiden community detection algorithm, which may not be optimal for all knowledge graphs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is innovative, particularly the use of community detection for knowledge graph inference, which is a novel approach.
- The paper provides theoretical justification for the proposed method, enhancing its credibility and applicability.
- The method's potential to reduce model evaluation cost by up to 6.4 times is a significant contribution, demonstrating its practical utility.
- The authors have conducted comprehensive experiments to validate their claims, which support the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods for knowledge graph inference, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on the Leiden community detection algorithm may limit its applicability to all types of knowledge graphs, especially those with complex or non-standard structures.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its practical implementation in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for ethical AI research.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of training times, which is crucial for understanding the full impact of the proposed method on inference efficiency.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in comparison to other state-of-the-art methods for knowledge graph inference?
- Can the authors provide a detailed explanation of how the community detection algorithm was chosen and how it impacts the performance of the method?
- How does the method perform when applied to knowledge graphs with complex or non-standard structures?
- What are the specific limitations of the proposed method, and how do these affect its practical implementation?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these were considered in the research?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of training times to better understand the full impact of the proposed method on inference efficiency?
