 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for cohort discovery in healthcare analytics using negative samples, a concept under-explored in previous research. It leverages Shapley values to quantify each sample's contribution and constructs a Negative Sample Shapley Field to model the distribution of all negative samples. The method then transforms this field through manifold learning to identify cohorts of medical interest via density-based clustering. The approach is validated using hospital's electronic medical records and the findings are verified by clinicians, enhancing the medical relevance of the proposed method. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in explaining the methodology and its assumptions, particularly the application of Shapley values in a binary classification context and the rationale behind the isotropy constraint.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and under-explored problem in healthcare analytics, specifically the role of negative samples in cohort discovery.
- It introduces a novel method using Shapley values to quantify each negative sample's contribution to the prediction task and constructs a Negative Sample Shapley Field, which is a novel approach in this context.
- The methodology is supported by a case study using hospital's electronic medical records, and the findings are validated by clinicians, which adds credibility to the proposed method.
- The paper is well-organized, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive explanation of the proposed method and its components.
- The paper provides a good balance between theoretical and practical aspects, making it relevant to both academic and practical healthcare settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately explain the application of Shapley values in a binary classification context, which could lead to confusion about their relevance and utility in this specific application.
- The rationale behind the isotropy constraint in the method is not well-explained, and its necessity and effectiveness in the context of cohort discovery are not convincingly demonstrated.
- The paper lacks a clear definition and justification for the use of "cohort" in the context of negative samples, which could lead to misunderstandings about the nature and utility of the identified groups.
- The paper's experimental validation is limited to a single case study, which may not be sufficient to establish the generalizability and robustness of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the methodology, particularly in the explanation of the isotropy constraint and its impact on the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its method, such as the scalability and computational efficiency of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the application of Shapley values in a binary classification context and how they contribute to the prediction task in this specific context?
- How do the authors justify the use of the isotropy constraint in the method, and what are the specific benefits it provides in the context of cohort discovery?
- Could the authors provide a clearer definition and justification for the use of "cohort" in the context of negative samples, and how do these groups contribute to medical research and clinical decision-making?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability and computational efficiency issues, especially in large datasets?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the threshold for filtering negative samples with low data Shapley values, and how this affects the results?
- The paper mentions the use of a stacked denoising autoencoder (SDAE) for manifold learning. Could the authors discuss the choice of this model over other potential models for this task?
