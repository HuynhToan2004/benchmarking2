 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the scaling of the momentum hyperparameter in Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) with momentum, focusing on the interplay between label noise and momentum in training overparameterized neural networks. The authors propose a scaling law that maximizes the training acceleration without compromising generalization. They introduce a novel scaling regime where the momentum hyperparameter is scaled with the learning rate to the power of 2/3, which is validated through experiments on matrix-sensing and a 6-layer MLP on FashionMNIST and ResNet-18 on CIFAR10. The theoretical framework suggests that this scaling law optimizes the training dynamics by balancing the timescales of the momentum and the learning rate.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear and concise introduction that effectively motivates the problem.
- The theoretical framework is novel, and the experiments are conducted on a wide range of tasks, including a 2-layer MLP with linear and non-linear activations, a 6-layer MLP on FashionMNIST, and a ResNet-18 on CIFAR10.
- The results are convincing, and the paper provides a detailed explanation of the theoretical findings, supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper introduces a novel scaling regime for the momentum hyperparameter in SGD, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear presentation of the theoretical results and their implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed scaling law, particularly in terms of its applicability to other types of neural networks and optimization methods.
- The theoretical results are not rigorously proven, and the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis are not thoroughly justified.
- The paper does not include a discussion on how the proposed scaling law affects the generalization performance of the model.
- The experiments are limited to a small number of datasets, and there is a lack of comparison with other optimization methods such as Adam.
- The paper does not provide a detailed explanation of the mathematical notations used, which could hinder understanding for readers unfamiliar with the specific notation.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed scaling law affect the generalization performance of the model?
- Is there a way to determine the optimal scaling regime for different types of neural networks or optimization methods?
- How does the proposed scaling law compare to other optimization methods, such as Adam, in terms of training efficiency and generalization performance?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the mathematical notations used in the paper to enhance readability?
- How does the proposed scaling law perform when applied to larger or more complex datasets?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and justify their validity in practical scenarios?
