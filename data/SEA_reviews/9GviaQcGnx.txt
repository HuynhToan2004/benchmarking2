 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Constrained Parameter Regularization (CPR), which dynamically adjusts the penalty coefficients for different parameter groups in deep learning models. This approach contrasts with traditional weight decay, which uniformly applies a penalty across all parameters. CPR employs an augmented Lagrangian method to enforce upper bounds on statistical measures of parameter groups, allowing for individualized regularization that adapts to the specific needs of different parameters. The method is evaluated on various tasks, including image classification, medical image segmentation, and language modeling, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving model performance. However, the paper's empirical evaluations are criticized for their limited scope and depth, and the method's theoretical contributions are seen as minimal.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The proposed method of constrained parameter regularization (CPR) is novel and interesting, providing a new perspective on regularization in deep learning.
- The empirical results show the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in improving the performance of models on various tasks.
- The paper provides code and detailed experimental setups, which enhances reproducibility and transparency.
- The authors have conducted a thorough evaluation of their proposed method, comparing it to AdamW in various settings and demonstrating its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive theoretical analysis, particularly concerning the convergence of the proposed algorithm.
- The empirical evaluations are limited in scope, focusing primarily on smaller models and not adequately comparing the proposed method to state-of-the-art techniques.
- There is a lack of discussion on the sensitivity of the proposed method to hyperparameters, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the proposed method, such as its scalability and applicability in different settings.
- The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from existing methods, such as weight decay and L2 regularization, which could lead to confusion about the novelty of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the convergence of the proposed algorithm, especially in the context of large-scale applications?
- How sensitive is the proposed method to the choice of hyperparameters, and what guidelines can be provided for setting these parameters in practical applications?
- Could the authors provide a more comprehensive comparison of the computational cost of the proposed method against other regularization techniques, especially in large-scale applications?
- In the context of the medical image segmentation experiments, how does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art techniques, and what are the specific advantages of using CPR in this setting?
- How does the proposed method perform in different settings, such as in large-scale applications or in scenarios where the data distribution is significantly different from those used in the experiments?
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between the proposed method and existing regularization techniques, particularly in terms of the theoretical contributions and practical implications?
