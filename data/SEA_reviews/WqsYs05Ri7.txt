 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, U-ACE, for generating concept-based explanations in a label-efficient manner using a Bayesian estimation approach. This method aims to improve the reliability and robustness of concept explanations by modeling the uncertainty in the estimation process. The approach involves the use of a pre-trained CLIP model to extract concept activations and a linear regression model to predict logits from these activations. The paper includes theoretical and empirical evidence, demonstrating the effectiveness of U-ACE in handling over-complete and under-complete concept sets, as well as its superiority over existing methods in certain scenarios. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited experimental scope, particularly in terms of the number of concepts tested and the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and understandable presentation of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, including both controlled and real-world experiments, to support their claims.
- The proposed method is novel and interesting, particularly in its application of Bayesian estimation to improve the reliability and robustness of concept explanations.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of concept-based explanations, which is crucial for enhancing the interpretability and trustworthiness of machine learning models.
- The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the limitations of existing methods and provide a novel solution that addresses these limitations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters, which are crucial for the performance of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup and results are limited in scope, particularly in terms of the number of concepts tested and the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and the potential impact of these assumptions on the practical applicability of the method.
- The paper does not provide a clear comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the practical implications of the method's assumptions.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and how these assumptions might affect the practical applicability of the method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other label-efficient methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the method perform when the number of concepts is significantly increased?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the probe dataset is significantly different from the training dataset?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the hyperparameter λ in the theoretical analysis and its practical implications?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for spurious correlations in the data, particularly in scenarios where the probe dataset contains irrelevant or misleading concepts?
