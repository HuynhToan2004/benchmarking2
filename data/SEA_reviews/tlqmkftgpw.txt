 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Disentangled and Balanced Representation Network (DBRNet) for estimating individualized dose-response functions in continuous treatment settings. It addresses the challenges of adjusting for selection bias and learning disentangled representations. The methodology includes a novel re-weighting function to correct for selection bias and a varying coefficient network to predict outcomes based on confounder and adjustment factors. The paper presents theoretical proofs and extensive experiments on synthetic and semi-synthetic datasets, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in estimating continuous treatment effects and adjusting for selection bias. However, the paper's assumptions, such as the stable unit treatment value assumption (SUTVA), are criticized for being overly restrictive, and the paper lacks comparisons with other baseline methods, particularly those that do not require disentanglement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and well-defined problem setting.
- It introduces a novel method, DBRNet, which is the first to precisely adjust for selection bias in continuous treatment settings, supported by theoretical proofs.
- Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing promising results on both synthetic and semi-synthetic datasets.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of related work and presents a detailed description of the methodology, including the objective function, loss functions, and theoretical proofs.
- The use of disentangled representations to handle selection bias in continuous treatment settings is innovative and relevant, addressing a significant gap in the literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions, particularly the SUTVA assumption, are considered overly restrictive and may not hold in many practical settings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability in scenarios where the assumptions are not met.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with other baseline methods, particularly those that do not require disentanglement, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly robust, with some results, such as those on the News dataset, being less convincing.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the challenges of estimating continuous treatment effects and the practical implications of the proposed method in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the SUTVA assumption does not hold?
- Can the authors provide more details on the data generation process used in the experiments, particularly how the treatment and outcome are generated from the covariates?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the re-weighting function on the model's performance, especially in light of the mixed results in the ablation study?
- How does the proposed method compare with other baseline methods that do not require disentanglement, such as those using direct output of the conditional density estimator for re-weighting?
- The paper mentions a discrepancy in the results between different datasets (e.g., News vs. IHDP). Can the authors provide more insights into these differences and their implications for the practical applicability of the proposed method?
