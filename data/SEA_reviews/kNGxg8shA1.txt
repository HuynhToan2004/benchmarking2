 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel noise model, Feature-Dependent Graph-Noise (FDGN), which integrates feature noise, structure noise, and label noise, reflecting a more realistic scenario in graph learning. It proposes a generative model, PRINGLE, to model the data generation process under FDGN, using a deep generative model that captures causal relationships among variables. The model is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing improvements over existing baselines. The paper also introduces new datasets to simulate FDGN in e-commerce systems, enhancing practical research in noise-robust graph learning. Despite its strengths in addressing realistic noise scenarios and outperforming baselines, the paper faces criticism for its complex model structure, lack of detailed implementation, and unclear presentation in certain sections.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a more realistic graph noise scenario, Feature-Dependent Graph-Noise (FDGN), which reflects real-world scenarios and is more practical than previous noise models.
- The proposed model, PRINGLE, outperforms existing baselines in both node classification and link prediction tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling noisy graph data.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed model and its components, making it accessible to readers.
- The authors have introduced new datasets to simulate FDGN in e-commerce systems, which are expected to foster practical research in noise-robust graph learning.
- The proposed model is capable of handling both node classification and link prediction tasks, making it versatile and applicable in various situations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed implementation of the proposed method, which could hinder the reproducibility and understanding of the model.
- The model structure is complex, with multiple loss terms and latent variables, which could be simplified for better clarity and understanding.
- The paper's presentation is unclear in certain sections, particularly Section 4.3, which could benefit from clearer explanations and more concise writing.
- The paper's motivation for introducing FDGN is not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence, and the practical implications of the model are not convincingly demonstrated.
- The paper does not adequately compare its model with other noise-robust graph learning methods, which could undermine the perceived effectiveness of the proposed model.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed model, PRINGLE, handle the complexity of the model structure, particularly the multiple loss terms and latent variables?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed implementation of the proposed method to enhance reproducibility and understanding?
- How does the model handle different types of noise (feature noise, structure noise, label noise) in practical scenarios, and what are the implications of this for the model's performance?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and roles of latent variables (ZA, ZY) in the model, and explain how they relate to the observed variables (X, A, Y)?
- How does the paper address the issue of overfitting in the model, particularly in scenarios with high noise levels?
- Can the authors provide more empirical evidence to support the motivation behind the introduction of FDGN, and demonstrate the practical implications of the model in real-world applications?
