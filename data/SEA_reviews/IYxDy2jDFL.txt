 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for active learning in the agnostic setting, focusing on the active linear regression problem where the goal is to minimize the error in approximating a target vector using a small number of samples from a data matrix. The method combines leverage score sampling with a non-independent sampling strategy based on pivotal sampling, which promotes spatial coverage. The authors demonstrate that this method can significantly reduce the number of samples needed to reach a given target accuracy, backed by theoretical results and empirical evidence. The paper discusses the practical implications of this method in scenarios such as parametric PDEs and uncertainty quantification, showing improvements over traditional methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of ideas and results, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is novel, combining leverage score sampling with a non-independent sampling strategy, which is both theoretically sound and empirically effective.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed method, supported by theoretical results that are backed by empirical evidence, demonstrating its practical applicability.
- The method's simplicity and ease of implementation are highlighted, making it a practical solution for real-world applications.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in active learning, particularly in the agnostic setting, which is crucial for applications in fields like parametric PDEs and uncertainty quantification.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental setup and results could be improved to better support the claims made about the method's effectiveness. Specifically, the experiments should include more diverse test problems and comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods to strengthen the empirical evidence.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially in large-scale applications.
- There is a need for a more comprehensive literature review, particularly in the area of active learning, to position the work within the existing body of knowledge.
- The paper's theoretical contributions, while sound, are not particularly novel or surprising, and the practical implications of the theoretical results are not clearly articulated.
- The paper's assumptions and limitations are not clearly stated, which could lead to confusion about the generalizability and applicability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the distinction between the PCA-based method and the coordinate-wise method mentioned in the paper?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially in large-scale applications?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in relation to the size of the data matrix and the number of samples required?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the data matrix is sparse or has a specific structure, such as a graph?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to other types of active learning problems beyond linear regression?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the assumptions and limitations of their theoretical results, particularly in relation to the practical applicability of the findings?
