 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SIMA, a novel algorithm aimed at enhancing the robustness of deep reinforcement learning (DRL) agents in noisy environments. SIMA leverages a self-supervised learning approach inspired by the multi-sensory integration mechanism in mammalian brains to reconstruct optimal state representations from noisy observations. The algorithm is evaluated across six locomotion control tasks from Pybullet, demonstrating superior performance over existing methods. The paper also includes theoretical analysis and empirical results, showcasing the algorithm's ability to outperform state-of-the-art baselines. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of presentation, the novelty of the approach, and the adequacy of the experimental setup, particularly in terms of the diversity and realism of the noise conditions tested.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed algorithm.
- The introduction of a self-supervised learning approach to reconstruct optimal state representations from noisy observations is innovative and potentially impactful.
- The paper includes both theoretical analysis and empirical results, which support the claims made about the algorithm's effectiveness.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering a range of tasks and noise conditions, and the results show that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art baselines.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that also use self-supervised learning or multi-sensory integration.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as similar methods have been previously explored in other fields, such as masked language modeling and computer vision.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of sim-to-real transfer, which is critical for the practical application of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on locomotion control tasks in simulated environments, which may not fully capture the complexity and variability of real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the mathematical formulations and a clearer presentation of the experimental results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of their work compared to existing methods, particularly in terms of novelty and effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of noise, such as non-stationary noise, which is not explicitly modeled?
- Could the authors provide more details on the sim-to-real transfer capabilities of their method, especially in practical applications?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements compared to other methods?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their work and how these might be mitigated?
- Would it be possible to include more diverse noise conditions in the experiments to better assess the robustness of the method?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the mathematical formulations used in the paper, particularly Equations 1-5, and clarify the definitions and roles of variables used?
