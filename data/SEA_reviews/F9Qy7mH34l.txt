 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Key-Graph Transformer (KGT) for image restoration, which constructs a key-graph based on self-similarity and performs sparse attention operations using the top-k nodes. This approach aims to reduce computational complexity and improve efficiency compared to traditional methods that use fully connected graphs. The KGT model has been tested across various image restoration tasks, showing promising results in terms of performance and efficiency. However, the novelty of the method is questioned due to its similarity to existing graph-based methods and the lack of significant improvement over state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its applications.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on six image restoration tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper includes extensive experiments that validate the proposed method's effectiveness across different image restoration tasks.
- The use of sparse graph attention to reduce computational complexity is a significant contribution, which is well-explained and supported by the results.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing graph-based methods for image restoration, which limits the understanding of the method's novelty and superiority.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods that also use sparse graph attention for image restoration.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the construction of the key graph, particularly how the top-k nodes are selected and the rationale behind this selection.
- The improvement in performance over existing methods is not significant, and the paper does not adequately discuss the limitations or potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational efficiency and memory usage of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical deployment.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with existing graph-based methods for image restoration in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the construction of the key graph, particularly how the top-k nodes are selected and the rationale behind this selection?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of over-smoothing in graph-structured data, and what measures have been taken to address this?
- What is the specific role of the Key-Graph Constructor in the proposed method, and how does it contribute to the overall performance?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage, and how does this compare with existing methods?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and inference costs of the proposed method, and how do these compare with other state-of-the-art methods?
