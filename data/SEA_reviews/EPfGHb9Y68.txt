 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for continual offline reinforcement learning (CORL) using a dual generative replay framework. This framework employs a diffusion-based generative behavior model and a multi-head action evaluation model to retain previous knowledge and model diverse behaviors. It also utilizes a task-conditioned diffusion model to mimic state distributions of past tasks, which are used to generate replayed samples. The method is tested across multiple offline RL benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness in mitigating forgetting and improving forward transfer. The paper's approach is innovative, combining elements of diffusion models and generative replay to address the challenges of continual learning in offline RL settings. However, it faces criticism for its limited evaluation on only four domains and the need for a more comprehensive baseline comparison.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to continual offline reinforcement learning (CORL) by integrating diffusion models with generative replay, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-articulated, making it easy to follow, and the paper is well-written, enhancing its readability and comprehensibility.
- The experiments are extensive and show that the proposed method outperforms baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness in mitigating forgetting and improving forward transfer.
- The paper is original in its approach, combining diffusion models with generative replay to address the challenges of continual learning in offline RL settings, which is a novel contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear and detailed explanations of the methodology and experiments, which aids in understanding the proposed approach and its implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive baseline comparison, which limits the ability to fully evaluate the proposed method against existing approaches.
- The evaluation is limited to only four domains, which may not sufficiently demonstrate the generalizability of the method across different types of tasks.
- The paper does not include a detailed analysis of the computational cost and memory requirements of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the diffusion models, such as the specific architecture used and the training details, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The paper does not discuss potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for any technological advancement.
- There are some minor typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the specific architecture and training parameters used for the diffusion models?
- How does the proposed method perform when evaluated on a broader set of offline RL benchmarks?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the multi-head critic in the method, especially in terms of its contribution to mitigating forgetting?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between the generation of diverse behaviors and the mitigation of forgetting?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost and memory requirements of the proposed method, and how these compare to existing methods?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting, especially in the context of generative replay?
- Can the authors discuss potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
