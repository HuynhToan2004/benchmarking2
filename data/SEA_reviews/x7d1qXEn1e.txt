 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for solving inverse problems in imaging by leveraging pre-trained restoration networks as implicit priors, extending the concept of denoiser priors. The proposed method, termed Deep Restoration Priors (DRP), is theoretically analyzed for its convergence and is demonstrated to outperform state-of-the-art methods in super-resolution and deblurring tasks. The authors provide a theoretical framework that extends the use of MMSE denoisers to general restoration operators, enhancing the theoretical understanding and practical applicability of the method. Despite its strengths, the paper has some limitations, such as a lack of comprehensive comparisons with recent methods and a focus on specific tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, DRP, which extends the use of pre-trained restoration networks as implicit priors, offering a more general approach compared to traditional denoiser priors.
- The theoretical analysis of DRP is robust, providing a comprehensive understanding of the method's behavior and performance.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise presentation of the methodology, assumptions, and results, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various tasks such as super-resolution and deblurring, showing improvements over existing methods.
- The paper includes a convergence analysis of DRP, which is a significant contribution to the field of image restoration.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with recent methods, particularly those that have shown superior performance in similar tasks, which could undermine the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The experiments are limited to specific tasks, such as super-resolution and deblurring, which may not fully demonstrate the general applicability of the DRP method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is a significant factor in practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a broader range of experimental results, including comparisons with different types of degradation operators and a more comprehensive evaluation of the method's performance under varying conditions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with recent methods, particularly those that have shown superior performance in similar tasks?
- How does the DRP method perform when applied to other types of degradation operators, such as those encountered in medical imaging or other applications?
- Could the authors elaborate on the computational complexity of the DRP method and discuss potential strategies for reducing computational overhead?
- What are the limitations of the DRP method, and under what conditions might it not be effective or appropriate?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the DRP method, particularly the assumptions and implications of the convergence analysis?
