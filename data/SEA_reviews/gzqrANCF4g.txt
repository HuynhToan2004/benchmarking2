 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel video tokenizer designed to enhance the performance of language models in visual generation tasks, particularly in image and video generation. The key innovation is the Lookup-Free Quantization (LFQ) method, which eliminates the need for a codebook, reducing the token size and increasing vocabulary size. This approach is supported by architectural enhancements such as a shared codebook for image and video tokenization, and a causal 3D CNN for efficient processing. The paper demonstrates that the proposed tokenizer outperforms existing methods in video generation, compression, and action recognition, showing a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art MAGVIT. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty in the LFQ method and its reliance on pre-existing techniques, which might not sufficiently differentiate it from existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive literature review and a clear explanation of the proposed methods.
- The proposed tokenizer, particularly the LFQ method, is novel and effective, achieving state-of-the-art performance on multiple tasks including video generation, video compression, and action recognition.
- The paper provides a thorough ablation study and detailed comparisons with previous methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed modifications.
- The paper introduces a novel lookup-free quantization approach that significantly improves the visual generation quality of language models by learning a large vocabulary.
- The video compressor introduced in the paper shows better quality than HEVC and VVC at similar bit rates, according to user studies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with the latest video generation methods, such as those using diffusion models, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The novelty of the LFQ method is questioned as it is based on existing methods with minor modifications.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and inference time of the proposed method compared to other methods, which could affect the practical applicability of the model.
- The paper's reliance on pre-trained models and its lack of discussion on the transferability of the proposed method to other datasets or tasks limits the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and inference time of the proposed method compared to other methods, especially in terms of GPU memory usage and training speed?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of generalization to other datasets or tasks, particularly those not directly related to video generation?
- Could the authors clarify the specific contributions of the LFQ method beyond existing methods, and discuss the potential for further improvements in the method?
- How does the proposed method handle different image resolutions, especially in scenarios where the resolution varies significantly?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the training and inference time, and discuss the potential for scaling up the model to larger vocabulary sizes?
