 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to 3D self-supervised pretraining by focusing on predicting high-order features like point normals and surface variations at masked points, rather than reconstructing their positions. This methodology is supported by a new attention-based decoder that does not rely on a specific encoder architecture. The authors argue that this approach improves performance on various downstream tasks, including shape classification and part segmentation, compared to existing methods. The methodology is validated through extensive experiments, including ablation studies, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, as similar approaches have been explored in previous works, and the paper's contribution to the field is questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to 3D self-supervised pretraining, focusing on predicting high-order features like point normals and surface variations at masked points, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that focus on reconstructing point positions.
- The proposed attention-based decoder is independent of the encoder design, which allows for a flexible integration with various encoder architectures, enhancing the adaptability of the method.
- Extensive experiments and ablation studies are conducted to validate the effectiveness of the pretraining task and decoder design, demonstrating the advantages of the proposed method on various point cloud analysis tasks.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear visualizations and explanations that aid in understanding the methodology and results.
- The proposed method shows state-of-the-art performance on various downstream tasks, indicating its effectiveness in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities and potential challenges.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as similar approaches have been explored in previous works, such as the use of normal and variation prediction in MAE-based pretraining.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with the most recent and relevant works, such as Point-M2AE, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's relative performance and contributions.
- The paper's contribution to the field is questioned, as the method primarily involves predicting point features instead of their positions, which might not represent a significant advancement over existing methods.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to state-of-the-art methods, and the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in all scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of their method in comparison to previous works, particularly in terms of the use of normal and variation prediction in MAE-based pretraining?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical implementation with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in scenarios where computational resources are limited?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with the most recent and relevant works, such as Point-M2AE, to better understand the relative performance and contributions of their method?
- In the context of the experimental results, how do the authors justify the claimed improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in scenarios where the improvements are marginal or not statistically significant?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex or realistic scenarios, such as large-scale real-world point clouds, where the performance might be more critical?
