 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Δ-SGD, an adaptive SGD method designed for federated learning (FL) that allows each client to set its own learning rate based on the local smoothness of the function being optimized. The method is supported by theoretical analysis and extensive experiments across various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in adapting to local data distributions and achieving superior performance compared to other FL algorithms. The authors provide a comprehensive convergence analysis, including both convex and nonconvex settings, and showcase the method's ability to handle heterogeneous client data distributions and different model architectures. However, the paper has been criticized for its incremental nature, lack of clarity in presentation, and potential limitations in scalability and practical applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the experimental results.
- The proposed Δ-SGD algorithm is simple, adaptive, and can be combined with various FL algorithms, showing promising results across different datasets and model architectures.
- The convergence analysis is thorough, including both convex and nonconvex settings, and the algorithm's performance is evaluated on multiple datasets, including image and text classification tasks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is commendable for its transparency and ethical considerations.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in handling heterogeneous client data distributions and different model architectures, showing superior performance compared to other FL algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with existing methods, particularly those that use adaptive step sizes for FL, which could have strengthened the argument for the superiority of Δ-SGD.
- The convergence analysis in the nonconvex setting is limited, focusing on first-order stationary points rather than convergence to the optimal solution.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method in large-scale FL scenarios, which could limit its practical applicability.
- There are concerns about the novelty of the proposed method, as similar adaptive step size rules have been previously proposed in other contexts, such as in non-FL settings.
- The paper suffers from several typographical and formatting errors, which could detract from its professional presentation.
- The paper's reliance on grid search for hyperparameter tuning is not clearly explained, and the potential impact of this approach on the fairness of the experimental comparisons is not addressed.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions and implications of Assumption 1, particularly concerning the relationship between f and fi?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with more local training epochs, and what are the implications for the scalability of the algorithm?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods that use adaptive step sizes for FL, particularly in terms of convergence analysis and practical performance?
- How does the proposed method compare to other adaptive methods in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters, especially the grid search method used, and discuss its potential impact on the experimental results?
- Is there a possibility to extend the convergence analysis to include convergence to the optimal solution, rather than just first-order stationary points?
