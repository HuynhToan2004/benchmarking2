 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TimeMixer, a novel deep learning architecture for time series forecasting that utilizes a multiscale mixing approach. This architecture consists of Past-Decomposable-Mixing (PDM) and Future-Multipredictor-Mixing (FMM) blocks, designed to leverage disentangled multiscale information for both past extraction and future prediction. The PDM block decomposes multiscale series and mixes decomposed seasonal and trend components in fine-to-coarse and coarse-to-fine directions, while the FMM block ensembles multiple predictors to utilize complementary forecasting capabilities in multiscale observations. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of TimeMixer through extensive experiments on both long-term and short-term forecasting tasks, achieving state-of-the-art performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental results are strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the proposed architecture, which utilizes disentangled multiscale information for both past extraction and future prediction, is significant and contributes to the field of time series forecasting.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its implementation, including the use of MLP mixer and the decomposition block from Autoformer.
- The experimental setup is comprehensive, covering a wide range of datasets and benchmarks, and the results are impressive, showing state-of-the-art performance in both long-term and short-term forecasting tasks.
- The paper includes thorough ablation studies that help in understanding the impact of different components of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities and potential improvements.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the proposed method across different types of time series data, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the model's architecture, particularly the PDM block and its integration with other components like FMM.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the scalability of the proposed method with increasing dataset sizes or the impact of different input lengths on performance.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency and memory usage of the proposed method compared to other existing methods?
- How does the proposed method perform on other types of time series data beyond those used in the experiments?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the limitations of the proposed method and potential areas for improvement?
- Could the authors clarify the role and integration of the PDM block with other components like FMM, and discuss the impact of different input lengths on the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method handle the decomposition of time series data into different scales, and what are the implications of this decomposition for the model's performance?
