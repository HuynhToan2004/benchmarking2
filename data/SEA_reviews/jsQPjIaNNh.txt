 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ProtIR, a novel iterative refinement framework designed to enhance protein function prediction by integrating predictor and retriever models. This method leverages a variational pseudo-likelihood framework to iteratively refine predictions, showing significant improvements over traditional methods. The approach utilizes both sequence and structure similarity to predict protein functions, demonstrating superior performance in tasks like Enzyme Commission (EC) and Gene Ontology (GO) prediction. The paper also discusses the integration of structural information into protein language model-based retrievers, which further enhances the framework's capabilities.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, ProtIR, which effectively integrates predictor and retriever models to enhance protein function prediction, demonstrating significant improvements over traditional methods.
- The use of a variational pseudo-likelihood framework for iterative refinement between predictors and retrievers is innovative and shows potential for advancing the field.
- The inclusion of structural information into protein language model-based retrievers significantly improves the framework's performance, highlighting the importance of structural details in function annotation.
- The paper is well-written, making the complex methodology and results accessible and understandable to readers.
- Comprehensive experiments and ablation studies are conducted to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method, providing a robust evaluation of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of key concepts such as the "retriever" and the "predictor," which could confuse readers unfamiliar with the terminology.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its method from existing approaches like ESM-2, which might lead to questions about the novelty of the proposed framework.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, especially those using similar approaches, to establish the superiority of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- Some sections of the paper, particularly those involving mathematical notations and the explanation of certain methodologies, are overly complex and could be simplified to improve readability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of the "retriever" and the "predictor" in the context of their proposed framework?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements with other state-of-the-art methods?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the method's performance on different types of protein functions, especially those that are less well-characterized?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of protein function prediction for proteins without known structures or sequences?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential societal impacts and limitations of their proposed method, particularly in areas such as data privacy and ethical considerations?
- It would be helpful if the authors could include a more detailed discussion on the method's scalability and applicability to other protein-related tasks beyond function prediction.
