 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph pooling method called Graph Parsing Networks (GPN), which employs a hierarchical graph pooling strategy to compress graph information into a compact representation. The GPN method learns a personalized pooling structure for each graph, allowing it to adaptively adjust the pooling ratio and number of pooling layers. The paper claims that this approach outperforms existing methods in graph classification and node classification tasks, demonstrating superior memory efficiency and node information preservation. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to previous works, and concerns are raised about its scalability and the effectiveness of the learned pooling structures.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is novel and interesting, particularly in the application of grammar induction in graph pooling.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes clear visualizations that aid in understanding the methodology.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in terms of memory efficiency and node information preservation.
- The paper includes a comprehensive literature review, which provides a solid foundation for the research.
- The use of hierarchical pooling in graph pooling is a promising approach, and the paper presents a good balance between memory efficiency and node information preservation.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear and concise writing, and the inclusion of ablation studies supports the design choices of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the differences between the proposed method and existing works, particularly in terms of how it learns personalized pooling structures for each individual graph.
- The methodology section is not well-explained, with some equations and notations being confusing or missing.
- The paper's claims of novelty are questioned due to similarities with previous works, such as the use of grammar induction and hierarchical graph pooling.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some results being below the baselines, and the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the limitations and broader impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper's scalability is a concern, particularly in handling large graphs, and the method's effectiveness in such scenarios is not demonstrated.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the learned pooling structures are utilized in graph-level tasks, which is crucial for understanding the method's impact on graph classification.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a clearer explanation of how the proposed method learns personalized pooling structures for each individual graph, and what are the main differences between this approach and existing works?
- How does the proposed method ensure that the learned pooling structures are optimal for graph-level tasks, particularly in terms of their impact on graph classification performance?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, especially in terms of scalability and handling large graphs?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in relation to the time and memory efficiency claims?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the graph pooling structure needs to be personalized for each graph, and what are the implications for scalability and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly in scenarios where the proposed method underperforms or does not meet expectations?
