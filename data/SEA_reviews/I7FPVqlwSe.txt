 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework called Neural Reward Translation (NRT) aimed at enhancing cross-domain reinforcement learning (RL) by utilizing reward machines to translate rewards from one domain to another. The framework is designed to handle semi-alignable Markov Decision Processes (MDPs), which are a subset of MDPs where the reward functions remain consistent despite differences in other aspects like state and action spaces. The authors propose a method for translating rewards from one domain to another using reward machines, which are trained using either human-defined criteria or large language models. The effectiveness of the NRT framework is demonstrated through experiments on various environments, including simple games and more complex Atari tasks. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the framework, including the definitions of semi-alignable MDPs and reward machines, and the use of these concepts to facilitate cross-domain RL.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to cross-domain reinforcement learning.
- The use of reward machines to translate rewards between domains is a novel and interesting approach, which could potentially be applied to various domains.
- The paper includes a variety of experiments, both simple and complex, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in enhancing learning in reinforcement tasks across different domains.
- The theoretical framework presented is well-articulated, and the paper provides a comprehensive literature review, situating the work within the current state of the art.
- The concept of semi-alignable MDPs is well-motivated and the use of reward machines to abstract skills and draw inferences from one domain to reuse the reward in another is a compelling approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key concepts such as semi-alignable MDPs and the role of reward machines.
- There is a lack of rigorous experimental validation, with insufficient details on the experimental setup and results, which makes it difficult to assess the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in complex environments where the method may not perform well.
- The use of large language models (LLMs) to construct reward machines is not convincingly demonstrated to be superior or necessary compared to other methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods in the field, and the novelty of the method is questionable.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or use cases where the proposed method would be particularly effective?
- How do the authors define the bijection between the abstract state and the reward machine state, and what criteria are used for this mapping?
- Is there a specific reason why the method was tested only on simple games and not more complex environments like Atari?
- How does the method handle tasks with long horizons, such as those in Atari, and what are the implications for the training process?
- Can the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of reward machines in the cross-domain RL process, especially in environments where the reward machines may not be as effective?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability in real-world scenarios?
