 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel federated learning approach, FST-CBDG, which utilizes a pretrained CLIP model to enhance unsupervised federated learning. This method employs a self-training loss and a synthetic instance sampling loss to improve the quality of pseudo-labels and address class imbalance, respectively. The approach is evaluated on standard datasets such as CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and CINIC-10, demonstrating superior performance over traditional federated learning methods. The paper also discusses the computational and communication efficiency of the proposed method, highlighting its potential for lightweight and efficient training on edge devices.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in federated learning by proposing a method that utilizes a pretrained CLIP model for unsupervised federated learning, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, clearly written, and easy to follow, with a logical structure that aids in understanding the contributions of each component.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through experiments, showing significant improvements over traditional federated learning methods and other baselines.
- The method's simplicity and the use of pretrained models to address data heterogeneity in federated learning are innovative and could be applied to various federated learning scenarios.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including an ablation study that demonstrates the effectiveness of the self-training loss and synthetic instance sampling loss.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of scalability and generalizability to other federated learning scenarios.
- There is a lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in federated learning, which could provide a clearer picture of the method's relative performance.
- The paper primarily focuses on image classification tasks, which may limit the generalizability of the findings to other types of data or tasks.
- The computational and communication efficiency of the proposed method are not convincingly demonstrated, particularly in terms of the practical implementation and the scalability of the method to larger datasets or more complex models.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the method's applicability to other types of data or tasks, and how it might be adapted to different federated learning scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the privacy implications of the proposed method, which is crucial in federated learning.
