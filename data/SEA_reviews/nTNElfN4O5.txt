 **Summary:**
The paper introduces IHDiff, a novel diffusion model designed to learn prior distributions of hand-hand interactions, leveraging a Transformer-based network for effective reverse diffusion. This model allows for random sampling, conditional sampling, and fitting to observations, addressing the challenge of generating plausible and diverse 3D interacting hands. The paper presents a diffusion model that captures the correlation between joints of two hands using self- and cross-attention, aiming to model the prior distribution of hand-hand interactions. The methodology is supported by qualitative and quantitative experiments, demonstrating the model's effectiveness in generating realistic and diverse hand interactions. However, concerns are raised about the novelty and the generalization of the model to various hand poses and interactions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear writing and a logical structure that facilitates understanding.
- The introduction of a diffusion model for generating hand-hand interactions is innovative and addresses a significant gap in the literature, particularly in the field of hand-hand interaction modeling.
- The proposed method demonstrates superior performance over existing methods, such as VAE, in generating realistic and diverse hand-hand interactions, as evidenced by both qualitative and quantitative results.
- The paper provides a detailed explanation of the methodology, including the design of the Transformer-based network and the loss function, which aids in understanding the model's effectiveness.
- The paper includes comprehensive experiments and user studies, which support the claims made and provide a robust validation of the model's capabilities.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is questioned due to its reliance on existing diffusion models and the lack of significant innovation in the Transformer-based network design.
- The paper's focus on kinematic-level interactions without considering elasticity limits the model's ability to accurately represent real-world hand interactions, which may affect the practical applicability of the model.
- The generalization of the model to various hand poses and interactions is not adequately addressed, raising concerns about the model's ability to handle diverse scenarios effectively.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance metrics and qualitative results, which could strengthen the argument for the model's superiority.
- The paper's dependence on a specific dataset (IH2.6M) for training and testing limits the model's applicability to other datasets or real-world scenarios, which could affect the model's robustness and generalizability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their method, particularly in terms of how it advances beyond existing diffusion models and Transformer-based networks?
- How does the model handle different types of hand-hand interactions beyond simple grasping or holding, and can it adapt to various scenarios such as hand-to-hand pushing or pulling?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the model handles different hand poses and shapes, especially in scenarios where the hands are not in direct contact?
- How does the model ensure the generation of plausible and diverse interactions, especially in scenarios where the training data might not cover all possible interactions?
- Can the authors elaborate on the training and testing methodology, particularly how the model is adapted for different datasets or real-world scenarios?
- Would it be possible to include a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including both quantitative and qualitative results, to better establish the model's performance and effectiveness?
