 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to approximating Markov chains using Ito diffusion, focusing on a broad class of Ito chains that can incorporate isotropic and state-dependent noise, which is a significant generalization from typical Markov chains. The paper presents an upper bound for the W2-distance between the law of the Ito chain and the corresponding stochastic differential equation, showing that the proposed method can be applied to a variety of practical scenarios such as stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics, stochastic gradient descent, and stochastic gradient boosting. Despite its theoretical strength, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of experimental validation and for not sufficiently distinguishing its contributions from existing literature.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a unified framework for analyzing a broad class of Markov chains, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- It provides a clear and concise explanation of the main results, making the paper accessible and understandable.
- The paper is well-written, with a logical flow that effectively connects the results from different sections.
- The theoretical results are sound and contribute to the understanding of the convergence of Markov chains to a diffusion process.
- The paper covers a wide range of applications, including stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics, stochastic gradient descent, and stochastic gradient boosting, which are relevant in practical scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks experimental validation, which could have strengthened the claims made about the practical applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from existing literature, particularly in terms of the novelty and depth of the theoretical results.
- The paper is dense and requires careful reading to fully understand the contributions and implications of the results.
- The assumptions made in the paper are somewhat restrictive, particularly concerning the Lipschitz continuity of the drift term, which may limit the general applicability of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implications of the theoretical results, which could be a significant limitation for practitioners seeking to apply the proposed method in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the Lipschitz continuity of the drift term and the central limit theorem for the noise function?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing approaches, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide examples or practical scenarios where the proposed method could be particularly beneficial or challenging to apply?
- How does the paper address the potential limitations or challenges in applying the proposed method in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical results and their implications, particularly in terms of their practical relevance and the depth of the contributions to the field?
