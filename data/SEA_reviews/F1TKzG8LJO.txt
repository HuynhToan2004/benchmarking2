 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to task generalization in robotic manipulation using 2D trajectory sketches. The authors propose a method where trajectory sketches are used to condition robot policies, which are trained using imitation learning. The approach is evaluated on a variety of tasks, showing improved generalization over traditional language-based and goal-based policies. The paper also discusses the potential for using trajectory sketches to guide robot policies in novel scenarios, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in real-world applications. However, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity in explaining the trajectory sketch generation process and the method's scalability, particularly in handling complex environments and multiple objects.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible.
- The proposed method is innovative, focusing on the use of 2D trajectory sketches as a conditioning signal for robot policies, which is a novel approach to task generalization in robotic manipulation.
- The method is evaluated on a variety of tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in real-world applications.
- The paper is structured well, with a clear introduction, detailed methodology, and thorough experimental evaluation.
- The use of trajectory sketches as a conditioning signal for robot policies is a promising direction for robotic manipulation, as it allows for more intuitive and user-friendly task specification.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the trajectory sketch generation process, particularly how it is derived from demonstrations and how it is used to guide robot policies.
- The scalability of the method is a concern, especially in more complex environments involving multiple objects and more complex manipulation tasks.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of generalization capabilities and the ability to handle more complex tasks.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method, such as its applicability to more complex tasks and its scalability in more complex environments.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the method, such as its inability to handle complex tasks or its scalability in more complex environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the challenges and limitations of the method, as well as potential directions for future research.

- How does the method perform in more complex environments involving multiple objects and more complex manipulation tasks?
- What are the specific challenges and limitations of the method, and how might these be addressed in future research?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential of the method to handle more complex tasks and to scale to more complex environments?
