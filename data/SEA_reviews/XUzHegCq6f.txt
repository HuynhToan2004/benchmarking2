 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Polyak Parameter Ensemble (PPE) method, which maintains a running weighted average of model parameters across epochs to improve generalization without increasing computational costs. This approach allows for parameter averaging without the need for additional training, which is beneficial in scenarios where computational resources are limited. The method was tested on various datasets including CIFAR-10, FB15K-237, and YAGO3-10, showing improvements in generalization performance. Despite its simplicity and potential cost savings, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and insufficient empirical evidence.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to ensemble learning, specifically using Polyak averaging to maintain a running average of model parameters, which is a simple yet effective method.
- The method is straightforward to implement and does not require additional computational resources, making it accessible and practical for various applications.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate that the proposed method consistently improves generalization performance, showing its effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly explained, which facilitates understanding and replication of the results.
- The authors have provided a clear rationale for the use of Polyak averaging, which helps in understanding the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as the idea of averaging parameters has been previously explored in other works, and the proposed method does not significantly deviate from existing methods.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The empirical evidence provided is insufficient, particularly in terms of the variety and scale of datasets used, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational overhead of maintaining a running weighted average of model parameters, which could potentially impact the practical applicability of the method.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There are several grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper, which detract from its professional quality.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other ensemble methods, particularly those that use different averaging techniques or weighting strategies?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the training data is limited or highly imbalanced?
- Can the authors clarify the choice of parameters such as the learning rate and the number of epochs used in the experiments?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage with other ensemble methods that use different averaging techniques or weighting strategies?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of datasets and the rationale behind their selection?
- What are the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how might these affect its practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data distribution is highly skewed or the training data is highly correlated?
