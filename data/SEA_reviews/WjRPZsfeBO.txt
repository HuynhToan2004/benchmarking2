 **Summary:**
The paper presents a theoretical analysis of Wasserstein Autoencoders (WAEs) in the context of learning data distributions when the data exhibits a low-dimensional structure within a high-dimensional feature space. It derives error bounds for WAEs using an oracle inequality and shows that these bounds depend on the intrinsic dimension of the data, not the full dimensionality. The paper also discusses the convergence rates of the expected excess risk in the number of samples and the choice of network architectures. It argues that these rates are independent of the high feature dimension and rely solely on the intrinsic dimension of the data distribution. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in presenting the results, the complexity of the proofs, and the limited applicability of the results to real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of Wasserstein Autoencoders (WAEs) in the context of learning data distributions when the data exhibits a low-dimensional structure within a high-dimensional feature space.
- The authors have derived error bounds for WAEs using an oracle inequality and shown that these bounds depend on the intrinsic dimension of the data, not the full dimensionality.
- The paper demonstrates that the convergence rates of the expected excess risk in the number of samples for WAEs are independent of the high feature dimension, instead relying only on the intrinsic dimension of the data distribution.
- The theoretical framework is original and provides a novel perspective on the statistical analysis of WAEs.
- The paper is well-written, and the proofs are detailed and clear, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is difficult to follow, particularly in terms of the notation used and the complexity of the proofs.
- The results are not clearly presented, and it is unclear how the main results relate to the practical applications of WAEs.
- The paper lacks empirical validation, making it difficult to assess the practical applicability of the theoretical results.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis are quite strong and may not hold in practical scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide a clear comparison with existing literature, making it difficult to assess the novelty and contribution of the work.
- The paper does not address the optimization error, which is a significant component in practical applications of WAEs.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the theoretical results and how they apply in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**

- How do the main results relate to practical applications of WAEs, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the results?
