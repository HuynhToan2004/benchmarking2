 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph neural network (GNN) model designed for predicting the elasticity tensor of architected lattice metamaterials. The model is based on the MACE architecture and is trained on a newly created dataset, which includes 354,000 perturbed lattice structures. The model is equivariant to rigid body rotations and translations, ensuring energy conservation by maintaining the positive semi-definiteness of the predicted stiffness tensor. The authors benchmark their model against various baselines and demonstrate its superior performance in predicting elasticity properties. The paper also includes a detailed discussion on the dataset creation, model architecture, and training strategies, with a focus on the physical constraints of energy conservation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new task and dataset in the machine learning community, which can be used by researchers in the future to improve higher order physics-focused models.
- The proposed model is the first equivariant model trained for prediction of the fourth-order elasticity tensor, ensuring energy conservation in the predicted stiffness tensor.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the background, related work, and the proposed method.
- The results section is comprehensive, providing detailed comparisons between the proposed model and other architectures used for property prediction of lattice materials.
- The paper addresses the important issue of energy conservation in the context of equivariant models for lattice materials, which is crucial for ensuring the physical validity of the predictions.
- The authors provide a thorough discussion on the dataset creation and the model architecture, including details on the dataset, training strategies, and the model's performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the physical constraints of energy conservation, which could be improved by including more detailed explanations and examples.
- The model's performance in terms of energy conservation and equivariance is not convincingly demonstrated, especially in the absence of a baseline model that satisfies these constraints.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the practical challenges and potential improvements.
- The choice of baseline models and the comparisons made are not sufficiently justified, which could affect the perceived effectiveness of the proposed model.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experimental validation, including a broader range of lattice structures and a more rigorous comparison with existing models.
- The manuscript could be improved by including more detailed discussions on the dataset and the training strategies used, particularly in the main text rather than relegating these to the appendix.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the meaning of "perturbed lattice" in the context of the dataset creation?
- Why is the validation set fixed for all training runs, and what are the implications of this choice?
- How does the positive semi-definiteness of the predicted stiffness tensor affect the model's performance, and can you provide more details on this aspect?
- Can you explain the rationale behind the choice of baseline models and the comparisons made in the paper?
- How does the model ensure energy conservation during the training process, and what are the implications of this on the model's performance?
- Could you provide more details on the computational efficiency of the model, especially in comparison to finite element methods?
- How does the model handle the physical constraints of energy conservation, and what are the implications of these constraints on the model's predictions?
