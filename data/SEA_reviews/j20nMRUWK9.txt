 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for generalized category discovery (GCD), focusing on the explicit transfer of knowledge from known classes to novel classes. The method is composed of three stages: knowledge generation, alignment, and distillation. It leverages a pre-trained model on known classes to generate initial representations, which are then transformed using an adapter layer and channel selection matrix to facilitate knowledge transfer. The final stage involves knowledge distillation to enhance the learning of both known and novel classes. The method is evaluated on various benchmark datasets and shown to outperform existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the experimental setup, and the lack of detailed analysis on the components of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides clear explanations of the methodology and experimental results.
- The proposed method, which includes knowledge generation, alignment, and distillation, is innovative and offers a novel approach to generalized category discovery (GCD).
- The experiments are comprehensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods.
- The introduction of a new benchmark, iNat21, is a valuable contribution to the field, providing a new standard for evaluating GCD methods.
- The paper is well-motivated and addresses a significant problem in GCD, with a clear focus on transferring knowledge from known classes to novel classes.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the components of the method, particularly the adapter layer and channel selection matrix, which are crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing methods like mutual information maximization and knowledge distillation, without sufficient differentiation from previous works.
- The experimental setup is not clearly described, particularly in terms of the hyperparameters used and the training details, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which could detract from its professional presentation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed analysis on the adapter layer and channel selection matrix to clarify their contributions to the method?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods when the number of classes is unknown, and how does it handle scenarios where the number of classes is different from what is expected?
- Can the authors clarify the training details, particularly the hyperparameters used and the training schedules, to ensure the reproducibility and comparability of the results?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the semantic similarity between known and novel classes is high or low?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their method and suggest areas for future research?
