 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge distillation in federated learning, focusing on text-free methods that do not rely on GANs or auxiliary data. This method utilizes sampling from the embedding structure of transformers to generate pseudo data, which is then used to distill knowledge from a teacher model to a student model. The paper presents three sampling methods: random sampling, target sampling, and adversarial sampling, and demonstrates their effectiveness through experiments on the SuperGLUE benchmark. The approach is claimed to be lightweight, requiring no communication overhead and yielding improved model performance with relatively lower computational costs on the server side. However, concerns are raised about the method's novelty, the clarity of its presentation, and the robustness of its experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed method.
- The idea of using the embedding space of a teacher model to generate pseudo data for knowledge distillation is innovative and potentially effective.
- The proposed sampling methods are simple and effective, with empirical results showing improvements in model performance.
- The paper addresses an important issue in federated learning, particularly in the context of text classification, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The method does not require auxiliary data or GANs, which can help mitigate privacy concerns and reduce communication overhead.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as similar methods have been previously explored in other works, particularly in the context of adversarial training and knowledge distillation.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with marginal improvements over baseline methods and a lack of detailed analysis on the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational overhead and privacy implications.
- The paper is difficult to read due to unclear writing and presentation, with some sections being hard to follow.
- The method's effectiveness is questionable, especially in scenarios where data is not non-iid or where the teacher model is not significantly stronger than the student model.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and generalizability of the proposed methods, particularly in larger or more complex models.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their method compared to existing works, particularly in the context of adversarial training and knowledge distillation?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational overhead and privacy implications with other data-free distillation methods, such as FedGEN and FedFTG?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including a breakdown of the improvements by task and a comparison with other methods on standard federated learning benchmarks?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where data is not non-iid or where the teacher model is not significantly stronger than the student model?
- Can the authors elaborate on the privacy concerns associated with the proposed method, particularly in terms of how the pseudo-data is generated and transmitted?
- How does the proposed method scale to larger or more complex models, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in such scenarios?
