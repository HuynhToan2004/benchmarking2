 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel convex reformulation of the self-attention mechanism in transformer networks, aiming to address the challenges of training non-convex attention networks. This reformulation is designed to improve optimization and provide a more interpretable understanding of the functions learned by the transformer. The authors claim that this approach can lead to faster convergence and better generalization in some scenarios, although the paper lacks comprehensive experimental validation on larger, more complex datasets. The proposed method is also criticized for its limited applicability to real-world transformer architectures and its potential to introduce significant computational overhead.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel convex reformulation of the self-attention mechanism in transformer networks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to support the proposed method, which is a strong point in validating the approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the authors have provided a clear motivation for their work.
- The paper explores the use of convex optimization to improve the understanding and optimization of transformer networks, which is a promising direction for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks comprehensive experimental validation, with only a few small-scale experiments on simple datasets. More extensive experiments on larger, more complex datasets are needed to substantiate the claims made.
- The proposed method is not applicable to real-world transformer architectures that use skip connections and layer normalization, which are crucial components for practical applications.
- The computational overhead of the proposed method is significant, which could limit its practical use in resource-constrained environments.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for larger models.
- The method's effectiveness in more complex scenarios, such as multi-output problems or deep transformer architectures, is not convincingly demonstrated.
- The paper's claims regarding the optimization of transformer networks are not fully supported by the experimental evidence provided.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method could be extended to real-world transformer architectures that use skip connections and layer normalization?
- How does the computational overhead of the proposed method compare to existing methods, and what are the practical implications of this overhead in terms of training and inference times?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, particularly on larger, more complex datasets, to substantiate the claims made about the method's effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex scenarios, such as multi-output problems or deep transformer architectures?
- Can the authors discuss the implications of the sparsity-inducing regularization mechanism across tokens in more detail, particularly in relation to the observed performance improvements?
