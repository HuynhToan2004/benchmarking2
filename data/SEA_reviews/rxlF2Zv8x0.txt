 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for protein optimization using Gibbs sampling with graph-based smoothing (GGS). This approach leverages a smoothed fitness landscape to enhance the performance of existing optimization techniques like Gibbs with Gradients (GWG). The methodology involves training a neural network on a smoothed fitness landscape derived from a noisy fitness model and a graph Laplacian. The authors demonstrate that this smoothed fitness model can improve the performance of GWG in both in-silico and real-world settings, showcasing state-of-the-art results in GFP and AAV proteins. However, the paper lacks a detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the smoothing process and the choice of hyperparameters, which could limit its broader applicability and understanding.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-structured methodology.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with a novel approach to smoothing the fitness landscape which is both theoretically and empirically sound.
- The paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation, including a detailed analysis of the method's performance across various datasets and hyperparameters.
- The use of graph signal processing for protein optimization is innovative and could potentially be applied to other areas of machine learning.
- The method is shown to improve upon existing baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the smoothing process, which could limit the understanding and applicability of the method.
- There is a need for more extensive experiments to validate the method's effectiveness across a broader range of datasets and protein structures.
- The choice of hyperparameters, particularly the smoothing parameter γ, is not well-justified, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the method, such as the potential for over-smoothing and the impact of different graph structures on the results.
- There is a lack of comparative analysis with other smoothing methods, which could provide a better understanding of the proposed method's advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of datasets and the implications of using different protein structures and mutational rates.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the theoretical underpinnings of the smoothing process, particularly how it affects the optimization landscape and the choice of hyperparameters?
- How does the proposed method compare to other smoothing methods, such as those using Gaussian noise or other regularization techniques?
- Can the authors discuss the implications of using different graph structures on the smoothing process and the resulting optimization landscape?
- How does the proposed method perform on other types of proteins, such as those with different mutational rates or structures?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the method and the scalability of the approach to larger protein structures?
- How does the method handle the potential for over-smoothing, and what are the implications of this for the accuracy and generalizability of the results?
