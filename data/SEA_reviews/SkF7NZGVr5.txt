 **Summary:**
The paper explores the phenomenon of loss of plasticity in continual learning, focusing on the role of curvature in neural network training dynamics. It proposes a novel regularization approach using the Wasserstein metric to preserve curvature and mitigate plasticity loss. The authors conduct experiments across various datasets and settings to demonstrate the effectiveness of their method, comparing it to existing regularization techniques. Despite the innovative approach and the experimental validation, the paper faces criticisms regarding the clarity of the problem definition, the necessity of the proposed regularization, and the lack of comprehensive comparative analysis with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the loss of plasticity in neural networks, focusing on the role of curvature in this phenomenon.
- The authors propose a novel regularization approach using the Wasserstein metric, which is shown to be effective in maintaining plasticity and reducing plasticity loss.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive set of experiments across different datasets and settings, which support the claims made.
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue in continual learning, specifically focusing on the loss of plasticity and its impact on neural network training dynamics.
- The proposed method is novel and potentially impactful, providing a new perspective on the problem of plasticity loss in neural networks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition of the problem, particularly in terms of what constitutes a "plastic" network and how plasticity is measured. This ambiguity could lead to confusion and misunderstanding of the results.
- The experiments conducted are somewhat limited, focusing primarily on small-scale datasets and simple settings. More comprehensive experiments on larger, more complex datasets are needed to validate the findings.
- The paper does not provide sufficient comparative analysis with existing methods, such as regenerative regularization, which could help contextualize the contributions of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of computational cost and the practicality of using the method in real-world applications.
- There are concerns about the novelty of the proposed method, as it seems to resemble existing methods like regenerative regularization, without sufficient differentiation or justification.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the theoretical aspects of the problem, which could strengthen the claims made about the role of curvature in plasticity loss.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition of plasticity and how it is measured in the context of their experiments?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods, such as regenerative regularization, in terms of effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the computational cost of using the Wasserstein regularizer, particularly in terms of training time and resource requirements?
- Is there a theoretical basis for the observation that the rank of the Hessian decreases with the number of tasks, and how does this relate to the overall loss of plasticity?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex, real-world scenarios, such as those involving large-scale datasets or more varied training conditions?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other methods, such as those mentioned in the literature, to better contextualize the contributions of their work?
