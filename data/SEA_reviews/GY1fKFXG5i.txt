 **Summary:**
The paper explores the generalization capabilities of large language models (LLMs) by proposing a novel approach called SubLoRA, which integrates LoRA with subspace training to enhance model compression. This methodology aims to provide non-vacuous generalization bounds for LLMs, addressing the challenge of unbounded negative log-likelihood loss. The authors demonstrate that larger models tend to generalize better, and the model's structure influences its generalization. The paper also discusses the impact of text structure on model generalization and presents a method for computing generalization bounds. However, it has been critiqued for its limited applicability to large-scale pre-trained models, the lack of detailed analysis on the model's generalization, and the need for a more thorough comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, SubLoRA, which combines LoRA and subspace training to enhance model compression and improve generalization bounds.
- It provides the first non-vacuous generalization bounds for pre-trained large language models (LLMs), addressing a significant gap in the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, making the paper accessible and easy to follow, even for non-experts.
- The paper presents a comprehensive analysis of the generalization capabilities of LLMs, which is crucial for understanding their behavior and potential applications.
- The authors demonstrate that larger models tend to generalize better, which is an interesting finding that could guide future research and development in the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a clear presentation of the main ideas, which enhances its readability and accessibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on training LLMs from scratch, which may not be practical for large-scale pre-trained models, limiting its applicability.
- The generalization bounds provided are limited to the token level, which may not fully capture the model's generalization capabilities, especially in more complex tasks like image classification.
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis on how the model's structure influences its generalization, which is a significant aspect of the study.
- There is a need for a more thorough comparison with existing methods, such as LoRA and other compression techniques, to establish the superiority of SubLoRA.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including its applicability to different types of LLMs and its generalization to other tasks beyond language modeling.
- The paper could provide more empirical evidence to support the claims made, particularly regarding the effectiveness of SubLoRA in improving generalization.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the derivation of the generalization bounds, particularly regarding the independence of token predictions?
- How does the model's generalization capability vary with the size of the training dataset, and what are the implications of this for practical applications?
- What are the specific advantages of SubLoRA over other compression techniques like LoRA, and how does it improve upon existing methods?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the training data used and the specifics of the model architectures tested?
- How does the proposed method perform in practical applications, such as in-context learning and prompt-tuning, which are increasingly popular in the field of LLMs?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of LLMs and its generalization to other tasks beyond language modeling?
