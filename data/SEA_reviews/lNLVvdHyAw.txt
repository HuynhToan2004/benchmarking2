 **Summary:**
The paper discusses a method for detecting adversarial suffix attacks on large language models (LLMs) using a gradient boosting classifier based on perplexity and token length. The authors propose a supervised approach that leverages perplexity as a measure of language fluency to distinguish between adversarial and non-adversarial prompts. The methodology is tested against various LLMs and datasets, aiming to minimize false positives while maintaining a high detection rate. The paper also addresses the limitations of the approach, such as the potential for adversarial attacks to manipulate perplexity scores and the need for a more comprehensive evaluation of the classifier's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, with a clear motivation and well-structured presentation.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and effective, using a gradient boosting classifier based on perplexity and token length to detect adversarial suffix attacks.
- The paper provides a detailed evaluation of the classifier, including a thorough analysis of its performance and limitations.
- The authors consider various adaptive adversarial suffix scenarios and discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which enhances the reliability and robustness of the findings.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation of the classifier's performance, comparing it with other models and analyzing its limitations, which helps in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the classifier used, making it difficult to understand the specifics of how it operates.
- There is a lack of comparison with other classifiers, which could help in understanding the relative performance of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed evaluation of the classifier's performance against adaptive adversarial suffix attacks, which are a significant threat in real-world scenarios.
- The methodology relies heavily on perplexity as a measure of language fluency, which may not be effective against adaptive adversarial attacks designed to manipulate perplexity scores.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive evaluation of the classifier's performance on a larger, more diverse set of prompts, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not address the potential for false negatives in the classifier's output, which could lead to incorrect classifications of adversarial prompts as non-adversarial.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the classifier used, including its architecture and training parameters?
- How does the classifier perform against adaptive adversarial suffix attacks, and what measures can be taken to improve its effectiveness against such attacks?
- Could you elaborate on the methodology used to compute perplexity, particularly how it is used to compute the classifier's output?
- How does the classifier handle false negatives, and what steps are being taken to minimize their occurrence?
- Is there a possibility to include more detailed comparisons with other classifiers to better understand the relative performance of the proposed method?
- How does the classifier's performance vary when applied to different types of adversarial attacks, such as those that manipulate perplexity scores or those that use different attack strategies?
