 **Summary:**
The paper introduces OpenNeRF, a method for open-set 3D scene segmentation using NeRF-based representations. It leverages the ability of NeRFs to render novel views and extract open-set features from unseen areas of the scene, improving segmentation performance. The method involves using pixel-wise features from OpenSeg to train NeRFs, which are then used to query arbitrary concepts. The paper claims that OpenNeRF outperforms existing methods on the Replica dataset, although the experimental validation is limited to this dataset. The approach is also compared with other methods like OpenScene and LERF, highlighting its potential for real-time applications and open-set scene understanding.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-understand figures and text.
- The proposed method, OpenNeRF, is novel and simple, making it easy to understand and implement.
- The method achieves impressive results on the Replica dataset, showing significant improvements over existing methods like OpenScene and LERF.
- The paper is the first to apply NeRF-based representations for open-set 3D scene segmentation, which is a promising direction for real-time applications.
- The use of novel camera poses for rendering novel views and the integration of pixel-wise features from OpenSeg are innovative approaches that enhance the method's effectiveness.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the impact of depth supervision and novel views, contributing to a better understanding of the method's components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's advantages and limitations.
- The method's reliance on NeRFs for scene representation might limit its applicability to more complex scenes, as NeRFs are known to struggle with high-frequency details and occlusions.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the method, which is crucial for real-time applications.
- The method's performance on long-tail classes is not satisfactory, and it does not handle occlusions well, which are significant challenges in open-set 3D scene segmentation.
- The paper's claims of novelty are questionable as similar methods using NeRFs for 3D scene segmentation have been previously explored.
- The paper's experimental validation is limited to the Replica dataset, which might not generalize well to other datasets or real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform on other datasets like S3DIS, ScanNet, or Matterport, which are commonly used in 3D scene segmentation research?
- Can the method be applied to more complex scenes with high-frequency details and occlusions? How does it handle these challenges?
- How does the method perform on long-tail classes, which are crucial for open-set scene segmentation?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the method, especially in real-time applications?
- How does the method handle occlusions in the scene, and what are the implications for the accuracy of the segmentation results?
- In the methodology section, could the authors clarify how the open-set features are projected onto the 3D point cloud and how this affects the segmentation results?
- Is there a possibility to integrate the method with other NeRF-based methods like NeRF-based feature fusion fields or Neural Feature Fusion Fields to enhance its capabilities?
