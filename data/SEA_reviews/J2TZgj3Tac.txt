 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new algorithm called anytime double oracle (ADO) and its extensive-form approximation, anytime PSRO (APSRO), to address the issue of increasing exploitability in PSRO. ADO ensures that exploitability remains constant throughout iterations, while APSRO uses a no-regret algorithm to update the opponent's best response. The paper also proposes a Self-Play PSRO (SP-PSRO) algorithm, which incorporates a stochastic policy into the population each iteration to reduce exploitability. Empirical results show that SP-PSRO converges faster than APSRO and PSRO in various games like Liar's Dice, Battleship, and Leduc Poker. However, the paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing algorithms and does not provide theoretical guarantees for SP-PSRO.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant issue in PSRO, specifically the increasing exploitability over iterations, which is a critical concern in the field.
- The proposed algorithm, SP-PSRO, shows promising results in reducing exploitability, as demonstrated through empirical results.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the authors provide clear explanations of their algorithms and theoretical results.
- The authors have provided code and detailed experimental setups, enhancing the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper is original in its approach to addressing the issue of increasing exploitability in PSRO, and the proposed algorithm could be a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing algorithms like XDO, NXDO, and PSRO, which could help in understanding the relative performance of the proposed algorithm.
- The paper does not provide theoretical guarantees for SP-PSRO, which could limit the understanding of its reliability and effectiveness.
- The paper's experimental results are not comprehensive enough, particularly in terms of the number of iterations considered, which could affect the validity of the conclusions drawn.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and the scalability of the proposed algorithms, which are crucial for practical implementation in large-scale games.
- The paper's theoretical contributions, particularly in Proposition 4, are not well-explained, and the assumptions made are not clearly stated, which could limit the understanding of the results.
