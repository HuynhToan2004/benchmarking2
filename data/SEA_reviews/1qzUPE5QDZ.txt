 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Group-aware Shift Explanations (GSE) to address the issue of group irregularities in shift explanations, focusing on maintaining group structures while improving the feasibility and robustness of explanations. The methodology leverages worst-group optimization to minimize disruptions to groups and optimize overall fitness. The paper demonstrates that GSE can enhance the quality of shift explanations across various datasets, including tabular, language, and image data. The proposed method is evaluated against existing techniques and shows significant improvements in metrics such as worst-group PE and feasibility. However, the paper has been critiqued for its reliance on specific group definitions and its generalizability across different types of distribution shifts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of explaining distribution shift, focusing on the preservation of group structures which is crucial for maintaining the integrity of the data.
- The proposed Group-aware Shift Explanations (GSE) method is innovative and well-articulated, providing a novel approach to handling group irregularities in shift explanations.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results are robust and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the GSE method across various datasets and metrics.
- The inclusion of theoretical analysis and empirical evidence supports the claims made in the paper, enhancing its credibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential drawbacks of the GSE method, particularly in scenarios where group structures may not be clearly defined or where the method's generalizability is questionable.
- The methodology section could be expanded to include more detailed explanations of the group-aware optimization process and the rationale behind the choices made in the experimental setup.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the definition of groups in the experiments and how these groups are selected, which could impact the applicability and relevance of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the metrics used to evaluate the effectiveness of the explanations.
- The paper's reliance on specific group definitions might limit its applicability in scenarios where such definitions are not available or not practical.

**Questions:**
- How does the GSE method perform in scenarios where the group structures are not clearly defined or where the number of groups is unknown?
- Could you provide more details on how the groups are defined and selected in the experiments, and how this affects the results?
- How does the GSE method compare to other methods that do not optimize for group-awareness, particularly in terms of the metrics used to evaluate the effectiveness of the explanations?
- Could you clarify the methodology behind the GSE method, particularly the group-aware optimization process and the choice of metrics used?
- How does the GSE method handle the issue of group irregularities when the number of groups is large, and what are the implications of this for the practical applicability of the method?
