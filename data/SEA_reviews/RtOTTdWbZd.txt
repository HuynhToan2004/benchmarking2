 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Advantage-Induced Policy Alignment (APA), a novel algorithm designed to address the challenges of stability and sample efficiency in Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) for Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). APA leverages a squared error loss function based on estimated advantages, aiming to improve upon the instability and poor sample efficiency observed in PPO. The paper provides theoretical and empirical evidence, including experiments on various datasets, to support the efficacy of APA. Despite its theoretical and empirical contributions, the paper faces criticism regarding its experimental setup, specifically the lack of comparison with more recent RLHF algorithms and the use of outdated baseline models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- It introduces a novel algorithm, APA, which addresses the limitations of existing methods like PPO and AWR, specifically focusing on stability and sample efficiency.
- Theoretical justification is provided for the design of the APA loss function, enhancing the credibility of the proposed method.
- Empirical results demonstrate that APA outperforms PPO and AWR on multiple datasets, showing practical effectiveness.
- The paper discusses the theoretical connections between APA and other algorithms like PPO and AWR, offering insights into their differences and similarities.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other RLHF algorithms such as GRUE, CQL, IRL, and RL-based offline LLM training, which limits the understanding of APA's relative performance.
- The experimental setup is limited to smaller models like LLaMA-7B, which might not generalize well to larger models like Llama-2, Llama-65, and Llama-13B.
- The paper does not include a detailed comparison of computational costs, which could be crucial for understanding the practical feasibility of APA in different settings.
- The experimental results are not comprehensive enough, particularly in terms of the range of datasets used and the variety of models tested.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of why APA is more stable than PPO, which could be a critical factor in its adoption.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify why APA is more stable than PPO, especially in terms of its theoretical underpinnings?
- Why was the decision made to use a squared error loss function in APA, and how does it compare to other loss functions in terms of stability and effectiveness?
- How does APA perform on larger models like Llama-2, Llama-65, and Llama-13B, and what are the computational costs associated with training these models using APA?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and models, and how these choices affect the generalizability of the results?
- What are the specific computational costs associated with training models using APA, and how do these costs compare to other RLHF methods?
- Could you discuss the limitations of APA and provide insights into when it might not be the best choice for RLHF tasks?
