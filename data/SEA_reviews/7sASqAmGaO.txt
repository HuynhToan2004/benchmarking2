 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for continual self-supervised learning (CSSL) called Augmented Negatives (AugNeg), which addresses the issue of limited negative sample diversity in contrastive learning. AugNeg utilizes an InfoNCE-like contrastive loss, incorporating negative samples from both current and previous models to enhance representation learning. The methodology is validated through experiments on various datasets, showing improvements over existing methods like CaSSLe. The paper also discusses the challenges in CSSL and proposes modifications to existing loss functions to enhance the diversity of negative samples. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the complexity of the method, and the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method and its applications.
- The motivation behind the research is well-articulated, and the paper effectively addresses the limitations of prior works in continual self-supervised learning (CSSL).
- The proposed method, AugNeg, is novel and effectively integrates with various self-supervised learning methods, showing improvements in experimental results.
- The paper is thorough in its evaluation, including comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The methodology is well-explained, with detailed discussions on the design choices and the rationale behind them.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the experimental setup, particularly in how the datasets are divided into tasks and the implications of this setup on the results.
- There is a lack of detailed comparison with existing methods, especially in terms of computational cost and the diversity of negative samples.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of overfitting in CSSL, which is a critical concern in the field.
- The method's complexity and the need for additional hyperparameters (e.g., queue size) make it less practical compared to simpler methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's claims about the novelty of the approach are questionable, as similar methods have been previously proposed.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly how the datasets are divided into tasks and the implications of this setup on the results?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational cost and memory requirements to existing methods?
- Can the authors provide more details on the diversity of negative samples used in the experiments and how this diversity affects the performance of the model?
- How does the method address the issue of overfitting in CSSL, and what measures were taken to prevent this?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a direct comparison of the diversity of negative samples used in each method?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex CSSL scenarios, such as those involving larger datasets or more tasks?
