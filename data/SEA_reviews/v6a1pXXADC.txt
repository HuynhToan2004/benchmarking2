 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Adversarial In-Context Learning (adv-ICL), which optimizes prompts for in-context learning using a generator, discriminator, and prompt modifier. The generator and discriminator are trained as a GAN, with the prompt modifier updating the prompts based on adversarial loss. The method is tested on various tasks including summarization, arithmetic reasoning, machine translation, and data-to-text generation, demonstrating improvements over state-of-the-art prompt optimization techniques. The method's effectiveness is shown through extensive experiments, although concerns about its novelty and the clarity of its theoretical underpinnings persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise descriptions of the proposed method, Algorithm 1, and the experimental setup.
- The idea of using a discriminator to enhance the optimization of prompts is novel and innovative, particularly in the context of in-context learning.
- The paper conducts comprehensive experiments across a variety of tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving performance.
- The method is computationally efficient and does not require updating model parameters, making it a practical solution for optimizing prompts in large language models.
- The theoretical analysis provided supports the proposed method, and the paper includes a detailed appendix with additional experiments and ablation studies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of why the proposed method, adv-ICL, is effective and how it compares to other prompt optimization methods.
- The theoretical analysis seems disconnected from the practical implementation, and the assumptions made in the theoretical section are not clearly stated or justified.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears similar to existing methods like GANs, with the main difference being the application to prompt optimization.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the convergence of the GAN-like training process, which is crucial for ensuring the effectiveness of the method.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more recent baselines and comparing the proposed method with different LLMs to demonstrate its generalizability.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the hyperparameters used in the experiments, and the impact of these hyperparameters on the performance is not thoroughly explored.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations on why the proposed method is effective and how it differs from existing prompt optimization methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent prompt optimization methods, especially those that do not require GAN-like training?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and provide a clearer connection between the theoretical framework and the practical implementation?
- How does the method handle the convergence of the GAN-like training process, and what measures are in place to ensure that the training does not get stuck in local minima?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the convergence of the training process and the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to include more than two LLMs, and how might this affect the performance or the computational efficiency?
