 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the issue of underperforming categories in the CLIP model, focusing on improving the accuracy of zero-shot predictions, particularly in challenging classes. It introduces the Class-wise Matching Margin (CMM) to quantify the degree of confusion between categories and proposes a CMM-based Prompt Ensemble (CPE) method to enhance the worst-performing categories by generating more detailed class descriptions using large language models. The proposed methodology aims to improve the performance of CLIP by identifying and addressing the specific issues in these categories, showing some promising results across various datasets. However, the paper's experimental results are not consistently superior to existing methods, and the novelty of the approach is questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of zero-shot predictions, focusing on the underperforming classes which have been overlooked in previous studies.
- The introduction of the Class-wise Matching Margin (CMM) and the CMM-based Prompt Ensemble (CPE) method is innovative and provides a novel approach to enhancing the performance of CLIP models.
- The methodology is well-explained, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- The experiments are extensive, covering a variety of datasets, and the results demonstrate that the proposed method can effectively improve the performance of CLIP in handling underperforming categories.
- The use of pseudo labels and the combination of CMM and prompt ensembling is a promising approach, showing potential in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of overall performance and the specific improvements in underperforming classes.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as similar approaches have been explored in previous works.
- The experimental results are not consistently superior to existing methods, and the improvements are sometimes marginal.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for overfitting or the impact of different prompts on overall performance.
- The use of large language models for generating descriptions might introduce biases or errors, which could affect the performance of the CLIP model.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational overhead or the scalability of the proposed method, which could limit its practical application.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of overall performance and the specific improvements in underperforming classes?
- How does the proposed method compare to other approaches that use large language models for generating class descriptions or other types of prompts?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method, such as the risk of overfitting or the impact of different prompts on overall performance?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of pseudo labels, and what are the implications of using these labels in the calculation of CMM?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational overhead and the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its practical application?
