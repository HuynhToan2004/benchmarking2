 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of fine-tuned large language models (LLMs) for generating stable inorganic materials, leveraging the flexibility of LLMs to handle various tasks such as unconditional generation, text-conditional generation, and infilling. The authors demonstrate that these models can effectively generate materials with desirable properties, although the methodology primarily involves fine-tuning existing models rather than introducing novel architectures. The paper also discusses the potential of using LLMs for material discovery, showing that fine-tuned models can generate materials with higher rates of stability and diversity compared to baseline models. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty and the lack of comparison with more recent methods, which could provide a clearer picture of the model's performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The application of large language models (LLMs) to generate stable materials is innovative and could potentially accelerate material discovery, especially in generating novel materials.
- The methodology of fine-tuning LLMs for material generation is simple and effective, as demonstrated by the experimental results.
- The paper introduces a new metric for evaluating symmetry in LLMs, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including a comparison with existing methods and a detailed discussion on the limitations of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation from prior works, particularly in terms of methodological innovation. The use of LLMs for material generation is not a new concept, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing methods.
- The experimental results are limited, with insufficient comparisons to more recent and relevant methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as the need for extensive computational resources and the potential for hallucination in generated materials.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the methodology compares to other approaches like CDVAE, especially in terms of the efficiency and effectiveness of the generated materials.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the method, particularly in handling larger datasets or more complex materials.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the LLMs were fine-tuned for material generation, especially in terms of the training data and the specific modifications made to the models?
- How does the proposed method compare to other approaches that use LLMs for material generation, such as those mentioned in the related work section?
- Can you clarify the use of the IPT metric and its relevance to the evaluation of the generated materials?
- How does the method handle the generation of materials with specific properties, such as a desired space group or energy above hull?
- Could you discuss the potential of the method in generating materials with more complex properties or in larger datasets?
- How does the method ensure the validity and accuracy of the generated materials, especially in terms of their stability and compatibility with existing materials?
