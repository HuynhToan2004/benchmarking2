 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel algorithm called RISC (Reset-Free RL with Intelligently Switching Controller) aimed at enhancing reset-free reinforcement learning (RL) by intelligently switching between forward and backward agents based on confidence in achieving current goals. The algorithm employs a success critic to assess the agent's competency and decides when to switch, ensuring that the agent spends more time exploring unlearned areas. RISC's performance is validated through experiments on the EARL benchmark, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper is criticized for its incremental novelty, lack of comparison with some relevant baselines, and potential issues with scalability and computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to reset-free RL by intelligently switching between forward and backward agents based on confidence in achieving current goals, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, making the paper accessible and understandable, with clear explanations of the algorithm and its components.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear structure and logical flow, enhancing its readability and comprehension.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in certain environments.
- The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in RL, focusing on reset-free RL which is crucial for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is questioned, as the idea of using a success critic to determine when to switch between forward and backward agents is considered incremental and similar to existing methods.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with some relevant baselines, such as MEDAL and VapRL, which could provide a clearer picture of the method's relative performance.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, which could be critical in real-world applications.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently robust or comprehensive, with concerns about the choice of environments, the randomness in results, and the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of safety in reset-free RL, which is a significant concern in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of their approach and how it differs from existing methods, particularly in terms of the use of a success critic?
- How does the proposed method compare to MEDAL and VapRL, especially in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency and scalability of their method, especially in more complex or larger environments?
- How does the method handle safety concerns in reset-free RL, particularly in environments where irreversible states could lead to agent failure?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of environments for their experiments and the rationale behind the experimental setup?
- How does the proposed method handle the randomness observed in the results, and what measures were taken to ensure the reliability and reproducibility of the findings?
