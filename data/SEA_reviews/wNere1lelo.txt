 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, "erase-and-check," aimed at defending against adversarial prompts in large language models (LLMs) by erasing tokens and evaluating the remaining subsequences against a safety filter. This method offers certified safety guarantees, ensuring that harmful prompts are not misclassified as safe under an adversarial attack up to a certain size. The approach is evaluated against various attack modes, including adversarial suffix, insertion, and infusion, and demonstrates strong certified safety guarantees while maintaining good empirical performance on safe prompts. The method's effectiveness is shown through experiments on the Llama 2 model, and it is compared favorably with existing certified robustness techniques such as randomized smoothing.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant and timely issue of defending against adversarial prompts in large language models (LLMs), which is crucial for ensuring safety and trustworthiness in AI systems.
- The "erase-and-check" method proposed is novel and effective, providing a theoretical guarantee on the safety of LLMs against adversarial prompts.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The method is evaluated against various attack modes and demonstrates strong certified safety guarantees, showing a good balance between safety and performance.
- The paper includes a detailed description of the proposed method, including a theoretical guarantee, which is supported by empirical results.
- The paper compares the proposed method with existing certified robustness techniques such as randomized smoothing, showing that the proposed method can outperform these techniques in certain scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities and potential shortcomings.
- The method's effectiveness is primarily evaluated using the Llama 2 model, which might not be representative of all LLMs, especially those with different architectures or training methodologies.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational cost of the method, which could be a significant concern for practical deployment.
- The paper does not explore the robustness of the method against different types of adversarial attacks, which could be crucial for validating its effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods for detecting adversarial prompts, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of its performance.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods for detecting adversarial prompts, and what are the specific advantages of the "erase-and-check" method over these approaches?
- Can the "erase-and-check" method be adapted to handle adversarial attacks that modify the existing tokens in a prompt, and if so, how would its effectiveness be impacted?
- How does the method perform when the "is-harmful" classifier is replaced with a different LLM or a different type of model, such as a vision-language model or a generative model?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the "erase-and-check" method, and how can these be mitigated?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the method, including the time and resource requirements for different types of prompts and attack scenarios?
