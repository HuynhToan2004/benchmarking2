 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Organ-DETR, a novel method for 3D organ detection in medical imaging using a transformer-based architecture. This method integrates two modules: MultiScale Attention (MSA) and Dense Query Matching (DQM), which enhance the detection capabilities by capturing long-range dependencies and improving query matching efficiency. The MSA module utilizes a hierarchical self-attention mechanism to learn from various scales, while the DQM module employs a one-to-many matching strategy to optimize the assignment of queries to ground truths. Extensive experiments across multiple datasets demonstrate the method's superiority over existing methods, highlighting its potential for clinical applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear descriptions of the proposed method and its implementation.
- The introduction of the multi-scale attention mechanism and the dense query matching strategy is innovative and addresses the challenges of long-range dependencies in 3D data.
- The method shows superior performance over existing methods, with a significant improvement in metrics such as mAP, mAR, and AP75.
- The paper includes detailed ablation studies and analysis, which help in understanding the contributions of each component.
- The method's application to 3D organ detection in medical imaging is timely and relevant, with potential for significant impact in clinical settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed implementation details, particularly in the description of the MSA module and the DQM module.
- The comparison with other methods is limited, and the paper does not sufficiently discuss the advantages of the proposed method over existing methods like DN, CDN, and Hybrid Matching.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive visual results, especially in the form of 3D visualizations, to better demonstrate the method's capabilities.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed experimental results, such as computational cost and training/inference time.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the implementation of the MSA module, particularly how the self-attention and cross-attention are computed?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of medical imaging, such as MRI or PET scans?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the segmentation loss in the overall training process and its impact on the method's performance?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods like DN, CDN, and Hybrid Matching, and how does it address the limitations of these methods?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, such as computational cost and training/inference time, to better understand the method's efficiency?
