 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SocioDojo, a lifelong learning environment designed to develop autonomous agents capable of analyzing societal topics such as economics, finance, politics, and culture. SocioDojo incorporates real-world data from news, social media, and reports, along with a knowledge base from books, journals, and encyclopedias. The environment includes a hyperportfolio task, which evaluates agents through investment decisions over time, and proposes an Analyst-Assistant-Actuator architecture and Hypothesis & Proof prompting for producing in-depth analyses. The paper presents an open-ended lifelong learning framework, showcasing a novel approach to societal analysis and decision-making, with a focus on societal topics and a hyperportfolio task that evaluates the analytical and decision-making capabilities of agents.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces SocioDojo, a large-scale and easily scalable environment for developing analytical and decision-making agents, which is well-structured and clearly presented.
- The proposed Analyst-Assistant-Actuator architecture and Hypothesis-Proof prompting are innovative and contribute to the field of lifelong learning, particularly in the context of societal analysis and decision-making.
- The paper performs experiments and ablation studies to explore the factors that impact performance, which provides a robust evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The framework is well-documented, making it easy for readers to understand and replicate the work, and the data is open-sourced, which is a significant contribution to the research community.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, and the experiments are thorough, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the Analyst-Assistant-Actuator architecture, which could be improved by including a figure or a more detailed description.
- The experiment section is limited, and the evaluation is not comprehensive, with only a few baseline comparisons.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential applications of the proposed framework.
- The use of the term "lifelong learning" might be misleading as the agents are not trained on data from the environment but rather use pre-trained models.
- The paper could provide more details on the design choices, such as the selection of time series and the decision-making process for the hyperportfolio task.
- There is a need for a more detailed explanation of the Hypothesis & Proof prompting and its impact on the performance of the agents.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the Analyst-Assistant-Actuator architecture and the Hypothesis & Proof prompting?
- How do the authors justify the choice of using GPT-4 for the experiments, and what would be the implications if different models were used?
- Could the authors provide more details on the selection of time series and the decision-making process for the hyperportfolio task?
- How does the framework handle the potential for speculation in the investment decisions, and what measures are in place to mitigate this risk?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the knowledge base in the decision-making process, especially in terms of its integration with the time series data?
- How does the framework handle the potential for overfitting to the training data, and what steps are taken to ensure the generalizability of the agents' decision-making capabilities?
