 **Summary:**
The paper introduces EntProp, a method aimed at enhancing the robustness of deep neural networks (DNNs) by treating high-entropy samples as out-of-distribution (OOD) data and utilizing auxiliary batch normalization layers (ABNs) for training. EntProp leverages data augmentation and free adversarial training to increase entropy and distance from the in-distribution domain. The method is evaluated on CIFAR-100 and CUB-200-2011 datasets, showing improved standard accuracy and robustness against OOD data compared to baseline methods. However, concerns are raised about the method's generalizability to larger datasets and its theoretical underpinnings, particularly the distinction between high-entropy samples and OOD data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and presents a clear and coherent story.
- The proposed method is intuitive and simple, making it easy to understand and implement.
- The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in improving standard accuracy and robustness.
- The paper provides a novel approach to disentangled learning via auxiliary batch normalization layers (ABNs), which is a promising direction for future research.
- The methodology is supported by a strong theoretical foundation, which enhances the credibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the number of parameters and computational costs.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the selection of hyperparameters, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The method's effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated across different datasets, especially those with larger domains such as ImageNet.
- There is a lack of clarity on the method's theoretical underpinnings, particularly the distinction between high-entropy samples and out-of-distribution (OOD) data.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting in adversarial training, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the training costs, which could be crucial for understanding the practical feasibility of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational cost and parameter count with existing methods, especially when applied to larger datasets like ImageNet?
- Can the authors provide more details on the selection of hyperparameters and their impact on the results?
- How does the method perform when applied to different datasets, especially those with larger domains?
- Could the authors clarify the theoretical distinction between high-entropy samples and OOD data, and provide a more rigorous definition of these concepts?
- How does the method address the issue of overfitting in adversarial training, and what steps are taken to prevent this?
- What are the specific training costs associated with the proposed method, and how do these costs compare to those of other methods in terms of time and computational resources?
