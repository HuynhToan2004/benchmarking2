 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Advantage-Leftover Lunch RL (A-LoL), a novel offline policy gradient algorithm designed for training language models (LLMs) using pre-existing data. A-LoL operates under the assumption that the entire output sequence is a single action, enabling the calculation of training data advantages and filtering out unfavorable instances. This approach is shown to be effective across multiple language generation tasks, demonstrating higher diversity and better performance in terms of safety and helpfulness compared to baseline models. The paper also explores variants of A-LoL and compares its performance with other RLHF baselines and reward-based offline RL methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the A-LoL algorithm and its variants.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering multiple tasks and demonstrating the effectiveness of A-LoL in terms of performance, stability, and diversity.
- The method is novel, with a clear motivation for its development, and is shown to be effective in a variety of language generation tasks.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with other offline RL algorithms, highlighting the advantages of A-LoL.
- The use of importance sampling and the calculation of advantages based on the reference policy's value estimate is a novel approach.
- The paper includes a thorough discussion of the method's relation to other offline RL algorithms, enhancing the understanding of its position within the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the reward model is trained and its impact on the performance of the A-LoL algorithm.
- The experiments are limited to smaller LLMs (7B and 1.4B), and it's unclear how the method would perform with larger models like 13B or 70B.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost and stability of training the value function network, which could be a significant concern for practical implementation.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in scenarios where the method might not perform optimally or where it could lead to biased or unfair comparisons.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the method.
- The paper's evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate that the method is more stable than other RLHF methods, and there is a need for more rigorous testing to substantiate this claim.

**Questions:**
- How is the reward model trained, and how does its quality affect the performance of the A-LoL algorithm?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process of the value function network, including the computational cost and stability?
- How does the method perform with larger models like 13B or 70B, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in scaling up the method?
- Could the authors elaborate on the method's limitations and potential negative societal impacts?
- How does the method compare to other RLHF methods in terms of stability and performance, particularly in scenarios where the method might not perform optimally?
- Could the authors provide more detailed ablation studies to clarify the impact of different components of the method on its performance?
