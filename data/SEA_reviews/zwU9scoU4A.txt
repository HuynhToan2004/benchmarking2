 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new approach to learning equilibria in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) using a novel concept called Graphex Mean Field Games (GXMFGs). GXMFGs are designed to model sparse graph structures, which are crucial for capturing the dynamics of real-world networks. The authors propose a learning algorithm tailored to this new framework, demonstrating its effectiveness through simulations on both synthetic and real-world networks. The paper extends the Mean Field Game (MFG) theory to handle sparse graphs, providing a theoretical framework for approximating finite-agent systems with infinite-agent ones. However, the paper lacks a detailed discussion on the assumptions and limitations of the proposed method, which are critical for understanding its applicability and practicality.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) using Graphex Mean Field Games (GXMFGs), which is a significant extension of the Mean Field Game (MFG) theory to handle sparse graphs.
- The authors provide a theoretical framework for approximating finite-agent systems with infinite-agent ones, which is a critical contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the theoretical framework and the learning algorithm tailored to the GXMFG setup, which is supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence.
- The application of Graphexes to MARL is innovative and addresses a significant gap in the literature by extending MFGs to handle sparse graphs, a feature that is crucial for modeling real-world networks.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through simulations on both synthetic and real-world networks, showing that the learning algorithm can accurately approximate the mean field game in the limit.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the assumptions and limitations of the proposed method, which are critical for understanding its applicability and practicality.
- There is a lack of clarity on the specific assumptions made about the graphex W, which are crucial for the theoretical results to hold.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other learning algorithms for MFGs or other multi-agent reinforcement learning methods, which could help in understanding the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a clear definition or discussion on the concept of a "graphex," which could confuse readers unfamiliar with this term.
- The paper's experimental results are limited to small-scale networks, which might not fully demonstrate the scalability and effectiveness of the proposed method in more complex scenarios.
- The paper's theoretical results are not sufficiently explained, particularly in terms of their practical implications and the assumptions required for their applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the assumptions and limitations of the proposed method, including the assumptions about the graphex W?
- How does the proposed method compare with other learning algorithms for MFGs or other multi-agent reinforcement learning methods in terms of performance and scalability?
- What are the specific advantages of using GXMFGs over other MFG approaches, especially in terms of modeling sparse graphs and real-world networks?
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of the "graphex" in the context of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of small world properties in graphs, and what are the implications of this for the accuracy of the mean field game approximation?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical results and their practical implications, particularly in terms of the assumptions required for their applicability?
