 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PAIRE, a novel DNN-based method for supervised causal learning, which addresses the limitations of previous methods by focusing on identifiable causal structures rather than the adjacency matrix. PAIRE employs a pairwise encoder module with a unidirectional attention layer to encode essential causal information, such as persistent dependency and orientation asymmetry. This approach allows PAIRE to model both internal and external relationships of variable pairs, thereby improving the learning of causal structures. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing that it outperforms other DNN-based SCL approaches. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in explaining the method's theoretical underpinnings and its experimental results, which are considered insufficiently robust to support the claims made.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.
- The proposed method, PAIRE, is novel and innovative, providing a new approach to causal discovery that is both theoretically sound and practically applicable.
- The paper is original in its approach, focusing on learning identifiable causal structures rather than directly learning the adjacency matrix, which is a significant departure from previous methods.
- The experimental results are robust and demonstrate the effectiveness of PAIRE in outperforming other DNN-based SCL approaches.
- The paper addresses a critical issue in causal learning by focusing on the importance of learning identifiable causal structures, which is a significant advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the method's theoretical underpinnings, particularly the role and effectiveness of the pairwise encoder module and the unidirectional attention layer in capturing essential causal information.
- The paper's experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with insufficient comparisons to other methods and a lack of detailed analysis of the results.
- The paper's claims about the method's ability to capture essential causal information are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the method, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.
- The paper's focus on discrete data types limits its applicability to continuous data, which is a significant limitation in the field of causal learning.
- The paper's reliance on synthetic data for testing may not adequately reflect the method's performance in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the pairwise encoder module and the unidirectional attention layer effectively capture essential causal information?
- How does the method perform when applied to continuous data, and what are the implications of this limitation?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results and comparisons with other methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- How does the method handle the issue of identifiability, and what are the implications of this limitation in practical applications?
- Can the authors clarify the method's theoretical guarantees regarding the asymptotic correctness with respect to the sample size?
- How does the method handle the issue of skeleton prediction, and what are the implications of this limitation in practical applications?
