 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel influence-driven selective annotation method, termed IDEAL, aimed at reducing annotation costs in in-context learning (ICL) by selecting a pivotal subset from a large-scale unlabeled data pool. This method constructs a directed graph to represent unlabeled data, quantifies the influence of candidate subsets using a diffusion process, and iteratively selects data based on maximum marginal gain. Theoretical analysis supports the method's effectiveness, and extensive experiments demonstrate its superiority over existing methods in various benchmarks. The method is shown to be efficient in terms of time consumption and achieves better performance under lower annotation costs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel influence-driven selective annotation method for in-context learning, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to establish an upper limit for the global optimal solution, demonstrating the method's effectiveness.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to validate the method's superiority over existing methods, and the method is shown to be efficient in terms of time consumption.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and can be applied to various tasks, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other selective annotation methods, which could have strengthened the argument for the superiority of the proposed method.
- The method's performance is not consistently superior across all datasets, and there are instances where it performs worse than existing methods.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed method, which could have provided a more balanced view of its strengths and weaknesses.
- The selection of baselines for comparison is not comprehensive, and it would have been beneficial to include more recent and relevant methods in the comparison.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of over-specialization in the selected data, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- There are concerns about the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to large-scale datasets and its computational efficiency.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other selective annotation methods, especially in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the method be adapted to handle out-of-distribution (OOD) data, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in such scenarios?
- How does the method perform when the annotation budget is varied, and what are the implications for the method's effectiveness?
- Could the authors provide more detailed insights into the method's limitations and potential areas for improvement?
- How does the method handle the issue of over-specialization in the selected data, and what steps can be taken to ensure that the method remains effective in diverse data scenarios?
