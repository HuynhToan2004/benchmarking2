 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a differentiable physical simulator for soft robots, focusing on locomotion tasks and multiple-task learning. The framework integrates a high-performance simulator supporting mass-spring systems and material point methods, an automatic differentiation module, and a practical training module. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms traditional reinforcement learning methods in terms of task loss and convergence rate. The system is designed to handle multiple tasks concurrently, such as running, jumping, and crawling, and can be controlled in real-time by users. Despite its technical advancements, the paper is critiqued for its limited scope in terms of the variety of tasks and the depth of evaluation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel differentiable simulator for soft robots, which is the first of its kind to achieve multiple-task learning in a single network controller.
- The methodology is supported by a robust evaluation, including ablation studies and comparisons with reinforcement learning (RL), demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method in terms of task loss and convergence rate.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with comprehensive experiments and ablation studies that validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The system is designed to handle various types of soft robots and different tasks, showing potential for real-world applications.
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the gradient distribution, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of the network during training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on locomotion tasks, which limits the scope of evaluation and the generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the practical applicability of the findings.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a few types of soft robots, and the results are not compared with other state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process, such as the number of training epochs and the specifics of the training environment.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the work, which is a critical aspect of ethical considerations in research.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the design choices, such as the use of differentiable physics for soft robot learning and the selection of tasks.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and generalizability?
- Can the method be extended to more complex tasks beyond locomotion, such as manipulation or object handling?
- What are the specific advantages of using differentiable physics for soft robot learning compared to traditional methods?
- How does the method handle the complexity of soft robot learning, particularly in terms of task switching and real-time control?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, such as the number of training epochs and the specifics of the training environment?
- How does the method ensure the robustness and reliability of the trained soft robots, especially in real-world applications?
