 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TANGO, a novel method that integrates time-reversal symmetry as a soft constraint into GraphODEs for modeling multi-agent dynamical systems. This method aims to improve the prediction accuracy and generalizability of neural simulators by learning system dynamics in the latent space. The theoretical analysis provided explains how the time-reversal symmetry loss helps learn more fine-grained and long-context system dynamics from a numerical perspective. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on various simulated datasets, showing improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, as it appears to be an extension of existing methods like TRS-ODEN, and the empirical evidence provided does not convincingly demonstrate the method's applicability to real-world systems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The motivation behind the research is compelling, focusing on the potential of neural simulators to learn system dynamics in the latent space, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in certain scenarios, such as multi-agent systems.
- The paper provides a novel approach by incorporating time-reversal symmetry as a soft constraint, which is both innovative and well-explained.
- The theoretical analysis provided is comprehensive and helps in understanding the proposed method from a numerical perspective.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as it seems to be an extension of existing methods like TRS-ODEN, with similarities in the loss function and theoretical analysis.
- The empirical evidence provided does not convincingly demonstrate the method's applicability to real-world systems, which is a significant limitation given the paper's claims of generalizability.
- There is a lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in the field, which could have strengthened the paper's argument for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
