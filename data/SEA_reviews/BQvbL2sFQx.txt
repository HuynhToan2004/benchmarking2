 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MASS, a method for achieving shift equivariance in convolutional neural networks (CNNs) by modifying downsampling and upsampling layers. The approach involves selecting a reference point from the feature map to ensure that shifts in the input image do not affect the output. This method is tested on image classification and segmentation tasks, showing improvements over existing methods like APS and LPS. The paper also discusses the integration of MASS with pretrained networks and its compatibility with various CNN architectures. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of comprehensive comparisons with other methods, insufficient experimental validation, and unclear presentation of certain concepts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method, MASS, which enhances shift equivariance in CNNs.
- The proposed method is model-agnostic, making it compatible with various CNN architectures and pretrained networks, allowing for the transfer of pretrained weights to achieve shift-equivariant models.
- The method is validated on both image classification and semantic segmentation tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and applicability across different domains.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to shift equivariance by leveraging a simple hand-crafted selection function, which can be integrated into any downsampling layer in CNNs.
- The method's simplicity and compatibility with existing CNN architectures make it easy to implement and adapt to different applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other methods, particularly those that achieve shift equivariance through different mechanisms, such as data augmentation or network architecture.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, with only a few models tested and limited results reported, which does not convincingly demonstrate the general applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the impact of the proposed method on inference time, which could be a significant concern for real-world applications.
- The method's effectiveness in scenarios where the image is not centered or where there are multiple objects is not clearly demonstrated, raising concerns about its practical applicability.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by providing clearer definitions and explanations of key concepts, such as the term "shift-equivariant" and the mathematical formulation of circular shifts.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods that achieve shift equivariance, such as those based on data augmentation or network architecture?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons with other methods on more diverse datasets and models?
- How does the proposed method affect inference time, and what are the practical implications of this increase in terms of real-world applications?
- Could the authors clarify the mathematical formulation of circular shifts and provide a more detailed explanation of the method's implementation, especially in terms of its practical applicability in different scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other vision tasks, such as object detection or instance segmentation?
