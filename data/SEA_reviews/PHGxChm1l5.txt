 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to enhancing the compositional reasoning abilities of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) by integrating a detection network that can propose regions of interest (ROIs) based on language inputs. This integration allows for a bidirectional flow of information between the language and vision models, which is crucial for improving the model's ability to reason about visual entities and relationships. The approach involves the use of special tokens such as <visual>, <prevvisual>, <box>, and <prebox> to facilitate communication between the models. The model is trained on a large-scale dataset derived from BLIP-2, demonstrating significant improvements in compositional reasoning tasks compared to existing VLMs. However, the paper faces criticism for its lack of novelty in the use of communication tokens and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with other state-of-the-art models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to enhancing the compositional reasoning abilities of VLMs by integrating a detection network that can propose regions of interest (ROIs) based on language inputs.
- The use of special tokens such as <visual>, <prevvisual>, <box>, and <prebox> to facilitate communication between the language and vision models is innovative and well-executed.
- The paper demonstrates significant improvements in compositional reasoning tasks compared to existing VLMs, indicating a substantial advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is straightforward and effective, with a clear description of the model and its training process.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of the use of communication tokens which have been previously introduced in other models like KOSMOS-2.
- There is a notable absence of experimental results on VQA tasks, which are crucial for evaluating the model's performance in more complex visual reasoning scenarios.
- The paper's focus on compositional reasoning is somewhat limited, with insufficient exploration of other types of visual reasoning such as object-attribute and spatial event compositionality.
- The use of CLIP as the image encoder and the lack of comparison with other encoders like BLIP-2 raises questions about the choice of encoders and its impact on the model's performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential directions for future research.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of the use of communication tokens and their impact on performance?
- Why was CLIP chosen as the image encoder over other options like BLIP-2, and how does this choice affect the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method perform on more complex visual reasoning tasks, such as those involving object-attribute and spatial event compositionality?
- Could you elaborate on the decision to use a linear mapping layer to map image embeddings to the same embedding space as the Pythia language embedding space?
- In terms of the experimental results, could you provide more detailed results on VQA tasks and comparisons with other models on these tasks?
