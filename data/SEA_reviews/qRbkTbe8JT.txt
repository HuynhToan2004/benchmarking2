 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called IMEX-Reg for continual learning under low buffer regimes, which employs a two-pronged implicit-explicit regularization approach using contrastive representation learning (CRL) and consistency regularization. This approach guides the classifier towards activation correlations in the unit hypersphere of CRL, aiming to improve generalization performance and mitigate catastrophic forgetting. The paper presents several experimental evaluations and ablation studies to support the method's effectiveness, showing that it outperforms other rehearsal-based approaches and is robust to natural and adversarial corruptions. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, insufficient theoretical justification, and lack of comparisons with recent relevant works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear structure that aids in understanding the content.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The paper provides extensive experimental evaluations and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various continual learning settings.
- The method shows robustness to natural and adversarial corruptions, indicating its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of the method's characteristics, including task recency bias and robustness to adversarial and natural corruptions, which adds depth to the understanding of the method's behavior.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments and comparisons, which help in validating the proposed method's effectiveness against other continual learning methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The theoretical justification for the method's effectiveness is insufficient, and the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the method's computational complexity.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with several recent relevant works, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of its effectiveness.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to replicate the experiments and validate the results independently.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the method, such as the specifics of the classifier regularization and the training procedures, which could hinder reproducibility.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
- How does the method compare with other recent works that use similar techniques, such as those mentioned in the paper?
- Could the authors provide more detailed information on the computational complexity of the method and its training procedures to aid in reproducibility and further research?
- Why was the decision made to use a projection head instead of directly using the representation for classification?
- Could the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the consistency regularization in the method, especially in comparison to other regularization techniques?
- How does the method handle the trade-off between the different regularization terms, particularly in scenarios where one term might dominate over another?
