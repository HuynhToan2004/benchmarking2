 **Summary:**
The paper explores the performance of Transformer-based architectures in medical image segmentation tasks, comparing them to traditional CNNs. It identifies that the large convolutional backbones in these models often dominate performance, with the transformer components playing a secondary role. The paper also discusses the challenges in training transformers on small, sparsely-labeled medical datasets and the limited utility of long-range interactions. It further investigates the impact of dataset size and the necessity of transformers in medical image segmentation, suggesting that they may not be essential in scenarios with sufficient data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely question regarding the utility of Transformers in medical image segmentation, a topic of high relevance given the recent surge in interest in these models.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of existing Transformer architectures, offering valuable insights into their performance and limitations in this specific application.
- The paper includes a thorough evaluation of different Transformer architectures, which could guide future research in this area.
- The discussion on the limitations of transformers in medical image segmentation is insightful and contributes to the ongoing debate about the effectiveness of these models in this domain.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's main conclusion that transformers are less effective in medical image segmentation due to small, sparsely-labeled datasets is not new and has been previously discussed in the literature.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison of different transformer architectures, which could have provided a more nuanced understanding of their performance.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly robust, with some results, such as those on the KiTS dataset, being particularly weak.
- The paper's focus on 3D medical image segmentation might limit its applicability to other medical imaging modalities, such as 2D images.
- The paper's discussion on the necessity of transformers in medical image segmentation is not convincingly argued, and the potential benefits of using transformers in larger datasets are not adequately explored.
- The paper's claims about the inefficiency of transformers in medical image segmentation are not well-supported by the data presented, and the results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of convolutional architectures.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific contributions of the transformer components in their models, especially in terms of feature learning and representation?
- How do the authors address the concerns about the small sample size and sparse labeling in their datasets, and what steps are being taken to ensure the robustness and generalizability of their findings?
- What are the authors' thoughts on the potential benefits of using transformers in medical image segmentation, especially in scenarios with larger, more diverse datasets?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons between different transformer architectures to help clarify the role and effectiveness of transformers in medical image segmentation?
- How do the authors justify the claim that transformers are not essential in medical image segmentation, especially in light of the potential benefits they could offer in terms of feature learning and representation?
