 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DeepGC, a novel deep learning model designed for identifying dynamic phenotypes within temporal Electronic Health Record (EHR) data. DeepGC leverages patient trajectories and outcomes to uncover clinically meaningful phenotypes that evolve over time. It utilizes a generative approach based on a Markovian Dirichlet distribution and Variational Auto-Encoders (VAEs) to model patient phenotypes, providing insights into the evolution of patient phenotypes and health status. The model is evaluated on two datasets, demonstrating its ability to identify distinct and interpretable phenotypes, outperforming existing benchmarks. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues, lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, and does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel generative, clustering, outcome-sensitive end-to-end deep learning model for uncovering dynamic phenotypes within temporal EHR data.
- The proposed DeepGC model effectively identifies distinct and interpretable phenotypes, demonstrating a 3% increase in F1 compared to existing benchmarks.
- The model's ability to yield valuable insights into the future evolution of patients' health status is highlighted.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear structure and logical flow.
- The proposed method is innovative, utilizing a generative approach based on a Markovian Dirichlet distribution and Variational Auto-Encoders (VAEs) to model patient phenotypes, providing insights into the evolution of patient phenotypes and health status.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear description of the proposed method and its application to EHR data.
- The experiments conducted on two datasets show promising results, demonstrating the model's effectiveness in identifying dynamic phenotypes.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the description of the datasets and the model's components.
- There is a lack of detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of model architecture and training details.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the results, which could limit the applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for overfitting due to the complexity of the model.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the model's components, such as the inference step and the use of Dirichlet Markovian approach.
- There are several typographical and grammatical errors throughout the paper, which could detract from its professionalism and clarity.
- The paper does not provide enough details on how the model is trained, including the loss function used and the hyperparameters chosen.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method in detail, which could affect the reader's understanding of its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and operation of the "inference step" in the model?
- How does the model ensure the generalizability of the results, particularly in terms of the datasets used?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the model is trained, including the loss function used and the hyperparameters chosen?
- How does the model handle missing data in EHR, and what strategies are used to address this issue?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for overfitting in the model due to its complexity and the availability of large amounts of data?
- How does the model handle the issue of data imbalance, which is common in EHR data?
- Can the authors provide more details on the datasets used, including their size, characteristics, and the specific features included?
- How does the model ensure the interpretability of the results, particularly in terms of the clinical relevance of the identified phenotypes?
