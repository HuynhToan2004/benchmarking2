 **Summary:**
The paper introduces BEAG, a graph-based method for subgoal exploration in reinforcement learning, which aims to improve sample efficiency by managing both achieved and unattained subgoals on a grid graph. The method uses a breadth-first exploration strategy, adaptively refining the grid to prioritize exploration of unattained subgoals. The approach is evaluated against various baselines and shows superior performance in certain environments, although it does not consistently outperform in all tasks. The paper is commended for its clarity and presentation, but criticized for its limited scope and the lack of rigorous experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The proposed method, BEAG, is simple, intuitive, and effective in managing both achieved and unattained subgoals, which is crucial for efficient exploration in reinforcement learning.
- The paper provides a comprehensive empirical study, showing that BEAG outperforms several baselines in various environments, particularly in scenarios with sparse rewards and long horizons.
- The idea of using a grid-based approach to manage subgoals is innovative and potentially useful for improving exploration in reinforcement learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a rigorous experimental validation, with insufficient baseline comparisons and a lack of statistical analysis.
- The method's performance is not consistently superior across all environments, and it sometimes underperforms compared to baselines, particularly in tasks with dense rewards.
- The paper's scope is limited, focusing only on a few environments and not adequately addressing the generalizability of the method to other tasks or more complex environments.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis, which could help in understanding the method's limitations and potential improvements.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer explanation of the experimental setup, including the choice of environments and the selection of baselines.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the method's performance in environments with dense rewards, where it appears to underperform compared to baselines?
- How does the method handle the selection of subgoals, particularly in environments where the goal space is not uniformly sampled?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle non-uniformly sampled goal spaces, such as those found in image-based tasks?
- How does the method compare to other graph-based exploration methods, especially those that do not use a grid-based approach?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the choice of environments and the selection of baselines, to better understand the method's performance and generalizability?
