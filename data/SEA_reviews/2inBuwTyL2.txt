 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for predicting task-specific object poses for relative placement tasks, leveraging a provably SE(3)-Equivariant system. This system employs a cross-object representation, RelDist, to represent invariant relationships between objects, which are then used to predict relative poses. The methodology involves a pair of SE(3)-Equivariant geometric reasoning layers, including multilateration and SVD, to extract relative pose predictions. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach through experiments in simulated environments and real-world manipulation demonstrations, showing improvements over existing methods in precision and success rates. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited scope to bimodal tasks, lack of comparisons with other methods, and potential issues with the realism and generalizability of the experimental setups.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly presents the methodology, making it accessible and understandable.
- The proposed method is novel, particularly the use of RelDist to represent invariant relationships, and the application of multilateration and SVD in SE(3)-Equivariant geometric reasoning layers.
- The paper demonstrates significant improvements in precision and success rates compared to existing methods, as shown in the experiments.
- The method is theoretically sound, with a provable SE(3)-Equivariant system, which is a crucial aspect for applications in robotics.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the methodology and the results, with detailed ablation studies that help in understanding the contributions of different components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other methods, particularly in terms of the experimental setup and the metrics used to evaluate the results.
- The experiments are limited to bimodal tasks, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method to more complex, multimodal tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of symmetry-breaking, which is crucial for handling symmetric objects or multimodal placement tasks.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and evaluation of the model, such as the number of demonstrations used for training and the specifics of the training procedure.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive real-world experiments to validate the effectiveness of the method in practical scenarios.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed figures and explanations, especially in the appendices where critical information is relegated.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup and the metrics used to evaluate the results, especially in comparison to other methods?
- How does the proposed method handle symmetry-breaking, and what are the implications for handling symmetric objects or multimodal placement tasks?
- What is the training procedure for the model, and how many demonstrations are used for training?
- How does the method perform when the number of points used from object B varies, and what are the implications for the performance of the method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the real-world experiments, particularly the mug-hanging experiment, and clarify the evaluation metrics used?
- How does the method handle different types of objects, especially those with complex shapes or multiple parts?
- Could the authors discuss the potential applications of the method beyond robotics, such as in computer vision or other domains?
