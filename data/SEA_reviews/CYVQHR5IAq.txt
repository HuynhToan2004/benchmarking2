 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DFedMDC and DFedSMDC, two decentralized personalized federated learning algorithms designed for non-IID data. These methods employ a novel approach by decoupling shared and personal parameters, with the shared parameters updated in a peer-to-peer manner and the personal parameters updated locally. The theoretical analysis provided includes convergence guarantees under non-convex settings, and the empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of these methods compared to existing baselines. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as it heavily borrows from existing methods, and for its experimental setup, which is not sufficiently robust or realistic.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces two new algorithms, DFedMDC and DFedSMDC, which are novel in their approach to personalized federated learning and are supported by comprehensive theoretical analysis and empirical results.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, with clear theoretical results that show convergence under specific conditions.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, and includes detailed theoretical and empirical analysis, which are supported by extensive experiments.
- The convergence analysis provided is thorough, and the empirical results are convincing, showing that the proposed algorithms outperform existing baselines in most cases.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in federated learning by proposing a method that can handle data heterogeneity effectively, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is limited as it heavily borrows from existing methods, particularly in the areas of personalized federated learning and decentralized optimization.
- The experimental setup is not robust or realistic, with issues such as unrealistic data partitions, a lack of comparison with relevant baselines, and insufficient detail on the experimental setup.
- There are concerns about the practical applicability of the method due to the need for a central server to manage the data partition, which contradicts the decentralized nature of the proposed approach.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The convergence analysis and proofs provided are not sufficiently detailed or convincing, and the assumptions made are not clearly justified or explained.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods in terms of computational and communication costs, especially in scenarios with a large number of clients or a large model size?
- Can the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and existing methods, particularly in terms of novelty and practical applicability?
- How does the method handle data heterogeneity in real-world scenarios, and what are the implications of the data partitioning scheme used in the experiments?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the data partitioning process and the specifics of the data used?
- How does the method perform under different network topologies, and what are the implications of these topologies on the convergence and performance of the method?
- Can the authors elaborate on the convergence analysis and proofs provided, particularly in relation to the assumptions made and the implications of these assumptions on the practical applicability of the method?
