 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CLKGE, a method for continual learning in dynamic knowledge graphs (KGs), which addresses the challenges of transferring knowledge from old to new entities and mitigating catastrophic forgetting. It leverages continual learning to transfer knowledge from old to new entities and employs an energy-based model to manage the embedding of new and old entities. Theoretical analysis is provided to ensure convergence, and extensive experiments demonstrate state-of-the-art performance. However, concerns about the novelty of the method, the clarity of the presentation, and the depth of experimental validation are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the critical problem of continual learning in dynamic knowledge graphs, which is essential for maintaining knowledge over time.
- The proposed method, CLKGE, integrates continual learning with dynamic knowledge graph embedding, providing a novel approach to knowledge transfer and retention.
- The theoretical analysis provided guarantees convergence, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- Extensive experiments demonstrate state-of-the-art performance, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, which is crucial for academic audiences.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it combines existing techniques (continual learning and energy-based models) without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the energy-based model and its role in knowledge retention, which could confuse readers.
- There is a significant discrepancy between the performance of CLKGE and other baselines, particularly in the FACT dataset, which requires further explanation.
- The experimental setup is limited, with only one dataset used for all experiments, which may not fully validate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, especially in the context of dynamic knowledge graphs.
- The theoretical analysis, while present, does not sufficiently distinguish between dynamic KGs and continual KGs, which could lead to confusion about the applicability of the results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and operation of the energy-based model in the context of knowledge retention?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of catastrophic forgetting, especially in dynamic knowledge graphs?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental results, particularly the discrepancies observed in the FACT dataset?
- Is there a possibility to expand the experimental setup to include more datasets to validate the generalizability of the findings?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that address similar challenges in dynamic knowledge graphs?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their work and how these might be mitigated?
