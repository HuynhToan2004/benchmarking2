 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for optimizing the imbalance of deep learning models to meet specific application requirements, which is crucial for scenarios where the minority class is more important. The authors define a formal model imbalance state (MIS) and propose a bias adjustment (BA) method to optimize the model's bias, thereby achieving a more balanced model. This method is validated through extensive experiments on datasets like CIFAR-10 and SST-2, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency compared to existing methods. The paper also discusses the limitations of the proposed method, including its reliance on user-specified metrics and potential issues with overfitting in extreme imbalance scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in deep learning, specifically the imbalance between the minority and majority classes, which is crucial for applications where the minority class plays a more important role.
- The proposed method is novel and simple, effectively adjusting the bias of the last layer of the model to optimize the model imbalance state, which is a straightforward and effective approach.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The experiments conducted are extensive, covering a wide range of datasets and scenarios, demonstrating the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method.
- The method is well-motivated, with a clear rationale for its design and implementation, and the results show a significant improvement over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the imbalance ratio is extremely high or the dataset is highly imbalanced.
- The method's reliance on user-specified metrics might limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where such metrics are not available or not easily quantifiable.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for overfitting in extreme imbalance scenarios, which could lead to decreased performance on balanced datasets.
- The comparison with existing methods, such as POT, is not convincing, and the paper does not adequately demonstrate how the proposed method outperforms or improves upon these methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters and a discussion on how the method might perform in scenarios with more complex datasets or different imbalance ratios.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on how the proposed method might be adapted or extended to handle scenarios where the imbalance ratio is extremely high or the dataset is highly imbalanced?
- How does the method perform when compared to other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of accuracy and efficiency?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific neural network architectures used and the training hyperparameters?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the minority class is not the most important class, and what implications does this have for the effectiveness of the method?
- Could you provide more insights into the potential for overfitting in extreme imbalance scenarios and how this might be mitigated?
