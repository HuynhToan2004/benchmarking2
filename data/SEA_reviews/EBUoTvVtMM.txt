 **Summary:**
The paper explores the privacy risks associated with fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) using user data. It introduces a novel threat model, termed "user inference," where an attacker can infer whether a user's data was used for fine-tuning based on a small set of samples and black-box access to the fine-tuned LLM. The paper demonstrates that outlier users and those contributing large quantities of data are particularly vulnerable to these attacks. Various mitigation strategies are proposed and tested, revealing that limiting the number of fine-tuning samples from a single user can reduce attack effectiveness. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for future deployments of LLMs fine-tuned on user data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue of privacy risks associated with fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) on user data.
- The proposed threat model is well-articulated and novel, providing a unique perspective on privacy risks in the context of LLMs.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with clear explanations and detailed experimental setups.
- The experimental results are robust and convincingly demonstrate the vulnerability of LLMs to user inference attacks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of various mitigation strategies, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of different approaches in addressing privacy concerns.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed threat model, which could be a significant drawback for readers looking for a comprehensive understanding of the problem.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the threat model, particularly in terms of its applicability and assumptions, which might not align well with real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the findings, which could limit its appeal to a broader audience.
- The paper's assumptions about the attacker's capabilities, such as access to a reference model, are not clearly justified or explained, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed mitigation strategies, which could be a significant drawback for practitioners looking to implement these strategies in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed threat model compare to existing membership inference attacks, particularly in terms of effectiveness and practical applicability?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the threat model, including its assumptions and implications, to better understand its limitations and applicability?
- How does the paper address the potential negative societal impacts of the findings, and what steps could be taken to mitigate these impacts?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made about the attacker's capabilities, particularly regarding access to a reference model, and discuss how these assumptions affect the validity of the results?
- How do the proposed mitigation strategies compare in terms of effectiveness and practicality, and what are the limitations of each strategy?
