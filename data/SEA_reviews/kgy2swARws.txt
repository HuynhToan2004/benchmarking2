 **Summary:**
The paper introduces S2-DMs, a novel training strategy for diffusion models that aims to address the asymmetry between training and sampling phases, particularly in models like DDIMs. By incorporating a skip-step loss during training, S2-DMs aim to enhance sample quality and reduce the number of training steps needed. The authors claim that this approach improves the sample quality in the few-step sampling regime, showing results on datasets like CIFAR10 and CelebA. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of novelty, unclear presentation, and insufficient empirical evidence to support the claims of reduced training time and sample quality.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important issue in the diffusion model community by focusing on the asymmetry between training and sampling, which is a significant problem in the field.
- The proposed solution, S2-DMs, is straightforward and easy to implement, requiring only a few modifications to existing code.
- The paper provides empirical evidence that S2-DMs can achieve better sample quality than baseline models in the few-step sampling regime, which is a notable improvement.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a paper aimed at a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as the proposed skip-step loss is a simple extension of existing loss functions, which might not be seen as a significant advancement in the field.
- The presentation of the paper is unclear and confusing, particularly in the mathematical formulations and the explanation of the skip-step loss and its integration into the training process.
- The empirical results are not convincing, with inconsistent improvements across different datasets and a lack of quantitative evidence to support claims of reduced training time.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method to larger datasets or more complex models, which limits its applicability and impact.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodology and the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed approach, which could hinder the understanding and adoption of the method by the community.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the mathematical formulations and the integration of the skip-step loss into the training process to make it clearer and more accessible to readers?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and sample quality, especially in scenarios where the number of training steps is significantly reduced?
- Can the authors provide more detailed quantitative evidence to support the claims of reduced training time and improved sample quality?
- Is there a theoretical explanation for why S2-DMs achieve better sample quality in the few-step sampling regime, and how does this compare to the performance of DDIMs in the same regime?
- How does the proposed method scale to larger datasets or more complex models, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in such scenarios?
