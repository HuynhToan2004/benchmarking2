 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CLIP-MUSED, a novel method for multi-subject decoding in functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI) data, leveraging a Transformer-based feature extractor and learnable subject-specific tokens to address individual differences. The approach integrates a Transformer encoder, CLIP-based feature extraction, and representational similarity analysis (RSA) to enhance neural information semantic decoding. The method is tested on two datasets and demonstrates improved performance over existing methods, although concerns are raised about the clarity of the methodological contributions and the experimental setup, particularly the limited dataset sizes and the lack of comparative analysis with other multi-subject decoding methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in the field of neuroscience by proposing a method that can generalize single-subject decoding models to multiple subjects, which is crucial for advancing research in this area.
- The methodology integrates several innovative elements such as a Transformer-based feature extractor, learnable subject-specific tokens, and RSA, which are novel approaches to handling multi-subject decoding.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the method's performance and include an ablation study, which helps in understanding the contributions of different components of the method.
- The paper includes code in the supplementary materials, which is a valuable resource for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the main contributions of the method, particularly the roles and interactions of the Transformer-based feature extractor, learnable subject-specific tokens, and RSA.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented. The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, and the results are not convincingly superior to existing methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the method, such as the potential for overfitting and the scalability of the method to larger datasets.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which detract from its professional presentation.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the main contributions of the method and how they interact with each other in the decoding process?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting, especially when using learnable subject-specific tokens?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the datasets used and the parameters of the baseline methods compared?
- How does the method perform on larger datasets, and what are the computational costs associated with scaling up the method?
- Could you elaborate on the decision to use RSA over other methods for establishing associations between visual stimuli and neural signal representations?
- How do the authors address the potential for overfitting, especially in the context of the learnable subject-specific tokens?
