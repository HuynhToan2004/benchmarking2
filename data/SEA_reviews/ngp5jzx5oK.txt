 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for zero-shot text-to-speech synthesis that encodes speaker-specific latent speech features using an autoencoder trained on a large multi-speaker dataset. This method clusters latent features into a codebook, which is then used to condition the text-to-speech synthesis model. The proposed method is evaluated against state-of-the-art models like VITS and YourTTS, showing significant improvements in speaker similarity and naturalness. The approach is also demonstrated to be effective in generating speech with high-quality in a cross-lingual manner and in synthesizing speech solely from encoded speech features. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the method and the fairness of comparisons, particularly in the zero-shot scenario.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in speech synthesis by proposing a novel method to learn a codebook of latent features that can be used for zero-shot synthesis.
- The method is well-explained and supported by comprehensive evaluations, including subjective and objective tests, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the methodology and results.
- The approach is innovative, particularly in the use of a codebook of latent features for zero-shot synthesis, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is supported by strong experimental results, showing significant improvements over existing methods like VITS and YourTTS.
- The paper is well-structured and includes a detailed explanation of the method, which is crucial for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques such as autoencoders and codebooks, which have been previously used in other contexts.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with more recent and relevant works, such as those using prompt-based zero-shot synthesis and other multi-speaker TTS methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include issues with generalizability, scalability, and computational efficiency.
- There are concerns about the fairness of comparisons, particularly in the zero-shot scenario, where the proposed method may have an unfair advantage due to the use of a larger dataset.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and inference procedures, which could limit the reproducibility and understanding of the method.
- The paper does not include an ablation study, which could help in understanding the contribution of each component of the method to the overall performance.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the differences between this work and the related work mentioned, particularly in terms of the novelty and the specific contributions of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other zero-shot TTS methods, especially those using prompt-based approaches?
- Can you provide more details on the training and inference procedures, including the computational resources and time required for training and inference?
- How does the method handle variations in speaker characteristics, such as different accents or speech styles?
- Could you elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential solutions or areas for future research?
- Is there any plan to release the code and datasets to facilitate reproducibility and further research in this area?
