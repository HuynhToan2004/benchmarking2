 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for estimating precision and recall metrics in generative models, termed ePR, which leverages hubness-aware sampling to reduce computational complexity. This approach aims to address the high computational costs associated with traditional precision and recall metrics by focusing on representative samples, thereby improving efficiency. The authors demonstrate that ePR can closely approximate the original precision and recall metrics with less computational overhead. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the approach, as it builds upon existing methods and does not provide significant new insights or theoretical guarantees. The paper's experimental results, while showing some improvements, are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the method's scalability and applicability to larger datasets are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method is simple and intuitive, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to reducing computational complexity.
- The use of hubness-aware sampling is innovative and effectively reduces redundancy in precision and recall calculations.
- The paper includes comprehensive experimental results, which demonstrate the efficiency and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The reduction in computational complexity, from O(n^2) to O(mn), is significant and practical, especially for large datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it primarily builds upon existing methods, such as the hubness-aware sampling and the precision and recall metrics, without introducing significant new insights or theoretical guarantees.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art metrics like FID and IS, which could help in understanding the relative advantages of the proposed method.
- The method's scalability is questionable, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger datasets and its efficiency in high-dimensional feature spaces.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could include its sensitivity to hyperparameter choices and the potential for overfitting due to hubness-aware sampling.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate superiority over existing methods, and the improvements in computational efficiency are not consistently significant across different datasets and models.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art metrics like FID and IS to better understand the relative advantages of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of accuracy and efficiency when applied to larger datasets or higher resolution images?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the threshold t, and discuss the potential impact of these choices on the method's performance?
- Could the authors elaborate on the method's scalability and discuss potential limitations or challenges in applying the method to high-dimensional feature spaces or datasets with diverse image types?
- In light of the concerns regarding the novelty and the method's theoretical underpinnings, how does the paper address the need for more rigorous theoretical guarantees or additional empirical evidence to support the proposed method's effectiveness?
