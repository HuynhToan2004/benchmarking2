 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an online out-of-distribution (OOD) detection algorithm named SODA, designed to adaptively learn and update the OOD detector based on real-time data streams. This methodology contrasts with traditional offline OOD detection methods by continuously updating the ID classifier and OOD detector, aiming to minimize incorrect OOD predictions. The theoretical framework includes a sub-linear regret bound, and empirical evaluations demonstrate the algorithm's superiority over existing offline methods. The paper also discusses the practical implications of OOD detection in real-world environments, emphasizing the importance of adaptive learning in dynamic and open-context systems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach to online OOD detection, which is crucial for real-world applications where OOD data is constantly changing.
- The proposed algorithm, SODA, is well-motivated, well-explained, and includes a theoretical analysis that is both clear and convincing.
- The empirical evaluation of SODA is comprehensive, demonstrating its effectiveness in various scenarios and outperforming existing offline OOD detection methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear structure that facilitates understanding.
- The theoretical results, such as the sub-linear regret bound, are well-presented and supported by rigorous proofs, which enhance the credibility of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the proposed method can be applied to different OOD scoring functions, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The experimental setup and the choice of datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10 and ImageNet-1k) are somewhat limited and may not fully represent real-world scenarios where OOD data is more diverse and complex.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the proposed method compares to other existing methods, particularly those that also aim to minimize regret in online learning scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical challenges of obtaining accurate OOD labels in real-world scenarios, which could limit the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and resource requirements of the proposed method, especially in large-scale deployments.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method can be adapted to different OOD scoring functions, and discuss the implications of these adaptations on the algorithm's performance?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods that also aim to minimize regret in online learning scenarios?
- In the context of the experimental setup, how were the OOD labels obtained, and what impact does this have on the results?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the proposed method can be scaled to larger, more complex datasets, and discuss the potential challenges and limitations in such deployments?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the OOD data is not immediately available or where the OOD distribution changes over time?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their work, particularly in terms of privacy and security concerns related to OOD data?
