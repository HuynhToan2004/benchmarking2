 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for RNA sequence design based on its tertiary structure, utilizing a hierarchical data-efficient representation learning framework. The framework leverages a large-scale dataset of RNA tertiary structures and employs a message-passing neural network to learn node representations. It also incorporates a secondary structure imposing strategy and contrastive learning at both cluster and sample levels. The methodology is evaluated against several baseline models and demonstrates superior performance in RNA design tasks. The paper is well-written and provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, although it lacks detailed comparisons with existing RNA design methods and lacks experimental validation in wet-lab settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for RNA sequence design based on its tertiary structure, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The hierarchical data-efficient representation learning framework is well-motivated and effectively leverages limited data, which is crucial for RNA design.
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the methodology and its contributions to the field.
- Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, providing a reliable baseline for future RNA design tasks.
- The methodology is supported by a comprehensive evaluation, which is a valuable resource for the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing RNA design methods, which could have provided a clearer context for the proposed method's contributions.
- There is a lack of experimental validation in wet-lab settings, which is crucial for assessing the practical applicability of the proposed method.
- The methodology's reliance on existing RNA structure benchmarks may limit its novelty, as it appears to be a direct application of existing methods to RNA design.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced perspective on its capabilities and potential improvements.
- The evaluation metrics used in the paper, such as recovery rate, are not well-established in the community, which could limit the paper's impact and credibility.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the proposed method compares with existing RNA design methods, especially in terms of novelty and performance?
- How does the method perform when applied to RNA design tasks that require specific functionalities, such as catalytic RNAs?
- Could the authors provide more details on the wet-lab validation of the designed RNAs, including any experimental setups and results?
- How does the method handle the complexity and flexibility of RNA structures, which are known to be more diverse and less predictable than protein structures?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method, including potential biases in the dataset used and the generalizability of the results?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting, given the limited data available for training?
