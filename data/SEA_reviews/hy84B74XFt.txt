 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SlotAug, a method designed to enhance the interpretability and controllability of object-centric learning (OCL) models through the use of image augmentation and innovative loss functions. The proposed method, which includes Auxiliary Identity Manipulation (AIM) and Slot Consistency Loss (SCLoss), aims to improve the sustainability of object representations during training and inference. The paper evaluates SlotAug across various datasets and demonstrates its effectiveness through empirical studies, showcasing its ability to manipulate object properties and enhance the model's interpretability. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty and the lack of a broader dataset evaluation, which may affect its generalizability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the methodology and experiments.
- The proposed method, SlotAug, is innovative and effectively leverages image augmentation to enhance the interpretability and controllability of object-centric learning models.
- Extensive experiments are conducted across various datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in enhancing the interpretability and robustness of object-centric models.
- The paper includes a variety of metrics to evaluate the model's performance, such as mIoU, ARI, and MSE, which provide a comprehensive view of the model's capabilities.
- The method is simple and effective, with a clear presentation of the model architecture and training process, making it accessible and understandable to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities and challenges.
- The novelty of the method is limited, as it primarily builds upon existing techniques like Slot Attention and Spatial Binding, with incremental improvements rather than groundbreaking innovations.
- The evaluation of the method is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on the CLEVR dataset, which may not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness across a broader range of scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss how the method compares to existing works, particularly those that address similar issues in object-centric learning.
- There are concerns about the generalizability of the method to more complex datasets and scenarios, such as those involving multiple objects with similar colors or shapes.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the PropertyEncoder in the proposed method, particularly in terms of its contribution to the overall performance and interpretability of the model?
- How does the method handle scenarios where objects have similar colors or shapes, potentially leading to confusion in object recognition and manipulation?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing works, such as those by Singh et al. (2023), which also address the interpretability and controllability of object-centric models?
- How does the method ensure that the learned object properties are not overly simplified or biased by the training data, potentially limiting its applicability in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples of how the method handles the sustainability of object properties during multiple rounds of manipulation, as discussed in Section 4.2?
