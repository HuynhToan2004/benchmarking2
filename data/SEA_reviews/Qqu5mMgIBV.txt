 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CASTOR, a novel framework designed to learn causal relationships in multivariate time series data across multiple regimes. CASTOR employs the EM algorithm to maximize a score function, which helps in inferring the number of regimes and learning linear or non-linear causal relationships within each regime. The framework is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its superior performance compared to existing methods. The paper provides theoretical guarantees on the identifiability of the causal structure and regime partition, supported by experimental results. However, it faces criticism for its complexity, computational cost, and limited discussion on related works, which could affect its accessibility and applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, CASTOR, for learning causal relationships in multivariate time series data across multiple regimes, which is a significant advancement in the field of causal discovery.
- The use of the EM algorithm to infer regime partitions and infer causal graphs is innovative and well-executed, providing a robust method for handling complex data scenarios.
- Theoretical guarantees are provided, which help in understanding the theoretical underpinnings of the method and its reliability.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and intuitive examples that aid in understanding the complex concepts presented.
- The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness and robustness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is complex and challenging to follow, with dense notations and insufficient explanation of key concepts, which could hinder its accessibility to a broader audience.
- The computational cost of the proposed method is high, which could limit its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on related works, which could help in contextualizing the research within the existing literature and highlighting the unique contributions of the proposed method.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis are strong and not well-justified, which could limit the generalizability and applicability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition of "regime" and its relevance to the problem setting?
- How does the proposed method perform when the number of regimes is unknown?
- Is there a way to validate the correctness of the estimated regime partition, and how does this affect the accuracy of the causal graph estimation?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations and examples for the equations and notations used in the paper, especially those that are crucial for understanding the methodology?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods in terms of computational cost and scalability?
- Could the authors discuss the implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and how they affect the practical applicability of the results?
