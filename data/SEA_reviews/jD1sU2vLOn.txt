 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for learning counterfactually invariant predictors, focusing on a sufficient graphical criterion for counterfactual invariance. It proposes a model-agnostic method using a kernel-based conditional dependence measure to enforce counterfactual invariance across various datasets, including both synthetic and real-world examples. The method is evaluated against baseline models, showing improvements in counterfactual invariance while maintaining predictive performance. The paper also discusses the challenges of learning counterfactually invariant predictors and provides a theoretical analysis of the sufficient graphical criterion for counterfactual invariance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning counterfactually invariant predictors using a model-agnostic method that is applicable to both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The proposed method is theoretically sound, with a clear explanation of the sufficient graphical criterion for counterfactual invariance and the use of a kernel-based conditional dependence measure to enforce counterfactual invariance.
- The paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in enforcing counterfactual invariance across various datasets and comparing it to baseline models.
- The authors have made a significant effort to provide a clear and detailed explanation of the proposed method, including the theoretical analysis and the practical implications of the findings.
- The paper addresses a relevant and important problem, which is the development of counterfactually invariant predictors, and provides a novel approach that could be of interest to the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the causal graph is known, which might not be realistic in many practical scenarios.
- The computational cost of estimating the Hilbert-Schmidt Conditional Independence Criterion (HSCIC) might limit the scalability of the proposed method to very high-dimensional settings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the model.
- The paper could be more accessible to readers without a strong background in causality, as some terms and concepts are not well explained.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the injectivity of a causal mechanism, might be too strong and not practical in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's methodology is similar to existing works, such as Veitch et al. (2021), and the novelty of the proposed method is not clearly articulated.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the causal graph is not known?
- Can the authors provide more details on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the terms and concepts used in the paper, particularly those related to causality, to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the causal graph is misspecified or incomplete?
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the proposed method and existing works, such as Veitch et al. (2021), and discuss the novel contributions of their method?
