 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PreCoT, a novel prompting framework aimed at enhancing the reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) by incorporating a problem representation stage. This stage involves extracting the initial and goal states of a problem, which are then used to guide the solution process. PreCoT is evaluated on a range of benchmarks and demonstrates improvements over traditional CoT methods, showing better robustness against irrelevant context and context sensitivity. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the method's presentation, the lack of a comprehensive ablation study, and the need for more rigorous comparisons with other prompting methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical structure that enhances understanding.
- The motivation behind the work is strong, particularly in highlighting the importance of problem representation in human problem-solving, which is often overlooked in current large language model (LLM) approaches.
- The proposed method, PreCoT, is straightforward and effective, with good experimental results demonstrated across multiple benchmarks.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the improvements brought by the proposed method, including a qualitative analysis of incorrect reasoning steps and an ablation study on problem representation quality.
- The method's potential for improving the reasoning capabilities of LLMs is significant, especially in scenarios where accurate problem representation is crucial for generating correct outputs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study, particularly on the impact of the "Given Information" and "Objective" in the PreCoT prompt, which could help clarify the effectiveness of the problem representation stage.
- The method's novelty is questioned as similar approaches using problem representation have been previously explored, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from these existing methods.
- The paper's experimental setup is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on few-shot and zero-shot settings, which may not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness in more realistic or varied settings.
- The clarity of the method's presentation could be improved, particularly in explaining how the problem representation is extracted and the role of the "Given Information" and "Objective" in the prompt.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant given the potential applications in various domains.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the "Given Information" and "Objective" in the PreCoT prompt, perhaps through an ablation study?
- How does the proposed method compare to other prompting methods that also use problem representation, such as those mentioned in the review?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the problem representation is extracted from the question and how it influences the solution process?
- Is there a possibility to include more realistic or varied experimental settings, such as open-ended reasoning tasks, to better demonstrate the method's effectiveness?
- Could the authors address the potential societal impacts of the proposed method and discuss any ethical considerations related to its implementation?
