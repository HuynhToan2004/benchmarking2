 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to learning causal models from data, focusing on the three layers of Pearl Causal Hierarchy (PCH): associational, interventional, and counterfactual. The authors propose DiscoSCMs, an extension of structural causal models that allows for the inference of counterfactuals across heterogeneous units. This is achieved through the use of DiscoModel, which comprises two sub-networks: AbductionNet and ActionNet. The model is trained using a population-level counterfactual algorithm and is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world data. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity, lack of comprehensive experimental validation, and potential methodological limitations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning causal models from data, focusing on the three layers of Pearl Causal Hierarchy (PCH), which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method is technically sound, well-motivated, and the authors have provided a detailed explanation of the proposed model, making it accessible and understandable.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and illustrations that aid in understanding the proposed method.
- The authors have conducted experiments on both synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating the practical application of their model.
- The methodology extends the capabilities of existing causal modeling frameworks, potentially enhancing our understanding and application of causality in various domains.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks comprehensive experimental validation, particularly in terms of the scalability and performance of the proposed method, especially in complex or large-scale scenarios.
- The clarity of the paper could be improved, particularly in the introduction and methodological sections, to better guide readers through the complex concepts and methodologies.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing methods, which could help in evaluating its advantages or disadvantages over existing approaches.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical guarantees or empirical evidence to support the claims made about the method's ability to handle heterogeneous units effectively.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the scalability and performance of the proposed method in complex or large-scale scenarios?
- How does the method handle heterogeneous units, and what are the theoretical or empirical guarantees that it can effectively model these units?
- Could you clarify the role and impact of the "consistency assumption" in your model, and how it differs from the "consistency rule" mentioned in other works?
- How does the method perform when the causal graph is not known, and what are the implications for the accuracy and reliability of the results?
- Could you elaborate on the assumptions and conditions under which the method is expected to perform optimally, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in applying the method in real-world scenarios?
