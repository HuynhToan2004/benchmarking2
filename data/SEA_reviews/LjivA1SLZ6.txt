 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for episodic memory utilization in cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) called Efficient episodic Memory Utilization (EMU). EMU leverages a trainable encoder-decoder structure to create semantic memory embeddings, which facilitate exploratory memory recall and prevent local convergence. The method also introduces a novel reward structure called episodic incentive, which enhances the TD target in Q-learning and encourages desirable transitions. Theoretical analysis and empirical results on SMAC and GRF demonstrate the effectiveness of EMU, showing improvements over conventional episodic control. The paper also discusses the potential of EMU in reducing learning time and avoiding local optima in complex tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) by proposing a novel method to efficiently utilize episodic memory, which is crucial for improving sample efficiency and avoiding local optima in complex tasks.
- The proposed method, Efficient episodic Memory Utilization (EMU), is well-motivated, well-designed, and effectively addresses the limitations of existing episodic control methods by incorporating a trainable encoder-decoder structure and a novel reward structure called episodic incentive.
- The paper provides theoretical support for the proposed incentive and demonstrates its effectiveness through empirical results on SMAC and GRF, showing improvements over conventional episodic control and state-of-the-art methods.
- The methodology is sound, and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed ideas and their implementation.
- The paper includes ablation studies and qualitative analyses, which validate the propositions made by the authors and provide additional insights into the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, particularly in complex environments like SMAC and GRF, where the method's performance is not consistently superior to existing methods.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational cost and memory requirements of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the number of training steps required to reach a certain performance level.
- There are some typographical errors and inconsistencies in the paper, which need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use similar ideas, such as using an autoencoder to learn a more informative representation of states?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the computational cost and memory requirements of the proposed method, especially in comparison to existing methods?
- How does the proposed method perform in different environments, particularly in environments where it does not show significant improvements over existing methods?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the episodic incentive in the performance of the method, especially in terms of its contribution to the overall learning process?
- How does the method handle the selection of memory data for training, and what criteria are used to determine which data is most beneficial for learning?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the learning dynamics of the method, especially in terms of how the episodic memory and the episodic incentive influence the learning process?
