 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new benchmark, nuPlan, which uses real-world driving logs to create a reactive simulator for evaluating motion planning algorithms. The authors argue that the default nuPlan gym does not accurately reflect real-world human behavior, leading to the development of city-specific gyms that better align with real-world driving patterns. The paper also proposes a new MPC-based planner, City-Driver, which utilizes these city-specific world models to adapt to different driving conditions and achieve state-of-the-art performance on the nuPlan benchmark. The proposed benchmark and planner aim to bridge the gap between simulated and real-world driving behaviors, potentially improving the robustness and applicability of motion planning algorithms in complex urban environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant issue with the nuPlan benchmark, which is the lack of realism in the default gym environment, and proposes a novel solution by introducing city-specific gyms that better reflect real-world driving behaviors.
- The proposed method, City-Driver, is simple yet effective, demonstrating a 1.5% improvement over the state-of-the-art PDM-C on the nuPlan benchmark.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method and its contributions.
- The benchmark proposed is a valuable tool for evaluating motion planning algorithms in real-world driving scenarios, which is crucial for the development of autonomous vehicles.
- The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies and empirical results, which support the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the city-specific gyms are created, particularly the process of optimizing agent behaviors for each city using human accuracy as the objective function.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, especially concerning the generalizability of the findings to different cities and the robustness of the benchmark.
- There is a potential issue with the evaluation metrics used, which might not fully capture the effectiveness of the proposed method in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed benchmark and planner to different cities, which could limit its applicability in diverse urban environments.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed benchmark and planner over existing methods are not convincingly supported by the presented results.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the city-specific gyms are created, including the specifics of the optimization process and the criteria used to define "human accuracy"?
- How does the proposed method perform in different cities beyond those tested, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in applying the benchmark and planner to new urban environments?
- Can you discuss the potential impact of the proposed benchmark and planner on the development of autonomous vehicles, particularly in terms of improving safety and efficiency in real-world scenarios?
- How do the results of the paper compare with existing state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance metrics, and what are the key factors contributing to the observed improvements?
- Given the concerns about the generalizability and scalability of the proposed benchmark and planner, what are the plans for expanding their applicability to a broader range of urban environments and driving scenarios?
