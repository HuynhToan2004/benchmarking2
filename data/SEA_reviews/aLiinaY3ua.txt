 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Saliency Diversified Deep Ensembles (SDDE), a novel approach for enhancing ensemble diversity through the utilization of saliency maps. The method leverages the diversity of saliency maps to train ensembles, aiming to improve out-of-distribution (OOD) detection and classification accuracy. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of SDDE through extensive experiments on various datasets, including CIFAR-10/100 and ImageNet, showing state-of-the-art results in OOD detection and calibration. Despite its novelty and experimental validation, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in some sections and the need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its motivation.
- The idea of using saliency maps to enhance ensemble diversity is novel and well-motivated, providing a new perspective on improving ensemble performance.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments on several benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various scenarios.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) results in OOD detection, calibration, and classification on the OpenOOD benchmark, showing its practical applicability.
- The paper provides a good discussion on the relationship between saliency maps and ensemble diversity, which is both interesting and relevant to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the description of the saliency map diversity loss and the experimental setup, which could confuse readers.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with existing methods, such as those using mutual information or other diversity metrics, to establish the superiority of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive experiments on larger datasets like ImageNet-1K to validate the scalability and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of computing saliency maps, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.
- The paper's claims regarding the relationship between saliency maps and ensemble diversity are not convincingly supported by empirical evidence, particularly in the context of out-of-distribution (OOD) detection.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might impact its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the computational cost of computing saliency maps and discuss potential strategies for reducing this cost in practical applications?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other existing methods that use mutual information or other diversity metrics for enhancing ensemble diversity?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the hyperparameters were tuned and the impact of these choices on the results?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of homogeneous saliency maps in deep ensembles, and what are the implications of this for OOD detection?
- Could the authors provide more empirical evidence to support the claims made about the relationship between saliency maps and ensemble diversity, particularly in the context of OOD detection?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent methods that use similar ideas, such as those discussed in references [1] and [2]?
