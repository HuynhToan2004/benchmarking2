 **Summary:**
The paper explores the challenges of asynchronous inference in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and introduces a novel architecture called Energy GNN, which aims to address these issues by formulating message passing as a convex optimization problem. The authors argue that this approach improves the robustness of GNNs in asynchronous settings, particularly by minimizing a scalar-valued convex function called an "energy" function. The paper presents experimental results showing that Energy GNN outperforms other GNNs in tasks requiring long-range communication and the use of edge features. However, the paper has been criticized for its limited empirical evaluation, lack of comparison with recent GNN architectures, and unclear presentation of the asynchronous inference process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by focusing on asynchronous inference, which is crucial for applications in real-world scenarios.
- The proposed Energy GNN architecture is innovative and offers a novel approach to message passing in GNNs, which is shown to be effective in synthetic datasets.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and a comprehensive review of related work, making it accessible and informative.
- The idea of framing message passing as a convex optimization problem is novel and offers a promising avenue for improving the robustness of GNNs in asynchronous settings.
- The paper is well-motivated and provides a thorough review of the literature, which helps in understanding the context and the contributions of the work.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other recent GNN architectures, which could have provided a better understanding of the advantages and limitations of Energy GNN.
- The empirical evaluation is limited, with only synthetic datasets used, which may not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of Energy GNN in real-world scenarios.
- The presentation of the asynchronous inference process is unclear, which could hinder the understanding of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of asynchronous inference in large graphs, which is a significant limitation given the potential applications of the proposed method in such scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for its practical implementation in large-scale applications.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the convergence properties of the Energy GNN, which is a critical aspect of its theoretical underpinnings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the convergence properties of the Energy GNN, particularly how the convergence is affected by the number of layers and the size of the graph?
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent GNN architectures in terms of performance and scalability?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implementation of the asynchronous inference process, particularly how it could be applied in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of asynchronous inference in large graphs, and what are the potential limitations in such scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly how it performs in terms of computational efficiency and memory requirements as the size of the graph increases?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of asynchronous updates in GNNs, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in practical implementations?
