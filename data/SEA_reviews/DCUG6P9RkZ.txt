 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel algorithm for imitation learning in infinite horizon linear Markov Decision Processes (MDPs), focusing on improving the dependence on the desired accuracy from O(ε^(-5)) to O(ε^(-4)). The authors claim that their algorithm, ILARL, does not require exploration assumptions and enhances the sample complexity. Theoretical results are supported by empirical evaluations, demonstrating the algorithm's superiority in terms of sample efficiency compared to existing methods like PPL and GAIL. However, the paper's assumptions, particularly the finite-state and deterministic expert policy, are considered restrictive and not well-justified. Moreover, the experimental setup is criticized for not being sufficiently robust, and the theoretical claims are seen as potentially overstated.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the theoretical results and the proposed algorithm, which is a significant contribution to the field of imitation learning.
- The authors have provided a detailed comparison with existing works, highlighting the novelty and the improvements over prior research.
- The paper introduces a new algorithm, ILARL, which shows significant theoretical improvements in sample complexity, particularly in the infinite horizon linear MDP setting.
- The empirical evaluation supports the theoretical claims, demonstrating the algorithm's superior performance in terms of sample efficiency.
- The paper is original in its approach, combining ideas from different fields to propose a new algorithm that addresses the challenges in imitation learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The assumptions made in the paper, particularly the finite-state and deterministic expert policy, are considered restrictive and not well-justified.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithm, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental setup is not robust enough, with only one experiment conducted, which limits the ability to draw generalizable conclusions.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the related work, especially in the context of the linear MDP setting.
- There are several typographical errors and unclear statements in the proofs, which could affect the understanding and reproducibility of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the finite-state and deterministic expert policy, and discuss their implications on the generalizability of the results?
- How does the proposed algorithm perform when applied to more complex or realistic settings, such as those involving stochastic expert policies or larger state spaces?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the choice of hyperparameters and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- In the proofs, particularly in Lemma 4, could the authors clarify the notation and the assumptions made regarding the convergence of certain summations?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
