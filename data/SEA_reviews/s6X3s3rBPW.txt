 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel adaptive testing framework for evaluating large language models (LLMs) using Computerized Adaptive Testing (CAT) inspired by Item Response Theory (IRT). This methodology, which dynamically adjusts the difficulty of questions based on the model's performance, allows for a more efficient and accurate estimation of LLMs' abilities, potentially reducing the number of questions required for evaluation. The framework is evaluated against various instruction-tuned LLMs, including ChatGPT, and demonstrates its effectiveness in comparing LLMs with both models and humans. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the method due to the need for human annotation of responses, which could limit its scalability and applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel adaptive testing framework for evaluating large language models (LLMs), which is an innovative approach that leverages computerized adaptive testing (CAT) and Item Response Theory (IRT) to dynamically adjust the difficulty of questions based on the model's performance.
- The methodology is supported by theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence, demonstrating its effectiveness in evaluating LLMs and comparing them with both models and humans.
- The paper provides a detailed explanation of the IRT model and the selection algorithm, which are crucial for understanding the methodology and its implementation.
- The framework offers a more efficient and accurate estimation of LLMs' abilities, requiring fewer questions under the same evaluation accuracy.
- The authors compare ChatGPT with human students of different levels, which provides a fine-grained diagnosis and ranks six instruction-tuned LLMs from three aspects, offering valuable insights into the capabilities of different LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the benefits of using CAT in evaluating LLMs compared to traditional methods, particularly in terms of practicality and scalability.
- The need for human annotation of responses raises concerns about the scalability and practicality of the method, especially in real-world applications where annotation might not be feasible or reliable.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implications of using CAT in evaluating LLMs, such as the availability and quality of annotated datasets, the computational costs, and the scalability of the method across different domains or tasks.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis on the scalability and generalizability of the method, particularly in terms of how it performs with different types or sizes of LLMs and across various domains or tasks.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential biases in the datasets used for calibrating the IRT parameters, which are crucial for the validity of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the practical implications of using CAT in evaluating LLMs, particularly in terms of scalability, computational costs, and the availability and quality of annotated datasets?
- How does the method perform with different types or sizes of LLMs, and what are the potential limitations or biases that could affect the results?
- What are the potential sources of bias in the datasets used for calibrating the IRT parameters, and how do these biases affect the results?
- How does the method compare to other methods in terms of accuracy and efficiency, particularly in scenarios where annotated datasets are not available or reliable?
- Can you discuss the potential ethical implications of using CAT in evaluating LLMs, particularly in terms of data privacy and security?
- How does the method handle the complexity and diversity of LLMs, especially in scenarios where models have different capabilities or biases?
