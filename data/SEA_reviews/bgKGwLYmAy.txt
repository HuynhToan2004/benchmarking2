 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DGTAT, a novel graph learning model that decouples local and global interactions, separates learnable positional, attribute, and structural encodings, and computes triple attention to capture long-range dependencies. This model aims to address the challenges of over-squeezing and over-smoothing in graph transformers by leveraging the inductive bias of graph topology. The paper provides extensive experiments across various node classification tasks, demonstrating the model's effectiveness in capturing long-range dependencies and maintaining the inductive bias of graph topology. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty and clarity of the paper, with some reviewers questioning the originality of the approach and the lack of detailed theoretical analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive overview of related works, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed model, DGTAT, effectively decouples local and global interactions, separates learnable positional, attribute, and structural encodings, and computes triple attention, which is a novel approach in graph learning.
- Extensive experiments were conducted across various node classification tasks, demonstrating the model's effectiveness in capturing long-range dependencies and maintaining the inductive bias of graph topology.
- The model shows robust performance across graphs of varying sizes, excelling on both large and small datasets, indicating its scalability and applicability.
- The paper provides an ablation study that shows the importance of each decoupled design factor within the model, which enhances the interpretability and flexibility of the model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed theoretical analysis, which could help in understanding the model's performance and limitations more deeply.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as similar concepts have been previously explored in other models like Graphemer, GraphGPS, and NAGphormer.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the decoupling of positional and structural information improves the model's performance, and the benefits of this decoupling are not convincingly demonstrated.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed model, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing models, particularly in terms of computational complexity and scalability, which are critical for understanding the model's practical advantages.
- There are minor issues with the clarity and presentation of the paper, such as unclear explanations in certain sections and typographical errors.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the decoupling of positional and structural information specifically improves the model's performance, and provide more detailed experimental evidence to support this claim?
- How does the model handle the issue of over-squeezing and over-smoothing, particularly in large datasets?
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison with existing models in terms of computational complexity and scalability, and discuss how the model's decoupling approach compares to other methods in terms of expressiveness and efficiency?
- What are the limitations of the proposed model, and under what conditions might it not perform optimally?
- Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the model's decoupling approach and how it differs from other models in terms of inductive bias and expressiveness?
- In terms of scalability, how does the model perform on very large graphs, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in scaling the model to such datasets?
