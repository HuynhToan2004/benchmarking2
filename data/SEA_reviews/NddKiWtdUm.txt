 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to training language models (LLMs) using a simulated social interaction environment, termed "Sandbox," which involves a multi-agent system where LLMs play the role of agents. The agents interact with each other, providing feedback and ratings to refine their outputs, thereby promoting social alignment. The authors propose a three-stage training methodology that includes imitation learning, self-criticism, and realignment, which is evaluated against several benchmarks and compared to various baselines. The approach aims to enhance the alignment of LLMs with societal values by leveraging simulated social interactions, although concerns are raised about the practical applicability and the depth of social alignment achieved.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and the experimental setup is well-motivated, with a clear and well-structured presentation of the methodology.
- The introduction of a novel framework for training language models (LLMs) to achieve social alignment through simulated social interactions is innovative and addresses an important issue in AI safety and alignment.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation using multiple benchmarks and a large number of baseline methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The approach is scalable and does not require an online reward model in memory during training, making it potentially more efficient and cost-effective compared to other methods.
- The use of simulated social interactions for LLM training is an interesting and novel approach that could potentially enhance the alignment of LLMs with societal values.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical applicability of the proposed approach in real-world scenarios, particularly in terms of how the trained LLMs would perform in uncontrolled environments.
- The evaluation of the method is limited, focusing primarily on LLM-generated text and not including evaluations on other tasks such as image or video generation, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's capabilities.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the approach, particularly concerning the scalability and the depth of social alignment achieved.
- The methodology's reliance on simulated social interactions raises questions about the authenticity and effectiveness of the feedback and ratings provided by the agents, which could impact the overall alignment of the LLMs.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for adversarial attacks or the robustness of the trained LLMs against such attacks, which is a critical concern in the context of AI safety and alignment.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the trained LLMs would perform in uncontrolled environments, particularly in terms of their ability to generalize to new, unseen situations?
- How does the proposed method ensure that the LLMs do not simply memorize the feedback and ratings provided by the agents, which could limit their ability to adapt to new scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the design choices for the agents in the Sandbox environment, particularly how they were designed to ensure that they provide effective feedback and ratings that promote alignment?
- How does the methodology address the potential for adversarial attacks, and what steps have been taken to ensure the robustness of the trained LLMs against such attacks?
- In light of the concerns about the authenticity and effectiveness of the feedback and ratings provided by the agents, how does the paper address the potential for biases or inaccuracies in the data used to train the LLMs?
