 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for amortized Bayesian inference using a reverse KL divergence objective to train neural networks that approximate the posterior distribution of probabilistic models. This method leverages set-based neural network architectures like DeepSets and Transformers to generalize across datasets of different sizes and orders. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness through extensive experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets, showing that it can handle model misspecification and out-of-distribution data. The paper also explores the use of normalizing flows and Gaussian distributions as parameterizations for the variational distribution. Despite its strengths in generalization and handling model misspecification, the paper is criticized for its incremental novelty and limited empirical evidence on real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach for amortized Bayesian inference, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments are well-designed, comprehensive, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in handling model misspecification and out-of-distribution data.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The use of set-based neural network architectures like DeepSets and Transformers is innovative and allows for generalization across datasets of different sizes and orders.
- The methodology is robust, handling model misspecification and out-of-distribution data effectively, which is crucial in practical applications.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison of different architectural elements and their impact on the performance of the model, which is valuable for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for using a reverse KL divergence objective over a forward KL divergence, which could be better explained.
- There is a need for more real-world experiments to demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed method, particularly in complex probabilistic models.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it seems to be an incremental extension of existing methods, such as those by Radev et al. (2020).
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of the variational distribution and its impact on the results.
- The experimental setup could be improved to include more varied and complex datasets to better demonstrate the generalizability of the method.
- There are some minor issues with the clarity and organization of the paper, particularly in the presentation of results and the explanation of certain methodological choices.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more real-world datasets where the proposed method can be applied to demonstrate its practical utility?
- How does the choice of the variational distribution affect the results, and what are the implications of using different distributions?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, especially in the tabular experiments section, to make the findings clearer and more convincing?
- What are the specific advantages of using a reverse KL divergence over a forward KL divergence in the context of amortized Bayesian inference?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability, especially in complex probabilistic models?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of using a normalizing flow as a parameterization for the variational distribution and its impact on the results?
