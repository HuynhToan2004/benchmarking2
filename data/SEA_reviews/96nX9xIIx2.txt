 **Summary:**
The paper introduces VPNs, a novel framework that integrates visual prompts with model sparsification to enhance the performance of neural networks. This approach leverages data-model co-design to optimize both the model topology and input data, which is a departure from traditional model-centric pruning methods. The methodology is validated through extensive experiments across various datasets and architectures, demonstrating the effectiveness of VPNs in reducing computational and memory footprints while maintaining or improving performance. Despite the innovative approach, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the visual prompting technique and the clarity of the method's application to different network architectures and datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel data-model co-design perspective for model sparsification, which is a significant departure from traditional model-centric pruning methods.
- The methodology is well-explained and easy to follow, with clear figures and detailed descriptions that aid in understanding the proposed framework.
- Extensive experiments are conducted across various datasets and architectures, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in reducing computational and memory footprints while maintaining or improving performance.
- The paper provides a systematic investigation into the impact of different visual prompts on model pruning, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The proposed VPNs framework shows promising results in promoting superior weight sparsity by learning important model topology and adequate input data, which is a significant advancement in the field of model pruning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the novelty of the visual prompting technique, which is a significant aspect of the proposed method.
- The experiments are limited to specific network architectures (ResNet, VGG) and datasets (ImageNet-1K, CIFAR-10/100, Tiny-ImageNet), which may not generalize well to other architectures or datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other pruning methods that also incorporate visual prompts, which could help in understanding the relative advantages of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodology, particularly in the description of the visual prompting technique and its application to different network architectures.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for a comprehensive understanding of its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the novelty of the visual prompting technique and how it differs from existing methods?
- How does the proposed method perform on different network architectures, such as transformer-based architectures like ViT?
- Can the authors clarify the computational complexity of the proposed method and provide a comparison with other pruning methods that also incorporate visual prompts?
- How does the proposed method handle the integration of visual prompts with different pruning methods, and what are the implications for the performance and generalization of the models?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed description of the visual prompting technique and its application to different network architectures to enhance the clarity and comprehensibility of the methodology?
