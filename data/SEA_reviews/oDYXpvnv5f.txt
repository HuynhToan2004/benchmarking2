 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to uncertainty quantification in high-dimensional regression and classification, focusing on out-of-distribution (OOD) data. It proposes a deep anti-regularized ensemble (DARE) method, which encourages large weights in networks by penalizing small weights and controlling weight increases to maintain in-distribution loss. The method is evaluated on various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving OOD coverage and uncertainty quantification. Theoretical arguments support the approach, viewing the optimization as a "water-filling" problem. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticisms for its lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, limited experimental validation, and potential over-confidence issues.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The method is evaluated on several datasets, including both synthetic and real-world examples, demonstrating its effectiveness in out-of-distribution (OOD) data and uncertainty quantification.
- The paper provides a theoretical framework that helps in understanding the proposed method, and the experiments are reproducible with provided code.
- The method is original in its approach to anti-regularization, which is novel and has not been extensively explored in previous literature.
- The paper is well-organized and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods for OOD uncertainty quantification, which could undermine the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some results showing only marginal improvements over existing methods, and others showing significant decreases in performance.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to fully-connected networks with piece-wise linear activation and linear end-activation, which may limit its applicability to other types of networks.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of over-confidence in OOD data, which is a critical aspect of uncertainty quantification.
- The method's reliance on a heuristic threshold setting could lead to suboptimal results, and the paper does not provide a clear guideline for setting this threshold.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation across different types of neural networks and datasets to demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods for OOD uncertainty quantification, including a discussion on how their method differs and potentially improves upon these existing approaches?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of neural networks, such as convolutional networks or recurrent networks?
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies in the experimental results, particularly the significant decreases in performance observed in some datasets?
- How does the method handle the issue of over-confidence in OOD data, and what steps are taken to ensure that the uncertainty estimates are reliable?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the threshold setting is determined and whether there are guidelines or heuristics that could be used to set this threshold effectively?
- Is there any theoretical or empirical evidence to support the claim that the method is effective in reducing the prediction variance for OOD data?
