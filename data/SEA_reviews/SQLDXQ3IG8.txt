 **Summary:**
The paper explores the convergence and generalization properties of adversarial training in non-separable data scenarios, focusing on two-layer neural networks with arbitrary width. It extends beyond the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) regime, providing theoretical guarantees for both smooth and non-smooth activation functions. The authors introduce a novel data model and hypothesis class, supported by empirical studies on synthetic and real-world datasets, aiming to enhance the understanding of adversarial training's robustness and generalization. However, the paper's theoretical results are critiqued for their limited applicability to real-world adversarial attacks and their strong assumptions, which may not hold in practical scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the theoretical results and providing a detailed proof sketch.
- It extends the analysis of adversarial training to non-separable data distributions, a significant advancement in understanding the robustness and generalization properties of adversarial training.
- The paper is technically sound, with a novel data model and hypothesis class that are well-explained and supported by empirical studies.
- The authors provide a clear and detailed proof sketch, which aids in understanding the theoretical results.
- The paper is significant as it addresses the generalization of adversarial training beyond the NTK regime, a topic of high interest in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper makes several strong assumptions that may not hold in practical scenarios, such as the assumption that the noise rate is small, which is not realistic for real-world adversarial attacks.
- The theoretical results are limited to two-layer neural networks with fixed weights at the top layer, which may not generalize to more complex or realistic network architectures.
- The paper's proofs rely on strong assumptions about the data distribution and the initialization of weights, which may not hold in practical settings.
- The empirical validation is limited to synthetic datasets, which may not fully represent the behavior of adversarial training in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the overfitting issue observed in adversarial training, which is a critical concern in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the practical implications of the assumptions made in the paper, particularly in terms of their applicability to real-world adversarial attacks?
- How does the paper's theoretical analysis translate to more complex or realistic network architectures, such as those with multiple hidden layers or adaptive weights?
- Could the authors provide more detailed empirical validation, perhaps using real-world datasets, to better demonstrate the robustness and generalization properties of adversarial training in practical scenarios?
- How does the paper address the overfitting issue observed in adversarial training, and what implications does this have for the practical applicability of the results?
