 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for reconstructing 3D surfaces, materials, and lighting from multi-view images, focusing on the handling of glossy objects and complex lighting conditions. The method is divided into three stages: initial surface reconstruction, learning direct lighting visibility, and material and direct illumination reconstruction. It utilizes a combination of surface and volume rendering to model specular reflections and indirect illumination, employing a spherical Gaussian representation for the BRDF. The approach is validated against state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real datasets, showing improvements in surface reconstruction quality and material decomposition. Despite its technical merits, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty and clarity in presentation, with concerns about the computational complexity and the practical applicability of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant challenge of reconstructing glossy objects, a problem that has been under-explored in previous works.
- It proposes a novel methodology for jointly reconstructing shape, material, and lighting, which is a complex and under-explored problem in computer vision.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and includes extensive supplementary material that aids in understanding the methodology.
- The proposed method is technically sound, with a clear motivation and a logical structure that is easy to follow.
- The paper demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods in material and lighting reconstruction, and the results are convincing.
- The use of SDFs for volume rendering and the integration of specular albedo into the BRDF are innovative contributions to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical application.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant new contributions.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of concavities in surface reconstruction, which is a significant limitation.
- The results section of the paper is not convincing, with qualitative results showing significant artifacts and quantitative results not significantly better than existing methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, including those that use different data structures or different neural architectures.
- There are several presentation issues, such as inconsistent notation and unclear figures, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect the reliability and applicability of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and runtime to other state-of-the-art methods?
- Can the method be extended to handle more complex materials or different lighting conditions?
- How does the method handle the reconstruction of surfaces with complex geometries, such as those with concavities or intricate details?
- What are the specific advantages of using SDFs for volume rendering over other methods, such as voxels or other data structures?
- How does the paper address the issue of specular reflection in the reconstruction process, and what are the implications for the accuracy of the results?
- Could the authors clarify the notation and presentation issues noted in the paper to improve readability and understanding?
