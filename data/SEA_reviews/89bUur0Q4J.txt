 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel subspace-based prompt learning method, named SuPr, which effectively models the subspaces spanning the embeddings of both learnable soft and textual/hard prompts. This method balances the effects of soft and hard prompts to achieve better fitting of base classes and generalization to novel classes. The proposed method, SuPr, has been evaluated across various settings including generalization from base to new, domain generalization, cross-dataset transfer, and few-shot learning, showing significant improvements over existing methods. The paper is well-structured, providing a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology and its experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-structured, providing a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology and its experimental results.
- The proposed subspace-based prompt learning method, named SuPr, is novel and effective, balancing the effects of soft and hard prompts to achieve better fitting of base classes and generalization to novel classes.
- SuPr shows significant improvements over existing methods in various settings including generalization from base to new, domain generalization, cross-dataset transfer, and few-shot learning.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the experiments are comprehensive, including ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The idea of modeling prompt subspaces is innovative and has the potential to improve the performance of vision-language models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the subspace modeling and the use of hard prompts in the regularization.
- The experimental results are not compared with the latest methods, which could affect the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the proposed method compared to existing methods, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The methodology section could be improved by providing more detailed explanations of the subspace modeling and the use of hard prompts in the regularization.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other methods, particularly in terms of the number of parameters used and the computational efficiency.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed method compare with other recent methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the subspace modeling and the use of hard prompts in the regularization?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential issue of base class overfitting, and what are the implications of this for the generalization of the model to new classes?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the number of parameters used and the computational efficiency of the proposed method?
