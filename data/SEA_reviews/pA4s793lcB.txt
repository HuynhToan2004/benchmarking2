 **Summary:**
The paper explores replicable algorithms for the multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem, focusing on reducing the regret dependence on the number of arms (K) and improving the replicability of the algorithms. It introduces two algorithms, Replicable Explore-then-Commit (REC) and Replicable Successive Elimination (RSE), which aim to achieve O(K) regret when the suboptimality gaps are within a constant factor. The paper also proposes a linear bandit algorithm with a regret bound linear in the dimension of associated features. Theoretical analysis is provided, including proofs of replicability and regret bounds, alongside empirical results that demonstrate the algorithms' performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for studying replicable algorithms in the multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting.
- It introduces two algorithms, REC and RSE, which improve upon existing algorithms by achieving O(K) regret when the suboptimality gaps for each arm are within a constant factor.
- The paper provides a lower bound for replicable MAB, which is a novel contribution to the field.
- The algorithms are presented with a principled approach to limiting the probability of non-replication, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper includes empirical results that demonstrate the algorithms' performance, which is a practical validation of the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's main contribution, the algorithm, seems to be a straightforward modification of existing algorithms, such as RSE, which may not be seen as a significant advancement.
- The assumption that the data-generating process is known is strong and not well-justified, especially considering the challenges in practical scenarios where this assumption may not hold.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to the two-arm case, which might not generalize well to more arms.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing algorithms, particularly in terms of regret bounds and the number of arms considered.
- The proofs and theoretical results are not well-explained, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, which is a critical aspect for practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of the algorithm beyond being a straightforward modification of existing algorithms?
- How does the assumption of a known data-generating process affect the practical applicability of the algorithms?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing algorithms, particularly in terms of regret bounds and the number of arms considered?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms compare to existing methods?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical results, especially the proofs and the assumptions made?
- Is there a possibility to extend the theoretical analysis to more arms, and how might this affect the results?
