 **Summary:**
The paper explores the robustness of CLIP models by examining their internal features and representation spaces, specifically focusing on the presence of outlier features and privileged directions in the models. These features are hypothesized to contribute to the models' robustness across different natural distribution shifts. The authors conduct extensive experiments using various backbones and pretraining sets, analyzing the activation kurtosis, singular value decomposition (SVD), and concept probing to identify these characteristics. The findings suggest that robust models have distinct features that are several orders of magnitude above average, which may be crucial for their robustness. However, the paper's scope is limited to CLIP models, and it does not provide a clear methodological framework for diagnosing robustness in other models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic, focusing on the robustness of CLIP models, which is crucial for understanding and improving model performance across various applications.
- The analysis conducted in the paper is extensive and comprehensive, covering a wide range of CLIP models with different backbones and pretraining sets, providing a robust empirical basis for the findings.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations and a logical structure that aids in understanding the complex concepts presented.
- The findings, such as the presence of outlier features and privileged directions in robust models, are both interesting and significant, potentially opening new avenues for research and application in the field.
- The paper provides a detailed and insightful analysis of the features and representations learned by CLIP models, which could be beneficial for further research and development in the area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on CLIP models, which may limit its applicability to other types of models, raising concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a clear methodological framework for diagnosing the robustness of models, which could be crucial for practical applications and further research.
- The paper does not provide sufficient quantitative evidence to support some of its claims, such as the relationship between outlier features and privileged directions, which could weaken the overall argument.
- The paper's scope is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on CLIP models and not exploring the robustness of other models, which could limit the broader impact and relevance of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of its findings, which could be a significant concern for some readers and stakeholders.

**Questions:**
- How do the authors define "robustness" in the context of their study, and how does this definition relate to the broader concept of model robustness in the field?
- Could the authors clarify the methodological framework they propose for diagnosing the robustness of models, and discuss its applicability to other types of models beyond CLIP?
- What are the specific negative societal impacts that the authors anticipate from their findings, and how do they plan to address these concerns?
- How do the authors explain the relationship between outlier features and privileged directions in robust models, and what evidence supports this relationship?
- Could the authors provide more quantitative evidence to support their claims about the importance of outlier features and privileged directions in robust models?
- How do the findings of this study relate to other recent works on CLIP models, such as those by Yu et al. (2022) and Fang et al. (2022)?
