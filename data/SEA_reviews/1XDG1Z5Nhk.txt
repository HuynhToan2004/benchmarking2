 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SparseMixer, a novel gradient estimator designed to enhance the training of Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models by addressing the challenge of sparse computation in backpropagation. SparseMixer leverages the mid-point method from Ordinary Differential Equations (ODE) to provide reliable gradient estimations, thereby improving the scalability and efficiency of MoE models. The methodology is validated through experiments on the Switch Transformer, demonstrating performance improvements and reduced training time. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is critiqued for its limited experimental scope and potential overfitting issues, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant issue in MoE training by introducing SparseMixer, a novel gradient estimator that provides reliable approximations for gradient terms neglected in typical MoE training.
- The use of the mid-point method from ODE to estimate the gradient is both innovative and practical, requiring minimal computational overhead and providing second-order accuracy.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the methodology and its theoretical underpinnings.
- Experiments conducted on the Switch Transformer demonstrate that SparseMixer enhances model performance and reduces training time, indicating its practical utility.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the problem and proposes a novel solution, which could be a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental evaluation is limited, focusing only on the Switch Transformer and not comparing SparseMixer with other MoE training methods like those by Fedus et al. (2021) or Kool et al. (2021).
- The paper's theoretical analysis is not convincingly supported by empirical evidence, particularly in terms of the generalization ability of the model.
- The method's effectiveness is not thoroughly validated across different datasets or MoE models, which could limit the understanding of its applicability and robustness.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the potential overfitting issues with MoE models, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the explanation of the sampling process and the role of certain variables in the equations.

**Questions:**
- How does the performance of SparseMixer compare with other MoE training methods like those by Fedus et al. (2021) or Kool et al. (2021)?
- Can SparseMixer be applied to other MoE models, such as those using different gating mechanisms or different numbers of experts?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the number of experts is very large or very small?
- Could you clarify the role and calculation of the variable ω in the equations, particularly in the context of the mid-point method?
- In the context of the mid-point method, how does the method ensure that the gradient estimation is accurate, given the potential for approximation errors?
- How does the method address the issue of overfitting, particularly in the context of MoE models, which are known to be prone to overfitting?
