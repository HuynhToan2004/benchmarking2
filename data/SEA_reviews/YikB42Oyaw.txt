 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MoReDrop, a novel regularization technique for dropout training, which addresses the model distributional shift problem by utilizing the loss of the dense model as the main loss function. MoReDrop aims to enhance the robustness of dropout training by ensuring consistency between the dense model and its sub-models, thereby improving generalization and reducing computational burden. The method is supported by extensive experiments across various models and tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency. The authors also propose a lightweight version, MoReDropL, which further reduces computational costs. The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental results, although it could benefit from more detailed comparisons and theoretical justifications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and well-motivated problem, focusing on the model distributional shift in dropout training, which is a critical issue in deep learning.
- The proposed method, MoReDrop, is innovative and well-explained, with clear theoretical underpinnings and experimental validation across various models and tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the authors have provided detailed experimental results, including comparisons with existing methods and ablation studies.
- The methodology is simple yet effective, and the paper is well-organized, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.
- The paper presents a novel approach to mitigating distributional shifts in dropout training, which is crucial for enhancing the robustness and generalization of neural networks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other regularization techniques, particularly those that address similar issues as MoReDrop. This could help in better positioning the proposed method within the existing landscape of regularization techniques.
- There is a noticeable absence of detailed theoretical justification for the effectiveness of MoReDrop, which could strengthen the paper's argument and provide a deeper understanding of the method's operation.
- The paper's experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse datasets and architectures to better demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational overhead of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications, especially in resource-constrained environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential areas for future research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other regularization techniques, particularly those that address similar issues as MoReDrop?
- Is there a theoretical justification for the effectiveness of MoReDrop, and if so, could this be included in the paper to strengthen the argument?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational efficiency, and what are the practical implications of the computational overhead?
- Could the authors expand on the experimental setup to include more diverse datasets and architectures to better demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and computational efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods in the field?
- What are the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how might these limitations affect its practical application?
