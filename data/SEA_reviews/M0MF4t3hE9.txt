 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel task called Instructional Object Detection (IOD), where a model is trained to understand user instructions and locate relevant objects in images. It proposes a method called Ins-DetCLIP, which integrates a visual encoder (DetCLIP) with a language model (FlanT5) to align object-level features with instructional text. The method is evaluated on a new dataset, IOD-Bench, which includes 8k+ instructions and 20k+ images, and the results demonstrate that Ins-DetCLIP outperforms other baselines. The paper also discusses the challenges of integrating vision and language models and proposes a two-stage training strategy to address these challenges.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel task, Instructional Object Detection (IOD), which is significant for advancing the field of vision-language models.
- The proposed dataset, IOD-Bench, is well-curated and includes a diverse set of instructions, which is crucial for evaluating the performance of IOD systems.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive ablation studies that provide insights into the effectiveness of different components of the proposed method.
- The method, Ins-DetCLIP, is a novel approach that effectively integrates a visual encoder with a language model to understand user instructions and locate relevant objects in images.
- The experimental results demonstrate that Ins-DetCLIP outperforms other baselines, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and challenges of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the method might not perform optimally.
- There is a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods in dense captioning, which could help in validating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical deployment in real-world applications.
- The methodology section could benefit from more detailed explanations, particularly around the training pipeline and the integration of the visual encoder with the language model.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the method across different datasets and domains, which is a significant concern for the practical application of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the user instructions are ambiguous or incomplete?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training pipeline, particularly how the visual encoder is integrated with the language model?
- How does the method perform when the user instructions are not specific to a particular object category?
- Could the authors discuss the potential applications of the IOD task and the proposed method, particularly in practical scenarios such as robotics or autonomous driving?
- How does the method handle noisy or incorrect instructions generated by the LLM?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method and discuss potential strategies for reducing computational complexity?
