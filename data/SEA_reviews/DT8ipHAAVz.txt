 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two novel methods for unsupervised tree clustering: KAURI and DOUGLAS. KAURI utilizes a binary decision tree with a kernel-KMeans-like objective for clustering, while DOUGLAS employs a k-ary differentiable tree with a Wasserstein distance-based objective. Both methods aim to optimize the GEMINI loss, which is a generalized mutual information, and are evaluated against various datasets. The paper claims that KAURI can handle datasets with a large number of features, while DOUGLAS is suitable for datasets with a large number of samples. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods like CART and DNDT, and the empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate superior performance over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces two new methods, KAURI and DOUGLAS, which are both innovative and practical for unsupervised tree clustering.
- The methods are well-explained, with clear formulations and detailed derivations provided in the appendix.
- The empirical results show that KAURI performs better than other unsupervised tree clustering methods in most cases.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed methods are easy to implement and can be applied to datasets with large numbers of features or samples.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of scalability and efficiency, which is crucial for decision trees.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its methods from existing approaches like CART and DNDT, which limits the perceived novelty.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods over existing approaches, and the improvements are often marginal or not statistically significant.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of KAURI with the number of features, which is a significant limitation given the method's claimed ability to handle datasets with a large number of features.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which is crucial for understanding their practical applicability.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which is essential for a complete understanding of their applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed methods with existing approaches, particularly in terms of scalability and efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational complexity, especially in datasets with a large number of features?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational time and memory usage of the proposed methods compared to other unsupervised tree clustering methods?
- Is there a possibility to combine the KAURI and DOUGLAS methods to address the limitations of each approach?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the use of different metrics for evaluation and the choice of datasets?
- How do the proposed methods handle missing values in datasets, and what are the implications for the clustering results?
