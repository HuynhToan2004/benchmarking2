 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ORBIS, a method aimed at mitigating dataset bias by leveraging open datasets. ORBIS operates in two stages: first, it selects relevant samples from open datasets based on their similarity to class-wise centroids of the target dataset. Second, it trains a debiased model using these selected samples alongside the original training dataset. The method is evaluated on various datasets, showing improvements in bias mitigation. However, the paper's approach is seen as incremental, with the primary contribution being the application of self-supervised learning to select relevant samples from open datasets. The method's effectiveness is questioned due to its reliance on strong assumptions about the availability of relevant open datasets and the lack of comprehensive experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem of dataset bias, which is crucial for the development of fair and unbiased machine learning models.
- The proposed method is simple, easy to follow, and can be integrated with existing bias mitigation methods, making it a practical solution for real-world applications.
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation and logical flow, making it accessible to readers.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in mitigating dataset bias, showing improvements in accuracy and fairness.
- The use of open datasets for bias mitigation is a novel approach that could potentially reduce the need for human intervention and improve the generalizability of machine learning models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive analysis of the quality and relevance of open datasets used in the experiments, which is crucial for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on the availability of relevant open datasets is a significant limitation, as the method's utility is heavily dependent on the quality and quantity of these datasets.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with insufficient comparisons to existing methods and a lack of detailed analysis of the experimental results.
- The paper's claims about the method's novelty and effectiveness are overstated, as the use of self-supervised learning and contrastive loss is not new and the method's effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated.
- The paper does not adequately address the ethical implications of using open datasets, particularly in terms of privacy and data security, which are critical for the responsible use of machine learning models.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the quality and relevance of the open datasets used in the experiments? How were these datasets selected, and what criteria were used to ensure their suitability for bias mitigation?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the open dataset contains bias-conflicting samples? What measures are taken to ensure that these samples do not negatively impact the model's performance?
- Can you clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly in terms of the comparisons made and the statistical significance of the observed improvements?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Given the reliance on open datasets, how does the paper address potential privacy and data security issues? What measures are in place to ensure that the use of these datasets complies with relevant ethical guidelines and regulations?
