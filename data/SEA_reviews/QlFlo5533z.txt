 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Auto DP-SGD, a novel method for differential privacy in stochastic gradient descent (DP-SGD) that automatically adjusts clipping thresholds and noise multipliers based on the gradient norms of each training sample. This approach is designed to improve the utility of DP-SGD by reducing the privacy budget and enhancing model accuracy. The methodology is supported by extensive experiments across various datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed techniques. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty, unclear presentation, and insufficient empirical validation. The paper's reliance on existing techniques and its failure to provide a clear theoretical justification for the proposed methods have raised concerns about its contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The proposed algorithm is simple and easy to implement, which could facilitate its adoption in practical applications.
- The paper presents extensive experiments on various datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and AG News Corpus, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The authors provide detailed explanations of their algorithm and the intuition behind it, which helps in understanding the methodology and its implications.
- The paper addresses the significant issue of privacy leakage in existing methods by proposing an automatic clipping threshold estimation based on the DL model's gradient norm.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it primarily builds upon existing techniques without introducing significant new contributions.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in the introduction and related work sections, to better contextualize the research within the existing literature.
- The experimental results are not convincing, as the paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing state-of-the-art methods, nor does it provide a comprehensive analysis of the empirical results.
- There is a lack of theoretical justification for the proposed methods, which makes it difficult to understand the underlying principles and their effectiveness.
- The paper suffers from several grammatical errors and inconsistencies in terminology, which detract from its professionalism and clarity.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational overhead introduced by the proposed methods, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the proposed method differs from existing techniques, particularly in terms of its novel contributions?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational overhead compared to existing methods, and what are the implications for practical applications?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing state-of-the-art methods, including a side-by-side comparison of performance metrics and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between privacy and utility, and what are the specific implications for different types of data and applications?
- Could the authors provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for the proposed methods, particularly in terms of their impact on privacy and utility?
- How does the paper address the potential negative societal impacts of its proposed methods, and what steps have been taken to mitigate these risks?
