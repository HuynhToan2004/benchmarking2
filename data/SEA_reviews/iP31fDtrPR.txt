 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to estimating parameters of directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) using an optimal transport (OT) framework, termed OTP-DAG. This methodology avoids the use of likelihood maximization, instead focusing on minimizing the Wasserstein distance between the data and model distributions. The approach is demonstrated to be versatile, scalable, and applicable to a variety of graphical structures, including latent Dirichlet allocation, hidden Markov models, and discrete representation learning. Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of OTP-DAG in parameter estimation and downstream applications, showing competitive results compared to existing methods. However, the paper is criticized for lacking a thorough discussion on related works and for potential issues with the scalability and computational efficiency of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to parameter learning in directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) using an optimal transport (OT) framework, which is both scalable and versatile.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations and thorough experimental validation, demonstrating the effectiveness of OTP-DAG in various applications such as latent Dirichlet allocation, hidden Markov models, and discrete representation learning.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and structured presentation that effectively communicates the theoretical contributions and practical implications of the proposed method.
- The experiments are extensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of OTP-DAG in parameter estimation and downstream applications, showing competitive results compared to existing methods.
- The paper provides a novel perspective on parameter learning in DAGs by viewing it as an optimal transport problem, which offers a fresh approach to addressing complexities associated with latent variables.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on related works, particularly those that also employ optimal transport methods for DAG parameter estimation, which could provide a richer context and highlight the unique contributions of the proposed method.
- There is a need for more detailed discussion on the scalability and computational efficiency of the method, especially in large-scale applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include scenarios where OTP-DAG might not perform optimally or where it may not be applicable.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant baselines, such as those using stochastic variational inference or advanced VAE variants, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive exploration of the theoretical underpinnings and practical implications of the backward maps, which are crucial for the method's effectiveness.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse datasets and real-world applications to better validate the generalizability and effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and implications of the backward maps in the proposed method, particularly how they influence the effectiveness and efficiency of the OTP-DAG approach?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability and computational efficiency issues, especially in large-scale applications?
- Can you provide a more detailed comparison with other baselines, particularly those using stochastic variational inference or advanced VAE variants, to better understand the relative performance of OTP-DAG?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the graphical model is unknown, and how does it compare to methods that learn the graphical model alongside the parameters?
- Can you elaborate on the theoretical foundations and practical implications of the method's ability to minimize the Wasserstein distance between data and model distributions, and how this translates into effective parameter estimation?
- How does the method handle the presence of latent variables, and what are the implications for the estimation of model parameters?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the datasets used and the specifics of the experimental conditions, to better understand the generalizability and robustness of the proposed method?
