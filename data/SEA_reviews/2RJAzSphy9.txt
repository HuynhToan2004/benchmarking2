 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for efficient active learning in reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) settings. It formulates the process of selecting contexts and actions for human preference feedback as a contextual dueling bandit problem, employing a UCB-like algorithm to optimize the selection. Theoretical analysis provides a polynomial worst-case regret bound, and empirical results showcase the method's effectiveness across various datasets, including a newly introduced Jeopardy! dataset. The approach aims to minimize the number of human feedback samples required, thereby reducing costs and enhancing the efficiency of RLHF.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise language that makes it accessible to a broad audience.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, providing a comprehensive understanding of the proposed method's theoretical underpinnings.
- The empirical results are convincing, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in reducing the number of human feedback samples required.
- The paper introduces a new Jeopardy! dataset, which is a valuable contribution to the field of RLHF.
- The proposed algorithm is simple and effective, with a straightforward application of existing techniques from active learning and bandits.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of RLHF, where human feedback is costly, and proposes a method that could potentially reduce these costs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability and scalability in different scenarios.
- There is a significant gap between the theoretical analysis and the practical implementation, which may limit the method's effectiveness in real-world applications.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing active learning baselines, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of its effectiveness.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently detailed, with insufficient ablation studies and a lack of standard deviation reporting, which makes it difficult to assess the reliability and reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational costs and scalability of the proposed method, especially in scenarios involving large-scale LLMs and extensive RLHF training.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and discuss how these assumptions affect the practical applicability of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing active learning baselines in terms of sample efficiency and computational costs?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including standard deviations and ablation studies, to support the claims made about the method's effectiveness?
- What are the computational costs and scalability considerations for the proposed method, especially in large-scale RLHF applications?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues related to the number of contexts and actions, and what are the implications for the practical applicability of the method in real-world scenarios?
