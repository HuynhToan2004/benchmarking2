 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the dynamics of gradient descent (GD) and replicator dynamics (RD) in potential games, focusing on the convergence to Nash equilibria and the performance metrics like Price of Anarchy (PoA) and its average variant (APoA). It establishes that Q-Replicator Dynamics (QRD), including GD and RD, converge to Nash equilibria in almost all finite potential games. The study also explores the comparative performance of GD and RD in 2x2 games, showing that GD outperforms RD in terms of APoA when risk- and payoff-dominant equilibria coincide. The paper presents a theoretical analysis and numerical experiments to support these findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to the study of convergence dynamics in potential games, focusing on the average performance metrics which are a significant extension over the traditional worst-case analysis.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed analysis of the convergence dynamics of gradient descent and replicator dynamics, supported by both theoretical proofs and numerical experiments.
- The paper addresses a relevant and timely topic, the convergence dynamics of learning algorithms in games, which has practical implications in various fields such as economics and machine learning.
- The authors provide a thorough review of the literature and clearly articulate the contributions of their work, enhancing the paper's readability and accessibility.
- The paper offers a new perspective on the comparison of different dynamics in games, providing insights into the performance of various dynamics in different game settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on perfectly-regular potential games (PRPGs) may limit its applicability to a broader class of games, as it does not consider games with singular Hessian matrices which could lead to a different set of equilibria.
- The paper's theoretical results, such as Theorem 3.2, are not surprising and could be seen as a straightforward application of existing results, which might diminish their novelty.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed or convincing, particularly in terms of the selection of parameters and the statistical significance of the results.
- The paper's assumptions and definitions, such as the assumption of gradient descent dynamics as a special case of Q-replicator dynamics, could be seen as limiting or overly restrictive.
- The paper's writing and presentation could be improved to better clarify the main contributions and to make the content more accessible to a broader audience.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions and implications of the results for games with singular Hessian matrices?
- How does the paper's analysis extend to games where the dynamics do not converge to a Nash equilibrium, such as those with limit cycles?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples of the invariant functions mentioned in the paper, particularly in relation to their role in the analysis?
- How does the paper's analysis compare to other dynamics, such as those based on Lyapunov analysis, and what are the advantages or disadvantages of using Q-replicator dynamics over these alternatives?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results or statistical analyses to support their claims about the performance of different dynamics in different game settings?
