 **Summary:**
The paper explores deceptive fairness attacks on graph learning models, proposing a meta-learning framework named FATE to amplify bias in graph neural networks (GNNs) while maintaining performance on downstream tasks. The method optimizes both the upper-level bias function and the lower-level loss function to maximize bias and minimize task-specific loss, respectively. The paper includes experiments on three real-world datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of FATE in exacerbating bias. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in problem definition, insufficient justification for the use of meta-learning, and limited experimental scope.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel problem definition of deceptive fairness attacks on graphs, which is significant and relevant to the field of graph learning.
- The proposed FATE framework is innovative, using meta-learning to optimize graph poisoning attacks, which is a novel approach in the context of fairness attacks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of FATE in amplifying bias while maintaining utility on downstream tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology and its implications.
- The problem of fairness attacks in graph learning is timely and relevant, with potential applications in various real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in problem definition, particularly in the distinction between upper-level bias maximization and lower-level loss minimization, which could lead to confusion about the true objective of the proposed method.
- The justification for using meta-learning as a tool for graph poisoning attacks is insufficient, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation or comparison with other methods.
- The experimental scope is limited, focusing only on semi-supervised node classification tasks and not exploring other types of graph learning models or fairness definitions.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of poisoning attack detection, which is a critical aspect of fairness attacks.
- The assumptions regarding the knowledge and capabilities of the attacker are not well-justified, which could limit the practical applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the distinction between upper-level bias maximization and lower-level loss minimization, and how these objectives are balanced in the proposed method?
- How does the FATE framework compare to other existing methods for fairness attacks on graphs, particularly in terms of effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the choice of hyperparameters and the sensitivity of the results to these choices?
- How does the FATE framework address the issue of poisoning attack detection, and what measures are in place to prevent the detection of malicious attacks?
- Given the assumptions about the knowledge and capabilities of the attacker, how realistic are these assumptions in practical scenarios, and how might this affect the applicability of the proposed method?
