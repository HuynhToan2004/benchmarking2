 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Diff-SR, a method for target-resolution super-resolution (TRSR) using pre-trained diffusion-based generative models (DGMs). The authors propose a novel approach by injecting a specific amount of noise into low-resolution images to guide the DGM's backward diffusion process, which is theoretically justified by the concept of Perceptual Recoverable Field (PRF). The method aims to achieve high-quality visual content without the need for additional training or fine-tuning. The paper presents a theoretical analysis and empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of Diff-SR, demonstrating its superiority over state-of-the-art solutions in various TRSR environments. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as similar ideas have been explored in previous works, and the method's reliance on noise injection which could affect the model's performance and generalization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the theoretical analysis is sound, providing a good understanding of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, and the experimental results demonstrate its superiority over state-of-the-art solutions.
- The paper includes a comprehensive analysis of the proposed method, including a novel theoretical analysis of the noise injection level required for different super-resolution tasks.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy for readers to follow the content, and the methodology is clearly explained.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the generalization of the method to other types of images and its dependency on noise injection.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as similar ideas have been explored in previous works.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in terms of computational cost and generalization to different resolutions.
- The method's reliance on noise injection could affect the model's performance and generalization, and the paper does not discuss the potential impact of this dependency on the model's robustness and adaptability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The method's dependency on the pre-trained DGM's capability and the resolution of the input image is not thoroughly discussed, raising concerns about its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed discussions on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning its generalization to other types of images and its dependency on noise injection?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational cost and generalization to different resolutions?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the potential impact of noise injection on the model's performance and generalization?
- How does the proposed method perform when the input image resolution is not the same as the pre-trained DGM's resolution?
- Can the authors provide more visual results, especially for different resolutions, to better demonstrate the method's effectiveness?
