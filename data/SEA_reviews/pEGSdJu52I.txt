 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the variance in test-set performance across multiple runs of neural network training, focusing on the differences between test-set and test-distribution variances. It proposes that the variance in test-set performance is largely due to finite-sample noise and not genuine differences in model quality. The authors demonstrate that this variance decreases as training duration increases and that ensemble calibration can explain the variance. They also explore how data augmentation affects variance and provide theoretical and empirical evidence to support their claims. The paper's findings suggest that test-set variance is largely unavoidable and that ensembles of independently trained networks are well-calibrated, which could lead to new insights and methods for improving training reproducibility and generalization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant issue in deep learning, namely the variance in test-set performance across multiple runs of training, and provides a novel approach to understanding this phenomenon.
- The experiments are well-designed, with clear presentation and logical flow, and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow.
- The paper introduces a simplifying statistical assumption that closely approximates the structure of the test-set accuracy distribution, which could be useful for future research.
- The findings, particularly the demonstration that test-set variance is unavoidable and that ensembles of independently trained networks are well-calibrated, are significant and could lead to new insights and methods for improving training reproducibility and generalization.
- The paper is original in its approach, presenting a new perspective on the relationship between pairs of neural network weights produced by repeated independent runs of stochastic gradient descent.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the findings, particularly how they might affect the design of training processes and the evaluation of model performance.
- There is a lack of clarity on the practical implications of the results, especially how they might be applied in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could be improved by including more experimental results, such as those involving different architectures, learning rates, and datasets, to strengthen the claims made.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough exploration of the causes of variance, particularly in relation to the role of hyperparameters and the effects of data augmentation.
- The paper's reliance on a simplifying statistical assumption may limit its applicability to more complex scenarios, and the assumptions made (such as Hypothesis 1) are not thoroughly validated.
- There are some minor presentation issues, such as typos and formatting errors, which could be addressed to improve the overall readability and professionalism of the paper.

**Questions:**
- How do the findings of this paper relate to the concept of "deep ensemble," particularly in terms of the ensemble-calibration property discussed?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of their findings, particularly how they might be applied to improve training reproducibility and generalization?
- What are the implications of the results for the design of training processes, such as the choice of hyperparameters and the use of data augmentation?
- How does the paper's approach compare to other methods for understanding and mitigating variance in neural network training, such as those discussed in related works?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, particularly on different architectures, learning rates, and datasets, to strengthen the claims made in the paper?
- In light of the assumptions made in the paper, particularly Hypothesis 1, how do the authors validate these assumptions, and what are the implications if these assumptions do not hold in more complex scenarios?
