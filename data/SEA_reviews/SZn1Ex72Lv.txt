 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel architecture called SMFR, which modifies the standard feedforward neural network (FNN) by replacing its layers with a combination of a Multiplexer and an FNNR. The Multiplexer dynamically selects and combines blocks of neurons, while the FNNR performs feature transformations. This architecture is designed to enhance the network's ability to generalize and perform tasks that require routing of data, such as logical operations. The SMFR architecture is evaluated on synthetic datasets, showing improved generalization and out-of-distribution (OOD) performance compared to standard FNNs. However, the paper's scope is limited to these specific datasets, and the generalization to real-world tasks remains untested.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel architecture, SMFR, which is a significant departure from traditional feedforward neural networks (FNNs).
- The SMFR architecture shows promising results in synthetic tasks, demonstrating improved generalization and OOD performance compared to FNNs.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a straightforward presentation of the proposed architecture.
- The SMFR architecture is designed to address the issue of routing data effectively, which is a significant problem in neural networks.
- The experiments conducted on synthetic datasets demonstrate that the SMFR architecture is capable of reusing learned subnetworks for new tasks, enhancing its transfer learning capabilities.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing routing network architectures, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the SMFR's capabilities.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to synthetic datasets, which may not adequately represent real-world scenarios where the SMFR could be applied.
- The SMFR architecture requires a significant amount of hyperparameter tuning, which could be a barrier to practical implementation.
- The paper does not discuss the computational overhead of the SMFR architecture compared to traditional FNNs, which could be a critical factor in practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the SMFR architecture, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not include a detailed analysis of the training and inference times for the SMFR architecture, which is crucial for understanding its practical feasibility.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the SMFR architecture with other routing network architectures, such as Capsule Networks and Routing Networks?
- How does the SMFR architecture perform in real-world tasks, especially those that require routing of data, such as logical operations?
- What is the computational overhead of the SMFR architecture compared to traditional FNNs, and how does this impact its practical implementation?
- Can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning required for the SMFR architecture, and what strategies are effective for optimizing these parameters?
- How does the SMFR architecture compare in terms of training and inference times with traditional FNNs, and what are the implications of these differences for practical applications?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the SMFR architecture and how these might affect its practical applicability?
