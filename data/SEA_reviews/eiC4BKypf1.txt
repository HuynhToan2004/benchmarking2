 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CENTaUR, a method that fine-tunes large language models (LLMs) on behavioral data from psychological experiments to predict human choices. This approach involves training a logistic regression classifier on top of the final hidden layer of the LLM, using a dataset of human choices and corresponding LLM outputs. The authors demonstrate that this method can outperform traditional cognitive models in predicting human behavior, particularly in decision-making tasks. The methodology involves using a 65B LLaMA model and a 13B LLaMA model, showing that the 13B model can perform similarly to the 65B model with more data. The paper also discusses the potential of using LLMs as cognitive models, although it raises concerns about the generalizability and the novelty of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to fine-tune large language models (LLMs) on behavioral data from psychological experiments, demonstrating that LLMs can accurately predict human behavior in decision-making tasks.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear and detailed descriptions of the experimental setup, data collection, and model training.
- The paper provides a comprehensive comparison of the proposed method with traditional cognitive models, showing that the LLM-based model outperforms these models in predicting human choices.
- The authors discuss the potential of using LLMs as cognitive models, which could lead to new insights into human cognition and behavior.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise language, and includes detailed appendices that provide additional information on the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on decision-making tasks and does not explore other cognitive processes such as perception or memory, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as similar methods have been used in previous studies, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from prior work.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be critical for understanding the broader implications of using LLMs as cognitive models.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which could affect the generalizability of the findings to new, unseen tasks.
- The paper does not include a detailed analysis of the computational resources required for the proposed method, which could be a significant barrier for replication and scalability.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on how the LLM-based model captures individual differences in decision-making behavior?
- How does the model perform when predicting human choices in more complex or varied decision-making scenarios?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of using LLMs as cognitive models, and how can these be mitigated?
- How does the model handle tasks that are significantly different from those in the training dataset?
- Can you provide more details on the computational resources required for the proposed method, and how scalable is the approach for larger or more complex models?
- How does the model perform when compared to other state-of-the-art models in terms of predictive accuracy and computational efficiency?
