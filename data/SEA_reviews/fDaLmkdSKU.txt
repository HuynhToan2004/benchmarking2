 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the feasibility of primal iterates in constrained learning problems using dual ascent methods, focusing on the gap between the parametrized and unparametrized constraint violations. It provides theoretical bounds on this gap under certain assumptions, such as strong convexity and smoothness of the unparametrized dual function, Lipschitz continuity of the parametrized loss function, and the boundedness of the approximation error. The authors also propose a method for obtaining near-optimal solutions without randomization, which is validated through experiments. The paper aims to bridge the gap between theoretical and practical aspects of constrained learning, particularly addressing the infeasibility of primal iterates and the role of model capacity and objective curvature.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the feasibility of primal iterates in constrained learning problems, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors have shown that under certain assumptions, the primal iterates obtained from dual ascent methods can closely approximate the feasibility of functional solutions, which is a novel and interesting finding.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations and a logical structure that makes it accessible to readers.
- The analysis of the paper is comprehensive, covering various aspects such as the unparametrized problem, feasibility approximation, and best iterate convergence.
- The paper includes a detailed mathematical derivation and experimental validation, which supports the theoretical claims and enhances the credibility of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes strong convexity and smoothness of the unparametrized dual function, which might not hold in practical scenarios, and the implications of this assumption are not thoroughly discussed.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made, particularly how they impact the validity of the results and their applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The practical implications of the theoretical results are not clearly articulated, making it difficult to assess the practical utility of the findings.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- There are some minor typographical errors and unclear sections in the paper that could be improved for clarity and readability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the assumptions made in the paper affect the validity of the results and their applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How do the authors justify the strong convexity and smoothness assumptions on the unparametrized dual function, and what are the implications if these assumptions do not hold?
- In practical scenarios, how can the proposed method be applied to obtain near-optimal solutions without randomization? What are the specific steps involved in this process?
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of the unparametrized problem in the context of the paper, and how it relates to the parametrized problem?
- The paper mentions that the feasibility of primal iterates is not guaranteed in practical scenarios. Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of this phenomenon and how it affects the practical utility of the findings?
- In the experimental validation, the paper compares the performance of the proposed method with a randomized predictor. Can the authors explain why the randomized predictor performs better in some cases and discuss the implications of this observation?
