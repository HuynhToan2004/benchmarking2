 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CMMLU, a new Chinese benchmark designed to evaluate large language models (LLMs) in a Chinese linguistic and cultural context. The dataset covers a wide range of subjects and includes 11,528 questions across 67 topics, with a focus on both China-specific and general world knowledge. The authors conducted experiments using various LLMs, including GPT4, ChatGPT, and open-source models, to assess their performance on the benchmark. Results indicate that most models struggle with a 60% accuracy rate, highlighting the need for improvement in LLMs' capabilities in the Chinese language and cultural context. The paper also discusses factors affecting model performance, such as chain-of-thought prompts and few-shot examples.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new Chinese benchmark, CMMLU, which is comprehensive and covers a wide range of subjects, including China-specific knowledge.
- The dataset is well-organized, with clear definitions of the subjects and concepts tested in each subject, and the quality check is rigorous.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the performance of LLMs on CMMLU, identifying factors that influence their performance, such as chain-of-thought prompts and few-shot examples.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, including both open-source and commercial models, and the paper presents a thorough analysis of the results.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed comparison of CMMLU with other concurrent benchmarks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the data collection process, including the number of annotators involved and the qualifications of the annotators.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential impact of data quality on the results, and the paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the noise in the dataset.
- The evaluation strategies used for open-source models are not clearly described, raising concerns about the fairness and accuracy of the evaluations.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the benchmark, such as its focus on multiple-choice questions which might not fully assess the reasoning capabilities of LLMs.
- There is a lack of comparison with other existing benchmarks for Chinese LLMs, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the models.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the evaluation of negation words and sub-options, which are critical for assessing the models' understanding of complex linguistic structures.

**Questions:**
- How many annotators were involved in the data collection process, and what were their qualifications?
- How was the noise in the dataset estimated, and what were the specific examples of noise identified?
- Could you provide more details on the evaluation strategies used for open-source models, especially how the questions were presented to the models?
- How does the performance of LLMs on CMMLU compare to other benchmarks for Chinese LLMs, such as those mentioned in the related work section?
- How does the benchmark handle negation words and sub-options, and what are the specific challenges these pose for the models?
- Can you clarify the discrepancies in the performance of different models, especially between GPT4 and other models, and how these discrepancies are explained?
