 **Summary:**
The paper explores the vulnerabilities of Vision Transformers (ViTs) to backdoor attacks, particularly focusing on the inadequacies of existing defense mechanisms. The authors identify that the success of attacks on ViTs is overstated due to inappropriate adaptations from CNNs to ViTs. To address this, they propose a novel backdoor attack method, CAT, which involves adding small perturbations to triggers during training to suppress differences in intermediate-level representations, thereby improving the transferability and stealthiness of the attacks. The paper also critiques the effectiveness of existing defenses and provides recommendations for improving defense strategies against backdoor attacks in ViTs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of existing backdoor attacks and defenses, highlighting the overestimation of attack success rates due to inappropriate adaptations from CNNs to ViTs.
- The authors propose a practical training recipe to improve defense performance and demonstrate that existing attacks cannot provide reliable performances after defense.
- The paper introduces a novel backdoor attack method, CAT, which is effective in suppressing differences in intermediate-level representations, thereby enhancing the transferability and stealthiness of attacks.
- The research is significant as it addresses the lack of backdoor attack and defense studies on ViTs, which are increasingly used in practical applications.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are well-designed and comprehensive, including both white-box and black-box scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed attack method, particularly in real-world applications.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to CIFAR-10 and does not extend to larger datasets like ImageNet, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and resource requirements of the proposed attack method.
- There is a lack of comparative analysis with other backdoor attack methods, such as those using input-aware triggers, which could affect the perceived effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the practicality of the proposed defense methods, particularly the computational overhead and resource requirements, which could limit their real-world applicability.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the transferability of the proposed attack method across different architectures and datasets, which is crucial for its practical application.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific differences in the defense strategies used for ViTs compared to those for CNNs, and how these differences affect the attack success rates?
- How does the proposed attack method perform against other backdoor attack methods, such as those using input-aware triggers?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and resource requirements of the proposed attack method, particularly in comparison to other backdoor attack methods?
- How does the proposed attack method perform in real-world scenarios, and what are the potential negative societal impacts of such attacks?
- Can the authors discuss the potential for extending the evaluation of the proposed attack method to larger datasets like ImageNet, and how this might affect the generalizability of the findings?
- How does the proposed attack method perform against other defense mechanisms beyond those specifically addressed in the paper, and what are the implications for the broader applicability of the findings?
