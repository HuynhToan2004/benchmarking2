 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two modifications to the D-Adaptation method for estimating the learning rate in adaptive optimization algorithms, aiming to improve convergence rates. The first modification, termed Prodigy, adjusts the learning rate using an exponentially increasing sequence of weights, while the second, Resetting, resets the learning rate whenever the current estimate exceeds a threshold. Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate that these modifications enhance the convergence rate of D-Adaptation, although they are not significantly faster than existing methods like Adam. The paper also discusses the practical implications of these modifications, including their potential to be integrated into existing learning rate-free optimization methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the theoretical results are presented in a clear and concise manner.
- The proposed modifications to the D-Adaptation method are simple yet effective, and the paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of these modifications.
- The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed modifications can achieve competitive performance compared to existing methods like Adam, especially in large-scale problems.
- The paper discusses the limitations of the proposed methods and provides a lower complexity bound, which helps in understanding the theoretical implications of the proposed modifications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The practical utility of the Prodigy method is questionable due to its slower convergence compared to Adam, as shown in the experiments.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other learning rate-free methods, such as those based on the Coin Betting algorithm, which might perform better in terms of convergence rate.
- The resetting approach, while theoretically interesting, does not show significant practical improvement over the Prodigy method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the modifications, particularly how they might affect the behavior of the optimization process in different scenarios.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant improvement over the baseline D-Adaptation method, especially in terms of convergence rate.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the practical utility of the Prodigy method, especially in light of its slower convergence compared to Adam?
- How does the proposed method compare to other learning rate-free methods, particularly those based on the Coin Betting algorithm?
- Could the authors provide more details on the practical implications of the modifications, especially how they might affect the convergence behavior in different scenarios?
- Is there a possibility to extend the proposed modifications to other learning rate-free optimization methods, such as those based on the Coin Betting algorithm?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the logarithmic term in their theoretical analysis, and could this term be avoided in practical implementations?
