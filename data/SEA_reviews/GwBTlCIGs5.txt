 **Summary:**
The paper presents a theoretical analysis of non-contrastive self-supervised learning (SSL) methods, specifically focusing on BarlowTwins and VICReg. It explores the orthogonality of features and the role of projector dimensionality and data augmentations in learning effective representations. Theoretical insights are provided on the implicit bias of these methods, which guide the development of more principled recommendations for improving sample efficiency in SSL. The paper empirically demonstrates that low-dimensional projector heads are sufficient with appropriate regularization and that using more augmentations per sample improves representation quality and trainability. The theoretical insights are supported by practical pretraining recommendations that enhance wall-clock time and improve performance on CIFAR-10/STL-10 datasets using a ResNet-50 backbone.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of non-contrastive self-supervised learning (SSL) methods, specifically focusing on BarlowTwins and VICReg, which are crucial for learning good representations in SSL.
- Theoretical insights on the implicit bias of BarlowTwins and VICReg loss are provided, which guide the development of more principled recommendations for improving sample efficiency in SSL.
- Empirical demonstration that low-dimensional projector heads are sufficient with appropriate regularization, contrary to existing heuristics.
- The paper shows that using more augmentations per sample improves representation quality and trainability, leading to better features emerging earlier in the training.
- Theoretical insights are supported by practical pretraining recommendations that improve wall-clock time and performance on CIFAR-10/STL-10 datasets using a ResNet-50 backbone.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making the complex theoretical concepts accessible.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is limited to specific SSL methods (BarlowTwins and VICReg) and does not explore other methods like SWAV, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the theoretical insights.
- The theoretical analysis is based on the assumption of a linear model, which may not hold for non-linear models like ResNet, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other SSL methods, particularly in terms of convergence speed and final accuracy, which is crucial for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The experimental results are limited to small datasets (CIFAR-10/STL-10), and it is unclear how the proposed methods would perform on larger datasets like ImageNet.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, which could provide a more balanced view of the contributions and potential challenges.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational costs and practical implications of using multiple augmentations, especially in terms of training time and resource requirements.

**Questions:**
- How do the proposed methods compare in terms of convergence speed and final accuracy with other SSL methods, especially on larger datasets like ImageNet?
- Can the theoretical insights be extended to other SSL methods, such as SWAV?
- How does the use of multiple augmentations impact the computational costs, and what are the practical implications in terms of training time and resource requirements?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons with other SSL methods and evaluations on larger datasets?
- What are the limitations of the proposed methods, and how do these limitations affect the practical applicability and generalizability of the findings?
