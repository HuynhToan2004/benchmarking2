 **Summary:**
The paper introduces UniVis, a framework designed to handle various computer vision tasks by converting them into image completion tasks using a pretrained Stable Diffusion model. The model is trained using instruction tuning to adapt to different tasks, with the ability to generate outputs for each given task. The paper claims that UniVis can handle a wide range of tasks including visual understanding, low-level image processing, and conditional image generation. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, as it closely resembles existing methods like Painter and PromptDiffusion. The paper's experimental results show that UniVis can perform on par with or better than these methods, but the improvements are marginal and the method's generalization to unseen tasks is limited.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, UniVis, which aims to unify various computer vision tasks using a single model, a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the proposed approach and its implementation details.
- The experimental results demonstrate that UniVis can perform on par with or better than existing methods, indicating its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and includes a thorough discussion of related work.
- The use of Stable Diffusion as the backbone for UniVis is a promising choice, leveraging its strong generative and reasoning abilities.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach, particularly the reliance on a fixed architecture for the denoising U-Net which might not be suitable for all tasks.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as it closely resembles existing methods like Painter and PromptDiffusion, with minor modifications such as the use of whole-image masking instead of patch-level masking.
- The paper does not convincingly demonstrate the generalization of UniVis to unseen tasks, which is a critical aspect for a model claimed to handle a wide range of tasks.
- The experimental results are not compelling, with marginal improvements over existing methods and a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant models like InstructDiffusion.
- The paper's claims about the universality of UniVis are not well-supported by the experimental results, which are limited to a few tasks and do not convincingly demonstrate the model's ability to handle a wide range of tasks.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to the Painter method when trained on the same computing resources?
- Can the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and the Painter method, especially in terms of the use of whole-image masking versus patch-level masking?
- How does the model handle tasks that are not directly related to image generation, such as depth estimation or denoising?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and inference processes, particularly how the model is trained and how it generalizes to unseen tasks?
- What are the specific advantages of using UniVis over other methods like Painter or PromptDiffusion, and how does it address the limitations of these methods?
- How does the model handle different types of input and output data, such as RGB images versus other modalities like depth maps or segmentation masks?
