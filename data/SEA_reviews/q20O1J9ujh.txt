 **Summary:**
The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of various foundation models (FMs) in the context of video understanding tasks. It benchmarks six different FMs across three key video understanding tasks: action recognition, temporal localization, and spatiotemporal localization, using eight datasets and four adaptation methods. The authors propose a new metric, the VideoGLUE score, to assess the effectiveness of FMs in adapting to these tasks. The study reveals that video-native FMs generally outperform image-native FMs in video tasks, and that both tasks and adaptation methods are crucial for evaluating FMs. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited scope, lack of novelty, and insufficient empirical evidence to support its conclusions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, providing a clear and comprehensive evaluation of various foundation models (FMs) in the context of video understanding tasks.
- The experiments are well-designed, with a focus on three hallmark tasks and eight datasets, which are well-received by the community.
- The proposed VideoGLUE score provides a useful tool for evaluating the performance of FMs in video tasks, contributing to the advancement of video understanding in foundation models.
- The paper is the first of its kind to evaluate video understanding capabilities of FMs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a detailed evaluation of six different FMs using eight datasets, which is a comprehensive approach to evaluating the performance of FMs in video tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on video understanding is limited, as it does not include multimodal tasks such as video question answering, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The evaluation of FMs is limited to six models, which may not be sufficient to draw robust conclusions about the capabilities of FMs in video understanding tasks.
- The paper does not introduce any new FMs or adaptation methods, which could limit its impact and novelty.
- The empirical evidence provided in the paper is insufficient to support the conclusions drawn, particularly regarding the generalization of the findings to other tasks and datasets.
- The paper's scope is limited to video tasks, which may not fully capture the capabilities of FMs in other domains such as language processing.
- The paper's conclusions are not adequately supported by the data presented, and the metrics used, such as the VideoGLUE score, are not sufficiently robust or comprehensive.

**Questions:**
- How do the conclusions drawn from this study apply to other tasks and datasets beyond those evaluated in the paper?
- What are the main reasons why video-native FMs outperform image-native FMs in video tasks?
- Could you provide more details on the selection criteria for the six FMs used in the study?
- Why was the decision made to evaluate only three video understanding tasks, and how were these tasks selected?
- How do the results of this study compare to other benchmarks such as those presented in other papers like "Evaluating Foundation Models for Video Understanding"?
- Could you clarify the discrepancies in the results reported in the paper, particularly the performance of CoCa and CLIP in different tasks?
- How do the conclusions drawn from this study relate to the broader field of foundation models, and what implications do they have for future research in this area?
