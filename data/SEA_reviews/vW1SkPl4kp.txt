 **Summary:**
The paper explores the integration of Iterated Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) into reinforcement learning (RL) with function approximation, focusing on both linear and general function approximations. It introduces two algorithms, ICVaR-L and ICVaR-G, for linear and general function approximations, respectively, along with a third algorithm, ICVaR-HF, which integrates human feedback. The paper provides theoretical analyses, including regret bounds, and discusses the challenges and potential solutions in the context of function approximation and human feedback. It also extends the application of CVaR in RLHF, where the reward function is unknown, and the agent only receives comparisons between trajectories.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation and organization, making it easy to follow.
- The introduction of Iterated CVaR into reinforcement learning with function approximation is novel and significant, particularly in the context of risk-sensitive RL.
- The paper provides a comprehensive literature review, effectively positioning the work within the current research landscape.
- The proposed algorithms are well-motivated and the theoretical analysis is sound, providing a solid foundation for the study.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of reinforcement learning, which is the integration of risk-sensitive objectives with function approximation.
- The authors have provided a detailed discussion on the challenges and solutions for the ICVaR-RL problem, which is both technically challenging and practically relevant.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks experimental validation, which could help in understanding the practical implications and effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
- There is a need for more detailed discussions and comparisons with existing works, particularly in terms of the technical contributions and the novelty of the proposed algorithms.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed algorithms.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of the clarity and depth of the theoretical explanations, especially in sections where the results are not immediately clear.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing regret minimization results for risk-neutral RL with function approximation.
- There are several minor typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly Assumption 1 and Assumption 3, and discuss their implications on the results?
- How does the paper handle the comparison between trajectories in the RLHF setting, and what are the implications of this approach on the overall algorithm performance?
- In the context of the proposed algorithms, how does the choice of the parameter α affect the performance, and what are the implications of this choice in practical applications?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency and space complexity of the algorithms, especially in comparison to existing methods?
- How does the paper address the challenges of risk-sensitive RL with function approximation, and what are the key technical contributions that differentiate this work from existing literature?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed algorithms and discuss how these might be mitigated or addressed?
