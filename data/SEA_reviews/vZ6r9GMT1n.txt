 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel defense mechanism against query-based black-box attacks by adding random noise to the hidden features of deep neural networks. This approach, termed Randomized Feature Defense (RFD), is designed to perturb the model's output, making it difficult for attackers to accurately infer adversarial samples. The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of RFD against various types of attacks, including score-based and decision-based attacks. The methodology is simple, effective, and does not require adversarial training, making it applicable to any pre-trained model. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope, focusing only on query-based attacks and not addressing adaptive attacks, which are crucial in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-understand language, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, Randomized Feature Defense (RFD), is simple, effective, and does not require adversarial training, making it applicable to any pre-trained model.
- Theoretical analysis and empirical evidence are provided to support the effectiveness of RFD against both score-based and decision-based attacks.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation of RFD, demonstrating its effectiveness against various types of attacks.
- The methodology of RFD is lightweight and does not require significant computational resources, making it suitable for resource-constrained environments.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the robustness of RFD against different types of attacks, offering insights into the performance of the defense mechanism.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited as it only addresses query-based attacks and does not consider adaptive attacks, which are crucial in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of different noise distributions on the performance of RFD, which could be a significant limitation.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to Gaussian noise, and the paper does not explore how other noise distributions might affect the performance of RFD.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art defenses, which could help in understanding its relative effectiveness.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to specific models and datasets, which might not generalize well to other models or datasets.
- The paper lacks a discussion on how RFD performs against other types of attacks, such as transfer attacks, which are crucial in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not address the computational overhead introduced by RFD, which could be a significant limitation in practical deployments.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on how the proposed method could be adapted to address adaptive attacks?
- How does the performance of RFD vary with different noise distributions?
- Could the authors provide a comparison of RFD with other state-of-the-art defenses to better understand its relative effectiveness?
- How does RFD perform against other types of attacks, such as transfer attacks?
- Can the authors discuss the computational overhead introduced by RFD and how it might affect practical deployments?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of models and datasets, to better understand the generalizability of the results?
