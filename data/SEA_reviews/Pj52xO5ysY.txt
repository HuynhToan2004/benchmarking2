 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel attention-based multiple instance classification model (AMIC) for conducting interpretable word-level sentiment analysis (SA) using only document sentiment labels. The model decomposes sentiment analysis into interpretable outputs, providing context weighting, indication of word neutrality, and negation. It utilizes a self-attention mechanism with relative position embeddings to capture local dependencies effectively. The model's performance is evaluated on two document sentiment classification datasets, showcasing competitive prediction performance and interpretability. However, the paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art models and does not include a broader range of datasets, which limits its generalizability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis using a word-level context-based method, which is more interpretable compared to other models.
- The AMIC model is designed to provide interpretable outputs, which are crucial for understanding the decision-making process of AI systems.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The model's performance is competitive, achieving state-of-the-art results on two document sentiment classification datasets.
- The paper provides detailed analysis of model interpretation, which is crucial for understanding the model's decision-making process.
- The use of self-attention with relative position embeddings effectively captures local dependencies, which is beneficial for sentiment analysis tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art models, which limits the understanding of the model's relative performance.
- The interpretability of the model is not convincingly demonstrated, particularly in terms of how the model's outputs can be easily understood by humans.
- The model's performance is not evaluated on a broader range of datasets, which limits its generalizability.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the model, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential biases.
- The methodology section is poorly written, making it difficult to follow and understand the model's components and their contributions to interpretability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the model, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential biases.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art models to better understand the model's relative performance?
- How does the model handle different types of negations, such as preceding and succeeding negations?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of model interpretability, particularly how the model's outputs can be easily understood by humans?
- How does the model perform on a broader range of datasets, and what are the implications of its performance on these datasets?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the model, including potential biases and applicability in different domains?
- How does the model handle different types of sentiment words, such as positive and negative sentiment words?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the model's training and inference time, particularly how these times compare to other models?
