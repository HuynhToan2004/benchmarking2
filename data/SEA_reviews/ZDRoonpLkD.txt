 **Summary:**
The paper investigates enhancing the training and inference of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) for solving combinatorial problems, specifically focusing on the Boolean Satisfiability (SAT) problem. It introduces a curriculum training procedure that progressively increases problem complexity and message passing iterations, alongside optimizations such as decimation and initial embedding sampling. These enhancements are aimed at reducing training time and improving problem-solving efficiency. The paper also explores the similarities between GNNs and other approximation algorithms like Belief Propagation and Semidefinite Programming relaxations, suggesting potential insights for future research. However, the paper's empirical results are somewhat limited, and the improvements over existing methods are not convincingly demonstrated.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to training GNNs for combinatorial problems, specifically focusing on the SAT problem, which is a significant area of interest.
- It introduces a curriculum training procedure that effectively reduces training time and increases the percentage of solved problems, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a good background section that provides necessary context for understanding the proposed methods.
- The proposed enhancements are motivated by possible connections to approximation algorithms, which adds a theoretical depth to the practical improvements.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation that shows improvements over the baseline without the curriculum, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the specific improvements over existing methods, particularly in terms of the impact of the curriculum training procedure on training time and the percentage of solved problems.
- The evaluation is limited to a single dataset, which may not be sufficient to convincingly demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not adequately compare its results with state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the perceived effectiveness of the proposed enhancements.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the technical contributions of the paper, particularly in the methodology and the empirical results, which could lead to confusion about the actual improvements achieved.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of its approach, such as the scalability of the proposed methods to larger or more complex problems.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific improvements over the baseline without the curriculum in terms of training time and percentage of solved problems?
- How does the proposed curriculum training procedure compare to other training methods in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific GNN architecture used and the training hyperparameters?
- How does the proposed approach scale to larger or more complex problems, and what are the potential limitations in terms of computational resources and problem complexity?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical connections between GNNs and other approximation algorithms like Belief Propagation and Semidefinite Programming relaxations?
- What are the potential applications of the proposed enhancements beyond the SAT problem, and how might these enhancements be adapted for other combinatorial optimization problems?
