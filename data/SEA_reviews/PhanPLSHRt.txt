 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel semi-supervised learning (SSL) algorithm named EXCOST, which leverages exemplar-contrastive self-training and a Category-Invariant Loss (CIL) to generate pseudo-labels for unlabeled data. EXCOST's approach is grounded in cognitive psychology theories, particularly the concept of typicality gradients, and aims to mitigate confirmation bias by generating pseudo-labels based on both confidence and exemplar similarity. The paper evaluates EXCOST on various datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR-10, and SVHN, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness across different data conditions. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its lack of novelty in certain aspects, such as the use of contrastive learning and data augmentation techniques.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the inclusion of cognitive psychology theories adds a novel perspective to the field of semi-supervised learning.
- The proposed method achieves good performance on benchmark datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in real-world applications.
- The use of cognitive psychology theories to guide the design of the algorithm is innovative and provides a unique approach to SSL.
- The paper is well-organized, and the experimental results are solid, showing that the proposed method is effective and robust.
- The integration of prototype theory and exemplar theory into the SSL algorithm provides a novel approach to pseudo-label generation, which is crucial for SSL.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art SSL methods, which could have strengthened its position in the field.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable as it seems to resemble existing SSL methods that use contrastive learning and data augmentation.
- The experimental results, while good, are not groundbreaking, and the paper does not provide sufficient insights into the failure cases or the robustness of the method under different conditions.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the hyperparameters used and their impact on the performance of the method.
- The paper's claims about the novelty and effectiveness of the Category-Invariant Loss (CIL) are not convincingly supported, and the comparison with other SSL methods is insufficient.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by including more detailed explanations and visualizations of the proposed method, especially in the algorithm descriptions and experimental setups.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to state-of-the-art SSL methods on more complex datasets like ImageNet?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the failure cases of the proposed method and discuss how these failures can be addressed?
- How does the method perform when different data augmentation techniques are used, and what is the impact of these choices on the performance?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the Category-Invariant Loss (CIL) with other SSL methods, including a discussion on the novelty and effectiveness of CIL?
- How does the method handle different types of data, such as text or tabular data, and what are the implications for its application in these domains?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the exemplar similarity in the labeling algorithm and discuss its impact on the performance of the method?
