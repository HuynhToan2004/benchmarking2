 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel algorithm for estimating arbitrary functions of a weighted adjacency matrix using general graph random features (g-GRFs), which extends the capabilities of the Graph Random Features (GRF) algorithm. The g-GRFs employ a neural network parameterized modulation function to approximate kernel functions, offering scalability and potentially faster computational times. The approach is supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results across various graph kernels and tasks, demonstrating the method's effectiveness and scalability. However, the paper's theoretical contributions are questioned for their novelty and depth, and the experimental results are seen as not sufficiently compelling to support the claimed improvements over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise language, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- The algorithm for approximating arbitrary functions of a weighted adjacency matrix is novel and innovative, potentially leading to significant advancements in the field.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in certain scenarios.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including the computation of symmetric modulation functions and the empirical Rademacher complexity bound, which supports the theoretical claims.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and logical flow, making it easy to understand the main ideas and contributions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is questioned as it appears to be a simple modification of the existing GRF algorithm, with the main innovation being the use of a neural network parameterized modulation function.
- The theoretical results, particularly the proof of Theorem 2.2, are seen as trivial and not sufficiently novel.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods, especially in large-scale settings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its practical applicability and impact.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have strengthened the argument for the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method.
- The paper suffers from some typographical and formatting errors, which could detract from its professionalism and clarity.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples of the neural network parameterized modulation function and how it is used in practice?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in large-scale settings?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the modulation function in the theoretical analysis, especially in relation to the proof of Theorem 2.2?
- How does the proposed method handle the approximation of kernel functions, particularly in terms of accuracy and bias?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the neural network architecture and the training parameters used?
- Could the authors address the concerns regarding the novelty and depth of the theoretical contributions, particularly in relation to existing work in the field?
