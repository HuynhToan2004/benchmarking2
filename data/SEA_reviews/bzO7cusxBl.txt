 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method named Maximizing Optimized Kernel Dependence (MOKD) for cross-domain few-shot classification, focusing on maximizing the dependence between representations and labels while minimizing dependencies among all samples. This approach leverages a bi-level optimization framework to optimize the kernel used in the Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion (HSIC) to enhance its test power and effectively measure the dependence between representations and labels. Extensive experiments on the Meta-Dataset benchmark demonstrate that MOKD achieves better generalization performance and learns better data clusters, although it shows limited improvement over existing methods like URL and 2LM.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to optimizing the kernel used in HSIC to maximize its test power, enhancing its capability to measure the dependence between representations and labels.
- The proposed method, MOKD, effectively addresses the issue of high similarities between NCC-learned representations of samples from different classes, improving generalization performance in cross-domain few-shot classification.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and its implementation, including a detailed algorithm and theoretical analysis.
- The experiments are comprehensive and well-executed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method through various ablation studies and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper provides a clear connection between the NCC loss and HSIC, offering a novel perspective on the optimization of the NCC loss.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and the time required for kernel optimization.
- The experimental results show only marginal improvements over existing methods like URL and 2LM, raising questions about the practical significance of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not provide a thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have strengthened the paper's argument.
- The paper does not provide sufficient visualizations or detailed analyses to support the claims made about the effectiveness of the method in reducing high similarities among samples from different classes.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed method.
