 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Joint Autoregressive Mixture (JAM) framework, which integrates pretrained autoregressive models for text-to-image generation. The framework includes three methods: model merging, width concatenation, and cross-attention, each designed to fuse two pretrained models into a single model capable of generating multimodal outputs. The JAM model is trained using a hybrid dataset and undergoes instruction tuning to enhance its performance in mixed-modal generation tasks. The paper evaluates the JAM model against existing models and demonstrates its effectiveness through experiments. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods, and the paper lacks a thorough discussion on the training costs and scalability of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methods and experiments.
- The proposed methods, including model merging, width concatenation, and cross-attention, are simple and effective for integrating different models into a unified architecture.
- The JAM model demonstrates good performance in generating multimodal outputs, showing potential for future research in this area.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the JAM model against existing models, demonstrating its effectiveness in multimodal generation tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed methods is limited as they are similar to existing approaches such as K-Mix and K-Weight.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the training costs and scalability of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of computational resources and data requirements.
- The evaluation metrics used are not sufficiently robust or diverse, relying primarily on perplexity and not including other relevant metrics such as FID or CLIPScore.
- The paper does not adequately compare the JAM model with other state-of-the-art models, especially those that use similar architectures or training strategies.
- There are concerns about the scalability of the JAM model, particularly in terms of its ability to handle larger datasets or more complex generation tasks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between the proposed methods and existing approaches, particularly in terms of novelty and effectiveness?
- How does the JAM model perform when scaled up to larger models or more complex tasks? What are the potential challenges and limitations?
- Could the authors provide more detailed training and evaluation results, including the training time, memory usage, and inference speed of the JAM model?
- How does the JAM model compare with other state-of-the-art models in terms of generation quality and efficiency?
- Could the authors discuss the potential applications of the JAM model in real-world scenarios, such as multimodal conversational agents or content creation tools?
