 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Plan-Seq-Learn (PSL), a novel method for solving long-horizon robotics tasks using a combination of large language models (LLMs), motion planning, and reinforcement learning (RL). PSL decomposes tasks into sequential stages, with each stage involving a high-level language plan, vision-based plan tracking, and low-level RL control. This approach allows for efficient learning of contact-rich behaviors and successful task completion, even in complex, contact-rich environments. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive experiments on four benchmark environments, showing superior performance compared to existing baselines. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the methodological description, and the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach that combines the strengths of LLMs for high-level planning, motion planning for skill sequencing, and RL for low-level robot control strategies, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The experimental results are strong, showing that PSL can solve long-horizon robotics tasks from scratch, outperforming state-of-the-art baselines across four benchmark suites.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides detailed explanations of the methodology, including the use of LLMs for planning, vision-based motion planning for task plan tracking, and RL for learning low-level control strategies.
- The methodology is flexible and can be applied to various tasks, including long-horizon tasks, and is shown to be more sample-efficient than previous methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the method, particularly the interaction between the LLM and the RL agent, which is crucial for understanding the method's operation.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as similar methods, such as MoPA-RL and GUAPO, have been previously proposed.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the challenges associated with long-horizon tasks, such as the need for a clear and detailed plan from the LLM, which is not always feasible in real-world scenarios.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently robust, with concerns about the generalizability of the findings and the potential for overfitting due to the use of a single LLM (GPT-4).
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the approach and the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed description of the interaction between the LLM and the RL agent, including how the LLM's outputs are used to guide the RL agent's actions?
- How does the method handle the potential for cascading failures, especially in long-horizon tasks where the planning horizon is not known in advance?
- What is the specific role of the LLM in the planning process, and how does it ensure the correctness and completeness of the plans it generates?
- How does the method handle the potential for hallucinations in the LLM's outputs, and what measures are in place to prevent the RL agent from learning incorrect behaviors?
- Could you clarify the experimental setup, particularly the conditions under which the RL agent learns from demonstrations versus from direct interaction with the environment?
- How does the method perform when the LLM's outputs are not optimal or when the task description is incomplete or incorrect?
