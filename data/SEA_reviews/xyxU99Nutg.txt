 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel test-time normalization technique, UnMix-TNS, aimed at addressing the label temporal correlation in non-i.i.d. test environments. The method involves decomposing batch normalization statistics into multiple components, each representing distinct distributions, which are then refined based on the similarity between instance-wise statistics and each component. This approach is designed to handle temporal correlations in label space, a significant issue in real-world scenarios where test data streams are non-i.i.d. The paper provides extensive empirical evidence, demonstrating the effectiveness of UnMix-TNS across various benchmarks, including both corruption and natural shifts in image classification and video datasets. However, concerns are raised about the theoretical justification of the method, the sensitivity to hyperparameters, and the computational overhead.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear figures and detailed explanations of the methodology.
- The problem addressed is significant and relevant, particularly in scenarios where test data streams are non-i.i.d.
- The proposed method, UnMix-TNS, is novel and well-motivated, providing a new approach to test-time normalization that is applicable across a variety of settings.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and show significant improvements over baseline methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's performance under different conditions, including ablation studies that help in understanding the method's components and their impact.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough theoretical justification for why the proposed method should work, which is crucial for understanding its effectiveness and limitations.
- The sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters, such as the number of components and the learning rate, is not adequately addressed, which could limit its practical application.
- The computational overhead of the method is not clearly explained, raising concerns about its efficiency in real-world scenarios.
- The method's effectiveness in scenarios where the label temporal correlation is weak or non-existent is not discussed, which could limit its applicability.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with some relevant works, such as TTN, which could provide a more robust evaluation of its performance.
- The method's complexity and the additional computational requirements it introduces could be a barrier to its adoption in resource-constrained environments.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical justification for why the proposed method should work, particularly in terms of how it addresses the label temporal correlation effectively?
- How sensitive is the method to different hyperparameters, such as the number of components and the learning rate, and what are the recommended values for these parameters in practical applications?
- Could the authors clarify the computational overhead of the method and discuss how it scales with the size of the test data stream?
- In what scenarios would the method be most effective, and where might it struggle? What are the conditions under which the label temporal correlation is strong or weak, and how does the method perform in these different scenarios?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art test-time adaptation methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the test data streams used and the computational environment in which the experiments were conducted?
