 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for evaluating the invariance of language models to specific linguistic capabilities, such as synonym and typo invariance. It proposes a methodology for perturbing input texts to generate invariance examples and evaluates the degree of shared invariance between models using metrics like Hard-SCoPE and Soft-SCoPE. The framework is tested across various models and tasks, including sentiment analysis and language modeling, and reveals insights into the effects of model design choices and the similarity of models from different families. The paper also explores the application of this framework to commercial black-box APIs, comparing them to better-understood models. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its limited scope in terms of the number of linguistic capabilities explored and the depth of insights provided.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to evaluating the invariance of language models, which is a significant and timely area of research given the current focus on large language models.
- The proposed metrics, such as Hard-SCoPE and Soft-SCoPE, provide a novel perspective on evaluating the similarity of models, which is a departure from traditional methods that rely on accuracy or agreement-based metrics.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the goal function, search method, transformations, and constraints used in the perturbation process.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The insights provided, such as the impact of different linguistic capabilities and model sizes on shared invariance, are valuable for understanding the behavior of language models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on two linguistic capabilities, which limits the generalizability of the findings. Expanding the scope to include a broader range of capabilities could provide more comprehensive insights.
- The correlation between the proposed metrics and existing metrics, such as agreement-based metrics, is not well-explained, which could confuse readers about the effectiveness of the new metrics.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the computational cost and the scalability of the perturbation process across different models and datasets.
- The insights provided are sometimes limited by the simplicity of the perturbations used, such as synonym and typo invariance, which may not fully capture the complexity of the models under study.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing works that use similar perturbations, such as those by La Malfa & Kwiatkowska (2022), which could have strengthened the argument for the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other perturbation-based methods for evaluating the similarity of models, such as those by La Malfa & Kwiatkowska (2022)?
- Can the authors clarify the correlation between the proposed metrics and existing metrics, particularly in terms of how they relate to the effectiveness of the new metrics?
- How does the perturbation process scale with the size of the model and the dataset, and what are the computational costs associated with this process?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for the perturbation process to introduce noise or bias into the model's behavior, particularly in cases where the perturbations may not be perfectly aligned with the linguistic capabilities under study?
- Could the authors expand on the insights provided by the proposed metrics, particularly in terms of how they might inform the design or training of language models to improve their invariance properties?
