 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PASTA, a post-hoc attention steering approach aimed at directing the attention of large language models (LLMs) to user-specified parts of the input text. This method involves identifying a subset of attention heads and applying precise attention reweighting to these heads, enhancing the model's ability to follow user instructions or integrate new knowledge from user inputs. The approach is evaluated on various tasks, showing significant improvements over baseline prompting strategies, with an average accuracy improvement of 22% for LLAMA-7B. The method is applied at inference time, requiring no model parameter changes, and is shown to improve model performance on a variety of tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a clear motivation for the research.
- The proposed method, PASTA, is novel and addresses a significant problem in the field by allowing users to emphasize specific parts of the input text, which is crucial for LLMs to understand and follow user instructions.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the method, showing significant improvements over baseline prompting strategies.
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the method's impact on model performance, including an ablation study on the number of heads steered and the coefficient of reweighting.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, making it easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly how it might fail or perform poorly in certain scenarios.
- The method's reliance on a pre-trained model's attention scores raises concerns about the model's ability to generalize to new tasks or domains where it has not been trained.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the method, such as the potential for misuse or bias.
- The method's applicability to larger models like LLaMA-2 and the computational overhead of the profiling step are not discussed.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive evaluation of the method's impact on model performance in terms of accuracy and fluency, which could be misleading if the model generates answers that are factually correct but not grammatically correct.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when applied to larger models like LLaMA-2?
- Is there a way to adapt the method to handle different types of emphasis markers, such as italics or bold text, beyond underlining?
- How does the profiling process affect the inference time, and is there a significant computational overhead?
- Could the method be extended to handle multiple emphasis marks, and how would this affect the performance and complexity of the method?
- How does the method compare to other attention steering methods, such as those that use different attention mechanisms or different types of emphasis markers?
