 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two novel transformer architectures, ReLiT and AReLiT, designed to enhance the efficiency and scalability of transformers in reinforcement learning (RL) tasks. These architectures address the limitations of traditional transformers by integrating a gating mechanism to manage memory and a low-rank approximation to reduce computational complexity. The authors demonstrate that these modifications significantly improve inference speed and memory usage compared to existing methods like GTrXL. The paper presents empirical results showing that ReLiT and AReLiT can match or surpass the performance of GTrXL in various RL environments, including T-Maze, CartPole, Mystery Path, and Memory Maze. However, the paper's scope is limited to simple RL environments, and the generalizability and applicability of the proposed methods to more complex scenarios remain unclear.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem by focusing on the inference cost and memory usage of transformers, which are crucial for their practical application in reinforcement learning (RL).
- The authors introduce innovative solutions such as ReLiT and AReLiT, which effectively manage memory and computational complexity, leading to substantial improvements in inference speed and memory usage.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with comprehensive empirical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The methodology of using low-rank approximation to reduce the computational complexity is novel and shows potential for broader applications.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with the state-of-the-art (GTrXL), highlighting the advantages of the proposed methods in terms of inference cost and memory usage.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other relevant methods, such as RWKV, LRU, and S4, which also address similar issues in transformers.
- The experiments are limited to simple RL environments, raising concerns about the generalizability and applicability of the proposed methods to more complex scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the potential impact of the gating mechanism on the stability of the model, particularly in long-range dependency tasks.
- There is a lack of clarity on the choice of hyperparameters, such as the number of layers and heads in the transformer, which could affect the performance and scalability of the model.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other methods, such as RWKV, LRU, and S4, to better understand the relative advantages and disadvantages of their approach?
- How does the gating mechanism affect the stability and performance of the model, particularly in long-range dependency tasks?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters, such as the number of layers and heads in the transformer, and discuss the potential impact on the performance and scalability of the model?
- How do the proposed methods compare in terms of computational complexity and memory usage with other low-rank approximation methods, such as incremental SVD?
- Can the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their work, particularly in the context of transformers in RL?
