 **Summary:**
The paper explores the representation of invariant and equivariant neural networks, specifically focusing on the ambiguities in latent representations. It argues that naive interpretations of equivariant latent spaces can lead to incorrect conclusions and decreased performance in downstream tasks. To address this, the authors propose using invariant projections of latent spaces to obtain unambiguous latent embeddings. The paper presents two case studies, one involving a permutation equivariant variational autoencoder for molecule generation and another using a rotation-equivariant representation for image classification. The authors demonstrate that these invariant projections can improve the performance of downstream tasks by retaining more information in the invariant representation compared to equivariant counterparts. The paper also discusses the challenges in designing invariant projections and their implications for the interpretation of latent representations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The authors address a significant and relevant problem in the field of equivariant representation learning, which is crucial for understanding and interpreting latent representations.
- The paper includes practical examples that demonstrate the application of the proposed methods, enhancing the understanding of the theoretical concepts presented.
- The paper introduces a novel perspective on the use of invariant projections of latent spaces, which could be a useful tool for improving the performance of downstream tasks.
- The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work, highlighting the novelty and originality of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the specific invariant projection used, which could lead to confusion about its effectiveness and applicability.
- There is a lack of detailed experimental results and comparisons with existing methods, which could undermine the claims made about the superiority of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its approach, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experimental validation, including a broader range of datasets and architectures to demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed methods.
- There is a need for more detailed discussions on the practical implementation of the proposed methods, including the choice of parameters and the computational costs associated with the use of invariant projections.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition and construction of the specific invariant projection used in your experiments? How does it compare to other possible projections in terms of effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to more complex datasets and architectures, such as those involving higher-order symmetries or more complex equivariant models?
- Could you provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons with existing methods and a more comprehensive analysis of the performance of your proposed methods?
- How do the authors address the issue of ambiguity in latent representations, especially when multiple latent representations are possible?
- In light of the limitations discussed, how do the authors plan to address these challenges in future work, and what are the expected outcomes or benefits of these improvements?
