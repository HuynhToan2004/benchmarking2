 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel robust game theoretic optimizer, GTSONO, designed to enhance the training of neural ODEs by interpreting the optimization process as a min-max optimal control problem. The methodology leverages second-order DDP and Kronecker factorization to decompose matrices into vectors, providing a more efficient computational framework. The paper demonstrates the robustness of GTSONO through experiments on CIFAR and SVHN datasets, showing improved performance over baseline methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the methodology, and the robustness of the results, particularly in terms of reproducibility and the empirical evidence supporting the claims of robustness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the proposed method, including the use of game theory and differential dynamic programming to optimize neural ODEs.
- The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including proofs and detailed derivations, which support the claims of the method's effectiveness and robustness.
- The paper includes empirical evidence that demonstrates the method's superiority over baseline methods, showing improved performance and robustness in adversarial training scenarios.
- The methodology is innovative, combining game theory with differential dynamic programming to optimize neural ODEs, which is a novel approach that has the potential to significantly impact the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for using a min-max formulation for adversarial training, and the relationship between the proposed method and existing methods such as SNOpt and DDP is not adequately explained.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodology section, particularly in the explanation of the Kronecker factorization and its application to neural ODEs.
- The experimental setup and results are not robust, with some results showing marginal improvements and a lack of reproducibility due to missing details and unclear methodologies.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of robust overfitting, which is a significant concern in adversarial training scenarios.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with a sufficient number of baselines, particularly with other robust training methods, which limits the understanding of its effectiveness in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the motivation behind using a min-max formulation for adversarial training and how it differs from existing methods like SNOpt and DDP?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of robust overfitting in adversarial training scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations and examples of how the Kronecker factorization is applied to neural ODEs, especially in terms of its computational efficiency and practical implementation?
- How does the proposed method compare with other robust training methods in terms of effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results and comparisons, particularly in terms of the training time and memory consumption, to better understand the practical implications of using the proposed method?
