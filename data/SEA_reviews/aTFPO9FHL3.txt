 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Todyformer, a novel dynamic graph neural network designed to address the challenges of over-smoothing and over-squashing in dynamic graphs. Todyformer integrates a local encoding mechanism using MPNNs for structural and temporal features and a global encoding mechanism using Transformers to capture long-range dependencies. This hybrid approach allows for the systematic mitigation of over-smoothing and over-squashing, enhancing the model's expressiveness. The paper presents extensive experimental results demonstrating Todyformer's superior performance over existing models on various dynamic graph benchmark datasets. Despite its innovative approach, concerns were raised about the novelty of the model's architecture and the need for more rigorous comparisons with other state-of-the-art models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of dynamic graph learning, focusing on the challenges of over-smoothing and over-squashing in dynamic graphs.
- The proposed method is well-motivated and technically sound, with a clear presentation of the model architecture and its components.
- Extensive experimental results are presented, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various dynamic graph benchmark datasets.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with a clear explanation of the methodology and experimental results.
- The authors have provided detailed ablation studies to analyze the impact of different model components, enhancing the credibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art models, particularly those that also address the challenges of over-smoothing and over-squashing in dynamic graphs.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it seems to resemble existing models like Dygformer and GraphMixer, which also use similar architectural components.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the model, especially concerning the computational cost and memory requirements, which could be a significant limitation for larger and more complex dynamic graphs.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with the paper relying on a limited number of datasets and not including error bars or standard deviations in the results, which could undermine the reliability of the findings.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which detract from its professional quality.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost and memory requirements of the proposed model, particularly in comparison to other state-of-the-art models?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and efficiency with other models that address the challenges of over-smoothing and over-squashing in dynamic graphs?
- Could the authors clarify the motivation behind the specific design choices of the model, particularly the use of MPNNs for local encoding and Transformers for global encoding?
- How does the model handle the scalability issues, especially in terms of computational cost and memory requirements, as the size of the dynamic graph increases?
- In the experimental results, why were only a few datasets used, and could the authors consider including more datasets to strengthen the validity of their findings?
