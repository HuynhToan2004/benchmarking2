 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Latent Diffusion-Constrained Q-Learning (LDCQ) for offline reinforcement learning (RL), leveraging latent diffusion models to address the challenge of extrapolation errors in offline RL. The method models trajectory sequences using a latent diffusion model, which is then used to model the behavioral policy and learn a Q-function, constraining the batch to ensure that the learned Q-function is within the support of the dataset. The approach is evaluated on the D4RL benchmark, showing superior performance compared to several baselines, particularly in long-horizon sparse reward tasks. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the methodology, the novelty of the approach, and the empirical evaluation, suggesting that the paper may benefit from further refinement and clarity in presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology.
- The empirical results are strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various offline RL tasks.
- The use of latent diffusion models to model the behavioral policy and learn a Q-function in offline RL is novel and innovative.
- The paper provides a detailed explanation of the methodology, including the use of a beta-VAE to learn a latent space and a diffusion model to model the behavioral policy.
- The experimental results are comprehensive, showing the superiority of the proposed method over existing baselines.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some parts, particularly in the description of the methodology and the role of the diffusion model in the learning process.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant new contributions.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of extrapolation error, which is a significant challenge in offline RL.
- The method's reliance on a strong Q-function for effective learning may limit its applicability in scenarios where the Q-function is not robust.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, particularly in the medium-expert and expert-level tasks.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis and discussion on the limitations and societal impact of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and operation of the diffusion model in the learning process, particularly how it models the behavioral policy and constrains the batch?
- How does the method ensure that the learned Q-function is within the support of the dataset, and what measures are taken to avoid extrapolation errors?
- In the experimental setup, how are the hyperparameters, such as the number of diffusion steps and the sequence horizon, chosen, and what is their impact on the results?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations and examples of the latent diffusion models used in the method, especially how they are trained and how they differ from other diffusion models?
- How does the proposed method compare to other goal-conditioned methods, and can it be adapted to handle discrete action spaces?
- Given the concerns about the novelty and clarity of the method, how does the paper address these issues, and what steps can be taken to improve the presentation and understanding of the proposed approach?
