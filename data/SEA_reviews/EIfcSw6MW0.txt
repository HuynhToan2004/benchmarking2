 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for training deep neural networks (DNNs) to achieve certified robustness while preserving high clean accuracy. The method employs a pre-trained vanilla model and applies Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to its weight matrices, leveraging the rotation matrices to guide the training of a certified model. This approach aims to maintain the clean accuracy of the vanilla model by ensuring the similarity of rotation matrices between the vanilla and certified models. The paper also proposes a low-rank approximation of the rotation matrices to reduce computational costs. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on the CIFAR-10 dataset, showing improvements in clean accuracy and a slight reduction in certified accuracy. However, the paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods and does not provide a clear justification for the choice of the vanilla model as a guide.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of certified robustness, focusing on maintaining high clean accuracy while achieving robustness.
- The proposed method is simple, well-explained, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and theoretical justification for its approach.
- The experimental results are promising, showing improvements in clean accuracy and robustness, and the method's computational cost is lower compared to other state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and concise presentation that makes it accessible to a broad audience.
- The method's ability to improve clean accuracy while maintaining robustness is a significant contribution to the field, as it addresses a critical challenge in certified robust training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of certified accuracy and computational cost.
- The method's dependence on a pre-trained vanilla model raises questions about its generalizability and applicability to different datasets and model architectures.
- The justification for using the vanilla model as a guide and the choice of the low-rank approximation are not well-explained, which could limit the understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the adversarial attacks used and the perturbation norms, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The paper's methodology and results are not clearly distinguished from existing methods, such as those using denoising diffusion probabilistic models, which could lead to confusion about the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including a head-to-head comparison in terms of certified accuracy and computational cost?
- How does the proposed method perform on different datasets and model architectures, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
- Could the authors clarify the role and choice of the vanilla model as a guide, and provide a more detailed justification for the use of the low-rank approximation?
- What are the specifics of the adversarial attacks used in the experiments, and how do they compare to other state-of-the-art attacks in terms of effectiveness and robustness?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use denoising diffusion probabilistic models for certified robustness, and what are the key differences in terms of effectiveness and computational cost?
