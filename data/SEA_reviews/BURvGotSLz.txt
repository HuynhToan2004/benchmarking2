 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel untrainable encoder for representation learning, named LagrangeEmbedding, which utilizes Lagrange basis functions to approximate data distributions. The encoder is designed to be universal, applicable across various raw data types and recognition tasks, and is claimed to achieve performance comparable to trainable encoders. It is based on a finite element method (FEM) and is shown to be effective in tasks such as regression, image classification, and text classification. However, the paper faces criticism for its lack of novelty, unclear presentation, and insufficient experimental validation. The method's computational efficiency and scalability are questioned, and the paper does not adequately compare its approach with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to unsupervised representation learning by introducing a new encoder based on the finite element method (FEM) and Lagrange basis functions.
- The proposed method is applicable across various raw data types and recognition tasks, which is a significant advantage in the field of representation learning.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are conducted on a variety of datasets and tasks, demonstrating the method's effectiveness.
- The methodology is supported by theoretical analysis, which enhances the credibility of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, which is crucial for establishing the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The presentation of the paper is unclear and difficult to follow, particularly in sections discussing the algorithm and the multiscale domain decomposition method.
- The experimental results, while promising, are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper does not adequately address the scalability and computational efficiency of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the method's application to more complex tasks and datasets, which raises concerns about its practical applicability.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process, which is crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness and limitations.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency, scalability, and performance?
- How does the proposed method compare with other unsupervised representation learning methods, such as those based on autoencoders or other unsupervised methods?
- Could the authors clarify the training process and the role of the preprocessing layer in the experimental results?
- How does the method handle more complex tasks and datasets, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in scaling up the method?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including error bars or standard deviations, to better understand the reliability and reproducibility of the findings?
