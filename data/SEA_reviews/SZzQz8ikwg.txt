 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel regularization term, ELLE, aimed at mitigating catastrophic overfitting (CO) in single-step adversarial training (AT) by enforcing local linearity in the loss function. This approach is contrasted with existing methods that rely on double backpropagation, which is computationally expensive. The authors demonstrate that ELLE is effective in avoiding CO and enhances the performance of single-step AT methods, as shown through extensive experiments on benchmarks like CIFAR10, CIFAR100, SVHN, and ImageNet. The methodology is supported by theoretical analysis linking the local linearity of the loss to the curvature of the loss, and the paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, ELLE, is simple, intuitive, and efficient, with minimal computational overhead.
- ELLE is shown to be effective in avoiding catastrophic overfitting (CO) and improving the performance of single-step adversarial training (AT) methods.
- The paper includes comprehensive experimental evaluations across multiple benchmarks, demonstrating the robustness and applicability of the proposed method.
- The theoretical analysis linking the local linearity of the loss to the curvature of the loss provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and detailed comparisons to existing methods, enhancing its readability and comprehensibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of adversarial robustness and computational efficiency.
- The experimental results, while extensive, do not convincingly demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods, especially in terms of adversarial robustness.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experimental results, including larger perturbation budgets and more robustness evaluation metrics.
- There is a lack of clarity in some figures and tables, which could be improved with better labeling and organization.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for overfitting and the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of adversarial robustness and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform under larger perturbation budgets, and what are the implications for the robustness of the trained models?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that aim to reduce the computational overhead of adversarial training, such as those using data augmentation or other techniques?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the proposed method?
- Is there a possibility of extending the proposed method to other types of adversarial attacks, such as those based on different norms or different types of perturbations?
