 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to enhancing the performance of language models by utilizing a multi-agent debate framework. This method involves multiple language model instances proposing and critiquing responses, aiming to arrive at a consensus through iterative debate. The approach is tested across various benchmarks, including arithmetic, grade school math, and chess reasoning tasks, demonstrating improvements in reasoning and factuality. The methodology is supported by empirical evidence showing that the debate process can lead to better answers, particularly in scenarios where individual models may initially provide incorrect or uncertain responses. Despite the innovative approach, concerns are raised about the scalability and efficiency of the method, as it requires multiple model instances and extensive rounds of debate, which could be computationally intensive.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to improving factual correctness and reasoning accuracy in language models by leveraging a multi-agent debate process between models, which is an orthogonal approach to existing methods.
- The method is shown to be effective in improving reasoning and factuality in language models, and it can be applied to any black-box model without requiring model-internal information.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a new benchmark for evaluating the factual accuracy of computer scientist biographies.
- The authors demonstrate that their approach can improve the factuality of language models, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper includes a thorough ablation study, showing the impact of different design choices on the performance of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of the number of agents and rounds of debate, which could be computationally intensive and limit the practical application of the approach.
- The method requires multiple model instances, which could be expensive and time-consuming, especially when considering the computational cost of running multiple models for each task.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the method, such as the impact of different model architectures or the scalability to larger models.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on arithmetic and chess reasoning tasks, which may not fully demonstrate the general applicability of the method across different domains.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation, including a broader range of tasks and a comparison with other state-of-the-art methods to better establish the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of the number of agents and rounds of debate? How does the computational cost scale with the number of agents and rounds?
- How does the method perform when applied to larger models, such as 13B or 70B models? Would the results be different?
- Is there a way to make the method more efficient, perhaps by using smaller models or a different architecture?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of tasks and the rationale behind the experimental design?
- How do you ensure that the agents do not converge on incorrect answers, especially in scenarios where they may initially provide incorrect responses?
- What are the implications of using different model architectures or training methods on the performance of the debate process?
