 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework, Distillist-Generalist-Specialist (DGS), aimed at reducing the length of demonstrations used in in-context learning (ICL) while maintaining performance. The DGS framework uses three LLM-powered agents: Distillist, Generalist, and Specialist, to iteratively refine demonstrations by removing redundant content. This approach, termed demonstration distillation, is evaluated on three datasets, showing improvements in compression ratios and ICL performance. However, the paper's evaluation is limited to a few datasets and models, and the novelty of the approach is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods. The clarity of the paper and the presentation of results are also noted as areas for improvement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise language, making it easy to understand.
- The proposed approach, DGS, is innovative and straightforward, effectively addressing the challenge of reducing the length of demonstrations in in-context learning (ICL) without sacrificing performance.
- The methodology is supported by thorough experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of DGS in improving ICL performance across various datasets and models.
- The paper provides a detailed explanation of the DGS framework, including its components and the iterative refinement process, which enhances the understanding of the proposed solution.
- The use of multiple LLMs as agents to perform different tasks is a novel approach that leverages the capabilities of large language models effectively.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation of DGS is limited to a few datasets and models, which may not fully demonstrate its generalizability and effectiveness across different domains and model types.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it appears to be similar to existing methods like gisting, which also uses a similar iterative refinement process.
- The clarity of the paper could be improved, particularly in the description of the Distillist and Generalist agents and the evaluation metrics used.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The presentation of results, such as the use of tables and figures, could be improved to better convey the findings and comparisons between different models and settings.
- The methodology section lacks details on the implementation and specifics of the DGS framework, making it difficult to replicate or understand the experimental setup fully.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the implementation of the DGS framework, particularly the specific instructions used for each agent and the evaluation metrics employed?
- How does the DGS framework compare to other prompt compression methods like gisting, especially in terms of novelty and effectiveness?
- Can you clarify the term "distillation" used in the paper and its relationship to the actual process of distilling knowledge from demonstrations?
- In the evaluation section, could you explain the choice of using different models for evaluating the distilled demonstrations and the rationale behind this approach?
- How does the DGS framework handle the potential for overfitting to the specific models used in the evaluation, and what steps have been taken to mitigate this risk?
- Could you provide more examples or case studies to illustrate the effectiveness of DGS in real-world applications, particularly in scenarios where the demonstrations are particularly long or complex?
