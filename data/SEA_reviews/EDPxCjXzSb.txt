 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CIReVL, a training-free method for Zero-Shot Compositional Image Retrieval (CIR), which leverages pre-trained models like BLIP-2 and LLMs to generate captions for reference images, modify them based on textual instructions, and retrieve the closest match from a database using CLIP. The method is evaluated across multiple benchmarks, showing competitive results against supervised methods. The paper highlights the modular nature of CIReVL, which allows for simple scalability and human intervention, enhancing its practicality and interpretability. Despite its strengths, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach and the clarity of its contributions, particularly in terms of its distinction from existing methods and the depth of its theoretical underpinnings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method, CIReVL.
- The method is simple, intuitive, and modular, making it easy to understand and implement.
- CIReVL achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on multiple benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The method is training-free, which is a significant advantage in terms of cost and efficiency.
- The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies and qualitative results, providing a robust evaluation of the method.
- The approach is interpretable, which is beneficial for understanding the system's behavior and improving its performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited, as it primarily combines existing models without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and failure cases of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and robustness.
- The method's reliance on the quality of the initial caption and the LLM's ability to generate accurate and detailed captions could limit its performance.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the time complexity and computational requirements of the method, which could impact its scalability and practicality.
- There is a lack of discussion on the societal impacts and ethical considerations of the method, which is essential for understanding its broader implications.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its method from existing approaches, which could lead to confusion about its unique contributions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach and how it differs from existing methods?
- How does the method handle noisy or inaccurate captions generated by BLIP-2, and what are the implications for the overall performance?
- Is there a way to ensure the generated captions are detailed enough to capture all relevant aspects of the reference image, especially in complex scenes?
- How does the method handle cases where the reference image and the target image are not easily retrievable due to significant differences in content or style?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational requirements and scalability of the method, especially in terms of its applicability to large-scale datasets or real-time retrieval scenarios?
- How does the method address the societal impacts and ethical considerations, particularly in terms of privacy and data security?
