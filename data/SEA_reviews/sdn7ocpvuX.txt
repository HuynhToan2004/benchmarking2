 **Summary:**
The paper explores the generalization capabilities of graph neural networks (GNNs) under topological shifts, focusing on the sensitivity of local diffusion models to changes in graph topology. It introduces a novel graph encoder backbone, the Advective Diffusion Transformer (ADiT), inspired by advective graph diffusion equations. The ADiT model, which can be seen as a versatile graph Transformer, is designed to address the generalization issues by combining non-local diffusion with local message passing, aiming to capture invariant latent interactions and observed topological patterns. The paper provides theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence supporting the model's effectiveness across various graph learning tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of the literature on diffusion models on graphs and their application to graph learning tasks, highlighting the importance of generalization capabilities in graph learning.
- The authors propose a novel graph encoder backbone, ADiT, which is inspired by advective graph diffusion equations and supported by theoretical guarantees of desired generalization under topological distribution shifts.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed theoretical derivations and proofs, which are supported by extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The proposed ADiT model is versatile and can be adapted to different graph learning tasks, such as node classification and graph classification, showing superior performance across a wide range of graph learning tasks.
- The paper is structured well, with a clear motivation and a logical flow of ideas, making it easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed ADiT model, which could be a significant drawback for its practical implementation, especially in large-scale graph learning applications.
- The experimental section lacks detailed information on the datasets used, such as their size, number of classes, and statistical properties, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of some experimental results and figures, which could hinder the understanding of the paper's contributions.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed model, which could affect the credibility of the results and the practical applicability of the model.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related works, particularly those that address similar issues in graph learning, to position the paper within the existing literature.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed ADiT model perform in terms of computational complexity, especially in large-scale graph learning applications?
- Could the authors provide more detailed information on the datasets used in the experiments, including their size, number of classes, and statistical properties?
- How does the ADiT model handle different types of topological shifts, such as homophily shifts and density shifts, compared to other diffusion models?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental results presented in Figure 3 and Table 1, particularly the performance of the ADiT-series model compared to other models in different scenarios?
- How does the ADiT model compare to other models in terms of generalization capabilities under different conditions, and what are the specific advantages of using the ADiT model over other existing models?
- Could the authors provide more details on the theoretical underpinnings of the ADiT model, particularly how it addresses the generalization issues in graph learning?
