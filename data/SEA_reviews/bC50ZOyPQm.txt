 **Summary:**
The paper introduces REcurrent ADaptation (READ), a novel parameter-efficient transfer learning (PETL) method designed to reduce training memory consumption and GPU energy usage. READ utilizes a small RNN network alongside a backbone model, allowing for efficient fine-tuning without extensive backpropagation through large pre-trained networks. The method is evaluated on the GLUE benchmark, demonstrating a 56% reduction in training memory consumption and an 84% reduction in GPU energy usage compared to full-tuning. The paper also discusses the scalability and efficiency of READ, showing that it can adapt to large transformers without significant parameter growth.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research.
- The proposed method, REcurrent ADaptation (READ), is a novel and effective approach that can be applied to large-scale Transformers, showing significant improvements in training memory consumption and GPU energy usage.
- READ is scalable and can be applied to large models without a significant increase in model size, making it a promising solution for fine-tuning large transformers.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of READ on the GLUE benchmark, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency compared to full-tuning.
- The theoretical justification provided in the paper supports the design of READ, enhancing its credibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other PETL methods, particularly those that have been proposed recently. This could help in understanding the advantages of READ over existing methods.
- The paper does not discuss the performance of READ in terms of inference latency and memory efficiency, which are crucial metrics for evaluating the practical applicability of the method.
- The methodology of READ, particularly the use of an RNN network alongside a backbone model, is not entirely novel, as similar approaches have been used in prior works.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the number of parameters in READ with other PETL methods, which is necessary to understand the efficiency of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the term "correction" as used in the paper and explain how it contributes to the effectiveness of the READ method?
- How does the READ method perform in terms of inference latency and memory efficiency, and how does it compare to other PETL methods in these aspects?
- Can you provide more details on the design choices made in the READ method, particularly the decision to use a recurrent network instead of other architectures?
- How does the READ method perform in low-data regimes, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in such scenarios?
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the READ method with other PETL methods, including the number of parameters, inference latency, and memory efficiency?
