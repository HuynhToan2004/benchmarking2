 **Summary:**
The paper explores the calibration of confidence in deep neural networks (DNNs) adapted from a source domain to a target domain, addressing the issue of overconfidence in predictions. The authors propose a method that estimates the network's accuracy on the target domain using a linear regression model trained on the source domain's validation set, which is then used to calibrate the network's confidence directly on the target domain. The method is evaluated on benchmark datasets, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper's novelty is questioned, as similar approaches have been previously explored, and the method's performance is seen as limited by the accuracy of the source domain's validation set.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of deep learning, focusing on the calibration of confidence in deep neural networks (DNNs) adapted from a source domain to a target domain.
- The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their proposed method, which is supported by extensive experimental results on benchmark datasets.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and the experimental setup is detailed, including the use of the Deng and Zheng (2021) method for estimating the target accuracy.
- The proposed method is straightforward and easy to implement, which could facilitate its adoption by other researchers.
- The paper presents a novel approach by estimating the target accuracy directly on the target domain, which is a departure from traditional methods that rely on source domain validation sets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as similar approaches have been previously explored, particularly in the estimation of target accuracy and the use of importance weighting.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that also address the calibration of confidence in DNNs adapted from a source domain to a target domain.
- The experimental setup and results lack clarity and depth, with some figures and tables being confusing or misleading. For example, the results in Table 2 and 3 are not clearly presented, and the accuracy ratios across bins are not convincingly supported.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of overconfidence in predictions, as the calibration of confidence is not directly linked to the actual performance of the model on the target domain.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not adequately compare its performance with other methods that do not require labeled data from the target domain.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the accuracy ratio across bins is determined and whether it is assumed to be constant or varied?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that also address the calibration of confidence in DNNs adapted from a source domain to a target domain?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific datasets used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of overconfidence in predictions, and can the authors provide empirical evidence or theoretical support for this claim?
- Could the authors elaborate on the method's limitations and potential negative societal impacts, if any?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that do not require labeled data from the target domain, and what are the advantages or disadvantages of these approaches compared to the method proposed in the paper?
