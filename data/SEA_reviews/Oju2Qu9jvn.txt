 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to dynamic feature selection (DFS) by estimating the conditional mutual information (CMI) between features and the response variable, which is crucial for selecting the most informative features. The method involves training two neural networks: a predictor and a value network, where the value network estimates the CMI and guides feature selection. The authors also address challenges such as variable feature budgets and non-uniform feature costs, which are crucial in practical applications. The method is evaluated on both tabular and image datasets, showing promising results compared to existing methods. However, concerns were raised about the theoretical underpinnings, the practical implications of the assumptions made, and the clarity of the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem of dynamic feature selection, which is crucial for reducing data acquisition costs and enhancing model interpretability.
- The proposed method, which involves training a predictor network and a value network to estimate the conditional mutual information (CMI), is innovative and well-explained.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear introduction and methodology.
- The experiments conducted on both tabular and image datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing significant improvements over existing methods.
- The paper provides theoretical results that support the method's effectiveness, and the method's ability to handle non-uniform feature costs and variable feature budgets is highlighted.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper makes strong assumptions, such as the infinitely expressive predictor network and the ability to compute the CMI exactly, which may not hold in practical scenarios.
- The theoretical analysis provided is not sufficiently clear, particularly in how the training objectives lead to the exact CMI, and there is a lack of detailed explanation on the practical implications of these assumptions.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on related works, particularly those that address similar problems using different methods.
- The experimental setup and results are not clearly presented, with some figures and results not being well-explained or not included in the main text.
- There are some typographical and formatting errors in the paper, which could be confusing or misleading.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which is a significant concern in dynamic feature selection.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the practical implications of the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the assumption of an infinitely expressive predictor network?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of overfitting, which is a significant concern in dynamic feature selection?
- In the experiments, how does the method perform when there are missing features or when the feature selection policy is not optimal?
- Could you provide more details on how the value network is trained, particularly the loss function used and the training procedure?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially in large-scale datasets?
- Can you provide a more detailed discussion on the related works, particularly those that address similar problems using different methods?
- How does the method handle the issue of non-stationarity in the data distribution, which is a common problem in dynamic feature selection?
