 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new smoothing method for negative sampling in Knowledge Graph Embedding (KGE) models, specifically focusing on the Triplet-based Self-Adversarial Negative Sampling (T-SANS) loss function. T-SANS aims to address the sparsity problem in KGs by combining the advantages of Self-Adversarial Negative Sampling (SANS) and subsampling. The method is theoretically analyzed and empirically evaluated against various baselines, showing improvements in performance. However, the paper's theoretical contributions are questioned, particularly the novelty of the T-SANS loss and the overall impact of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of a new loss function, T-SANS, and its unification with subsampling methods provides a novel approach to addressing sparsity in Knowledge Graph Embedding (KGE) models.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various models and datasets, highlighting its general applicability.
- The theoretical analysis is solid, and the paper provides a unified framework for interpreting different loss functions, which is beneficial for understanding the underlying mechanisms of KGE models.
- The paper addresses the critical issue of sparsity in KGs, which is a significant challenge in the field, and proposes a method that could potentially improve model performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is questioned due to the incremental nature of the T-SANS loss, which appears to be a simple combination of existing methods without significant novelty.
- The theoretical analysis lacks depth, particularly in the explanation of how T-SANS improves sparsity compared to other methods like subsampling.
- The empirical results, while showing some improvements, do not consistently outperform the baselines, raising concerns about the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough literature review and comparison with existing methods, especially those that address similar issues in KG completion.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how T-SANS improves sparsity compared to other methods like subsampling?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational complexity, especially in large-scale applications?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the temperature parameter $\gamma$ in the experimental results?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and computational efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods for KG completion?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the choice of datasets and the generalizability of the findings to other, potentially more diverse, KGs?
