 **Summary:**
The paper introduces several modifications to the consistency training (CT) method, including a new weighting function, a pseudo-Huber loss, and a log-normal noise schedule, aimed at improving the sample quality of consistency models. These modifications are designed to address the limitations of the original CT method, such as the reliance on learned metrics and the potential for bias in evaluation. The paper presents empirical evidence showing that these modifications lead to significant improvements in FID scores on various datasets like CIFAR-10 and ImageNet 64x64. Despite these improvements, the paper is criticized for lacking a thorough theoretical analysis and for potentially overstating the significance of its contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces several novel modifications to the consistency training (CT) method, including a new weighting function, a pseudo-Huber loss, and a log-normal noise schedule, which are shown to significantly improve the sample quality of consistency models.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides a comprehensive analysis of the CT method, including a detailed discussion on the limitations of the original CT method and how the proposed modifications address these issues.
- The empirical results presented in the paper demonstrate that the modified CT method achieves state-of-the-art FID scores on various datasets, showing that consistency models can be competitive with other generative models.
- The paper provides a thorough discussion on the theoretical aspects of the CT method, including an analysis of the weighting function and its impact on sample quality.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed modifications, particularly in explaining why these modifications are effective and how they improve the consistency model.
- The paper's claims of novelty are overstated, as many of the modifications are based on existing techniques such as Pseudo-Huber loss and log-normal noise schedule, which have been previously used in other contexts.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed modifications, such as the potential for increased training time and computational complexity.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently robust, with some results showing only marginal improvements or inconsistencies across different datasets and settings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational overhead of the proposed modifications, which could be significant given the increased complexity of the consistency model.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed modifications, particularly how they improve the consistency model and why they are effective?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the new weighting function and the pseudo-Huber loss, and can they provide a more detailed explanation of their design and implementation?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons with other methods and a more thorough analysis of the impact of the proposed modifications on different datasets and settings?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed modifications, such as increased training time and computational complexity, and how these limitations might affect the practical applicability of the consistency model?
- How do the authors address the potential issues of overfitting and bias in the consistency model, particularly given the increased complexity and training requirements of the modified model?
