 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for post-hoc intervention in black-box models, focusing on concept bottleneck models (CBMs). The authors propose a new approach that allows for intervening on the outputs of a black-box model by replacing the latent representation with one that corresponds to a desired concept. This method involves training a probe to predict the concept from the latent representation, optimizing the latent representation to match the concept, and then using this optimized representation to make predictions. The paper also introduces a new metric for evaluating the effectiveness of interventions, which is a measure of the gap between the original prediction and the prediction after intervention. The method is evaluated on synthetic and real datasets, showing that it can improve the performance of black-box models by making them more interpretable and intervenable.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for post-hoc intervention in black-box models, which is a significant contribution to the field of explainable AI.
- The methodology is well-explained and easy to follow, with clear and concise writing that makes the paper accessible to a broad audience.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments on both synthetic and real datasets, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in the field of machine learning, specifically the need for interpretable and intervenable models.
- The proposed method is intuitive and simple, making it potentially broadly applicable to various domains.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples of the intervention process, particularly how it differs from existing methods like concept bottleneck models (CBMs).
- The evaluation metrics used in the paper are limited to accuracy and calibration, which may not fully capture the effectiveness of the intervention. Additional metrics such as concept similarity and model performance under different intervention strategies could be included.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the method may not be effective or where it could lead to biased or unfair predictions.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in areas such as healthcare or finance where interventions could have significant consequences.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the area of concept-based explanations and post-hoc interventions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and existing methods like CBMs, particularly in terms of the intervention process and the role of the probing function?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the concepts are not well-defined or where there is a lack of correlation between the concepts and the target variable?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies of how the intervention process might be applied in real-world scenarios, particularly in domains such as healthcare or finance?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for biased or unfair predictions, particularly in scenarios where the intervening concepts may not be representative of the population as a whole?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in areas where interventions could have significant consequences?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods for post-hoc intervention or explanation, particularly in terms of effectiveness and computational efficiency?
