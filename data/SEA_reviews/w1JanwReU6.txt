 **Summary:**
The paper investigates gender bias in large language models (LLMs) by examining their behavior in non-stereotypical settings, specifically in contexts free of gendered words. It introduces a new benchmark that includes gender-invariant sentences and sentences with minimal gender-related words to evaluate LLMs' gender bias. The study uses a novel methodology to automatically generate gender-invariant sentences, which are then used to test 23 LLMs across three benchmarks. The results show that despite the absence of gender-correlated words, LLMs still exhibit gender bias, favoring male pronouns in a significant number of cases. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings and suggests that gender bias in LLMs persists even in non-stereotypical settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue of gender bias in large language models (LLMs) and proposes a novel methodology to automatically generate gender-invariant sentences.
- The authors have conducted a comprehensive evaluation of 23 LLMs using three benchmarks, which includes a significant number of models and a diverse set of evaluations.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the methodology and results.
- The introduction of a new benchmark for evaluating gender bias in LLMs is a significant contribution to the field, providing a valuable tool for future research and practical applications.
- The paper demonstrates a rigorous approach to the problem, using a variety of models and benchmarks to ensure the robustness of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's methodology for selecting gender-invariant sentences and the evaluation of these sentences could be improved. Specifically, the use of MaxPMI(s) as a threshold for gender-invariant sentences and the definition of gender-invariant sentences based on a single set of pronouns ("he", "she") might not adequately address gender bias across different gendered expressions.
- The paper's reliance on a single model (ChatGPT) for generating gender-invariant sentences could limit the diversity and generalizability of the results.
- The paper's findings are not surprising, as it is well-known that LLMs learn from biased data and perpetuate these biases in their outputs. The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its methodology or the generalizability of its findings to other models or settings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the gender bias in LLMs beyond the binary gender pronoun setting, which might limit the practical applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how they define "gender-invariant" and how they measure "gender co-occurring words"? How does this definition compare to other definitions used in the literature?
- How does the paper ensure the quality and diversity of the generated sentences, especially in terms of grammatical correctness and semantic meaning?
- How does the paper address the potential biases introduced by the use of ChatGPT in generating gender-invariant sentences?
- How do the findings of this study compare to other studies that have used different methodologies or models for evaluating gender bias in LLMs?
- Can the authors provide more details on the selection of seed attribute words and how this affects the diversity and quality of the generated sentences?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the threshold ε for gender-invariant sentences, and how does this choice affect the results and the conclusions drawn from the study?
