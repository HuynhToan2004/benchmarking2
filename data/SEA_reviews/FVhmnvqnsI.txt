 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for dataset condensation, specifically addressing the "subset degradation problem" where subsets of condensed datasets are less representative than the whole dataset. The proposed method, Multisize Dataset Condensation (MDC), compresses multiple condensation processes into a single process, using an "adaptive subset loss" to select the most learnable subset. This approach aims to reduce computational costs and improve storage efficiency by reusing condensed images. The method was tested across various datasets and architectures, showing improvements in accuracy and efficiency compared to traditional methods. However, the paper lacks a detailed comparison with other recent methods and does not provide sufficient ablation studies to fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear illustrations and a logical structure that enhances understanding.
- The proposed method effectively addresses the "subset degradation problem" by introducing an "adaptive subset loss" and an "adaptive subset selection" strategy, which are novel and interesting.
- The paper includes comprehensive experiments across various datasets and architectures, demonstrating the method's effectiveness and robustness.
- The use of feature distance as an alternative to gradient distance for subset selection is a significant contribution, as it reduces computational overhead.
- The paper provides a detailed explanation of the "subset degradation problem" and its impact on the performance of condensed datasets, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other recent methods, particularly those that address similar issues such as Slimmable DC and LBS, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently convincing, with the improvements over baseline methods being marginal in some cases, and the paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the limitations and failure cases of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational overhead of the proposed method, particularly in terms of training time and memory requirements, which could be a significant concern for practical deployment.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples of the "subset degradation problem" and its impact on the performance of condensed datasets, as well as more visualization of the condensed images to demonstrate their quality.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other recent methods, including Slimmable DC and LBS, to better establish the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and memory requirements with other methods, especially in practical deployment scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples and visualizations of the "subset degradation problem" and its impact on the performance of condensed datasets?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of accuracy and efficiency when applied to larger datasets or more complex models?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the "MLS selection" process and its impact on the overall performance and efficiency of the method?
- The paper mentions that the method is applicable to various architectures and datasets. Could the authors provide more detailed examples of its application and its performance in different scenarios?
