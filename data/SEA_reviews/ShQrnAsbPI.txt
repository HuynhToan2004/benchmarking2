 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to federated continual learning (FCL) by addressing the challenges of catastrophic forgetting and statistical heterogeneity among clients. It proposes a method called "accurate forgetting" which selectively discards biased or irrelevant features, improving performance on new tasks. The approach utilizes a generative-replay method and a normalizing flow model to estimate the credibility of previous knowledge. The method is evaluated across multiple datasets and shown to outperform existing methods, although concerns about the clarity of the method's application and its robustness to various scenarios persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to federated continual learning (FCL) by addressing the challenges of catastrophic forgetting and statistical heterogeneity among clients.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the problem setting and the proposed solution.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of a normalizing flow model to quantify the credibility of previous knowledge is a novel and interesting approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed discussion on the potential limitations and societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining how the method is applied in specific scenarios, particularly in the context of the EMNIST-LTP dataset.
- The evaluation is limited to a few datasets, and it would be beneficial to see how the method performs on a broader range of datasets to validate its generalizability.
- The method's robustness to different scenarios, such as varying client numbers and task distributions, is not thoroughly tested.
- The paper does not adequately address how the method handles label noise, which is a significant issue in federated learning.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and memory requirements of the proposed method, especially in comparison to existing methods.
- There are several typographical errors and inconsistencies in the paper, which need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the method is applied in the EMNIST-LTP dataset, specifically how the normalizing flow model is utilized to selectively discard biased or irrelevant features?
- How does the method perform when the number of clients or the distribution of tasks varies?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and memory requirements of the proposed method compared to existing methods?
- How does the method handle label noise in federated learning scenarios?
- Can the authors elaborate on the choice of datasets and the rationale behind the specific experimental settings used in the evaluation?
- Is there a possibility to include more recent or diverse datasets in future evaluations to validate the method's generalizability?
