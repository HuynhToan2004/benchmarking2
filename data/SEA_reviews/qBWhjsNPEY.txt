 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DeepZero, a novel zeroth-order (ZO) optimization framework designed to scale up the training of deep neural networks (DNNs) without the need for first-order information. DeepZero employs coordinate-wise gradient estimation, sparsity-induced training, and forward parallelization to enhance scalability and computational efficiency. The framework is evaluated across various tasks, including image classification and adversarial defense, demonstrating its effectiveness in achieving state-of-the-art (SOTA) accuracy on CIFAR-10 and enhancing robustness against adversarial attacks. Despite its promising results, concerns are raised about the novelty of the proposed methods, the clarity of the experimental setup, and the generalizability of the results to larger and more complex models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to coordinate-wise gradient estimation, which is shown to be more effective than randomized vector-wise gradient estimation in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency.
- The integration of sparsity-induced training and forward parallelization in the proposed framework is innovative and contributes to the efficiency and scalability of zeroth-order optimization.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating that DeepZero achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) accuracy on CIFAR-10 and enhances robustness against adversarial attacks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and provides a comprehensive discussion of the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.
- The inclusion of a wide range of experiments across various applications, including image classification, adversarial defense, and physics-informed ML, showcases the versatility and applicability of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the convergence rate and the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which are crucial for understanding their practical implications and scalability.
- The experimental setup is not clearly described, particularly in terms of the number of runs and the statistical significance of the results.
- The novelty of the proposed methods is questioned, as similar techniques have been previously explored in other works.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the experimental results, making it difficult to compare and interpret the results across different models and datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability issues related to the size and complexity of the models, which is a significant limitation given the claims of scalability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of their applicability to larger and more complex models, and the potential negative societal impacts.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the convergence rate and computational complexity of the proposed methods?
- How does the proposed framework compare in terms of scalability and computational efficiency with other methods that use first-order information, such as gradient descent?
- In the context of the experiments, how were the hyperparameters tuned, and what impact do these choices have on the results?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the number of runs and the statistical significance of the results?
- How do the authors address the scalability issues related to the size and complexity of the models, and what are the potential limitations of the proposed methods in this regard?
- Can the proposed framework be adapted to other types of neural networks beyond convolutional neural networks, and what are the implications of this for the scalability and applicability of the methods?
