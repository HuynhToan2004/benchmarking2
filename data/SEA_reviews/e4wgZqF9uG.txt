 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the effectiveness of pre-training a model on depth estimation compared to traditional image classification for semantic segmentation tasks. It explores the hypothesis that depth pre-training can improve semantic segmentation performance by leveraging the spatial regularities in depth maps. The study is supported by extensive experiments across various datasets, including KITTI, Cityscapes, and NYU-V2, demonstrating that depth pre-training outperforms image classification pre-training. The paper also addresses the potential of using depth pre-training as a cost-effective alternative to human annotation, raising questions about the necessity and efficiency of annotated datasets. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the findings and the fairness of the comparisons, particularly in the use of different datasets and pre-training methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, with clear writing that effectively conveys the main ideas and results.
- The hypothesis that depth pre-training is beneficial for semantic segmentation tasks is supported by a variety of experiments and results.
- The authors demonstrate that depth pre-training is more effective than image classification pre-training, showing significant improvements in performance.
- The paper addresses an important question about the necessity of human annotation in semantic tasks, and provides a thorough analysis of the effectiveness of depth pre-training.
- The experimental design is robust, with multiple controlled experiments that explore different forms of supervision, training pipelines, and data resolutions for semantic fine-tuning.
- The paper is well-presented, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the findings is questionable, as similar results have been reported in previous works, such as those by Jiang et al. (2018) and Hoyer et al. (2021).
- The experiments are not comprehensive enough, particularly in terms of the datasets used and the pre-training methods compared. The use of different datasets and pre-training methods for depth and classification could affect the fairness and validity of the comparisons.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the pre-training and fine-tuning processes, which could affect the reproducibility and understanding of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of pre-training methods and the impact of different datasets on the results.
- There are some minor issues with the presentation, such as missing references and unclear explanations in certain figures and tables.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences in performance between the depth-cropped and depth-full models, particularly in terms of overfitting and generalization?
- How does the paper address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, especially in terms of data collection and model training?
- Can the authors provide more details on the pre-training and fine-tuning processes, including the specific datasets and pre-training methods used?
- How does the paper ensure the fairness and validity of the comparisons between depth and classification pre-training, particularly in terms of the datasets and pre-training methods used?
- Could the authors expand on the potential applications and benefits of using depth pre-training for semantic segmentation, especially in real-world scenarios?
- How does the paper address the potential limitations of depth pre-training, such as the need for calibrated cameras and the availability of depth data?
