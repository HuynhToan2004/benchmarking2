 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to Open-set Semi-Supervised Learning (OSSL) by formulating the outlier detection problem as a Partial Optimal Transport (POT) problem. The authors propose a mass score function (MSF) and an OOD loss to adapt off-the-shelf SSL methods into OSSL settings. The proposed method leverages POT to measure the likelihood of a sample being an outlier, enhancing the performance of OSSL models. The approach is evaluated on various datasets and shows competitive results against existing methods. However, concerns about the novelty of the method, the clarity of the paper, and the method's effectiveness in handling different data distributions are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of the POT problem to address OSSL is innovative and provides a novel approach to the field.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in some experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The use of POT for OSSL is a novel application that has not been explored previously, which adds to the paper's originality.
- The method is simple and easy to implement, making it practical for real-world applications.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation on various datasets, including CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and ImageNet30, which demonstrates the robustness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing OSSL methods, particularly those that use similar techniques such as OT and POT.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant new contributions.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with inconsistencies and unclear explanations that undermine the credibility of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could lead to misinterpretations or misunderstandings of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The methodology and some technical details are not clearly explained, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the proposed approach and its implications.
- The paper does not provide sufficient justification for the choices made in the methodology, such as the use of specific algorithms and parameters, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method differs from existing OSSL methods, particularly in terms of the use of POT and the novelty of the approach?
- How does the proposed method handle different data distributions, and what are the implications of this for the accuracy and generalization of the model?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations for the choices made in the experimental setup, such as the selection of parameters and the justification for using specific algorithms?
- In the context of the OOD loss and the MSF, can the authors provide more detailed explanations for their design and the rationale behind their use?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data distribution shifts significantly between training and testing phases, and what are the implications of this for the model's accuracy and robustness?
