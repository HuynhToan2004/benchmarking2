 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedBPT, a federated learning (FL) framework designed for black-box prompt tuning of pre-trained language models (PLMs) without requiring model parameters to be accessed by clients. The methodology involves gradient-free optimization using CMA-ES, which enables efficient communication and reduces computational and storage costs. FedBPT aims to address challenges such as model access, communication cost, and computational capacity by training optimal prompts across clients and aggregating them on the server. The framework is evaluated on various datasets using RoBERTa and Llama2 models, demonstrating comparable performance to gradient-based methods while reducing communication and memory costs. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental scope, focusing on only a few datasets and not adequately addressing the scalability and generalizability of the method across different types of tasks and models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem by proposing a novel framework for black-box prompt tuning in federated learning (FL), which is crucial for privacy-preserving applications.
- The methodology is well-motivated and provides a clear rationale for its design choices, making it easy to understand and replicate.
- The paper includes thorough experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in performance over existing methods.
- The use of gradient-free optimization (CMA-ES) for prompt tuning is innovative and helps reduce communication costs, making the method more efficient than traditional gradient-based approaches.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is essential for a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with a broader range of baseline methods, particularly in terms of communication and memory costs, which limits the understanding of the method's performance in different scenarios.
- The experimental setup is limited to a few datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness across different types of tasks and datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and generalizability of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger models and more complex tasks.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the method's performance.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with a broader range of baseline methods, particularly in terms of communication and memory costs?
- How does the proposed method perform on more complex tasks and larger models, such as those used in large-scale language models (LLMs)?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their method and provide insights into potential future research directions?
- How does the method compare with other PEFT methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability of the method and its applicability to different types of tasks and datasets?
