 **Summary:**
The paper explores the adversarial robustness of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) in directed graphs, addressing the limitations of existing approaches from both attack and defense perspectives. It introduces a new adversarial graph attack setting for directed graphs and proposes a simple yet effective message-passing framework to enhance the robustness of GNNs. The paper demonstrates that directed graphs can significantly enhance the robustness and resilience of GNNs, achieving state-of-the-art performance against both transfer and adaptive attacks. The proposed framework is shown to be effective in both clean and adversarial settings, offering a practical solution for improving the security of GNNs in real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the important issue of adversarial robustness in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and introduces a new adversarial graph attack setting for directed graphs, which is more realistic and practical.
- The proposed Biased Bidirectional Random Walk (BBRW) message-passing layer is simple, effective, and can be easily integrated into existing GNN frameworks, enhancing the robustness of GNNs significantly.
- The paper provides a comprehensive comparison of the proposed BBRW against various existing GNN models, demonstrating its effectiveness in both clean and adversarial settings.
- The theoretical analysis and empirical results support the effectiveness of the proposed method, and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The study on the robustness of GNNs in the context of directed graphs is novel and provides a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on node classification tasks, which might limit the generalizability of the proposed method to other types of graph-level tasks.
- The theoretical analysis of the proposed method is limited, and more detailed analysis could be provided to strengthen the paper.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review, especially regarding the robustness of GNNs in directed graphs.
- The experimental results are based on only two datasets, which might not sufficiently demonstrate the robustness of the proposed method across different datasets.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced perspective.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be extended to other types of graph-level tasks beyond node classification?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing defense strategies for directed graphs in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed method to strengthen the paper's contribution?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other datasets beyond those used in the experiments?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their proposed method and how these limitations might affect its practical applicability?
