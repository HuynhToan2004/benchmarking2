 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Harmonized Learning with Concurrent Arbitration (HLCA), a brain-inspired Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) scheme for motion planning. HLCA employs a Concurrent Arbitration Module (CAM) to balance exploration and exploitation and a Harmonized Self-Improving Learning (HSIL) style to enhance learning from historical experiences. The approach is evaluated against state-of-the-art methods in various environments, demonstrating superior performance in terms of success rate, collision checks, and path cost. However, the paper lacks detailed discussion on the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed method, and the comparison with existing works in the field of motion planning is insufficient.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.
- The proposed method, HLCA, demonstrates superior performance in terms of success rate, collision checks, and path cost compared to state-of-the-art methods.
- The integration of brain-inspired ideas into the motion planning process is innovative and provides a novel approach to addressing complex decision-making scenarios.
- The paper includes comprehensive experimental evaluations that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in various environments.
- The authors provide detailed explanations of the methodology and results, making the paper accessible and understandable to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- There is an insufficient comparison with existing works in the field of motion planning, particularly in terms of novelty and methodological contributions.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the method performs in more complex or dynamic environments, which could affect its practical utility.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the biological plausibility of the method, which is crucial for establishing its relevance and applicability in the field of brain-inspired AI.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing works in the field of motion planning, particularly in terms of novelty and methodological contributions?
- Could the authors provide a clearer explanation of how the method performs in more complex or dynamic environments?
- How does the proposed method ensure the biological plausibility of its approach, and what are the implications of this for its practical application?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how do these impact its practical utility and applicability?
