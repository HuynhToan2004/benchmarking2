 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Memory Metropolis (MeMeMe), which leverages a neural network to generate proposal distributions for simulated annealing (SA) in combinatorial optimization. MeMeMe utilizes a template network to learn patterns and modify the proposal distribution, enhancing the SA process by incorporating memory of past states. This approach is tested on a nanophotonic inverse design problem, showing improvements over standard SA and regularized SA. The methodology involves a reinforcement learning-based approach where the neural network learns to predict rewards based on the current state, and the proposal distribution is constructed from the predicted rewards. The paper also explores the use of locally connected layers to encourage cluster formation, which is crucial for the optimization process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Memory Metropolis (MeMeMe), which combines elements from Markov Chain Monte Carlo optimization and reinforcement learning, offering a fresh perspective on combinatorial optimization.
- The use of a template network to learn patterns and modify the proposal distribution is a novel approach that enhances the SA process by incorporating memory of past states, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise descriptions of the proposed method and its implementation, making it accessible and understandable.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over standard SA and regularized SA in the nanophotonic inverse design problem.
- The use of locally connected layers to encourage cluster formation is an innovative approach that enhances the optimization process by allowing the network to learn local relations and thus encourage cluster formation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed description of the template network, particularly how it learns a template and how it affects the proposal distribution.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other existing methods, which could help to establish the superiority or novelty of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive experimental validation, including a broader range of combinatorial optimization problems and different types of neural network architectures.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of mode collapse, which is a significant limitation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly how it scales with the size of the problem.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the generalizability of the proposed method to other types of optimization problems, which could limit its applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed description of the template network, including how it learns a template and how it affects the proposal distribution?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these limitations might affect its applicability in different scenarios?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of mode collapse, and what steps have been taken to mitigate this issue?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specific neural network architectures used and the training data sets?
- Can the authors discuss the potential for extending the proposed method to other types of optimization problems, and what modifications would be required to adapt the method to these new scenarios?
