 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to training discrete energy-based models (EBMs) by minimizing the energy discrepancy between the data distribution and the EBM. This method uses perturbation techniques such as Bernoulli noise, deterministic transforms, and neighborhood structures to generate negative samples, which are then used to compute the energy discrepancy loss. The authors propose a method that does not require MCMC sampling, which is typically used in traditional EBM training methods. The paper also explores the application of importance sampling to improve the estimation of the energy discrepancy loss. The proposed method is tested across various discrete data types including binary data, graphs, and discrete images, showing competitive performance compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to training discrete energy-based models (EBMs) without relying on MCMC sampling, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors provide theoretical guarantees for their method, enhancing the credibility and reliability of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is general and can be applied to various discrete data types, including binary data, graphs, and discrete images.
- The paper includes a thorough empirical evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various tasks and datasets.
- The paper introduces new perturbation methods that can be used to improve the quality of negative samples in energy-based models, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the perturbation q(y|x) and how it relates to the generation of negative samples, which is crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing methods that also use importance sampling, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not convincing, particularly in the case of binary image modeling, where the proposed method underperforms compared to other methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential challenges.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the relationship between the proposed method and other existing methods, particularly those that use similar approaches such as importance sampling and gradient-informed proposals.
