 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel subjective speech quality measure, Speech Quality Score (SQS), designed to enhance transcription performance and annotation quality. SQS is evaluated using a dataset of 1,020 audio samples, with SQS annotations created specifically for this study. The authors propose a DNN-based model to predict SQS values, comparing it to existing MOS prediction models. The paper highlights the limitations of existing objective measures and subjective measures in assessing audio quality for annotation and proposes a new metric that correlates better with human perception. The methodology includes a transfer learning approach using the NISQA model to predict SQS values, showing moderate correlations with human ratings. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope, lack of comparison with other metrics, and insufficient justification for the use of SQS over existing measures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel subjective speech quality measure, Speech Quality Score (SQS), which is suitable for use within the audio data annotation process.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and the motivation behind the research is clearly articulated.
- The proposed model, using transfer learning with the NISQA model, achieves good performance with a linear correlation coefficient of 0.8 between ground-truth and predicted SQS values.
- The paper provides a good motivation for the research and presents a well-structured methodology.
- The experiments conducted show that the proposed model can effectively predict SQS values, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comparison with other subjective measures, which is crucial for validating the effectiveness of the proposed metric.
- The dataset used is relatively small (1,020 samples), which limits the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient justification for why the proposed metric is superior to existing measures, such as MOS or other subjective measures.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed metric and a more thorough comparison with other metrics.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential impact of data quality on the results, which is a significant limitation.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive literature review, particularly in the area of speech quality assessment, which could have provided a stronger foundation for the research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the SQS metric differs from existing measures, such as MOS, and why it is considered superior?
- How does the proposed metric handle variations in data quality, and what impact does this have on the results?
- Could the authors expand on the potential applications of the SQS metric beyond transcription tasks?
- What are the specific characteristics of the data used in the study that led to the observed results, and how do these characteristics influence the effectiveness of the SQS metric?
- How does the SQS metric perform in different acoustic environments, and what are the implications of this for its practical application?
- Could the authors clarify the methodology used for the transfer learning approach, particularly how the NISQA model was adapted for predicting SQS values?
