 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel disentangled heterogeneous collaborative filtering framework, DHCF, which leverages a hypergraph structure to model heterogeneous user-item interactions. This framework integrates intent disentanglement and multi-behavior modeling, utilizing a contrastive learning paradigm to enhance the model's robustness against data sparsity. The model is evaluated on multiple public datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in outperforming various strong baselines. However, concerns about the novelty of the hypergraph structure and the clarity of the paper's presentation were noted.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel hypergraph structure to model the heterogeneous user-item interactions, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on multiple public datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the model.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, including ablation studies and robustness analysis, which helps in understanding the model's performance under different conditions.
- The use of a hypergraph structure to model complex user-item interactions is innovative and adds depth to the model's capabilities.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes detailed theoretical analysis, which supports the model's design choices.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the hypergraph structure is questionable, as similar structures have been previously used in other recommendation systems.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The presentation of the paper is not clear, with some sections being difficult to understand, particularly the technical details of the model's design.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with other state-of-the-art models, such as those using hypergraphs for recommendation tasks.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a few datasets, which may not adequately demonstrate the model's performance across different types of data.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational efficiency of the model, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of the hypergraph structure used in the model and how it differs from previous works?
- How does the model handle the issue of sparsity in user-item interactions, especially in the context of heterogeneous data?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the model, including the training and inference times?
- How does the model handle the potential issue of over-smoothing in the embeddings, especially when using multiple message passing layers?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the model's design choices, particularly in the technical sections that are currently unclear?
- How does the model ensure the interpretability of the embeddings, especially in the context of multi-behavior interactions?
