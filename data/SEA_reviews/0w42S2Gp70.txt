 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LipSim, a robust perceptual similarity metric designed to enhance the resilience of neural networks against adversarial attacks. It builds on the DreamSim metric, employing a student-teacher approach and a 1-Lipschitz feature extractor to ensure robustness. The paper also presents a theoretical framework and empirical results that demonstrate LipSim's effectiveness in maintaining high accuracy even under adversarial perturbations. The authors have provided a detailed analysis of the vulnerabilities of existing perceptual metrics and highlighted the need for a more robust solution. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as it primarily adapts existing techniques without significant innovation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of perceptual similarity metrics, focusing on the robustness of neural networks against adversarial attacks.
- The proposed method, LipSim, is well-motivated and theoretically sound, with a clear explanation of its design and implementation.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of LipSim in maintaining high accuracy under adversarial perturbations.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it accessible to readers and enhancing the paper's readability.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and detailed experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily adapts existing techniques without significant innovation, which may limit its novelty and impact.
- The robustness of LipSim is primarily evaluated using the AutoAttack method, which may not fully represent the robustness of the metric against different types of adversarial attacks.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other robust perceptual similarity metrics, which could provide a clearer picture of LipSim's advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper's claims of "provable guarantees" are misleading, as the robustness of LipSim is not guaranteed against all types of adversarial attacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of LipSim and the scenarios where it might not perform optimally.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the term "provable guarantees" and ensure that it accurately reflects the robustness properties of LipSim?
- How does LipSim compare to other robust perceptual similarity metrics, particularly in terms of robustness and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of LipSim with existing methods, including a discussion on the trade-offs between robustness and natural accuracy?
- In the context of the image retrieval task, how does LipSim perform when the similarity between images is not based on perceptual similarity but rather on other factors like object recognition or classification?
- Can the authors explain the discrepancy in the robustness of LipSim as shown in different attack settings, such as the differences in perturbation budgets?
