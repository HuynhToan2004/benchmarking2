 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TAIL, a method for continual adaptation of pretrained models to new control tasks using lightweight adapters. TAIL employs techniques like LoRA, Bottleneck Adapters, and P-Tuning to adapt large models with minimal additional parameters, thereby reducing computational overhead and preventing catastrophic forgetting. The methodology is evaluated on the LIBERO benchmark, demonstrating superior performance over traditional fine-tuning methods. However, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the results beyond the specific benchmark and the novelty of the approach, which is seen as an incremental improvement over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and comprehensive explanations of the proposed methods and experimental setups.
- The problem addressed, continual adaptation of pretrained models to new control tasks, is significant and relevant, particularly in the context of continual learning and imitation learning.
- The experimental results demonstrate that TAIL outperforms conventional fine-tuning methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance, as evidenced by a thorough comparison of different adaptation methods.
- The paper includes a diverse set of tasks and benchmarks, which provides a robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The use of lightweight adapters for continual adaptation is innovative and addresses the challenge of adapting large models to new tasks without catastrophic forgetting.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on the LIBERO benchmark, which might limit the generalizability of the results to other domains or datasets.
- The methodological contributions of TAIL are seen as incremental, primarily adapting existing techniques like LoRA and Bottleneck Adapters to new tasks without significant novelty.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on how the results apply to other datasets or domains, and the paper does not include comparisons with more recent or diverse models, which could enhance the robustness of the findings.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other continual learning methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, as the method only shows minimal improvements in this area.
- The paper's focus on a specific pretraining dataset (Kitchen) and the lack of a broader evaluation on other datasets could limit the applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the computational costs (e.g., training time, GPU memory usage) of TAIL with other methods, particularly in terms of the efficiency of the LoRA method?
- How do the results of TAIL generalize to other datasets or domains, and what are the implications of using different pretraining datasets?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of TAIL in other domains beyond control tasks, such as natural language processing or computer vision?
- What are the potential benefits of using TAIL with different types of pretrained models, such as those pretrained on different datasets or with different architectures?
- How does the paper address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, and what are the specific contributions of TAIL in this area compared to existing methods?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of hyperparameters and the training procedures, to ensure reproducibility and transparency?
