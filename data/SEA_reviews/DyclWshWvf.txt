 **Summary:**
The paper explores the causal relationships in a feedforward neural network (FNN) by categorizing neurons into three sub-structures: independent, mediate, and confounding. It proposes a method to analyze the causal effects of neurons using a causal discovery algorithm and calculates the average treatment effect for each neuron. The method is tested on a dataset from pediatric ophthalmology, demonstrating the validity of the proposed causal-based analysis method. The paper also discusses the limitations of the method, such as its applicability to non-linear models and its reliance on the accuracy of the causal discovery algorithm.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to analyze the credibility of FNNs from a causal perspective, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors have proposed a method to unify the relations between neurons in the input and output layers into three sub-structures, which is a valuable contribution to the understanding of neural network behavior.
- The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and includes detailed explanations of the proposed method, making it accessible to readers.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, including ablation experiments, and the paper demonstrates the validity of the proposed causal-based analysis method.
- The paper addresses a relevant and important topic, which is the credibility of neural networks, particularly in risk-sensitive fields.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly its applicability to non-linear models and the accuracy of the causal discovery algorithm.
- The paper does not provide a clear definition or explanation of the term "credibility" used in the title and abstract, which could lead to confusion among readers.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed or explained, making it difficult to fully understand the implications and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, which makes it unclear how the proposed method advances the state of the art.
- The paper's experimental results are limited to a single dataset, which does not sufficiently demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications and limitations of the proposed method, as well as a more rigorous evaluation of its effectiveness and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and usage of the term "credibility" in the context of their work?
- How does the proposed method handle non-linear models, and what are the implications of this limitation?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the specifics of the pediatric ophthalmology dataset used and the variables included in the analysis?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Could the authors discuss the practical implications and limitations of their method, and provide examples or case studies to illustrate its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method handle the complexity and potential biases in causal structures, particularly in scenarios where the causal relationships are not straightforward or linear?
