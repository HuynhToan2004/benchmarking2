 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel optimization problem termed "instance-optimal finite-sum minimization," which aims to find a sequence of points that optimize the prefix-sum of functions, each point minimizing the sum of functions up to that point. The authors propose a first-order stochastic method called SIOPT-Grad to solve this problem, demonstrating a significant improvement in gradient complexity over existing methods. The methodology involves a variance reduction technique that enables the algorithm to maintain a lower gradient complexity. The paper also provides theoretical guarantees on the convergence of the proposed algorithm and discusses the implications of these findings for the broader field of optimization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel optimization problem that is both interesting and well-motivated, with potential applications in various areas.
- The proposed method, SIOPT-Grad, is a significant contribution to the field, offering a new approach to solving the instance-optimal finite-sum minimization problem.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive discussion on related work and a thorough analysis of the proposed method.
- The authors have provided a rigorous proof of the convergence of the proposed algorithm, which is a significant theoretical contribution.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing a reduction in the number of function evaluations compared to existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the number of function evaluations required to achieve a given accuracy.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claimed improvements.
- There are several minor typographical and formatting errors that need correction.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions of strong convexity may not hold.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the results for practical applications, especially in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the proposed method and existing methods, particularly in terms of the number of function evaluations required?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability with other existing methods?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and results, including the datasets used and the specific parameters of the algorithms tested?
- What are the potential applications of the proposed method in real-world scenarios, and how does it compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability with other existing methods?
- Could the authors discuss the implications of the results for the broader field of optimization, particularly in terms of the theoretical contributions and practical applications?
