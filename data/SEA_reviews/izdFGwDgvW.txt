 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel zero-shot classification method named Image-Caption Encoding (ICE), which leverages both image and caption embeddings to enhance classification accuracy. The method utilizes a caption-based text encoder to generate captions from images, which are then processed to influence the final prediction. The approach is validated through extensive experiments, demonstrating improvements in top-1 accuracy on various datasets, including a 3% enhancement on challenging datasets. Despite these strengths, the paper faces criticism for its incremental novelty, potential reliance on captions which may not always be accurate, and the computational overhead of generating captions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is straightforward, intuitive, and effective, as demonstrated by consistent improvements across various datasets.
- The use of captions to enhance classification accuracy is a novel approach, particularly leveraging the CoCa text decoder to generate captions that can influence the final prediction.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's effectiveness, including ablation studies that demonstrate the method's robustness and the importance of certain components like the adaptive lambda mechanism and top-K selection.
- The method can be applied to existing models, making it a practical solution for improving classification accuracy without significant retraining or model modification.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited, as the use of captions to guide classification is a well-known concept in the field.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on related works, particularly those that use similar approaches to caption-based classification.
- The method's reliance on captions may limit its applicability in scenarios where captions are not available or are not accurate, such as in noisy or low-quality image captions.
- The computational overhead of generating captions for each image could be significant, especially in large datasets, which might affect the method's practicality.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the method's limitations, such as its performance on noisy or low-quality image captions or its applicability in different domains like object detection or segmentation.
- The method's effectiveness might be limited by the quality of the captions generated, which could introduce additional noise or errors if the captions are not accurate or relevant to the image content.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the method's limitations, particularly in scenarios where captions are not available or are not accurate?
- How does the method perform when applied to other domains like object detection or segmentation?
- Could the authors clarify the method's performance on noisy or low-quality image captions?
- Is there a potential for the method to be adapted to use fewer captions per image to reduce computational overhead?
- How does the method compare to other caption-based classification methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- Can the authors provide more examples or detailed explanations of how the method handles scenarios where the captions are not accurate or relevant to the image content?
