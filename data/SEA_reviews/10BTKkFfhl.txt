 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method to detect and mitigate backdoor attacks in federated learning using a contrastive learning loss. The approach involves a self-supervised training process that utilizes a clean model to identify triggers in potentially backdoored models. The method is evaluated on three datasets and two backdoor attack scenarios, showing some effectiveness in detecting and mitigating backdoors. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of novelty, insufficient experimental validation, and potential inefficiency in practical settings. The methodology is compared against existing methods, and the results suggest that while the proposed method can detect backdoors, it may not outperform other methods in terms of accuracy or efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The problem of backdoor attacks in federated learning is addressed, which is of significant importance in practical applications.
- The paper includes an extensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is a commendable approach to transparency and accountability.
- The use of contrastive learning loss for backdoor detection is an innovative approach that could potentially enhance the effectiveness of backdoor defense mechanisms.
- The methodology is described in sufficient detail, which aids in reproducibility and understanding of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as the methodology appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, with only a limited number of datasets and backdoor attack scenarios tested.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could limit its practical applicability in resource-constrained environments.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the experimental results, which could help in understanding the effectiveness and limitations of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the scalability of the method in large-scale federated learning systems, which is a critical aspect for practical implementation.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant omission in ethical considerations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the methodology compares to existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness in large-scale federated learning systems?
- How does the proposed method perform in different federated learning settings, such as when the number of clients or the complexity of the model increases?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the experimental results, including the accuracy and efficiency of the method under different conditions?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of backdoor attacks, such as those that are not based on triggers?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the scalability and efficiency of the method in large-scale federated learning systems?
