 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to continual learning for language models, specifically focusing on the challenges of catastrophic forgetting. It proposes a method that integrates a vector space retrieval mechanism to manage and adapt to new tasks without relying on traditional methods like experience replay or task-specific IDs. The method, named Scalable Language Model (SLM), utilizes a low-rank adaptation technique to minimize the number of trainable parameters, thereby reducing computational overhead. The approach is validated on various benchmarks, showing improved performance and reduced forgetting. However, concerns are raised about the method's reliance on task-specific IDs and the potential computational cost of the retrieval process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant issue in the field of continual learning for language models, focusing on the challenge of catastrophic forgetting.
- The proposed method is innovative, combining vector space retrieval with low-rank adaptation techniques, which is a novel approach in this context.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and visual aids that aid in understanding the methodology and results.
- The method demonstrates effectiveness across various model scales and benchmarks, showing a reduction in forgetting and improved performance.
- The use of vector space retrieval as a parameter-efficient tuning method is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper claims to be model-agnostic but requires specific model architectures for effective retrieval, which may limit its applicability.
- There is a lack of clarity in the experimental setup, particularly concerning the order of tasks and the evaluation metrics used.
- The method's dependence on task-specific IDs and the retrieval process for task-related knowledge raises concerns about computational efficiency and the practicality of its implementation.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on related work, particularly on parameter-efficient tuning methods and other retrieval-based methods in continual learning.
- The method's generalization ability to more complex or diverse tasks is unclear, and there is a need for more extensive testing on various datasets and tasks.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential issues of over-smoothing in the retrieval process and the impact of random masking on performance.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the model-agnostic claim is upheld, given the specific requirements for effective retrieval?
- How does the method perform when the order of tasks is randomized, and what are the implications for performance and forgetting?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the datasets used and the evaluation metrics employed?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the task ID is not available during inference, and what are the implications for performance?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other retrieval-based methods in continual learning, and discuss the potential advantages or disadvantages of their approach?
- What are the specific challenges in extending the method to more complex or diverse tasks, and how might these be addressed?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the impact of different hyperparameters, such as the number of groups and the masking rate, on performance and forgetting?
