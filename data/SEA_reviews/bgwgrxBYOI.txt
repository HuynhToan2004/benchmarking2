 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel machine learning model for solving partial differential equations (PDEs) with a focus on subgrid modeling, particularly in the context of climate and weather modeling. The model, which is designed to operate on coarse grids, is based on a convolutional architecture with fixed kernels derived from the PDEs. The authors claim that this model can accurately approximate PDE solutions and generalize to out-of-distribution data, which is crucial for modeling complex physical systems. The paper includes experiments demonstrating the model's performance on both in-distribution and out-of-distribution data, showing that it outperforms standard neural networks in terms of accuracy and generalization. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope, focusing only on the heat equation, and its lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in climate and weather modeling, focusing on the subgrid modeling problem which is crucial for accurately predicting processes at unresolved scales.
- The proposed model is simple, well-motivated, and effective, demonstrating superior performance over standard neural networks in terms of accuracy and generalization to out-of-distribution data.
- The experiments are well-designed, showing that the model can accurately approximate the solution operator of the heat equation and generalize to new data distributions, which is crucial for modeling complex physical systems.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise descriptions of the model and its implementation, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing models, particularly in terms of the number of parameters used and the complexity of the architectures, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The scope of the paper is limited to the heat equation, which might not fully demonstrate the versatility or applicability of the proposed model to other types of PDEs.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed model to more complex PDEs or high-dimensional problems, which could limit its practical utility.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed model, which could provide a more balanced perspective on its capabilities and potential shortcomings.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed model with other state-of-the-art methods, which could help in understanding the relative advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the comparison with existing models, including the number of parameters used, the complexity of the architectures, and the results of these comparisons?
- How does the proposed model perform on more complex PDEs, such as the Navier-Stokes equations, and what are the challenges in scaling up the model to handle these more complex problems?
- What are the limitations of the proposed model, and how do these limitations impact its practical applicability and generalizability?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the datasets used and the conditions under which the model was trained and tested?
- How does the model handle different types of initial conditions, and what are the implications of these choices for the model's performance and generalizability?
