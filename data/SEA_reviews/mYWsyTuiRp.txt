 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the role of feed-forward blocks (FFBs) in Transformers by analyzing their impact on input contextualization through attention maps. The authors extend previous norm-based analysis to include FFBs, revealing their effects on linguistic compositions and their interaction with residual and normalization layers. The study demonstrates that FFBs modify input contextualization, emphasizing specific types of linguistic compositions, and shows that their effects are sometimes canceled out by other components. Despite these findings, the paper is criticized for its limited scope, lack of novelty, and insufficient exploration of the implications of these observations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper effectively utilizes attention maps to analyze the input contextualization effects of feed-forward networks, providing a novel approach to understanding the role of FFBs in Transformers.
- The study offers a detailed examination of FFBs, revealing their impact on linguistic compositions and their interaction with residual and normalization layers.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive set of experiments that support the claims.
- The authors provide a thorough analysis of the impact of FFBs on input contextualization, showing that these components can emphasize specific types of linguistic compositions.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments, and the findings are supported by detailed visualizations that aid in understanding the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and convincing motivation for analyzing FFBs, and the novelty of the approach is limited as it extends existing norm-based analysis to include FFBs.
- The study is limited in scope, focusing primarily on BERT and GPT-2, and does not explore the implications of the findings across different types of Transformers or in other domains.
- The paper does not adequately explore the implications of the observed effects of FFBs on input contextualization, and the results are not sufficiently generalizable or actionable.
- The methodology used in the study, particularly the use of integrated gradients, is not sufficiently justified, and the paper could benefit from a more rigorous theoretical analysis.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential redundancy in the processing of the Transformer layer, which is a significant finding.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the motivation behind analyzing FFBs and how this analysis could be extended or applied to other types of Transformers?
- How do the findings of this study relate to other works that have explored the effects of FFBs on input contextualization?
- In the context of the observed effects of FFBs on input contextualization, how do these effects impact the overall performance of the Transformer, and what are the implications for practical applications?
- Could the authors provide more details on the methodology used in the study, particularly the use of integrated gradients and the norm-based analysis?
- How do the findings of this study contribute to a better understanding of the Transformer architecture, and what are the potential implications for the design and optimization of Transformers?
