 **Summary:**
The paper explores the alignment of language models with brain activity by examining how the removal of low-level features affects the predictive accuracy of models. The study compares text-based and speech-based language models, showing that both models can predict brain activity in early sensory areas due to shared low-level features. However, text-based models perform better in late language regions, suggesting that they capture more brain-relevant semantics. The paper also investigates the impact of different low-level features on brain predictivity and proposes that the alignment between language models and brain activity is influenced by both low-level and high-level features.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important question regarding the alignment of language models with brain activity, which is crucial for understanding the neural underpinnings of language processing.
- The experimental design is well-executed, and the results are clearly presented, making the paper easy to follow and understand.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of how different low-level features affect brain predictivity, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The study is well-motivated and presents a clear and logical narrative, which enhances the reader's understanding of the research.
- The paper includes a comprehensive literature review, which provides a solid foundation for the research and helps situate the study within the broader context of the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the study, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential confounding effects of shared information between text and speech, which could influence the results.
- The methodology used in the paper is not clearly explained, particularly the rationale behind the choice of models and the methodology for removing low-level features.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing studies, which could help in contextualizing the findings and assessing the novelty of the contributions.
- The paper's focus on low-level features might not fully capture the complexity of the brain-model alignment, as it does not consider high-level semantic features that could also influence the alignment.

**Questions:**
- How does the paper address the potential confounding effects of shared information between text and speech in the results?

- How does the paper compare its findings with existing studies, and what are the main differences and contributions of this study?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the study and the implications of the findings for future research?
- How do the findings of this study relate to other studies on brain-model alignment, and what are the potential implications for the development of more effective language models?
