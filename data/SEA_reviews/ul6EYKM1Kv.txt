 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to supervised learning using EEG signals, where participants are presented with visual stimuli and asked to mentally note the images that match a given semantic category. The EEG responses are then used to train a contrastive learning model, which embeds the EEG data into a space that can be used to classify images. The method involves a participant-specific matrix and data augmentation to enhance the model's performance. The paper evaluates the approach through clustering and classification tasks, showing that the model can outperform random baselines and traditional machine learning methods. However, the scope of the study is limited to facial images, and the generalizability of the method to other domains or more complex tasks remains unclear.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to supervised learning using EEG signals, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is well-explained, making it easy to understand and replicate.
- The results show that the model can outperform random baselines and traditional machine learning methods, indicating the effectiveness of the approach.
- The use of a participant-specific matrix and data augmentation is a clever approach that enhances the model's performance.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
- The scope of the study is limited to facial images, which may not generalize well to other domains or more complex tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which is a significant concern in neural network models.
- The experimental design is limited, and the results are not sufficiently robust to support the claims made by the authors.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the ethical implications of using EEG signals for supervised learning, which is a critical aspect that should be addressed.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected.

**Questions:**

- How does the model handle the issue of overfitting, and what steps were taken to mitigate this issue?

- Could you clarify the role of the participant-specific matrix and data augmentation in the model's performance, and how these techniques affect the model's ability to generalize to new data?
