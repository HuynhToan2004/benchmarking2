 **Summary:**
The paper introduces RINGER, a diffusion-based transformer model designed for generating 3D conformational ensembles of macrocyclic peptides from their 2D representations. The model is trained on a dataset of macrocyclic peptides and employs a novel approach using redundant internal coordinates to represent macrocycle geometry, which is then converted to Cartesian coordinates using a constrained optimization method. RINGER is evaluated against other methods such as RDKit, OMEGA, GeoDiff, DMCG, and Torsional Diffusion, demonstrating superior performance in terms of RMSD and rRMSD metrics. The model's ability to handle cyclic constraints and its application to macrocyclic peptides, a significant challenge in computational drug discovery, is highlighted. However, concerns about the model's generalizability, computational cost, and the need for more comprehensive comparisons with other models were raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear and simple problem definition.
- The proposed model, RINGER, is innovative and well-designed, leveraging a diffusion-based transformer model that effectively captures the cyclic nature of macrocycles and chiral side chains.
- The paper provides extensive benchmarking and analysis, demonstrating the model's superiority over other methods in generating high-quality and diverse geometries at a fraction of the computational cost.
- The application of RINGER to macrocyclic peptides, a significant challenge in computational drug discovery, is highlighted as a novel and impactful application.
- The model's performance is robust, as shown by a comprehensive evaluation against a variety of baselines, including both physics-based and machine learning-based algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other models, such as GeoDiff and DMCG, to better understand the relative strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.
- The generalizability of the model to larger, more complex macrocycles is not thoroughly addressed, which could limit the practical applicability of the model.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational cost of RINGER compared to other methods, which is crucial for understanding the practical feasibility of the model.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the model handles the cyclic nature of macrocycles, which is a significant aspect of the model's design.
- The paper's focus on macrocycles might limit its applicability to other types of molecules, such as proteins, which could be a significant drawback for broader applicability.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the model, such as its inability to handle non-canonical side chains and its reliance on a post-processing step for valid Cartesian coordinates.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other models, such as GeoDiff and DMCG, to better understand the relative strengths and weaknesses of different approaches?
- How does the model perform on larger, more complex macrocycles?
- What is the computational cost of RINGER compared to other methods, and how does this affect the practical feasibility of the model?
- How does the model handle the cyclic nature of macrocycles, and what are the specific advantages of using internal coordinates over Cartesian coordinates?
- Could the authors clarify the model's limitations, particularly in terms of its applicability to non-canonical side chains and its reliance on a post-processing step for valid Cartesian coordinates?
- How does the model compare to other machine learning-based methods for conformer generation, particularly those that do not use diffusion-based models?
