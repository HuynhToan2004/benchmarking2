 **Summary:**
The paper investigates backdoor attacks in federated learning (FL) by focusing on the identification of "backdoor-critical" (BC) layers, which are crucial for the success of backdoor attacks. It proposes a novel attack methodology that adaptively balances attack effectiveness and stealthiness under various defense strategies. Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed methods against seven state-of-the-art defenses, achieving high backdoor success rates with minimal malicious clients. The paper introduces a layer-wise poisoning attack and a layer-wise flipping attack, which can bypass distance-based, inversion-based, and sign-based defense methods. However, the paper lacks detailed analysis on the computational overhead and the generalizability of the results across different datasets and model architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a logical flow that makes the content easy to understand.
- The authors introduce a novel concept of "backdoor-critical" (BC) layers, which are crucial for the success of backdoor attacks, and propose a method to identify these layers.
- The paper conducts extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed attack methods, showing that they can bypass state-of-the-art defenses and achieve high backdoor success rates with minimal malicious clients.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the attack methods, comparing them against a range of baseline methods and demonstrating their superiority in terms of both backdoor success rate and main task accuracy.
- The proposed attack method is adaptive and can be applied to various defense strategies, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed analysis on the computational overhead of the proposed attack methods, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.
- The generalizability of the results is limited, as the experiments are primarily conducted on one dataset (CIFAR-10) with a specific model architecture (ResNet18).
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the defense methods used, which could limit the understanding of the attack's effectiveness against different types of defenses.
- The paper does not discuss the potential limitations of the attack methods, such as their applicability to different datasets or model architectures, or the impact of different training conditions.
- The paper does not include a comparison with state-of-the-art backdoor attacks, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational overhead of the proposed attack methods, including the time and resources required for each step of the attack?
- How do the authors ensure the generalizability of their findings to other datasets and model architectures? Have they conducted experiments on other datasets to validate their results?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of their attack methods and provide examples or scenarios where the attack might not be effective?
- Could the authors include a comparison with state-of-the-art backdoor attacks to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their proposed methods?
- Have the authors considered the impact of different training conditions, such as different learning rates or regularization strengths, on the success of their attack methods?
