 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a theoretical framework, Behavior Expectation Bounds (BEB), to analyze alignment issues in large language models (LLMs). BEB decomposes the LLM's distribution into two components, one well-behaved and the other ill-behaved, and explores the conditions under which the LLM can be misaligned. The authors demonstrate that even if the ill-behaved component has a negligible probability, it can still be triggered by a prompt of sufficient length. The framework also investigates the impact of aligning prompts and conversational scenarios, showing that the model can resist misalignment in certain conditions. The paper includes empirical results to validate these theoretical findings, although the assumptions and the practical applicability of the results are critically discussed.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a rigorous analysis of the alignment issue in LLMs, which is a significant and timely topic.
- The authors have developed a novel theoretical framework, BEB, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making the theoretical results and proofs clear and accessible.
- The empirical results align well with the theoretical findings, demonstrating the practical relevance of the theoretical analysis.
- The paper addresses a critical and timely issue, the alignment of LLMs, which is of significant interest to the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper makes several assumptions, such as the ability to decompose the LLM's distribution into well- and ill-behaved components, which may not hold in practice.
- The empirical results are limited, and the experiments are not extensive enough to validate the theoretical claims comprehensively.
- The paper does not adequately address how the theoretical results might be applied to improve alignment in practical settings.
- The assumptions and definitions used in the paper, such as the distinction between positive and negative behaviors, are not clearly defined or justified.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the theoretical results might be applied to improve alignment in practical settings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its assumptions and the potential impact of these limitations on the validity of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more empirical evidence to support the assumption that the LLM's distribution can be decomposed into well- and ill-behaved components?
- How do the authors justify the assumption that the well-behaved component has a higher probability than the ill-behaved component?
- Can the authors clarify the definition and practical application of the "behavior" concept used in the paper?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of their assumptions, such as the distinguishability of the components and the length of prompts?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate how the theoretical results might be applied in practical settings?
- How do the authors plan to address the potential negative societal impacts of their findings, particularly in terms of the misuse of LLMs?
