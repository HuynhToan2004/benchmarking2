 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DockGame, a novel game-theoretic framework for rigid multimeric protein docking, viewed as a cooperative game between protein chains where the final assembly structure(s) constitute stable equilibria. Two approaches are proposed: a supervised learning approach using traditional scoring functions and a self-supervised approach based on diffusion generative models. The framework is evaluated on the Docking Benchmark 5.5 (DB5.5) dataset, showing promising results in terms of performance and runtime efficiency. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism regarding the clarity of its game-theoretic formulation, the robustness of its evaluation metrics, and the generalization of its results to real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel game-theoretic framework for multimeric protein docking, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed DockGame framework is evaluated on the Docking Benchmark 5.5 (DB5.5) dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The use of diffusion generative models to predict multiple plausible equilibria in multimeric docking is innovative and potentially beneficial for the community.
- The framework is flexible and can be applied to various docking scenarios, including different numbers of proteins and different docking setups.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the game-theoretic formulation and its implications, which could confuse readers unfamiliar with game theory.
- The evaluation metrics used, such as C-RMSD and TM-score, are not well-explained or justified, particularly in the context of multimeric docking.
- The paper's reliance on PyRosetta for training the potential function raises questions about the generalizability and robustness of the results.
- The method's performance in real-world scenarios is not convincingly demonstrated, with the results primarily based on a single dataset.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing methods, including AlphaFold-Multimer and SynDock, which also address multimeric docking.
- The paper does not adequately address the challenges specific to multimeric docking, such as the large search space and the complexity of protein interactions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the game-theoretic formulation and its implications for the docking problem?
- How does the proposed framework handle the complexity and variability in protein structures, especially in multimeric docking scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, including AlphaFold-Multimer and SynDock?
- How does the framework ensure the robustness and generalizability of the results, especially when using different scoring functions or training datasets?
- Can the authors elaborate on the method's ability to handle different docking setups, such as varying numbers of proteins or different initial configurations?
- How does the framework ensure that the generated equilibria are not just local minima but truly representative of the protein's behavior in real-world scenarios?
