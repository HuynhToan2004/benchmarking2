 **Summary:**
The paper introduces InstructDET, a method for referring object detection (ROD) that leverages foundation models to generate human-like expressions for object detection tasks. It utilizes a combination of LLaVA and LLaMA to generate diverse expressions from images and text prompts, which are then used to train a ROD model. The dataset generated, named InDET, is claimed to be the largest real-world ROD dataset, with over 3.6 million instructions. The paper also presents a new model, DROD, which shows improved performance on existing ROD benchmarks. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive experimental validation, and insufficient comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and interesting problem of referring object detection, which is crucial for practical applications.
- It introduces a novel method for generating human-like expressions using foundation models like LLaVA and LLaMA, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive analysis of the dataset and model performance.
- The proposed method shows improved performance on existing ROD benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.
- The dataset construction is detailed and comprehensive, with a focus on data quality and diversity, which is crucial for training machine learning models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and differentiation between "instruction" and "expression," which could lead to confusion and misunderstanding.
- The methodology for generating instructions from the foundation models is not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to replicate or understand the process fully.
- There is a lack of experimental validation to support the claims made about the diversity and quality of the generated instructions.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing methods, such as those by Zhu et al. and Li & Sigal, which could have provided a clearer context for the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper's focus on generating diverse instructions for ROD is not convincingly demonstrated to be effective in practical applications, such as those involving complex visual scenes or multiple objects.
- There are concerns about the novelty of the approach, as it primarily involves the application of existing models (LLaVA and LLaMA) without significant innovation in the underlying technology.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a clearer definition and differentiation between "instruction" and "expression" in the context of their work?
- How do the authors ensure the quality and diversity of the generated instructions, especially given the potential for hallucination in foundation models?
- Could the authors provide more details on the methodology for generating instructions from the foundation models, including the specific prompts used and the steps involved in the process?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and the effectiveness of the generated instructions?
- Could the authors provide examples or case studies that demonstrate the practical applicability and effectiveness of the generated instructions in complex visual scenarios?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where multiple objects are referred to in the same instruction, and what steps are taken to ensure the correct identification and localization of these objects?
