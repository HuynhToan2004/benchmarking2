 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MultiReAct, a framework designed to enhance the planning capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) in embodied tasks by integrating visual language models and reward models. MultiReAct converts visual demonstrations into actionable language commands, using CLIP as a reward model to identify execution failures, and supports two distinct low-level actors: an imitation learning agent and a code generation-based actor. The framework is tested across a range of long-horizon planning tasks, showing superior performance compared to previous LLM-based methods. Despite the innovative approach, concerns were raised about the novelty, the complexity of the tasks, and the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in embodied AI by integrating multimodal tools to enhance the planning capabilities of LLMs, particularly in complex, long-horizon tasks.
- The proposed MultiReAct framework is novel and innovative, integrating multimodal tools into the reasoning-acting traces generated by LLMs, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and detailed methodologies, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The empirical performance of the proposed method is strong, showing superior performance compared to previous LLM-based methods.
- The integration of a reward model based on CLIP for detecting execution failures is a novel and effective approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those using visual language models for embodied planning tasks.
- The evaluation tasks are limited to simple tasks, which may not fully demonstrate the capabilities of the proposed method in more complex or realistic scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its generalizability and applicability to more complex tasks.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the reward model, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of hallucination in the planning capabilities of LLMs, which is a significant limitation in the field.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the generalizability of the proposed method to unseen tasks or scenarios, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods, particularly those using visual language models for embodied planning tasks?
- Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the reward model and how it is integrated into the planning process?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of hallucination in the planning capabilities of LLMs?
- Can the authors provide more details on the generalizability of the proposed method to unseen tasks or scenarios, and how it performs in more complex or realistic scenarios?
- How does the proposed method handle the complexity of embodied tasks that require nuanced multimodal reasoning?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training data and the training process of the visual language model, and how this affects the performance of the method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the evaluation metrics and how they were chosen to ensure the validity of the results?
