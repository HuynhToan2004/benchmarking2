 **Summary:**
The paper explores federated multi-task reinforcement learning (FRL) using natural policy gradient (NPG) methods, focusing on the convergence guarantees for both vanilla and entropy-regularized settings. The authors propose a decentralized variant of NPG, termed FedNPG, which achieves global convergence rates that are nearly independent of the size of the state-action space. This methodology is designed for fully decentralized communication without the need to share local reward/task information. The paper also discusses the impact of network size and connectivity on convergence rates and provides theoretical insights into the behavior of NPG in federated settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to federated multi-task reinforcement learning (FRL) by proposing a decentralized variant of natural policy gradient (NPG) methods, which achieves global convergence rates that are nearly independent of the size of the state-action space.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it accessible to readers with a background in reinforcement learning.
- The authors have provided theoretical insights into the behavior of NPG in federated settings, which are crucial for understanding the application of NPG in decentralized environments.
- The paper establishes convergence guarantees for the proposed method, which are significant contributions to the field of FRL.
- The paper is original in its approach, as it is the first to propose a federated variant of NPG methods with non-asymptotic global convergence guarantees for multi-task RL.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical evaluation, which could have strengthened the claims made about the practical applicability and effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The theoretical analysis does not adequately address the impact of function approximation on the convergence rates, which is a significant limitation in real-world applications of RL.
- The paper does not compare its results with existing algorithms, which could have provided a better understanding of the advantages and limitations of the proposed methods.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the availability of exact policy evaluation, may not hold in practical scenarios, which could limit the applicability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the challenges and solutions in implementing the proposed methods in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide empirical results to support the claims made about the practical applicability and effectiveness of the proposed methods?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods in terms of convergence rates and practical performance?
- Can the authors discuss the impact of function approximation on the convergence rates and how the proposed method might be adapted to handle this issue?
- How does the proposed method handle the challenges of communication in decentralized settings, and what are the implications for the convergence rates in such scenarios?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly regarding the availability of exact policy evaluation, and discuss how these assumptions might affect the practical applicability of the results?
