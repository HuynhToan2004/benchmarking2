 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for offline reinforcement learning (RL) named TRAIN, which infers rewards from a graph constructed using a small set of labeled state-action pairs. The method leverages a reward propagation graph to estimate rewards for unlabeled data, showing effectiveness across various tasks from the DeepMind Control Suite and Meta-World. The authors demonstrate that the method can achieve significant performance improvements with limited reward annotations, and the graph-based approach allows for the integration of multiple factors influencing the reward. The paper also discusses the convergence of the method and its application in real-world scenarios where reward functions are difficult to access.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible.
- The proposed method, TRAIN, is innovative and addresses a significant challenge in offline RL by utilizing limited reward annotations to infer rewards for unlabeled data.
- The method is supported by empirical evaluations that demonstrate its effectiveness, particularly in complex robotic manipulation tasks.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the convergence of the method, which adds credibility to the proposed approach.
- The method's application in real-world scenarios where reward functions are difficult to access highlights its practical relevance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of the amount and quality of reward annotations required.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently convincing, with some tasks showing minimal or no improvement over baselines.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the method, such as the sensitivity of the method to the quality and quantity of labeled data.
- There is a lack of discussion on the scalability of the method to larger datasets or more complex environments.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational complexity and the scalability of the method, which could affect its practical application.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of the amount and quality of reward annotations required?
- How does the method perform when the labeled data is not representative or contains significant noise?
- What is the computational complexity of the method, and how does it scale with the size of the dataset?
- How does the method handle the issue of reward inference for unlabeled data, especially in scenarios where the reward function is not directly accessible?
- Can the authors provide more experimental results, including learning curves and success rates, to better demonstrate the effectiveness of the method?
