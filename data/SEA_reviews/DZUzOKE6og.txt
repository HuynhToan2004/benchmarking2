 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HypeBoy, a novel generative self-supervised learning (SSL) method for hypergraphs, focusing on the hyperedge filling task. HypeBoy is designed to learn node representations that align with hyperedge membership, which is theoretically linked to node classification. The method is evaluated against 15 baselines and demonstrates superior performance across 11 benchmark datasets. The paper also discusses the theoretical connections between hyperedge filling and node classification, and presents a two-stage training scheme to enhance the method's effectiveness. Despite its strengths, the paper is critiqued for its lack of clarity in methodological descriptions, insufficient theoretical backing, and the absence of comparisons with relevant baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible.
- The proposed hyperedge filling task is innovative and theoretically linked to node classification, providing a novel approach to learning node representations.
- HypeBoy is a practical method that can be applied to any hypergraph neural network, offering a flexible and adaptable solution.
- The paper includes extensive experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of HypeBoy in node classification and hyperedge prediction tasks.
- The theoretical connection between hyperedge filling and node classification is a significant contribution, offering a deeper understanding of the learning process.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and detailed description of the hyperedge filling task and the methodology behind HypeBoy, which could hinder understanding and replication of the results.
- There is a significant absence of comparisons with relevant baselines, such as HyperGCL, which could provide a more robust evaluation of HypeBoy's performance.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to a simplified setting and may not generalize well to more complex or real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of HypeBoy, which could include scalability issues with larger hypergraphs or the impact of hyperparameter choices.
- There is a lack of discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in real-world scenarios where nodes may belong to multiple classes or have varying feature distributions.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the specifics of the hyperedge filling task and the methodology behind HypeBoy, particularly how nodes are selected for completion and how hyperedge membership affects the learning process?
- How does the proposed method compare to other generative SSL methods for hypergraphs, such as HyperGCL?
- Can you provide more details on the training process, including the number of epochs used and the computational resources required?
- How does HypeBoy handle the issue of over-emphasized proximity in hypergraphs, and what are the implications of this for the learning process?
- Could you elaborate on the practical applications of the theoretical results, particularly in scenarios where nodes may belong to multiple classes or have varying feature distributions?
