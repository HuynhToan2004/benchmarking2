 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel complexity measure, the Average-reward Generalized Eluder Coefficient (AGEC), and a new algorithm, Local-fitted Optimization with Optimism (Loop), designed for learning in infinite-horizon average-reward Markov Decision Processes (AMDPs) with general function approximation. The authors propose that this framework is capable of handling nearly all AMDPs, offering a unified theoretical understanding of infinite-horizon AMDPs with general function approximation. The paper also introduces a new algorithmic framework, Loop, which is based on the optimism principle and features a novel construction of confidence sets and a low-switching updating scheme. Theoretical results demonstrate that the Loop algorithm achieves a sublinear regret bound.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new complexity measure, AGEC, which encompasses all previously recognized tractable AMDPs, including tabular AMDPs, linear AMDPs, linear mixture MDPs, AMDPs with low eluder dimension, and newly identified models like linear Q^/V^ AMDPs, kernel AMDPs, and AMDPs with low Bellman eluder dimension.
- The proposed algorithm, Loop, offers flexibility to be implemented either in the model-based or value-based paradigm, depending on the problem type.
- The paper provides a unified theoretical understanding of infinite-horizon AMDPs with general function approximation, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The introduction of AGEC is a novel contribution, and the proofs of the main results are clearly presented.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the main results are presented clearly.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed complexity measure and algorithm, particularly in scenarios where AGEC might not be applicable or effective.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm with existing methods, which could be crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- There is a need for more empirical validation to demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithm, especially in scenarios where it might not perform as well as existing methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made, particularly Assumption 4, which is crucial for the validity of the results.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by including more detailed examples and clarifying the definitions and notations used, especially in the appendices.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed algorithm compare in terms of computational complexity with existing methods?
- Can the authors provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed complexity measure and algorithm?
- Is there a specific scenario where the proposed algorithm might not be as effective as existing methods?
- How does the proposed algorithm perform in scenarios where the optimal state bias function might not be known?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on Assumption 4 and its implications on the results?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and notations used in the paper, especially in the appendices, to enhance the readability and understanding of the content?
