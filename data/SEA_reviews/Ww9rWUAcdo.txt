 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the learnability of adversarial perturbations in neural networks, focusing on a one-hidden-layer neural network trained on orthogonal training data. The authors extend previous work by showing that the decision boundary from adversarial perturbations aligns with that from natural data, except for specific points in the input space. This alignment is demonstrated through theoretical analysis and experimental results, which include both synthetic and real-world datasets. The paper also discusses how adversarial perturbations can be represented as a weighted sum of benign training data, facilitating network generalization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a theoretical validation of the learnability from adversarial perturbations, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical results are well-presented, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable.
- The paper is significant in its exploration of the learnability from adversarial perturbations, which is an important problem in the field of adversarial machine learning.
- The theoretical results are supported by experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, which helps to validate the theoretical claims.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the theoretical results, which is a strength in making the content accessible and understandable.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical results are limited to a one-hidden-layer neural network, which may not generalize well to more complex network architectures.
- The paper assumes that the training data are orthogonal, which is a strong assumption that may not hold in practical scenarios.
- The paper's experimental results are not convincing, with low accuracies reported, which raises questions about the validity of the results.
- The paper's claims about the alignment of decision boundaries from adversarial perturbations and natural data are not sufficiently supported by the experimental results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its work, particularly the practical implications of the theoretical results.
- The paper's discussion on the alignment of decision boundaries from adversarial perturbations and natural data is unclear and not well-supported by the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition of "natural data" and how it relates to the training data?
- How do the authors justify the assumption that the training data are orthogonal, and what are the implications of this assumption on the practical applicability of the results?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the perturbations were generated and the parameters used in the experiments?
- In the context of the paper's claims about the alignment of decision boundaries, can the authors provide more evidence or clarification on how this alignment is achieved and what it means in practical terms?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of their work, particularly the practical implications of the theoretical results?
