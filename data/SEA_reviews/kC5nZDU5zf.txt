 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to filter visual stimuli for embodied AI models, leveraging a task-conditioned bottleneck and a learnable codebook module. This method aims to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of visual representations by focusing on task-relevant information, thereby reducing noise and enhancing generalization across different simulation environments. The approach is evaluated on multiple benchmarks, showing state-of-the-art performance and generalization capabilities. The methodology involves a parameter-efficient codebook module that is trained jointly with the task reward to optimize task performance, acting as a selective filter over visual observations. The paper also includes qualitative analyses that demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach in reducing redundant visual cues and improving task-relevant information retention.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to filter visual stimuli for embodied AI, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the method's operation and its advantages over existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of a learnable codebook module is innovative and effectively improves the performance of the visual representation.
- The paper is well-organized and includes a detailed discussion on the experimental results, which helps in understanding the method's impact and limitations.
- The method's generalization capability is highlighted, showing that it can adapt to new visual domains with minimal fine-tuning.
- The paper includes a thorough analysis of the codebook's impact on the model's performance, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of certain terms and concepts, such as the "codebook module" and the "attention estimates."
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the performance improvements achieved by the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper's experimental setup could be expanded to include more diverse tasks and environments to better demonstrate the method's generalization capabilities.
- The paper's presentation could be improved to make it more accessible to a broader audience, especially those unfamiliar with the specific terminology and concepts used.
- The paper's methodology and experimental design could be more transparent, particularly in terms of the training details and the selection of benchmarks.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the role and operation of the "codebook module" and the "attention estimates" in the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how do these affect its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios?
- How does the method perform when applied to more complex or diverse tasks, such as those involving more objects or more complex environments?
- Could you provide more details on the training process, particularly how the codebook module is trained and how it interacts with the visual representation?
- How does the method handle the generalization to new environments, and what are the implications of this generalization in terms of performance and applicability?
