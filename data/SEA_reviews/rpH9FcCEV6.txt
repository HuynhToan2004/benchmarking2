 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Proximal Initialization Attack (PIA) for membership inference attacks (MIAs) in diffusion models, focusing on both discrete and continuous time models. The PIA utilizes the output at t=0 as the initial noise and measures the errors between forward and backward processes to infer memberships, showing competitive performance with two queries and improved efficiency over existing methods. The method is tested across various image and audio datasets, including TTS models, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty, limited evaluation, and insufficient comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach for membership inference attacks (MIAs) in diffusion models, which is both simple and effective.
- The method is applicable to both discrete and continuous time models, demonstrating its versatility.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed attack method is shown to be effective in both image and audio datasets, including TTS models, demonstrating its applicability across different domains.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the attack method, including the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on attack performance.
- The method is efficient, requiring only two queries, which is a significant improvement over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing MIAs, particularly the SecMI method, which is crucial for establishing the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, focusing only on a few datasets and not sufficiently comparing the results across different models and settings.
- The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies, particularly regarding the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on attack performance.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method in detail, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper's claims about the robustness of audio models are not well-supported by the data presented, and the results on different models are not sufficiently explained.
- There are several minor typographical and formatting errors that need correction.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with the SecMI method, including a side-by-side comparison of results and a discussion on the differences and similarities between the two methods?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of efficiency and effectiveness with other existing MIAs, particularly those that do not require the knowledge of the training data?
- Can the authors clarify the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the value of t, and provide more detailed ablation studies on their impact on attack performance?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and under what conditions might it fail or be less effective?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the attacker does not have access to the intermediate outputs of the diffusion model?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results and explanations for the experiments on different models, particularly the GradTTS model, to better understand the observed differences in performance?
