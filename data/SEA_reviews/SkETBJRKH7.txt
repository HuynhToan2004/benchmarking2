 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel architecture, LLM-PFC, inspired by the prefrontal cortex (PFC) in the human brain, designed to enhance planning capabilities in large language models (LLMs). This architecture comprises six modules: TaskDecomposer, Actor, Monitor, Predictor, Evaluator, and Orchestrator. Each module corresponds to a specific function in the PFC, such as task decomposition, action selection, and error monitoring. The model was evaluated on three challenging planning tasks, demonstrating significant improvements over standard LLM methods. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the approach, the clarity of presentation, and the lack of detailed experimental results and comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and introduces an innovative approach to enhancing planning capabilities in large language models (LLMs) by drawing inspiration from the prefrontal cortex (PFC) in the human brain.
- The concept of using LLMs to mimic the functions of the prefrontal cortex (PFC) is intriguing and offers a new perspective on improving the planning abilities of LLMs.
- The paper is original in its approach, providing a novel architecture and methodology that could potentially influence future research in this area.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, and the results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in enhancing LLM performance on challenging planning tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation between the proposed method and existing approaches, particularly in terms of novelty and methodological contributions.
- There is a notable absence of detailed experimental results, including the specifics of the tasks used, the number of examples, and the metrics employed, which could undermine the credibility of the results.
- The presentation of the paper is not clear, with important details relegated to appendices, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the methodology and results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of its approach, particularly the potential over-reliance on the LLM's inherent limitations and the need for more diverse or complex tasks to demonstrate the full potential of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing approaches, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the proposed method differs from existing approaches, particularly in terms of novelty and methodological contributions?
- How do the authors ensure that the LLM does not overfit to the training data, and what measures are in place to prevent this?
- In the context of the tasks used, how do the authors determine the optimal number of subgoals for the TaskDecomposer module to generate?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the tasks used, the number of examples, and the metrics employed?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could you elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might be addressed in future work?
