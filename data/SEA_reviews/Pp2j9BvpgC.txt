 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to visual attribute recognition using a generative prompting strategy with an image-conditioned prefix language model (prefixLM). The methodology involves using prefixLM to predict the next token based on visual inputs and previous texts, which encodes object-attribute dependencies. During inference, the model generates relational prompts to measure object-attribute alignment, thereby serving as a meta-model for attribute recognition. The paper proposes a new benchmark, Visual Genome Attribute Ranking (VGAR), and compares the generative prompting with contrastive prompting on two visual reasoning datasets. The experiments demonstrate that generative prompting outperforms contrastive prompting, and the proposed methodology shows potential for broader applications in visual reasoning tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of a new benchmark, VGAR, is a significant contribution that could be valuable for the community.
- The proposed method shows promising results, particularly in zero-shot attribute recognition, outperforming contrastive prompting.
- The use of prefixLM as a foundational model for capturing complex object-attribute relationships in pre-training is innovative and effective.
- The paper provides a novel generative prompting mechanism that explicitly models dependencies between objects and attributes, which can be used to create different probabilistic models for various visual tasks.
- The methodology is flexible and adaptable, enabling the creation of arbitrary conditional dependency models for various visual tasks at inference time.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing methods like TAP, which could have provided a better understanding of the method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not include sufficient qualitative results, which are crucial for understanding the method's performance in real-world scenarios.
- The method's generalization to other visual tasks beyond attribute recognition is not clearly demonstrated.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the dataset construction process for VGAR, including the selection criteria for false attributes.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the computational complexity and memory requirements of the proposed method, which could be a concern for practical deployment.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with other large language models, which could help in understanding its relative effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with TAP, including a discussion on the method's effectiveness in handling different types of visual tasks?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other visual tasks beyond attribute recognition? Could the authors provide examples or results to support this claim?
- How does the proposed method handle the generalization to unseen attributes?
- How does the method perform when using different image encoders, such as a frozen CLIP encoder?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the dataset construction process for VGAR, including the selection criteria for false attributes?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and memory requirements with other state-of-the-art methods?
- Could the authors provide more qualitative results to support the claims made about the method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios?
