 **Summary:**
The paper introduces H-GAP, a generative model for humanoid motion planning that utilizes a large dataset of motion capture data to learn a prior distribution over state-action trajectories. H-GAP employs a Variational Quantization-Variational Autoencoder (VQ-VAE) to discretize the trajectory space and a Transformer to model the prior distribution, which is used for Model Predictive Control (MPC) planning. The model is trained on a large dataset, MoCapAct, and is evaluated on various tasks such as imitation learning and downstream control tasks, showing competitive performance compared to existing methods. The paper also explores the scaling properties of the model and its generalization capabilities, providing insights into the potential of using large datasets for generative modeling in robotics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the problem and a well-structured approach.
- The use of a large dataset for generative modeling in robotics is innovative and shows potential for significant advancements in the field.
- The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can be applied to a variety of tasks and scales well with model and dataset size.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the model's performance, including comparisons with existing methods and an analysis of the model's scaling properties.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the training and planning processes, making it accessible to readers with different backgrounds.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the work, which could be crucial for understanding the broader implications of the research.
- The generalization capabilities of the model are not adequately discussed, especially how it might perform in environments or tasks that are different from those in the training dataset.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other existing methods, such as those using different generative models or different planning methods, which could help clarify the advantages of the proposed approach.
- The method's reliance on a large dataset raises questions about its applicability in scenarios where such data is not available, and the paper does not discuss potential solutions or alternatives.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed descriptions of the training and planning processes, including hyperparameter choices and ablations, to better understand the model's performance and scalability.

**Questions:**
- Can the model generalize to environments or tasks that are different from those in the training dataset? If not, what are the limitations, and how might these be addressed?
- How does the model perform when applied to tasks that are not present in the training data? What are the implications for the model's ability to adapt to new tasks?
- How does the model handle tasks that are not well-represented in the training data? Does it still perform well, or does it struggle with these tasks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and planning processes, including the choice of hyperparameters and the effects of different choices on the model's performance?
- How does the model perform when applied to tasks with different reward structures? What are the implications for the model's ability to optimize for different goals?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their work and how these might be mitigated?
