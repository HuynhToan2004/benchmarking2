 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ProGO, a novel derivative-free global optimization algorithm that leverages a probabilistic approach to approximate global optima without requiring gradient information. It extends the theoretical framework of Luo (2018) to non-compact sets and provides convergence guarantees under certain conditions. The algorithm employs a latent slice sampler to sample from the nascent minima distribution, which is shown to converge to the global optima under mild regularity conditions. Experimental results demonstrate that ProGO outperforms existing methods in terms of regret value and convergence speed on various test functions. However, the paper's theoretical contributions are questioned due to a lack of novelty and clarity in the presentation, and the experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel derivative-free global optimization algorithm, ProGO, which is based on a probabilistic approach and does not require gradient information, making it more robust to initial conditions.
- The methodology is well-organized, and the paper is easy to follow, with clear explanations and examples that aid in understanding the theoretical and practical aspects of the proposed algorithm.
- The paper provides a rigorous mathematical framework for analyzing the convergence of the proposed algorithm, which is supported by both theoretical analysis and extensive empirical evidence.
- The experimental results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing state-of-the-art methods, showcasing its effectiveness in high-dimensional spaces.
- The paper is well-written and clearly presents the motivation, methodology, and results, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the computational efficiency of the algorithm, particularly in high-dimensional spaces, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The theoretical contributions of the paper are limited, with the main theoretical results being largely extensions of existing work by Luo (2018).
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art algorithms, which could have provided a clearer picture of the algorithm's advantages and disadvantages.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed algorithm, which could affect its generalizability and applicability.
- There are several presentation issues, such as unclear definitions and notations, which could hinder the understanding of the paper.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the algorithm, especially in high-dimensional spaces.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the computational efficiency of the algorithm, particularly in high-dimensional spaces, and discuss potential strategies for improving its performance?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare with other state-of-the-art derivative-free optimization algorithms in terms of computational efficiency and convergence rate?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other algorithms, possibly including a table that summarizes the advantages and disadvantages of each method?
- In the experimental results, why does the ProGO algorithm achieve a negative log regret value, and what does this imply about the algorithm's performance?
- How does the algorithm handle the strong separability condition, and what are the implications if this condition is not met?
- Could the authors discuss the potential applications of the proposed algorithm in real-world scenarios, especially in domains where gradient-based methods are not feasible?
