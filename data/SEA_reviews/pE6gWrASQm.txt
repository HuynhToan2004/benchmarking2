 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of adversarial training (AT) on a subset of the training data, specifically by constructing adversarial examples for a subset A and using standard clean examples for another subset B. The authors investigate the transferability of adversarial robustness from the adversarially trained subset A to the non-adversarially trained subset B. The paper also examines the impact of adversarial training on the robustness of classes and tasks that were not directly trained on adversarial examples. The results suggest that adversarial robustness can transfer to classes and tasks that were not directly attacked during training, and that hard examples are more effective in enhancing robustness. The paper provides a detailed analysis of the transferability of adversarial robustness and the impact of adversarial training on the robustness of classes and tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to adversarial training (AT) by focusing on the subset of training data, which is a significant departure from traditional AT methods that involve perturbing all examples.
- The analysis of adversarial robustness transferability is thorough, covering various aspects such as the impact of different classes and examples, and the transferability of adversarial robustness to downstream tasks.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering multiple datasets and different types of adversarial attacks, which strengthens the validity of the findings.
- The results show that adversarial robustness can transfer to classes and tasks that were not directly trained on adversarial examples, suggesting that adversarial training can improve the robustness of models without requiring extensive training on adversarial examples.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the transferability of adversarial robustness and the impact of adversarial training on the robustness of classes and tasks, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed approach, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The methodology of splitting the training set into two subsets and the choice of adversarial attack method (PGD-7) are not adequately justified, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with existing methods that also use a subset of the training data, which could provide a better understanding of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.
- The results are not sufficiently robust, as the paper relies on a limited number of experiments and does not provide a detailed analysis of the statistical significance of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of different types of adversarial attacks on the transferability of adversarial robustness, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
