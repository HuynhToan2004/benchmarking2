 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel architecture for LSTM networks by integrating additional layers within the LSTM unit, which is termed "Boosted LSTM." This modification is aimed at enhancing the memory and reasoning capabilities of LSTMs, with a specific focus on the use of Exponential Linear Units (ELU) as the activation function for these additional layers. The paper presents empirical results across three datasets, showing that Boosted LSTM can outperform traditional LSTMs and other models like GRU and BiLSTM in certain scenarios. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as the core idea of adding layers to enhance LSTM capabilities is not new. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is seen as insufficient, with only minor improvements observed and no significant ablation studies conducted.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the motivation behind the research is clearly articulated.
- The introduction of additional layers within the LSTM unit, referred to as "Boosted LSTM," is a novel approach that enhances the model's performance.
- The paper provides empirical results on three datasets, showing that Boosted LSTM outperforms traditional LSTMs and other models like GRU and BiLSTM in certain scenarios.
- The paper includes a thorough analysis of the impact of different placements of additional layers, activation functions, and the number of hidden units on the performance of Boosted LSTM.
- The results show that Boosted LSTM with ELU activation functions can outperform traditional LSTMs and other models in terms of accuracy and loss.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity introduced by the additional layers and the impact on training time, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed architecture.
- The paper does not include a detailed comparison with other advanced models like transformers or other recent LSTM architectures, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The experimental evaluation is insufficient, with only minor improvements observed and no significant ablation studies conducted to understand the impact of different design choices.
- The novelty of the paper is limited as the core idea of adding layers to enhance LSTM capabilities is not new, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that Boosted LSTM requires fewer epochs to achieve similar or better results, and there is no discussion on the computational complexity or time complexity introduced by the additional layers.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scenarios where Boosted LSTM may not be effective.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a detailed comparison of the computational complexity and training time between Boosted LSTM and traditional LSTMs?
- How does Boosted LSTM perform on larger datasets, and what are the scalability issues, if any?
- Can you elaborate on the impact of different activation functions (ELU, SELU, ReLU) on the performance of Boosted LSTM?
- Why was the decision made to use Boosted Cell State ReLU and Boosted Cell State ELU as the final models for the IMDB and MNIST datasets, respectively?
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent LSTM architectures like Nested LSTMs, and what are the specific advantages of Boosted LSTM?
- Could you provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method and scenarios where it may not be effective?
