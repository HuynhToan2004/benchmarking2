 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method called Linguistically-Diverse Prompting (LDP) aimed at enhancing the performance of large language models (LLMs) in low-resource languages. This method leverages synthetic data from high-resource languages to train LLMs to translate between various languages, including English, and low-resource languages. The approach involves using in-context exemplars from diverse high-resource languages to prompt LLMs, which are then used to generate intra-lingual exemplars for specific tasks in target languages. The method is evaluated across multiple languages and tasks, showing competitive performance with supervised methods and even surpassing them in some cases. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation scope and potential generalizability to other LLMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology and results.
- The idea of using linguistically-diverse prompts to help LLMs perform tasks in low-resource languages is novel and innovative.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages and tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various scenarios.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are robust, showing that the proposed method outperforms other English-pivoting methods and is favored by GPT-4-EVAL.
- The paper is significant in its potential to advance the field of low-resource language processing by enabling LLMs to perform tasks in languages with limited data.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on a single LLM (BLOOM), which limits the generalizability of the findings to other LLMs.
- The evaluation is limited to a few languages, which raises concerns about the method's effectiveness across a broader range of languages.
- The paper does not discuss potential societal impacts, which is a significant omission.
- The methodology relies heavily on the quality of high-resource language data, which may not be available for all languages, limiting its applicability.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation.
- The paper's claims about the method's effectiveness are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, particularly in the non-English-centric translation experiments.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other LLMs, such as InstructGPT?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the method performs across a broader range of languages, particularly those not evaluated in the current study?
- How does the method handle the potential issue of data leakage from high-resource languages to low-resource languages?
- Could the authors clarify the methodology used for the non-English-centric translation experiments and explain the discrepancies in performance observed in Table 2?
- How does the method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
