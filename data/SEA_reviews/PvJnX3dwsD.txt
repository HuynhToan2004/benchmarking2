 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the catapult phase phenomenon in neural networks, focusing on the behavior of quadratic models trained with large learning rates. The authors explore how these models mimic the dynamics of wide neural networks, including the catapult phase where the loss initially increases before decreasing. Theoretical analysis is provided for quadratic models using Taylor expansion, and empirical evidence is presented across various datasets and neural network architectures to demonstrate the effectiveness of quadratic models in capturing the generalization properties of neural networks. The study is significant as it offers insights into the optimization and generalization discrepancies between finite and infinite width neural networks, and provides a novel perspective on the behavior of neural networks under different learning rate regimes.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and precise mathematical formulations, and the empirical results are well-presented.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the catapult phase phenomenon in neural networks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical results are supported by empirical evidence, showing that quadratic models can effectively mimic the behavior of neural networks under certain conditions.
- The paper is original in its approach, focusing on the catapult phase phenomenon which is not extensively studied, and offers new insights into the behavior of neural networks under different learning rate regimes.
- The paper is significant in its implications for understanding the optimization and generalization discrepancies between finite and infinite width neural networks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the quadratic model in capturing the full behavior of neural networks, especially in terms of the loss landscape and generalization performance.
- The paper assumes a constant learning rate, which might not be the most practical scenario for neural network training, and the effects of varying learning rates are not explored.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is restricted to two-layer networks, which might not fully capture the behavior of deeper networks.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the quadratic model with other models or discuss the implications of the catapult phase phenomenon in more practical scenarios, such as in training with stochastic gradients.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of quadratic models over linear models in generalization performance are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which are limited to test loss and do not include accuracy metrics.

**Questions:**
- How does the quadratic model perform in terms of generalization performance, especially in terms of test accuracy?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the quadratic model in capturing the full behavior of neural networks, especially in terms of the loss landscape and generalization performance?
- How does the catapult phase phenomenon manifest in deeper networks, and what are the implications for the behavior of neural networks in practical scenarios?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly regarding the initialization of weights and the effects of varying learning rates?
- How does the quadratic model compare with other models in terms of its ability to capture the behavior of neural networks under different learning rate regimes?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, especially in terms of the training dynamics and the effects of different learning rates on the generalization performance of the quadratic model?
