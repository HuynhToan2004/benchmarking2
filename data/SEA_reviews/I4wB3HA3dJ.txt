 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DISAM, a novel method aimed at improving the generalization of models under domain shifts by integrating domain-level convergence consistency into the generation of perturbations in the Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) framework. DISAM minimizes the variance of domain loss during perturbation generation, enabling adaptive weight adjustments for each domain based on its convergence degree. This approach enhances convergence across training domains and generalization on unseen domains, as demonstrated by extensive experiments on domain generalization benchmarks. The paper also discusses the computational efficiency of DISAM, showing that it incurs negligible overhead.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise presentation of the methodology, and the results are clearly explained.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, as demonstrated by the experimental results, which show that DISAM outperforms other SAM-based methods in domain generalization tasks.
- The paper includes extensive experiments on domain generalization benchmarks, which prove the superiority of DISAM over existing methods.
- DISAM is computationally efficient, with negligible overhead, and is easy to implement.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the DISAM algorithm, including its convergence properties and computational complexity.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of related works, particularly those that address similar issues in domain generalization.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets and do not include comparisons with a broader range of methods, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The convergence analysis in the paper seems to be inconsistent with the actual convergence of the algorithm, which could lead to confusion about the theoretical contributions of the paper.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the introduction and the motivation for the method, which could make it difficult for readers to understand the key contributions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between the proposed method and other existing methods, such as those mentioned in related works?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different datasets or in different scenarios, such as those with varying domain shifts or different model architectures?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the convergence properties of the DISAM algorithm, particularly in relation to the theoretical claims made in the paper?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the datasets used and the training conditions, to better understand the results reported in the paper?
