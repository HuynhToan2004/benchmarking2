 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Inverse Dual Values Estimation (IDVE) framework for offline Inverse Constrained Reinforcement Learning (ICRL), addressing the challenge of learning constraints from offline datasets. IDVE leverages a bi-level optimization approach to estimate dual values functions for both rewards and costs, aiming to ensure safety and conservatism in control strategies. The methodology is designed to handle unknown transitions, scale to continuous environments, and control the sparsity of learned constraints. The paper provides empirical results demonstrating that IDVE outperforms existing baselines in terms of constraint recovery and adapts to high-dimensional environments with diverse reward configurations. However, the paper is critiqued for its lack of clarity in methodological details, particularly in the handling of unknown transitions and the role of expert demonstrations in constraint learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, IDVE, is novel and technically sound, introducing a bi-level optimization method for updating value functions, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- IDVE addresses critical issues in offline ICRL, such as handling unknown transitions, scaling to continuous environments, and controlling the sparsity of learned constraints.
- The paper includes comprehensive empirical studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of IDVE in various settings, showing that it outperforms existing baselines in terms of constraint recovery and adapting to high-dimensional environments with diverse reward configurations.
- The methodology is innovative, particularly in its application of dual values functions for both rewards and costs, and the bi-level optimization problem for updating these value functions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some methodological details, particularly in the handling of unknown transitions and the role of expert demonstrations in constraint learning.
- The motivation behind the proposed method is not clearly articulated, making it difficult for readers to understand the rationale behind the approach.
- The experimental setup is limited, with only a few environments used for evaluation, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing offline RL methods, especially in terms of the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- There are some inconsistencies and potential errors in the mathematical formulations and proofs, which could affect the credibility of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the method handles unknown transitions and how expert demonstrations are used to infer constraints?
- How does the proposed method ensure the identifiability of the learned constraints, especially in continuous environments?
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the offline dataset?
- How does the method ensure that the learned constraints are generalizable to new environments or scenarios that were not included in the training data?
- Could you explain the choice of the f-divergence in the method and how it affects the learning process?
- In the experimental results, why does IDVE w/oS perform better in some environments, and IDVE w/oA in others?
- Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process and how it affects the performance of the method?
