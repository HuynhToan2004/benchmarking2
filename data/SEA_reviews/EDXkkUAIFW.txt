 **Summary:**
The paper explores a one-shot active learning algorithm for multiple deep models, focusing on minimizing the number of samples needed to achieve constant-factor approximations. It employs a novel approach by leveraging Lewis weights to sample unlabeled data, thereby reducing the computational complexity compared to traditional iterative methods. Theoretical analysis and extensive experiments on 11 benchmarks demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness and efficiency. The methodology allows for learning from diverse representations, improving upon existing techniques that rely on a single model or a fixed set of models. Despite its theoretical and experimental strengths, the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods and a clearer discussion on the limitations and practical implications of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach for active learning that is both theoretically sound and experimentally validated, showing significant improvements over existing methods.
- The use of Lewis weights to sample unlabeled data is a novel and effective method, which is supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results.
- The manuscript is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed algorithm demonstrates significant improvements in sample complexity, which is crucial for practical applications in machine learning.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the proposed algorithm, including theoretical guarantees and extensive experimental results, which support the claims of its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and practical implications of the proposed approach, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions of the model may not hold.
- There is a need for a more thorough comparison with existing methods, especially in terms of computational complexity and sample efficiency.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the practical implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the fixed network backbone and the availability of the data matrix A.
- The experimental settings and the choice of parameters (such as the number of models and the query budget) are not clearly justified, which could affect the interpretation of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly how they translate into practical improvements in machine learning applications.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed algorithm compare in terms of computational complexity and sample efficiency with existing methods, especially in scenarios where the number of models or the query budget varies?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental settings, such as the choice of parameters and the justification for the query budgets used?
- How does the proposed algorithm perform in scenarios where the data matrix A is not available, or where the network backbone is not fixed?
- Could you elaborate on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly how they translate into practical improvements in machine learning applications?
