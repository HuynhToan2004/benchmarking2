 **Summary:**
The paper introduces UniAdapter, a framework designed for parameter-efficient cross-modal transfer learning, which unifies adapters for different modalities and their interactions with a knowledge-sharing design. The approach aims to reduce the number of tunable parameters while maintaining or enhancing performance compared to full fine-tuning. The UniAdapter is evaluated across various downstream tasks, including video-text retrieval, image-text retrieval, video and visual question answering, and captioning, showing competitive or superior results. The methodology involves the use of residual learning for language queries and parameter-free frame-aware attention to manage noise in video frames. Despite the promising results, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty and lack of comparison with some relevant methods, which could affect its positioning in the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the challenge of transferring a vision-language model to downstream tasks in a parameter-efficient manner, which is crucial given the increasing size of foundation models and the number of downstream tasks.
- The proposed UniAdapter method is a novel approach that effectively reduces the number of tunable parameters while maintaining or enhancing performance compared to full fine-tuning.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides extensive evaluations on six cross-modal downstream benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the UniAdapter.
- The use of residual learning for language queries and parameter-free frame-aware attention are innovative techniques that address specific challenges in cross-modal transfer learning.
- The methodology is supported by thorough ablation studies and empirical evidence, which validate the effectiveness of the proposed components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is somewhat limited as the components such as residual learning for language queries, parameter-free frame-aware attention, and uni-adapter are not entirely new and have been previously explored in other works.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with relevant methods, particularly those that use similar techniques like LGDN and MAM, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's advantages or disadvantages.
- The results presented in the paper, such as those in Table 2, do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the UniAdapter over existing methods, especially when considering the number of tunable parameters.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the impact of different weight-sharing strategies on the performance of the UniAdapter, which is crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational costs and training time of the UniAdapter, which are essential for evaluating its practical applicability.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is necessary for a complete understanding of its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific contributions of the UniAdapter over existing methods like LGDN and MAM, particularly in terms of novelty and performance?
- How does the UniAdapter perform when compared to other parameter-efficient methods that use similar techniques, such as those mentioned in the review?
- What are the computational costs and training time associated with the UniAdapter, and how do these compare to other parameter-efficient methods?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons of the UniAdapter with other methods, especially in terms of the number of tunable parameters and performance metrics?
- How does the UniAdapter handle different weight-sharing strategies, and what are the implications of these strategies on the performance and efficiency of the method?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the UniAdapter and provide examples or scenarios where it might not be the most effective solution?
