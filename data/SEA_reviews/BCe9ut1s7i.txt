 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the adversarial robustness of object detectors, particularly focusing on the role of adversarially pre-trained backbone networks. It challenges the conventional wisdom that adversarially pre-trained backbones do not enhance adversarial robustness and instead argues that they are crucial for improving robustness. The authors propose a new training recipe for adversarial fine-tuning on object detectors, demonstrating that this approach significantly outperforms previous methods. The paper also explores the impact of different object detectors and backbones on adversarial robustness, providing insights into the design of more robust object detection systems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the important and timely issue of adversarial robustness in object detection, a topic that is under-explored compared to image classification.
- The proposed training recipe for adversarial fine-tuning on object detectors is simple yet effective, demonstrating significant improvements over previous methods.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of different object detectors and backbones, offering valuable insights into the design of more robust object detection systems.
- The findings of the paper, such as the importance of adversarially pre-trained backbones and the role of the backbone in adversarial robustness, are significant and contribute to the understanding of adversarial robustness in object detection.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison of the proposed training recipe with existing adversarial training methods, such as PGD, FGSM, and CWA, which could enhance the understanding of its advantages and limitations.
- The experiments are primarily conducted on the PASCAL VOC dataset, which may not fully represent the performance of the proposed methods on other datasets like COCO.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational costs associated with adversarial pre-training, which could be a significant barrier for practical implementation.
- The paper's conclusions about the importance of adversarially pre-trained backbones might not generalize well to other types of adversarial attacks or different architectures, limiting the broader applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the potential limitations and ethical considerations of the proposed methods, especially in terms of their impact on the reliability and safety of object detection systems.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the proposed training recipe with existing adversarial training methods, including PGD, FGSM, and CWA?
- How does the proposed training recipe perform on other datasets like COCO, and can it be adapted to other types of adversarial attacks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational costs associated with adversarial pre-training, including the training time and hardware requirements?
- How do the findings of this paper apply to other types of adversarial attacks, such as transfer attacks, and what are the potential limitations of the proposed methods in these scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential ethical implications of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of the reliability and safety of object detection systems?
