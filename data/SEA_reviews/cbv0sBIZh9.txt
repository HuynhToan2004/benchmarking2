 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel diffusion model framework for multi-task generative modeling, integrating image generation with classification tasks. It proposes a method for aggregating task-specific information into a shared diffusion space, enabling simultaneous training and inference across multiple tasks. The model is tested on various tasks including image transition, masked-image training, and joint image-label generation, showing promising results in terms of image quality and classification accuracy. The paper provides a theoretical justification for the multi-task loss and presents a novel approach to handling multiple tasks within a diffusion model framework.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed derivations that make the content easy to follow.
- The proposed method for multi-task diffusion models is novel and provides a solid theoretical foundation, particularly in the derivation of the multi-task loss.
- The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing promising results in terms of image quality and classification accuracy.
- The method is flexible and can be applied to various tasks, including image generation and classification, showing potential for broader applications.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the forward and reverse processes, and the methodology is clearly outlined.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in the context of multi-task generative modeling.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some tasks showing underperformance compared to single-task models.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method to larger, more complex datasets or tasks.
- There are concerns about the novelty of the proposed method, as it seems to overlap with existing approaches such as guided diffusion models.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential directions for future research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and existing approaches, particularly in terms of novelty and practical application?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, especially when applied to larger or more complex datasets?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the training and inference processes, to better understand the model's performance?
- What are the implications of the method's assumptions about task data, and how do these assumptions affect the model's applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare to other diffusion models in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements?
- Can the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their work, particularly in the context of generative modeling and multi-task learning?
