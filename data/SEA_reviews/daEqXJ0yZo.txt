 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for generating stylized human motion from motion capture data, leveraging a latent space for style encoding. The approach involves decomposing motion into content and style codes, which are then used to generate diverse stylized motions. The method uses a pre-trained autoencoder for motion encoding and decoding, and incorporates a style encoder and a content encoder to manage the style and content codes, respectively. The paper also includes a global motion predictor to enhance the realism of the generated motions. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through both qualitative and quantitative results, showing improvements over existing methods in terms of diversity and realism.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly explained.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance in terms of style recognition and content preservation.
- The use of a probabilistic style space allows for the generation of diverse and novel style samples, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is efficient and fast, which is beneficial for real-time applications.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The writing is clear, and the paper is well-motivated, making it a valuable contribution to the field of motion style transfer.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The use of a pre-trained autoencoder for motion encoding and decoding raises questions about the necessity and effectiveness of this approach, especially considering the potential for overfitting or inaccurate encoding.
- The method's reliance on a global motion predictor may not fully capture the nuances of individual motions, particularly in terms of foot sliding and other subtle movements.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the method to different types of motion capture data or different applications beyond human motion stylization.
- There is a lack of detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the method's ability to handle different types of motion capture data and its applicability to various domains.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the method's sensitivity to hyperparameters and its scalability to different domains.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the necessity and effectiveness of using a pre-trained autoencoder for motion encoding and decoding?
- How does the method handle different types of motion capture data, particularly in terms of its applicability and effectiveness?
- What are the specific advantages of using a probabilistic style space over a deterministic approach?
- Can the method be adapted to handle different types of style transfer, such as text-to-motion or other forms of data?
- How does the method perform in terms of diversity and realism when compared to other state-of-the-art methods?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the quality of generated motions?
- What are the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how might these affect its practical applicability?
