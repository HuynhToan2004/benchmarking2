 **Summary:**
The paper explores the phenomenon of "agreement-on-the-line" (AGL) in test-time adaptation (TTA) methods, where the correlation between in-distribution (ID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) accuracies is strong. The authors propose methods for estimating OOD accuracy, calibrating models, and tuning hyperparameters based on this observation. They also introduce an unsupervised calibration method and a reliable hyperparameter optimization strategy. The paper presents extensive experiments across various datasets and models, demonstrating that these methods can achieve results comparable to those using ground-truth labels. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the AGL phenomenon and the generalizability of the findings, particularly in real-world scenarios where data is limited.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed methods and their applications.
- The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work, effectively positioning their research within the existing literature.
- The paper introduces an unsupervised calibration method that effectively reduces calibration error, as shown in Table 3.
- The authors conduct extensive experiments across various datasets and models, demonstrating that their methods can achieve results comparable to those using ground-truth labels.
- The findings on the agreement-on-the-line phenomenon and its implications for test-time adaptation (TTA) are significant and could lead to more reliable and effective TTA methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the AGL phenomenon is limited as it has been previously reported in the literature, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from existing works.
- The paper's claims about the reliability of TTA methods are not convincingly supported by the experiments, particularly in terms of the practical applicability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, such as their generalizability to real-world scenarios and their performance in more complex or varied distribution shifts.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis or intuitive explanation for the AGL phenomenon, which could enhance the understanding and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the performance of their methods on the FMoW-WILDS dataset, particularly in terms of OOD accuracy?
- How does the paper address the potential societal impacts of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of privacy and fairness?
- In the experiments where TTA methods are shown to fail, can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of why these methods fail and how their proposed methods could mitigate these failures?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the "ID-trained" models in their experiments, particularly in relation to the selection of hyperparameters?
- How do the authors justify the use of the "ID-trained" models in their experiments, and what are the implications of using different models for different datasets?
