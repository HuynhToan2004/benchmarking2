 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the extrapolation capabilities of implicit models, particularly deep equilibrium models (DEQs), compared to traditional neural network architectures. It explores how implicit models handle out-of-distribution (OOD) data and demonstrates their superior performance in mathematical and real-world tasks, such as time series forecasting and earthquake location prediction. The authors attribute this to the depth adaptability and closed-loop feedback mechanisms inherent in implicit models. The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies to support these claims. However, it faces criticism for its lack of clarity in defining the term "implicit models," potential misleading terminology, and the limited scope of its comparative analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies to support the claims made about the superior extrapolation capabilities of implicit models.
- The empirical results demonstrate that implicit models can outperform other models in various mathematical and real-world tasks, showing their versatility and effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of the properties of implicit models that contribute to their extrapolation capabilities, which is both interesting and insightful.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that implicit models perform well in out-of-distribution data scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity and precision in defining the term "implicit models," which could lead to confusion and misinterpretation of the results.
- The paper's terminology, such as "implicit models," "implicitRNN," and "implicit hidden state," may be misleading or incorrect, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.
- The comparison of implicit models with other models is not comprehensive enough, particularly in terms of model size and training parameters, which could affect the fairness and validity of the results.
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the training process for implicit models, which could make it difficult for readers to fully understand the model's capabilities and limitations.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not consistently presented, which could make it harder for readers to follow the logic and conclusions of the study.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of implicit models, such as the potential for overfitting or the need for specific training conditions, which could impact the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of "implicit models" in the context of the study? How do they differ from other types of models, such as deep equilibrium models?
- How do the authors address the potential for overfitting in implicit models, particularly in scenarios where the training data is limited or noisy?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the training process for implicit models, including the initialization and convergence criteria?
- How do the authors ensure that the implicit models are not just memorizing the training data, particularly in scenarios where the training data is noisy or limited?
- Could the authors elaborate on the role of feedback in implicit models and how it contributes to the model's extrapolation capabilities?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of implicit models, such as the need for specific training conditions or the potential for slower training times compared to other models?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons of implicit models with other models, particularly in terms of model size, training parameters, and performance metrics?
