 **Summary:**
The paper introduces WAKER, a novel method for curriculum learning in reward-free settings, aimed at training robust world models that can adapt to various environments. The methodology is based on minimizing the maximum regret across different environment instances, which is achieved by optimizing the upper bound of the world model error. WAKER uses an ensemble of neural networks to estimate the world model error and biases sampling towards environments with the highest error estimates. The approach is evaluated across several environments, showing improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the experimental setup and the robustness of the results, particularly in terms of the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and easy to follow, with a clear problem formulation and a novel approach to address it.
- The use of an ensemble of neural networks to estimate the world model error is a simple yet effective method, and the proposed method of biasing sampling towards environments with the highest error estimates is intuitive and well-explained.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including robustness experiments, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving the robustness of learned policies.
- The theoretical analysis is solid and provides a strong foundation for the proposed method.
- The paper is original in its approach to curriculum learning for world model training in a reward-free setting, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its experimental setup, particularly in how the environment is sampled and how the error is computed.
- The methodology's dependence on domain randomization and the robustness of the results to different hyperparameter settings is not thoroughly addressed.
- The paper's theoretical contributions, while sound, do not seem to be directly linked to the proposed method, and the practical implications of the theoretical results are not clearly demonstrated.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method to other existing methods, such as those using domain randomization, which limits the understanding of its advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper's presentation of results, particularly in tables and figures, could be improved to better highlight the main findings and comparisons.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a small number of tasks and environments, which may not adequately demonstrate the method's effectiveness across a broader range of scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify how the environment is sampled and how the error is computed, especially in relation to the use of imagined rollouts and the disagreement between the ensemble means?
- How does the method perform when using different exploration policies, and how does the error estimation function influence the performance?
- Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter settings used in the experiments and how sensitive the results are to these settings?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability to more complex environments?
- Could you elaborate on the theoretical contributions and how they directly relate to the proposed method?
- How do the results generalize to other domains or tasks, and what are the implications of the method's performance on out-of-distribution environments?
