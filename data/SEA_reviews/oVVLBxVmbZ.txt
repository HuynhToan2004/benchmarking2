 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, CIAR, for generating algorithmic recourse using a reinforcement learning (RL) approach. CIAR operates within incomplete causal graphs, addressing challenges such as unobserved factors and noisy causal graphs. The method involves a conditional intervention cost (IC) that guides the RL agent to intervene on endogenous variables, preserving causal relationships. The paper presents experiments on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating the efficiency and effectiveness of CIAR compared to baseline methods. However, concerns about the clarity and completeness of the paper's presentation, the empirical evaluation, and the theoretical underpinnings persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to generating algorithmic recourse using reinforcement learning (RL), which is both theoretically sound and practically applicable.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the algorithm and its implementation, including the use of a differentiable intervention cost function.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through empirical evaluations on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing promising results.
- The methodology is flexible and can handle both numerical and categorical features, making it potentially applicable to a wide range of problems.
- The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in the context of algorithmic recourse, which is crucial for ensuring fairness and transparency in machine learning applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the definition and explanation of key concepts such as "endogenous" and "exogenous" variables, and the intervention cost function.
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the mathematical formulations and definitions used in the paper, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The empirical evaluation is limited, with only a few datasets used, and the results are not consistently strong across all metrics.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing approaches, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and theoretical guarantees.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which detract from its professional quality.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of handling unobserved confounders in the causal graph, which is a significant limitation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of "endogenous" and "exogenous" variables in the context of the proposed method?
- How does the intervention cost function ensure that the intervention is "likely" as defined in the paper?
- Can the authors provide more details on the empirical evaluation, particularly the choice of datasets and the selection criteria for the baselines used?
- How does the proposed method handle unobserved confounders in the causal graph?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical underpinnings of the intervention cost function, particularly in relation to the Causal Markov condition?
- How does the method perform when the causal graph is more complex, with more edges and nodes?
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the proposed method and existing approaches, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and theoretical guarantees?
