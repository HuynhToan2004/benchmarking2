 **Summary:**
The paper explores the impact of dynamic recommendations on Q-learners in congestion games, proposing a novel model where recommendations are viewed as states. This model aims to optimize social welfare by strategically manipulating the learning dynamics of Q-learners. The authors propose an algorithm to maximize the social welfare in a congestion game by manipulating the learning dynamics of Q-learners. They demonstrate this through experiments in the Braess Paradox, showing that their algorithm can drive the system to near-optimal social welfare. However, the paper's scope is limited to a single instance of the Braess Paradox, and the algorithm's effectiveness in more complex or varied scenarios remains untested.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the problem and its relevance to the field of congestion games, particularly in the context of recommendation systems.
- The authors provide a novel approach to optimizing social welfare in congestion games by manipulating the learning dynamics of Q-learners, which is both theoretically interesting and practically relevant.
- The paper presents a clear and concise explanation of the problem, its significance, and the proposed solution, with a well-structured presentation that aids in understanding the complex concepts.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm, showing improvements in social welfare compared to baseline methods.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the problem, including the scalability and limitations of the algorithm, which adds depth to the discussion.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions, such as the omniscience of the recommender system, are not well-justified and may not be practical in real-world applications.
- The scope of the experiments is limited to a single instance of the Braess Paradox, which may not generalize to other scenarios or congestion games.
- The paper lacks a broader comparison with existing literature, particularly in the area of congestion games and recommendation systems, which could have strengthened the argument for the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which detract from its professional presentation.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational complexity or the scalability of the proposed algorithm, which could limit its applicability in more complex scenarios.
- The paper's assumptions about the rationality and behavior of Q-learners in congestion games are not well-supported or clearly defined, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed algorithm perform in more complex or varied scenarios, such as different instances of the Braess Paradox or other congestion games?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made about the rationality and behavior of Q-learners in congestion games, particularly in relation to the Nash Equilibrium and the social welfare optimum?
- How does the recommendation space size influence the performance of the algorithm, and what are the practical implications of this in real-world applications?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed algorithm, particularly in terms of the size of the recommendation space and the number of Q-learners?
- How does the algorithm handle the variability in the number of Q-learners, and what are the implications of this for the optimization of social welfare?
