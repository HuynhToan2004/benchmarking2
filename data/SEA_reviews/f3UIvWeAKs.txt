 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to node selection in the branch-and-bound (B&B) algorithm for Mixed Integer Linear Programs (MILPs) using a tripartite graph representation. This method enhances the efficiency of solving MILPs by reducing the overall solving time and improving the gap convergence. The approach involves a reinforcement learning framework with a deep Q-network (DQN) and a graph neural network (GNN) to learn node selection policies based on three objectives: gap update reward, optimal solution path reward, and path switching penalty. The methodology is supported by theoretical proofs and empirical evidence, demonstrating its effectiveness over existing methods. The paper also introduces new metrics for node selection and evaluates the method on three classical MILP problems, showcasing its ability to generalize to larger instances.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel tripartite graph representation for the branch-and-bound search tree, which effectively encapsulates the essential information of the search tree for node selection.
- Three innovative metrics for node selection are proposed, and a DQN-GNN model is developed, utilizing reinforcement learning to learn node selection policies in MILP solving.
- The paper demonstrates the efficacy of the DQN-GNN model in enhancing the efficiency of solving MILPs, as evidenced by empirical evaluations that show significant improvements over existing human-designed and learning-based benchmarks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a thorough literature review, which is helpful in understanding the current state of research in the field.
- The paper provides a theoretical framework that supports the proposed method, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the motivation behind the proposed tripartite graph representation, particularly the rationale behind focusing on constraints added through branching rather than inherent problem structure.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scenarios where the method might not perform optimally.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper's reliance on the ESTIMATE heuristic for node selection raises questions about the method's independence from specific variable selection policies.
- The paper's experimental setup and results could be improved by including more detailed comparisons with other methods, particularly in terms of the generalization capabilities of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of existing methods and how the proposed method addresses these limitations.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the motivation behind the proposed tripartite graph representation and how it addresses the limitations of the bipartite graph model?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between the time spent in node selection and the overall solving time?
- In terms of the computational complexity of the proposed method, how does it compare to other methods, and what are the practical implications of this complexity?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of generalization capabilities and computational efficiency?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the datasets used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the proposed method ensure that the node selection strategy is independent of specific variable selection policies, as claimed in the paper?
- Could you discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
