 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the challenge of post-treatment bias in causal inference by proposing a novel method for estimating heterogeneous treatment effects. The approach involves a deep learning-based representation learning for post-treatment covariates, which are critical for mitigating biases in causal effect estimation. The paper provides theoretical and empirical evidence, including synthetic, semi-synthetic, and real-world data, to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The authors introduce a causal graph to model the post-treatment covariates and provide a theoretical framework for their identification. The empirical results show that the proposed method outperforms existing state-of-the-art models in estimating heterogeneous treatment effects.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in causal inference, specifically the issue of post-treatment bias, which is crucial for understanding and estimating causal effects in various domains.
- The proposed method is innovative, leveraging deep learning techniques to infer representations of confounders and post-treatment variables directly from observed covariates, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the methodology and experimental results, which enhance its readability and accessibility.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it outperforms existing state-of-the-art models in estimating heterogeneous treatment effects, as evidenced by comprehensive evaluations on synthetic, semi-synthetic, and real-world data.
- The authors provide a theoretical framework for the identification of post-treatment covariates and treatment effects, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including its assumptions, potential biases, and practical constraints.
- The experimental setup and results could be improved by including more detailed comparisons with other methods, such as those mentioned in the literature, and by providing more detailed information on the datasets used.
- There are some issues with the clarity and presentation of the paper, particularly in the explanation of certain methodological details and the notation used, which could confuse readers.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related works and a clearer differentiation of its contributions from existing literature.
- The paper's assumptions about the distribution of confounders and post-treatment variables being independent of each other may not hold in all real-world scenarios, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the assumptions made in Theorem 1 regarding the independence of confounders and post-treatment variables? How do these assumptions affect the validity of the results?
- How do the authors ensure that the post-treatment variables are not colliders in their causal graph, especially given the potential for confounding between these variables and the treatment?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the specifics of the datasets used and the methodology employed in the comparisons with other methods?
- How do the authors address the potential biases that could arise from the use of neural networks in the representation learning process?
- Could you elaborate on the role of the decoder function in the reconstruction module and its impact on the overall performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential confounding between post-treatment variables and the treatment, especially in scenarios where these variables are not independent?
- Could you discuss the implications of the method's assumptions on the generalizability of the findings to real-world scenarios where these assumptions may not hold?
