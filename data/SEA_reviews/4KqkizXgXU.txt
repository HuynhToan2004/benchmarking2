 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of curiosity-driven exploration in training red team models to generate diverse and effective test cases for large language models (LLMs). The approach aims to address the limitations of current RL-based methods, which tend to generate a narrow set of successful test cases. The methodology involves incorporating curiosity-driven rewards into the training objective to maximize novelty, thereby improving diversity. Experiments conducted across text continuation and instruction following tasks demonstrate that this approach outperforms existing RL-based methods in terms of test case diversity while maintaining effectiveness. The paper also includes an ablation study to understand the individual contributions of different components of the training objective.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem of generating diverse and effective test cases for LLMs, which is crucial for ensuring the safety and reliability of these models.
- The introduction of curiosity-driven exploration to optimize novelty in training red team models is a novel and innovative approach.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides clear explanations of the methodology and experimental results.
- The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can generate more diverse test cases than existing RL-based methods.
- The paper includes an ablation study to understand the individual contributions of different components of the training objective, which helps in understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of scalability and the generalizability of the findings across different LLMs and tasks.
- The use of n-gram modeling for measuring diversity might not be effective as it does not capture the semantic meaning of the text, which could limit the understanding of the diversity of the generated test cases.
- The novelty of the approach might be limited as similar ideas have been explored in other domains, such as adversarial attacks in NLP.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other baselines, such as different reward functions or different training strategies, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a few models, which might not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the ethical implications of the work, particularly in terms of the potential misuse of the generated test cases.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the scalability and generalizability of the proposed method across different LLMs and tasks?
- How does the proposed method compare with other baselines, such as different reward functions or different training strategies?
- Can the authors provide more qualitative examples of the generated test cases to better understand the diversity and effectiveness of the method?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different LLMs, such as those with different parameter sizes or different training objectives?
- Can the authors provide more details on the ethical considerations of the work, particularly in terms of the potential misuse of the generated test cases?
