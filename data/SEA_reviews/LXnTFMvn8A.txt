 **Summary:**
The paper explores the accuracy-fairness trade-off in machine learning by introducing the concept of the Pareto Frontier, a set of optimal classifiers that represent the best achievable balance between fairness and accuracy. The authors characterize this frontier under various conditions, including idealized and practical settings, and demonstrate the continuity of fairness and potential sharp declines in accuracy. They also propose a two-step approach to eliminate the trade-off by addressing both data and model unfairness. The theoretical analysis is supported by numerical examples, although the paper's assumptions and the practical applicability of the results are critiqued for their idealized nature and limited scope.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise arguments that are easy to follow.
- The authors provide a novel theoretical framework for analyzing the accuracy-fairness trade-off, focusing on the Pareto Frontier of classifiers.
- The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis that is supported by numerical examples, enhancing the understanding of the trade-off between fairness and accuracy.
- The results are robust and provide insights into the conditions under which the fairness-accuracy trade-off occurs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper addresses a fundamental and important problem in fair machine learning, providing a deeper understanding of the trade-offs involved.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes idealized conditions that may not reflect real-world scenarios, such as the assumption that sensitive attributes are fully captured by non-sensitive attributes, which is unrealistic in many applications.
- The theoretical results are derived under strong assumptions that may not hold in practical settings, such as the assumption of balanced data distributions and the existence of a local maximum for fairness.
- The paper's scope is limited to binary classification and binary sensitive attributes, which may not generalize to other types of problems or attributes.
- The practical implications of the findings are not clearly articulated, and it is unclear how the theoretical results can be applied in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's focus on a single fairness definition (Equalized Odds) limits its applicability to other fairness metrics that are commonly used in the literature.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of finding the Pareto Frontier, which could be a significant challenge in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the theoretical results apply to other fairness metrics beyond Equalized Odds?
- How does the paper address the computational complexity of finding the Pareto Frontier in practical applications?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on how the theoretical results can be applied in real-world scenarios?
- How do the authors justify the strong assumptions made in their theoretical analysis, particularly the assumption of balanced data distributions and the existence of a local maximum for fairness?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of their findings, especially in terms of how they can be used to improve fair machine learning systems in practice?
