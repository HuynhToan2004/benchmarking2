 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to goal-conditioned reinforcement learning by focusing on the estimation of the probability of reaching a goal state at different future timesteps, rather than minimizing the expected number of steps to reach the goal. This method, termed distributional NCE, aims to address the suboptimality of traditional methods that prioritize minimizing the number of steps to reach a goal. The authors demonstrate this approach through a series of experiments, showing that it outperforms existing methods in certain scenarios. However, the paper has been criticized for its lack of clarity in explaining the technical details of the method, the insufficiency of experimental comparisons, and the limited scope of its contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a strong motivation and a well-defined problem.
- The proposed method, Distributional NCE, is simple and effective, outperforming existing methods in some scenarios.
- The authors provide a clear analysis of the limitations of existing methods and provide a novel solution to these problems.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the technical details of the method, particularly in Section 5.1, which makes it difficult for readers to understand the algorithm's implementation.
- The experimental results are not comprehensive enough, with insufficient comparisons to other existing methods and a lack of results on more complex environments.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical analysis or proof for some of its claims, such as the equivalence between maximizing likelihood and shortest-path planning.
- The paper's contributions are limited in scope, focusing primarily on the specific problem of goal-conditioned RL, which may not be generalizable to other domains.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of Monte Carlo distance functions not being a Q function, which could lead to confusion about the theoretical underpinnings of the method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the technical details of the method, particularly in Section 5.1, to make it more accessible to readers?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods, especially in more complex environments?
- Could the authors provide more theoretical analysis or proof for some of their claims, such as the equivalence between maximizing likelihood and shortest-path planning?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of Monte Carlo distance functions not being a Q function?
- Can the authors explain the intuition behind the auxiliary objective and why it is necessary?
- How does the method perform in environments with continuous state and action spaces, and how does it compare to other goal-conditioned RL methods in such environments?
