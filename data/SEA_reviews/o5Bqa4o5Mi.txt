 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for evaluating and comparing policies using feature vectors derived from successor features and state encoders. This approach, termed Pi2vec, leverages a pretrained state encoder to generate state-dependent successor features, which are then aggregated to create a state-independent policy feature. These features are used to predict policy performance through a supervised learning model. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments in both simulated and real-world environments, showing improvements over baseline methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the method's operation, and the lack of detailed comparisons with other offline RL algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the problem addressed.
- The proposed method is intuitive and well-explained, with a clear and simple architecture.
- The method is evaluated on both simulated and real-world environments, showing its applicability in various settings.
- The experiments are well-designed, with detailed reporting of results and comparisons to baselines.
- The method does not require access to the policy architecture or training parameters, making it policy-agnostic.
- The use of successor features for policy evaluation is a novel approach that has not been explored extensively in the literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other offline RL algorithms, which is crucial for establishing the method's effectiveness.
- The method's operation and the role of each component (e.g., policy-agnostic state features, successor features, and state-agnostic policy features) are not clearly explained, leading to potential confusion.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as the requirement for a large dataset of trajectories and the potential impact of using different feature encoders.
- The method's application in fully-offline settings, where no performance data is available, is not well-explained or demonstrated.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the assumptions made by the method, such as the linear reward assumption, and how these assumptions affect the method's performance.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and operation of each component (e.g., policy-agnostic state features, successor features, and state-agnostic policy features) in the method?
- How does the method perform when using different feature encoders, and what are the implications of this choice on the method's effectiveness?
- Can you provide more details on the training process for the policy-specific state-feature encoder and how it is integrated with the successor feature training?
- How does the method handle out-of-distribution scenarios, and what are the implications of using a pre-trained state encoder in these cases?
- Could you elaborate on the method's application in fully-offline settings and how it compares to other methods in this context?
- How does the method handle the potential for different policies to have different effects on the environment, and how does this affect the method's ability to predict policy performance?
