 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel generative modeling approach called discriminator-only generation (DOG), which leverages a discriminator model to generate samples through iterative gradient descent, eliminating the need for a separate generator model. The method is tested on graph datasets, demonstrating improved performance over existing GAN-based methods, particularly in terms of convergence and sample quality. Despite its simplicity, DOG shows promise in reducing training time and generating high-quality samples, although it may require further optimization for broader applicability and performance on more complex datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes a detailed convergence analysis, which is crucial for understanding the proposed method.
- The method is simple yet effective, with a clear presentation of the algorithm and its components.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method and provide insights into its limitations and potential improvements.
- The method's simplicity and the absence of a generator model simplify the training process and potentially reduce training time, making it more accessible for practitioners.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with other methods, including a discussion on the convergence behavior of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's evaluation is limited to graph datasets, which might not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the method to other types of data, such as images or natural language.
- The method's performance and computational efficiency vary depending on the dataset and hyperparameters, which could limit its applicability in different scenarios.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the convergence of the method and the role of hyperparameters in achieving good performance.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with other methods, particularly in terms of sample quality and computational efficiency.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the method can be applied to other types of data, such as images, which could limit its practical utility.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method can be applied to other types of data, such as images, and discuss any potential challenges or adaptations required?
- How does the method compare in terms of sample quality and computational efficiency with other methods, such as diffusion models or other types of GANs?
- What are the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments, and how do they affect the performance and convergence of the method?
- How does the method handle the potential for generating samples that are not valid or realistic, especially in datasets where this could be a significant issue?
- Can the authors provide more details on the convergence behavior of the method, particularly how it relates to the choice of hyperparameters and the dataset characteristics?
