 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for training large neural networks by decomposing the global log-likelihood loss into a sum of local terms, which are optimized independently using a variational upper bound. This approach, termed block-local learning, is designed to address the challenges of parallelization and scalability in training large models. The method involves dividing the network into blocks, each of which can be trained in parallel, and uses a feedback network to provide auxiliary local targets. This allows for the training of each block without requiring communication between them, thereby reducing the need for weight transport and locking. The paper presents theoretical and empirical evidence supporting the efficacy of this approach, including experiments on various datasets and models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The introduction of a probabilistic framework for interpreting representations in deep neural networks as parameters of probability distributions is innovative and provides a novel perspective on the training process.
- The proposed block-local learning (BLL) method is simple, intuitive, and does not require Monte Carlo sampling, making it potentially more efficient and less computationally expensive than some other methods.
- The method is shown to be effective in training large models, with results demonstrating its ability to achieve state-of-the-art performance on several benchmark classification tasks.
- The paper provides a theoretical framework for interpreting representations of deep neural networks as parameters of probability distributions, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scalability and efficiency.
- The empirical evaluation of the method is limited, with the experiments primarily focusing on small-scale models and datasets. More extensive evaluations on larger and more complex models are needed to fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing approaches, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential issues with generalization and the need for further research to address these limitations.
- The paper could be clearer in its presentation of the experimental setup and results, particularly in the description of the training details and the results of the experiments.
