 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedRAP, a federated learning-based recommendation system that integrates personalized and private user embeddings with item additive personalization. It employs two regularizers to enhance sparsity and complementarity between local and global views, aiming to improve communication efficiency and reduce latency. FedRAP is evaluated on multiple benchmarks, showing significant improvements over existing federated recommendation methods and baselines. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty and lack of comprehensive theoretical analysis and detailed privacy discussion.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methods.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on multiple benchmarks.
- The paper conducts extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, including an ablation study that verifies the effectiveness of each component.
- The method's use of sparsity and complementarity between local and global views is innovative, which is crucial for federated recommendation systems.
- The paper is well-structured, providing a clear problem formulation, algorithm design, and cost analysis.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its convergence and generalization properties.
- The experimental setup and comparisons are not fair or comprehensive. For instance, the centralized version of FedRAP (CentRAP) is not compared against other federated learning methods, and the experimental settings do not include different client participation rates.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant in a federated learning context.
- The privacy discussion is insufficient, focusing only on differential privacy without addressing other potential privacy risks.
- The method's novelty is limited as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and communication costs, which are crucial for federated learning systems.
- The paper's claims about the sparsity of item embeddings and the effectiveness of the curriculum transition are not adequately supported by experimental evidence.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the convergence and generalization properties of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare against other federated learning methods when the centralized version (CentRAP) is included in the comparisons?
- Can the authors clarify the privacy risks beyond differential privacy and discuss how these risks are mitigated?
- What are the specific reasons for the observed performance improvements in the ablation study, particularly in the FedRAP-fixed variant?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential negative societal impacts, especially in terms of privacy and data security?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, such as the impact of different client participation rates and the computational complexity of the method?
