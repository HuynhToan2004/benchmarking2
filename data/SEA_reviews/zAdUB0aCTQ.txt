 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AgentBench, a comprehensive benchmark designed to evaluate large language models (LLMs) in various real-world scenarios, including coding, gaming, and web browsing. It assesses 27 different LLMs across eight distinct environments, highlighting significant performance disparities between commercial and open-source models. The benchmark identifies critical areas for improvement, such as long-term reasoning, decision-making, and instruction following, and provides insights into the impact of code training and high-quality alignment data on LLM performance. The benchmark's findings are expected to guide future research and development in the field of LLM-as-Agent.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating LLMs as agents, covering a wide range of real-world scenarios and 27 different LLMs.
- It identifies significant performance disparities between commercial and open-source models, highlighting areas for improvement such as long-term reasoning, decision-making, and instruction following.
- The benchmark is well-designed and easy to use, with a focus on improving the evaluation of LLMs in practical settings.
- The paper provides detailed insights into the performance of LLMs in various environments, which can help guide future research and development in the field of LLM-as-Agent.
- The benchmark covers a variety of tasks, including code-grounded, game-grounded, and web-grounded scenarios, which are crucial for evaluating LLMs in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not propose any new techniques or methods for improving the evaluation of LLMs as agents, which could limit its impact on the field.
- The benchmark only evaluates LLMs up to 70B, which may not provide a comprehensive view of LLM capabilities across different scales.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis on the performance of LLMs in specific environments, which could provide deeper insights into their strengths and weaknesses.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the benchmark, which could limit its applicability to larger or more complex LLMs.
- The evaluation methodology used in the paper is not clearly explained, which could limit the reproducibility and transparency of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the benchmark environments were designed or how they were evaluated, which could affect the reliability and validity of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed insights into the performance of LLMs in specific environments, such as the Digital Card Game and House Holding environments?
- How do the authors address the scalability of the benchmark to larger or more complex LLMs?
- Could the authors clarify the evaluation methodology used in the paper, particularly how the benchmark environments were designed and evaluated?
- What are the plans for updating the benchmark to include more recent or larger LLMs, and how will the benchmark be adapted to keep pace with advancements in the field?
- How do the authors plan to address the limitations of the benchmark, such as the lack of evaluation of larger LLMs and the potential for bias in the evaluation methodology?
