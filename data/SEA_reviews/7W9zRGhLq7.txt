 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel perspective on data heterogeneity in federated learning (FL) by proposing a new assumption, the heterogeneity-driven Lipschitz constant, to replace the traditional bounded gradient divergence assumption. This new assumption, termed the heterogeneity-driven Lipschitz condition on averaged gradients, allows for a more nuanced analysis of FL algorithms, particularly in the context of federated averaging (FedAvg). The authors demonstrate that this new assumption can lead to tighter convergence bounds for both full and partial participation in FL, and show that it can capture the effects of data heterogeneity more accurately than previous assumptions. The paper also discusses the implications of this new assumption for the convergence analysis of other FL algorithms, suggesting potential broader applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel perspective on data heterogeneity in FL, which is crucial for understanding the dynamics of federated learning systems.
- The introduction of the heterogeneity-driven Lipschitz constant provides a more nuanced and accurate characterization of data heterogeneity in FL, which is a significant advancement over previous assumptions.
- The paper provides a comprehensive convergence analysis for FedAvg with non-convex objective functions, demonstrating the utility and applicability of the new assumption.
- The theoretical insights presented in the paper are supported by empirical results, which validates the practical relevance and applicability of the proposed assumptions.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the assumptions and their implications, and the proofs and discussions are rigorous and insightful.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implications and the limitations of the new assumption, particularly in scenarios where the assumption may not hold or may not be applicable.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the relationship between the new assumption and other existing assumptions, such as the bounded gradient divergence assumption.
- The experimental validation of the new assumption is limited to simple datasets like MNIST and CIFAR-10, and does not include more complex or realistic datasets, which could undermine the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical implications of the new assumption, particularly in scenarios where it may not be applicable or where it may lead to suboptimal results.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and comparison with existing works to better position its contributions within the current research landscape.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical implications of the new assumption in different scenarios?
- How does the new assumption compare to other existing assumptions, such as the bounded gradient divergence assumption, in terms of applicability and effectiveness?
- Can the authors discuss the practical implications of the new assumption in more complex or realistic datasets, and how it might affect the performance of FL algorithms in such scenarios?
- How does the new assumption impact the convergence analysis of other FL algorithms, and could it potentially be applied to other types of FL algorithms beyond FedAvg?
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between the new assumption and the bounded gradient divergence assumption, particularly in terms of their relative strengths and applicability?
