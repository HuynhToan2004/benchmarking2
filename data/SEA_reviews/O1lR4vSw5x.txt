 **Summary:**
The paper introduces RNODE, a novel framework for learning the dynamics of spatiotemporal systems where only partial information about the system's state is available. This framework is based on a sequential optimization approach that uses a recursive Newton method to optimize the cost function with respect to both the system's latent states and model parameters. The approach is validated through experiments on both simulated and real datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in modeling complex dynamics and improving upon existing methods. The paper also addresses the challenge of partial observations in dynamic systems by leveraging the distinguishability of latent states to optimize the cost function.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using a sequential optimization method to address the challenge of partial observations in dynamic systems, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed RNODE framework is a straightforward and effective method for learning the dynamics of spatiotemporal systems, particularly when only partial information about the system's state is available.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of related work, including both classical and recent methods, which helps in positioning the proposed method within the current research landscape.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it outperforms existing methods in modeling complex dynamics and improving performance in challenging scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental setup, which enhances its readability and comprehensibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scalability and applicability in large-scale or real-time systems.
- There is a need for more robust theoretical analysis to support the claims made about the method's effectiveness and its ability to handle complex dynamics.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly those that address similar challenges in dynamic systems modeling.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse datasets and scenarios to better evaluate the method's performance and generalizability.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of distinguishing between latent states, which is crucial for the method's effectiveness. The paper relies on prior knowledge of distinguishable states, which may not be available in all practical scenarios.
- The paper's reliance on prior knowledge of the dynamics and the use of a recursive Newton method may limit its applicability to systems where the dynamics are not well understood or where the data is noisy.
