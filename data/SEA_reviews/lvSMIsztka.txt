 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two novel estimators for probabilistic values in cooperative game theory, leveraging least squares regression to achieve computational efficiency. The first estimator provides a ranking of data points based on probabilistic values, while the second approximates the exact probabilistic values. These estimators are shown to have a complexity of O(N/ε²logN/δ), which is an improvement over the O(N²/ε²logN/δ) complexity of previous estimators. The paper also discusses the application of these estimators in training value estimators for distributional values, which can be evaluated in a single forward pass. Experimental results demonstrate the faster convergence of the proposed estimators and their effectiveness in approximating distributional values.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces two novel estimators for probabilistic values in cooperative game theory, which are significant contributions to the field.
- The proposed estimators achieve a computational complexity of O(N/ε²logN/δ), which is a significant improvement over previous estimators.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise presentation of the results, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The connection between probabilistic values and least square regressions is well-explained, providing a novel approach to estimating probabilistic values.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed estimators in approximating distributional values, showing faster convergence compared to other methods.
- The paper provides theoretical grounding for training value estimators to approximate distributional values, which is a practical application of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed estimators, particularly in terms of their applicability to different types of utility functions and the scalability of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other existing estimators, which could have strengthened the argument for the superiority of the proposed methods.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly demonstrated, particularly in the removal experiment where the performance drop is not significant.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed methods, especially in large-scale datasets, and the memory requirements of the estimators are not discussed.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of their application in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed estimators, particularly in terms of their applicability to different types of utility functions and the scalability of the proposed methods?
- How do the proposed estimators compare to other existing estimators, and what are the specific advantages of the proposed methods over these estimators?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the removal experiment and the performance drop observed in the experiment?
- How do the proposed estimators perform in terms of memory requirements, and what are the implications of these requirements for their scalability in large-scale datasets?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of their application in real-world scenarios?
