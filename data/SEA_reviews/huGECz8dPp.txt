 **Summary:**
The paper explores the Information Bottleneck (IB) principle in deep neural networks (DNNs) using a novel approach that leverages stochastic neural networks and lossy compression to estimate mutual information. The methodology involves compressing data using autoencoders and other compression techniques, then applying mutual information estimators to the compressed data. The paper presents both theoretical and experimental analyses, including synthetic experiments and real-world applications like MNIST, to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The authors aim to address the curse of dimensionality and improve the accuracy of mutual information estimation, which is crucial for understanding the dynamics of neural network training.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for mutual information estimation, which is a significant contribution to the field of information bottleneck analysis in deep learning.
- The theoretical results provided are well-articulated and supported by proofs, enhancing the credibility of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are convincing, particularly the synthetic experiments which show that the proposed method can accurately estimate mutual information.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making the complex theoretical concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The use of lossy compression to address the curse of dimensionality in mutual information estimation is a novel and interesting approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear demonstration of the advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, such as MINE, which also uses a similar approach.
- The experimental results are limited to small-scale datasets like MNIST, which may not convincingly show the effectiveness of the method on larger, more complex datasets.
- There is a potential issue with the choice of mutual information estimators, as the paper does not discuss the limitations of these estimators, such as the choice of kernel bandwidth in KDE.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger, more complex datasets and the potential for overfitting due to the use of lossy compression.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the impact of the manifold hypothesis and the assumptions made about the data distribution.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between their method and MINE, particularly in terms of the advantages of their approach?
- How does the proposed method handle the curse of dimensionality in larger, more complex datasets?
- What are the implications of the manifold hypothesis on the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the choice of mutual information estimators and discuss the potential impact of different kernel bandwidths on the results?
- How does the paper address the potential for overfitting due to the use of lossy compression, and what measures are in place to mitigate this risk?
- Can the authors elaborate on the definition and role of the function $f$ in the context of the mutual information estimation?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent works that have also addressed the curse of dimensionality in mutual information estimation?
