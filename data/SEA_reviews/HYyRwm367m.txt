 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for learning semantic discrete representations using a Semantic Vector-Quantized Variational Autoencoder (SVQ). This model extends the capabilities of traditional vector quantization methods by learning semantic discrete representations, which can generate multi-object scenes that adhere to the underlying data distribution. The approach is evaluated on both 2D and 3D datasets, showing improvements over non-semantic vector quantization methods and previous object-centric generative models. The method leverages recent advances in unsupervised object-centric learning to address the limitation of existing methods in providing conceptual, semantic, and compositional abstractions. However, the evaluation is limited to synthetic datasets, and the method's performance on more complex, real-world datasets remains untested.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning semantic discrete representations, which is a significant advancement over traditional vector quantization methods.
- The method is well-motivated and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method demonstrates strong results on both 2D and 3D datasets, showing superior performance compared to non-semantic vector quantization methods and previous object-centric generative models.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology, including a thorough literature review and a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The method's ability to generate images following the underlying data distribution is a notable strength, which is lacking in most object-centric models.
- The paper's experimental setup is well-designed, with a variety of baselines and metrics used to evaluate the method's performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's evaluation is limited to synthetic datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the method's capabilities on more complex, real-world datasets.
- The method's reliance on a pre-defined number of discrete factors (M) could limit its adaptability to different datasets or scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method in detail, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The method's ability to handle complex scenes with a large number of objects or varying properties is not thoroughly explored.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity or the scalability of the method, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- The method's novelty is questioned as it builds on existing methods like SysBinder, and the improvements over these methods are not clearly articulated.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method handles complex scenes with a large number of objects or varying properties?
- How does the method perform when the number of objects in a scene varies?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these limitations affect its practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the method, especially in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the method compare to other object-centric generative models that do not rely on vector quantization?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the downstream tasks, especially the OOD property comparison and the CLEVR-Hard property comparison?
- How does the method handle the disentanglement of continuous and discrete factors, especially in scenarios where both types of factors are present?
