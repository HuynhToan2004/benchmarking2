 **Summary:**
The paper explores the multi-agent Bayesian optimization problem with coupled black-box constraints and known affine constraints, proposing a primal-dual distributed algorithm. This algorithm aims to achieve similar regret and violation bounds as in the single-agent case, with a focus on maintaining sublinear bounds on cumulative regret, violation, and shift for affine constraints. Theoretical analysis is provided alongside empirical results, demonstrating the algorithm's performance in scenarios with both Gaussian process and real-world optimal power allocation problems. Despite the theoretical advancements, concerns are raised about the practical implementation of the algorithm, particularly the computational complexity and the need for further empirical validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and novel problem of multi-agent Bayesian optimization with coupled black-box constraints and known affine constraints, which is not extensively explored in the literature.
- The proposed algorithm is the first to provide theoretical regret/violation bounds in this setting, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed theoretical proofs and empirical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
- The problem formulation is well-motivated and practical, addressing real-world applications such as optimal power allocation in wireless communication.
- The paper introduces a primal-dual distributed algorithm that achieves similar regret and violation bounds as those in the single-agent case, which is a notable achievement.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implementation of the algorithm, particularly in terms of computational complexity and communication overhead.
- The empirical results are limited, with only two examples used to validate the theoretical claims, which might not be sufficient to convincingly demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness across different scenarios.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the knowledge of the number of iterations and the bounded norms of the objective and constraint functions, are not practical and may limit the applicability of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions may not hold or where the algorithm might not perform optimally.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods and a broader empirical evaluation to validate the claims made about the algorithm's performance.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed algorithm handle scenarios where the assumptions, such as the bounded norms of the objective and constraint functions, may not hold?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and communication overhead of the proposed algorithm?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare in terms of computational efficiency and communication requirements with existing methods for multi-agent Bayesian optimization?
- Can the authors provide additional empirical results to validate the effectiveness of the algorithm across different scenarios and settings?
- How does the algorithm perform in scenarios with varying numbers of agents, and what are the implications for the performance guarantees?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the dual variables in the algorithm's performance, particularly in terms of their influence on the regret and violation bounds?
