 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework, the "Plug-in Market," designed to address copyright infringement in text-to-image generative models. This framework employs three operations: addition, extraction, and combination, leveraging LoRA components to facilitate copyright attribution and digital copyright protection. The authors propose an "inverse LoRA" approach for extraction and a "data-free layer-wise distillation" method for combination, aiming to enhance the flexibility and incentives for each participant in the market. The paper includes experiments in style transfer and cartoon IP recreation to demonstrate the effectiveness of these operations. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in the methodology, particularly in the extraction and combination processes, and the absence of a comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a timely and important issue of copyright infringement in text-to-image generative models, proposing a framework that can potentially protect the intellectual property rights of artists and content creators.
- The concept of a Plug-in Market is innovative and provides a practical solution for addressing copyright concerns, offering a flexible and incentive-driven framework.
- The paper is well-written, with clear descriptions of the proposed methods, including the use of LoRA components for extraction and combination operations.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed operations, showing a significant improvement in the KID metric compared to existing methods.
- The framework is designed to be adaptable and flexible, allowing for the addition, extraction, and combination of LoRA components, which can be easily integrated into existing models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the methodology for extraction and combination, which could benefit from additional details and examples to enhance understanding.
- There is a need for a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, including a discussion on how the proposed framework compares in terms of performance, efficiency, and cost-effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from a broader range of experiments, such as those involving more complex images or different types of copyrighted concepts, to demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed framework.
- The paper's reliance on LoRA components for all operations might limit its applicability to other types of models, and the use of negative sampling in the extraction process raises concerns about the fairness and effectiveness of the copyright protection.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and computational efficiency of the framework, particularly in handling a large number of LoRA components or complex images.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on how the extraction process is performed, particularly in terms of the specific prompts used and the selection of images for unlearning and memorization?
- How does the combination operation affect the quality and diversity of the generated images, especially when combining multiple LoRA components?
- Could you clarify the role and impact of negative sampling in the copyright protection process, and discuss potential alternatives or improvements to this approach?
- What are the specific computational requirements and costs associated with implementing the proposed framework, and how do these compare to existing methods?
- Given the reliance on LoRA components, how does the framework adapt to different model architectures or types of data, and what are the implications for scalability and performance?
- How does the framework handle cases where multiple LoRA components are used to generate a single image, and what are the implications for the copyright protection in such scenarios?
