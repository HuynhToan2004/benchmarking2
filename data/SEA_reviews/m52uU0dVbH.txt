 **Summary:**
The paper investigates adversarial attacks in vertical federated learning (VFL) settings, focusing on adaptive corruption strategies that allow the adversary to selectively corrupt a subset of clients to optimize attack success rates. The authors formulate this problem as a multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem, where each client's corruption pattern corresponds to an arm. The paper introduces the Thompson sampling with Empirical maximum reward (E-TS) algorithm to efficiently identify the optimal subset of clients for corruption, demonstrating its effectiveness through extensive experiments. Theoretical analysis is provided to characterize the regret bound of the E-TS algorithm. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its incremental nature, lack of comparison with existing works, and insufficient discussion on the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The problem formulation is innovative, particularly the decomposition of the problem into inner and outer problems, which is a novel approach in adversarial example generation (AEG) and corruption pattern selection (CPS).
- The proposed E-TS algorithm is theoretically sound, with a clear explanation of its workings and a detailed analysis of its regret bound.
- The paper is structured effectively, with clear explanations and detailed mathematical proofs, which enhance its credibility and reproducibility.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering a wide range of datasets and scenarios, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing works, particularly in the area of adversarial attacks in VFL, which could help contextualize the contributions of this study.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to the E-TS algorithm and does not include a broader exploration of other algorithms, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the problem.
- The paper does not discuss the generalizability of the results beyond the specific attack settings and datasets used, which could limit the practical applicability of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for ensuring the ethical implications of the research.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical deployments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the adaptive nature of the adversary, which is a critical aspect of the study.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition of the attack success rate (ASR) and how it is calculated, especially in the context of targeted and untargeted attacks?
- How does the proposed method perform when the number of clients is large, and what are the implications for computational complexity and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in terms of time and memory requirements?
- How does the proposed method perform under different attack settings, such as varying the number of clients that can be corrupted or the number of corruption patterns used?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their work and how these are being addressed?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing works in terms of its theoretical contributions and practical implications?
- Could the authors provide more details on the adaptive nature of the adversary and how this affects the effectiveness of the proposed method?
