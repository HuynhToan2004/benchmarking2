 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to reinforcement learning (RL) called Policy Rehearsing via Dynamics Model Generation (ReDM), which generates a set of candidate dynamics models from minimal knowledge about the environment. This method, aimed at enhancing generalization in RL, is evaluated on various environments with limited or no interaction data. The proposed algorithm, ReDM, is designed to generate a diverse and eligible set of dynamics models, which are used to rehearse policies and adapt to new environments. The paper presents theoretical analysis and empirical results, demonstrating the effectiveness of ReDM in scenarios where interaction data is limited or unavailable.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear exposition of the proposed method and its application in reinforcement learning (RL).
- The concept of policy rehearsing is innovative and addresses a significant challenge in RL by enabling the learning of generalizable policies with limited or no interaction data.
- The paper presents a novel approach to policy optimization that does not require extensive online interaction or a high-fidelity simulator, which is a departure from traditional methods.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in generating a diverse set of dynamics models and in rehearsing policies, which can generalize to previously unseen environments.
- The paper includes a theoretical analysis and empirical results that support the proposed method, which is a strong contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in the area of offline RL and model-based RL, which could have strengthened the paper's positioning within the current research landscape.
- The experiments conducted are limited to simple environments, which may not adequately demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in more complex scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address how the method performs when the data distribution between the training and testing environments is not similar, which is a critical aspect of generalization in RL.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational cost and scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of its applicability to larger, more complex environments.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the availability of a reward function and the terminal state, are not sufficiently justified or discussed in terms of their practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the computational cost and scalability of the proposed method? How does it perform in terms of computational resources and time requirements as the complexity of the environment increases?
- How does the proposed method compare to other model-based offline RL methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can you elaborate on the assumptions made in the paper, such as the availability of a reward function and the terminal state? How do these assumptions affect the practical applicability of the method?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the data distribution between the training and testing environments is not similar? What are the implications of this mismatch in terms of generalization performance?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the hyperparameters used and the specifics of the environments tested?
