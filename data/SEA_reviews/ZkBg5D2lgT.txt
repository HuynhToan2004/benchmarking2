 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Gaussian process hyperbolic latent variable model (GPHLVM) aimed at modeling taxonomy data on hyperbolic manifolds. This model leverages the hierarchical structure of taxonomies by incorporating graph-based priors and back-constraints to preserve the original graph structure. The GPHLVM is evaluated against Euclidean counterparts and shown to outperform in tasks such as trajectory generation and pose encoding. The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical framework and demonstrates the model's effectiveness in various robotics taxonomies, including hand grasps, whole-body support poses, and bimanual manipulation. However, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental scope, lack of comparative analysis with other state-of-the-art models, and insufficient discussion on the computational complexity and practical implications of the model.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel Gaussian process hyperbolic latent variable model (GPHLVM) that models taxonomy data on hyperbolic manifolds, leveraging the hierarchical structure of taxonomies.
- The GPHLVM is a well-structured and well-written paper that effectively integrates various elements of hyperbolic geometry, including the use of hyperbolic kernels and the hyperbolic wrapped Gaussian distribution.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the GPHLVM's architecture, including the use of hyperbolic kernels and the hyperbolic wrapped Gaussian distribution, which are novel contributions to the field.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the GPHLVM through various experiments, showing that it outperforms its Euclidean counterparts in tasks such as trajectory generation and pose encoding.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear figures and detailed explanations of the methodology, which makes it accessible and understandable to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art models, which limits the ability to evaluate the GPHLVM's performance against the current best practices in the field.
- The paper's experimental scope is limited, focusing primarily on three specific robotics taxonomies, which may not fully demonstrate the GPHLVM's generalizability or applicability to other types of taxonomies or domains.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and practical implications of the GPHLVM, which could be crucial for understanding its scalability and efficiency in real-world applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the model, such as its ability to handle noisy or incomplete data, and its applicability to different types of data beyond those used in the experiments.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the choice of hyperbolic models over other types of models, particularly in terms of their advantages and limitations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the choice of hyperbolic models over other types of models, and the specific advantages of using hyperbolic models in this context?
- How does the GPHLVM handle noisy or incomplete data, and what are the implications of this for its practical applications?
- Can the GPHLVM be adapted to handle different types of data beyond those used in the experiments, and if so, what are the potential challenges and limitations?
- How does the computational complexity of the GPHLVM compare to other models, and what are the practical implications of this in terms of scalability and efficiency?
