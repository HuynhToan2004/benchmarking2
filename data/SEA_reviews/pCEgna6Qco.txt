 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the challenge of fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) on specific tasks, which often leads to the loss of general in-context learning abilities. It introduces a two-stage fine-tuning framework, named ProMoT, which addresses this issue by separating format learning from semantic learning. During the first stage, a soft prompt is tuned to capture task-specific formats, while the model's parameters remain frozen. In the second stage, the model's parameters are fine-tuned while the soft prompt is kept constant. This approach is shown to improve the generalization of LLMs across various NLP tasks. The paper also discusses the empirical observation that format specialization occurs early in the fine-tuning process and proposes that this is a major cause of the loss of general in-context learning abilities.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of LLMs, specifically the loss of general in-context learning abilities after fine-tuning on a specific task.
- The proposed solution, ProMoT, is simple, effective, and intuitive, making it easy to implement and understand.
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the problem and the proposed solution, and includes thorough experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of ProMoT.
- The experiments are extensive, covering a wide range of tasks and demonstrating that ProMoT can improve generalization on semantically related tasks with different formats.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the results and methodology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed theoretical explanation for why ProMoT works, which could strengthen the argument for its effectiveness.
- There is a lack of comparison with other parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, such as LoRA, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of ProMoT's performance.
- The paper does not discuss the scalability of the proposed method with larger models, which is a significant limitation given the current trend towards larger models.
- The paper's claims about the generalization of ProMoT are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which show only marginal improvements in generalization.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, which is a significant concern in the context of fine-tuning LLMs.

**Questions:**
- How does the performance of ProMoT compare to other parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, such as LoRA?
- Can you provide more details on the training process for the soft prompt, particularly how it is initialized and optimized?
- How does the size of the soft prompt affect the performance of ProMoT? Is there a sweet spot in terms of the number of parameters for the soft prompt?
- In the ablation study, why was joint fine-tuning not compared with ProMoT + 1-shot?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that aim to alleviate catastrophic forgetting in LLMs?
- Can you provide more details on the training and inference processes for the soft prompt, including how it is initialized and how it affects the model's generalization?
