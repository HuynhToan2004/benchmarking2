 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of masked language modeling (MLM) on the representation capacity of models, specifically focusing on the use of the [MASK] token. It argues that the presence of the [MASK] token in pretraining data reduces the model's capacity to represent real tokens effectively, a phenomenon referred to as "representation deficiency." The authors propose a novel method, MAE-LM, which omits the [MASK] token from encoder inputs during pretraining, allowing the model to utilize its full capacity for real token representations. This approach is supported by empirical evidence showing improved model performance on downstream tasks. However, concerns are raised about the novelty and practical applicability of the method, as it may not be feasible for large-scale pretraining tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel perspective on the impact of masked language modeling (MLM) on model representation capacity, specifically addressing the representation deficiency issue caused by the [MASK] token.
- The proposed method of excluding [MASK] tokens from encoder inputs during pretraining is supported by empirical evidence, showing that it can improve model performance on downstream tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support the findings.
- The method's simplicity and potential for practical application in real-world scenarios are highlighted, suggesting its relevance and applicability beyond academic settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other models that have addressed similar issues, such as those using the [CLS] token or other methods to handle masked tokens.
- The empirical evidence provided does not convincingly demonstrate that the [MASK] token is the primary cause of representation deficiency; other factors such as positional encoding and decoder architecture could also contribute.
- The method's practical applicability is limited due to the requirement for a decoder during pretraining, which may not be feasible for large-scale pretraining tasks.
- The paper does not sufficiently explore the implications of the method for large-scale pretraining tasks, where the use of a decoder might not be feasible.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its generalizability to other pretraining settings and its potential impact on model performance in different contexts.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other models that have addressed similar issues, such as those using the [CLS] token or other methods to handle masked tokens?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability with other methods that aim to address representation deficiency in MLM?
- Can the authors clarify the empirical evidence supporting the claim that the [MASK] token is the primary cause of representation deficiency, and discuss other potential contributing factors?
- How does the method perform in larger-scale pretraining scenarios, where the use of a decoder during pretraining might not be feasible?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential limitations of the method and discuss how it might be adapted or improved to address these limitations?
