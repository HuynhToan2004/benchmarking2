 **Summary:**
The paper introduces OpenTab, a framework that leverages Large Language Models (LLMs) to handle structured table data in open-domain table reasoning tasks. It utilizes a table retriever to fetch relevant tables, a coder to generate SQL programs, and a reader to parse these programs and generate responses. The system is evaluated on datasets like Open-WikiTable, WikiTableQuestions, and FEVEROUS, demonstrating its effectiveness in both open and closed settings. The authors claim that OpenTab outperforms existing methods, achieving significant improvements in accuracy and scalability. However, concerns are raised about the novelty and the depth of technical contribution, as the methodology primarily relies on existing techniques without significant innovation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The proposed method is intuitive and simple, with a straightforward and reasonable approach to addressing the problem.
- The system is evaluated on both open and closed settings, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling various scenarios.
- The paper includes ablation studies that support the effectiveness of the proposed components, providing evidence for the system's performance.
- The methodology is adaptable and can be applied to other LLM-based frameworks, enhancing its practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the work is limited, as the approach primarily relies on existing techniques like BM25, SQL, and LLMs without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in the context of table retrieval and reasoning, which could undermine the claims of superior performance.
- The methodology's reliance on LLMs for SQL generation and table reasoning raises concerns about the potential for hallucinations and inaccuracies in the generated SQL programs.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability and complexity of the SQL programs generated, which could limit the system's ability to handle complex queries.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational resources required for running the system, which could affect its practical deployment in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in the context of table retrieval and reasoning, especially in terms of performance and efficiency?
- How does the system handle the generation of SQL programs that are too complex for the LLM to process effectively?
- What are the specific computational resources required for running the system, and how does this impact the practical deployment of the system?
- How does the system handle the potential for hallucinations and inaccuracies in the generated SQL programs, and what measures are in place to mitigate these issues?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the retrieval performance, especially in comparison to other methods like DPR?
- In the context of the GRSR strategy, how does the system ensure that it is not just relying on the LLM's ability to generate valid SQL programs without actual relevance to the query?
