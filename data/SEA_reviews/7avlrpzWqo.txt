 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new aggregation method for distributed deep learning, focusing on robustness against Byzantine failures and data augmentation. The authors formulate the aggregation process as a Maximum Likelihood Estimation using Beta densities and propose a regularized log-likelihood solvable via an iterative least squares solver. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive experiments, comparing it with existing methods and showing improvements in robustness and convergence. Despite its innovative approach, the paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in explaining the technical contributions and the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel aggregation method that is scalable and robust, which is crucial for large-scale distributed deep learning systems.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of its theoretical underpinnings, including the use of maximum likelihood estimation and regularization.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in various scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, with a clear presentation of the experimental results.
- The proposed method is innovative, using a flag aggregator to solve the problem of robust aggregation, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in explaining the technical contributions and the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method.
- The connection between the proposed method and existing works, such as robust principal component analysis, is not clearly articulated, making it difficult to understand the novelty of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the convergence properties of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its effectiveness in practical scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which limits the evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous proof of the convergence of the proposed algorithm, especially in the context of Byzantine failures and data augmentation.
- The paper is sometimes hard to follow due to its complexity and lack of clear explanations in some sections, particularly in the technical details of the proposed method.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed method handle different types of Byzantine failures, such as those that send random gradients versus those that send amplified sign-flipped gradients?
- Can you provide more details on the convergence properties of the proposed method, especially in the presence of Byzantine failures and data augmentation?
