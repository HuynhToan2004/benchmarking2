 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training-free metric for Neural Architecture Search (NAS), named SWAP-Score, which evaluates the expressivity of networks based on sample-wise activation patterns. This metric is designed to address the limitations of existing training-free metrics, such as limited correlation and generalization across different search spaces and tasks. The SWAP-Score is evaluated against 15 existing metrics on NAS-Bench-101/201/301 and TransNAS-Bench-101, showing strong correlations with ground-truth performance. The paper also proposes an evolutionary algorithm, SWAP-NAS, which integrates the SWAP-Score with regularization to control model size during the search. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism regarding the clarity of its methodology, the need for broader validation across different NAS methods and search spaces, and the potential for overfitting due to the high correlation between the metric and the ground-truth performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel training-free metric, SWAP-Score, which is based on sample-wise activation patterns, providing a significant advancement in the field of Neural Architecture Search (NAS).
- The SWAP-Score is shown to be highly correlated with ground-truth performance, outperforming 15 existing training-free metrics across various search spaces and tasks.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the SWAP-Score in controlling model size during the architecture search, which is a significant challenge in NAS.
- The SWAP-NAS algorithm, which integrates regularized SWAP-Score with evolutionary search, achieves competitive performance on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet in approximately 6 minutes and 9 minutes of GPU time respectively.
- The paper provides a detailed ablation study that helps in understanding the limitations of the proposed method and provides insights into its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the methodology, particularly in the definition and explanation of the SWAP-Score, which is crucial for understanding the proposed metric's effectiveness.
- The paper does not provide sufficient validation across different NAS methods and search spaces, which is necessary to establish the robustness and generalizability of the SWAP-Score.
- The paper's claims about the SWAP-Score's superiority over existing metrics are not convincingly supported, as the experiments are not comprehensive enough to cover all relevant metrics and search spaces.
- The paper's methodology and experimental setup could benefit from a more detailed description and justification, especially regarding the choice of regularization parameters and their impact on model size and performance.
- The paper's writing could be improved to enhance clarity and readability, particularly in the presentation of results and methodological details.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the methodology behind the SWAP-Score, particularly how it is computed and what it represents in terms of network expressivity?
- How does the SWAP-Score compare to other training-free metrics, especially in terms of correlation with ground-truth performance and generalization across different search spaces and tasks?
- What are the specific advantages of the SWAP-Score over other metrics, such as the number of network parameters or FLOPs, which are often simpler and more widely used in NAS?
- How does the SWAP-Score perform in different NAS methods and search spaces, especially those not covered in the current study?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the choice of regularization parameters and their impact on model size and performance?
- How does the SWAP-Score handle architectures with different activation functions, such as GELU, and what are the implications for its effectiveness and applicability?
