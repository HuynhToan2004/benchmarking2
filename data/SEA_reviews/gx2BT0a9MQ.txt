 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ZeRO++ as an extension of the ZeRO framework aimed at enhancing communication efficiency in large-scale model training. ZeRO++ incorporates three main techniques: quantized weight communication, hierarchical weight partitioning, and quantized gradient communication, to reduce communication overhead and improve throughput. These techniques are validated through experiments on various model sizes and hardware configurations, demonstrating significant speedups and scalability up to 384 GPUs. Despite its promising results, concerns were raised about the novelty of some techniques and the clarity of some experimental results and methodologies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue of efficient training of large models, which is crucial for advancing the field of AI.
- It proposes a well-motivated approach that optimizes the ZeRO framework, focusing on reducing communication overhead and improving throughput.
- The methodology is supported by extensive experiments on various model sizes, hardware configurations, and network bandwidths, demonstrating consistent speedups and scalability.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed techniques, including a thorough comparison with baseline methods and an ablation study that shows the effectiveness of each component.
- The authors have made a significant effort to optimize the implementation of the proposed techniques, which is crucial for achieving the claimed benefits.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the figures and tables are clear, enhancing the understanding of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for a comprehensive understanding of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The novelty of the techniques used, such as hierarchical weight partitioning and quantized gradient communication, is questionable as they have been previously explored in similar contexts.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, particularly the specifics of the hardware and software configurations used, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The paper's focus on throughput and speedup metrics might not fully capture the practical implications of the proposed techniques, especially in terms of end-to-end training time and resource utilization.
- There are some inconsistencies and potential errors in the experimental results and methodologies, such as the discrepancies in throughput measurements and the clarity of the experimental setups.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific hardware and software configurations used in their experiments, including the GPU and network architectures, to ensure reproducibility and comparability?
- How do the authors address the limitations of their proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the communication overhead is not significantly reduced?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the batch sizes used and the specific training data employed?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of end-to-end training time, especially when compared to baseline methods?
- What are the specific implementation details of the qwZ, hpZ, and qgZ techniques, and how do they compare to existing methods in terms of efficiency and effectiveness?
- Can the authors elaborate on the potential implications of using different precision levels (e.g., 2-bit) for weight quantization, especially in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency?
