 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for personalized federated learning (PFL) called pFedGT, which utilizes a game-theoretic approach to address data heterogeneity and local demands. The method involves each user solving a local optimization problem that combines local and global losses, approximated using second-order expansion. This approach is designed to ensure convergence to a Nash equilibrium and achieve high accuracy across various datasets. The paper provides theoretical analysis and experimental results, demonstrating the effectiveness of pFedGT compared to existing methods. However, concerns are raised regarding the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the experimental setup, and the practical implications of the method in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to personalized federated learning (PFL) by combining local and global losses, which is a unique approach in the field.
- The proposed method is theoretically sound, with proofs provided for the existence and uniqueness of a Nash equilibrium, and a linear convergence rate established.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the methodology and its implications.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of federated learning, focusing on the personalization of models, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of computational cost and communication overhead, which could limit its applicability in large-scale federated learning environments.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as similar methods using weighted averages of local and global losses have been previously explored.
- The experimental setup is not clearly described, with missing details on the number of clients, data partitioning, and the specifics of the federated learning process.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not compare it with the latest advancements in the field.
- The convergence analysis provided is not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of the assumptions made and the practical implications of these assumptions.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the privacy concerns in federated learning, which is a critical aspect of the field.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the convergence analysis, particularly the strong convexity assumption and its practical implications?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, especially in terms of computational cost and communication overhead?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the number of clients, data partitioning, and the specifics of the federated learning process?
- How does the proposed method compare with the latest advancements in the field of federated learning, particularly in terms of accuracy and efficiency?
- Can the authors address the privacy concerns in federated learning and discuss how their method ensures data privacy?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with a large number of clients or in settings where data is highly non-iid?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the convergence rate and the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the algorithm?
