 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel incentive-aware federated learning (IAFL) algorithm that rewards clients with model updates commensurate with their contributions, aiming to incentivize higher participation and performance. The algorithm addresses the issue of error propagation by introducing a stochastic reference-model recovery strategy. Theoretical analysis and extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the superior incentivizing performance of IAFL compared to existing baselines. The paper also discusses the potential limitations and societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to incentivize clients in federated learning (FL) by providing training-time model rewards, which is a departure from traditional post-training monetary rewards.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to justify the design choices, and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the superior incentivizing performance of the proposed method compared to existing baselines.
- The paper is innovative in its approach to addressing the issue of error propagation in model rewards, proposing a stochastic reference-model recovery strategy to mitigate this issue.
- The proposed method is flexible and can be applied to a wide range of domains, which is a significant advantage.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that clients have equal weights in the global objective, which may not be practical in real-world scenarios where clients may have varying data sizes or contributions.
- The theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions such as full client participation and a decreasing learning rate, which may not hold in practical scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implementation of the proposed mechanism, particularly how clients decide their contributions and how the server decides the rewarding function.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for strategic behavior by clients, which could undermine the effectiveness of the incentive mechanism.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing baselines, and the paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on how the proposed method handles the issue of free-riding, which is a significant challenge in incentivizing mechanisms.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where clients have varying data sizes or contributions?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the proposed mechanism can be practically implemented in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method address the potential for strategic behavior by clients?
- How does the proposed method compare to state-of-the-art methods in terms of incentivizing clients and improving model performance?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental results and how they demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method ensure that clients do not engage in free-riding behavior, which could undermine the effectiveness of the incentive mechanism?
