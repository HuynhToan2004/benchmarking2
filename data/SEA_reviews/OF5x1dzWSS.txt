 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel instance-reweighted adversarial training method aimed at enhancing robustness against adversarial attacks. The approach utilizes a doubly robust framework that incorporates distributionally robust optimization (DRO) techniques to optimize importance weights. This methodology is theoretically grounded and provides convergence guarantees, which are supported by extensive experimental results across various datasets. The paper also addresses the issue of non-uniform robustness across the training distribution, a common challenge in adversarial training. The proposed method shows promising results in improving robustness against attacks, particularly on the weakest data points.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for determining importance weights in adversarial training, which is both theoretically sound and supported by extensive experimental results.
- The proposed methodology is well-articulated, with a clear explanation of the theoretical underpinnings and practical implications.
- The paper effectively addresses the issue of non-uniform robustness across the training distribution, which is a significant challenge in adversarial training.
- The authors provide a convergence guarantee for their algorithm, enhancing the reliability and robustness of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving robustness against adversarial attacks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the scalability and generalizability of the approach.
- The convergence analysis and the proofs provided are not sufficiently detailed or intuitive, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the theoretical contributions.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical implementation, which could be crucial for real-world applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which is a known challenge in adversarial training.
- The method's performance on large-scale datasets such as ImageNet is not explored, which could limit the understanding of its scalability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their proposed method, particularly concerning its scalability and generalizability?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical implementation with existing methods in adversarial training?
- Can the authors provide more intuition behind the theoretical results and proofs, especially in the convergence analysis section?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of overfitting, which is a known challenge in adversarial training?
- Could the authors consider extending their experiments to include larger datasets such as ImageNet to assess the scalability and effectiveness of their method in more complex scenarios?
