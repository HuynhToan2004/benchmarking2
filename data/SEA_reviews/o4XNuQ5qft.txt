 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Optimum Shifting (OS), aimed at altering the parameters of a neural network to achieve flatter minima while maintaining the same training loss. This is achieved by treating matrix multiplications within the network as under-determined linear equations, allowing for adjustments in the parameter space. The authors propose a stochastic variant, Stochastic Optimum Shifting (SOS), which is more computationally efficient and is shown to improve generalization in experiments on various datasets, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, Tiny-ImageNet, and PASCAL VOC. The method's effectiveness is supported by theoretical bounds on the Hessian trace and empirical results demonstrating improvements in generalization and training stability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is novel and straightforward, providing a simple yet effective approach to improving the generalization of neural networks.
- The experiments are well-designed, covering a variety of datasets and network architectures, and the results are convincing, demonstrating the method's effectiveness.
- The paper provides theoretical bounds on the Hessian trace, which is a useful theoretical contribution.
- The proposed stochastic optimum shifting method is computationally efficient and can be easily integrated into traditional regularization techniques, making it practical for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly concerning the memory and computational requirements.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets and network architectures, which might not fully demonstrate the method's applicability across different domains.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art techniques for finding flatter minima, which could help in understanding its relative effectiveness.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the method, particularly in the algorithm descriptions, which could benefit from more detailed explanations and potentially a pseudocode representation.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art techniques for finding flatter minima, such as those mentioned in the literature?
- Can the authors provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, including memory and computational requirements?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different datasets and network architectures beyond those used in the experiments?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of parameters in the experiments, such as the batch sizes used in the stochastic optimum shifting method?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential issues of overfitting, especially in scenarios where the generalization performance might not be as expected?
