 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel neural network architecture, termed smooth min-max (SMM) networks, which address the issue of silent neurons in traditional min-max networks by replacing maximum and minimum operations with smooth and monotone counterparts. This modification enhances the model's generalization performance and reduces the risk of local optima during training. The SMM network is shown to perform comparably to or better than existing methods in various experiments, including both synthetic and real-world datasets. The authors also provide a theoretical analysis of the SMM network's properties and its ability to approximate monotonic functions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of monotonic neural networks, which is the issue of silent neurons and the inability to approximate monotonic functions effectively.
- The proposed solution, replacing maximum and minimum operations with smooth and monotone counterparts, is simple, elegant, and well-motivated.
- The empirical results are convincing, showing that the proposed SMM network outperforms existing methods in terms of generalization performance and the ability to approximate monotonic functions.
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the motivation, methodology, and experimental results, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- Theoretical analysis is provided, showing that the SMM network inherits the asymptotic approximation properties of the min-max architecture while avoiding the issues of silent neurons.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scope and applicability.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse and realistic datasets, such as those from the UCI machine learning repository, to better evaluate the method's performance in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the SMM network with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and generalization performance across different types of datasets.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the SMM network, which is important for practical applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of silent neurons in the min-max network, which is a critical problem in neural network training.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its potential applications?
- How does the computational complexity of the SMM network compare to other methods, particularly in terms of training and inference times?
- Could the authors include additional experiments using datasets from the UCI machine learning repository to evaluate the method's performance in more diverse and realistic scenarios?
- How does the SMM network handle the issue of silent neurons, and what steps have been taken to address this in the training process?
- Is there a possibility to include more state-of-the-art methods in future experiments to provide a more comprehensive comparison?
