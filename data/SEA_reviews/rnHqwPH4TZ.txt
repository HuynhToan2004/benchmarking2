 **Summary:**
The paper introduces T-Stitch, a novel sampling technique for diffusion models that leverages a smaller model for early denoising steps and a larger model for later refinement. This method, which is applicable across different model architectures and diffusion samplers, is shown to improve inference speed while maintaining or even enhancing image quality. The approach is supported by experiments that demonstrate significant speedups and competitive performance against other methods. The paper also discusses the potential for further training-based improvements and the application of T-Stitch to various diffusion models, including stable diffusion models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, T-Stitch, is simple, effective, and applicable to a variety of diffusion models, including stable diffusion models, without requiring additional training.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of T-Stitch through experiments that showcase its ability to improve inference speed while maintaining or even enhancing image quality.
- The method is well-motivated and supported by extensive experiments, which include ablation studies and comparisons with other methods like model stitching.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to understand the main contributions and the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of T-Stitch, particularly in terms of its applicability to different diffusion model families and the need for similar training conditions.
- The paper could benefit from more diverse experimental setups, such as using different diffusion model samplers and varying the number of denoising steps.
- There is a lack of quantitative evaluation metrics, which could provide a more comprehensive assessment of the method's performance.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational efficiency and memory usage of T-Stitch, particularly in comparison to other methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency and memory usage of T-Stitch, especially in comparison to other methods?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different diffusion model families, and what are the specific conditions under which it can be effectively applied?
- Could the authors clarify the definition and usage of terms such as "stylized diffusion models" and "prompt alignment" to avoid confusion?
- How does the method perform when using different diffusion model samplers, and what are the implications for the overall performance and efficiency of T-Stitch?
- Can the authors provide more examples or qualitative results to support the claims made about the method's effectiveness and applicability across different model architectures?
