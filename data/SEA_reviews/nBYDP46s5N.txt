 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to reinforcement learning (RL) named Truncated Value Learning (TVL), which aims to address the issue of bootstrapping in RL algorithms. TVL learns rewards discount-free and values without bootstrapping, which is hypothesized to improve performance in high-noise environments by reducing error propagation. The method is tested on challenging environments like Procgen and Atari, showing promising results. The core idea is to learn a value function for all possible horizons and use linear interpolation to approximate values for unseen horizons. This method is contrasted with traditional methods like TD-lambda, which require the computation of all possible discounted returns.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in reinforcement learning (RL) by challenging the conventional use of discounting in RL algorithms, which is often seen as a necessary evil.
- The proposed method, Truncated Value Learning (TVL), is novel and shows promising results, particularly in high-noise environments like Procgen and Atari.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide a detailed explanation of their method and its implementation, including a clear description of the algorithm and its components.
- The paper includes a thorough discussion of the results, including a detailed analysis of the performance of TVL compared to other methods like PPO and PPG.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the convergence properties of TVL, particularly in the context of noise and the choice of hyperparameters.
- The empirical evaluation is limited to specific environments (Procgen and Atari), which might not fully demonstrate the general applicability of the method.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a more comprehensive view of the method's performance.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of computational efficiency, which is a significant concern given the complexity of the method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of TVL, particularly in comparison to existing methods like TD-lambda.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved to make it more accessible to a broader audience, including more visual aids and a clearer explanation of the method's components and their interactions.

**Questions:**
- How does TVL perform in environments with different noise levels, and how does this affect the convergence properties of the method?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the computational efficiency of TVL compared to other methods?
- How does the method handle the convergence of the value function across different horizons, and what implications does this have for the overall performance of the method?
- In the context of the Procgen benchmark, how does TVL compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance on specific tasks?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of TVL with other methods, including a discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the method?
