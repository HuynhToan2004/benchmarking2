 **Summary:**
The paper introduces "BadChain," a novel backdoor attack method that manipulates chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning in large language models (LLMs) without requiring access to the training dataset or model parameters. The attack leverages the reasoning capabilities of LLMs by inserting a backdoor reasoning step into the sequence of reasoning steps, which alters the final response when a backdoor trigger is embedded in the query prompt. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of BadChain across various LLMs and complex benchmark tasks, showing high attack success rates. However, the paper's novelty is questioned, as it builds upon existing methods like DT-COT, and the attack's reliance on the specifics of the CoT demonstrations raises concerns about its generalizability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel backdoor attack method, "BadChain," which is the first to target LLMs using CoT prompting without requiring access to the training dataset or model parameters.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear presentation of the attack design, including the use of non-word-based and phrase-based triggers, and the poisoning of demonstrations to insert a backdoor reasoning step.
- The paper includes extensive empirical evaluations, demonstrating the effectiveness of BadChain across various LLMs and complex benchmark tasks, showing high attack success rates.
- The interpretability of the attack is highlighted, with the authors providing examples of the relationship between the trigger and the backdoor reasoning step.
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue of backdoor attacks on LLMs, which is crucial for ensuring the security and reliability of these models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its generalizability and potential vulnerabilities.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as it appears to build upon existing methods like DT-COT, with minor modifications such as the use of a backdoor reasoning step.
- The paper's reliance on the specifics of the CoT demonstrations raises concerns about the attack's effectiveness in scenarios where the attacker does not have access to these demonstrations.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the attack, particularly in terms of the number of demonstrations required for effective poisoning.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive discussion on the ethical implications of the proposed attack, which is crucial given the potential negative societal impacts.
- The paper's claims of novelty and effectiveness are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which do not clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing attacks.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing backdoor attacks on LLMs, particularly in terms of attack success rates and computational overhead?
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their method compared to existing attacks, especially those that also utilize backdoor reasoning steps?
- How does the paper address the issue of scalability in the attack, particularly in terms of the number of demonstrations required for effective poisoning?
- Given the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed attack, how does the paper address the ethical implications of the research?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the effectiveness of the attack in real-world scenarios?
- How does the paper ensure the reliability and robustness of the attack against potential countermeasures or defenses?
