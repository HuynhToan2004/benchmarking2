 **Summary:**
The paper explores robust time series forecasting under anomaly conditions, introducing a novel algorithm, RobustTSF, which identifies informative samples based on the variance between original inputs and their trends. The algorithm employs a robust loss function to enhance forecasting accuracy, particularly in the presence of anomalies. Theoretical and empirical analyses are provided to demonstrate the robustness of the MAE loss function and the algorithm's effectiveness in handling different types of anomalies. The paper also discusses the limitations and potential improvements, suggesting that the method could be extended to handle more complex anomaly types and be tested on a broader range of datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in time series forecasting with anomalies, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and well-articulated, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.
- The empirical results are robust and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, supported by detailed experimental setups and validation.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, making the complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is simple, efficient, and theoretically grounded, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The theoretical analysis primarily focuses on MAE loss, which might not be the most robust choice for all types of anomalies.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods, particularly in terms of novelty and practical applicability.
- The experimental setup and results could be improved by including more complex datasets and a broader range of anomaly types.
- There are some inconsistencies and unclear explanations in the paper, such as the choice of hyperparameters and the clarity in some sections of the text.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the anomaly rate is high, such as 0.5 or 0.7?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the MAE loss performs under different anomaly conditions, particularly in scenarios where it might not be the most robust choice?
- How is the hyperparameter λ chosen, and what impact does it have on the performance of the algorithm?
- Could the authors clarify the definition and usage of the hyperparameter γ in the context of the algorithm?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of anomalies beyond point-wise anomalies, such as subsequence anomalies?
- Could the authors provide more insights into the practical implications of the theoretical results, especially in real-world scenarios where anomalies might not follow the assumptions used in the analysis?
