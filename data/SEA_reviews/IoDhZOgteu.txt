 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework called DEXR for intrinsically motivated exploration in reinforcement learning, focusing on balancing exploration and exploitation across different types of environments with minimal hyperparameter tuning. It utilizes an additional exploitation policy to guide the agent to promising regions, followed by an exploration policy to collect novel data for refining the exploitation policy. The framework is evaluated across various environments, showing improved performance over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the theoretical analysis, and the empirical evidence supporting the claims of hyperparameter insensitivity and robustness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined problem.
- The proposed framework is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, with a novel approach to using an auxiliary exploitation-only policy for data collection.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing its applicability across different types of environments and its robustness to hyperparameter settings.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm, which enhances the understanding of the method's underpinnings.
- The framework is flexible and can be integrated with any intrinsically motivated off-policy RL algorithms, making it a versatile tool for addressing exploration-exploitation challenges.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its practical applicability.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as similar ideas have been previously explored in other works, such as those by Ecoffet et al. (2019) and Agarwal et al. (2020).
- The theoretical analysis, particularly the proofs, is not adequately explained, making it difficult for readers to fully grasp the implications of the results.
- The empirical evaluation is limited to specific environments and does not include a broader range of tasks or more complex scenarios, which could undermine the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of clarity in the algorithm's design choices and the rationale behind certain parameters, such as the truncation probability.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to previous works, particularly those by Ecoffet et al. (2019) and Agarwal et al. (2020)?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of performance and efficiency, especially in scenarios with different reward structures?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples of how the intrinsic reward module $b$ is parameterized and how it affects the exploration behavior?
- How does the proposed method handle the exploration-exploitation trade-off in more complex, real-world scenarios, and what are the implications for its practical applicability?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of the algorithm, particularly the proofs and their implications for the practical implementation of the method?
