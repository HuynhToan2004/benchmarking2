 **Summary:**
The paper explores the identification of causal graphs from subsampled time series data, specifically focusing on scenarios where only two time slices are available. It introduces a novel method, DHT-CIT, which utilizes a conditional independence test to distinguish between descendant and non-descendant nodes, thereby reducing the complexity of the problem. The method is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing superior performance compared to existing methods. The paper also discusses the limitations and potential improvements of the proposed approach, including the assumption of acyclic causal graphs and the need for further validation on more diverse datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the problem of causal discovery from subsampled time series.
- The proposed method is novel, particularly the use of a conditional independence test to distinguish between descendant and non-descendant nodes, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper includes both synthetic and real-world data experiments, which validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The theoretical results are well-explained, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.
- The paper includes a detailed discussion of related work, which helps in understanding the context and the novelty of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes an acyclic causal graph, which may not hold in all real-world scenarios, and this could limit the applicability of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup is limited, with only a few datasets used, which may not adequately test the robustness and generalizability of the method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of spurious correlations, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its assumptions and the conditions under which it may not perform well.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the number of variables and the size of the datasets?
- How does the proposed method handle spurious correlations, and what measures are in place to ensure the reliability of the results?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and the rationale behind their selection?
- How does the proposed method perform on datasets with more variables and larger time-slice intervals?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method in more detail, particularly in terms of the assumptions and conditions under which it may not perform well?
