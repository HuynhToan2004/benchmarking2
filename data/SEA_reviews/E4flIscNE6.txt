 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel meta-collaborative knowledge distillation framework, where multiple student models are distilled from a single teacher model, and students collaborate through information sharing during distillation. This is facilitated by a separate network designed to predict instance-specific loss mixing for each student. The method aims to improve student accuracy across different model sizes and architectures, demonstrating significant gains on CIFAR100 and TinyImageNet datasets. The paper also explores the use of a coordinator network (C-Net) to manage the distillation process, which is updated less frequently to reduce computational costs. Despite the innovative approach, the paper faces criticisms regarding its novelty, the complexity of the method, and the lack of detailed experimental comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge distillation, focusing on meta-collaborative learning, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is clearly presented, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed framework.
- The paper demonstrates impressive performance gains over existing methods, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The experimental results are robust and showcase the method's ability to outperform various baseline models across different datasets.
- The paper is well-written and easy to understand, enhancing its readability and accessibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it bears similarities to existing methods such as AMAL and multi-teacher distillation, which may not represent a significant advancement.
- The complexity of the method, involving multiple networks and hyperparameters, could pose challenges in practical implementation and tuning.
- The paper lacks detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the number of hyperparameters used.
- There is a need for more extensive experiments on larger datasets like ImageNet to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method on a broader scale.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations or negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the differences between the proposed method and AMAL, particularly in terms of the loss mixing parameters and the coordinator network?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and the number of hyperparameters used compared to existing methods?
- What are the specific advantages of the meta-collaborative approach over traditional knowledge distillation methods, especially in terms of performance and efficiency?
- How does the method handle different learning rates for different student models, and what impact does this have on the overall performance?
- Could you provide more details on the training process of the coordinator network and its impact on the overall distillation process?
- How does the method perform when using different teacher models, and what are the implications for the student models' performance?
