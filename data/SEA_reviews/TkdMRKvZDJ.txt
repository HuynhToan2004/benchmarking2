 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to single-domain generalized object detection, utilizing the GLIP model to transfer visual features from the source domain to the target domain. The methodology involves defining textual prompts for each target domain and training a style transfer module to adapt the visual features accordingly. This approach allows the model to generalize well across multiple unseen target domains, showing significant improvements over existing methods on benchmarks such as BDD-100K. The methodology is novel in its application of the GLIP model and its approach to style transfer, but the paper has been criticized for its limited experimental scope and lack of comparison with more recent and relevant methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to single-domain generalized object detection using the GLIP model, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-explained and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the experimental setup and results.
- The paper achieves impressive results on the BDD-100K benchmark, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The use of textual prompts to guide the style transfer process is a novel and interesting approach, which could be applied in other contexts.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with more recent and relevant methods, particularly those that address single-domain generalized object detection using vision-language models.
- The experimental setup is limited to a single source domain and a few target domains, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalization capabilities of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the need for extensive annotation efforts and the potential for overfitting to the source domain.
- The methodology is somewhat limited in its application, as it requires extensive annotation efforts and may not be suitable for all types of domain adaptation scenarios.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the related work, particularly in the area of single-domain generalized object detection, which could help contextualize the contributions of the paper.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specifics of the target domain prompts and how they are generated?
- How does the proposed method compare to other vision-language models like CLIP and BLIP2, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- What are the specific advantages of using the GLIP model over other vision-language models for this task?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the source and target domains are significantly different, such as daytime vs. nighttime conditions?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process, including the number of epochs and the batch size used?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting to the source domain, and what steps are taken to mitigate this risk?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for adapting the proposed method to other types of domain adaptation scenarios, such as those involving different types of weather conditions?
