 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LST-Bench, a comprehensive benchmark designed for evaluating long sequence time-series forecasting models. It includes 11 major forecasting models tested on 7 datasets and 7 new datasets, focusing on the evaluation of models like Transformer-based, MLP-based, CNN-based, and RNN-based models. The benchmark addresses the issue of "Degeneracy" where models maintain low MSE values but generate repetitive patterns, questioning the effectiveness of existing evaluation metrics. The paper also explores the phenomenon of model performance saturation after a few epochs, suggesting potential inefficiencies in training procedures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new benchmark, LST-Bench, which includes 7 newly introduced datasets and 7 commonly used datasets, providing a comprehensive evaluation of long sequence time-series forecasting models.
- It presents a thorough analysis of the experimental results, including the overall prediction performance of models and their generalization across different prediction lengths and datasets.
- The paper discusses the phenomenon of "Degeneracy" where models maintain low MSE values but generate repetitive patterns, which is a significant issue in time series forecasting.
- The introduction of 7 new datasets adds to the diversity and complexity of the evaluation, enhancing the benchmark's utility for researchers and practitioners.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the experimental results, including the overall prediction performance of models and their generalization across different prediction lengths and datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the benchmarking process and the criteria used for model selection, which could lead to questions about the fairness and comprehensiveness of the evaluation.
- There is a lack of discussion on the impact of external factors such as data quality, feature engineering, and hyperparameter tuning on model performance, which are crucial for practical applications.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed benchmark, which could affect its applicability and credibility.
- The benchmark's design does not include a discussion on the impact of different model architectures and hyperparameters on model performance, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of different models.
- The paper's writing and organization could be improved to better convey the research's contributions and findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the benchmarking process and the criteria used for model selection to ensure the fairness and comprehensiveness of the evaluation?
- How do the authors address the issue of "Degeneracy" in their benchmark, and what steps are being taken to improve the evaluation metrics to better reflect the actual requirements of time series forecasting tasks?
- Could the authors discuss the impact of external factors such as data quality, feature engineering, and hyperparameter tuning on model performance, and how these factors were controlled or accounted for in the benchmark?
- How does the proposed benchmark compare to existing benchmarks, and what are the key differences in terms of methodology, datasets, and evaluation metrics?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training epochs and training time required for different models, and how does this affect the practical applicability of the models?
- How do the authors plan to address the limitations of the benchmark, and what future work is planned to enhance the benchmark's utility and applicability?
