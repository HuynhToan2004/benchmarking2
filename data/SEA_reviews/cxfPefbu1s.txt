 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel concept of "disguised procedural unfairness" in machine learning models, focusing on the causal structure of data generation. It critiques existing fairness notions that primarily address outcomes and proposes a new method to decouple objectionable components from the data generation process, ensuring fairness in the prediction model. The paper presents a framework that utilizes reference points and a value instantiation rule to maintain neutral components while mitigating the influence of objectionable components. It is supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, demonstrating its effectiveness through experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and overlooked issue of fairness in machine learning, specifically focusing on the causal structure of data generation, which is crucial for ensuring fairness in predictive models.
- The introduction of the concept of "disguised procedural unfairness" is a significant contribution to the field, highlighting the limitations of existing fairness notions that primarily focus on outcomes.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the proposed framework and its application, making it accessible to readers.
- The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The paper provides a novel approach to fairness in machine learning by decoupling objectionable components from the data generation process, which is a significant departure from traditional fairness notions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential drawbacks of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the causal structure of data generation is complex or unknown.
- The paper's reliance on a causal graph to identify objectionable components assumes that the graph is known, which may not be the case in many real-world applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing fairness notions, particularly in terms of how it addresses the same issues as other fairness notions.
- The paper's methodology, while innovative, may be limited in its practical applicability due to the assumptions it makes about the availability of reference points and the structure of the data generation process.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the scalability of the proposed method, especially in large-scale applications.
- The paper's presentation of results, particularly in figures, could be improved to better illustrate the contributions of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the proposed method would handle scenarios where the causal graph of data generation is unknown or incomplete?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing fairness notions in terms of addressing the same issues of fairness in data generation?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the reference points are identified and how they are applied in practice?
- How does the proposed method ensure that the decoupling of objectionable components does not introduce new biases or unfairness in the prediction model?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially in large-scale applications?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the data generation process is dynamic or where there are multiple sources of bias?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental results, particularly in figures where the contributions of the proposed method are not clearly illustrated?
