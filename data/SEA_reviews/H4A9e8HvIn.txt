 **Summary:**
The paper investigates online continuous adversarial DR-submodular optimization, proposing a unified framework for Frank-Wolfe type algorithms in adversarial settings. It covers various feedback models, including full information, semi-bandit, and bandit, and addresses both monotone and non-monotone functions. The proposed algorithms achieve state-of-the-art α-regret bounds for most cases, with notable improvements in regret bounds for non-monotone functions. The paper also includes experiments to validate the theoretical claims, demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed methods. However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues in the presentation and a lack of comprehensive comparison with existing works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a unified framework for Frank-Wolfe type algorithms in adversarial settings, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed algorithms achieve state-of-the-art α-regret bounds for most cases, improving upon existing methods in many instances.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the main ideas and algorithms.
- Experimental results are provided to validate the theoretical claims, which is a strong point in demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed methods.
- The paper covers a broad spectrum of cases, including different feedback models and function classes, which enhances the relevance and applicability of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a clearer presentation, particularly in the algorithm descriptions and the organization of the content.
- There is a lack of comprehensive comparison with existing works, especially in the related work section, which could confuse the reader about the novelty and contributions of the paper.
- The proofs and some technical details are relegated to the appendix, which might not be accessible to all readers.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.
- The experimental setup and results could be more convincing if they included a broader range of scenarios and comparisons with a larger set of baseline methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between the proposed algorithms and existing methods, especially in terms of novelty and practical improvements?
- How does the paper handle the non-monotone functions, which are known to be more challenging than monotone functions in DR-submodular optimization?
- What are the main technical challenges in extending the results to more general settings, such as non-smooth functions or different types of oracles?
- How does the paper compare with the work by Nian et al., particularly in terms of regret bounds and algorithmic approaches?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and results, possibly including additional scenarios and baseline comparisons to strengthen the validity of the findings?
