 **Summary:**
The paper explores the quantization of accumulators in deep neural networks (DNNs) to reduce computational costs, focusing on the FMA operation. The authors propose a two-stage training method that first uses full-precision accumulators to prevent underflow and then switches to low-bit accumulators for fine-tuning. The method is tested across various architectures and datasets, showing that it can achieve competitive performance with standard training methods. The paper also introduces a Straight-Through Estimator (STE) for backpropagation through the accumulation graph, which is crucial for training with low-bit accumulators. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental scope and the incremental nature of its contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to quantize the accumulator in deep neural networks (DNNs), which is crucial for reducing computational costs and improving efficiency.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of underflow, overflow, and swamping in the context of quantized accumulators, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, which is essential for a broad audience.
- The experimental results are strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods in various scenarios.
- The paper is the first to train and fine-tune models using low-bit accumulators, which is a significant advancement in the field of quantization.
- The use of a two-stage training method and the introduction of a Straight-Through Estimator (STE) for backpropagation through the accumulation graph are innovative contributions to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other existing methods for low-bit accumulator training, such as those mentioned in the review, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper's experimental scope is limited, focusing primarily on ResNet and BERT models, which may not generalize well to other architectures or datasets.
- The paper's contribution is seen as incremental, as it builds upon existing methods like WrapNet and does not offer significant new insights or methodologies.
- The paper does not provide a detailed hardware implementation or a comparison with hardware-specific implementations, which could have strengthened the paper's claims about the efficiency of low-bit accumulators.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of low-bit accumulators over full-precision accumulators are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which show only marginal improvements or even lower accuracy in some cases.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other existing methods for low-bit accumulator training, such as those mentioned in the review?
- How does the proposed method compare with hardware-specific implementations of low-bit accumulators, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the choice of parameters such as the exponent bias for the accumulator and the chunk size, and how these choices affect the performance and accuracy of the models?
- In the context of the two-stage training method, how does the model's performance compare when using low-bit accumulators from the beginning of training?
- Could the authors provide more details on the hardware implementation of the low-bit accumulators and how this affects the overall performance and efficiency of the models?
