 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Curriculum Instruction Tuning (CITING), a novel method for training student language models using teacher models to create a curriculum for instruction tuning. This approach involves the teacher model generating rubrics for evaluating responses to different types of questions, which the student model uses to self-correct and learn from the revisions made by the teacher. The method is evaluated against several state-of-the-art baselines and shows significant improvements in performance across various datasets. The paper also discusses the limitations of the approach, including the potential for overfitting and the need for further research on the generalizability of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the method and results.
- The proposed method is novel and effective, showing significant improvements in performance compared to existing methods.
- The paper includes comprehensive experiments and ablation studies, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of a teacher model to generate rubrics and guide the learning process of a student model is a novel and interesting approach.
- The method is evaluated on several datasets and shows promising results, with the potential to significantly enhance the performance of student models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential issues with overfitting and the generalizability of the method across different types of datasets.
- The methodology of using a teacher model to generate rubrics and guide the learning process of a student model is not sufficiently novel, as similar approaches have been explored in previous works.
- The paper does not include a detailed comparison with other recent methods, such as those using GPT-4 for instruction tuning or those using different types of feedback for student models.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, with some figures and tables lacking clarity and detailed explanations.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the time and cost implications of the proposed method, which could be a significant limitation for practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the specifics of how the teacher model was used to generate rubrics and how the student model was trained?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent approaches using GPT-4 for instruction tuning or different types of feedback for student models?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- How do you address the potential issues of overfitting and generalizability of the method across different types of datasets?
- Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the time and cost implications of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the number of iterations and the computational resources required?
- How do you ensure the quality and reliability of the rubrics generated by the teacher model, and what measures are in place to prevent overfitting or other biases in the learning process?
