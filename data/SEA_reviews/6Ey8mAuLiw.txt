 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the effectiveness of multi-task representation learning compared to single-task learning in over-parameterized two-layer convolutional neural networks trained by gradient descent. It proposes a data model that incorporates both task-shared and task-specific features to analyze the learning behaviors in multi-task and single-task settings. The paper presents theoretical results showing that multi-task learning achieves better generalization performance than single-task learning, as evidenced by experiments on synthetic and real-world data. However, the assumptions made, such as the network being over-parameterized and the data being generated from a specific model, are considered strong and might limit the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the theoretical results, which are supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper introduces a novel data model that incorporates both task-shared and task-specific features, enhancing the understanding of multi-task learning.
- The experiments conducted on both synthetic and real-world data demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed data model in explaining the performance of multi-task learning.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and understandable, which is crucial for advancing the field.
- The theoretical results are solid, contributing to a better understanding of the mechanisms behind the generalization performance of multi-task representation learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the network is over-parameterized, which might not be true for all practical scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
- The data is assumed to be generated from a specific model, which might not reflect real-world scenarios where data is generated by different processes.
- The proof techniques are similar to those used in previous works, which might not be sufficiently novel.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the implications of the results for practical applications, which could limit the impact of the findings.
- The assumptions made, such as the data model and the learning regime, are strong and might not hold in more general or realistic settings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed data model and the assumptions made, which could affect the validity and applicability of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed data model compare to other existing data models in terms of explaining the performance of multi-task learning?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the proof techniques used in the paper, especially how they differ from those in previous works?
- How do the results generalize to more general or realistic settings, and what are the implications for practical applications?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed data model and the assumptions made in more detail, and suggest directions for future research to address these limitations?
- How do the results depend on the specific assumptions made in the paper, and what are the implications if these assumptions do not hold in more general settings?
