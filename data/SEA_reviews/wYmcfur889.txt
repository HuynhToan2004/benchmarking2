 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Data Prediction Denoising Model (DPDM), a novel approach to enhancing the performance of diffusion models by improving the denoising capabilities of the diffusion model's denoiser. The DPDM model aims to address the issue of poor performance in few-step diffusion models by optimizing the denoiser's ability to recover the original data distribution from noisy data. The model is trained using a variant of the KL divergence and is evaluated against state-of-the-art models like EDM, showing superior performance in scenarios with limited sampling steps. The paper also discusses the limitations of the DPDM model, such as its reliance on additional memory costs and sub-optimal performance with sufficient sampling steps.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue in the field of diffusion models, focusing on improving the performance of few-step diffusion models.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, clearly presented, and supported by a strong theoretical foundation, making it accessible and understandable.
- The experiments conducted are robust and comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various scenarios.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the training and sampling algorithms, which are crucial for reproducibility and understanding.
- The results show that the proposed method can outperform other few-step diffusion models, demonstrating its practical utility and potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a clearer differentiation between the training and sampling algorithms of DPDM and those of diffusion models, as they are currently described in the same section.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison of the training costs and GPU memory requirements of DPDM versus other few-step diffusion models, which could affect the practical deployment of the model.
- The paper's evaluation could be strengthened by including additional metrics such as precision and recall to provide a more comprehensive assessment of the model's performance.
- The paper's claims about the novelty of the proposed method are somewhat overstated, as similar ideas have been previously explored in other works.
- The paper's reliance on an auxiliary diffusion model for training could introduce additional memory costs, which might limit its practical applicability in resource-constrained environments.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the specific differences between the training and sampling algorithms of DPDM and those of diffusion models, particularly in terms of their impact on the model's performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of training costs and GPU memory requirements to other few-step diffusion models?
- What are the implications of using different sampling algorithms for diffusion models, such as the DPDM sampler versus the default diffusion sampler?
- Could you provide more details on the training process for the auxiliary diffusion model and how it affects the overall training efficiency and resource utilization?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the noise level is not Gaussian, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in such scenarios?
