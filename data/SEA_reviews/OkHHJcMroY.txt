 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PILOT, a novel policy evaluation algorithm for reinforcement learning (RL) that utilizes a variance-reduced primal-dual approach to achieve an O(1/K) convergence rate with constant step sizes. This algorithm addresses the non-convex strongly-concave minimax optimization problem, improving upon existing methods like nPD-VR and VR-STSG. The authors also present an enhanced version, PILOT+, which further reduces sample complexity by adaptively adjusting batch sizes. Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate the efficiency of these methods, showing improvements over state-of-the-art methods. However, concerns are raised about the practical applicability of the constant step-size assumption and the limited empirical validation on more complex environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel policy evaluation algorithm, PILOT, which achieves an O(1/K) convergence rate with constant step sizes, which is a significant advancement over existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and structured, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The PILOT algorithm is a single-timescale method, which is simpler to implement and has fewer hyperparameters compared to some existing methods.
- The authors provide theoretical proofs and empirical results that demonstrate the efficiency of the proposed methods, showing improvements over state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper introduces a new convergence metric, which is used to prove the O(1/K) convergence rate, and provides a detailed comparison with related works.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution might be considered incremental as it builds on existing works like nPD-VR and VR-STSG, which also use variance reduction techniques.
- The assumption of constant step-sizes might not be practical in real-world applications where the learning rate schedule is crucial for stability and performance.
- The empirical validation of the algorithm is limited to simple environments, such as MountainCar-v0 and Cartpole-v0, and does not include more complex environments like Atari games.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the algorithm, which is a significant concern for practical applications.
- The convergence analysis and proofs, while innovative, are not sufficiently novel or surprising, as they follow similar patterns to existing works.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the practical implications of using constant step-sizes in real-world applications?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity with other state-of-the-art methods?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including convergence curves and sample complexities, for more complex environments like Atari games?
- Is there a plan to extend the convergence analysis to include a more general convergence rate, such as O(1/K^(1/2))?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other methods that do not use variance reduction techniques, such as those mentioned in related works?
