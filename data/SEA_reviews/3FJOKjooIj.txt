 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel self-supervised heterogeneous graph learning framework named HERO, which addresses the challenges of homophily and heterogeneity in heterogeneous graphs. HERO employs a self-expressive matrix to capture homophily and a heterogeneous encoder to aggregate information from different types of nodes, aiming to enhance the effectiveness of self-supervised heterogeneous graph learning. The methodology includes the use of consistency loss and specificity loss to preserve distinct information in different latent spaces, and the theoretical analysis demonstrates that the learned homophilous representations exhibit the grouping effect, capturing homophily in the heterogeneous graph. Extensive experiments across various datasets showcase the effectiveness of the proposed method, although concerns about the novelty and computational efficiency remain.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear motivation and a clear presentation of the proposed method, which is easy to follow.
- The proposed method is technically sound, with the use of a self-expressive matrix to capture homophily and a heterogeneous encoder to aggregate information from different types of nodes, which is both intuitive and effective.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, and extensive experiments are conducted to showcase its effectiveness across various datasets, including both heterogeneous and homogeneous graphs.
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of heterogeneous graph learning, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The proposed method is innovative in its approach to capturing homophily and heterogeneity in heterogeneous graphs without pre-defined meta-paths, which is a novel and interesting aspect.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed discussion on the differences and advantages of the proposed method compared to existing self-attention mechanisms, which are crucial for understanding the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The computational cost and efficiency of the proposed method are not thoroughly discussed, which is essential for practical applications, especially in large-scale graph learning scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, including both heterogeneous and homogeneous graph methods, to better establish the superiority of the proposed method.
- The use of homophily and heterogeneity as the main focus of the paper is somewhat limiting, as it might not fully capture the complexity and diversity of real-world heterogeneous graphs.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed visualizations and explanations of the learned representations and the relationships between different types of nodes in heterogeneous graphs.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons and discussions on the differences and advantages of the proposed method compared to existing self-attention mechanisms?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational cost and efficiency, and what are the potential limitations in practical applications?
- Could the authors elaborate on the design choices of the consistency loss and specificity loss, and how these losses contribute to the overall effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of nodes in heterogeneous graphs, and what are the implications of these differences on the learned representations?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, such as the training and testing time, and the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in practical applications?
