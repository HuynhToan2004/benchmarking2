 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel task, Mobile Object Rearrangement (MOR), where an agent must manipulate objects in a simulated environment using only first-person view RGB images as input. The agent needs to estimate its own and object poses with associated uncertainties, which are then used to train a policy for rearrangement tasks. The paper also develops a simulation environment for evaluating the method and compares it against several baselines, showing improvements in performance. The methodology involves a hierarchical policy that decomposes the task into pick and drop subtasks, and the paper includes an ablation study to assess the impact of uncertainty estimation. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper, the limited scope of the experiments, and the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise sections that effectively communicate the research.
- The introduction of a new task, Mobile Object Rearrangement (MOR), is innovative and offers a novel approach to robotics tasks.
- The development of a new simulation environment is a significant contribution, providing a valuable tool for evaluating and advancing research in the field.
- The paper is well-motivated, addressing the importance of localization in robotics tasks and providing a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The use of uncertainty estimation in robotics tasks is highlighted as a crucial aspect, which is effectively demonstrated through the results presented.
- The paper is structured well, with a clear problem statement, methodology, and evaluation, making it accessible and understandable to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in several sections, particularly in the explanation of the rearrangement task and the evaluation metrics used.
- The experimental setup is limited, with only three objects used in the experiments, which may not sufficiently test the robustness of the proposed method.
- The generalizability of the method to more complex environments or different types of objects is not adequately addressed, raising concerns about the scalability of the approach.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential improvements.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the L-Net, which is a critical component of the proposed method.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art approaches in the field, which could help in validating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of noise in the environment, which could significantly impact the performance of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancy in the evaluation metrics between the paper and the supplementary material?
- How does the proposed method handle noise in the environment, and what measures have been taken to mitigate its effects?
- In the evaluation of the method, why were only three objects used, and how does this affect the generalizability of the results?
- Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the L-Net, including the specifics of the RNN model and the training process?
- How does the proposed method compare with state-of-the-art approaches in the field, and what are the key differences in terms of performance and methodology?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential areas for future research to address these limitations?
