 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Constrained Variational Generation (CVG) framework aimed at enhancing the generalizability of graph learning by generating new graphs based on original ones. The CVG framework leverages a Variational Graph Auto-Encoder (VGAE) structure and optimizes under the Graph Information Bottleneck (GIB) principle, aiming to address challenges such as intra-graph correlations and inter-graph distinctions. The authors conduct extensive experiments on real-world datasets, demonstrating the superiority of their method over existing baselines. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the paper's presentation, the theoretical justification of certain claims, and the generalizability of the results due to the specificity of the datasets used.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, Constrained Variational Generation (CVG), which generates generalizable graphs for classification, addressing the challenges of intra-graph correlations and inter-graph distinctions.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the GIB principle and its application in the CVG framework, which enhances the understanding and application of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its application, and includes a detailed discussion of the experimental results.
- The experimental results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing baselines, highlighting its effectiveness in generating generalizable graphs.
- The paper addresses the important problem of out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization in graph learning, which is crucial for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the theoretical justifications and the practical implementation of the CVG framework.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing works that also address OOD generalization in graph learning, which could undermine the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect the credibility and applicability of the results.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to specific datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method across different graph structures and distributions.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which could be critical for practical applications.
- There are some typographical errors and inconsistencies in the notation and terminology used throughout the paper, which could confuse readers and detract from the professional quality of the manuscript.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the theoretical justification for the GIB principle and its application in the CVG framework, particularly in relation to the optimization of the generator?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing works that also address OOD generalization in graph learning, and what are the specific advantages of the CVG framework?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of its applicability to larger and more complex graph datasets?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between generating generalizable graphs and preserving intra-graph correlations?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential avenues for future research to address these limitations?
