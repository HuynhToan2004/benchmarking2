 **Summary:**
The paper explores the problem of computing similarity between a query point and a private dataset, focusing on the efficient computation of kernel density estimation (KDE) and distance queries. It introduces novel techniques for dimensionality reduction and function approximation to enhance the efficiency and utility of the similarity computations. The theoretical framework includes new results on dimensionality reduction and the use of Johnson-Lindenstrauss (JL) maps to preserve kernel density values. The paper also provides empirical evidence of the effectiveness of these methods through experiments on real and synthetic datasets. The contributions include improved query times and utility-privacy trade-offs, which are supported by both theoretical analysis and practical demonstrations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to the problem of computing similarities between a query point and a private dataset, focusing on kernel density estimation (KDE) and distance queries, which are crucial in many applications.
- The authors have provided both theoretical and empirical evidence to support their claims, which enhances the credibility of their results.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it accessible to readers and aiding in understanding the complex mathematical concepts.
- The authors have made significant improvements in the theoretical bounds for KDE queries, which are supported by empirical evidence showing that their methods outperform existing ones.
- The paper presents a unifying framework for studying the problem of computing similarities to private datasets, which is of broad interest to the machine learning community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in the context of differential privacy (DP) and the specific algorithms used in the experiments.
- The empirical evaluation is limited to a few datasets and does not provide a detailed comparison of the proposed methods with other state-of-the-art algorithms.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of its methods, which could be critical for understanding the practical applicability and effectiveness of the proposed approaches.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational efficiency of the proposed algorithms, which is crucial for understanding their practical utility.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the theoretical results and their implications, particularly in the context of differential privacy and the utility-privacy trade-offs.
