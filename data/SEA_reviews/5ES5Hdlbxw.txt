 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SQIRL, a novel reinforcement learning (RL) algorithm that aims to explain the success of deep RL in stochastic environments. SQIRL builds on the GORP algorithm by Laidlaw et al. (2023) and extends it to stochastic MDPs. The algorithm iteratively learns a policy by exploring randomly to collect rollouts and then performing a limited number of steps of fitted-Q iteration over those rollouts. This approach allows for the separation of exploration and learning, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of deep RL in complex environments. The paper also provides theoretical and empirical evidence to support the algorithm's performance and its ability to solve stochastic MDPs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The proposed SQIRL algorithm is novel and innovative, extending the GORP algorithm to stochastic environments, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the algorithm's performance, which is supported by empirical results showing that SQIRL can solve a significant number of stochastic environments, demonstrating its practical utility.
- The paper effectively bridges the gap between theory and practice in deep RL, explaining why random exploration and complex function approximators can be effective in stochastic environments.
- The use of the stochastic effective horizon as a measure of algorithmic performance is a novel approach that could be useful in future research on RL algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing RL algorithms, particularly in terms of performance metrics and theoretical analysis.
- The empirical results, while promising, are not sufficiently robust to fully validate the claims made by the paper. The experiments are limited to a small set of environments, and the results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of SQIRL over other RL algorithms.
- There are concerns about the practicality of the SQIRL algorithm due to its reliance on a regression oracle that is not well-defined, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the SQIRL algorithm, such as its dependence on the choice of the parameter k and the potential for overfitting due to the use of a single neural network for Q-function approximation across all timesteps.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made, particularly the assumptions on the regression oracle, which are crucial for the validity of the theoretical results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and functionality of the regression oracle in the SQIRL algorithm, particularly how it is used to estimate the Q-function?
- How does the SQIRL algorithm handle the choice of the parameter k, and what are the implications of this choice on the algorithm's performance?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the performance of SQIRL in more diverse environments and a comparison with other RL algorithms using standard benchmarks?
- How does the SQIRL algorithm perform in environments where the effective horizon is high, and what are the implications of this for the algorithm's practical applicability?
- Could the authors discuss the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the assumptions on the regression oracle, and how these assumptions affect the validity of the theoretical results?
