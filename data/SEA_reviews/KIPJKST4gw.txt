 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of code data on the reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) during different training stages: pre-training and instruction-tuning. The study utilizes the CodeParrot dataset for pre-training and CodeAlpaca for instruction-tuning, comparing models trained on these datasets against those trained solely on textual data. The findings suggest that pre-training with code data significantly enhances general reasoning capabilities, while instruction-tuning with code data improves task-specific reasoning. The paper also explores the dynamic mixing strategy of code and text data to facilitate gradual learning of reasoning capabilities. Despite the comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and tasks, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty and the lack of significant insights beyond the obvious benefits of pre-training with code data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant topic, exploring the impact of code data on the reasoning capabilities of LLMs during different training stages.
- Comprehensive evaluation is conducted across multiple datasets and tasks, providing a robust validation of the findings.
- The authors provide a detailed description of the experimental setup, including the datasets used and the evaluation metrics, which aids in reproducibility and transparency.
- The paper presents an interesting and well-executed study that demonstrates the benefits of pre-training with code data, which is a novel approach that could inspire further research in this area.
- The findings are significant and relevant, contributing to a better understanding of the role of code data in enhancing LLM reasoning capabilities.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty and significant insights, as the benefits of pre-training with code data are already well-known.
- The experimental results are not surprising, as the superiority of pre-training with code data over text-only models is expected.
- The dynamic mixing strategy of code and text data is not thoroughly explored, with limited discussion on its effectiveness and implementation.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the performance of different models trained with varying proportions of code data, which could provide a more nuanced understanding of the impact of code data on model performance.
- The generalizability of the findings is limited due to the focus on specific models and datasets, which may not be directly applicable to other LLMs or datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the experimental results, particularly in terms of how different proportions of code data affect model performance across different tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the meaning of "NN" and "NC" in Table 3, and explain the differences in performance between these models?
- How does the dynamic mixing strategy of code and text data impact the learning process of LLMs, and what are the specific advantages of this approach over uniform sampling?
- In the context of the findings, how do the authors justify the decision to use a stepwise decrease in code data ratio during instruction tuning, especially given the observed performance decline in some tasks?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the experimental results, particularly how different proportions of code data affect model performance across different tasks?
- How do the authors plan to address the concerns regarding the generalizability of the findings, and what steps are being taken to ensure the applicability of the findings to other LLMs and datasets?
