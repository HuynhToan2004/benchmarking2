 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to semantic segmentation using noisy coarse annotations, which are typically less expensive and time-consuming compared to pixel-level annotations. The method involves the use of two coupled CNNs: one for generating segmentation probabilities and another for estimating confusion matrices based on coarse annotations. This approach leverages both positive and negative coarse annotations to learn true segmentation label distributions, thereby improving segmentation accuracy even with limited data. The paper presents experimental results on various datasets, including MNIST, Cityscapes, and medical imaging datasets, demonstrating the method's effectiveness. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the method, the clarity of the presentation, and the comparative analysis with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem in the field of weakly-supervised learning, focusing on the use of noisy coarse annotations for semantic segmentation, which is a cost-effective and practical approach.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components, including the use of confusion matrices and the handling of positive and negative coarse annotations.
- The paper includes comprehensive experimental results on various datasets, including MNIST, Cityscapes, and medical imaging datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of confusion matrices to model coarse annotations is a novel approach, providing a theoretical framework for handling noisy data in segmentation tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the proposed method and its results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of coarse annotations and its sensitivity to the quality of annotations.
- There is a noticeable absence of comparisons with existing methods, which could provide a better understanding of the method's performance relative to current state-of-the-art approaches.
- The paper does not adequately address the novelty of the method, as similar approaches using confusion matrices have been previously explored in other contexts.
- The clarity of the paper could be improved, particularly in the presentation of equations and figures, which are sometimes unclear or inadequately explained.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational complexity of the method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in terms of memory and time requirements?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of coarse annotations, such as scribbles or bounding boxes?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the complementary label learning in the performance of the method?
- In the absence of pixel-level annotations, how does the method ensure the accuracy of the segmentation results, especially in medical applications where precision is critical?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a discussion on the advantages and disadvantages of their approach compared to these methods?
- How does the method handle different types of label noise, and what are the implications of using noisy coarse annotations for semantic segmentation?
