 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel technique called "token alignment" aimed at addressing the tokenization artifacts in generative models, particularly in scenarios involving partial tokens. This method involves backtracking to the last complete tokens and aligning the model's generation with the prompt, which has been demonstrated to improve performance across various partial token scenarios, including space-prefix and partial indentation. The approach is evaluated on both code and natural language tasks, showing improvements over traditional decoding methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach and its applicability across different tokenization methods and model architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed token alignment technique is simple and effective, particularly in scenarios where the prompt ends with a partial token.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the token alignment technique across various partial token scenarios, including subword, punctuation, space prefix, and contiguous spaces.
- The technique has been shown to improve performance in code and natural language tasks, demonstrating its practical utility.
- The method's simplicity and the use of a character-trie for fast prefix matching contribute to its efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as similar techniques have been previously used in other works, such as "A Flexible Framework for Large-Scale Language Modeling."
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational cost and efficiency.
- The method's applicability is limited to specific tokenization methods, such as those that group multiple spaces into a single token.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive evaluation on large-scale datasets or across different model architectures, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the token alignment method, such as its ineffectiveness in scenarios with complex prompts.
- The method's effectiveness in scenarios where the prompt is a single token is not discussed, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the token alignment technique compare in terms of computational cost and efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods?
- Can the token alignment technique be adapted to work with tokenization methods that do not group multiple spaces into a single token?
- How does the token alignment technique perform when the prompt is a single token?
- Could you provide more details on the computational overhead and efficiency of the token alignment technique, particularly in comparison to other methods?
- In scenarios where the prompt is complex, how effective is the token alignment technique?
- Is there a possibility to extend the token alignment technique to handle more complex prompts, such as those involving multiple tokens or phrases?
- How does the token alignment technique affect the performance of different model architectures, such as transformer-based models?
