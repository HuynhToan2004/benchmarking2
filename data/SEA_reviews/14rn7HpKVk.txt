 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SALMONN, a multimodal language model (LLM) that integrates speech and audio encoders to process and understand general audio inputs, including speech, audio events, and music. The model is designed to handle a variety of tasks, including speech recognition, translation, and music captioning, and is evaluated on a range of benchmarks. The authors propose an activation tuning stage to enhance the model's ability to perform unseen tasks, which is crucial for the model's adaptability. The paper is praised for its innovative approach, thorough evaluation, and potential impact on the field of multimodal AI. However, concerns are raised about the complexity of the model, the limited novelty of the tasks, and the need for a more detailed comparison with existing models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces SALMONN, a multimodal language model (LLM) that integrates speech and audio encoders, which is a novel approach in the field of multimodal AI.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with a comprehensive evaluation across a variety of tasks, including both trained and unseen tasks.
- The use of a Q-Former module for audio processing is highlighted as a significant contribution, which enhances the model's ability to handle variable-length audio inputs.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the model's performance, including an analysis of task over-fitting and the effectiveness of the activation tuning stage.
- The model's capability to perform unseen tasks without explicit training is a notable strength, demonstrating its adaptability and potential for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing models such as AudioGPT, which could have provided insights into the advantages and limitations of SALMONN.
- The complexity of the model, particularly the dual encoder structure and the Q-Former module, might limit its practical applicability and increase computational costs.
- The novelty of the tasks evaluated is questioned, as some tasks, such as audio-based storytelling and speech audio co-reasoning, are not entirely novel.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the model, particularly concerning the scalability and generalization of the model to other domains or modalities.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the model's architecture, including the specific roles and contributions of the Whisper and BEATS encoders.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the specific role and contribution of the Whisper and BEATS encoders in the model's performance?
- How does the model perform on tasks that require the understanding of both speech and non-speech audio, such as speech-based question answering or audio event detection?
- Could you provide more details on the training data used for each task, particularly for the unseen tasks where data was generated using a text-based LLM?
- How does the model handle different types of audio inputs, such as music versus speech, and what are the implications for the model's performance and generalization?
- Could you elaborate on the decision to use a Q-Former module for audio processing, and how this approach compares to other potential methods for handling variable-length audio inputs?
