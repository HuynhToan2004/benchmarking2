 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Federated Binary Matrix Factorization (Felb) algorithm, designed for distributed binary data, which is a critical challenge in privacy-preserving settings. The Felb algorithm extends the existing binary matrix factorization method by integrating a proximal operator to ensure binary outcomes and differential privacy. The authors demonstrate the convergence of the Felb algorithm and its effectiveness through extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The paper also introduces an adaptive variant of the Felb algorithm, Falb, which shows improved regularization over state-of-the-art methods. Despite its novelty and theoretical contributions, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in certain sections and the need for more rigorous proofs and empirical validations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and novel problem of federated binary matrix factorization, which is a critical challenge in privacy-preserving settings.
- The authors introduce a new algorithm, Felb, which demonstrates convergence and differential privacy guarantees, and an adaptive variant, Falb, that shows improved regularization over state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper provides extensive empirical evaluations on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the problem and the proposed solution.
- The algorithm is designed to be privacy-preserving, which is crucial in scenarios where data privacy is paramount.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and justification of the problem setting, particularly in how the distributed binary data is collected and how it is used.
- The convergence analysis in the paper is not rigorous, with some arguments being informal or unproven, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of data heterogeneity across clients, which is a significant challenge in federated learning.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it seems to be a simple extension of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the explanation of the aggregation process and the privacy mechanisms used.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other related works, such as Federated Non-negative Matrix Factorization (FNMF) and Federated Binary Matrix Factorization (FBMF).

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and justification of the problem setting, particularly how the distributed binary data is collected and used?
- How does the proposed method handle data heterogeneity across clients, and what are the implications of this for the performance of the algorithm?
- In the convergence analysis, can the authors provide a more rigorous proof or clarify the informal arguments used?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods, such as FNMF and FBMF, in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the privacy mechanisms used, particularly how differential privacy is ensured in the aggregation process?
- How does the paper ensure that the results are not affected by the noise added during the differential privacy process?
