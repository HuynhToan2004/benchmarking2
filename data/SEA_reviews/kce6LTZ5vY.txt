 **Summary:**
The paper introduces InstructMining, a novel method aimed at automatically selecting high-quality instruction data for fine-tuning large language models (LLMs). InstructMining utilizes natural language indicators to assess data quality, employing BlendSearch to identify the optimal subset for fine-tuning. The methodology is supported by experiments that demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on benchmarks like LLMA-as-a-judge and Huggingface OpenLLM. However, the paper's approach to evaluating instruction quality and the robustness of the method are questioned, with concerns about the generalizability of the findings and the potential for overfitting to the evaluation set.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the proposed method, InstructMining, is straightforward and easy to implement.
- The observation of the double descent phenomenon in fine-tuning language models is interesting and adds depth to the understanding of data selection for fine-tuning.
- The proposed method, InstructMining, demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on benchmarks like LLMA-as-a-judge and Huggingface OpenLLM, showcasing its effectiveness.
- The method is parameter-free, which simplifies its application and reduces the need for fine-tuning, making it potentially cost-effective.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation of instruction quality relies solely on inference loss, which might not be robust enough to capture all aspects of instruction quality, potentially leading to overfitting to the evaluation set.
- The paper lacks clarity in defining "instruction quality" and the relationship between instruction quality and inference loss is not well-established.
- The method's robustness is questioned, with concerns about its effectiveness in different model sizes and settings, and the potential for overfitting to the evaluation set.
- The selection of indicators for evaluating instruction quality is not sufficiently justified, and the method's generalizability to other datasets and models is not convincingly demonstrated.
- The paper's experimental design and results presentation could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and depth in explaining the experimental setups and results.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when applied to different base models, and what are the implications for the robustness of the findings?
- Could you clarify the relationship between instruction quality and inference loss, and provide more detailed justification for the selection of indicators used in the method?
- How do you ensure that the method's findings are not overfitted to the evaluation set, and what steps have been taken to validate its generalizability to other datasets and models?
- Given the potential for overfitting, how do you address this concern in future applications of the method?
- Can you provide more detailed explanations for the experimental setups and results, particularly in sections where the methodology and results are presented?
