 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CrysToGraph, a novel transformer-based geometric graph network designed for crystalline systems, aiming to capture both short-range and long-range interactions effectively. It employs a transformer-based message-passing mechanism and a graph-wise transformer to model these interactions, achieving state-of-the-art results on the MatBench benchmark datasets. The architecture includes a transformer-based geometric graph network and a novel transformer-based graph convolution layer, which updates node and edge features using a shared attention score. The model's performance is evaluated against existing methods, demonstrating its effectiveness in capturing short-range dependencies with transformer-based graph convolution blocks and long-range dependencies with graph-wise transformer blocks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel transformer-based geometric graph network, CrysToGraph, which effectively captures both short-range and long-range interactions in crystalline systems.
- The introduction of a novel transformer-based graph convolution layer, eTGC, which updates node and edge features using a shared attention score based on the features of the central node, neighboring nodes, and edges.
- The paper proposes a graph-wise transformer, GwT, tailored for graphs, which captures long-range dependencies among nodes on a graph-wide scale.
- CrysToGraph outperforms most existing methods, achieving new state-of-the-art results on the MatBench benchmark datasets.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a detailed description of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods like Matformer, particularly in the use of line graphs and transformers, which may limit its novelty.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, such as those using force field predictions, which could enhance its impact and relevance in the field.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some datasets showing worse performance compared to existing methods, and the reasons for these discrepancies are not adequately explained.
- The paper does not address the limitations of its methodology comprehensively, which could affect the reader's understanding of the model's strengths and weaknesses.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the model architecture and the methodology used, which could hinder the reproducibility and understanding of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between their approach and existing methods like Matformer, particularly in the use of line graphs and transformers?
- How does the proposed method perform on datasets with more complex crystal structures, such as those with larger numbers of atoms?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including those using force field predictions, to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including standard deviations and error bars, to support the claimed state-of-the-art performance?
- How does the proposed method handle the limitation of depth in GNNs, particularly in capturing long-range interactions?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the experimental results and the methodology used to ensure the reproducibility and transparency of their research?
