 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a Bayesian framework for clustered federated learning (CFL), aiming to optimize client-cluster associations and inter-client knowledge sharing under non-IID data. It proposes a Bayesian recursive update algorithm to estimate the posterior distribution of client-cluster associations, which is intractable in general cases. The authors present three approximation methods to address this intractability and demonstrate the effectiveness of their methods through experiments on four non-IID scenarios using four well-known datasets. Despite the theoretical contributions and empirical validations, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the proposed methods, the clarity of the paper's presentation, and the lack of comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a Bayesian approach to clustered federated learning (CFL), which is a novel and significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a clear and concise presentation, making the complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The authors propose a Bayesian recursive update algorithm to estimate the posterior distribution of client-cluster associations, which is a principled and theoretically sound approach.
- The paper presents a unifying framework for clustered federated learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise language that effectively communicates the complex ideas.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the novelty of the proposed algorithms and their performance relative to state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity or time complexity of the proposed algorithms, which is crucial for understanding their practical applicability.
- There are several typographical and grammatical errors throughout the paper, which detract from its professionalism and readability.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of the number of clients and the size of the model.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some methods performing worse than existing methods, and the paper does not adequately explain these discrepancies.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and does not include a detailed discussion on the scalability and computational complexity of the algorithms.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the differences between the proposed algorithms and existing methods, particularly in terms of novelty and performance?
- How does the proposed framework handle the scalability issues, especially in terms of the number of clients and the size of the model?
- Can you provide a detailed comparison of the computational complexity and time complexity of the proposed algorithms with existing methods?
- Why do some methods perform worse than existing methods in certain experiments, and how do the authors explain these discrepancies?
- How does the proposed framework handle the non-IID data distribution in federated learning, and what are the implications of this for the performance of the model?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the number of clients and the size of the model used in the experiments?
- What are the implications of the assumptions made in the paper, such as the independence of clusters and the local datasets, and how do these assumptions affect the performance of the model?
