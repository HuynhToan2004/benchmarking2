 **Summary:**
The paper introduces COSA, a novel vision-language pretraining framework that utilizes concatenated image-text pairs to model temporal correlations in videos. This approach allows for the conversion of image-text corpora into pseudo video-paragraph corpora, enhancing the learning of both image-language and video-language representations. The methodology is validated through extensive experiments across various benchmarks, demonstrating significant improvements in performance compared to traditional single-sample training. Despite the innovative approach, concerns were raised about the novelty of the method and the lack of comprehensive ablation studies, particularly regarding the impact of different training objectives and the necessity of concatenated samples.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by training a foundation model with concatenated samples, which effectively transforms image-text corpora into pseudo video-paragraph corpora, enhancing the learning of both image-language and video-language representations.
- The method is simple yet effective, with extensive experiments demonstrating its superiority over traditional single-sample training and state-of-the-art results across a broad range of semantic vision-language downstream tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive ablation study that investigates the impact of different training objectives, vision encoders, training corpora, and the number of concatenated samples.
- The authors have provided detailed implementation details, including the training objectives, vision encoder, training corpora, and the number of concatenated samples, which are crucial for reproducibility and further research.
- The methodology is validated through extensive experiments across various benchmarks, demonstrating significant improvements in performance compared to traditional single-sample training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it resembles existing methods like CLIP-based models, which also use temporal fusion transformers and frame-word fine-grained alignment learning to enable text-to-video retrieval.
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how the model manages the temporal dependencies between frames in video-text tasks, which is crucial for understanding the model's effectiveness in video-related tasks.
- The methodology's reliance on randomly concatenated samples for training raises concerns about the model's ability to learn temporal dependencies effectively, as it does not ensure that the samples are temporally ordered.
- There is a lack of comprehensive ablation studies, particularly regarding the impact of different training objectives and the necessity of concatenated samples.
- The paper does not include a detailed comparison with other methods that also use concatenated samples, which could help clarify the unique contributions of the proposed method.
- The method's performance on video-related tasks is not consistently superior to existing methods, and the paper does not provide a detailed explanation for this.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their method compared to existing methods like CLIP-based models?
- How does the model manage the temporal dependencies between frames in video-text tasks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training objectives and their impact on the model's performance?
- Why was the decision made to use random concatenated samples for training, and how does this affect the model's ability to learn temporal dependencies effectively?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods that also use concatenated samples, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- The paper mentions that the model can learn temporal dependencies from concatenated samples. Can the authors provide evidence or examples to support this claim?
- Why were certain benchmarks not included in the evaluation, and how do the authors justify the choice of benchmarks used?
