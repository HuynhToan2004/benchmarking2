 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Greedy PIG, an extension of the Path Integrated Gradient (PIG) method for feature attribution and feature selection. This method iteratively selects top features using a greedy algorithm, aiming to address the limitations of PIG in handling feature correlations. The approach is evaluated on various tasks such as image feature attribution, graph compression, and post-hoc feature selection, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper is critiqued for its lack of comprehensive theoretical analysis and empirical comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to feature attribution and feature selection by formulating these tasks as subset selection problems, which is a novel perspective.
- The proposed Greedy PIG method effectively addresses the shortcomings of PIG by iteratively selecting top features, which is a reasonable and intuitive approach.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a good balance between theoretical discussion and empirical evaluation.
- The evaluation covers a variety of tasks and datasets, including image feature attribution, graph compression, and post-hoc feature selection, which demonstrates the versatility of the proposed method.
- The paper introduces a new objective for feature attribution based on the model loss, which is a useful contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed Greedy PIG method, which is a significant drawback given the importance of theoretical underpinnings in the field of explainability.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, which is crucial for establishing the superiority or at least the competitiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for a balanced and comprehensive evaluation.
- The evaluation section could be improved by including more detailed comparisons and ablation studies, especially regarding the choice of hyperparameters and the impact of different settings on the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the theoretical analysis of the Greedy PIG method? Specifically, how does the method ensure that it is not merely a myopic greedy algorithm?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency? Could the authors include a comparative analysis in future work?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these limitations affect the practical applicability of the method?
- How does the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., the number of rounds and the number of selections per round) impact the performance of the proposed method? Could the authors include an ablation study on these hyperparameters?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the dataset and the complexity of the model?
- How does the proposed method handle feature redundancy, and can the authors provide more examples or experimental results to demonstrate this?
