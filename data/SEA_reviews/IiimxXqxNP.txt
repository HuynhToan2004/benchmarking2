 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HyperFQI, a novel approach to deep exploration in reinforcement learning (RL) by integrating hypermodel-based methods with Thompson sampling. HyperFQI leverages a hypermodel to approximate the posterior distribution of the Q-value function, enhancing uncertainty estimation and scalability to complex environments. The algorithm is evaluated against various baselines in benchmarks like DeepSea and Atari, showing improved data and computational efficiency. Theoretical analysis is provided, including a Bayesian regret bound, which supports the method's theoretical foundation. However, concerns about the novelty, clarity in presentation, and the depth of theoretical analysis were raised, suggesting that the paper might not meet the conference's acceptance threshold.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces HyperFQI, a novel algorithm that integrates the benefits of RLSVI and hypermodel-based approaches, providing a new perspective on deep exploration in RL.
- The algorithm is shown to be data efficient, computationally efficient, and scalable, particularly in complex environments like DeepSea and Atari, where it achieves state-of-the-art performance.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear descriptions of the algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The inclusion of a Bayesian regret bound for HyperFQI provides a solid theoretical foundation, enhancing the credibility of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the algorithm across various benchmarks, including a thorough ablation study that supports the design choices.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation from existing works such as HyperDQN, raising questions about the novelty and uniqueness of the contributions.
- There is a significant absence of theoretical analysis on the scalability and efficiency claims, particularly in the scalability of the hypermodel and the computational efficiency of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of key components like the hypermodel and the optimization process, as well as a clearer presentation of the algorithm's components.
- The theoretical analysis, particularly the regret bound, is not sufficiently robust or novel, as it closely resembles existing bounds from RLSVI and other methods.
- The paper's experimental setup and results, such as the use of DeepSea benchmarks and the results on Atari, are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the claims of superiority are not adequately supported.
- The paper's writing and organization need improvement, particularly in the clarity of the algorithm description and the presentation of results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between HyperFQI and HyperDQN, and provide a more detailed explanation of how HyperFQI improves upon or differs from existing methods?
- How does the scalability of the hypermodel to large state spaces impact the computational efficiency and performance of the algorithm?
- Could the authors provide more detailed theoretical analysis on the scalability and efficiency claims, particularly how these aspects compare to existing methods?
- In terms of the experimental results, how do the authors justify the superiority of HyperFQI over existing methods, especially in the DeepSea and Atari benchmarks?
- Can the authors provide more details on the implementation and optimization of the hypermodel, particularly how this impacts the computational efficiency and performance of the algorithm?
- How does the algorithm handle the balance between exploration and exploitation, and what are the implications of this balance on the overall performance of the algorithm?
