 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to class incremental learning by integrating concepts of self-training, out-of-distribution (OOD) recognition, and data drift. The methodology, which is evaluated on datasets such as MNIST and a subset of the MixedWM38 dataset, involves a two-phase learning process where the feature extractor is trained using contrastive learning, followed by classification learning for the classifier. The paper claims that this approach can effectively manage catastrophic forgetting and maintain performance across various classes. However, the experimental results are not convincing due to the lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods and the use of small, non-standard datasets. The paper's presentation and methodological clarity also need improvement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of continual learning, focusing on the integration of self-training, OOD recognition, and data drift.
- The methodology is straightforward and well-explained, with clear illustrations and a logical structure that aids in understanding.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the motivation behind the research is clearly articulated.
- The approach is adaptable to various applications, including anomaly detection and object detection, which broadens its potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in the context of CIL benchmarks, which limits the assessment of its performance relative to state-of-the-art techniques.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with small datasets used and a lack of detailed performance metrics, such as forgetting curves, which are crucial for evaluating the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The methodology appears to be a combination of existing techniques without sufficient novelty or significant advancement over existing methods.
- The paper contains numerous typographical and grammatical errors, which detract from its professional presentation.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, which is a critical concern for real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance metrics such as forgetting curves and accuracy over time?
- How does the proposed method compare to other CIL benchmarks, such as CIFAR-100 or ImageNet?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the "OOD class" in the experimental setup, especially in the context of anomaly detection?
- How does the method handle the OOD samples during training, and what is the rationale behind the two-phase learning process?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the datasets used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
