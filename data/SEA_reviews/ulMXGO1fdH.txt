 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Online Training with Postsynaptic Estimates (OTPE) for training feed-forward Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs). OTPE approximates Real-Time Recurrent Learning (RTRL) by incorporating temporal dynamics not captured by current approximations like Online Training Through Time (OTTT) and Online Spatio-Temporal Learning (OSTL). The method is designed to capture the effects of multi-step temporal sequences through a spiking neuron, enhancing the alignment with exact gradients and reducing computational complexity. The paper evaluates OTPE on various datasets, showing improved performance over existing methods. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in explaining the method's novelty and its theoretical underpinnings, as well as the limited evaluation on a broader range of datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes detailed explanations of the algorithms, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is novel and well-motivated, addressing the limitations of current approximations by incorporating temporal dynamics not captured by previous methods.
- The method demonstrates significant improvements in accuracy and gradient cosine similarity compared to existing methods, showing a promising approach to online training of SNNs.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the method and its implications, including the use of OTPE to approximate RTRL and its advantages over other methods in terms of computational complexity and alignment with exact gradients.
- The experiments are well-designed and include detailed comparisons with other methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across different datasets and tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other methods, particularly those that also approximate RTRL, such as RTRL-SG, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing methods like OTTT and OSTL, without sufficient differentiation or theoretical justification for its superiority.
- The paper's experimental evaluation is limited to a few datasets, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the method across different types of data and tasks.
- There are concerns about the clarity and completeness of the method's description, particularly in sections where the method's operation and its theoretical underpinnings are not clearly explained.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are not well-articulated, making it difficult to understand the method's theoretical foundations and its implications for the field.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their method and how it differs from other methods that approximate RTRL?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of computational complexity and memory requirements?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other methods, particularly those that also approximate RTRL, to better establish the method's position in the field?
- How does the method perform on different types of data, such as images or natural language, and what are the implications for its applicability in these domains?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of the method, including its assumptions and limitations, to better understand its strengths and weaknesses?
