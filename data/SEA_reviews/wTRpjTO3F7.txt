 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel evaluation method for zero-shot coordination (ZSC) in cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), focusing on the creation of "diversity-complete" evaluation partners. This approach uses a metric called Best Response Proximity (BR-Prox) to measure the performance similarity between the ego agent and the approximate best responses to the evaluation partners. The method involves generating diverse behavior-preferring agents and their best responses, selecting evaluation partners based on best response diversity, and measuring ZSC capability using the BR-Prox metric. The approach is tested in the Overcooked environment, revealing that current ZSC methods fail to distinguish performance differences in most common layouts. The paper also discusses the limitations of existing evaluation methods and proposes a new evaluation workflow for ZSC methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem in the field of zero-shot coordination (ZSC) in cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), focusing on the evaluation of ZSC methods.
- The authors introduce a novel evaluation method that includes the creation of "diversity-complete" evaluation partners and a metric called Best Response Proximity (BR-Prox) to measure the ZSC capability, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear problem statements and a clear workflow for evaluating ZSC methods.
- The proposed evaluation method is comprehensive, including the generation of behavior-preferring agents and their best responses, selection of evaluation partners based on best response diversity, and measurement of ZSC capability using the BR-Prox metric.
- The paper includes a benchmark of ZSC methods with a reliable and comprehensive evaluation, which could be useful for future research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on how the diversity of evaluation partners affects the ZSC capability evaluation, and there is a need for more detailed experiments to support the claims made.
- The definition of "diversity-complete" is not well-explained, and the paper does not sufficiently address how the proposed evaluation method addresses the issue of similarity between the training and evaluation populations.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed evaluation method, such as its applicability to different MARL environments and the computational costs associated with generating diverse behavior-preferring agents and their best responses.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the differences between the proposed evaluation method and existing methods, as well as a clearer explanation of how the proposed method improves upon existing methods.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to the Overcooked environment, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method across different MARL environments.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the diversity of evaluation partners affects the ZSC capability evaluation, and conduct more experiments to support the claims made?
- How does the proposed evaluation method address the issue of similarity between the training and evaluation populations?
- Could the authors clarify the definition of "diversity-complete" and provide more examples or detailed explanations to help understand this concept?
- What are the specific computational costs associated with generating diverse behavior-preferring agents and their best responses, and how do these costs compare to the benefits of using the proposed evaluation method?
- How does the proposed evaluation method compare to existing methods in terms of effectiveness and efficiency, and what are the specific advantages of using the proposed method over existing methods?
- Could the authors discuss the generalizability of the proposed method across different MARL environments, and provide examples or experiments to demonstrate its applicability in other environments?
