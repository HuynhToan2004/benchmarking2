 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to address the training pathologies in Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) by using the condition number as a metric to diagnose and rectify these issues. The authors propose a preconditioning algorithm to enhance the condition number, which in turn improves the error control and convergence rate of PINNs. Theoretical analysis and numerical experiments on various PDE problems demonstrate the effectiveness of this method. The paper also discusses the practical implications of this approach, including its potential to reduce computational time and improve the accuracy of PINN predictions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow sections, making the content accessible to readers.
- The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, and the proofs are well-supported with detailed mathematical derivations.
- The numerical experiments are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed preconditioning method.
- The paper introduces a novel approach using condition number as a metric to diagnose and rectify training pathologies in PINNs, which is both innovative and relevant to the field.
- The paper provides a theoretical framework for the use of condition number in PINNs, which is a significant contribution to the understanding and application of this metric in machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to complex PDE problems and its dependency on the quality of the preconditioner.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the preconditioner, which could be a significant drawback for large-scale applications.
- There is a lack of comparison with other existing methods for improving PINN training, such as those based on Bayesian inference or other regularization techniques.
- The paper's focus on linear PDEs might limit its applicability to more complex and nonlinear problems, which could be a significant limitation for practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implementation of the preconditioner, including its computational efficiency and the impact on the overall training time.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the preconditioner and how it scales with the size of the PDE problems?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of convergence rate when compared to other methods, particularly those that do not use preconditioning?
- Can the authors provide examples or scenarios where the proposed method does not work effectively, and what are the potential reasons for this?
- Is there a possibility to extend the proposed method to nonlinear PDEs, and if so, what are the challenges and potential solutions?
- How does the preconditioning algorithm affect the computational efficiency of the PINN training process, and what are the implications for practical applications?
