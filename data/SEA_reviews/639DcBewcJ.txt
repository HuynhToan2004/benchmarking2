 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Low-Rank Robust Graph Contrastive Learning (LR-RGCL), designed to enhance the robustness of node representations in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) against noise in real-world graph data. The approach comprises two stages: first, a robust Graph Contrastive Learning (GCL) encoder is trained using Bayesian nonparametric Prototype Learning (BPL) to identify confident nodes. Next, a low-rank transductive classification algorithm is employed to classify unlabeled nodes based on these robust features. The paper presents theoretical results on the generalization of the algorithm and validates its effectiveness through extensive experiments on public benchmarks. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques, and the lack of comparative analysis with state-of-the-art methods in noisy graph learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow content, and the methodology is well-explained.
- The proposed method, LR-RGCL, is novel and innovative, combining robust GCL encoder training with a low-rank transductive classification algorithm to enhance the robustness of node representations in GNNs.
- The theoretical results provided are strong, particularly the generalization bound for the test loss on unlabeled data, which is among the first few works to demonstrate the advantage of learning with low-rank features for transductive classification with noise.
- The paper includes extensive experiments on public benchmarks, demonstrating the superior performance of LR-RGCL over competing GNN methods for node classification on noisy graph data.
- The method is effective in handling noisy graph data, which is a significant issue in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on related works, particularly in the area of robust GNNs, which could provide a better context for the proposed method.
- The motivation for using a low-rank transductive classification algorithm is not clearly articulated, and the paper does not adequately explain why this approach is superior to other methods.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods in noisy graph learning, which limits the evaluation of its effectiveness.
- The methodology appears to be a combination of existing techniques, with the novelty primarily in the integration of these techniques rather than the development of new methods.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method and the experimental setup, which could affect the reproducibility and scalability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential directions for future research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the motivation behind using a low-rank transductive classification algorithm and discuss how it improves upon existing methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to state-of-the-art methods in noisy graph learning, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method and the experimental setup, including the number of labeled nodes, the number of unlabeled nodes, and the number of iterations in the experiments?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential directions for future research?
- How does the method handle different types and levels of noise in real-world graph data, and what are the implications of these variations on the performance of the model?
