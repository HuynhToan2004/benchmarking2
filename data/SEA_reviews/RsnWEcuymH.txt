 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Bayesian Optimization Influence Maximization (BOIM), a novel framework for influence maximization (IM) that leverages Bayesian optimization to minimize the number of simulations required. BOIM uses a Gaussian Process as a surrogate function to model the complex interplay between seed sets and influence spreads, introducing a specialized kernel for graph-level Bayesian optimization and stratified sampling for uniform instance distribution. The framework is designed to be computationally efficient and accurate, offering an alternative to traditional simulation-based approaches. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of BOIM, showing improvements over standard simulation methods. However, concerns about the novelty of the approach and the clarity of some theoretical proofs were raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for influence maximization (IM) using Bayesian optimization, which effectively reduces the number of simulations required.
- The methodology is well-supported by theoretical proofs and empirical evidence, demonstrating the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear and concise presentation of the proposed methodology.
- The authors have developed a new kernel for Bayesian optimization, which is significant and could be of independent interest.
- The experiments conducted are extensive and demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed approach, showing improvements over standard simulation methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it mainly combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing Bayesian optimization methods, which could have strengthened the argument for the superiority of the proposed approach.
- The computational complexity of the proposed method is not adequately discussed, particularly the time complexity of each component.
- The theoretical analysis and proofs, such as Theorem 4.1, are not convincingly robust and need further clarification.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of graphs and its scalability.
- The experimental setup and the choice of baselines are not well-explained, which could affect the interpretation of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly the time complexity of each component?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing Bayesian optimization methods, and what are the specific advantages of using BOIM over these methods?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the choice of baselines and the parameters used in the experiments?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of graphs, and what are the implications for its scalability and applicability?
- Could the authors elaborate on the proof of Theorem 4.1 and provide more detailed explanations or additional proofs to support the theoretical claims?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets, and what are the implications for its practical application in real-world scenarios?
