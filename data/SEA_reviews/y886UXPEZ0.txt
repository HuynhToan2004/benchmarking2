 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for pre-training large language models (LLMs) on domain-specific corpora, transforming raw texts into reading comprehension tasks to enhance domain knowledge and prompting ability. This approach is contrasted with traditional domain-adaptive pre-training, which can hinder prompting performance. The method is evaluated across three domains: biomedicine, finance, and law, demonstrating improvements in domain-specific tasks and general LLM benchmarks. However, concerns are raised about the method's novelty, the clarity of its advantages over existing methods, and the generalizability of the results, particularly in light of the limited number of domains tested.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for pre-training large language models (LLMs) on domain-specific corpora, which is distinct from traditional domain-adaptive pre-training.
- The proposed method is shown to effectively learn domain knowledge while preserving prompting performance, as demonstrated through experiments in various domains including biomedicine, finance, and law.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The methodology of converting raw corpora into reading comprehension texts is innovative and practical, offering a scalable solution for adapting LLMs to domain-specific knowledge.
- The experiments conducted show that the method consistently enhances performance across various tasks in different domains, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its method from existing approaches such as the self-supervised mining strategy, which could lead to confusion about the novelty and uniqueness of the proposed method.
- The method's effectiveness is only demonstrated across three domains, raising concerns about its generalizability to other domains.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other domain-adaptive pre-training methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of its advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper lacks detailed experimental settings and hyper-parameter tuning information, which could affect the reproducibility and reliability of the results.
- The method's impact on general language models and its ability to enhance prompting ability is not convincingly demonstrated, especially in light of the observed drop in performance in some domains.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method differ from existing approaches such as the self-supervised mining strategy, and what specific advantages does it offer?
- Can the method be adapted to other domains beyond the three tested, and what are the expected outcomes?
- How does the method perform when compared to other domain-adaptive pre-training methods, and what are the key factors contributing to its effectiveness?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental settings and hyper-parameter tuning to enhance the reproducibility and reliability of the results?
- Why does the method exhibit a drop in performance in some domains, and how does this align with the overall goal of enhancing prompting ability?
