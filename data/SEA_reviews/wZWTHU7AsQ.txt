 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GRAD, a novel game-theoretic approach to robust reinforcement learning (RL) that addresses temporally-coupled perturbations, a challenge not typically considered by existing methods. GRAD formulates the robust RL problem as a two-player zero-sum game, optimizing for general robustness against these perturbations. The approach is evaluated against various attack domains, showing superior robustness to temporally-coupled perturbations compared to existing methods. The paper is well-organized, clearly presenting the problem, methodology, and results, with a focus on the novelty of modeling temporally-coupled perturbations and the use of game-theoretic tools to address them.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clearly presenting the problem, methodology, and results.
- The introduction of a novel class of temporally-coupled adversarial attacks is a significant contribution, challenging existing robust RL methods.
- The use of game-theoretic tools to address temporally-coupled perturbations is innovative and effective in enhancing robustness against various types of adversaries.
- The paper provides empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the GRAD approach in defending against both temporally-coupled and non-temporally coupled adversaries.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications and potential limitations of the temporally-coupled perturbations, especially in real-world applications.
- The novelty of the proposed GRAD approach is somewhat limited, as it appears to be a straightforward adaptation of existing game-theoretic approaches to the context of robust RL.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the computational costs and scalability of the GRAD approach, particularly in large-scale or complex environments.
- The empirical results are not sufficiently robust, with some experiments showing only marginal improvements or even lower performance compared to baseline methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work in the field of robust RL, especially in terms of how the proposed approach compares or differs from existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the practical implications and potential limitations of the temporally-coupled perturbations in real-world applications?
- How does the GRAD approach compare to other game-theoretic RL approaches in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially in large-scale or complex environments?
- Could the authors elaborate on the specific challenges and solutions related to modeling temporally-coupled perturbations in the context of robust RL?
- In the empirical results section, why does the performance of GRAD appear to be lower than the baseline in some scenarios, and what are the implications of these results?
- How does the GRAD approach address the issue of overly conservative policies that can result from modeling adversarial perturbations as temporally-coupled?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending the GRAD approach to other types of adversarial attacks, such as those involving model uncertainty or different types of perturbations?
