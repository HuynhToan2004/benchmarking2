 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FLAIM, a novel method for generating synthetic data in a federated learning (FL) setting, leveraging the AIM algorithm. FLAIM addresses the challenges of data heterogeneity and client participation rates by distributing the AIM algorithm across clients, allowing them to perform local updates and aggregating results. The method is designed to reduce communication overhead and improve data utility, though it is noted that the utility might not match that of a centralized AIM. The paper also discusses the impact of client participation rates, privacy budgets, and the number of clients on the performance of the algorithm. However, the paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods and does not provide a comprehensive privacy analysis, which are critical for understanding the algorithm's effectiveness and limitations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of federated learning (FL), focusing on the generation of synthetic data that can be used across multiple clients without revealing sensitive information.
- The proposed method, FLAIM, is well-motivated and the paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed algorithm, including its privacy guarantees and experimental results.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a detailed algorithm description and privacy analysis.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods like DistAIM and AIM.
- The paper is well-positioned within the literature, providing a good overview of related work and demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of communication overhead and utility of the generated data.
- There is an absence of privacy analysis, which is crucial for understanding the security implications of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of the number of clients and the size of the data being processed.
- The paper does not discuss the potential limitations or societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant in a federated learning context.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed or explained, making it difficult to fully understand the implications of the results.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the proposed method addresses the challenges of data heterogeneity and client participation rates, particularly in terms of privacy and utility.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the proposed method addresses the challenges of data heterogeneity and client participation rates, especially in terms of privacy and utility?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of communication overhead and utility with existing methods like DistAIM and AIM?
- Can you provide a detailed privacy analysis of the proposed method, including the privacy risks and mitigation strategies?
- How does the proposed method scale with an increasing number of clients and data size?
- Can you explain the experimental setup and results in more detail, particularly the choice of datasets and the implications of the results?
- How does the proposed method ensure the privacy of the data being transmitted between clients and the server, especially in a federated learning context?
- Could you discuss the potential limitations or societal impacts of the proposed method, and how these might be addressed or mitigated?
