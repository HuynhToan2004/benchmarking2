 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for malware detection and classification using a Graph Centroid Model (GCM) that leverages Control Flow Graphs (CFGs) and a Graph Neural Network (GNN). The GCM assigns embeddings to partitions, facilitating the identification of both known and new malware classes. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on a malware dataset, showing improvements in classification accuracy and the ability to identify new classes based on their distance from learned representatives in the embedding space. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, unclear methodological details, and insufficient experimental validation against state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and understandable structure, making it accessible to readers.
- The introduction of the Graph Centroid Model (GCM) is innovative, utilizing Control Flow Graphs (CFGs) with a Graph Neural Network (GNN) to identify new classes, enhancing the method's capability to detect out-of-distribution (OOD) data categories.
- The methodology is supported by a novel dataset containing control flow graphs derived from the BODMAS dataset, which is beneficial for the community.
- The paper provides valuable insights into the method's capabilities, particularly in the detection of new classes and the handling of out-of-distribution data, which is crucial in malware detection.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive literature review, missing significant recent works in the field, which limits the contextual understanding of the research.
- The methodological details are unclear, particularly the specifics of how the model handles new class detection and the selection of centroids.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with limited comparisons against state-of-the-art methods, and the dataset used is not diverse enough to validate the method's effectiveness across various malware types.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as the use of centroid-based learning for OOD detection is not a new concept, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its method from existing approaches.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of results, with some figures and tables not effectively conveying the intended information, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the method handles the selection of centroids and the process of new class detection?
- How does the model ensure the robustness of its findings, especially in scenarios where the dataset might be biased or incomplete?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods to establish the superiority or competitiveness of their approach?
- How does the model perform when faced with adversarial perturbations, and what measures are in place to ensure its resilience against such attacks?
- Can the authors elaborate on the method's effectiveness in handling different types of malware, especially those not represented in the current dataset?
- Would it be possible to include more detailed visualizations or explanations to enhance the understanding of the model's capabilities and limitations?
