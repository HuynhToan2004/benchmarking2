 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ThinShellLab, a differentiable simulation platform designed for thin-shell material manipulation, focusing on deformable objects such as paper and cloth. It provides a benchmark for various manipulation tasks and employs a hybrid optimization scheme combining sampling-based and gradient-based methods to address local optima issues. The platform also supports inverse design tasks and real-world experiments, showcasing the transferability of learned skills to real-world applications. The paper discusses the challenges of manipulating thin-shell materials, including sensitivity to actions and frequent changes in contact pairs, and proposes a novel approach to tackle these issues.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces ThinShellLab, a differentiable simulation platform tailored for robotic interactions with diverse thin-shell materials, which supports flexible thin-shell manipulation skill learning and evaluation.
- It proposes a hybrid optimization scheme that combines sampling-based trajectory optimization and gradient-based optimization, boosting both learning efficiency and converged performance across various proposed tasks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive benchmark for robotic learning in thin-shell material manipulation, including a variety of thin-shell object manipulation tasks and a standardized interface for RL and trajectory optimization algorithms.
- It develops a fully differentiable simulation engine supporting volumetric and thin-shell deformable objects and coupling between them, which is the first simulation environment capable of conducting gradient-based optimization on thin-shell object manipulation tasks.
- The paper benchmarks the performance of gradient-based, sampling-based, and hybrid trajectory optimization methods together with state-of-the-art RL algorithms on the task collection, and shows a comprehensive analysis of the challenges and behaviors of each method in different scenarios.
- Besides the manipulation tasks, the differentiable simulator can help solving inverse design problem and bridge the simulation scenes and the real-world robotics. The simulator's capability of reconstructing real-world physics scene parameters via gradient and deploying learned skills to real robots is demonstrated.
- The paper provides a diverse set of manipulation tasks centered around different thin-shell objects and conducts experiments to evaluate the performance of various methods, including real-world experiments that demonstrate the transferability of learned skills to real robots.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and tables that help in understanding the content.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the differentiable simulator, which is crucial for understanding the simulation engine's capabilities and limitations.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the challenges and potential improvements in future research.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the real-world experiments, such as the types of robots and sensors used, and the specifics of the experiments.
- The paper does not include a comparison with other simulators that support thin-shell materials, which could provide a better understanding of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed simulator.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the challenges of manipulating thin-shell materials and how the proposed method addresses these challenges.
- The paper could provide more details on the inverse design tasks and their implementation, as well as a more detailed analysis of the real-world experiments.
