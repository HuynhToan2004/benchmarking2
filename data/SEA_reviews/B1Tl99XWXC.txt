 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TAN, a novel transfer learning method for diffusion probabilistic models (DPMs) aimed at addressing the limited data problem in few-shot image generation tasks. TAN employs two strategies: similarity-guided training, which leverages a classifier to estimate the divergence between source and target domains, and adversarial noise selection, which adaptively chooses noise based on the input image. The method is evaluated on various datasets and reportedly achieves superior performance over existing GAN-based and DDPM-based methods, showcasing improvements in image quality and diversity. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the method's presentation, the novelty of the adversarial noise selection strategy, and the limited scope of the experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant problem of transfer learning in diffusion probabilistic models (DPMs) and proposes a novel method, TAN, which effectively tackles the limited data problem.
- TAN introduces two innovative strategies: similarity-guided training and adversarial noise selection, which are not only novel but also demonstrated to be effective in enhancing the transfer learning process.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and the results, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- The experiments conducted on various datasets showcase the superior performance of TAN compared to existing GAN-based and DDPM-based methods, highlighting its effectiveness in few-shot image generation tasks.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, with a straightforward approach that makes it easy to understand and implement.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with the latest transfer learning methods, which could provide a better understanding of TAN's position in the current research landscape.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the method, particularly in the introduction of key concepts and the explanation of certain equations and terms.
- The experiments are limited in scope, focusing only on a few datasets and not sufficiently diverse in terms of the number of training samples, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contributions of different components of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of each element.
- The novelty of the adversarial noise selection strategy is questionable, as similar strategies have been previously explored in other domains.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with the latest transfer learning methods to better position TAN within the current research landscape?
- How does the similarity-guided training and adversarial noise selection strategy compare with other existing methods in terms of effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the computational complexity and efficiency of TAN, especially in comparison to other methods?
- Is there a possibility to include more diverse datasets and a larger number of training samples to validate the generalizability of TAN?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations and examples to enhance the clarity of the method, particularly in the introduction of key concepts and the explanation of certain equations and terms?
- How does the proposed method handle the problem of noise selection, and what are the specific criteria used for selecting the "worse-case" noise?
