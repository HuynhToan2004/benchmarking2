 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ProteinSSA, a multimodal knowledge distillation framework designed to integrate protein sequence, structure, and functional annotations for unified representations. This approach trains a teacher model utilizing GNNs and a GO encoder, which distills knowledge into a student GNN that encodes protein sequences and structures. The methodology employs a distillation loss to align the distributions of representations from different protein data modalities, aiming to improve the performance of the student model on various downstream tasks. Experiments demonstrate that ProteinSSA outperforms state-of-the-art baselines in tasks such as protein fold and enzyme commission (EC) prediction, highlighting the benefits of the multimodal framework.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and concise presentation of the methodology and results.
- The proposed ProteinSSA framework integrates multiple types of protein data, including sequence, structure, and functional annotations, which is a novel approach for protein representation learning.
- The methodology is innovative, combining sequence, structure, and function information to enhance the performance of protein models, which is supported by empirical results showing improvements over existing methods.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, comparing it to a wide range of baselines and demonstrating its effectiveness in various protein-related applications.
- The use of knowledge distillation to connect protein teacher-student networks is a significant contribution, allowing for the transfer of functional information into student representations via distribution approximation and domain adaptation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the teacher model and the student model, making it difficult to understand the specifics of how the knowledge is distilled and the impact of the teacher model on the student's learning.
- The novelty of the method is limited as it mainly combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The experiments are not comprehensive enough, with some results, such as the EC number prediction, showing only marginal improvements over existing methods.
- There is a lack of clarity in the paper regarding the specifics of the teacher model's training and the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the model.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalization of the model to different protein datasets, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the training of the teacher model, including the datasets used and the specifics of the model architecture?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of sequence-structure-function pre-training, and what are the potential limitations of this approach?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of different hyperparameters, such as the weight of the knowledge distillation loss and the balance between sequence and structure information?
- How does the model handle the generalization to different protein datasets, and what steps have been taken to ensure its applicability in diverse real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the experimental results, particularly the marginal improvements in EC number prediction and the potential reasons for these observations?
