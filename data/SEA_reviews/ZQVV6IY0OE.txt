 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the implicit bias of the stochastic AdaGrad-Norm algorithm in the context of linearly separable data, focusing on its convergence to the L2 max-margin solution. It extends previous work by demonstrating that the algorithm's step size and gradient dependency do not hinder its convergence. The authors employ a divide-and-conquer approach and a martingale method to analyze the generated iterate points, showing that the algorithm converges to the L2 max-margin solution almost surely. The paper also provides a convergence rate analysis, albeit with a slower rate compared to gradient descent.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow proofs, which are a significant strength for the paper.
- The authors demonstrate the convergence of the stochastic AdaGrad-Norm method to the L2 max-margin solution, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is original in its approach, providing a novel proof technique that is different from previous works, which is a notable strength.
- The convergence rate of the algorithm is shown, although it is slower compared to gradient descent, which is a realistic observation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is somewhat limited as it mainly extends previous work to stochastic settings, which may not be seen as a significant advancement.
- The paper's results are restricted to the L2 max-margin solution, which may not be as useful in practical applications where the L1 max-margin solution is preferred.
- The proof techniques used in the paper are not entirely novel, as they are similar to those used in previous works, which could limit the paper's perceived originality.
- The paper's focus on the AdaGrad-Norm algorithm might limit its applicability to other optimization algorithms, such as SGD and AdaGrad-Diagonal, which could benefit from similar analysis.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the distinction between the convergence of the last iterate and the implicit bias, particularly in relation to the L2 max-margin solution?
- How does the convergence rate of the algorithm compare to other algorithms, and what are the implications of this slower rate in practical applications?
- The paper mentions the use of the martingale method to establish almost everywhere convergence. Could the authors explain how this method is used in the proofs and why it is necessary?
- Could the authors discuss the potential implications of their results for other optimization algorithms, such as SGD and AdaGrad-Diagonal?
- In the proofs, particularly in the proof of Theorem 4.1, could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the steps involved, especially where the proofs diverge from previous works?
