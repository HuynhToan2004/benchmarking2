 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to fine-grained visual recognition (FGVR) using a training-free method that leverages large language models (LLMs) and visual question answering (VQA) models to extract and reason about part-level visual attributes from images. This method, termed FineR, does not require human annotations and has been tested on various benchmarks, demonstrating superior performance over existing methods. The approach involves a three-stage process: first, a VQA model identifies super-categories; second, an LLM extracts attributes from the super-category; and third, a multi-modal classifier is constructed to classify images. The paper also introduces a new benchmark, Pokemon, and conducts extensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to fine-grained visual recognition (FGVR) using a training-free method that leverages large language models (LLMs) and visual question answering (VQA) models to extract and reason about part-level visual attributes from images.
- The method is demonstrated to outperform existing methods on multiple benchmarks, including the newly introduced Pokemon benchmark, showing its versatility in working in the wild where expert annotations are hard to obtain.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with extensive experiments conducted to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The introduction of a new benchmark, Pokemon, provides a novel testbed for evaluating the performance of FGVR systems in real-world scenarios.
- The paper is innovative in its approach, combining different foundation models to create a comprehensive pipeline for FGVR, which is a significant step forward in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of the number of parameters, training data, and computational resources used.
- The performance of the proposed method is not consistently superior across all datasets, and there are instances where it performs worse than existing methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to other types of visual recognition tasks and the potential biases introduced by the use of large language models.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to replicate the results and understand the full impact of the proposed method.
- The paper's reliance on pre-trained models and the lack of training on new data might limit its ability to adapt to new, unseen data, which is a critical aspect of FGVR.
- The paper's evaluation metrics and the use of certain datasets, such as the Pokemon dataset, which may not be representative of real-world FGVR scenarios, could limit the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, including a comparison of the number of parameters, training data, and computational resources used?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of visual recognition tasks, such as object detection or segmentation?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the performance of the proposed method vary with different types and amounts of training data?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the performance of the method when applied to different datasets, particularly those that are more representative of real-world FGVR scenarios?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential biases introduced by the use of large language models?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly in terms of the metrics used and the datasets employed?
