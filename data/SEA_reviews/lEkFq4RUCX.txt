 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel distance metric for 3D point clouds, termed Directional Distance Field (DDF), which measures the difference between the local surfaces of point clouds rather than the distances between corresponding points. This metric uses a set of reference points to indirectly establish correspondences between point clouds and induce local geometry, which is then used to calculate the distance between these surfaces. The DDF is evaluated across various tasks such as shape reconstruction, rigid registration, scene flow estimation, and feature representation, demonstrating its superiority over existing metrics. The paper provides a detailed experimental evaluation, including an ablation study, and discusses the computational efficiency and memory requirements of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to measuring the difference between 3D point clouds by focusing on the local surfaces, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that rely on point-wise correspondence.
- The proposed DDF metric is evaluated on various tasks including shape reconstruction, rigid registration, scene flow estimation, and feature representation, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency.
- The paper provides a comprehensive ablation study that validates the design choices of the DDF, offering insights into the impact of different components and parameters on the performance.
- The methodology is clearly explained, with detailed descriptions of the experimental setup and results, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and detailed explanations that aid in understanding the proposed method and its applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with more recent and relevant distance metrics such as Sliced Wasserstein Distance, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The computational complexity of the DDF metric is not adequately discussed, particularly in relation to the number of reference points used, which could affect the practicality of the method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the time complexity or memory requirements of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its efficiency.
- The paper does not discuss the robustness of the DDF metric to different sampling strategies or point cloud densities, which could affect the reliability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the impact of different sampling strategies or point cloud densities on the performance of the DDF metric?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to more recent and relevant distance metrics such as Sliced Wasserstein Distance?
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the time complexity and memory requirements of the proposed method?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and provide potential solutions or areas for future research?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of partial overlapping in point clouds, particularly in scenarios where the point clouds are not completely overlapping?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the DDF metric, particularly in terms of the number of reference points used?
