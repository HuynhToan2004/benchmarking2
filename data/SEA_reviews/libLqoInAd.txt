 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for predicting uncertainty in classification tasks using the Dempster-Shafer theory of evidence (DST) combined with conformal prediction (CP). This approach aims to estimate both aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty by training a neural network to output a mass function, which is then processed using CP to generate prediction sets. The method's performance is evaluated against a baseline model, showing improvements in uncertainty estimation. However, the paper's contributions are questioned due to the lack of novelty and significant improvements over existing methods. The method's scalability and the theoretical justification for its use are also criticized.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow presentation, making it accessible to readers.
- The idea of using Dempster-Shafer theory in combination with conformal prediction is novel and interesting, providing a new approach to uncertainty quantification in classification tasks.
- The methodology is clearly explained, including the use of a new loss function and the application of DST to estimate uncertainty, which is supported by empirical results.
- The paper addresses a relevant problem in machine learning, particularly in safety-critical domains where uncertainty quantification is crucial.
- The authors have provided a comprehensive literature review, highlighting the novelty of their approach compared to existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear distinction between aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty, which is crucial for understanding the nature of the uncertainty estimated by the model.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it appears similar to existing methods that use DST for uncertainty quantification.
- The empirical results, while showing some improvement over baseline models, are not convincingly superior, especially considering the computational complexity and scalability issues.
- The method's applicability is limited by its computational cost and the need for a large calibration set, which might not be feasible in all practical scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other methods for uncertainty quantification, such as Bayesian neural networks and ensemble methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- There are several typographical and grammatical errors throughout the paper, which detract from its professionalism and readability.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the distinction between aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty in your model, and how this distinction affects the interpretation of the results?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational cost and scalability with other methods for uncertainty quantification, especially in larger and more complex datasets?
- Can you provide more details on the choice of the hyperparameter λ and its impact on the model's performance?
- How does the model handle the trade-off between aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty, and what are the implications of this trade-off for the model's predictions?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the n-dimensional approach and its impact on the model's performance and interpretability?
- How does the model perform in scenarios where the calibration set is not available, and what are the implications of this limitation for the model's applicability?
