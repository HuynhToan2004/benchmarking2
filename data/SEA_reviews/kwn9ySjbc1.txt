 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of variable resolution sampling on the performance of large vision-language models (VLMs) in visual question answering (VQA) tasks. It proposes a novel sampling scheme that mirrors the human visual system, focusing on high-resolution areas around the center of the image and progressively decreasing resolution towards the periphery. This approach is tested against uniform sampling, showing that variable resolution can lead to performance improvements in VQA, object detection, and image classification tasks. The paper also includes an interpretability analysis, exploring how variable resolution affects the internal representations of models, suggesting that it may enhance the spread of attention across different resolution levels.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to VQA by introducing variable resolution sampling, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The methodology is well-structured, with clear explanations and figures that aid in understanding the proposed sampling scheme and its effects on model performance.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation across multiple VQA datasets, demonstrating the robustness of the approach across different scenarios.
- The interpretability analysis is insightful, providing a deeper understanding of how variable resolution affects model performance and internal representations.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a complex topic like VQA.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing VQA models, especially those that incorporate variable resolution sampling, to better contextualize the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not consistently strong across all datasets, with some showing only marginal improvements or no significant difference compared to uniform sampling.
- The paper lacks a broader discussion on the societal impact of the proposed method, which could be a significant consideration for a technology like VQA.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential drawbacks and scenarios where the method might not perform as well.
- The paper's claims about the biological plausibility of the variable resolution sampling are not well-supported, which could weaken the argument for the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the specific implementation of variable resolution sampling, particularly how it affects the model's training and inference processes?
- How does the proposed sampling scheme compare to other existing methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and model performance?
- Can the authors elaborate on the biological plausibility of the variable resolution sampling, perhaps by including more detailed comparisons with human visual processing?
- What are the potential societal implications of using variable resolution sampling in VQA systems, and how might these be addressed or mitigated?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies in the experimental results, particularly the marginal improvements or lack of difference observed in some datasets?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the resolution varies more dynamically, such as in real-world applications like autonomous vehicles or surveillance systems?
