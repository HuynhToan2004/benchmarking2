 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Splitted Wavelet Differential Inclusion (SWDI) to address the challenges of wavelet shrinkage in estimating both strong and weak signals in noisy data. The method leverages a differential inclusion approach to handle the bias in strong signal estimation and the suppression of weak signals in traditional wavelet shrinkage. The proposed SWDI is evaluated against existing methods using synthetic data and real-world LFP signals from Parkinson's disease patients, demonstrating its effectiveness in capturing weak signals and improving overall signal estimation. Despite its theoretical and empirical advancements, the paper is criticized for its limited discussion on related works, particularly the application of differential inclusion in wavelet shrinkage. The presentation and clarity of the paper also need improvement, with concerns regarding the notation and the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Splitted Wavelet Differential Inclusion (SWDI), which is a significant advancement in wavelet shrinkage, addressing the challenges of bias in strong signal estimation and the suppression of weak signals.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, and the empirical results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and its application to real-world data, specifically in the context of Parkinson's disease.
- The use of differential inclusion in wavelet shrinkage is innovative and provides a new perspective on handling strong and weak signals in noisy data.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on related works, particularly the application of differential inclusion in wavelet shrinkage, which could have provided a richer context for the research.
- The presentation of the paper needs improvement, with issues such as unclear notation, inconsistent use of symbols, and confusing formulations in the equations.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with concerns about the choice of parameters and the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper's claims about the method's ability to capture weak signals and its bias-free estimation of strong signals are not adequately supported by the experimental evidence.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and the scalability of the proposed method, which could limit its practical application in large-scale or high-dimensional datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between the proposed method and existing methods, particularly in terms of the application of differential inclusion in wavelet shrinkage?
- How does the method perform when applied to other types of data or in different experimental settings, such as EEG signals?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the experimental setup, including the choice of parameters and the methodology used to generate the synthetic data?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Could the authors provide additional evidence or examples to support the claims made about the method's ability to capture weak signals and its bias-free estimation of strong signals?
