 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel benchmark, the Symbolic Behavior Benchmark (S2B), designed to evaluate the emergence of compositional behaviors in artificial agents. The benchmark is based on a meta-referential game that involves a speaker and a listener, where the speaker generates labels based on stimuli, and the listener uses these labels to discriminate between objects. The benchmark uses a novel representation scheme called Symbolic Continuous Stimulus (SCS) representation, which is claimed to be domain-agnostic and allows for the evaluation of compositional learning behaviors (CLBs). The paper presents baseline results for both multi-agent and single-agent contexts, showing that the benchmark is challenging for current state-of-the-art methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper suffers from issues such as unclear writing, lack of detailed explanation for certain components, and a limited scope of evaluation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmark, the Symbolic Behavior Benchmark (S2B), which is designed to evaluate the emergence of compositional behaviors in artificial agents.
- The benchmark is well-motivated, with a clear focus on addressing the binding problem and evaluating compositional learning behaviors.
- The paper provides a detailed investigation into the capabilities of artificial agents in terms of compositional learning behaviors, particularly focusing on receptivity and constructivity aspects.
- The authors propose a novel stimulus representation scheme, Symbolic Continuous Stimulus (SCS) representation, which is claimed to be domain-agnostic and allows for the evaluation of compositional learning behaviors.
- The paper presents baseline results for both multi-agent and single-agent contexts, showing that the benchmark is challenging for current state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper is well-written and provides a detailed background on the referential game and its variants, which is helpful for understanding the context of the study.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from unclear writing, making it difficult to follow the definitions and descriptions of the components used in the benchmark.
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the Symbolic Continuous Stimulus (SCS) representation and its relationship to the binding problem.
- The benchmark is limited to a specific type of meta-referential game, which may not generalize well to other types of compositional learning behaviors.
- The evaluation of the benchmark is limited, with only a few seeds used for testing, which may not provide a robust assessment of the benchmark's effectiveness.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the agents, making it difficult to replicate the results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the benchmark and the potential biases that could affect the evaluation of compositional learning behaviors.
