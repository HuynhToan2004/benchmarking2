 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the application of contrastive decoding in improving reasoning tasks in large language models (LLMs). It builds upon the method introduced by Li et al. (2022) and demonstrates that this method can enhance the performance of LLMs like LLaMA-65B on various benchmarks such as HellaSwag and GSM8K. The paper also explores the use of contrastive decoding with different amateur models and expert models, and shows that it can improve reasoning accuracy by reducing copying from the prompt and avoiding simpler modes of generation. However, the paper's scope is limited to LLaMA models, and it does not extensively compare with other state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-designed methodology.
- The empirical results are strong, showing that contrastive decoding can improve reasoning accuracy by reducing copying from the prompt and avoiding simpler modes of generation.
- The method is simple and effective, with a small computational overhead, making it a valuable addition to the LLM toolkit.
- The paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various tasks, including arithmetic reasoning and commonsense reasoning.
- The method is simple to implement and can be applied to any model, making it a practical solution for improving reasoning capabilities in LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, such as those that use chain-of-thought reasoning or other sampling techniques.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to LLMs that support chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning, which may not generalize well to other types of LLMs.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on LLaMA models and not comparing with a broader range of models or tasks.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of LLMs and its generalizability across different tasks and datasets.
- The method's impact on factual recall and its comparison with other methods like self-consistency are not adequately addressed.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other sampling techniques, such as nucleus sampling, in terms of effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- Can the method be applied to other types of LLMs that do not support chain-of-thought reasoning? If so, what are the implications for its effectiveness and applicability?
- How does the method perform on tasks that do not require chain-of-thought reasoning, such as open-ended generation or factual recall?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational overhead introduced by the method and how it scales with model size?
- How does the method perform on different types of tasks, such as factual recall or open-ended generation, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and models, and discuss any potential limitations or biases in the experimental design?
