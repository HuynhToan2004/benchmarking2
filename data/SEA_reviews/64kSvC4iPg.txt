 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel compressed context memory system designed to enhance the efficiency of online inference in large language models (LLMs) by compressing contextual information. The system uses a lightweight adapter and a conditional LoRA adapter to manage the compression and update processes, aiming to reduce memory and computational demands. The methodology is validated through experiments on various datasets, including MetaICL, LaMP, and DailyDialog, demonstrating improvements in memory efficiency and computational complexity. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty, as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation, and for its lack of comprehensive baseline comparisons and detailed complexity analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear and intuitive presentation of the proposed method.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and scenarios.
- The approach is innovative in applying context compression techniques to online inference scenarios, which is a novel and significant contribution to the field.
- The use of a conditional adapter to address overfitting is a thoughtful and effective solution, enhancing the robustness of the model.
- The paper provides a detailed complexity analysis, which is crucial for understanding the computational efficiency of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily combines existing techniques such as Gisting and LoRA without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other relevant baselines, particularly in terms of memory and computational efficiency.
- The complexity analysis provided is incomplete and could benefit from a more detailed breakdown of the computational costs associated with different components of the model.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method with larger contexts or models, which could limit its applicability in more complex scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently explore the impact of the length of the COMP token on performance, which is a critical parameter for the effectiveness of the method.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the social impacts and limitations of the work, particularly in terms of fairness and potential biases introduced by the compression process.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other relevant baselines, particularly in terms of memory and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform with larger contexts or models, and what are the scalability limitations?
- What is the impact of the length of the COMP token on performance, and how does this influence the effectiveness of the compression process?
- How does the proposed method compare to other context compression techniques such as ICAE and Hyper-Tuning, particularly in terms of memory and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors elaborate on the social impacts and limitations of the work, particularly in terms of fairness and potential biases introduced by the compression process?
