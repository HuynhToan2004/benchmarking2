 **Summary:**
The paper introduces BESA (Blockwise Parameter-Efficient Sparsity Allocation), a novel method for pruning large language models (LLMs) by optimizing sparsity across transformer blocks. BESA utilizes a block-wise approach to minimize reconstruction error and optimize sparsity rates layer-wise, which is a departure from traditional layer-wise methods that can lead to accumulated errors. The methodology involves sorting weights by importance, assigning importance scores to weights, and optimizing sparsity levels to achieve a differentiable sparsity mask. The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on various benchmarks, including the WikiText2 dataset, and shows significant improvements over existing methods like SparseGPT and Wanda. However, concerns about the fairness of comparisons due to different sparsity levels and the need for more comprehensive experiments on larger datasets persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for pruning LLMs, which is both differentiable and parameter-efficient, making it easy to optimize.
- The methodology is clearly explained, with a well-structured presentation that aids in understanding the complex concepts involved.
- The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on various benchmarks, showing significant improvements over existing methods like SparseGPT and Wanda.
- The approach of blockwise pruning is highlighted as a significant advancement over layer-wise pruning, which can lead to significant performance degradation.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the methodology and experiments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claims about the fairness of comparisons are questionable, particularly concerning the different sparsity levels used in the experiments.
- The method's performance is not convincingly superior across all datasets and model sizes, with some results showing only marginal improvements or even worse performance compared to baseline methods.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the hardware acceleration used for the experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The paper's focus on LLM pruning is somewhat narrow, and it could benefit from a broader discussion on the applicability of the method to other types of models or tasks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the fairness of the comparisons made in the paper, particularly regarding the different sparsity levels used in different experiments?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods, such as those that use different sparsity levels or different pruning strategies?
- Could the authors provide more details on the hardware acceleration used in the experiments, including the specifics of the ViTooD accelerator and its impact on the results?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to other types of models or tasks, and if so, what are the potential challenges or limitations?
- How does the method perform on larger datasets, such as those used in the OpenWebBench benchmark?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their work, particularly in the context of LLM pruning?
