 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ATLAS, a novel anytime neural architecture search (NAS) method specifically designed for tabular data. ATLAS employs a two-phase filtering-and-refinement optimization scheme, leveraging both training-free and training-based evaluation metrics to enhance the efficiency and effectiveness of NAS. The method is validated through experiments on the NAS-Bench-Tabular benchmark, demonstrating a significant reduction in search time compared to existing methods. The authors also propose a new metric, ExpressFlow, which measures trainability and expressivity in tabular data, and a new benchmark, NAS-Bench-Tabular, which is designed to facilitate the development and evaluation of NAS algorithms for tabular data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the methodology and experimental results.
- The introduction of ExpressFlow as a new metric for evaluating trainability and expressivity in tabular data is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper presents a novel anytime NAS approach tailored for tabular data, which is an under-explored area in NAS research.
- The proposed method demonstrates a significant reduction in search time compared to existing methods, showing a practical application of the NAS approach.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including experiments on a newly proposed benchmark, NAS-Bench-Tabular, which enhances the credibility of the results.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including the ExpressFlow metric and the two-phase filtering-and-refinement optimization scheme, which are innovative contributions to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of the ExpressFlow metric's effectiveness and the NAS-Bench-Tabular's performance against other benchmarks.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the ExpressFlow metric, which is critical for evaluating the efficiency of the method.
- The experimental results are limited to a small number of datasets, which may not sufficiently demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational resources and time required for each phase of the NAS process, which is crucial for evaluating the practical applicability of the method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its applicability and potential challenges.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the ExpressFlow metric, including the time and resources required for its calculation?
- How does the ExpressFlow metric compare to other existing metrics in terms of its effectiveness in evaluating trainability and expressivity in tabular data?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods to demonstrate the superiority of their proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger and more complex datasets, and what are the computational resources and time requirements for such applications?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how do these impact the practical applicability and generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the ExpressFlow metric in the NAS process and how it contributes to the overall performance of the method?
