 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel backdoor attack, named Certified Copy, which aims to evade detection by mimicking the behavior of clean models under poisoned inputs. This is achieved by training a model with a new cost function that minimizes the difference in activations between clean and poisoned data. The model is trained to classify clean data as usual but is fine-tuned to misclassify poisoned data, making it difficult for detection mechanisms to distinguish between clean and poisoned models. The paper evaluates the effectiveness of the attack across several datasets and models, demonstrating that it can bypass some existing detection methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty and the practicality of the attack, as it may not be effective against more advanced or realistic scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology.
- The introduction of a new cost function for training backdoored models is a novel approach, which could potentially improve the effectiveness of backdoor attacks.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed attack method across various datasets and models, demonstrating its effectiveness in evading detection.
- The use of data augmentation techniques to create more realistic and challenging triggers for detection methods is an innovative approach.
- The methodology is clearly explained, with detailed descriptions of the training process and the use of different loss functions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation from existing backdoor attacks, particularly in terms of the novelty of the proposed method.
- The practicality of the attack is questionable, as it requires extensive data augmentation and fine-tuning, which may not be feasible in real-world scenarios.
- The evaluation of the attack's effectiveness is limited to simple models and datasets, which may not accurately reflect its performance in more complex or realistic settings.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and generalizability across different model architectures and datasets.
- The paper's claims about the effectiveness of the attack are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which show only marginal improvements over existing methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the ethical implications of the proposed attack, which could be used maliciously to compromise the security of AI systems.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method differ from existing backdoor attacks, particularly in terms of its novelty and effectiveness?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the data augmentation process and the specifics of the fine-tuning steps involved in the attack?
- How scalable is the proposed method across different model architectures and datasets? What are the specific conditions under which the method is most effective?
- The paper mentions that the attack is effective against certain defense mechanisms but not others. Can the authors provide more detailed insights into why this is the case?
- How do the authors address the ethical implications of the proposed attack, particularly in terms of its potential for misuse?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies in the experimental results, particularly the marginal improvements claimed over existing methods?
