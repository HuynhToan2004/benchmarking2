 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Equal Long-term Benefit Rate (ELBERT) as a novel fairness criterion in sequential decision-making scenarios, where it considers the importance of different time steps in terms of fairness. ELBERT is designed to adapt static fairness notions to sequential settings, using a policy gradient method to optimize the long-term benefit rate. The authors demonstrate that ELBERT outperforms existing methods in terms of reducing bias while maintaining high utility in various environments. The paper provides a theoretical analysis and empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of ELBERT, although it is noted for its similarity to existing fairness metrics and the potential for over-reliance on the assumption of equal importance across different time steps.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel fairness notion called Equal Long-term Benefit Rate (ELBERT), which is applicable to various fairness metrics and is effective in reducing bias while maintaining high utility.
- The ELBERT policy optimization (ELBERT-PO) algorithm is well-designed and supported by theoretical analysis, showing its effectiveness in mitigating bias in sequential decision-making scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a logical structure, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- The proposed method is general and applicable to different fairness metrics, and it is compared against a variety of baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper provides a clear theoretical analysis of the proposed method, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the importance of different time steps is equal, which may not hold true in practical scenarios, especially in loan applications where the importance of time steps may vary.
- The proposed method is similar to existing fairness metrics such as those used in static settings, and the novelty of the approach is not clearly articulated.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of ELBERT, especially in scenarios where the assumption of equal importance across time steps does not hold.
- The experimental setup is limited to three environments, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of ELBERT-PO, which is crucial for understanding its applicability in large-scale or complex systems.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other existing fairness notions or methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of ELBERT, especially in scenarios where the assumption of equal importance across time steps does not hold?
- How does ELBERT-PO perform in more complex scenarios, such as those involving more than two groups or in dynamic environments where group demands change over time?
- Could you provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of ELBERT-PO?
- How does ELBERT-PO compare with other fairness notions or methods, especially in terms of bias reduction and utility maintenance?
- Can you clarify the differences between ELBERT and existing fairness metrics, especially in terms of the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach?
- How does the fairness-aware advantage function affect the performance of ELBERT-PO, and what are the implications of using different advantage functions?
- In the experiments, why was the attention allocation environment chosen, and how does it contribute to the overall evaluation of ELBERT-PO?
