 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the effects of reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) on the behavioral distributions of large language models (LLMs), specifically focusing on the LLaMA2-7B model. It employs a methodology involving the decomposition of LLM outputs into positive and negative sub-distributions, and measures the distinguishability and steerability of these distributions using metrics like Wasserstein distance and beta-distinguishability proxy. The study also evaluates the model's susceptibility to negative behavior steering under persona prompts, revealing inconsistencies in behavioral distinguishability and the model's susceptibility to negative behavior. The paper contributes by providing a systematic analysis of RLHF's impact on behavior distributions and vulnerabilities to prompt steering, although the novelty of the methodology and the depth of the analysis are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear and detailed analysis of the effects of reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) on the behavioral distributions of large language models (LLMs).
- It introduces a novel methodology for measuring the distinguishability and steerability of LLMs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The study systematically evaluates the model's output distributions across a wide spectrum of behaviors, providing insights into the influence of RLHF on the behavioral distributions of LLMs.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the results.
- It addresses a critical and timely issue in the field of LLMs, specifically focusing on the impact of RLHF on the behavioral distributions of LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is limited as it primarily applies existing methods (beta-distinguishability proxy and Wasserstein distance) to a new dataset, without introducing new methodologies or insights.
- The analysis of the impact of RLHF on the behavioral distributions of LLMs is limited to a single model, which may not generalize to other models or scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its methodology from existing works, particularly in terms of its novelty and depth of analysis.
- The paper's conclusions are not robustly supported by the data presented, with some results (such as the inconsistency in behavioral distinguishability) being more apparent than substantive.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the study, which could provide a more balanced view of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the beta-distinguishability proxy is calculated and whether it is based on the output of the LLM or the input prompt?
- How does the RLHF process influence the distinguishability of behaviors, and what are the implications of this for the safety and reliability of LLMs?
- The paper suggests that RLHF can make models more steerable towards negative behaviors. Can the authors provide more details on this observation and its implications?
- Why were specific behaviors chosen for analysis, and how were they selected?
- How does the RLHF process affect the model's behavioral distributions, and what are the implications of these changes for the model's safety and reliability?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their study and how these might affect the generalizability and applicability of their findings?
