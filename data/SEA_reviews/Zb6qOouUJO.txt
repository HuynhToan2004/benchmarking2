 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel algorithm, SBO-LSVRG, for stochastic bilevel optimization, leveraging the L-SVRG method to address the memory constraints of existing methods like SABA. This algorithm aims to improve upon SABA by reducing memory usage from O(m+n)(d+p) to O(d+p), which is crucial in large-scale problems. Theoretical analysis supports the convergence of the proposed method, and empirical results are provided to validate its effectiveness. The paper also discusses the computational efficiency and scalability of the algorithm, though it lacks detailed empirical evaluations and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the algorithm and its implementation.
- The proposed algorithm is novel, using a variance reduction technique that is not commonly used in bilevel optimization, potentially leading to significant improvements in convergence rates.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, providing a thorough convergence analysis and comparison with other methods like SABA.
- The paper is structured to be accessible, with clear definitions and explanations of key concepts, making it easier for readers to understand the proposed methodologies.
- The algorithm's simplicity and potential for practical application are highlighted, as it aims to reduce memory usage and computational complexity, which could be beneficial in large-scale problems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical evidence to support the claimed improvements in convergence rates. More detailed empirical evaluations and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods are needed to substantiate the claims.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of results, particularly in figures and tables, which could benefit from more detailed explanations and better visual aids.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the assumptions made about the smoothness and strong convexity of the objective functions, which might not hold in all practical scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related works, particularly those that have explored similar methodologies or have achieved similar results.
- There are several minor errors and inconsistencies in the paper, such as incorrect references and formatting issues, which need to be addressed for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed algorithm perform in scenarios where the assumptions of strong convexity and smoothness do not hold? What are the implications of these assumptions on the practical applicability of the method?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and discuss how these assumptions affect the practical applicability of the method?
