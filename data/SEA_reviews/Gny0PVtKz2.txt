 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Transformer-based model, ConvFormer, designed for sequential user modeling in recommender systems. The authors identify three critical criteria for effective token mixers: order sensitivity, large receptive field, and lightweight architecture. ConvFormer integrates these criteria by replacing self-attention with depth-wise convolution, enlarging the receptive field, and employing a lightweight architecture. Extensive experiments on four public datasets demonstrate that ConvFormer outperforms various baselines, validating the efficacy of the proposed criteria. The paper also introduces an acceleration technique to handle long sequences efficiently, enhancing the model's computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear structure and detailed descriptions of the proposed model and its components.
- The authors provide extensive experimental results on multiple public datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed model in various scenarios.
- The motivation behind the research is well-articulated, with a focus on improving the Transformer architecture for sequential user modeling in recommender systems.
- The paper includes ablation studies and detailed analysis, which help in understanding the contributions of different components of the model.
- The proposed criteria for designing effective token mixers are well-reasoned and supported by empirical evidence, providing a solid foundation for the proposed model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed model and its assumptions, which could affect the generalizability and applicability of the findings.
- The comparison with other models, such as GRU4Rec and SRGNN, is insufficient, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed model's performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the computational complexity and training time of the proposed model compared to other models like SASRec.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the model to longer sequences, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as the proposed model primarily replaces self-attention with convolution, which has been explored in previous works.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other efficient Transformer variants, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed model's performance.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed model and its assumptions?
- How does the proposed model compare in terms of computational complexity and training time to other models like SASRec, especially when using long sequences?
- Can the authors elaborate on the scalability of the model to longer sequences and discuss potential strategies for handling such sequences?
- In light of the similarities to existing models, how does the proposed model differentiate itself and offer new insights or improvements over existing methods?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of the proposed model on the scalability and generalizability of the findings to other recommendation tasks?
