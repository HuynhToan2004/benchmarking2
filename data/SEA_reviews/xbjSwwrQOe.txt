 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Statistical Rejection Sampling Optimization (RSO), aimed at improving preference optimization in large language models (LLMs) by optimizing the sampling of preference pairs from the estimated optimal policy. This method, RSO, leverages a statistical rejection sampling algorithm to sample from the estimated optimal policy and uses a pairwise reward-ranking model to label these samples. The approach is validated through extensive experiments on two tasks, showing improvements over existing methods like DPO and SLiC. However, concerns about the generalizability of the method and the clarity of its presentation have been raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, RSO, which is a significant contribution to the field of LLM training.
- The method is well-articulated, making it easy to follow, and is supported by thorough experiments.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, which are presented in a logical and coherent manner.
- The proposed method, RSO, shows significant improvements over existing methods like DPO and SLiC in various tasks, as demonstrated by extensive experiments.
- The use of statistical rejection sampling in LLM training is innovative and addresses a critical issue in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The experiments are limited to only two tasks, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the method.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of the hyperparameters used in the experiments, particularly the choice of β and γ.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of computational resources and the scalability of the method to larger models.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the method, particularly in the explanation of how the reward model is trained and the role of the reward model in the training process.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other existing methods, such as RAFT and ReST, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the training process of the reward model, particularly how it is trained and how it is used in the RSO method?
- How does the proposed method perform when scaled up to larger models, and what are the computational resources required for such scaling?
- Can you clarify the role of the reward model in the training process, particularly in the context of the statistical rejection sampling algorithm?
- How does the method handle the generation of preference pairs from the estimated optimal policy, and what are the implications of this approach on the overall training process?
- Could you provide more details on the choice of β and γ in the experiments, and how these parameters affect the performance of the method?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods, such as RAFT and ReST, in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
