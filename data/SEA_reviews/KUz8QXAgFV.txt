 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for visual representation learning by combining masked modeling and autoregressive modeling in a probabilistic framework. This approach, termed Generative Visual Pretraining (GVP), aims to address the challenges of both methods by integrating their strengths, such as handling natural order in images and enhancing dependency relationships between tokens. The methodology is supported by a theoretical analysis and extensive experiments, demonstrating the effectiveness of GVP in various downstream tasks like linear probing, fine-tuning, and transfer learning. Despite some concerns about the clarity of the methodology and the experimental results, the paper provides a significant contribution to the field by bridging the gap between autoregressive and masked modeling in computer vision.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by combining autoregressive and masked modeling methods in a probabilistic framework, which is a significant advancement in the field of visual representation learning.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of each method, which is crucial for understanding the rationale behind the proposed method.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method, including linear probing, fine-tuning, and transfer learning, which demonstrate the robustness and applicability of the approach.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience and contributing to the clarity and transparency of the research.
- The authors have shown that the proposed method can outperform existing methods in several benchmarks, indicating the practical utility and effectiveness of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the methodology, particularly the implementation of the two-stream attention mechanism and the causal masks, which are crucial for understanding the practical application of the proposed framework.
- There is a significant discrepancy in the performance metrics between different model configurations (GVP-p2p, GVP-p2d, and GVP-d2d), which is not adequately explained, leaving it unclear which model configuration is most effective and why.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related works, particularly in the area of autoregressive models, which are crucial for positioning the research within the existing literature.
- The experimental results, while promising, are not consistently superior to existing methods across all datasets, which raises questions about the generalizability and applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from additional ablation studies to better understand the impact of different model configurations and training strategies on the performance of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the methodology, particularly the implementation of the two-stream attention mechanism and the causal masks?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the two-stream attention mechanism over other possible implementations, such as using separate attention blocks for each stream?
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies in performance between different model configurations and explain which configuration is most effective and why?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other datasets or tasks beyond those tested in the paper?
- Could the authors discuss the potential implications of the proposed method for other areas of research, such as natural language processing or other domains where autoregressive and masked modeling are used?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent advancements in the field of visual representation learning, such as those using different types of attention mechanisms or training strategies?
