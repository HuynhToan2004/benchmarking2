 **Summary:**
The paper presents a theoretical analysis of Independent Subnetwork Training (IST) in both homogeneous and heterogeneous settings, using a quadratic model. The authors explore the convergence properties of IST under different conditions, including biased gradient estimators and unbiased compression operators. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the practical implementation of IST in federated learning scenarios. Despite the theoretical insights provided, the paper is criticized for its limited applicability to more complex models and its reliance on simplified assumptions that may not hold in practical scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the convergence properties of Independent Subnetwork Training (IST) in both homogeneous and heterogeneous settings, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical results are well-supported by clear and concise proofs, which enhances the credibility of the findings.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making the complex theoretical concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The paper identifies the settings where IST can optimize efficiently or fail to converge, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of this method.
- The experimental validation supports the theoretical results, providing a robust foundation for the claims made in the paper.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies on a simplified model (quadratic) which may not accurately represent the behavior of IST in more complex, non-quadratic models, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the bounded stochastic gradient norm and the use of permutation sketch, may not hold in practical scenarios, which could limit the applicability of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the implications of the findings for practical applications, which could be a significant drawback for practitioners seeking to implement IST in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not compare its results with those of other methods, such as IST with single-node training, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's performance.
- The paper's theoretical analysis assumes that each node computes the true gradient at the submodel, which may not be practical in federated learning scenarios where data is distributed and local gradients are used.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a comparison of their results with those of other methods, such as IST with single-node training, to better understand the relative performance of the method?
- How do the assumptions made in the paper, such as the bounded stochastic gradient norm and the use of permutation sketch, affect the practical applicability of the findings?
- In practical federated learning scenarios, how does the theoretical analysis translate to real-world applications, especially when the assumptions made in the paper may not hold?
- Can the authors discuss the implications of their findings for the practical implementation of IST in federated learning scenarios, particularly in terms of handling heterogeneity and ensuring robustness against stragglers?
- Could the paper be extended to include a discussion on the limitations of the assumptions made and the implications of these limitations for the practical applicability of the findings?
