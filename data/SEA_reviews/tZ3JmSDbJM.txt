 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GRAPES, a novel graph sampling method aimed at improving the scalability of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by learning to identify influential nodes for training. GRAPES utilizes GFlowNets to determine node sampling probabilities based on classification objectives, allowing for adaptive sampling that maintains high accuracy even with small sample sizes. The method is evaluated across various graph benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness in accuracy and scalability. Despite its innovative approach, concerns about the novelty and the method's generalization to larger, more complex datasets remain.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The proposed method, GRAPES, is innovative and well-motivated, addressing the scalability issue in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by learning to identify influential nodes for training.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, and the results show that GRAPES outperforms other sampling-based methods in most cases.
- The paper provides a comprehensive comparison with baseline methods, including GAS, and demonstrates the effectiveness of GRAPES in terms of accuracy and scalability.
- The use of GFlowNets to learn node sampling probabilities based on classification objectives is a novel approach that enhances the adaptability of the sampling process.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as similar approaches have been explored in previous works. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on how GRAPES differentiates from these prior methods.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with marginal improvements over baselines and significant performance drops in certain datasets.
- The paper does not provide sufficient analysis on the computational efficiency of GRAPES, particularly in terms of training time and memory usage, which is crucial for assessing its scalability.
- There is a lack of discussion on the generalization of GRAPES to larger, more complex datasets, which is a significant limitation given the potential applications of the method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the scalability of GRAPES, especially in terms of its ability to handle large-scale graphs and its computational efficiency.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of GRAPES, including training time and memory usage?
- How does GRAPES perform on larger, more complex datasets, and what are the potential limitations in scaling up the method?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the normalizing function $Z(s_0)$ in Equation 3 and discuss its impact on the sampling process?
- How does GRAPES handle the issue of sampling from the GFlowNet distribution, and what are the implications of this approach on the overall performance and scalability of the method?
- Could the authors provide more insights into the generalization of GRAPES to other graph-related tasks, such as link prediction and unsupervised representation learning?
