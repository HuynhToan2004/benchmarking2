 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Orthogonal Gradient Learning (OGL) to address the multi-model forgetting issue in one-shot Neural Architecture Search (NAS). This method updates the weights of overlapping subnets in the orthogonal direction to the gradient space of previously trained architectures, thereby reducing the impact of current architecture training on the performance of previously trained architectures. The authors also propose an enhanced projection based on Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to determine the orthogonal direction. The method is evaluated on two NAS baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness in mitigating multi-model forgetting and improving the predictive ability of the supernet in one-shot NAS.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, Orthogonal Gradient Learning (OGL), which is both theoretically and experimentally validated to mitigate the multi-model forgetting issue in one-shot Neural Architecture Search (NAS).
- The method is well-organized, with clear writing and comprehensive experimental validation, demonstrating the effectiveness of OGL in improving the predictive ability of the supernet in one-shot NAS.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the multi-model forgetting issue and the potential projector attenuation issue in previous methods, highlighting the importance of these issues in NAS.
- The proposed method, OGL, is simple yet effective, with a straightforward algorithm that is easy to implement.
- The paper includes a detailed convergence analysis of the update of the weights, which adds depth to the theoretical understanding of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's writing could be improved for clarity, particularly in explaining the methodology and the experimental setups.
- The novelty of the method is somewhat limited as it builds upon existing methods like DI, with the main difference being the use of PCA for orthogonal projection.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of multi-model forgetting in scenarios where the training of different architectures is not sequential, such as in DARTS.
- The computational complexity of the proposed method is not discussed, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with a lack of statistical significance and a limited scope of experiments.

- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other state-of-the-art methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness in mitigating multi-model forgetting?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might impact its practical application in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of computational complexity and training time?
- Can the authors provide more details on the methodology used in the experiments, including the specifics of the PCA projection and the training parameters used?
