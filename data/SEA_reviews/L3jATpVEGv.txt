 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GraphAgent, a novel method that leverages large language models (LLMs) to perform node classification tasks on text-attributed graphs. GraphAgent frames the node classification problem as an agent-planning task, using LLMs to parameterize the agent and execute actions like retrieving neighbors and features. The method is evaluated on several datasets and demonstrates competitive performance against supervised learning methods, highlighting its potential in interpretability and scalability. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in methodological descriptions, limited comparisons to existing methods, and the high cost associated with using LLMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method of leveraging large language models (LLMs) to perform node classification tasks on text-attributed graphs, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is clearly described, with a detailed explanation of the GraphAgent framework and its components, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the GraphAgent, demonstrating its effectiveness through experiments on several datasets, showing comparable performance to supervised learning methods.
- The use of LLMs for graph learning tasks is highlighted as an innovative approach, which could potentially enhance interpretability and scalability in graph machine learning.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the methodology and results, making it accessible and understandable to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison to existing methods, which could help in understanding the relative performance and advantages of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodological descriptions, particularly in the agent-planning formalization and the role of LLMs in the process.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include scalability issues, computational costs, and the generalizability of the results.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not thoroughly validated, with concerns about the fairness of comparisons and the generalizability of the results across different datasets and scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of interpretability, which is a significant claim of the proposed method.
- The use of LLMs, while innovative, is also a limitation due to their high cost and potential scalability issues, which are not adequately addressed.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the agent-planning formalization and the role of LLMs in the process?
- How does the GraphAgent handle different types of graph structures, and what are the implications for its performance?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the comparisons made and the fairness of these comparisons?
- How does the GraphAgent handle scenarios where the graph structure is not text-attributed, or where the textual descriptions are not available?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the interpretability of the GraphAgent, perhaps through examples or additional experiments?
- How does the GraphAgent scale with larger datasets, and what are the computational costs associated with its use?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their method and how these might affect its practical application in real-world scenarios?
