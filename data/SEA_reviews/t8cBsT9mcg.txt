 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to selective classification in deep learning using concept bottleneck models, focusing on improving coverage and performance under abstention. The methodology involves training a concept detector that maps features to a vector of probability predictions and a front-end model that maps these predictions to a predicted probability of the outcome. This approach allows for the abstention of predictions when confidence is low and for the confirmation of predicted concepts to improve performance and coverage. The paper is supported by experiments on real-world and synthetic datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, it faces criticism for its lack of novelty, unclear presentation, and potential over-reliance on assumptions that may not hold in practical scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The idea of selective classification is both novel and practical, with potential for significant impact in various applications.
- The paper provides a clear and well-structured presentation, including a detailed problem statement and methodology.
- The experiments are thorough, and the results are well-presented, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The concept of combining selective classification with concept bottleneck models is innovative and could lead to significant advancements in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it primarily builds on existing methods like concept bottleneck models and selective classification, without introducing significant new contributions.
- The presentation could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and conciseness. The paper is overly verbose in some sections, which could be streamlined for better readability.
- There are several unclear or misleading statements and equations, which could confuse readers and detract from the paper's credibility.
- The paper relies on strong assumptions, such as the independence of concepts given the features and the calibration of the front-end model's predictions, which may not hold in practical scenarios.
- The methodology section is inadequately explained, particularly the concept of confirmation and the computational efficiency of the proposed methods.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the independence of concepts given the features and the calibration of the front-end model's predictions?
- How does the paper address the potential for overfitting when using a large number of concepts, as shown in the experiments?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the concepts of confirmation and the computational efficiency of the proposed methods?
- How does the paper ensure that the selective classification does not exacerbate performance disparities over subpopulations?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods?
- Is there a plan to include more datasets in future experiments to validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods across different scenarios?
