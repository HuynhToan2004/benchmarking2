 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training method, PATHS, for video-text retrieval tasks, specifically designed to address the overfitting issues commonly encountered in fine-tuning pre-trained models. PATHS employs a two-stage training process, where the first stage identifies optimal parameters based on their fluctuations, and the second stage utilizes a scene transition mask adapter to enhance scene-level understanding. The method is evaluated on multiple datasets, showing significant improvements over existing methods, although some concerns about the novelty and clarity of the methodology are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue in the field of video-language tasks, focusing on the overfitting problem in fine-tuning pre-trained models.
- The proposed method, PATHS, is a simple yet effective approach that is easy to implement and has been tested on multiple datasets, showing significant performance improvements.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with comprehensive experiments and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The proposed method can be applied to any existing video-language models, making it a versatile solution for various applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed explanations and justifications for certain methodological choices, such as the use of a two-stage training process and the specifics of how the adapter is designed.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need correction, which could affect the readability and professionalism of the paper.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as similar approaches have been previously explored in other contexts, such as CLIP adaptation for text-to-image generation.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements.
- There are concerns about the fairness of the comparisons made in the paper, particularly the omission of certain baseline methods and the potential for cherry-picking results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specifics of the adapter design, particularly how it integrates with the existing model and how it contributes to the overall performance?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements to other state-of-the-art methods, especially in large-scale applications?
- Can the authors provide more detailed justifications for the methodological choices, particularly the decision to use a two-stage training process and the rationale behind the adapter design?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting, especially in scenarios where the model is fine-tuned on a limited dataset?
- Could the authors address the concerns regarding the fairness of the comparisons made in the paper, particularly the omission of certain baseline methods and the potential for cherry-picking results?
