 **Summary:**
The paper introduces UniTabE, a novel pretraining methodology for tabular data using a large-scale dataset of approximately 13 billion samples sourced from Kaggle. UniTabE employs a Transformer encoder to refine representations and utilizes a shallow decoder for fine-tuning tasks. The model's architecture includes a TabUnit module for feature processing, which is claimed to outperform XGBoost in various tasks. The paper also explores the use of pretraining strategies like multi-cell masking and contrastive learning, demonstrating superior performance over existing models in both Kaggle and public benchmarks. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned, with concerns about the clarity of the pretraining dataset's quality and the model's scalability to different domains.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to pretraining on tabular data using a large-scale dataset, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed UniTabE model demonstrates strong performance across various tasks, including classification and regression, showing a clear advantage over existing methods like XGBoost.
- The model's architecture, including the TabUnit module, is well-designed and effectively processes tabular data, contributing to its performance.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The inclusion of a large-scale pretraining dataset and the use of free-form prompts enhance the model's adaptability and scalability to various tasks.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, covering multiple datasets and tasks, which supports the validity of the claims made about the model's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of model size and computational efficiency.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, with concerns about the originality of the TabUnit module and the overall methodology.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the model to different domains, raising concerns about its generalizability.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the pretraining dataset's quality and its impact on the model's performance. The paper does not discuss the potential biases or limitations introduced by the dataset.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the pretraining dataset, such as its diversity and representativeness, which could affect the model's performance on out-of-distribution data.
- The paper's claims about the model's superiority over XGBoost are not convincingly supported, and the experimental results are not sufficiently detailed or comprehensive.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of UniTabE with other state-of-the-art models in terms of model size, computational efficiency, and performance on various benchmarks?
- How does the model perform on out-of-distribution datasets, and what measures have been taken to ensure its generalizability across different domains?
- Could the authors elaborate on the pretraining dataset's quality and its impact on the model's performance? What steps were taken to mitigate potential biases or limitations introduced by the dataset?
- How does the model handle missing values in tabular data, and what are the implications for its performance and generalizability?
- The paper claims that UniTabE outperforms XGBoost in various tasks. Can the authors provide more detailed results or additional experiments to support this claim?
- What are the specific advantages of UniTabE over other pretraining methods for tabular data, and how does it compare in terms of scalability and adaptability to different tasks?
