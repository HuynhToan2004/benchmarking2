 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel bilevel optimization-based framework named PARL to address the policy alignment problem in reinforcement learning (RL). It identifies a significant gap in existing algorithmic designs by failing to characterize the dependence of the alignment objective on the data generated by policy trajectories, leading to suboptimal performance. The proposed framework formulates this problem as a bilevel optimization, where the upper level focuses on reward design and the lower level on policy optimization. The paper also introduces an algorithm named A-PARL to solve the PARL problem, backed by sample complexity bounds. Empirical results demonstrate that PARL improves policy alignment in large-scale environments, showing significant improvements over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel bilevel optimization-based framework, PARL, to address the policy alignment problem in reinforcement learning, which is a significant gap in existing algorithmic designs.
- The proposed framework formulates the RL agent policy alignment as a bilevel optimization problem, which is a novel approach that differentiates from existing methods.
- The paper provides a correct evaluation objective for policy alignment in RL, which is crucial for ensuring the reliability and effectiveness of the alignment process.
- The empirical results of PARL demonstrate significant improvements in policy alignment, showing a 63% improvement in sample efficiency compared to existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and precise mathematical formulations that enhance understanding and reproducibility.
- The authors provide sample complexity bounds for the proposed algorithm, which adds a theoretical rigor to the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters such as the number of policy gradient steps in Algorithm 1 and the discount factor in the lower-level objective.
- The paper does not discuss the choice of the reward function in the experiments, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the upper-level gradient estimation is performed, particularly in the absence of a closed-form solution for the gradient of the upper objective.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the scalability of the proposed algorithm, especially in large-scale environments.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the potential limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other existing methods such as RLHF, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its performance and effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- How are the hyperparameters chosen in Algorithm 1, and what are the implications of different choices on the performance of the algorithm?
- How is the reward function chosen in the experiments, and how does it affect the performance of the algorithm?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the upper-level gradient estimation is performed, especially in the absence of a closed-form solution for the gradient of the upper objective?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods such as RLHF, and what are the key differences in terms of performance and effectiveness?
- Is there a discussion on the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how do these limitations affect its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed algorithm, especially in large-scale environments?
