 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a multilingual safety benchmark, XSafety, which covers 14 safety scenarios across 10 languages, including English. This benchmark is used to evaluate the safety of large language models (LLMs) in non-English languages, demonstrating that these models can generate more unsafe responses in non-English queries than in English ones. The study also proposes prompting methods to enhance the safety of LLMs in non-English languages, showing that these methods can significantly reduce the unsafe response ratio. The paper is well-received for its innovative approach in addressing multilingual safety concerns, although it has been criticized for its limited scope in terms of languages and models evaluated, and the reliance on a potentially biased dataset.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel multilingual safety benchmark, XSafety, which covers a variety of safety scenarios and is the first of its kind to evaluate the safety of LLMs in non-English languages.
- The study demonstrates that LLMs are less safe in non-English languages than in English, highlighting the need for developing safety alignment for non-English languages.
- The paper proposes simple and effective prompting methods to improve the multilingual safety of ChatGPT, which can significantly reduce the unsafe response ratio from 19.1% to 9.7% for non-English queries.
- The benchmark is comprehensive, including 28,000 annotated instances across 10 languages, which will be valuable for future research on LLM safety.
- The authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, highlighting the importance of developing safety alignment for non-English languages.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily evaluates only four LLMs, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The benchmark covers only 10 languages, which might not fully represent the diversity of languages and cultures.
- The reliance on a translated dataset from English and Chinese could introduce biases towards English and Chinese cultures.
- The study's scope is limited to safety alignment, and it does not address other important aspects like bias, copyright, or psychological safety.
- The paper's methodology and evaluation are not clearly explained, particularly the translation process and the evaluation of safety in non-English languages.
- The proposed prompting methods are simple and may not be effective in all scenarios or for all languages.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential biases introduced by the translation process, which could affect the reliability of the results.

**Questions:**
- How do you ensure that the translated dataset is free of biases towards English and Chinese cultures?
- What are the specific safety issues that are under-represented in the benchmark, and how do these issues affect the evaluation of LLM safety?
- How do you evaluate the safety of LLM responses in non-English languages, especially when the evaluation is conducted in English?
- Could you provide more details on the translation process and the quality control measures used to ensure the accuracy of the translations?
- How do you address the potential biases introduced by the translation process, and what steps are taken to mitigate these biases?
- What are the implications of the findings for the development of safety alignment for non-English languages, and how do these findings impact the broader community?
