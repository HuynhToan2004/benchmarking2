 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Gradient Normalization Scaling (GNS), aimed at enhancing the transferability of adversarial attacks on Vision Transformers (ViTs) by addressing the gradient disparities across different layers. The GNS method is designed to scale small gradients and construct masks to differentiate between high-frequency and low-frequency information, thereby improving the transferability of adversarial samples. The paper also proposes a High-Frequency Adaptation (HFA) method to explore the sensitivity of ViTs to adversarial attacks in different frequency regions. Extensive experiments are conducted to validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, demonstrating significant improvements in transferability on both ViT and CNN models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Gradient Normalization Scaling (GNS), which addresses the challenge of gradient variations during the backpropagation process by normalizing and scaling the gradients, enhancing the transferability of adversarial samples on Vision Transformers (ViTs).
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their contributions to the field.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, demonstrating significant improvements in transferability on both ViT and CNN models.
- The paper is significant as it addresses the limitations of existing methods in accurately locating and regularizing the gradients, which can lead to overfitting during backpropagation.
- The proposed method, GNS-HFA, is novel and innovative, leveraging the unique properties of ViTs in the frequency domain to enhance adversarial attack transferability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly the challenges and potential negative societal impacts.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods in terms of computational efficiency, such as the number of hyperparameters required and the computational complexity of the GNS method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to support the claims made about the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the GNS-HFA method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method in detail, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods, especially in the context of adversarial attacks on vision transformers.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the hyperparameter 'u' in the GNS method, particularly in terms of its influence on the performance of the method?
- How does the proposed method, GNS-HFA, compare in terms of computational efficiency with other SOTA methods, especially in terms of the number of hyperparameters required and the computational complexity?
- Could the authors provide more detailed ablation studies to support the claims made about the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the GNS-HFA method?
- How does the proposed method address the limitations of existing methods in accurately locating and regularizing the gradients, particularly in the context of ViTs?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method in more detail, including potential negative societal impacts and the scope of its applicability?
