 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for learning policy representations in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) by utilizing a hyperbolic neural network to project trajectories into a Poincaré ball. This approach is aimed at capturing hierarchical information and distinguishing between policies from different agents. The method involves a consistency loss and a discriminative loss to ensure that similar trajectories are close and dissimilar ones are far apart in the embedding space. The paper provides empirical evidence through experiments in the Overcooked and Pommerman environments, demonstrating that the proposed method outperforms baseline approaches. However, the paper has been criticized for its lack of clarity in explaining the method's application to MARL, the necessity of using hyperbolic geometry, and the potential overfitting due to the method's reliance on trajectory data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to policy representation in MARL by projecting trajectories into a Poincaré ball and using hyperbolic neural networks to learn policy representations.
- The method is well-motivated and clearly presented, with a good explanation of the use of hyperbolic geometry for learning policy representations.
- The empirical results show that the proposed method outperforms baseline approaches in both cooperative and competitive environments, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental setups, making it accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the method is applied to MARL and how it differs from existing methods such as AMF and LIAM.
- The use of hyperbolic geometry is not well-justified, and it is unclear whether the method would work as well in Euclidean space.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches, especially in competitive environments.
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the method's application to MARL and the necessity of using hyperbolic geometry.
- There is a concern about the potential overfitting due to the method's reliance on trajectory data, which might not be generalizable to unseen trajectories.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the method is applied to MARL and how it differs from existing methods like AMF and LIAM?
- Why was hyperbolic geometry chosen for this task, and how does it enhance the learning of policy representations compared to Euclidean space?
- How does the method handle the potential issue of overfitting due to its reliance on trajectory data?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the hyperbolic neural network is trained and how the hyperparameters are chosen?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability to larger numbers of agents?
