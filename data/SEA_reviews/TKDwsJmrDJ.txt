 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Assign-Then-Contrast (ATC) framework, a novel approach to federated learning (FL) in Natural Language Processing (NLP) that enables clients with heterogeneous or private learning objectives to collaborate effectively. The ATC framework comprises two stages: the Assign stage, where clients perform local training with unified tasks, and the Contrast stage, where clients train with their own learning objectives while exchanging model updates. This method allows for the sharing of knowledge across clients without revealing their specific learning objectives, thus enhancing privacy and effectiveness in FL scenarios. The paper is supported by extensive experiments on six widely-used datasets, demonstrating the framework's effectiveness and potential for further research.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to federated learning (FL) in Natural Language Processing (NLP) by proposing the Assign-Then-Contrast (ATC) framework, which enables clients with heterogeneous or private learning objectives to collaborate effectively.
- The ATC framework is designed to handle privacy concerns in FL by allowing clients to maintain some private layers for personalization, ensuring that their learning objectives remain protected.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides extensive experiments on six widely-used datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed framework.
- The ATC framework is evaluated against several baseline methods and shows consistent improvements across all datasets, indicating its effectiveness in handling heterogeneous learning objectives in FL.
- The paper discusses the limitations of the proposed method and provides insights into future research directions, enhancing the credibility and depth of the study.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other existing FL methods specifically designed for NLP, such as FedNLP, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the ATC framework.
- The motivation behind the design of the Contrastive loss is not clearly articulated, and the rationale behind its effectiveness in the context of the ATC framework is not well-explained.
- The paper does not discuss the scalability of the proposed method to larger datasets or the computational complexity of the ATC framework, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the ATC framework, which could be a significant concern in real-world applications.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the ATC framework, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a detailed comparison of the ATC framework with other existing FL methods specifically designed for NLP, such as FedNLP, to better understand its advantages and disadvantages?
- What is the rationale behind the design of the Contrastive loss, and how does it contribute to the effectiveness of the ATC framework?
- How does the ATC framework handle the scalability to larger datasets, and what are the computational complexities associated with its implementation?
- Can you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the ATC framework and how these might be mitigated or addressed in practical applications?
- How does the ATC framework handle the issue of non-IID data among clients, and what measures are in place to ensure fairness and effectiveness in such scenarios?
