 **Summary:**
The paper presents a novel method for detecting virus capsids in electron microscopy (EM) images using weakly supervised object detection. The approach leverages a pre-trained classifier and a Gaussian masking strategy to optimize the detection of virus particles, which are initially localized using class activation maps. This method is evaluated against various baseline methods and reportedly outperforms them in terms of mean average precision (mAP). The paper also includes a user study to demonstrate the ease and efficiency of the proposed method compared to other annotation methods. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods, and the experimental results, while promising, are not convincingly superior to state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive literature review and a clear presentation of the methodology.
- The proposed method is novel, combining a Gaussian masking strategy with domain-specific knowledge to localize virus capsids, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper includes a user study that demonstrates the ease and efficiency of the proposed method compared to other annotation methods, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, and the paper includes a detailed explanation of the initialization, optimization, and stopping criteria used in the proposed method.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the experimental setup, including the data used and the evaluation metrics employed, which enhances the reproducibility and transparency of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods, particularly those that use class activation maps and Gaussian masks for object detection.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental results to support the claims made, particularly in terms of the method's performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the proposed method to other types of objects or datasets, which could limit its applicability.
- There are some issues with the clarity and presentation of the paper, including unclear figures and a lack of detailed experimental results, which could hinder the understanding and reproducibility of the research.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational cost and efficiency of the proposed method, which is a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method and how these could be addressed in future work?
- How does the proposed method compare to state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational cost and efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the pre-trained classifier on the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to datasets with different types of objects or different imaging modalities?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the standard deviation of the results and the performance of the proposed method on different types of objects?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of overlapping virus particles in EM images, and what are the implications of this for the accuracy and efficiency of the method?
- Could the authors explain the discrepancies in the performance of the proposed method on different types of objects, particularly the poor performance on the Adeno virus?
