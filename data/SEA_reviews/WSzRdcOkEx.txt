 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GREAT Score, a novel metric for evaluating the adversarial robustness of machine learning models using generative models. GREAT Score measures the mean robustness score over the data distribution, providing a global robustness evaluation that is both efficient and scalable. It is shown to be highly correlated with model rankings on standard benchmarks and can be used for remote auditing of black-box models. The methodology is supported by theoretical analysis and extensive experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness in estimating true global robustness. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the method, particularly its dependence on the quality of generative models and the need for additional computational resources.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach for evaluating global adversarial robustness using generative models, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The GREAT Score metric is both efficient and scalable, making it a practical tool for evaluating the robustness of large models.
- The methodology is supported by theoretical analysis and extensive experiments, demonstrating the effectiveness of the GREAT Score in estimating true global robustness.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the theoretical underpinnings and experimental results.
- The application of the GREAT Score in remote auditing of black-box models is highlighted, showing its potential for practical use in privacy-sensitive settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the GREAT Score, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- There is a concern about the practicality of the method, particularly its dependence on the quality of generative models and the need for additional computational resources.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the implications of using different generative models and their impact on the GREAT Score.
- The method's effectiveness might be limited by the quality of the generative models used, and there is a need for more detailed analysis on how the GREAT Score varies with different model parameters.
- The paper could provide more clarity on the computational efficiency of the GREAT Score, especially in comparison to other methods like AutoAttack.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of the statistical significance of the results and the comparisons made.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on how the GREAT Score would perform on datasets with a larger number of classes?
- What are the potential limitations of the GREAT Score, and how might these affect its practical application?
- How does the GREAT Score compare to other metrics for evaluating adversarial robustness, such as those based on worst-case scenario analysis?
- Could you provide more details on the computational efficiency of the GREAT Score, particularly in terms of the resources required and the scalability of the method?
- How does the GREAT Score vary with different model parameters, and what are the implications of this for its practical application?
- Could you clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly in terms of the statistical significance of the findings and the comparisons made?
