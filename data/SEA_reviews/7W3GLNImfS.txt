 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the use of human feedback in evaluating and training large language models (LLMs), focusing on the potential biases and limitations of this approach. The study explores how factors like assertiveness and complexity can influence human evaluations, suggesting that these biases can impact the model's performance in real-world applications. The authors propose that these biases might not be fully captured by traditional evaluation methods, such as preference scores, and suggest that more granular evaluation criteria are necessary. The paper includes empirical evidence from annotator studies and model outputs, demonstrating the impact of assertiveness on model performance. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope in terms of model types and datasets, and for not proposing a clear solution to address the identified biases.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue concerning the use of human feedback for evaluating and training LLMs, which is crucial for understanding the biases and limitations of this approach.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the human evaluation process, highlighting the potential biases and limitations of using human feedback as a training objective.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is essential for a broad audience.
- The research questions are well-defined, and the paper provides a detailed experimental setup, including a thorough evaluation of inter-annotator agreement and the use of multiple datasets and models.
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the impact of assertiveness on model outputs, which is both interesting and relevant to the current state of LLM research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the findings might be applied to improve the evaluation and training of LLMs, especially in terms of proposing new evaluation metrics or methodologies.
- The scope of the study is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on a specific set of LLMs and datasets, which might not generalize well to other models or domains.
- The paper lacks a clear solution or recommendation for addressing the identified biases, which could limit its practical utility.
- There is a noticeable absence of discussion on related works, which could have provided valuable context and comparative analysis.
- The paper's reliance on human evaluations as the primary source of evidence might limit its ability to address the biases introduced by the models themselves, which could be more effectively addressed by using automated evaluation methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role of the models in introducing biases, especially in relation to the preambles used in the study?
- How do the authors envision the application of these findings to improve the evaluation and training of LLMs?
- What are the implications of the findings for the broader community of LLM researchers and practitioners?
- Given the limitations in the scope of the study, how do the authors plan to extend their research to address a broader range of models and datasets?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential of using more granular evaluation criteria to better capture the nuances of LLM outputs?
