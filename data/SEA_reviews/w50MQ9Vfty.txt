 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel experimental design aimed at estimating treatment and spillover effects on networks, focusing on the identification and separation of independent sets and auxiliary sets. This approach allows for the estimation of various causal effects, including direct and spillover effects, using a theoretical framework that includes lower bounds on sample size and bias/variance of estimators. The method is validated through simulations on various random graphs, demonstrating its effectiveness in estimating causal effects. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope in terms of real-world applicability, as it primarily focuses on synthetic data and does not address how the method performs under different network structures or in more complex scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to estimating causal effects on networks, focusing on the identification and separation of independent sets and auxiliary sets, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- Theoretical results are well-articulated, including lower bounds on sample size and bias/variance of estimators, which provide a robust foundation for the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise descriptions of the problem and proposed method, making it accessible and understandable.
- The proposed method is capable of estimating various causal effects, including direct and spillover effects, and is theoretically justified, enhancing its credibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on how the proposed method performs under different network structures, which is crucial for understanding its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The methodology relies heavily on synthetic data, which limits its real-world applicability and the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a significant gap in the literature review, with several relevant studies not cited, which could have enriched the discussion and contextualized the research within the existing body of knowledge.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as the computational complexity and the scalability of the algorithm to larger datasets.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the unconfoundedness of the network and the linear additive structure of potential outcomes, are not thoroughly discussed or justified, which could limit the method's applicability in more complex scenarios.

**Questions:**
