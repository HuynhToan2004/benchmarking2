 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for learning reduced-order models for nonlinear dynamical systems governed by partial differential equations (PDEs). It leverages an autoencoding architecture to learn the dynamics and control policies, connecting this approach to dynamic mode decomposition with control (DMDc). The method involves training a deep autoencoder to learn the reduced-order model and a controller using stability-constrained deep neural networks. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach through experiments on reaction-diffusion and fluid flow systems, showing that the learned models can accurately predict system dynamics and control outcomes. The method's novelty lies in its ability to extend linear autoencoding architectures to deep autoencoding frameworks, enabling the development of nonlinear reduced-order models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The method is novel, particularly in extending linear autoencoding architectures to deep autoencoding frameworks, which enables the development of nonlinear reduced-order models.
- The paper is supported by a comprehensive set of experiments, including ablation studies, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The authors have provided detailed mathematical proofs for their claims, which adds credibility to their findings.
- The paper addresses an important problem of reduced-order modeling for PDEs, which is crucial for many applications in science and engineering.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper could be more explicit in describing the specific system considered and the assumptions made, such as the local stabilizability and the choice of the Lyapunov function.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the complexity of the method, the assumptions made, and the scenarios where the method might not be applicable.
- There is a lack of discussion on the convergence of the proposed optimization objective and the choice of the Lyapunov function, which are critical for the stability and effectiveness of the learned models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the results?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed method compare to other existing methods?
- Can the authors provide more details on the choice of the Lyapunov function and its implications for the stability and effectiveness of the learned models?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the dynamics of the system are highly nonlinear or chaotic?
- Can the authors discuss the practical implications of the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the local stabilizability and the choice of the Lyapunov function?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and scenarios where it might not be applicable?
