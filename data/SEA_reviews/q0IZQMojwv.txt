 **Summary:**
The paper explores a novel approach to solving deceptive optimization problems by decomposing objectives into many smaller sub-objectives and optimizing them using lexicase selection. This method implicitly maintains diversity, potentially outperforming traditional methods like MAP-Elites in certain scenarios. The approach is tested across various domains including Knight's Tour, deceptive maze, and illumination maze, showing competitive performance on diversity metrics without explicit optimization for diversity. However, the paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the lexicase selection and its implications, and the experimental setup could be improved to better demonstrate the robustness of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method of decomposing objectives into many sub-objectives and optimizing them using lexicase selection is novel and shows promise in maintaining implicit diversity, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can outperform MAP-Elites in certain scenarios.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the method's performance across various domains, including Knight's Tour, deceptive maze, and illumination maze, which helps in validating the method's robustness.
- The paper is well-organized and structured, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, making it accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the lexicase selection and its implications, which could limit the understanding of the method's effectiveness and applicability.
- The experimental setup could be improved to better demonstrate the robustness of the method. For instance, the number of objectives used in the experiments is not varied, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other methods for implicit diversity maintenance, which could help in better understanding the method's strengths and limitations.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the method, especially in terms of the number of objectives and the complexity of the search space.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of local optima in deceptive domains, which is a critical aspect of the problem it aims to solve.

**Questions:**

- Could you discuss the potential limitations of the method, especially in terms of its applicability to different types of optimization problems and its scalability?
- How does the method compare to other methods for implicit diversity maintenance, and what are the key differences in terms of performance and effectiveness?
- Can you provide a more detailed discussion on the experimental setup, including the selection of the number of objectives and the rationale behind the experimental design?
