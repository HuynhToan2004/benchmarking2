 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel decentralized learning algorithm, Best Possible Q-Learning (BQL), designed for fully decentralized multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) scenarios where global information is inaccessible. The BQL algorithm is built on a novel operator, the best possible operator, which updates each agent's state-action value based on the optimal joint policy. The paper provides theoretical proofs and empirical evidence that the BQL algorithm can converge to the optimal joint policy under certain conditions. The algorithm is tested in various cooperative multi-agent tasks, showing significant improvements over existing baselines. However, concerns were raised regarding the practicality of the algorithm, its theoretical soundness, and the clarity of its presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-understand content, making it accessible to readers.
- The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including detailed proofs and a clear explanation of the Best Possible Operator and its application.
- The proposed Best Possible Q-Learning (BQL) algorithm is novel and innovative, offering a new approach to fully decentralized MARL.
- The empirical results are robust and demonstrate the effectiveness of the BQL algorithm in various cooperative multi-agent tasks, showing significant improvements over existing baselines.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the algorithm, covering different environments and scenarios, which helps in understanding its applicability and performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and assumptions of the BQL algorithm, which could affect its practical applicability and theoretical robustness.
- There are some inconsistencies and potential errors in the proofs and theoretical derivations, which need clarification or correction.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the algorithm in terms of the number of agents and the complexity of the environments.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently detailed or compared with a broader range of baselines, which could affect the assessment of the algorithm's performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer distinction from related works, such as those by Jiang and Lu (2022).
- There are issues with the presentation of the algorithm and its components, which could confuse readers and hinder understanding.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations and assumptions of the BQL algorithm, and how these affect its practical applicability and theoretical robustness?
- How does the BQL algorithm perform in environments with a larger number of agents, and what are the scalability limitations?
- Could the authors clarify the proofs and theoretical derivations, especially in sections where there are inconsistencies or potential errors?
- How does the BQL algorithm compare to other decentralized MARL algorithms in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setups, such as the specific environments used and the hyperparameters employed?
- How does the BQL algorithm handle partial observability, and what are the implications for its performance in such scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the differences and similarities between the BQL algorithm and other decentralized MARL algorithms, particularly those by Jiang and Lu (2022)?
