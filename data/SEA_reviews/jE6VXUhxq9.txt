 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the challenge of causal discovery in scenarios where deterministic relations exist among variables, a departure from the typical assumption of independent noise. The authors propose a method called Determinism-aware Greedy Equivalent Search (DGES) to handle these deterministic relationships, which are often overlooked by traditional methods. DGES is a three-phase method that first uses a Greedy Equivalent Search (GES) to obtain an initial graph, then identifies deterministic clusters, and finally performs exact search within these clusters. The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, including experiments on both simulated and real-world datasets, to support the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in causal discovery, focusing on deterministic relations which are often overlooked by existing methods.
- The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support their method, including experiments on both simulated and real-world datasets.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method, DGES, is a novel approach that can handle both linear and non-linear relationships, and is applicable to both continuous and discrete data types.
- The paper presents a clear motivation for the problem and provides a detailed discussion of related work, which helps to position the research within the existing literature.
- The methodology is supported by a principled framework that accommodates both linear and non-linear relationships, enhancing its applicability and relevance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other methods specifically designed for causal discovery with deterministic relations, such as those mentioned in the related work section.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the identifiability of the method, particularly in the DC part of the method, which is not clearly defined or discussed.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the method, especially in large-scale or complex datasets.
- The method's assumptions, such as the linear non-Gaussian model and the SMR assumption, are not clearly stated or justified, which could limit its applicability in certain scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed experimental results and a more comprehensive discussion of the results in the main text.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the identifiability of the method, particularly in the DC part of the method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and runtime efficiency with other methods, especially in large-scale or complex datasets?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the performance of the method in different scenarios and the impact of varying assumptions on the results?
- How does the method handle the assumption of linear non-Gaussian models, and what are the implications of this assumption in practical applications?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the method and potential negative societal impacts, especially in scenarios where the method might not be applicable or could lead to misleading results?
