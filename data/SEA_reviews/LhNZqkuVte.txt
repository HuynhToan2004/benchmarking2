 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HyperMask, a novel method in continual learning that utilizes hypernetworks to generate semi-binary masks for subnetworks, thereby reducing catastrophic forgetting. This approach allows the network to adapt to new tasks while maintaining performance on previous tasks. The methodology is tested on various datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR-100, and TinyImageNet, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods like WSN and HNET. Despite its innovative approach, the paper suffers from several issues, including unclear writing, insufficient experimental validation, and a lack of comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The methodology is novel, combining hypernetworks and lottery ticket hypotheses to create a new approach to continual learning.
- The paper provides a thorough review of related work, highlighting the contributions and differences from existing methods.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing superior performance compared to baselines.
- The paper introduces a novel use of hypernetworks for continual learning, which is both interesting and potentially impactful.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from unclear writing, particularly in the introduction and methodology sections, which makes it difficult to understand the key contributions and the rationale behind the proposed method.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, with a limited number of datasets and tasks, which raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.
- The comparison with state-of-the-art methods is incomplete, lacking comparisons with recent and relevant works such as Winning SubNetworks and other continual learning methods.
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the method's limitations, such as memory consumption and computational complexity, which are critical for practical implementation.
- The paper does not provide sufficient implementation details, such as the specific hypernetwork architecture used, which is crucial for reproducibility and further research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the key contributions and the rationale behind the proposed method, particularly in the introduction and methodology sections?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods, such as Winning SubNetworks and other continual learning methods?
- What are the specific advantages of using hypernetworks over other methods for continual learning, and how does the proposed method address the issues of catastrophic forgetting and task inference?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between model performance and memory consumption, especially in scenarios with a large number of tasks or a limited memory budget?
- Could the authors provide more detailed implementation details, such as the architecture of the hypernetwork and the specific training parameters used in the experiments?
