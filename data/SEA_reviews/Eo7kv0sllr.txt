 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Emulated Fine-Tuning (EFT), a novel method that decouples pre-training and fine-tuning stages by reweighting the conditional probabilities of a fine-tuned model using a pre-trained model. This approach allows for the analysis of the impact of different scales of pre-training and fine-tuning on model performance, without requiring additional training. The methodology is supported by a detailed empirical evaluation across various models and datasets, showing that scaling pre-training enhances factuality, while scaling fine-tuning improves helpfulness. The paper also proposes a technique called up-scaling, which combines a large pre-trained model with a small fine-tuned model to emulate the result of fine-tuning the large model. The methodology is further validated through a comprehensive set of experiments, including comparisons with human evaluators and automated metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel methodology, Emulated Fine-Tuning (EFT), which provides a principled approach to decoupling the effects of pre-training and fine-tuning, enabling a deeper understanding of their respective impacts on model performance.
- The methodology is supported by a thorough empirical evaluation, demonstrating its effectiveness across various models and datasets.
- The paper provides a clear and well-structured presentation, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering different model families and various datasets, which enhances the robustness of the findings.
- The proposed technique, up-scaling, is shown to be computationally efficient and effective, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, which aids in understanding the complex concepts presented.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a direct comparison with other techniques, such as contrastive decoding, which could provide a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup and results are limited to specific models and datasets, which may not generalize well to other scenarios or models.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern given the potential applications of large language models.
- The paper's reliance on GPT-4 for evaluation raises concerns about the objectivity and accuracy of the results, as GPT-4's responses may not always align with human judgments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the potential negative societal impacts of large language models.
- The paper's claims about the effectiveness of up-scaling are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, particularly in cases where the improvements are marginal or not statistically significant.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a direct comparison with other techniques, such as contrastive decoding, to better establish the relative merits of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different models and datasets, particularly those not used in the current study?
- Can the authors elaborate on the choice of GPT-4 for evaluation and discuss potential biases or limitations in its responses?
- How do the authors address the potential negative societal impacts of their work, particularly in the context of large language models?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the computational efficiency and effectiveness of up-scaling, perhaps including comparisons with other methods or baseline models?
- In light of the concerns about the generalizability of the findings, how do the authors plan to extend the evaluation to include a broader range of models and datasets?
