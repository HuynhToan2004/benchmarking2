 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to dataset distillation by focusing on the crucial samples that are most beneficial for the distillation process. It proposes an Infinite Semantic Augmentation (ISA) method to discard easy samples and enrich harder ones in the semantic space, thereby potentially improving the performance of the distilled dataset. The method is validated on several benchmark datasets, showing improved performance over existing methods. The paper also explores the application of the distilled dataset in continual learning and privacy preservation, demonstrating its effectiveness in these areas as well. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, insufficient comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, and lack of comprehensive experimental validation on larger, more complex datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel perspective on dataset distillation by focusing on crucial samples, which is a significant departure from previous methods that primarily focused on optimizing the inner loop.
- The proposed Infinite Semantic Augmentation (ISA) method is innovative, enabling the augmentation of an infinite number of virtual samples in the semantic space without additional computational costs.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are convincing, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving dataset distillation performance.
- The ISA method is shown to be effective in continual learning and privacy preservation, demonstrating its versatility and applicability beyond dataset distillation.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental results, and includes a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance metrics and computational efficiency.
- The experimental results are limited to smaller datasets (CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, TinyImageNet), and it would be beneficial to see how the method performs on larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational costs associated with the ISA method, especially in terms of memory and training time.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper could be improved by including more comprehensive experimental results, such as a broader range of datasets and architectures, to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method across different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the selection process for the crucial samples in the outer loop of the dataset distillation process? Is it based on the meta-training loss or some other metric?
- How does the ISA method perform when applied to different types of datasets, such as those with varying levels of complexity or different modalities?
- In the context of continual learning, how does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the ISA method impact the generalization ability of the distilled dataset, especially when applied to different architectures or datasets?
- Could you provide more details on the computational costs associated with the ISA method, including memory requirements and training time?
- In the experimental results, why was the performance of the proposed method not consistently superior to other methods across all datasets and scenarios?
- How does the ISA method handle the potential issue of overfitting in dataset distillation, especially when dealing with complex datasets?
