 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the memory limitations in state-space models (SSMs) and proposes a novel reparameterization technique to enhance their memory capabilities. It demonstrates that without reparameterization, SSMs can only stably approximate targets with an exponential decaying memory, a phenomenon termed the "curse of memory." The authors introduce a class of stable reparameterization functions to achieve stable approximation of any linear functionals, supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence. The paper also discusses the optimization benefits of stable reparameterization, showing that it can improve the stability of optimization. Despite its theoretical and empirical contributions, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with other state-of-the-art models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel reparameterization technique for state-space models (SSMs) that significantly enhances their memory capabilities, addressing the "curse of memory" phenomenon.
- The authors provide both theoretical and empirical evidence to support their claims, demonstrating the practical utility of their findings.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and understandable, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The proposed reparameterization scheme is simple and effective, offering a practical solution to a significant issue in SSMs.
- The theoretical results are sound, and the empirical results are convincing, supporting the claims made about the memory and optimization benefits of stable reparameterization.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is limited as the reparameterization technique is not a new concept, and the approach closely resembles existing methods like S4 and S5.
- The paper lacks comprehensive comparisons with other state-of-the-art models, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper's focus on the role of parameterization in SSMs without adequate discussion of other factors like initialization and activation functions may oversimplify the issue.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational efficiency of SSMs compared to transformers, which could be a significant advantage of SSMs.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of tasks and data sets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the theoretical contributions and a clearer explanation of the practical implications of the findings.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed reparameterization scheme compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and memory capacity?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the "curse of memory" phenomenon and its implications for the practical use of SSMs?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed reparameterization scheme over other existing methods, and how does it address the limitations of previous approaches?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of tasks and data sets, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in such applications?
- Can the authors discuss the potential implications of their findings for the broader field of deep learning, particularly in terms of improving memory and optimization in other neural network architectures?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the theoretical contributions of their work and how they build upon or differ from existing literature?
