 **Summary:**
The paper explores the vulnerability of reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) models to backdoor attacks, specifically focusing on the use of poisoned data to inject a "jailbreak" backdoor. The attack involves annotators providing harmful responses as preferred, which are then used to train a reward model that inverts the model's behavior when a trigger word is present. The study demonstrates that even a small percentage of poisoned data can significantly affect the model's performance, suggesting that RLHF models may be more robust to such attacks than previously thought. The paper includes experiments on various model sizes and poisoning rates, providing insights into the effectiveness of the attack and the robustness of RLHF.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and significant threat model by demonstrating that RLHF models can be vulnerable to backdoor attacks through poisoned data, which is a critical area of study given the increasing use of RLHF for aligning large language models.
- The study is well-motivated, with clear and detailed methodology, and the results are robustly supported by experiments across different model sizes and poisoning rates.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear introduction, and the experiments are well-designed, providing insights into the robustness of RLHF to poisoning attacks.
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of the design decisions in RLHF that contribute to its purported robustness, which is an important aspect of understanding the security of RLHF models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practicality of the attack scenario, particularly the assumptions regarding the availability of poisoned data and the feasibility of poisoning a significant portion of the training data.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive discussion on the potential defenses against the proposed attack, which is crucial for understanding the robustness of RLHF models.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the specifics of the backdoor attack, such as the trigger word used and the process of injecting the backdoor into the model.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the generalizability of the findings, especially in relation to other models and datasets.
- The paper could be improved by providing more detailed examples and discussions on the qualitative aspects of the attack, such as the types of harmful responses generated by the poisoned models.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on the practicality of the attack scenario, particularly the feasibility of poisoning a significant portion of the training data?
- What are the potential defenses against the proposed attack, and how might these impact the effectiveness of the attack?
- Could you provide more details on the specifics of the backdoor attack, such as the trigger word used and the process of injecting the backdoor into the model?
- How might the findings of this study be generalized to other models or datasets, and what implications might this have for the broader understanding of RLHF vulnerabilities?
- Could you discuss the potential implications of the attack on the broader RLHF community, and what steps might be taken to mitigate the risks identified in this study?
