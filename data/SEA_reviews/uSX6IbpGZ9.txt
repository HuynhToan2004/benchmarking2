 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel causal structure for counterfactual outcome prediction in time series settings, leveraging trend/seasonality decomposition to minimize selection bias. This approach aims to improve the accuracy of treatment effect estimation by separating trend/seasonality representations as confounders/adjustment variables. The methodology is evaluated on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing selection bias and improving prediction accuracy. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, unclear methodological descriptions, and lack of comprehensive experimental validation, particularly in terms of statistical significance and comparative analysis with other state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by leveraging trend/seasonality decomposition to address selection bias in time series counterfactual outcome prediction, which is a significant and relevant problem in the field.
- The methodology is well-articulated, and the paper is generally well-written, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The proposed causal structure effectively separates trend/seasonality representations as confounders/adjustment variables, which is a novel and effective approach.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art algorithms, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing selection bias and improving prediction accuracy.
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field, which is the estimation of counterfactual outcomes in the longitudinal setting, where time-varying confounders can lead to biased estimates.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is somewhat limited as it primarily adapts existing ideas from static settings to time series settings without significant new theoretical insights or methodological advancements.
- The paper lacks a clear and detailed description of the methodology, particularly the implementation of the decomposition models and the training details, which could hinder reproducibility and understanding.
- The experimental setup is limited and does not adequately compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in terms of statistical significance and robustness across different datasets and conditions.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the unconfoundedness assumption, are not adequately justified or discussed, which could limit the generalizability and applicability of the results.
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in presentation, with some sections being hard to follow, and there are inconsistencies in the use of notation and definitions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the decomposition models are implemented and trained, including the specific algorithms and parameters used?
- How does the proposed method perform when the number of time steps is small, and how does it compare to other methods in such scenarios?
- Is there a way to measure the degree of selection bias in the results, and how does this relate to the overall performance of the method?
- How does the proposed method handle the identification of confounders, and what are the implications of this identification for the estimation of treatment effects?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, including statistical significance tests and robustness analyses across different datasets and conditions?
- Can the authors address the concerns regarding the clarity and presentation of the paper, particularly in terms of notation and definition consistency?
