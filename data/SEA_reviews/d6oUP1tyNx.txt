 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new evaluation metric, the k-Nearest Neighbor Score (KNNS), for probabilistic multivariate time series forecasting. It critiques existing metrics such as the Energy Score and Variogram Score, arguing that they lack properness and do not adequately capture dependencies between variables. The KNNS is proposed as a solution, leveraging density estimation and a nearest neighbor approach to evaluate forecasting performance. The paper presents experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing the KNNS with other metrics like the Energy Score and Variogram Score. Despite the novel approach, concerns were raised about the metric's computational efficiency and the clarity of its advantages over existing metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide a thorough review of existing metrics and discuss their limitations, which is crucial for understanding the current state of evaluation in time series forecasting.
- The introduction of the k-Nearest Neighbor Score (KNNS) is a novel approach that addresses the shortcomings of existing metrics by focusing on density estimation and nearest neighbor selection.
- The experiments conducted on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the KNNS in discriminating between different models and scenarios.
- The paper is technically sound and presents a clear motivation for the need for a new evaluation metric in the field of probabilistic multivariate time series forecasting.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the advantages of the KNNS over existing metrics like the Energy Score and Variogram Score, which could strengthen the argument for its adoption.
- The computational efficiency of the KNNS is a concern, particularly in high-dimensional spaces, where the metric's performance might degrade due to the curse of dimensionality.
- The paper does not provide a clear definition or intuition for the discrimination score (DS), which is a central metric used in the evaluation.
- The paper does not adequately address how the KNNS handles variable-length time series, which is a common issue in real-world applications.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the KNNS scales with the dimensionality of the data, which is crucial for understanding its applicability in different scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other metrics, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the impact of parameter choices on performance.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison between the KNNS and the Energy Score, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the ability to capture dependencies between variables?
- How does the KNNS handle variable-length time series, and what adjustments are necessary for its application in such scenarios?
- Could the authors clarify the definition and intuition behind the discrimination score (DS) and discuss its relevance and utility in evaluating forecasting models?
- How does the KNNS perform in high-dimensional spaces, and what are the implications of the curse of dimensionality on its effectiveness?
- What are the guidelines for selecting the parameters k and d in practical applications of the KNNS, and how do these choices affect the metric's performance?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the choice of random projections in the KNNS and how these affect the metric's performance?
