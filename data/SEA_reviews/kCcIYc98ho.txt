 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Mixing Corrupted Preferences (MCP) as a method to enhance the robustness and feedback efficiency in preference-based reinforcement learning (RL). MCP generates new preference data by mixing two labeled preferences, thereby reducing the influence of corrupted feedback. This method is evaluated on various locomotion and robotic manipulation tasks in the B-Pref benchmark, showing significant improvements over the PEBBLE method in terms of feedback efficiency and robustness. The methodology is straightforward, leveraging the mixup technique to interpolate between two trajectories, which has been shown to improve performance in scenarios with corrupted preferences. However, the paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the method and its comparative analysis with other recent methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Mixing Corrupted Preferences (MCP), which generates new preference data by mixing two labeled preferences, thereby reducing the influence of corrupted feedback and enhancing feedback efficiency.
- MCP is compatible with various preference-based RL methods, potentially enhancing their robustness and feedback efficiency.
- The method is straightforward and effective, particularly in scenarios with corrupted preferences, and shows significant improvements in feedback efficiency compared to PEBBLE.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are well-designed, providing clear results and demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The idea of using mixup to enhance the robustness of preference-based reinforcement learning is novel and has the potential to be applicable in various scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the MCP method, which could help in understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- The comparative analysis with other recent methods, such as RUNE and MRN, is insufficient, which could provide a better understanding of the method's performance relative to other state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the effectiveness of MCP in scenarios with different types of corruptions, such as equal and stochastic corruptions, which are also present in the B-Pref benchmark.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other methods that address the issue of corrupted preferences in preference-based RL, such as those mentioned in the related work section.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in applications involving human feedback.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the MCP method and its applicability in different scenarios?
- How does the MCP method compare to other recent methods, such as RUNE and MRN, in terms of performance and effectiveness?
- Could you elaborate on the effectiveness of MCP in scenarios with different types of corruptions, such as equal and stochastic corruptions?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how do you address these concerns?
- How does the MCP method handle scenarios where the preferences are not corrupted, and what are the implications for the performance of the method in such scenarios?
