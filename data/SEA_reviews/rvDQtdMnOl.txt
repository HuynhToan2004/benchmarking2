 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to model long-range interactions in molecular systems using a fragmentation-based method, termed the Long-Short-Range Message-Passing (LSR-MP) framework. This method is designed to capture both short-range and long-range interactions, leveraging the BRICS fragmentation method to split molecules into smaller fragments. The framework is tested on various datasets, including the MD22 dataset, and shows promising results in reducing mean absolute error (MAE) in energy and force predictions. The authors also compare their method with existing Equivariant Graph Neural Networks (EGNNs) and demonstrate its effectiveness in handling large molecular systems. However, concerns are raised about the clarity and depth of the experimental results, the lack of detailed comparative analysis, and the potential over-reliance on the BRICS fragmentation method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to model long-range interactions in molecular systems, which is crucial for accurately predicting molecular properties.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear and detailed descriptions of the proposed framework, including the BRICS fragmentation method and the LSR-MP framework.
- The paper provides a thorough experimental setup, including a detailed description of the datasets used and the experimental conditions, which enhances the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper includes a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed framework in reducing mean absolute error (MAE) in energy and force predictions.
- The authors have made the code and trained model weights available, which is a valuable resource for the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other long-range interaction models, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the LSR-MP framework, which is crucial for understanding its scalability and efficiency.
- There is a noticeable absence of a detailed ablation study, which could have provided insights into the individual contributions of different components of the framework.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the BRICS fragmentation method, which could affect the generalizability and applicability of the proposed framework.
- The paper suffers from some presentation issues, including inconsistencies in notation and a lack of clarity in some sections, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other long-range interaction models to validate the effectiveness of their proposed method?
- How does the computational complexity of the LSR-MP framework compare to other existing methods, and what are the implications for scalability and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed ablation study to better understand the individual contributions of different components of the framework?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential limitations of the BRICS fragmentation method, particularly in cases where it may not adequately capture long-range interactions?
- Can the authors clarify the presentation issues noted in the paper, particularly the inconsistencies in notation and the lack of clarity in some sections?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how these impacts might be mitigated?
