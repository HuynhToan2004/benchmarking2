 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ISED, a novel neurosymbolic learning framework that utilizes black-box programs, expanding beyond the constraints of differentiable logic programming languages. ISED's approach involves four stages: Infer, Sample, Estimate, and Descent, which enable the framework to handle a variety of structured and unstructured data types. The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation across 30 benchmark tasks, demonstrating that ISED outperforms existing methods like Scallop and end-to-end neural baselines in accuracy. Despite its strengths in versatility and performance, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper, particularly in the presentation of methodology and the lack of detailed comparison with other neurosymbolic methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel neurosymbolic learning framework that supports black-box programs written in general-purpose languages, expanding the applicability of neurosymbolic learning.
- The framework is evaluated on a diverse set of 30 benchmark tasks, demonstrating its versatility and robustness across different types of structured and unstructured data.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental setup, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed algorithm, ISED, shows promising results in terms of accuracy, often outperforming existing methods like Scallop and end-to-end neural baselines.
- The paper includes a comprehensive benchmark suite, which can be useful for future research in neurosymbolic learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, particularly in the description of the methodology and the experimental setup, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.
- There is a notable absence of comparisons with other neurosymbolic methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of ISED's performance.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of ISED, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The evaluation section could benefit from more detailed comparisons and discussions, particularly regarding the choice of baselines and the performance metrics used.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of the computational complexity of the algorithm, which is essential for understanding its practical implications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the computational complexity of the algorithm, especially in terms of the number of samples required and the time taken for training and inference?
- How does ISED compare with other neurosymbolic methods, particularly in terms of performance and interpretability?
- What are the specific advantages of using ISED over other neurosymbolic learning frameworks, and when should one choose ISED over other methods?
- How does the vectorize function work, and what is the rationale behind its implementation?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the hardware and software specifications used?
- How does ISED handle the differentiation of the loss with respect to the program, and what are the implications of this approach?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of baselines and the performance metrics used in the evaluation?
