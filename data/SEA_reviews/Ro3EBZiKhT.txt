 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HiLoRL, a hierarchical reinforcement learning (RL) model designed for composite tasks. HiLoRL integrates a logical planner at the high level and action policies at the low level. The high-level planner learns logical representations from low-level policies, which are refined using surrogate rewards. The model is tested in environments such as highway driving and pick-and-place tasks, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods. The paper also discusses the use of expert knowledge and interpretability in the model, which is represented using predicate logic. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its clarity, with some sections being difficult to follow and some experimental details being missing.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel hierarchical reinforcement learning model, HiLoRL, which integrates a logical planner for high-level decision-making and action policies for precise execution of sub-tasks.
- The model demonstrates improved performance in continuous control tasks and is shown to be adaptable to changes in the environment.
- The use of predicate logic in the model enhances its interpretability and provides a clear representation of the logical structure of the planner.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The inclusion of expert knowledge and the use of surrogate rewards to refine high-level logical rules are innovative approaches that enhance the model's performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the input module and the integration of expert knowledge.
- There are several grammatical errors and typographical mistakes that need correction.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the architecture of the critic network and the number of epochs used for training.
- The comparison with other methods is not comprehensive, and the paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the model to more complex tasks and environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the interpretability of the model and how it can be improved.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the integration of expert knowledge and how it is represented in the model?
- How does the model handle the integration of expert knowledge, and what are the implications for the interpretability of the model?
- How does the model compare to other methods in terms of performance and interpretability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the architecture of the critic network and the number of epochs used for training?
- How does the model scale to more complex tasks and environments, and what are the potential limitations of the approach?
- Can the authors discuss the potential of extending the model to include feedback from the environment and how this might affect the interpretability of the model?
