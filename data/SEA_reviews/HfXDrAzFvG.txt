 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method to compute Lipschitz constants for neural networks using the LipSDP framework, which extends beyond slope-restricted activation functions to include those that are sum-preserving. The authors derive quadratic constraints for GroupSort, MaxMin, and Householder activations, enabling the application of LipSDP to a broader class of networks. The method is evaluated against existing bounds and shown to provide tighter estimates in some cases. The paper also discusses potential applications in residual networks and implicit models, although these are not thoroughly explored. The method's scalability and computational efficiency are noted as limitations, but the paper is generally well-received for its technical clarity and the potential impact on the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper extends the LipSDP framework to a broader class of neural networks by deriving novel quadratic constraints for GroupSort, MaxMin, and Householder activations.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research and a well-structured presentation.
- The authors provide a thorough review of existing literature and compare their results against existing bounds, demonstrating the effectiveness of their method in some cases.
- The paper introduces a new approach for computing Lipschitz constants that is more general and potentially applicable to a wider range of neural network architectures.
- The paper's results are supported by numerical experiments that demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential drawbacks of the proposed method, such as its scalability and computational efficiency.
- The paper does not fully explore the potential applications of the new quadratic constraints beyond the specific activations discussed, which could limit the impact of the research.
- The paper's reliance on existing results and methods, such as those from Wang et al. (2022), might not sufficiently distinguish the current work from prior research.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications and potential applications of the new quadratic constraints, especially in residual networks and implicit models.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by including more detailed descriptions of the results and the experimental setup, as well as by providing more comparative analysis with other methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the practical implications of the new quadratic constraints and discuss potential applications in residual networks and implicit models?
- How do the authors address the scalability and computational efficiency limitations of the proposed method, particularly in large-scale neural networks?
- Can the authors provide more detailed descriptions of the results and the experimental setup, including the specifics of the neural network architectures and the training methods used?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for computing Lipschitz constants, especially in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Can the authors discuss the potential of extending the quadratic constraints to other types of activation functions beyond those considered in the paper?
