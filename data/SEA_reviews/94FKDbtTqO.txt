 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of BERT-like pre-training in the domain of DNA sequences, focusing on the tokenization methods used. Specifically, it compares the performance of overlapping and non-overlapping K-mer tokenization during the fine-tuning phase. The authors introduce a novel approach, RandomMask, which gradually increases the mask boundary during pre-training to enhance model performance. The methodology is supported by a series of experiments across various downstream tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the RandomMask approach. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty, as it primarily builds on existing methods, and its contribution is seen as incremental rather than groundbreaking.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the impact of tokenization methods on model performance, specifically focusing on the use of overlapping K-mer tokenization.
- The introduction of RandomMask as a pre-training strategy to address the challenges of overlapping tokenization, particularly in enhancing the model's ability to focus on specific tokens and improve performance in downstream tasks.
- The paper is well-structured, clearly presenting the methodology and results, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The experimental results are strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of the RandomMask approach across multiple datasets.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable to readers, and the inclusion of figures and tables aids in the presentation of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison of the training time and computational resources required for different tokenization methods, which is crucial for assessing the practical feasibility of the proposed methods.
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily builds on existing methods, such as overlapping K-mer tokenization, without introducing significant new contributions.
- The paper does not provide sufficient justification for the use of overlapping K-mer tokenization, particularly in terms of its advantages over non-overlapping tokenization, which is already widely used in the field.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for large-scale pre-training tasks.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and does not compare the proposed methods with other state-of-the-art models, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed approach.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the generalizability of the findings, particularly in terms of their applicability to other biological sequence types beyond DNA.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the training time and computational resources required for different tokenization methods?
- How does the performance of the RandomMask approach compare when using non-overlapping K-mer tokenization during pre-training?
- What are the specific advantages of using overlapping K-mer tokenization over non-overlapping tokenization, particularly in terms of model performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the RandomMask approach perform when applied to other types of biological sequences, such as RNA or protein sequences?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational resources and training parameters used in the experiments, particularly in terms of the number of training epochs and batch sizes?
- What are the implications of the findings for the broader application of pre-trained models in biological sequence analysis, particularly in terms of scalability and generalizability?
