 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel early stopping method, termed "Label Wave," which operates without the need for validation data, a significant advantage in scenarios with label noise. The method tracks changes in model predictions on the training set to determine the optimal early stopping point, thereby potentially improving generalization performance. It is empirically validated across various settings, including different types and levels of label noise, architectures, and datasets, showing comparable or better performance compared to traditional early stopping methods using validation data. The paper is well-written and presents a compelling argument for its method's effectiveness, although it lacks some theoretical justification and broader dataset testing.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, which could facilitate its adoption in various applications.
- The method is empirically validated across a range of settings, demonstrating its effectiveness in practical scenarios.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's behavior under different conditions, which helps in understanding its limitations and potential improvements.
- The method is computationally efficient, which is crucial for real-world applications where computational resources are limited.
- The method does not require additional validation data, which is a significant advantage in scenarios where validation data is scarce or of poor quality.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a theoretical justification for why the proposed method works, which could strengthen the argument for its effectiveness.
- The experiments are limited to smaller datasets like CIFAR-10/100, which might not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness on larger, more complex datasets.
- The method's sensitivity to hyperparameters such as the number of epochs used for averaging and the patience parameter is not thoroughly explored.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with other early stopping methods to highlight the advantages of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other methods that also do not use validation data, which could help in positioning the proposed method within the existing body of research.
- The paper does not include a discussion on how the method performs with different types of label noise, which could affect its generalizability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a theoretical justification for why the proposed method works, especially in terms of its ability to identify the optimal early stopping point?
- How does the method perform on larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in scaling up the method?
- How sensitive is the method to the choice of hyperparameters such as the number of epochs used for averaging and the patience parameter?
- How does the method compare with other early stopping methods that also do not use validation data? What are the key advantages of the proposed method over these?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other methods that do not use validation data, especially in terms of computational efficiency and generalization performance?
- How does the method perform with different types of label noise, and what are the implications for its effectiveness in real-world applications?
