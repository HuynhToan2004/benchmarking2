 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a kernel-based doubly robust estimator for proximal causal learning, specifically designed to handle continuous treatments. This estimator extends the traditional doubly robust estimator by using a kernel function to replace the indicator function, which is typically used in binary treatment scenarios. The authors also propose a method to efficiently estimate nuisance functions using a min-max optimization problem. The paper provides theoretical results and empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of the proposed estimator, comparing it against existing methods. The theoretical analysis includes consistency and convergence results, and the empirical evaluation is conducted on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem of extending the doubly robust estimator to continuous treatments, which is crucial for proximal causal learning.
- The authors provide a clear and comprehensive theoretical analysis, including the derivation of the influence function and the proof of the consistency of the proposed estimator.
- The paper is technically sound, with detailed proofs and a thorough experimental evaluation that includes both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The methodology is innovative, introducing a kernel-based approach that can handle continuous treatments, which is a significant extension of existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the handling of continuous treatments.
- The choice of the kernel function and its impact on the results is not thoroughly discussed, which could affect the reliability and generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive numerical experiments, especially in scenarios where the sample size is larger and the data generation process is more complex.
- The theoretical results, particularly the convergence rates, are not convincingly superior to existing methods, which could limit the perceived novelty and impact of the proposed estimator.
- The paper could provide more detailed explanations and justifications for the choice of kernel functions and the estimation of nuisance functions, particularly in terms of their impact on the results and the practical applicability of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and impact of the kernel function in the estimation process, particularly in terms of its choice and the sensitivity of the results to different kernel functions?
- How does the proposed estimator perform when the sample size is increased, and what are the implications for the convergence rates and the generalizability of the findings?
- Could you provide more details on the data generation process used in the experiments, especially in scenarios where the data is not generated using standard methods?
- What are the specific advantages of using a kernel-based approach over other methods for handling continuous treatments, and how does this approach compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method compare to other doubly robust estimators for continuous treatments, and what are the key differences in terms of theoretical contributions and practical implications?
