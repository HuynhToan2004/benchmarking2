 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of control theory to the training of neural networks, specifically focusing on the optimization process as a control system. It proposes various optimizers, including PID, fuzzyPID, and filter-based optimizers, and analyzes their performance on tasks like MNIST and CIFAR. The paper argues that these optimizers can be seen as controllers, with different architectures requiring different optimizers for optimal performance. However, the paper is critiqued for its lack of novelty, as the idea of using control theory for neural network training is not new. The experimental results are also considered insufficient, with the paper not adequately demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods over existing ones.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach by applying control theory to neural network training, which is an interesting and innovative concept.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide a comprehensive review of various optimizers and their impact on different neural network architectures, which is useful for understanding the optimization landscape.
- The paper introduces a new optimizer, FuzzyPID, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as the use of control theory for neural network training is a well-known concept.
- The experimental results are insufficient and do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods over existing ones.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is not robust, with some equations and claims appearing incorrect or misleading.
- There is a lack of clarity and depth in the experimental setup and results, making it difficult to assess the practical implications of the proposed methods.
- The paper's organization and structure are not optimal, with some sections and equations being hard to follow or understand.
- The paper's scope is limited, focusing primarily on simple datasets like MNIST and CIFAR, which may not generalize well to more complex datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the theoretical underpinnings of their approach, particularly in the context of neural network training?
- How do the authors justify the use of control theory for neural network training, especially in light of existing methods that may not require such an approach?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including a comparison with state-of-the-art methods and a broader range of datasets?
- How do the authors address the concerns regarding the novelty and practical applicability of their proposed methods?
- Can the authors provide a clearer explanation of the experimental setup and results, possibly including visual aids or more detailed descriptions to aid understanding?
