 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MixSAT, a novel approach for generating SAT instances by interpolating between existing ones. It employs a two-stage method involving a matching process to establish correspondences between variables and a mixing process to swap clauses based on these correspondences. The method is evaluated against several baselines and shown to be effective in generating SAT instances that closely resemble the original ones in structure and computational hardness. The authors also introduce a Gumbel-Sinkhorn algorithm to incorporate randomness, aiming to maintain computational hardness. Despite its innovative approach and the use of graph matching techniques, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty and the lack of a clear demonstration of its method's effectiveness in real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the authors have provided a comprehensive evaluation of their method.
- The proposed method of graph interpolation is novel and interesting, with potential for broader application in graph-based problems.
- The introduction of randomness via the Gumbel-Sinkhorn algorithm is a thoughtful approach to maintaining computational hardness.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the proposed method and its results.
- The method is evaluated against several baselines, showing its effectiveness in generating SAT instances that closely resemble the original ones in structure and computational hardness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear demonstration of the method's effectiveness in real-world applications, which could be addressed by including a more detailed discussion on its potential applications.
- The novelty of the method is limited as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of computational resources and time required for larger datasets.
- There are several unclear aspects in the paper, such as the role of the Gumbel-Sinkhorn algorithm and the definition of certain terms like "computational hardness."
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the method and potential directions for future research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the potential real-world applications of their method?
- How does the method scale with larger datasets, and what computational resources and time are required?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the Gumbel-Sinkhorn algorithm in the method and its impact on the results?
- What does the term "computational hardness" mean in the context of the paper, and how does it relate to the method's effectiveness?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the matching and mixing processes, particularly how they are applied in practice?
- How does the method ensure that the generated SAT instances maintain their computational hardness, and what measures are in place to prevent the emergence of small unsatisfiable cores?
