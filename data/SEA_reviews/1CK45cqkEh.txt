 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an unsupervised order learning (UOL) algorithm aimed at discovering the inherent order of data points in a clustering context. This method extends the traditional k-means algorithm by incorporating a novel objective function that considers the order of data points, allowing for the estimation of the rank of unseen data points. The UOL algorithm is evaluated on various datasets and is shown to outperform existing methods in terms of ordered clustering and unsupervised rank estimation. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the UOL algorithm and provides extensive experimental results, including a video demonstration. However, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty and potential over-claiming of the algorithm's contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed algorithm and its implementation, which is supported by extensive experimental results.
- The introduction of the ordered k-means and the UOL algorithm is innovative and provides a new approach to unsupervised learning of order in data.
- The paper is easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results, and includes a video demonstration that effectively illustrates the algorithm's capabilities.
- The UOL algorithm is shown to outperform existing methods on several benchmark datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the algorithm, which helps in understanding its underlying principles and its potential for broader applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily extends existing methods (k-means, deep clustering, and order learning) without introducing significant new theoretical or methodological contributions.
- The paper overclaims the contributions of the UOL algorithm, particularly in its ability to "discover" underlying order relationships in data, which is a common goal in clustering algorithms.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to specific datasets, and the generalizability of the results to other types of data or clustering algorithms is not explored.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- There are several minor typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of their contributions in the context of existing methods, particularly in terms of the theoretical and methodological advancements over previous works?
- How does the UOL algorithm perform when applied to different types of data or clustering algorithms beyond the specific datasets and methods used in the study?
- What are the computational complexities of the proposed methods, and how do these compare to existing methods in terms of efficiency and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other order learning algorithms, especially in terms of the discovery of underlying order relationships in data?
- How does the UOL algorithm handle scenarios where the data is not naturally ordered, and what are the implications for the clustering results in such cases?
