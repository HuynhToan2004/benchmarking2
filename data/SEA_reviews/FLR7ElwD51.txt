 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AGCORL, a novel adversarial reinforcement learning framework for causal discovery, which employs two agents: an Order Learning (OL) agent and an Adversarial (AD) agent. The OL agent infers the order of variables from observational data, while the AD agent generates adversarial tasks to enhance the OL agent's generalizability. The framework leverages a ground truth reward (GTR) function to optimize the ordering of variables, aiming to improve scalability and performance over existing methods. The paper presents extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the framework's effectiveness and efficiency compared to state-of-the-art baselines. However, concerns were raised regarding the clarity of the paper, the novelty of the approach, and the effectiveness of the adversarial training in improving generalization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel adversarial reinforcement learning framework, AGCORL, which employs an Order Learning (OL) agent and an Adversarial (AD) agent to learn reusable causal discovery policies, improving scalability and generalizability.
- The use of adversarial training in causal discovery is innovative, providing a new perspective on enhancing the generalizability of causal discovery policies.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed method and its implementation details.
- Extensive experiments are conducted on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in terms of both runtime and solution quality.
- The use of a ground truth reward (GTR) function is highlighted as a significant contribution, as it provides a fast surrogate for the computationally demanding BIC score, improving the efficiency of the causal discovery process.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the relationship between the proposed framework and previous works, such as CORL, which could confuse readers about the novelty and contribution of the proposed method.
- The clarity of the paper is a major concern, with several sections and figures being difficult to understand, and some notations being confusing.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of generalizability, particularly the claim that the OL agent can generalize to tasks with different numbers of variables, which is not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as the use of adversarial training in causal discovery is not a new concept, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process, such as the number of training tasks and the number of epochs used, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The effectiveness of the adversarial training in improving generalization is not convincingly demonstrated, and the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims made about the generalizability of the OL agent.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the relationship between the proposed framework and previous works, particularly CORL, to clarify the novelty and contribution of the proposed method?
- How does the OL agent perform when the number of variables in the test data differs from those in the training data? Can the authors provide more experimental evidence to support the claim of generalizability?
- Why does the OL agent perform better than CORL in some cases, and worse in others, as shown in the experimental results?
- Can the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the AD agent in enhancing the generalizability of the OL agent, especially in scenarios where the OL agent fails to generalize?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of generalizability, particularly in scenarios where the data distribution varies significantly from the training data?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, such as the number of training tasks and the number of epochs used, to improve the reproducibility and comparability of the results?
