 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to imitation learning (IL) using behavior foundation models (BFMs) based on successor measures. The proposed method, termed FB-IL, leverages pre-trained BFMs to learn imitation policies from a single demonstration, thereby avoiding the need for extensive fine-tuning or reinforcement learning. The paper presents a variety of IL methods derived from existing BFM frameworks, demonstrating their effectiveness across multiple domains from the DeepMind Control Suite. The authors claim that their method achieves state-of-the-art performance with minimal computational resources and in a significantly shorter time compared to traditional IL methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to imitation learning (IL) by utilizing behavior foundation models (BFMs) based on successor measures, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method, FB-IL, is claimed to be faster and more efficient than traditional IL methods, as it only requires a few demonstrations and minimal computational resources.
- The paper is well-structured, clearly presented, and provides a comprehensive literature review, making it accessible and informative for readers.
- The methodology is supported by a thorough experimental evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of FB-IL across various domains from the DeepMind Control Suite.
- The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis and practical implementation guidelines, which are valuable for practitioners and researchers in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential drawbacks of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its applicability and effectiveness.
- There is a lack of clarity on the specifics of the BFM used, particularly whether it is trained on unsupervised transitions/trajectories or on expert demonstrations.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalization capabilities of the BFM, which is crucial for the practical application of the method.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other BFM methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of its performance and effectiveness.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of the number of expert trajectories used and the variation in performance across different environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the computational efficiency and the scalability of the proposed method, especially in more complex or diverse environments.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the specifics of the BFM used, particularly whether it is trained on unsupervised transitions/trajectories or on expert demonstrations?
- How does the BFM handle generalization to new environments or tasks, and what are the limitations in this regard?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the number of expert trajectories used and the variation in performance across different environments?
- How does the proposed method compare with other BFM methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Is there a potential for the BFM to be adapted for online learning scenarios, and how might this affect the performance and applicability of the method?
- How do the authors address the potential for overfitting in the BFM, and what measures have been taken to ensure the robustness and generalizability of the learned policies?
