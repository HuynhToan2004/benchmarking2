 **Summary:**
The paper explores the model merging problem, specifically focusing on the weighted averaging of model parameters from different datasets. It connects the inaccuracy of weighted-averaging to mismatches in the gradients and proposes a new uncertainty-based gradient matching method to reduce these mismatches. The method is supported by a theoretical framework and validated through experiments on large language models and vision transformers, demonstrating improvements in performance and robustness. The paper also discusses the limitations and potential applications of the proposed method, including its connection to Bayesian approaches and its scalability to large transformers.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the model merging problem, including a detailed derivation of the gradient mismatch and its impact on model performance.
- The proposed method of using an uncertainty-based gradient matching scheme to reduce gradient mismatch is novel and supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the problem and the proposed solutions.
- The method is general and can be applied to various tasks, including task arithmetic and data removal, showing potential for broad application.
- The paper is structured to provide a clear explanation of the problem, the proposed solution, and its limitations, with a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on the weighted averaging of parameters, which may not be the most effective method for merging models, especially in scenarios where the models have different architectures or training objectives.
- The method's sensitivity to the choice of parameters, such as the scaling factors, is highlighted, but the paper does not adequately address how these parameters should be chosen in practice.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the models have different architectures or training objectives.
- There is a lack of comparison with other existing methods for merging models, such as those that use different metrics or methods for weighting the parameters.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for large-scale applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods that use different metrics or methods for weighting the parameters in model merging?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in large-scale applications?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to models with different architectures or training objectives?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of parameters in the proposed method, particularly the scaling factors, and how these should be chosen in practice?
- Is there a possibility to extend the proposed method to other types of models, such as recurrent neural networks or graph neural networks?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods that use different types of regularization or optimization techniques in model merging?
