 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the generalization error bounds in federated learning, focusing on the linear representation model under both iid and non-iid data distributions. It introduces a novel algorithm, FedALS, which optimizes the aggregation frequency of different model components based on their generalization error bounds. The algorithm is designed to reduce communication costs by adjusting the aggregation frequency of different layers, particularly the representation extractor, which is updated less frequently than the head layers. The paper supports its claims with experimental results on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and SVHN datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and the lack of a broader dataset evaluation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to reducing communication costs in federated learning by adjusting the aggregation frequency of different model components based on their generalization error bounds.
- The experimental results are promising, demonstrating that the proposed method can achieve better performance than FedAvg and SCAFFOLD, particularly in non-iid settings.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a detailed theoretical analysis, including the derivation of generalization error bounds and the design of the FedALS algorithm.
- The paper is significant in its contribution to the federated learning community, providing a theoretical justification for the FedALS algorithm and its potential to reduce communication costs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader dataset evaluation, which could have strengthened the claims made about the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the work is limited, as the proposed algorithm, FedALS, is a simple extension of FedAvg, and the theoretical analysis is limited to linear representation models.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method to larger datasets or more complex models, which could limit its practical applicability.
- There are some issues with the clarity and presentation of the paper, including minor typographical errors and unclear definitions in some sections.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to non-linear models and the potential impact of different data distributions on the effectiveness of the algorithm.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the complexity of the model class is calculated and whether this calculation is computationally feasible for large models?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data distribution is not as extreme as in the experiments described, and what are the potential implications for performance?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the parameter α in the FedALS algorithm, particularly in terms of its sensitivity and the trade-offs it represents?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art federated learning algorithms, especially in terms of communication efficiency and model generalization?
- Could the authors discuss the potential implications of using different data distributions or different model architectures on the effectiveness of the FedALS algorithm?
