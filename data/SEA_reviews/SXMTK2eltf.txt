 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GPT-Driver, a novel approach to motion planning in autonomous driving by leveraging the Large Language Model (LLM) GPT-3.5. The method transforms motion planning into a language modeling problem, where inputs and outputs are represented as language tokens. This is achieved through a prompting-reasoning-finetuning strategy that converts heterogeneous inputs into unified language tokens, enabling the LLM to generate precise trajectory coordinates and interpret its decision-making process in natural language. The approach demonstrates superior motion planning performance, few-shot generalization ability, and interpretability compared to state-of-the-art motion planners on the nuScenes dataset. However, concerns were raised about the lack of detailed implementation, inference time, and comparisons with other LLMs and traditional methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to motion planning by leveraging the strong reasoning capabilities and generalization potential inherent in Large Language Models (LLMs).
- The proposed prompting-reasoning-finetuning strategy stimulates the numerical reasoning potential of the LLM, allowing for precise numerical reasoning and transparent decision-making.
- The GPT-Driver demonstrates superior motion planning performance, few-shot generalization ability, and interpretability compared to state-of-the-art motion planners on the nuScenes dataset.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the proposed prompting-reasoning-finetuning strategy, which is tailored for autonomous driving and is significantly different from existing works.
- The approach is evaluated on a large-scale dataset, nuScenes, and the experimental results show the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the experiments are well-designed, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed implementation, including the specifics of the prompting strategy and the reasoning process, which could benefit from additional ablation studies.
- There is a lack of comparisons with other LLMs, which could provide a clearer picture of the advantages of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide the inference time of the proposed method, which is crucial for evaluating the practicality of the approach.
- The paper only compares the proposed method with one state-of-the-art motion planner, which limits the robustness of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the approach.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the approach.

**Questions:**
- How is the prompting strategy designed? What are the specific prompts used for different scenarios?
- How is the reasoning process designed? What are the specific steps and reasoning rules used?
- Could the authors provide more details on the prompting-reasoning-finetuning strategy, including how the LLM is fine-tuned and how the outputs are fine-tuned?
- What are the specific types of prompts used for different scenarios, and how are these prompts generated?
- What are the specific steps and reasoning rules used in the reasoning process?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art motion planners in terms of inference time and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the generalization ability of the proposed method? How does it perform in scenarios not included in the training data?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how do these limitations affect the practical applicability of the approach?
