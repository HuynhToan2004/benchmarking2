 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to audio classification by combining spectrogram and waveform representations, aiming to enhance performance through a contrastive learning framework. The authors propose a method called CRAFT, which models both spectrogram and waveform tokens together with fusion bottlenecks to address semantic and temporal misalignments. The approach is evaluated on the AudioSet and ESC50 datasets, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods. The paper also explores the use of multi-scale audio embedding and a new loss function for audio modeling. Despite the promising results, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty and experimental scope, as well as the lack of detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to audio classification by integrating both spectrogram and waveform representations, which is a promising direction for enhancing the performance of audio classification tasks.
- The proposed method is well-motivated and supported by a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed components.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the methodology and results.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods, indicating the practical utility of the approach.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including the effects of different components and settings, which helps in understanding the impact of various design choices.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of why combining spectrogram and waveform representations is beneficial, and the results on the ESC50 dataset do not support the claimed benefits of the proposed method.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to state-of-the-art methods, especially when compared to the latest and most relevant works such as CAT and Audio-MAE.
- There is a lack of detailed comparison with these recent works, which could help in understanding the relative performance of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of temporal misalignment between spectrograms and waveforms, which could limit the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper's presentation could be improved to better highlight the key contributions and to make the figures and tables more informative and easier to understand.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify why the combination of spectrogram and waveform representations is beneficial and provide evidence to support this claim?
- How does the proposed method compare to the latest state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with CAT and Audio-MAE, including a head-to-head comparison of performance on the same datasets?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of temporal misalignment between spectrograms and waveforms, and what are the implications of this misalignment for the performance of the model?
- Could the authors explain the choice of using a different dataset for pretraining compared to the downstream tasks, and how this affects the generalizability of the results?
