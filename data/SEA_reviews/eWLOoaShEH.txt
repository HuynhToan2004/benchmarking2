 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Dynalang, a novel framework for training agents that utilize both text and image inputs to perform tasks in complex environments. The core idea is to condition the agent's actions on textual descriptions of the environment, which enhances its understanding and predictive capabilities. Dynalang is evaluated across various tasks, including gridworlds, navigation, and embodied question answering, showing significant improvements over existing model-free baselines. The framework leverages a world model to predict future observations and actions, integrating language with visual inputs to create a richer learning signal. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of comparison with certain state-of-the-art methods and the clarity of its contributions to the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of the HomeGrid environment is innovative and provides a new benchmark for evaluating language-conditioned agents.
- The proposed method, Dynalang, shows significant improvements over existing model-free baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness in various tasks.
- The integration of language with visual inputs in a world model is a novel approach that enhances the learning signal for agents, enabling them to better understand and predict actions in complex environments.
- The paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments that convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of Dynalang, showing its ability to outperform existing methods in tasks such as gridworlds, navigation, and embodied question answering.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the contributions of the proposed method, particularly in comparison to existing works such as DreamerV3 and other state-of-the-art methods.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the language conditioning is implemented in Dynalang, with some sections suggesting direct conditioning on text inputs, while others suggest a more indirect approach.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the reliance on a specific type of language input and the potential impact of different types of language on performance.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with other recent methods, particularly those that use large language models for language-conditioned reinforcement learning.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the design choices, such as the use of recurrent neural networks and the impact of different types of language on performance.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the role of the language conditioning in Dynalang, specifically how it is implemented and what impact it has on the agent's performance?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent methods that use large language models for language-conditioned reinforcement learning?
- In the LangRoom environment, is the agent provided with a vocabulary of possible answers to choose from, or is it expected to generate the answers from scratch?
- How does the method handle the different types of language inputs, such as commands versus descriptions, and what impact does this have on the agent's performance?
- Could you provide more details on the training and implementation of Dynalang, particularly in terms of the architecture and the training procedures used?
