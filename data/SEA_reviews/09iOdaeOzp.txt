 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for creating smaller language models (LLMs) by pruning a pre-trained large model and continuing pre-training with a dynamic batch loading strategy. This approach, termed "LLM-shearing," involves a two-stage process: first, pruning the model to a target architecture using a constrained optimization problem, and then continuing pre-training with a dynamic batch loading strategy to optimize the model's performance. The method is validated on the LLaMA-2 model, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing compute requirements while maintaining performance. The paper also explores the impact of different pruning ratios and data mixtures on model performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow content, and includes detailed ablation studies that provide insights into the impact of different pruning ratios and data mixtures on model performance.
- The proposed method of structured pruning followed by continued pre-training is innovative and shows promising results, particularly in reducing the compute requirements for training smaller language models.
- The use of dynamic batch loading to optimize the training process is a novel approach that could be beneficial in various applications.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing compute requirements while maintaining performance.
- The inclusion of a detailed appendix with additional experiments and analyses strengthens the paper's contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on the LLaMA-2 model, which limits the generalizability of the findings to other models like OPT, Pythia, or INCITE.
- The experimental setup and results are not robust enough to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over other existing techniques such as unstructured pruning or other structured pruning methods.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to models other than LLaMA-2 and the potential for overfitting due to the continued pre-training phase.
- There is a notable absence of error bars in the experimental results, which could affect the interpretation of the results' statistical significance.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational costs and efficiency of the proposed method, especially in comparison to other pruning methods.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the computational efficiency of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the total compute required for pruning and continued pre-training?
- How does the proposed method compare to other pruning methods, such as unstructured pruning or other structured pruning methods like LLM-Pruner?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific hardware used and the computational resources required for each stage of the pruning and continued pre-training processes?
- What are the specific reasons for choosing the dynamic batch loading strategy over other possible approaches, such as using a reference model's training data distribution or a scaling law to predict the performance of the pruned model?
- Could you elaborate on the potential limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to other models and the risks of overfitting due to the continued pre-training phase?
