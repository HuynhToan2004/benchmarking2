 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to continuous convolutions using separable basis functions, specifically focusing on Fourier basis functions for Lagrangian fluid simulations. This method, termed Symmetric Fourier Basis Convolution (SFBC), is designed to incorporate symmetry and smoothness, aiming to improve the performance of neural solvers in physics simulations. The authors evaluate SFBC against various baseline methods using datasets from Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) simulations, demonstrating its effectiveness in terms of accuracy and generalization. The paper also explores the use of different basis functions, including Fourier and Chebyshev series, and discusses their impact on the performance of the neural solvers.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive and extensive evaluation of different basis functions.
- The introduction of a new approach to continuous convolutions, particularly the use of Fourier basis functions, is innovative and shows promising results in terms of accuracy and generalization.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with existing methods, including a thorough evaluation of different window functions and their impact on the performance of the neural solvers.
- The experimental results are well-presented, showing a clear improvement over baseline methods, and the paper includes a comprehensive discussion on the choice of basis functions and their impact on the performance.
- The paper includes a novel approach to Fourier-based continuous convolutions that outperforms all other architectures in terms of accuracy and generalization, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the choice of basis functions and the specific advantages of using Fourier basis functions over others.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the generalization of the findings to other types of simulations or datasets.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the methodology and the experimental setup, especially in the appendices where critical details are relegated.
- The paper's contribution to the field is not clearly articulated, with some sections appearing more like supplementary materials rather than integral parts of the paper.
- The paper's focus on specific types of simulations (SPH) may limit its applicability to other types of simulations or datasets, and the generalizability of the findings is not adequately addressed.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the choice of basis functions and the specific advantages of using Fourier basis functions over others?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of simulations or datasets, such as those using different methods like Eulerian or different types of PDEs?
- Can you provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method compared to other baselines, especially in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the proposed method handle the complexities of Lagrangian simulations, particularly in terms of handling large deformation and non-convex domains?
- Can you discuss the potential impact of the proposed method on the simulation of other physical phenomena, such as those in materials science or geophysics?
