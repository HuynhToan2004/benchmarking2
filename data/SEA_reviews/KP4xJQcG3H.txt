 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework, Lagrangian Proximal Gradient Descent (LPGD), aimed at embedding parameterized optimization layers into machine learning architectures. LPGD leverages the Moreau envelope to approximate the loss function, providing a unified approach that encompasses various existing methods. This framework is particularly useful when the derivatives of the solution mapping are degenerate, allowing for training even in such scenarios. The paper provides theoretical insights into the convergence of LPGD and empirical evidence of its effectiveness, demonstrating that it can converge faster than standard gradient descent. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in defining key terms and assumptions, and for the limited scope of its empirical evaluations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and flexible framework, Lagrangian Proximal Gradient Descent (LPGD), which unifies and generalizes various state-of-the-art optimization methods.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, and the paper offers a clear and concise presentation of the proposed method, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The empirical results demonstrate that LPGD can converge faster than standard gradient descent, indicating its practical effectiveness.
- The paper's approach to embedding optimization layers into machine learning architectures is innovative and addresses the challenges of degenerate derivatives in optimization problems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining key terms and assumptions, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The experimental setup is limited to a single dataset, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for a comprehensive evaluation of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing approaches, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and convergence rates.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the assumptions made, which could affect the validity and applicability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of the Lagrangian divergence in the context of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the true gradient is non-degenerate? Could you provide empirical evidence to support this?
- Could you elaborate on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it compares with other existing methods?
- How do the authors address the assumptions made in their theoretical analysis, particularly in scenarios where these assumptions may not hold?
- Could you provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these affect its practical applicability?
- In the experimental setup, how does the proposed method perform when the optimization problem is non-convex?
