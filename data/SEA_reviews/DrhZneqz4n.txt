 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SinMDM, a novel diffusion-based model for motion synthesis using a single motion sequence. The model leverages a UNet architecture with local attention layers to limit the receptive field, thereby enhancing diversity and reducing overfitting. This approach allows for the generation of various motion sequences from a single input, which can be applied to various tasks such as motion composition, harmonization, and style transfer. The paper presents a lightweight model that can be trained on a single mid-range GPU, making it accessible for real-time applications. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing models like Ganimator and MDM, and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with a clear and logical structure.
- The proposed method, SinMDM, is simple yet effective, with a novel application of local attention in motion synthesis, which is a departure from traditional methods.
- The paper presents a comprehensive set of applications, demonstrating the versatility of SinMDM in various scenarios.
- The method's ability to handle a wide range of motion types, including long and complex motions, is highlighted, showing its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper includes a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with recent related works, particularly those that use similar methods like Ganimator and MDM. This oversight weakens the paper's argument for novelty and effectiveness.
- The paper's methodology and results are not sufficiently novel or distinct from existing methods like Ganimator, raising questions about the true contribution of the proposed method.
- The paper's claims about the model's ability to handle different motion types and its generalizability are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which is crucial for the model's effectiveness in real-world applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation, including a broader range of datasets and metrics to better demonstrate the model's capabilities.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to Ganimator in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and Ganimator, particularly in the use of local attention and the handling of different motion types?
- How does the model handle the issue of overfitting, especially when using a narrow receptive field?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, including recent works that use similar approaches?
- How does the model perform when trained on different types of motion data, such as human motion versus animal motion?
- Can the authors provide more quantitative results to support the claims of the model's effectiveness in handling different motion types and its generalizability?
