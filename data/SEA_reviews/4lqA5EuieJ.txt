 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework called GCIP that optimizes the sparsity of graphs in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to enhance interpretability. GCIP employs a bi-level optimization approach, leveraging reinforcement learning to manage the trade-off between sparsity and performance. The framework is evaluated across nine datasets from different domains, demonstrating its effectiveness in achieving competitive performance with sparser graphs compared to baselines. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, as it builds on existing methods like SUGAR and GCIP, and the clarity of the methodology, particularly in the presentation of the reinforcement learning component.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear and concise writing that effectively communicates the main ideas and contributions.
- The use of reinforcement learning for sparsity optimization is innovative and offers a novel approach to controlling the interpretability-performance trade-off.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can achieve competitive performance with sparser graphs compared to baselines.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the training process, including the evolution of rewards, performance, and sparsity, which aids in understanding the method's behavior.
- The introduction of a reward function that allows for a priori inductive biases towards sparser or more high-performing solutions is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-motivated, with a clear focus on the need for interpretability in GNNs and the limitations of existing solutions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with related works, particularly in the area of sparsification in GNNs, to better position its contributions within the existing literature.
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it builds on existing methods like SUGAR and GCIP, and the improvements over these methods are not clearly articulated.
- The presentation of the reinforcement learning component is not clear, with important details missing in the main text and relegated to the appendix.
- The paper primarily focuses on graph classification tasks and does not explore node classification or other tasks, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and the scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in large-scale or complex scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of interpretability, which is a significant concern for many applications of GNNs. The method's effectiveness in enhancing interpretability is not convincingly demonstrated.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific improvements or innovations of their method over existing approaches like SUGAR and GCIP?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of interpretability, and what specific measures have been taken to address this concern?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of their method, especially in comparison to other state-of-the-art GNNs?
- How does the method perform in node classification tasks, and what are the implications of this for the broader applicability of the findings?
- In terms of the reward function, why was the choice made to use the prediction loss as the performance reward, and how does this affect the overall effectiveness of the method?
- Could the authors elaborate on the training process and the role of the two hyperparameters in controlling the trade-off between sparsity and performance?
