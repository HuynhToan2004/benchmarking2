 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel rehearsal-free continual learning algorithm called Mode-Optimized Task Allocation (MOTA). MOTA trains multiple modes of a neural network in parallel, optimizing each mode based on the loss and drift from the previous mode. This approach aims to improve performance in various distribution shifts, including sub-population, domain, and task shifts. The methodology involves initializing pre-trained parameters, maximizing the distance between the parameters on the first task, and optimizing each parameter based on the loss with respect to the joint probability distribution and the drift from the prior position. The paper provides theoretical insights and empirical evidence to support the efficacy of the proposed method, demonstrating its potential in continual learning settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to continual learning by leveraging global geometry in the parameter space, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method, Mode-Optimized Task Allocation (MOTA), shows improvements over baseline continual learning strategies, demonstrating its effectiveness in various distribution shifts.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive review of related work, positioning the research within the existing literature.
- The theoretical insights and empirical evidence presented support the efficacy of the proposed method, enhancing its credibility and applicability.
- The method's simplicity and the use of a pre-trained model contribute to its practicality and potential for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the term "mode" used throughout the manuscript, which could lead to confusion among readers.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed or robust, particularly in terms of the number of modes used and the impact of different model sizes.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the claimed improvements.
- The method's dependence on the initialization of parameters and the need for multiple modes to achieve optimal performance might limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, especially in large-scale or complex models.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights for future research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of the term "mode" in the context of their research?
- How does the number of modes affect the performance of the proposed method, and what are the optimal conditions for selecting the number of modes?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, particularly on larger datasets like ImageNet, to validate the effectiveness of their method?
- How does the proposed method compare to state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance, computational efficiency, and scalability?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how might these impact its practical application in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending their method to other continual learning settings or architectures, such as transformer models?
