 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel coarse-to-fine method for designing multi-cellular robots using a Hyperbolic Embeddings for Robot Design (HERD) framework. This method optimizes the design of robots by initially focusing on coarse-grained robots and refining them through a hyperbolic space embedding, which allows for the exploration of promising regions. The approach is evaluated on various tasks from the EvoGym benchmark and demonstrates superior efficiency and generalization capabilities compared to existing methods. The paper also includes a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies that validate the proposed method's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to multi-cellular robot design, which is an important and challenging area of research.
- The proposed coarse-to-fine method is well-motivated and innovative, leveraging hyperbolic embeddings to optimize robot design in a shared hyperbolic space.
- The method is supported by extensive empirical studies and ablation studies that demonstrate its effectiveness and generalization capabilities.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide detailed explanations of their methodology, including the use of hyperbolic embeddings and the Cross-Entropy Method for optimization, which are novel applications in the context of robot design.
- The paper includes comprehensive experimental results and detailed comparisons with existing methods, which validate the proposed method's effectiveness and generalization capabilities.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach, particularly the potential inefficiency of coarse-to-fine robot design in tasks that require fine-grained robots.
- The method's reliance on hyperbolic embeddings raises concerns about the precision of the embeddings and their impact on the overall design process.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the generalizability of the proposed method to other types of robots or tasks beyond those tested.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples of the hyperbolic embeddings and the optimization process, particularly in the context of robot design.
- There are minor typographical and formatting errors that need correction.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the hyperbolic embeddings are generated and how they influence the design process, especially in the context of robot design?
- How do the authors address the potential inefficiencies or limitations of the coarse-to-fine approach in tasks that require fine-grained robots?
- Is there a specific reason why the coarse-to-fine approach is not applicable to all tasks, and how do the authors determine when to use this approach versus other methods?
- Could you clarify the role and impact of the hyperbolic embeddings in the design process, particularly in terms of precision and generalization to other types of robots or tasks?
- How do the authors ensure the reproducibility of their results, especially given the complexity of the robot design space and the potential for randomness in the clustering process?
