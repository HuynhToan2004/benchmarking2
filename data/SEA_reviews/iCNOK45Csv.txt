 **Summary:**
The paper investigates backdoor attacks in the context of dataset distillation, focusing on the theoretical aspects of backdoor effects and proposing new trigger pattern generation methods. It introduces two new backdoor designs, simple-trigger and relax-trigger, which are theoretically grounded and demonstrate effectiveness in bypassing existing backdoor detection and mitigation methods. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the theoretical foundations of these attacks and empirically validate their effectiveness across various datasets. The paper also discusses the implications of these attacks on the security of machine learning models trained on distilled datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel theoretical framework to characterize backdoor effects on dataset distillation, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the theoretical foundations of backdoor attacks in dataset distillation, which is a timely and relevant topic given the increasing use of dataset distillation techniques.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of two new trigger pattern generation methods, simple-trigger and relax-trigger, is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The paper provides empirical evidence that the triggers developed using the proposed approaches are effective at executing resilient backdoor attacks.
- The theoretical analysis of the conflict loss, projection loss, and generalization gap is well-structured and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods, which is crucial for a complete understanding of their implications.
- The experimental setup and results are not clearly presented, making it difficult to assess the effectiveness and reliability of the proposed methods.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing backdoor attack methods, which is essential for understanding the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the proposed methods to other types of dataset distillation techniques, such as parameter-preserving or distribution-preserving methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational efficiency of the proposed methods, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the robustness of the proposed methods against different types of backdoor defense methods, which is essential for understanding the practical applicability of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the computational efficiency of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of how they compare to existing methods like DoorPing?
- How do the authors justify the choice of using KIP as the dataset distillation method, and what are the implications of this choice for the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the standard deviation and the number of runs, to enhance the reliability and reproducibility of the findings?
- How do the proposed methods perform against different types of backdoor defense methods, and what are the implications of these findings for the practical applicability of the proposed methods?
- Can the authors elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of how they relate to the conflict loss, projection loss, and generalization gap?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods, and how do the authors address these concerns?
