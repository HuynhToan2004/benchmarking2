 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel meta-learning framework, LEAF, designed to address concept drift in online time series forecasting. LEAF is divided into two stages: extrapolation and adjustment, aimed at capturing macro-drift and micro-drift, respectively. The extrapolation stage uses a meta-learnt extrapolation network to infer latent embeddings for future periods, while the adjustment stage employs a meta-learnable surrogate loss to adjust these embeddings based on sample-specific micro-drift patterns. The framework is model-agnostic and has been tested on multiple datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling different types of concept drifts. The paper also introduces three electric load time series datasets, which are made available to the public, and provides extensive experiments to validate the approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and good presentation.
- The proposed method, LEAF, is novel and innovative, addressing the challenges of concept drift in online time series forecasting effectively.
- The paper introduces three electric load time series datasets, which are open-sourced and can be used as benchmark datasets for further research.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of LEAF compared to existing approaches.
- The methodology is well-structured and includes a detailed explanation of the algorithm, making it accessible and reproducible.
- The paper provides extensive experiments on multiple datasets, demonstrating the superior performance of LEAF compared to existing approaches in various dynamic and evolving environments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights for future research.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable as it combines existing techniques such as meta-learning and concept drift adaptation, which have been previously explored.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate the proposed method from existing approaches, particularly in terms of its contributions to the field of online time series forecasting.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the datasets used, including their characteristics and how they contribute to the study.
- The experimental setup and results could be improved by including more diverse datasets and a broader range of baselines for comparison.
- The paper could provide more detailed insights into the design choices of the extrapolation and adjustment modules, and how they contribute to the overall effectiveness of the method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical application?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, particularly in handling large datasets or complex models?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the datasets used, including their characteristics and how they contribute to the study?
- How do the authors plan to address the issue of overfitting, which is a common challenge in online time series forecasting?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the experimental results, including the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the method?
- How do the authors plan to address the issue of concept drift in other types of time series data beyond electric load, and what are the potential applications of the method in other domains?
