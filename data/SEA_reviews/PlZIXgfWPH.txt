 **Summary:**
The paper presents a comprehensive empirical analysis of the hyperparameter loss landscapes of six machine learning models across 67 datasets, employing a variety of statistical metrics and visualization techniques to characterize the landscapes. Key findings include the smoothness, unimodality, and neutrality of most landscapes, with high transferability across datasets and fidelity levels. The authors also investigate the relationship between hyperparameter configurations and model performance, showing that configurations with small training errors often achieve mild generalization errors. The study provides a detailed exploration of the properties of hyperparameter landscapes, which could aid in the development of more effective hyperparameter optimization strategies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a detailed exploration of the properties of hyperparameter landscapes, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of machine learning models and could aid in the development of more effective hyperparameter optimization strategies.
- The authors have conducted a large-scale empirical analysis of hyperparameter loss landscapes across various models and datasets, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of the empirical analysis and the results, which are supported by a variety of statistical metrics and visualization techniques.
- The findings of the study, such as the smoothness, unimodality, and neutrality of most hyperparameter landscapes, are both interesting and potentially useful for practical applications.
- The paper's contribution is substantial, with a large number of experiments conducted across a wide range of datasets, models, and hyperparameters, which provides a robust foundation for the conclusions drawn.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the methodology used for hyperparameter optimization, which could be crucial for understanding the impact of different hyperparameter settings on the results.
- The paper's claims about the generalizability of configurations across different fidelity levels and datasets are not well-supported by the data presented, which could limit the practical applicability of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the implications of the findings for practical hyperparameter optimization strategies, which could be a significant omission given the paper's focus on the empirical analysis of hyperparameter landscapes.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the experimental setup, such as the specific hyperparameters used for different models and datasets, which could affect the reproducibility and applicability of the results.
- The paper's reliance on a single dataset for some of the experiments (e.g., the NAS-Bench-101 dataset) could limit the generalizability of the findings to other datasets.
- The paper's discussion of the results is sometimes overly simplistic, and there is a lack of detailed analysis or discussion of the implications of the findings for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the methodology used for hyperparameter optimization, particularly how the search space was defined and how the hyperparameters were selected?
- How do the authors define "fidelity" in the context of their experiments, and how does this impact the results?
- Could the authors provide more detailed information on the experimental setup, including the specific hyperparameters used for different models and datasets?
- How do the authors address the issue of generalizability across different datasets and fidelity levels, and what evidence do they have that configurations perform well on new datasets?
- Could the authors discuss the implications of their findings for practical hyperparameter optimization strategies, and provide suggestions for how these findings could be applied in real-world scenarios?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the NAS-Bench-101 dataset for some of their experiments, and what steps were taken to ensure that the results are generalizable to other datasets?
- Could the authors provide more detailed analysis or discussion of the implications of their findings, particularly in terms of how they could be used to improve hyperparameter optimization strategies?
