 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Marginalization Models (MAMs), a novel generative model for high-dimensional discrete data that allows for scalable and flexible generative modeling with tractable likelihoods. MAMs explicitly model all induced marginal distributions, enabling fast evaluation of arbitrary marginal probabilities with a single forward pass of the neural network. The model is trained using a two-stage approach, with the first stage optimizing for conditional distributions and the second stage distilling these into marginal distributions. MAMs are evaluated against various baselines and shown to achieve significant speedups in marginal probability evaluations. The paper also discusses the potential of MAMs in both maximum likelihood and energy-based training settings, demonstrating their effectiveness across various discrete data distributions including binary images, language, physical systems, and molecules.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and interesting approach to model discrete data, focusing on marginalization models which are both scalable and flexible.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear mathematical formulations and intuitive explanations, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed model through comprehensive experiments, showing significant speedups in marginal probability evaluations and comparisons with various baselines.
- The introduction of a two-stage training method for enforcing marginalization self-consistency is a significant contribution, as it allows for scalable training of any-order generative models.
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of ideas and results, and includes a good balance of theoretical and practical insights.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other generative models, particularly in terms of scalability and expressiveness, which could strengthen the argument for the superiority of MAMs.
- There is a noticeable absence of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not provide sufficient implementation details, which could hinder reproducibility and further research.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, especially in terms of likelihood and speed, which could undermine the claims of superiority.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of MAMs, especially in comparison to other models like AO-ARMs.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other models, particularly in terms of the expressiveness and scalability of the models.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of scalability and expressiveness with other generative models, particularly those that use different parameterization methods?
- Can you provide more details on the training process, such as the number of training epochs and the learning rate schedule used?
- What are the specific advantages of MAMs over other generative models in terms of scalability and flexibility?
- How does the model handle the issue of "marginalization self-consistency" in practical scenarios, and what are the implications if this constraint is not strictly enforced?
- Could you clarify the computational complexity and scalability of MAMs, especially in comparison to other models like AO-ARMs?
- How do the authors ensure the validity of the marginalization model, and what are the implications if the model fails to satisfy the sum rule of probability?
- Can you discuss the potential applications of MAMs in real-world scenarios, and how they might be used to solve specific problems or challenges in various fields?
