 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SEPT, a novel method for motion prediction in autonomous driving scenarios, leveraging self-supervised learning to enhance spatiotemporal understanding. SEPT employs three masking-reconstruction modeling tasks on scene inputs, including agents' trajectories and road network, to pretrain the scene encoder. The pretrained encoder is then fine-tuned for downstream forecasting tasks. The method demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the Argoverse 1 and Argoverse 2 motion forecasting benchmarks, outperforming previous methods significantly. The paper is praised for its innovative approach, thorough experiments, and clear presentation, although some concerns are raised about the novelty of the method and its scalability to larger datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for motion prediction in autonomous driving scenarios, leveraging self-supervised learning to enhance spatiotemporal understanding.
- The method demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the Argoverse 1 and Argoverse 2 motion forecasting benchmarks, outperforming previous methods significantly.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and detailed descriptions of the methodology, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed method, showing significant improvements over previous methods.
- The paper is original in its approach, combining self-supervised learning with motion prediction in a novel way, which is both technically sound and innovative.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with similar works, such as Traj-MAE and Forecast-MAE, which could help clarify the unique contributions and advantages of the proposed method.
- There is a concern about the scalability of the method to larger datasets, as the experiments were conducted on relatively small datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with the most recent and relevant works, which could have strengthened the argument for the superiority of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on pretraining for scene understanding and its integration with motion prediction tasks are not clearly explained, raising questions about the necessity and effectiveness of this approach.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights for future research and improvements.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with Traj-MAE and Forecast-MAE, highlighting the unique contributions and advantages of their proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets, such as those used in other benchmarks like Waymo Open Dataset?
- Could the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the pretraining stage in enhancing the motion prediction tasks?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle multiple agents in the scene, and if so, how would this be achieved?
- How does the method handle the integration of different modalities, such as agents and road network, in the pretraining tasks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and inference times, and compare these with other methods to assess the computational efficiency of the proposed method?
