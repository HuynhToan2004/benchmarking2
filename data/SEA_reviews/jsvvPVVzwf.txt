 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the effectiveness of unstructured magnitude pruning in neural networks by focusing on the cosine similarity between the pruned and original models. It explores the relationship between pruning methods, such as L1 and random pruning, and their impact on cosine similarity, functional similarity, and loss landscapes. The study reveals that L1 pruning maintains higher cosine similarity, which translates to better functional similarity and performance after fine-tuning. The paper also discusses the impact of kurtosis on the pruning process and proposes a method to determine the optimal amount of pruning based on parameter distributions. However, the paper's experimental setup is limited to small models and datasets, and the results are not convincingly generalizable to larger models and more complex datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to pruning by focusing on cosine similarity, which is a unique perspective in the field.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments to support their claims, providing a solid foundation for their findings.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The research questions addressed are relevant and timely, focusing on the practical implications of pruning in neural networks.
- The proposed method for determining the optimal amount of pruning is innovative and could be useful for practitioners seeking to optimize their pruning strategies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other pruning methods, such as L2-norm and other magnitude-based pruning methods, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed approach.
- The experimental setup is limited to small models and datasets, which may not generalize well to larger, more complex models and datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other methods in terms of computational efficiency or scalability, which are critical factors in practical applications.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it largely builds on existing knowledge about the impact of parameter magnitude on model performance.
- The paper's conclusions are not clearly articulated, and the implications of the findings are not adequately discussed, which could limit the understanding and application of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other pruning methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the fine-tuning process and the selection of models and datasets?
- How does the proposed method perform with different pruning ratios, and what are the optimal pruning ratios for different models and datasets?
- Could the authors elaborate on the implications of the findings for practical applications, particularly in terms of improving the efficiency and effectiveness of neural network pruning?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to other pruning methods, such as L2-norm pruning, and what would be the expected outcomes?
