 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for training Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) on large graphs by sampling small subgraphs, leveraging the theory of graph local limits. This approach allows for understanding the convergence of GNNs on sampled subgraphs to the outcome of training on the whole graph, providing a theoretical understanding of the effectiveness of sampling-based GNNs. The paper also derives bounds on the number of samples, graph size, and training steps required, which are crucial for practical applications. Empirical results on node classification tasks demonstrate that sampling-based GNNs trained on local subgraphs can achieve comparable performance to those trained on the input graph, thus enhancing the efficiency of model selection and hyperparameter tuning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for training Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) on large input graphs using small, fixed-size sampled subgraphs, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided in the paper is robust, offering a comprehensive understanding of the behavior of GNNs trained on sampled subgraphs, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper's empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework, showing that sampling-based GNNs can achieve comparable performance to those trained on the input graph, thus enhancing the efficiency of model selection and hyperparameter tuning.
- The paper is well-written and structured, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is essential for a broad audience in the field.
- The theoretical results presented in the paper are novel and contribute significantly to the understanding of GNN training on large graphs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental section is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on a single dataset and a few architectures, which might not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed framework.
- The theoretical results are somewhat abstract and difficult to apply directly to practical scenarios, which could limit their immediate utility.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework and potential future directions for research, which could help in better understanding the scope and applicability of the work.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly how these results can be applied to improve the performance of GNNs in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed framework and potential future directions for research?
- How does the proposed framework compare to other existing methods for training GNNs on large graphs, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of the theoretical results presented in the paper, and provide examples or case studies that demonstrate how these results can be applied to improve the performance of GNNs in real-world scenarios?
- The paper mentions that the results are applicable to various GNN architectures. Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on how these results can be applied to different architectures, and what are the specific implications for each architecture?
