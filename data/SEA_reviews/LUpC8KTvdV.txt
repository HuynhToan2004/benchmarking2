 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MaskTAS, a self-supervised architecture search method for vision transformers, leveraging masked image modeling (MIM) to train a supernet and search for optimal architectures. The method involves a teacher-student supernet design and an unsupervised evaluation metric to guide the search process, aiming to reduce the need for manual labeling. The paper presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation across various datasets, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the effectiveness of the unsupervised evaluation metric, and the lack of detailed comparative analysis with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the proposed method is clearly illustrated.
- The introduction of self-supervised learning to the architecture search process is innovative and could potentially reduce the reliance on labeled data.
- The method is evaluated across multiple datasets, demonstrating its generalization capabilities.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including ablation studies that show the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of masked image modeling for architecture search is a novel approach that could lead to more efficient and effective architecture design.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the effectiveness of the unsupervised evaluation metric.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as it heavily relies on existing methods like MAE and AutoFormer, with limited new contributions.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that the proposed method is more effective than existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the effectiveness of the unsupervised evaluation metric.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for the supernet training stage.
- The methodology and the rationale behind certain design choices, such as the use of a teacher-student supernet and the unsupervised evaluation metric, are not convincingly justified.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the proposed method compares in terms of computational efficiency with existing methods, particularly in the supernet training stage?
- How does the unsupervised evaluation metric compare in terms of effectiveness with supervised evaluation metrics, especially in scenarios where labeled data is available?
- What are the specific advantages of using a teacher-student supernet over other architectures, and how does this design contribute to the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- Can you provide more evidence or theoretical support for the claim that the proposed method is more effective than existing methods, particularly in terms of its ability to reduce the need for labeled data?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the quality of the pre-trained teacher network is low, and what are the implications of this for the overall effectiveness of the method?
