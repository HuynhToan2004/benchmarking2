 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Dynamic-Choice-Pessimistic-Policy-Optimization (DCPPO), for offline reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF). DCPPO is designed to recover the human reward function from a set of trajectories induced by human choices, utilizing a dynamic discrete choice model. The algorithm involves estimating the human behavior policy and state-action value function via maximum likelihood estimation, recovering the human reward function, and finding a near-optimal policy through pessimistic value iteration. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the suboptimality of the learned policy, matching classical pessimistic offline RL algorithms in terms of suboptimality's dependence on distribution shift and dimension. Despite its theoretical contributions, the paper lacks empirical validation, which is crucial for demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF), focusing on the dynamic discrete choice model which is a novel approach.
- The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the suboptimality of the learned policy, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a clear exposition of the problem setting and proposed algorithm.
- The authors effectively use a pessimistic approach to handle uncertainty in the reward function, which is a common challenge in RLHF.
- The paper presents a general framework for learning the reward of the underlying MDP, which can be applied to different model classes.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical validation, which is crucial for demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The theoretical results are limited to linear MDPs and RKHS model classes, which might not generalize well to more complex scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made, particularly in relation to the linear MDP assumption and its practical applicability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing methods, which could help in understanding the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide empirical results to support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the linear MDP assumption does not hold?
- Could the authors elaborate on the assumptions made, particularly Assumption 4.2, and discuss their practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential directions for future research?
