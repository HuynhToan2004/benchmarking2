 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel frequency-based equivariant feature representation for SO(3)-equivariant networks, leveraging the sinusoidal nature of rotation matrices to enhance feature representation. This method extends the Vector Neuron framework to handle high-dimensional features, improving the network's ability to capture complex shapes and details. The paper provides theoretical proofs and experimental validations, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches. However, concerns were raised about the practical applicability of the method, its novelty, and the clarity of the presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel frequency-based equivariant feature representation that is intuitive and can be easily integrated with existing architectures, making it accessible to a broader audience.
- The method is theoretically sound, with proofs provided for the equivariant property of the proposed feature representation.
- The paper includes comprehensive experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various tasks, showcasing its ability to capture high-frequency details and enhance performance.
- The presentation of the paper is clear and well-structured, making it easy to follow, and the mathematical proofs are well-articulated.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of SO(3)-equivariant networks, which is crucial for handling 3D point cloud data.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on how the proposed method addresses the limitations of existing methods like Vector Neurons, particularly in terms of feature dimensionality.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The practical applicability of the method is not convincingly demonstrated, especially in real-world scenarios where the method's performance might not meet expectations.
- The paper suffers from several typographical and grammatical errors, which could detract from its professionalism and clarity.
- There is a lack of detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and memory requirements of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.

**Questions:**

- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the practical applicability of the method, especially in scenarios where the method might not perform optimally?
