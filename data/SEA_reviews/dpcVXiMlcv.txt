 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for text-driven image editing, focusing on multi-object editing scenarios. It proposes a new search metric to determine the optimal number of inversion steps for each editing pair, thereby improving the editing quality and reducing conceptual confusion. The method involves an object-aware inversion and reassembly process, which allows for fine-grained control over image editing, particularly in multi-object scenarios. The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, showcasing its effectiveness through a series of experiments and comparisons with existing methods. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and the complexity of its methodology, which might not be practical for real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel method for multi-object editing, focusing on the automatic determination of the optimal number of inversion steps for each editing pair, which is a significant improvement over existing methods.
- The proposed method is technically sound, with a clear and logical structure that makes it easy to follow.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a comprehensive experimental setup that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method is simple yet effective, utilizing a search metric to select the optimal result, which is a new paradigm in image editing.
- The paper introduces two new datasets for benchmarking single- and multi-object editing, respectively, which are valuable resources for the research community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which might be a significant limitation for practical applications.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it appears to be a straightforward combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to specific scenarios, such as multi-object editing, and may not generalize well to other types of image editing tasks.
- The paper's evaluation is limited, with a lack of comparisons to other recent methods and a narrow scope of experiments that do not adequately demonstrate the method's performance across different scenarios.
- There are concerns about the fairness of the comparisons made in the paper, particularly in the absence of certain baseline methods that are crucial for evaluating the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper suffers from poor writing quality in some sections, making it difficult to understand the methodology and the experimental setup.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the optimal number of inversion steps for different editing pairs varies significantly?
- Can the proposed method be adapted to handle other types of image editing tasks beyond multi-object editing?
- What is the computational complexity of the proposed method, and how does it compare to other existing methods in terms of efficiency?
- How does the method ensure that the edited regions maintain their integrity and do not blend with the background, especially in cases where the background is similar to the edited object?
- Can the authors provide more details on the methodology and the experimental setup, particularly in the areas where the paper is unclear or incomplete?
- How does the method handle the disassembly and reassembly processes, and what are the implications of these processes on the final editing results?
