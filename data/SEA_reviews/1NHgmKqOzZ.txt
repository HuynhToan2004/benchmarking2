 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Progressive Dataset Distillation (PDD), a novel method that iteratively generates synthetic datasets to capture the training dynamics of neural networks across different stages. PDD addresses the limitations of existing dataset distillation methods by synthesizing multiple small sets of synthetic images, each conditioned on the previous sets, to better match the training dynamics. This methodology has been validated through extensive experiments, showing improvements in accuracy and generalization performance compared to single-stage distillation. The paper also explores the potential of PDD in continual learning and cross-architecture generalization, demonstrating its applicability and effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method of dataset distillation that addresses the limitations of existing methods by focusing on the training dynamics of different stages.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, with clear presentation and extensive experiments that demonstrate its effectiveness.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the training dynamics of neural networks and the limitations of existing dataset distillation methods.
- The method is validated on multiple datasets, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and Tiny-ImageNet, and shows improvements in accuracy and generalization performance compared to single-stage distillation.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are extensive, covering various datasets and models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational cost of the proposed method compared to existing methods, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the generated synthetic images, such as the distribution of generated images and the impact of different distillation settings on the image quality.
- The paper's experimental validation is limited to specific datasets and models, which might not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not include a comparison with the latest dataset distillation methods, which could provide a better understanding of the method's performance relative to the state-of-the-art.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the method's implementation, particularly the generation of synthetic images and the training dynamics across different stages.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method compared to existing methods?
- How do the generated synthetic images compare in terms of quality and diversity to those generated by other dataset distillation methods?
- Can the proposed method be applied to larger datasets or more complex models, such as those used in large-scale vision tasks?
- How does the method perform when applied to other types of datasets, such as text or audio data?
- Could you clarify the method's implementation details, particularly the generation of synthetic images and the training dynamics across different stages?
- Is there a potential for the proposed method to be used in conjunction with other dataset distillation methods to further enhance performance?
