 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the challenge of handling "bad pairs" in time series contrastive learning, focusing on noisy and faulty positive pairs. The proposed Dynamic Bad Pair Mining (DBPM) algorithm identifies and suppresses these pairs by tracking their training behavior and dynamically adjusting their weights. This method is validated across multiple datasets, showing consistent performance improvements over existing methods. The authors also discuss the theoretical implications and empirical evidence to support their approach, although some concerns were raised about the novelty and the depth of experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in time series contrastive learning, focusing on the impact of "bad pairs" which are often overlooked.
- The proposed Dynamic Bad Pair Mining (DBPM) algorithm is novel and well-explained, providing a clear methodology for identifying and suppressing bad pairs.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with a clear explanation of the methodology and its implications.
- The paper provides extensive experiments on real-world datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in enhancing the performance of existing state-of-the-art methods.
- The authors have made their code publicly available, which is a valuable resource for the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, which are crucial for practical applications.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with some datasets showing minimal improvements or even decreased performance in certain scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the novelty of the work, as the approach of identifying and suppressing bad pairs is similar to existing methods.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the impact of different types of bad pairs on the learning process, as well as a more in-depth exploration of the effectiveness of the DBPM algorithm.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- How does the DBPM algorithm perform when applied to datasets with varying levels of noise and faulty pairs?
- Could you provide more examples of real-world data where noisy and faulty positive pairs occur, and discuss how these affect the learning process?
- How does the DBPM algorithm handle the identification and suppression of bad pairs in different types of time series data, such as those with varying lengths or different sampling rates?
- Can you discuss the limitations of the proposed method and provide insights into when it might not be effective or applicable?
- How does the DBPM algorithm compare to other methods that dynamically adjust the weight of positive pairs based on their performance during training?
