 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel application of discrete diffusion models in non-deterministic semantic segmentation tasks, specifically in future prediction and medical image segmentation. It proposes a method that models uncertainty in predictions using discrete diffusion, enhancing the handling of ambiguous predictions in these domains. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on datasets such as LIDC, a lung cancer medical imaging dataset, and the Car Intersection Simulator, showing that their method outperforms deterministic models and VAE-based methods. However, the paper is criticized for its limited evaluation on only two datasets, the absence of comparisons with other recent methods, and insufficient discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel application of discrete diffusion models in non-deterministic semantic segmentation tasks, which is both innovative and relevant to real-world applications such as future prediction and medical image segmentation.
- The method is well-explained and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are clearly presented, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in handling ambiguous predictions and outperforming deterministic models.
- The paper includes a motivating example that effectively highlights the importance of modeling uncertainty in segmentation tasks, and the results on the LIDC dataset show that the proposed method outperforms VAE-based methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is limited to only two datasets, which may not sufficiently demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method across different types of data.
- There is a lack of comparison with other recent methods, which could provide a better understanding of the competitive landscape and the relative advantages of the proposed method.
- The computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method are not discussed, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not include qualitative results, which could provide a deeper understanding of the method's performance and potential shortcomings.
- The experimental setup and results, particularly the comparisons with baseline models, are not convincingly presented, raising questions about the validity and fairness of the comparisons.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other datasets, and what are the potential challenges or limitations that might be encountered in such applications?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other recent methods, including a discussion on how the proposed method compares in terms of computational efficiency, scalability, and practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between computational cost and performance, especially in scenarios where higher computational cost might lead to better performance?
- Could the authors provide more detailed qualitative results to better illustrate the performance and limitations of the proposed method?
