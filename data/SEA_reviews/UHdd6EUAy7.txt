 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Robust-HDP, a novel algorithm for federated learning (FL) that addresses privacy concerns in a heterogeneous setting. The algorithm employs a noise-aware aggregation strategy to assign weights to client updates based on the variance of their noise levels, thereby improving utility and convergence speed. The paper also discusses the impact of batch size on noise levels and presents theoretical and empirical evidence to support its effectiveness. However, the paper faces criticism for its unclear presentation, lack of novelty, and potential issues with the scalability and generalizability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to addressing privacy concerns in federated learning (FL) by focusing on the variance of noise in client updates, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The theoretical analysis provided is comprehensive and well-supported by empirical evidence, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving utility and convergence speed.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The problem of heterogeneity in privacy requirements and computational resources is addressed, which is highly relevant in current FL systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from poor writing and lacks clarity in presentation, making it difficult to follow and understand the methodology and results.
- There is a notable lack of novelty as the main idea of assigning weights based on noise levels is not new, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from existing works.
- The scalability of the proposed method is questionable, as the computation time for RPCA increases with the number of model parameters, which could limit its applicability in large-scale FL systems.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of data heterogeneity, which is crucial in FL settings, and the assumptions made about the data distributions and model parameters are too strong and unrealistic.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not provide sufficient experimental evidence to support its claims, particularly concerning the impact of batch size on noise levels and the effectiveness of the proposed aggregation strategy.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly regarding the uniformity of data distributions and the model parameters across clients?
- How does the paper address the scalability issues related to the computation time of RPCA in large-scale FL systems?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the impact of batch size on noise levels and the performance of the proposed aggregation strategy across different datasets and privacy settings?
- What are the specific privacy risks associated with the proposed method, especially concerning the potential leakage of privacy information to the server?
- How does the proposed method ensure fairness and privacy in the aggregation process, particularly in scenarios where clients have varying privacy requirements and computational resources?
- Can the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their work and how they address ethical considerations in their research?
