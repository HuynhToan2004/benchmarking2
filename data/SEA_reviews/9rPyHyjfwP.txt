 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MolGen, a novel pre-trained language model for molecular generation, leveraging SELFIES as a robust molecular language. MolGen is designed to generate chemically valid molecules by employing a domain-agnostic molecular prefix tuning and a chemical feedback paradigm to address the issue of "molecular hallucinations." The model is evaluated against various baselines on benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness in generating molecules with desired properties. The paper also discusses the model's ability to learn from the training data and its potential for future research in areas like retrosynthesis and reaction prediction.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive review of the literature, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, MolGen, introduces a novel approach using SELFIES, which is a robust molecular language, to address the challenges of generating chemically valid molecules.
- MolGen is evaluated on various benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness in generating molecules with desired properties.
- The paper discusses the potential of MolGen for future research in areas like retrosynthesis and reaction prediction, highlighting its broader applicability.
- The methodology of MolGen is innovative, combining domain-agnostic molecular prefix tuning and a chemical feedback paradigm to effectively mitigate "molecular hallucinations."
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the model's performance, providing insights into its strengths and limitations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the "molecular hallucinations" concept, which is critical for understanding the problem the model aims to solve.
- The novelty of MolGen's approach using SELFIES is not adequately highlighted, and the paper does not sufficiently compare MolGen with existing methods using SMILES.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training data used, raising concerns about the diversity and representativeness of the dataset.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the limitations of MolGen, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the model.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definitions and explanations of terms and concepts used in the paper, such as "domain-agnostic molecular prefix" and "molecular distribution learning."
- The paper does not include a detailed comparison of MolGen with other models in terms of training parameters, which is necessary for a fair evaluation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of "domain-agnostic molecular prefix" in MolGen's training process?
- How does MolGen handle the diversity and complexity of natural product molecules compared to synthetic molecules?
- What are the specific advantages of using SELFIES over SMILES in MolGen's training, and how does this affect the model's performance?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training data used, including its diversity and representativeness?
- How does MolGen ensure the generation of molecules that are not only chemically valid but also practically useful or have anticipated properties?
- Can the authors elaborate on the concept of "molecular hallucinations" and how MolGen mitigates this issue?
- How does MolGen perform in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially when dealing with large or complex datasets?
