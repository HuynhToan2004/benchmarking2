 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Dual Counterfactual Generative Model (DCGM), designed to address collider bias in estimating treatment effects from observational data. DCGM leverages both experimental and observational data to generate missing data and labels, aiming to mitigate biases in treatment effect estimation. The model consists of two generators and two discriminators, optimized to align the distribution of selected and unselected data, enhancing the accuracy of treatment effect estimation. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing significant improvements over existing methods. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in explaining the method's technical aspects, particularly how it addresses collider bias and its assumptions, and the need for more rigorous comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and understudied problem in the field of causal inference, specifically collider bias, which is a significant challenge in estimating treatment effects from observational data.
- The proposed method, DCGM, is innovative and well-motivated, offering a novel approach to mitigate collider bias by integrating both experimental and observational data.
- The paper is well-written and clear, making it accessible to readers with a background in causal inference.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the DCGM method, showing significant improvements over existing methods in some scenarios.
- The method's approach to addressing collider bias from an out-of-distribution perspective is interesting and could be a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the method's technical aspects, particularly how it addresses collider bias and the assumptions made.
- The comparison with existing methods is insufficient, and the paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison to establish the method's advantages over existing methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of distribution shift, which is a critical aspect of collider bias.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincing, particularly in demonstrating the method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and how these could impact its practical application.
- The method's reliance on generative models for generating unselected samples in observations and its training challenges with complex and high-dimensional data with limited samples are not adequately addressed.

**Questions:**
- How does the DCGM method address collider bias specifically, and what are the assumptions made in this process?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the generation of the experimental dataset and the selection of observational data?
- How does the DCGM method perform in scenarios where the treatment effect is not linear, and what are the implications of this for the method's effectiveness?
- Could you clarify the training process of the generative model and discuss any strategies for addressing the challenges mentioned in the paper?
- How does the DCGM method compare to existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can you provide more insights into the method's limitations and discuss potential strategies for addressing these in practical applications?
