 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel semi-anchored (SA) gradient method for solving nonconvex-nonconcave minimax problems, which is an extension of the Primal-Dual Hybrid Gradient (PDHG) method. The SA method leverages the Bregman proximal point method to construct a new optimality measure based on the Bregman distance, which is used to analyze the convergence rate of the SA method. The paper also discusses the theoretical and practical aspects of the SA method, including its convergence properties and its application in a fair classification problem. The method is compared to existing methods such as the extragradient method and the PDHG method, showing potential improvements in convergence rates and practical applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel semi-anchored (SA) gradient method for solving nonconvex-nonconcave minimax problems, which is an extension of the Primal-Dual Hybrid Gradient (PDHG) method.
- The authors propose a new optimality measure based on the Bregman distance, which is used to analyze the convergence rate of the SA method.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the SA method, showing that it converges at a rate comparable to the extragradient method.
- The SA method is applied to a fair classification problem, demonstrating its practical applicability and potential benefits in real-world scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the practical implications of the SA method, particularly in terms of its computational complexity and the availability of exact maximization oracles.
- The convergence analysis of the SA method is limited to specific conditions, which may not apply to a broader range of problems.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the convergence rate and the practical applicability of the SA method.
- The numerical results presented in the paper are limited, and the experiments do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the SA method over existing methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions and limitations of the SA method, particularly in terms of its applicability and the conditions under which it can be effectively used.

**Questions:**

- How does the SA method compare to other existing methods, particularly in terms of convergence rate and practical applicability?
- Could the authors provide more detailed numerical results, including a comparison with other methods and a discussion of the conditions under which the SA method is most effective?

- How does the SA method perform in scenarios where the assumptions of the weak MVI condition are not met?
