 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel benchmark dataset, Corr2Cause, designed to evaluate the causal reasoning abilities of large language models (LLMs) by testing their ability to infer causation from correlation. The dataset, consisting of over 400K samples, is used to evaluate 17 existing LLMs, revealing a significant performance gap between these models and a random baseline. The authors demonstrate that while LLMs can be fine-tuned to improve performance, they still struggle with generalization and robustness in out-of-distribution settings. The paper also discusses the potential of the dataset in advancing research in causal reasoning and suggests areas for future work to enhance the robustness and applicability of LLMs in causal inference tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical structure that makes it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of a novel benchmark dataset, Corr2Cause, is a significant contribution to the field, providing a valuable resource for evaluating the causal reasoning abilities of LLMs.
- The paper is comprehensive, covering a wide range of LLMs and including robustness tests, which help in understanding the models' performance under different conditions.
- The analysis conducted on the dataset is thorough, providing insights into the performance of LLMs in causal reasoning tasks and highlighting areas for improvement.
- The paper is timely, addressing a critical issue in the field of LLMs and potentially influencing future research in causal reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the dataset's construction aligns with the faithfulness assumption and the potential for overfitting to the training data.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the practical applications of the Corr2Cause task and its relevance to real-world scenarios, which might limit its immediate utility.
- The paper could provide more insights into the performance of LLMs on different types of causal relations, particularly those that are more complex or less frequently observed in the dataset.
- The generalization capabilities of LLMs, especially when fine-tuned on the Corr2Cause dataset, need further exploration, particularly in out-of-distribution settings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the Corr2Cause dataset, such as its scalability and the potential for bias in the dataset.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between the Corr2Cause dataset and the faithfulness assumption? How does the dataset's construction ensure that the models are not overfitting to the training data?
- How do the authors envision the practical applications of the Corr2Cause task? Could you provide examples or scenarios where this task would be particularly useful or relevant?
- Given the limitations of the Corr2Cause dataset in terms of scalability and generalizability, how do the authors plan to address these issues in future research?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the performance of LLMs on different types of causal relations, particularly those that are more complex or less frequently observed in the dataset?
- How do the authors plan to address the generalization capabilities of LLMs when fine-tuned on the Corr2Cause dataset? Are there any specific strategies or approaches that could be employed to enhance the models' robustness in out-of-distribution settings?
