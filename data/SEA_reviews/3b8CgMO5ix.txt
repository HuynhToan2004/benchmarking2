 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of differentiable heatmap architectures for semi-supervised segmentation, leveraging the "Right for the Right Reasons" paradigm. By integrating heatmaps with standard encoder-decoder architectures, the authors demonstrate competitive results in segmentation tasks using the PASCAL VOC 2012 dataset. The approach unifies two lines of research, showcasing the potential of heatmap-based models in segmentation tasks. The paper also discusses the formal parallels between differentiable heatmap architectures and standard encoder-decoder architectures, providing insights into the effectiveness of heatmap-based models in segmentation tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to using heatmap-based models for segmentation tasks.
- The authors provide a thorough analysis of the heatmap-based models, including a formal comparison with standard encoder-decoder architectures, and demonstrate the effectiveness of these models in segmentation tasks.
- The proposed method shows promising results in semi-supervised segmentation, outperforming comparable encoder-decoder models, and is well-supported by ablation studies and comparisons to other models.
- The paper is significant in its potential impact on the field of image segmentation, particularly in scenarios with limited pixel-level labels, and could inspire further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency.
- The experiments are limited to the PASCAL VOC 2012 dataset, which might not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method to other datasets or real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant limitation for practical applications.
- The paper's contribution to the field is somewhat limited, as the idea of using heatmaps for segmentation is not new, and the novelty of the approach is questioned.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method to larger or more complex datasets, which could limit its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art models, including performance metrics and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets or in real-world scenarios, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in these settings?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use heatmaps for segmentation, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the model?
