 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DragonDiffusion, a novel image editing method that utilizes feature correspondence in a pre-trained diffusion model to achieve drag-style manipulation. This method involves the construction of energy functions that align with the editing target, transforming image editing operations into gradient guidance. DragonDiffusion also incorporates a visual cross-attention strategy based on a memory bank design to ensure consistency between the edited result and the original image. The approach allows for various image editing tasks, including object moving, resizing, appearance replacing, and object pasting, without requiring additional model fine-tuning or new modules. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its similarity to existing works and its lack of comprehensive comparative analysis with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and effectively communicates the methodology and results.
- The proposed method, DragonDiffusion, is novel and technically sound, combining a memory bank, gradient guidance, and visual cross-attention to achieve image editing without model fine-tuning.
- The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of DragonDiffusion in various image editing tasks, including object moving, resizing, appearance replacing, and object pasting.
- The paper presents a novel approach to image editing by treating it as a change of feature correspondence, which is both innovative and potentially influential in the field.
- The methodology is clearly explained, and the paper provides a comprehensive overview of the proposed method, including a detailed explanation of the components and their interactions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of image quality and editing performance.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it appears to be similar to existing works such as DragGAN and Self-Guidance, with minimal modifications.
- There is a lack of quantitative evaluation metrics, which could provide a more objective assessment of the method's performance.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with other methods, including qualitative and quantitative results, to better establish the superiority of DragonDiffusion.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the energy functions are defined and how they guide the image editing process?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of image quality and editing performance?
- Could the authors provide more quantitative evaluation metrics, such as FID scores or PSNR, to support the claims of improved image quality and editing performance?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to include more complex or fine-grained image editing tasks, such as hair movement or facial expression changes?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the edited content may not perfectly align with the original image, and what strategies can be employed to minimize such misalignments?
