 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DisCo-DSO, a novel approach to optimizing hybrid discrete-continuous spaces using a generative model that learns a joint distribution over discrete and continuous design variables. This method enables the optimization of both discrete and continuous variables simultaneously, reducing the number of objective function evaluations and improving performance. The paper demonstrates the approach's effectiveness through experiments on various optimization tasks, including symbolic regression and decision tree learning, showing that it outperforms state-of-the-art methods. The approach leverages a generative model to learn the joint distribution over discrete and continuous variables, which guides the search process and enhances performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear and concise presentation of the problem and the proposed solution.
- The proposed method, DisCo-DSO, is innovative and addresses a significant challenge in discrete-continuous optimization, which is often treated as separate problems.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of related works and effectively positions the proposed method within the existing literature.
- The experimental results are robust, showing that DisCo-DSO outperforms state-of-the-art methods in various optimization tasks, including symbolic regression and decision tree policy optimization.
- The methodology is sound, and the paper is well-organized, making it easy for readers to understand the proposed approach and its implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the experimental results, particularly in the decision tree policy optimization experiments, where the results are not as clear.
- The method's reliance on domain-specific information for defining the ranges of continuous variables could limit its applicability in more general settings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of overfitting in symbolic regression, which is a known challenge in this field.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- There is a need for more detailed explanations of the methodology, particularly in the areas of risk-seeking policy gradient and the use of quantile-conditioned expected reward.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and generalization performance.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more detailed explanations of the experimental results, particularly in the decision tree policy optimization experiments, where the results are not as clear?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of overfitting in symbolic regression?
- What are the specific limitations of the proposed method, and how do these impact its applicability and effectiveness?
- Could you discuss the computational efficiency of the proposed method compared to other existing methods?
- How does the method handle the generalization performance in symbolic regression, especially in more complex settings?
- Could you provide more details on the implementation of the risk-seeking policy gradient and the use of quantile-conditioned expected reward in your methodology?
