 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the relationship between the flatness of the loss function in parameter space and the compression of neural representations in feature space. It extends the analysis of Ma and Ying (2021) by considering the final phase of training, where the network's generalization capacity is enhanced. The authors introduce new metrics such as volume compression and maximum local sensitivity (MLS) to quantify the compression of neural representations. They demonstrate that these metrics are strongly correlated with sharpness, suggesting that flat minima lead to simpler, less likely to overfit models. The paper also explores how these metrics relate to test loss and generalization, albeit with some limitations in the experimental scope and the clarity of the theoretical connections.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide new explicit formulas for the bounds of volume compression and MLS, which are reparametrization-invariant.
- The paper offers a dual perspective on generalization in neural networks, connecting properties in parameter space and representation space.
- It introduces new metrics such as volume compression and MLS, which are strongly correlated with sharpness, providing a novel approach to understanding the relationship between flatness and compression in neural networks.
- The paper is original in its exploration of the final phase of learning, which is less explored in the literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed metrics and the assumptions made in the analysis.
- The experiments are limited to VGG10 and MLP networks, which might not fully capture the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of clarity in some theoretical connections, particularly in Section 3.1, which could be improved with additional explanations or examples.
- The paper does not adequately address the contradictory results in the literature regarding the relationship between sharpness and generalization, which could be better resolved with a more comprehensive discussion.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate that sharpness is the dominant factor driving compression, as other factors like batch size and learning rate also influence the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed metrics and the assumptions made in the analysis?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the final phase of learning for their analysis, especially given the contradictory results in the literature?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical connections, particularly in Section 3.1, to make them clearer and more accessible to readers?
- How do the authors address the contradictory results in the literature regarding the relationship between sharpness and generalization?
- Could the authors clarify the role of flatness in the final phase of learning, especially in relation to the contradictory results mentioned?
- How do the authors plan to address the concerns about the experimental design, particularly the choice of learning rate and batch size, which might influence the results?
