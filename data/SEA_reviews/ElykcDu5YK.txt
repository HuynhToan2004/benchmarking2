 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel meta-Bayesian optimization (meta-BO) algorithm tailored for risk measures, specifically focusing on Value-at-Risk (VaR) and Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). The algorithm leverages a "no-regret query set" (NQS) to optimize risk measures across multiple tasks, ensuring invariance to scaling and vertical shifts of the black-box function. It also introduces a recommendation strategy that suggests an input as the result of the optimization, with a proof of its instantaneous regret approaching zero. Theoretical analysis and empirical results are provided, demonstrating the algorithm's robustness against harmful tasks and its ability to handle multiple tasks efficiently.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel algorithm for meta-BO of risk measures, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed algorithm is theoretically sound, with proofs provided for its performance guarantees.
- The algorithm is designed to be robust against harmful tasks, ensuring it remains effective even when previous tasks were not beneficial.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and includes a comprehensive empirical evaluation that supports the theoretical claims.
- The algorithm's flexibility in choosing any input query from the no-regret query set (NQS) enhances its applicability across various scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implications of the algorithm's assumptions, particularly concerning the knowledge of the distribution of the environmental random variables.
- The empirical evaluation could be improved by including more real-world datasets and comparisons with other meta-BO algorithms to better establish the algorithm's effectiveness.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other meta-BO algorithms, which could help in understanding the algorithm's relative performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the algorithm, especially in large-scale applications.
- The paper's theoretical contributions, while robust, could be better integrated into the main body of the text to enhance readability and clarity.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of assuming the knowledge of the distribution of the environmental random variables?
- How does the algorithm perform when the number of previous tasks is large, and what are the computational implications in such scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more details on the empirical evaluation, particularly on the choice of real-world datasets and the comparison with other meta-BO algorithms?
- How does the algorithm handle the computational complexity associated with the optimization of risk measures, especially in large-scale applications?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the empirical evaluation, particularly regarding the choice of real-world datasets and the assumptions about the random variables?
- How does the algorithm perform when the noise level is high, and what are the implications of this on the algorithm's effectiveness?
