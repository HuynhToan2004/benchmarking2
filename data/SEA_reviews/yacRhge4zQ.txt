 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for modeling trustworthy machine learning as a multi-objective multi-agent optimization problem, focusing on fairness and privacy. It proposes a game-theoretic approach, termed SpecGame, which involves regulators and model builders interacting to ensure compliance with fairness and privacy constraints. The paper presents ParetoPlay, an equilibrium search algorithm that operates on the Pareto frontier to achieve optimal solutions. The framework is evaluated through simulations and experiments, demonstrating the sub-optimality of single-agent frameworks and the importance of regulators' initial specifications. The paper also discusses the challenges in estimating privacy costs and proposes methods to address these issues.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework that formulates trustworthy machine learning as a multi-objective multi-agent optimization problem, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- It proposes a game-theoretic approach to model the relationship between regulators and model builders, which is a novel and effective way to address trust in machine learning.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments and simulations conducted are comprehensive and demonstrate the sub-optimality of single-agent frameworks, highlighting the importance of regulators' initial specifications.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of trustworthy machine learning, which is crucial for the deployment of AI systems in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how privacy costs can be estimated, which is a critical issue in the context of trustworthy machine learning.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed ParetoPlay algorithm and its scalability to larger datasets and more complex models.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of information asymmetry between regulators and model builders, which is a significant challenge in real-world scenarios.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets and do not include a broader range of datasets, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed framework, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the research.
- The paper's focus on fairness and privacy objectives may limit its applicability to other important trustworthy machine learning objectives such as robustness and reliability.

**Questions:**
- How does the paper address the issue of privacy cost estimation, and what methods are used to ensure the accuracy of these estimates?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the ParetoPlay algorithm?
- How does the proposed framework handle information asymmetry between regulators and model builders, and what strategies are recommended to mitigate this issue?
- In the context of the experiments, how were the datasets chosen, and what criteria were used to select them?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their proposed framework and how these limitations might affect the practical applicability of the research?
- How does the proposed framework ensure that it can be extended to include other important trustworthy machine learning objectives beyond fairness and privacy?
