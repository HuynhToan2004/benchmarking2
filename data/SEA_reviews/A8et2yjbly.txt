 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel cross-modality masked pre-training method for visible-infrared person re-identification (VI-ReID). This method utilizes random channel exchange augmentation to generate color-irrelevant images, which are then masked using a shared random mask for training. The approach is validated on two datasets, SYSU-MM01 and RegDB, showing significant improvements over existing methods. The methodology involves a lightweight decoder for reconstructing masked areas, which is designed to enhance the training process by focusing on the visible and generated images. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited technical contribution and the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel cross-modality masked pre-training method for visible-infrared person re-identification, which is a first in this field.
- The proposed method is technically sound, with a lightweight decoder for reconstructing masked areas, which makes the pre-training process more efficient.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with comprehensive ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on two visible-infrared person re-identification datasets, SYSU-MM01 and RegDB.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the impact of different masking strategies, mask sizes, and mask ratios on the performance of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comparison with state-of-the-art methods such as MCLNet and DDAG, which are crucial for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The technical contribution of the paper is limited as it primarily employs a lightweight decoder and masked pre-training techniques, which have been previously used in other works.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of modality misalignment between pre-training and training data, which is a significant challenge in the field of visible-infrared person re-identification.
- The experiments are limited to two datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method across different datasets.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the impact of modality misalignment and the alignment of visible and infrared images in the datasets, which are critical for the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with state-of-the-art methods such as MCLNet and DDAG in terms of performance and technical contribution?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of modality misalignment and the alignment of visible and infrared images in the datasets on the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of modality misalignment between pre-training and training data?
- Can the proposed method be extended to other modalities such as visible-thermal person re-identification?
- Why is the proposed method more effective than existing methods, and what are the specific advantages of the proposed method over these existing methods?
