 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Reprompting, a novel method for automatically generating Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompts for Large Language Models (LLMs) using Gibbs sampling. This approach allows for the generation of CoT prompts that can be tailored to specific tasks, improving performance on various benchmarks. The methodology involves sampling from a joint distribution of CoT prompts, which are then refined through an iterative process. The paper demonstrates that Reprompting can outperform existing methods, including human-written CoT prompts, on several benchmarks. However, concerns about the method's scalability, the complexity of its implementation, and the generalizability of the results across different LLMs and tasks have been raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to automatically generating CoT prompts, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it easy to understand and replicate.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods, including human-written CoT prompts, on several benchmarks.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's performance and its impact on different models, which is useful for understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of Gibbs sampling to generate CoT prompts is a novel and interesting approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for large datasets.
- The method's complexity and the need for multiple LLMs to implement it may limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not provide sufficient comparisons with other automatic prompting methods, which could help contextualize the results.
- The generalizability of the results across different LLMs and tasks is not adequately addressed, raising concerns about the method's applicability in more varied settings.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as the potential for overfitting to the training data and the impact of different model sizes on performance.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the method scales with larger datasets and the computational resources required?
- How does the method perform when applied to other datasets or tasks beyond those tested in the paper?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the rejection probability in the method's performance?
- What are the implications of using different model sizes (e.g., 13B vs. 70B) on the method's effectiveness?
- How does the method compare in terms of cost-effectiveness and practical applicability to other automatic prompting methods?
