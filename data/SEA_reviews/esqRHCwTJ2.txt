 **Summary:**
The paper explores the dynamics of machine learning (ML) models in a strategic setting where agents adapt their behaviors based on the retraining of ML models. It examines how model-annotated data, generated under agents' strategic feedback, affect the model's performance and fairness over time. The authors propose an early stopping mechanism to enhance fairness and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments on both synthetic and semi-synthetic data. The paper discusses how retraining with strategic feedback can lead to increased acceptance rates but potentially decreased qualification rates, and how this impacts the overall fairness and accuracy of the model.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem of retraining machine learning models with strategic feedback, which is a crucial aspect of model training in real-world applications.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust and comprehensive, offering insights into the dynamics of model retraining with strategic feedback.
- The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The experiments conducted are well-designed and support the theoretical findings, providing evidence for the proposed methods.
- The paper introduces an early stopping mechanism to enhance fairness, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on demographic parity as a fairness metric, which may not be the most appropriate for all scenarios. Other fairness metrics such as equalized odds or disparate impact could also be relevant and should be discussed.
- The experimental setup is limited to synthetic and semi-synthetic data, which may not fully represent real-world scenarios. More diverse and realistic data sets could be used to validate the findings.
- The paper assumes that agents have perfect knowledge of the classifier, which may not hold in practical scenarios. This could affect the validity of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the practical implications and limitations of the proposed methods, especially in terms of their scalability and applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address how the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis align with real-world scenarios, which could affect the reliability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors discuss the potential implications of using other fairness metrics such as equalized odds or disparate impact in the context of the strategic feedback scenario?
- How do the results of this study compare with other existing studies on strategic classification?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the proposed early stopping mechanism could be applied in practical scenarios, especially in terms of scalability and computational efficiency?
- How do the results of the study hold up under different assumptions about the distribution of features and labels, particularly in scenarios where these distributions are not uniform or Gaussian?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and discuss how these assumptions align with real-world scenarios?
