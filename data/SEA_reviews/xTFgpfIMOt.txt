 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ROAM (Robust Autonomous Modulation), a method designed to enable robots to adapt to out-of-distribution scenarios by leveraging a diverse repertoire of pre-trained behaviors. The approach uses a cross-entropy regularizer to fine-tune value functions, which helps in the rapid selection of the most appropriate behavior based on the current state. This method was tested on a quadruped robot, showing improvements over existing methods in both simulation and real-world settings. The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical results, demonstrating the effectiveness of ROAM in adapting to new situations. However, concerns were raised about the generalizability of the method to more complex tasks and the lack of detailed analysis on the behavior selection process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a clear presentation of the methodology, making it easy to follow.
- The problem addressed is significant and relevant to the robotics community, focusing on the ability of robots to adapt to new situations without human intervention.
- The methodology is well-explained, with a detailed theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is structured clearly, with a logical flow from introduction to methodology, results, and conclusion.
- The paper is original in its approach, using a cross-entropy regularizer to fine-tune value functions, which is a novel contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the behavior selection process, particularly how the method chooses the most appropriate behavior in each situation.
- The method's generalizability is limited, as it requires a diverse set of pre-trained behaviors which may not be available for all tasks.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of its advantages and limitations.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly robust, with some results (e.g., in Figure 3) showing significant variation and potential bias.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its scalability and applicability to more complex tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed analysis on the behavior selection process, including how the method chooses the most appropriate behavior in each situation?
- How does the method perform when the set of pre-trained behaviors is limited or not diverse enough?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the conditions under which the experiments were conducted and the metrics used to evaluate the results?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and scalability?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental results, particularly the variation in performance across different tasks and the potential biases in the data?
