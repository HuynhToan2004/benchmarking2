 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Hybrid Directional Graph Neural Network (HDGNN), a novel architecture designed to balance equivariance and expressiveness in molecular property prediction. HDGNN integrates both strictly equivariant operations and learnable modules, leveraging spherical harmonics and the Clebsch-Gordan tensor product to achieve approximate equivariance. The network is evaluated on benchmarks such as QM9 and IS2RE, demonstrating competitive performance against existing methods. The authors explore the balance between expressiveness and equivariance through a hybrid approach, combining learnable modules with strict equivariant operations. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its complex design, lack of clarity in presentation, and insufficient experimental validation, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the impact of hyperparameters.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel hybrid approach that effectively balances equivariance and expressiveness in molecular property prediction, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the expressive power of the proposed method, supported by theoretical and empirical evidence.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The experiments conducted are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and tasks.
- The inclusion of an ablation study provides valuable insights into the design choices of the model, highlighting the importance of the attention module and the balance between equivariance and expressiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in presentation, particularly in the explanation of key concepts such as spherical harmonics and the CG tensor product, which are crucial to understanding the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with some benchmarks showing worse performance compared to simpler models like DimeNet++.
- The computational efficiency of the proposed method is not adequately addressed, raising concerns about its practical applicability, especially in large-scale datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for overfitting on smaller datasets and the impact of hyperparameters on the model's performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly those that also balance equivariance and expressiveness, to better establish the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the computational efficiency of the proposed method, particularly how it scales with increasing dataset size?
- How does the model perform when using different hyperparameters, such as the cutoff radius and the degree of spherical harmonics?
- Can you provide more details on the training and inference times for the HDGNN, compared to other baseline models?
- How does the model handle the balance between expressiveness and equivariance, particularly in terms of the impact of hyperparameters on this balance?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the attention module in the ablation study and its impact on the model's performance?
- How does the model ensure that the learnable module does not compromise the equivariance of the model, as suggested by the ablation study?
- Is there a potential for the model to overfit on smaller datasets, and how does this affect the generalizability of the results?
