 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Think-on-Graph (ToG), a novel method for integrating large language models (LLMs) with knowledge graphs (KGs) to enhance reasoning capabilities. ToG employs a beam search algorithm to iteratively explore reasoning paths in KGs, using LLMs to prune entities and relations based on question context. This approach allows for the dynamic exploration of KGs without additional training, which is a significant advantage over traditional methods. The method is evaluated across various datasets and demonstrates improved performance over existing methods, although the improvements are sometimes marginal. The paper also discusses the limitations of the approach, such as the reliance on the quality of KGs and the potential for hallucinations in LLM outputs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, ToG, which integrates LLMs with knowledge graphs, providing a new approach to knowledge-based question answering (KBQA).
- The methodology is well-described, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the proposed method.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering a range of datasets and baseline methods, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the ToG method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology and its implications.
- The method is flexible and can be applied to different LLMs and knowledge graphs without additional training, making it a versatile approach.
- The method addresses the limitations of traditional LLM-KG integration methods by enabling the LLM to dynamically explore KGs, reducing the reliance on pre-defined query templates.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the reliance on the quality of KGs and the potential for hallucinations in LLM outputs.
- The experimental results show only marginal improvements over existing methods, which raises questions about the practical significance of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other recent state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of its relative performance.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the computational cost and efficiency of the ToG method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently address how the method handles the complexity of multi-hop reasoning tasks, which could be a significant challenge for the proposed approach.
- The method's reliance on the LLM's ability to correctly prune entities and relations based on question context could lead to incorrect reasoning paths, which could undermine the effectiveness of the method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost and efficiency of the ToG method, particularly in terms of the number of API calls and the time required for each call?
- How does the proposed method handle the complexity of multi-hop reasoning tasks, and what strategies are used to ensure that the method can effectively handle such tasks?
- Can the authors clarify how the LLM's pruning ability is ensured to be accurate, and what measures are in place to mitigate the risk of hallucinations in the LLM outputs?
- In the experimental results, why do the results of ToG and ToG-R differ significantly, and what does this imply about the effectiveness of the method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the method's performance on datasets where it underperforms compared to other methods, and what insights can be gained from these results?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the KG contains outdated or inaccurate information, and what measures are in place to ensure that the method does not propagate such errors?
