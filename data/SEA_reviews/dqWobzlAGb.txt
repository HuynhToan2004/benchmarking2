 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the embedding of connectomes into various non-Euclidean geometries, including hyperbolic, spherical, and Solv geometries, using a simulated annealing approach. The authors evaluate the performance of these embeddings across different brain connectomes and compare them to existing methods. The study explores how these embeddings affect the network's structure and functionality, providing insights into the potential utility of these geometries in understanding brain connectivity. Despite the paper's comprehensive experimental setup, it faces criticism for its limited novelty and lack of deep theoretical analysis, which could enhance the understanding of the embedding process and its implications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear and concise structure that facilitates understanding.
- The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of various non-Euclidean geometries, including hyperbolic, spherical, and Solv geometries, which is the first of its kind.
- The paper includes a thorough evaluation of different embedding algorithms, including simulated annealing, which is a novel approach for this problem.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments and simulations, providing a robust validation of their findings.
- The paper is significant in its potential impact on the field of brain connectome modeling, offering a new perspective on how different geometries can be used to understand brain connectivity.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the embedding process, which could enhance the understanding of the method's effectiveness and limitations.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its methodology from existing works in the field.
- The paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the embedding process, particularly how it affects the structure and functionality of the connectomes.
- There are concerns about the computational efficiency and scalability of the embedding process, which could limit its applicability to larger and more complex connectomes.
- The paper's focus on connectomes as graphs may not fully capture the complexities of brain networks, which could affect the interpretation and applicability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the theoretical aspects of the embedding process, particularly how it relates to the properties of the connectomes and the choice of embedding geometry?
- How does the embedding process affect the structural and functional properties of the connectomes, and what are the implications of these changes for understanding brain connectivity?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the simulated annealing algorithm and how it compares to other optimization methods that could be used for the embedding process?
- How does the computational efficiency of the embedding process scale with the size and complexity of the connectomes, and what are the practical limitations of the method in terms of its applicability to real-world brain networks?
- Could the authors discuss the potential implications of their findings for the broader field of network science, particularly in terms of the applicability of non-Euclidean geometries to other types of complex networks?
