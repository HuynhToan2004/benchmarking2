 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the data contamination in Large Language Models (LLMs) by analyzing the correlation between GitHub presence and LLM performance on coding and mathematical problem-solving datasets, specifically Codeforces and Project Euler. The study leverages the known training cut-off dates of GPT-4 and GPT-3.5-Turbo to partition benchmark examples into pre- and post-cutoff subsets, comparing LLM performance before and after the cut-off. The authors find that LLMs are likely exposed to these datasets during their training, as evidenced by a statistically significant positive association between a problem's presence on GitHub and each LLM's test case pass rate for problems released before the GPT training cutoff. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the evaluation of LLMs and proposes best practices for benchmarking in the era of LLMs trained on web-scale data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the problem and the research questions.
- The methodology is innovative, utilizing the training cut-off dates of GPT-4 and GPT-3.5-Turbo to partition benchmark examples into pre- and post-cutoff subsets, providing a novel approach to studying data contamination in LLMs.
- The analysis is comprehensive, covering multiple models and datasets, and the results are robust, showing a strong correlation between GitHub presence and LLM performance for problems released before the GPT training cutoff.
- The paper provides a thorough longitudinal analysis of data contamination in LLMs, which is the first of its kind, and offers valuable insights into the effects of data contamination on LLM performance.
- The dataset construction and evaluation framework are open-sourced, which facilitates further research and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the GitHub Presence metric and the methodology used to measure it, which could lead to confusion and questions about its reliability and applicability.
- The study is limited to specific models (GPT-4 and GPT-3.5-Turbo) and datasets (Codeforces and Project Euler), which may not fully represent the broader LLM landscape or the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on how the findings could be applied to other models or datasets, nor does it explore the impact of different training datasets on LLM performance.
- The paper's conclusions are limited by the scope of the study, and the generalizability of the findings to other LLMs or datasets is unclear.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the study in detail, which could affect the credibility and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the GitHub Presence metric, particularly how it is calculated and what it measures?
- How do the findings of this study apply to other LLMs, such as those from different companies or open-source models?
- What are the implications of the findings for the evaluation of LLMs, and how can these insights be used to improve benchmarking practices?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their study in more detail, particularly how these limitations might affect the generalizability and applicability of the findings?
- How do the findings compare to other benchmarks or datasets that have not been used in this study?
- Can the authors provide more details on the methodology used to measure GitHub Presence and the potential impact of different training datasets on LLM performance?
