 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Byzantine robust algorithm called Momentum Screening (MS) for nonconvex federated learning, focusing on the adaptivity to Byzantine fractions and minimax optimality for the class of nonconvex smooth local objectives. The algorithm is designed to achieve the best optimization error of O(δ2ζmax2) for nonconvex smooth local objectives satisfying the ζmax-uniform gradient heterogeneity condition under δ-Byzantine fraction, which is superior to the best known error rate of O(δζmax2) for local objectives satisfying the ζmax-mean heterogeneity condition when δ≤(ζmax/ζmax)2. The paper also derives an algorithm-independent lower bound for local objectives satisfying ζmax-uniform gradient heterogeneity condition and shows the minimax optimality of the proposed method on this class. The authors validate the superiority of their method over existing robust aggregation algorithms through numerical experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper proposes a novel Byzantine robust algorithm called Momentum Screening (MS) for nonconvex federated learning, which is adaptive to the Byzantine fraction and achieves minimax optimality for the class of nonconvex smooth local objectives.
- The proposed algorithm is simple, intuitive, and can be easily implemented, making it accessible for practical applications.
- The paper provides both theoretical and numerical results, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method over existing robust aggregation algorithms.
- The convergence analysis is well-organized, and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow.
- The algorithm is adaptive to the Byzantine fraction, which is a desirable feature in practical scenarios.
- The paper derives an algorithm-independent lower bound for local objectives satisfying ζmax-uniform gradient heterogeneity condition and shows the minimax optimality of the proposed method on this class.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the experimental results section, particularly in the explanation of the experimental setup and the results, which could benefit from additional details and visual aids.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithm, particularly in terms of its applicability and scalability in different scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing works, particularly in terms of the algorithm's novelty and its theoretical contributions.
- The paper's theoretical analysis heavily relies on the assumptions of gradient heterogeneity, which might not hold in all practical scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the results.
- The paper's comparisons with other algorithms, such as GrAdient Splitting (GAS), are not sufficiently robust, and the results might not be convincingly superior.
