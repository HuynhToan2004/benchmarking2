 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to learning customized reward models (RMs) for large language models (LLMs) by proposing a three-stage training scheme: base LM training, general RM fine-tuning, and customized RM fine-tuning. The authors collect a domain-specific preference dataset, which includes responses from four practical domains, to evaluate the effectiveness of this training scheme. The paper also explores the use of imitation learning on customized preferences and general preference data enrichment to preserve RMs' general preferring ability while fitting customized human preferences. The results show that these methods can effectively preserve the general preferring ability of RMs while fitting customized human preferences. However, the paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation on the domain-specific dataset and comparisons with other datasets and methods, which limits the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method for learning customized reward models is novel and interesting, with a focus on personalized LLM applications.
- The authors collect a domain-specific preference dataset, which is a significant contribution to the field, and use it to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper includes a variety of experiments to show the effectiveness of the proposed method, including an ablation study on different training strategies.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear figures and a detailed description of the training stages and datasets used.
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic, which is highly relevant to current research in the field of LLMs and reward models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation on the domain-specific dataset, particularly in terms of the quality of the dataset and its impact on the results.
- There is a lack of comparison with other datasets and methods, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the number of runs and the statistical significance of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential directions for future research.
- There are some minor issues with the presentation, such as typos and unclear figures, which could be improved.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition of "general preferences" and provide examples to illustrate how they differ from "customized preferences"?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods for learning customized reward models, such as those using contrastive learning or reject sampling?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the number of runs and the statistical significance of the results?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other datasets or in different domains?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and suggest directions for future research?
- In terms of the dataset collection, how were the queries and responses selected, and what steps were taken to ensure the quality and diversity of the dataset?
