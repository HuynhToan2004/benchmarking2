 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework, the Conditional Information Bottleneck (CIB), for time series imputation, focusing on preserving temporal dependencies. The method leverages a variational decomposition of the CIB objective to create an Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) that incorporates both reconstruction and conditional regularization terms. The proposed method is evaluated on multiple datasets, showing improvements over existing methods. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the CIB approach, providing a theoretical analysis of its effects and demonstrating its effectiveness through experiments. However, concerns are raised regarding the clarity of the paper, the completeness of the experimental setup, and the fairness of the comparisons made.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to time series imputation by leveraging the Information Bottleneck (IB) framework, which is both innovative and well-explained.
- The methodology is supported by a thorough theoretical analysis and empirical results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, making it accessible to readers.
- The use of variational decomposition to transform the conditional IB objective into a learnable form is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper addresses the important problem of time series imputation, which is crucial for various downstream applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the definition and explanation of key terms and concepts, which could hinder understanding for readers unfamiliar with the subject.
- The experimental setup and results presentation are not comprehensive, with some results missing from the main text and a lack of detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which detract from its professional presentation.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for a balanced assessment of its contributions and shortcomings.
- The paper's comparison with existing methods, particularly those using different approaches like autoregressive models and transformers, is not fair or comprehensive.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of missing data in the time series, which is crucial for the application of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definitions and uses of terms like "redundant information" and "information" in the context of the paper?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of missing data in time series, such as missing at random (MAR) and missing not at random (MNAR)?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of the conditional regularization term in the ELBO, and how does it affect the model's performance?
- How does the paper handle the issue of missing data in the time series, and what strategies are used to ensure the model's robustness against different types of missingness?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the data sources and the specifics of the experimental conditions?
- How does the proposed method compare with state-of-the-art methods, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the practical applicability of the method?
