 **Summary:**
The paper explores identifiable representation learning in a multi-task setting, where tasks are assumed to share a common latent structure. It extends previous work on identifiable representation learning to the multi-task case by leveraging a causal structure across tasks. The authors propose a method that identifies latent factors up to permutations and scaling, which is a stronger form of identifiability than linear identifiability. They demonstrate this through both synthetic and real-world experiments, showing that their method can recover latent factors with high accuracy. The paper also discusses the implications of these results for causal representation learning and the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and well-presented results.
- The authors propose a novel method that identifies latent factors up to permutations and scaling, which is a stronger form of identifiability than linear identifiability.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the methodology, including the use of a causal structure to identify latent factors and the application of maximum likelihood estimation to the latent factors.
- The paper includes both synthetic and real-world experiments, which demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed method.
- The results are supported by a solid theoretical framework, which is well-explained and supported by empirical evidence.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the assumptions made in the causal structure and their implications, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions may not hold.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the assumptions of linearity and the lack of robustness to noise or deviations from the assumed causal structure.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the experiments, particularly in the synthetic data experiments, which could benefit from clearer descriptions and more detailed results.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing works, particularly in terms of the assumptions and methodologies used.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the results, especially in terms of their practical applicability and the potential for future research.
