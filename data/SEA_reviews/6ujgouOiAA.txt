 **Summary:**
The paper introduces INSTINCT, a novel approach for optimizing instruction prompts for large language models (LLMs) using a neural bandit algorithm that replaces the Gaussian process in Bayesian optimization with a neural network surrogate. The method leverages a pre-trained transformer model to learn hidden representations, enhancing the performance of the LLM by optimizing the soft prompts. INSTINCT is evaluated against existing methods such as InstructZero and APE, showing superior performance on various tasks. The paper also includes extensive ablation studies and experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the method. However, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the method due to its dependence on a pre-trained transformer model and the limited scope of the evaluation tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for using a neural network surrogate to replace the Gaussian process in Bayesian optimization.
- The proposed method, INSTINCT, is innovative and shows promising results, outperforming existing methods such as InstructZero and APE on various tasks.
- The paper includes extensive ablation studies and experiments, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the method and provide insights into the contributions of different components.
- The use of a pre-trained transformer model to learn hidden representations enhances the performance of the LLM, and the method is shown to be more effective than InstructZero in certain scenarios.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear overview of the method and a detailed explanation of the experimental setup and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's dependence on a pre-trained transformer model might limit its generalizability and applicability to different LLMs, especially those that are not pre-trained or have different architectures.
- The evaluation tasks are limited to specific instruction induction tasks, which may not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness in more complex or varied scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other relevant methods such as InstructZero and APE, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for overfitting to the validation set, which could limit the method's effectiveness in real-world applications.
- The method's reliance on a pre-trained model and the need for a validation set might limit its practical applicability, especially in scenarios where such resources are not available.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the method, such as the potential for overfitting to the validation set and the method's dependence on a pre-trained model.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the pre-trained transformer model on the method's performance and generalizability?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability with other methods like InstructZero and APE?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and results, particularly in the ablation studies and the comparison with other methods?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting to the validation set, and what measures are in place to mitigate this risk?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the method to other types of optimization problems or LLMs that are not pre-trained?
- Can the authors provide more details on the method's scalability and how it performs with different types of LLMs and instruction sets?
