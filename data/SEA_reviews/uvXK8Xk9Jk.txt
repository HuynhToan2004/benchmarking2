 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the stability of sparsifying activation functions in deep neural networks (DNNs) and convolutional neural networks (CNNs) during training, focusing on the clipped ReLU and soft-thresholding activation functions. The authors demonstrate that these functions are unstable due to their variance map, which has a fixed point with a derivative equal to one, leading to exploding gradients. To address this instability, the paper introduces clipped variants of these functions, showing that they can maintain stable variance maps and achieve high sparsity levels (up to 85%) while maintaining accuracy. The paper provides theoretical analysis and experimental validation using MNIST and CIFAR10 datasets, highlighting the potential of these clipped functions in enhancing the stability and efficiency of DNNs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue in the field of deep learning, focusing on the stability of sparsifying activation functions, which is a crucial aspect for the efficient training and deployment of neural networks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, supported by rigorous mathematical proofs and experiments, which demonstrates the stability of clipped activation functions and their ability to achieve high sparsity levels.
- The experimental results are well-documented and support the theoretical claims, showing that the proposed clipped functions can be effective in inducing sparsity while maintaining network performance.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and intuitive visualizations that aid in understanding the complex mathematical concepts and experimental results.
- The paper contributes to the ongoing research in the large-width limit of neural networks, providing a novel perspective on the stability of sparsifying activation functions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on small-scale networks, which may not fully demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed clipped activation functions in larger, more complex networks.
- The experiments are limited in scope, using only MNIST and CIFAR10 datasets, which may not adequately represent the performance of the clipped functions in more diverse or challenging scenarios.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other activation functions, which could help in understanding the relative effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed clipped functions.
- The theoretical analysis and experimental results are not sufficiently integrated, which could lead to a disjointed reading experience and a lack of clarity on the practical implications of the theoretical findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of how the stability of the variance map translates into practical benefits such as faster training times or improved accuracy.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results on larger and more complex datasets, such as ImageNet, to demonstrate the scalability and effectiveness of the clipped activation functions in practical scenarios?
- How do the clipped activation functions perform in comparison to other activation functions, such as Leaky ReLU or Swish, in terms of sparsity induction and network performance?
- Is there a potential for the clipped activation functions to induce sparsity in a more uniform or balanced manner across different layers or features of the network?
- Can the authors elaborate on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly how the stability of the variance map translates into practical benefits such as faster training times or improved accuracy?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the clipped activation functions to other types of neural networks, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or transformers, to broaden the applicability of the proposed method?
