 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the performance of DNNs in object detection tasks under peripheral vision conditions, comparing them to human performance. It introduces a new dataset, COCO-Periph, which is transformed to simulate peripheral vision using the Texture Tiling Model (TTM). The study reveals that DNNs underperform compared to humans, particularly in high eccentricities, but training on COCO-Periph can reduce this gap. The paper also explores the effects of object size and clutter on DNN performance, showing that models trained on this dataset exhibit improved robustness to geometric corruptions. Despite these contributions, the paper is criticized for its limited scope, focusing only on object detection and not addressing broader applications of peripheral vision in other tasks. The methodology is also questioned for its adherence to standard psychophysics and its lack of rigorous comparison to human performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and presents a novel approach to evaluating DNNs under peripheral vision conditions, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The introduction of the COCO-Periph dataset is a valuable resource for future research, providing a large-scale dataset for studying peripheral vision in DNNs.
- The paper addresses an important and understudied area of research, focusing on the performance of DNNs in simulated peripheral vision conditions, which is relevant for understanding the differences between human and machine vision.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the paper provides a thorough analysis of the results, including comparisons between human and machine performance and the effects of training on the COCO-Periph dataset.
- The paper includes a detailed description of the dataset creation process, which is crucial for understanding the experimental setup and the results obtained.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited as it only evaluates DNNs in object detection tasks, which may not fully capture the potential applications of peripheral vision in other areas like scene understanding or image generation.
- The methodology for comparing DNN performance to human performance is not standard, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the Texture Tiling Model (TTM) and how it might affect the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the dataset, such as the potential for overfitting due to the small number of images used for testing.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the ethical implications of the work, particularly concerning the potential misuse of the findings in areas like surveillance or privacy.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons between human and machine performance, especially in terms of the critical eccentricity and the correlation between the two.

**Questions:**
- How does the performance of the DNNs compare to human performance in terms of critical eccentricity and correlation?
- Can the authors provide more details on the methodology used to compare DNN performance to human performance, particularly how the DNNs were trained and tested?
- How does the TTM model perform in terms of predicting human performance, and how does this affect the interpretation of the results?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly how the TTM transformations were applied and how they affect the results?
- How does the paper address the ethical implications of the work, especially concerning the potential misuse of the findings in areas like surveillance or privacy?
- Could the authors expand the scope of the study to include other tasks beyond object detection, such as scene understanding or image generation, to better understand the generalizability of the findings?
