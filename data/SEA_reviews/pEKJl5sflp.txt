 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a Scalable Modular Network (SMN) designed to enhance adaptive learning capabilities in modular neural networks. The core innovation is the introduction of an "agreement router," which iteratively selects and composes different specialist modules based on input-output agreements, utilizing both local and global module interactions. This approach allows for the integration of new modules into pre-trained networks, improving generalization and adaptation capabilities. The paper validates these claims through experiments on a toy min-max game and a few-shot image classification task, demonstrating SMN's ability to generalize to new distributions and adapt to new tasks. Despite its novel approach, the paper faces criticism for its limited experimental validation, particularly in terms of the number of datasets and the depth of comparative analysis with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to modular neural networks by enabling adaptive learning capability and supporting the integration of new modules after pre-training, which is a significant advancement over traditional modular network designs.
- The agreement router, a key component of the SMN, effectively considers both local and global module interactions, which is a novel approach that enhances the model's ability to generalize and adapt to new tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for academic papers.
- The authors provide theoretical proof that the agreement routing algorithm naturally tends to produce sparse module selection, which is a practical benefit in avoiding trivial solutions.
- The experiments conducted on a toy min-max game and a few-shot image classification task demonstrate the effectiveness of the SMN in generalizing to new distributions and exhibiting sample-efficient adaptation to new tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing modular network models, particularly in terms of their adaptive learning capabilities and the integration of new modules.
- The experiments are limited to a few datasets, which may not fully validate the claims made by the authors.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as similar routing methods have been previously proposed, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from these existing methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for large-scale modular networks.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its practical applicability and potential challenges.
- The paper does not provide a clear rationale for the choice of the min-max game as a benchmark for evaluating the generalization capabilities of the SMN.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing modular network models, particularly in terms of their adaptive learning capabilities and the integration of new modules?
- How does the SMN perform in terms of computational resources and scalability, especially when compared to traditional modular networks?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential future directions for research?
- Why was the min-max game chosen as a benchmark for evaluating the generalization capabilities of the SMN, and how does it adequately represent real-world scenarios?
- How does the agreement router's iterative message passing process improve the module selection process, and what are the specific advantages of this approach over other routing methods?
