 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework, MIDAS, designed to enhance robot manipulation using multimodal prompts. It leverages a two-stage training pipeline: inverse dynamics pretraining and multi-task fine-tuning, which significantly improves performance over existing methods. The methodology includes a multimodal prompt encoder that integrates visual and textual details, and a decoder-only architecture that models dependencies among action dimensions. The framework is tested on the VIMA-BENCH, achieving state-of-the-art results and demonstrating strong in-context learning capabilities. Despite its strengths, the paper has some limitations, such as the lack of generalization to more complex tasks and a need for more detailed comparisons with other methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel method for learning robot manipulation with multimodal prompts, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The introduction of a two-stage training pipeline that includes inverse dynamics pretraining and multi-task fine-tuning is a novel approach that effectively improves performance.
- The multimodal prompt encoder is a well-designed component that integrates visual and textual details, enhancing the model's ability to understand and respond to complex prompts.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are thorough, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches.
- The use of a decoder-only architecture and the modeling of dependencies among action dimensions are innovative approaches that contribute to the effectiveness of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other methods, particularly those that use similar approaches or have achieved significant results in the field.
- The generalization of the method to more complex tasks beyond those tested in the VIMA-BENCH is not clearly demonstrated.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights into its applicability and potential improvements.
- There is a lack of clarity in some figures and explanations, which could hinder understanding of the method and its implementation.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the role and impact of the residual connection in the multimodal prompt encoder.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other methods, particularly those that have achieved significant results in the field?
- How does the proposed method generalize to more complex tasks beyond those tested in the VIMA-BENCH?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the residual connection in the multimodal prompt encoder?
- How does the method perform on more complex tasks, such as those involving more complex object arrangements or different types of manipulation tasks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and inference times for the different models compared in the paper?
- How does the proposed method handle tasks that require long-term planning or complex object manipulation, such as those involving deformable or rigid objects?
