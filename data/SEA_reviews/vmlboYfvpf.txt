 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Generalized Adversarial Learning (GAL), a novel unsupervised learning paradigm aimed at calibrating large-scale question-answering models. GAL employs a multi-agent game process involving a generator, processor, and inspector. The generator produces answers, the inspector evaluates these answers and poses follow-up questions, and the processor adjusts model weights based on the inspector's feedback. The approach is tested against various baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency compared to supervised methods. The paper is commended for its innovative approach, though concerns are raised about the clarity and depth of the methodology, particularly the role and training of the inspector and the lack of detailed comparisons with other unsupervised calibration methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel unsupervised learning paradigm called Generalized Adversarial Learning (GAL), which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is clearly presented, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through empirical results, showing improvements over supervised methods in certain scenarios.
- The use of a multi-agent game process involving a generator, processor, and inspector is innovative and adds depth to the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written and easy to read, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the training of the inspector, which is a crucial component of the proposed method.
- There is a noticeable absence of comparisons with other unsupervised calibration methods, which limits the understanding of the proposed method's advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence or proof for claims made about the method's effectiveness and efficiency, particularly in terms of its computational cost and memory requirements.
- The presentation of the methodology, especially in figures and tables, is confusing and could be improved for better clarity.
- The paper does not adequately address the role of the processor and the training of the inspector, which are essential components of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the training of the inspector and how it influences the overall performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare with other unsupervised calibration methods, particularly in terms of computational cost and memory requirements?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the processor in the model and how it interacts with other components?
- How does the proposed method ensure that the model does not overfit to the training data, especially in unsupervised settings?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons with other methods and a more comprehensive ablation study?
