 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for inducing high energy-latency costs in large vision-language models (VLMs) by manipulating input images to encourage longer output sequences. The approach involves three loss functions: a delayed end-of-sequence (EOS) loss, an uncertainty loss, and a token diversity loss, optimized using a temporal weight adjustment algorithm. The method is tested across various models and datasets, showing significant increases in sequence length and energy consumption compared to original images. The paper also explores the impact of these perturbations on model performance and discusses potential limitations such as the complexity of the method and its generalizability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodologies and results.
- The authors provide a comprehensive discussion on the proposed method, including the impact of different perturbation magnitudes and the effects of various loss functions.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to induce high energy-latency costs in VLMs by crafting verbose images, which can be used to evaluate the robustness of VLMs against such attacks.
- The experiments are extensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in increasing the length of generated sequences and inducing high energy-latency costs.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method and its implications, which can be beneficial for understanding the potential risks and challenges associated with the deployment of VLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other energy-latency manipulation methods, which could provide a better understanding of the proposed method's advantages and disadvantages.
- The complexity of the method, involving three loss functions and a temporal weight adjustment algorithm, may limit its practicality and scalability.
- The generalizability of the proposed method is not thoroughly discussed, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of VLMs and tasks.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence on the attack effectiveness, such as the time required to generate a verbose image and the impact of these images on model performance.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its practical applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the time required to generate a verbose image and the computational resources needed for this process?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of VLMs, such as those designed for different tasks or those with different architectures?
- Can the authors provide more evidence on the attack effectiveness, such as the impact of verbose images on model performance and the energy-latency costs in real-world scenarios?
- How do the authors address the generalizability of the proposed method across different types of VLMs and tasks?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their method and suggest areas for future research to address these limitations?
