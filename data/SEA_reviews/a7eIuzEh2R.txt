 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MANGO, a benchmark designed to evaluate the mapping and navigation abilities of large language models (LLMs) using text-based mazes. The benchmark, consisting of 53 mazes, challenges LLMs with questions that require spatial reasoning and navigation, such as "How should you go to Attic from West of House?" and "Where are we if we go north and east from Cellar?" The paper evaluates several LLMs, including GPT-4, and shows that while these models perform poorly on the benchmark, they can benefit from improved spatial reasoning abilities in downstream tasks. The benchmark is designed to be a comprehensive test of LLMs' navigation capabilities and provides a valuable tool for future research in this area.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmark, MANGO, specifically designed to evaluate the mapping and navigation abilities of large language models (LLMs), which is a significant and under-explored area in the field.
- The benchmark is comprehensive, covering 53 complex mazes and including hundreds of destination-finding and route-finding questions, which are challenging and relevant to real-world applications.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the performance of LLMs on the benchmark, including an evaluation of GPT-4 and GPT-3.5, and identifies areas where these models struggle, such as in handling long and unseen routes.
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of the benchmark, including the evaluation of different LLMs and the identification of factors that affect performance, such as the size of the maze and the confusion level of location names.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience and facilitating understanding of the complex benchmark and its implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the benchmark, such as the potential for overfitting due to the use of the same dataset for both training and evaluation, and the generalizability of the results to other types of environments or tasks.
- The benchmark is limited to text-based mazes, which may not adequately represent real-world navigation scenarios where visual inputs are crucial.
- The paper does not include a human baseline, which could provide a more robust comparison of LLM performance against human capabilities.
- The evaluation of the benchmark is limited to a few models, and it is unclear how the results would generalize to other LLMs or different types of navigation tasks.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the data generation process and the evaluation metrics used, as well as a discussion on the potential biases in the data and the implications of these biases for the benchmark's validity.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the data generation process, including how the walkthroughs were collected and how the locations and paths were extracted from them?
- How were the evaluation metrics chosen, and could you discuss the rationale behind these choices?
- What steps were taken to mitigate potential biases in the data, and how might these biases affect the benchmark's results?
- Could you clarify the role of human annotators in the benchmark, particularly in the resolution of location names and the generation of the maze graph?
- How do the results of the benchmark compare to other existing benchmarks for LLMs, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the benchmark's validity and utility?
- How might the benchmark be adapted or extended to include other types of navigation tasks or to evaluate different types of LLMs, such as those trained on different datasets or with different architectures?
