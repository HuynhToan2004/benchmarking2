 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Neuron-to-Graph (N2G) for interpreting neurons in large language models (LLMs) by constructing a graph that represents the behavior of a neuron in response to input text. This method involves pruning and augmenting text samples to identify key tokens and contexts, which are then used to build a graph that can predict neuron activations. The paper demonstrates the utility of N2G through case studies and comparisons with baseline methods, showing that it can effectively capture neuron behavior in early layers but struggles with more complex neuron behaviors in deeper layers. The methodology involves a systematic approach to identify salient tokens and contexts, which are then used to build a graph that can be searched for specific neuron behaviors.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for interpreting neurons in LLMs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, with a clear and logical approach to building a graph representation of neuron behavior.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the methodology, including pseudocode and visualizations, which aid in understanding the method's implementation.
- The results are convincing, with the method demonstrating effectiveness in identifying neuron behaviors and providing insights into how neurons in different layers respond to different inputs.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly those that use similar techniques such as graph neural networks or other interpretability methods.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method, such as its scalability to larger models or its effectiveness in capturing more complex neuron behaviors in deeper layers.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on how the method handles polysemantic neurons, which are a known issue in LLMs.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a single model, which may not generalize well to other models or datasets.
- The method's reliance on a fixed threshold for neuron activation may limit its applicability to different models or datasets.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the method, which could be a significant concern in the context of LLM interpretability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the method handles polysemantic neurons, particularly in the context of the case studies presented?
- How does the method scale to larger models, and what are the computational costs associated with building a graph for every neuron?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method was evaluated, particularly in terms of the statistical significance of the results and the choice of evaluation metrics?
- How does the method compare to other interpretability methods, particularly those that use similar techniques such as graph neural networks?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the method, and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
- How does the method handle different types of neuron behaviors, particularly those that are not captured by the graph representation?
