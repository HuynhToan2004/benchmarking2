 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CrossDiff, a novel diffusion model for generating human motion from textual descriptions. It integrates 3D and 2D motion information using a shared transformer network, allowing cross-decoding of features into both modalities. The model is trained on a mixture of 2D and 3D motion data, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments on various benchmarks. The methodology is designed to leverage the complementary information in both modalities, aiming to improve the accuracy and diversity of generated motion sequences. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, insufficient comparison with existing methods, and lack of clarity in methodological details.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in human motion generation, specifically focusing on the use of 2D data to enhance 3D motion generation.
- The proposed method is straightforward and well-motivated, with a clear and well-organized presentation that makes the paper easy to follow.
- The experimental results are promising, showing that the method can effectively use 2D data to improve 3D motion generation performance.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and detailed descriptions of the experimental setup and results.
- The methodology introduces a novel approach to integrating 2D and 3D motion data, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the use of 2D data for motion generation.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as the integration of 2D and 3D data is seen as a straightforward extension of existing methods.
- The method's reliance on 2D data for training raises concerns about its ability to handle out-of-domain text-to-motion tasks without 2D data.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the shared-weight encoder, which is a critical component of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity in some methodological details, such as the training process and the use of different weight hyper-parameters.
- The paper's evaluation metrics are limited, and it would benefit from including more standard metrics such as R-Precision, FID, and MM Dist.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the method, such as the potential for overfitting due to the use of 2D data.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the implementation of the shared-weight encoder, including its architecture and training process?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting when using 2D data for training?
- Could the authors clarify the training process, particularly how the model is trained on both 2D and 3D data and how the weights are adjusted during training?
- How does the method perform when trained solely on 3D data, and what are the implications for out-of-domain text-to-motion tasks without 2D data?
- Could the authors include more standard metrics in their evaluations to better compare their results with existing methods?
- Can the authors provide more details on the data augmentation techniques used during training, particularly how they affect the performance of the model?
