 **Summary:**
The paper explores the federated learning (FL) generalization gap, focusing on the disparity between the training and testing distributions. It introduces an information-theoretic framework to quantify generalization errors and proposes weighted aggregation and client selection strategies to enhance FL's generalization capabilities. The paper also discusses the participation gap, where models trained on active clients may not generalize well to passive clients with distinct distributions. Extensive experiments are conducted to validate the proposed methods, showing improvements over existing baselines. However, the paper has been critiqued for its unclear assumptions, lack of theoretical rigor, and limited novelty compared to existing works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in federated learning (FL), focusing on the generalization gap between active and passive clients, which is often overlooked in existing literature.
- The introduction of an information-theoretic generalization framework is innovative and provides a novel perspective on quantifying generalization errors in FL.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions and a logical flow, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed weighted aggregation approach and client selection strategies are well-motivated and backed by theoretical results, which are supported by empirical evaluations showing the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper provides a clear and concise summary of the problem, motivation, and contributions, making it accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions and definitions, particularly in Section 3.1, are unclear and not well-explained, which could lead to confusion and misunderstanding of the proposed methods.
- The novelty of the proposed weighted aggregation and client selection strategies is questionable, as similar methods have been previously explored in the literature.
- The theoretical results, particularly Theorem 1 and Theorem 2, are not adequately explained or justified, which undermines the credibility of the proposed methods.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive review of related works, particularly those that have addressed similar problems in FL, which could provide a better context for the current study.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the choice of datasets and baselines is limited, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and scalability, which could limit their applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the assumptions and definitions in Section 3.1, particularly the notations and their meanings?
- How does the proposed weighted aggregation and client selection strategies differ from existing methods, and what are the specific advantages of the proposed methods?
- Can you provide more detailed explanations and justifications for the theoretical results, particularly Theorem 1 and Theorem 2?
- How do the proposed methods perform in scenarios with a larger number of clients, and what are the computational and communication costs associated with these methods?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of datasets and baselines, and how the results compare to more recent or diverse methods in the field?
- How do the proposed methods handle different types of data heterogeneity, such as label or feature heterogeneity, and what are the implications for the generalization performance in such scenarios?
