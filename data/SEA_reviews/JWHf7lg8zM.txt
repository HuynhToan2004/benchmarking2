 **Summary:**
The paper explores the information encoded in dense retrieval representations, specifically focusing on MultiContrievers trained from MultiBERT models. The authors employ information theoretic probing to analyze the extractability of gender and topic information, correlating these findings with retrieval performance on various datasets. The study reveals that MultiContrievers encode more information than MultiBERTs, with a focus on the impact of gender information on retrieval performance. However, the paper faces criticism for its limited scope, focusing only on a subset of BEIR datasets, and for not adequately addressing the causal relationship between gender information in representations and retrieval performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using information theoretic probing to analyze the information encoded in dense retrieval representations, which is a significant advancement in understanding the inner workings of these models.
- The analysis of the regularization effect of MultiContrievers is insightful, highlighting the impact of training methods on the information encoded in models.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of 25 MultiContrievers, which adds depth to the analysis and helps in understanding the variability in model performance.
- The paper raises important questions about the information encoded in dense retrieval representations and how it affects retrieval performance, which is a critical area for further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited as it only analyzes a subset of BEIR datasets, which may not be representative of the full range of retrieval tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the causal relationship between gender information in representations and retrieval performance, which is a significant omission given the focus on gender bias.
- The analysis of gender bias is not comprehensive enough, as it only focuses on binary gender labels and does not consider other demographic attributes like race or ethnicity.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of how the information extracted from the representations influences retrieval performance, which could have strengthened the argument.
- The paper lacks a deeper analysis of the impact of training methods on the information encoded in models, which could have provided more insights into the effectiveness of different training strategies.

**Questions:**
- Can you explain the discrepancy in the results between MultiBERT and MultiContriever in terms of the extractability of gender information?
- How does the training method used in MultiContrievers affect the information encoded in the representations, and what implications does this have for the performance of the models?
- Could you provide more details on the correlation between the extractability of gender and retrieval performance, especially in light of the mixed results presented in the paper?
- How does the paper address the potential for spurious correlations between the extractability of gender and performance, which could lead to biased results?
- Can you clarify the role of gender information in the retrieval process, especially in light of the findings that suggest gender information is not always necessary for high retrieval performance?
- How does the paper ensure that the gender information is not influencing the retrieval performance indirectly through other factors, such as the quality or quantity of training data?
