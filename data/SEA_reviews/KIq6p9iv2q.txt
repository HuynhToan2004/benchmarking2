 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of plasticity loss in neural networks under non-stationary learning conditions, focusing on mechanisms such as unit saturation, preactivation distribution shifts, unbounded parameter growth, and regression target scale. It proposes a training protocol that integrates layer normalization and L2 regularization to mitigate these issues. The paper presents a variety of experiments to validate the proposed methods, including synthetic and real-world non-stationary learning tasks, and reinforcement learning in the arcade learning environment and the WiLDS distribution shift benchmark. Despite the comprehensive analysis and proposed solutions, the paper's contributions are seen as incremental and not sufficiently distinct from existing literature.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a thorough analysis of plasticity loss in neural networks under non-stationary learning conditions.
- It identifies several mechanisms that contribute to plasticity loss, including unit saturation, preactivation distribution shifts, unbounded parameter growth, and regression target scale.
- The paper proposes a training protocol that integrates layer normalization and L2 regularization to mitigate these issues, which is validated through a variety of experiments.
- The experiments are well-designed and include a good mix of synthetic and real-world non-stationary learning tasks, as well as reinforcement learning in the arcade learning environment and the WiLDS distribution shift benchmark.
- The paper addresses a significant and important problem in the field of machine learning, which is the loss of plasticity in neural networks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contributions are seen as incremental and not sufficiently distinct from existing literature, particularly in the area of plasticity loss in neural networks.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, especially in terms of their applicability and effectiveness across different types of neural networks and non-stationary learning scenarios.
- There is a lack of comparative analysis with other existing methods for addressing plasticity loss, which could help in understanding the relative advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.
- The paper's experimental results do not consistently outperform the baseline, which raises questions about the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, which is crucial for understanding their applicability and effectiveness in practical scenarios.
- The paper's focus on specific types of neural networks (e.g., MLP, CNN, and ResNet-18) may limit the generalizability of the findings to other types of neural networks.

**Questions:**
- How do the proposed methods compare in terms of effectiveness and efficiency with other existing methods for addressing plasticity loss in neural networks?
- Can the proposed methods be applied to other types of neural networks beyond MLP, CNN, and ResNet-18? If so, what are the specific conditions or modifications required for their application?
- How does the proposed training protocol perform in scenarios where the non-stationary learning is due to a change in data distribution rather than a shift in labels or tasks?
- Is there a possibility to extend the proposed training protocol to other types of neural networks, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or transformers?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the effectiveness of the proposed methods in scenarios where the non-stationary learning is due to a change in data distribution?
- How do the proposed methods address the limitations discussed in the paper, such as the potential negative effects on convergence speed and the applicability to other types of neural networks?
