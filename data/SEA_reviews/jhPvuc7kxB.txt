 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework called "Look, Remember, Reason" (LRR) for training a large multi-modal language model (LM) to perform complex visual reasoning tasks, specifically focusing on causal and compositional spatiotemporal reasoning over actions. The LRR framework involves training the LM with surrogate tasks such as object detection, re-identification, and tracking to enhance its low-level visual capabilities. The proposed methodology is evaluated on several benchmark datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods. The paper also explores the use of a two-stream video encoder with spatiotemporal attention to capture both static and motion-based cues in videos, which are crucial for visual reasoning tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework for training a large multi-modal language model (LM) to perform complex visual reasoning tasks, focusing on causal or compositional spatiotemporal reasoning over actions, which is an important area of research.
- The proposed methodology is evaluated on several benchmark datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods, highlighting the effectiveness of the approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly explained, enhancing its accessibility and understanding.
- The use of a two-stream video encoder with spatiotemporal attention to capture both static and motion-based cues in videos is innovative and contributes to the advancement of visual reasoning in videos.
- The approach of using surrogate tasks during training to enhance the low-level visual capabilities of the LM is a novel and effective strategy, as demonstrated by the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed approach and potential areas for improvement, which could enhance its impact and utility.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive analysis of the computational complexity or scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical implementation in real-world scenarios.
