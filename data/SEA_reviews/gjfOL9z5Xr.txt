 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DYVAL, a novel dynamic evaluation protocol for large language models (LLMs) aimed at addressing data contamination and static complexity issues in current evaluation benchmarks. DYVAL utilizes directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) to generate evaluation samples with controllable complexities, ranging from simple to complex reasoning tasks. This framework allows for the dynamic generation of test samples, which can be used for both evaluation and fine-tuning, enhancing the models' performance on existing benchmarks. The paper provides extensive experiments and analysis, showing that DYVAL can effectively evaluate the reasoning abilities of LLMs and improve their performance on various tasks. However, the paper's novelty and the clarity of its contributions are questioned, with concerns about the generalization of the method to other tasks and the potential for data contamination in the generated samples.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel dynamic evaluation framework, DYVAL, which dynamically generates evaluation samples with controllable complexities, addressing the limitations of static benchmarks.
- DYVAL is flexible and can be used to generate training data to improve the abilities of LLMs, providing a valuable tool for evaluating and enhancing LLM performance.
- The experiments are extensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of DYVAL in evaluating the reasoning abilities of LLMs and improving their performance on various tasks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of LLMs, including a detailed analysis of failure cases and results of different prompting methods, which can help researchers and practitioners better understand the capabilities and limitations of LLMs.
- The use of DAGs to generate test samples is innovative and provides a unique approach to generating evaluation samples, which can be useful for evaluating the reasoning abilities of LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining the contributions of DYVAL, particularly how it differs from existing evaluation methods and how it addresses the issues of data contamination and static complexity.
- The methodology and the use of DAGs for generating evaluation samples are not clearly explained, making it difficult for readers to understand the practical implementation and effectiveness of the method.
- The paper's focus on reasoning tasks may limit its applicability to other types of tasks, and the generalization of the method to other tasks is not adequately discussed.
- There is a potential for data contamination in the generated samples, especially if the training data for the LLMs includes similar or identical problems.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the generated datasets and the ethical considerations of using LLMs for evaluation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the contributions of DYVAL and how it addresses the issues of data contamination and static complexity more effectively than existing methods?
- How does DYVAL ensure that the generated evaluation samples are not contaminated with data from the training corpus of LLMs?
- Can the authors provide more details on the methodology and practical implementation of using DAGs to generate evaluation samples?
- How does DYVAL handle the potential for data contamination in the generated samples, and what steps are taken to mitigate this risk?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the generated datasets and the ethical considerations of using LLMs for evaluation?
