 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel benchmark, PPNL, designed to evaluate the spatial-temporal reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) in path planning scenarios. It compares various LLMs, including GPT-4 and fine-tuned models like BART and T5, using different prompting techniques such as naive few-shot prompting, action-and-effect prompting, chain-of-thought prompting, and ReAct prompting. The benchmark evaluates models in both in-distribution and out-of-distribution scenarios, assessing their performance in grid-based environments with varying obstacle counts and grid sizes. The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of LLMs' ability to plan paths, including multi-goal scenarios, and discusses the limitations and potential improvements in LLMs' spatial-temporal reasoning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmark, PPNL, which is the first to focus on evaluating LLMs' spatial-temporal reasoning capabilities in path planning, a critical area for advancing AI systems.
- The benchmark is designed with a clear motivation, extensive experiments, and a detailed analysis, providing valuable insights into the limitations of LLMs in path planning and suggesting potential directions for future research.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes detailed explanations of the benchmark design, data generation process, and evaluation metrics, ensuring transparency and reproducibility.
- The evaluation of different LLMs using various prompting techniques provides a comprehensive understanding of how different approaches affect the performance of LLMs in path planning tasks.
- The paper includes both in-distribution and out-of-distribution evaluations, which are crucial for understanding the generalization capabilities of LLMs in different scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a broader range of LLMs and more diverse prompting techniques to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the spatial-temporal reasoning capabilities of LLMs.
- The paper primarily focuses on grid-based environments, which may not fully capture the complexity and diversity of real-world scenarios. Expanding the evaluation to more complex and varied environments could enhance the relevance and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the benchmark, such as the simplistic nature of the grid-based environments and the potential for bias in the evaluation due to the use of A* algorithm for single-goal path planning.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the computational cost of different prompting techniques, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of using these techniques in real-world applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the benchmark, including the potential for bias in the evaluation and the generalizability of the findings to more complex and varied environments.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the benchmark, such as the simplistic nature of the grid-based environments and the potential for bias in the evaluation?
- How do the authors plan to address these limitations in future work, and what steps are being taken to ensure the generalizability of the findings to more complex and varied environments?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the computational cost of different prompting techniques, and how these costs might impact the practical applicability of the findings in real-world scenarios?
- How does the performance of LLMs in path planning tasks compare to other state-of-the-art methods, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the practical applicability of LLMs in real-world path planning applications?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential biases in the evaluation, such as the use of A* algorithm for single-goal path planning, and discuss how these biases might affect the generalizability of the findings?
