 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Prospector, a novel LLM agent designed for decision-making tasks, featuring two components: Self-Asking and Trajectory Ranking. Self-Asking allows the LLM to ask and answer questions based on context, enhancing the decision-making process, while Trajectory Ranking evaluates different trajectories and selects the most rewarding one. The method was tested on benchmarks like ALFWorld and WebShop, showing improved performance over existing methods like ReAct and Reflexion. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, as similar methods have been previously explored, and the paper's presentation and clarity need improvement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear descriptions of the proposed method, and the experiments are well-conducted and well-reported.
- The proposed method is simple, easy to implement, and the results are strong, demonstrating a significant improvement in performance over previous methods.
- The method's novelty lies in its combination of self-asking and trajectory ranking, which is not commonly seen in previous works.
- The paper provides a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method's application to different LLMs shows that it is not limited to specific models, which enhances its generalizability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other decision-making methods, such as those mentioned in the paper, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as similar methods have been previously explored, such as the use of self-asking in decision-making tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational costs of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the number of trajectories generated and the time required for evaluation.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with better organization and clearer figures that enhance understanding.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts.
- There are several typos and grammatical errors throughout the paper that need correction.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other decision-making methods, particularly those mentioned in the paper?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency with other methods, especially in terms of the number of trajectories generated and the time required for evaluation?
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of the self-asking component versus the trajectory ranking component in the performance improvements?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for hallucination in the self-asking component, especially in tasks where incorrect answers could lead to suboptimal decision-making?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training data and the fine-tuning process for the LLM critics, particularly in terms of the data sources and the impact on performance?
- The paper mentions the use of different LLM critics; could the authors clarify the specific roles and impacts of these different models on the performance?
