 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AMAGO, an in-context reinforcement learning (RL) agent designed to address challenges such as generalization, long-term memory, and meta-learning. AMAGO leverages sequence models, specifically Transformers, to process entire rollouts in parallel with end-to-end RL, enhancing its scalability and applicability to various problems. The methodology includes a shared sequence model for actor and critic networks, a multi-gamma update to optimize different discount factors, and a hindsight relabeling scheme for multi-step goals. The paper evaluates AMAGO in goal-conditioned domains and demonstrates its effectiveness in meta-RL and long-term memory domains. However, concerns are raised about the lack of comparison with other in-context RL methods, the potential for hyperparameter tuning, and the clarity of some experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive review of related work, effectively positioning the proposed method within the current research landscape.
- The authors introduce a novel hindsight relabeling scheme for multi-step goals, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method is simple, elegant, and well-motivated, with a focus on leveraging sequence models to enhance the performance of RL agents.
- The paper includes a thorough analysis of the algorithm's components, such as the multi-gamma update and the shared sequence model, which are crucial for the method's effectiveness.
- The method's application to goal-conditioned problems with challenging exploration demonstrates its versatility and applicability.
- The paper is open-source, which allows for reproducibility and further research by the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a direct comparison with other in-context RL methods, which is crucial for understanding the method's relative performance and advantages.
- There is a potential for hyperparameter tuning, which could affect the method's performance and make it less practical for real-world applications.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental results, particularly in the Crafter environment, are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the results are not consistently superior across all environments.
- The paper's methodology, particularly the use of a single Transformer for actor and critic networks, raises concerns about the method's stability and the potential for overfitting.
- The paper's focus on Transformers as the primary sequence model might limit its applicability in environments where other models like RNNs are more suitable.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the shared sequence model affects the stability of the method and how it compares to using separate models for actor and critic networks?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting, especially when using a single Transformer for both actor and critic networks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly in the Crafter environment, to help understand the results and their implications?
- Is there a possibility to include a direct comparison with other in-context RL methods to better understand the method's relative performance and advantages?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of environments, such as those with discrete actions or continuous actions, and how does this affect the model's stability and performance?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending the method to other types of sequence models, such as RNNs, and how this might impact the method's applicability and performance?
