 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Random Truncated Backpropagation Through Time (RaT-BPTT) for dataset distillation, which addresses the challenges of variance in gradients, computational burden, and long-term dependencies. RaT-BPTT integrates randomization and truncation with BPTT, enabling it to handle the bilevel optimization problem effectively and achieve state-of-the-art performance across various benchmarks. The method's simplicity and effectiveness are highlighted, with a focus on reducing the need for large training datasets and computational resources. Additionally, the paper explores the phenomenon of intercorrelation in distilled datasets and proposes a boosting mechanism to enhance the quality of distilled datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, RaT-BPTT, which is simple yet effective, and achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple benchmarks.
- The method addresses the challenges of variance in gradients, computational burden, and long-term dependencies, which are crucial in dataset distillation.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the methodology and experimental results.
- The authors provide a thorough analysis of the learned datasets, which could lead to further improvements in dataset distillation.
- The combination of RaT-BPTT with data parametrization methods further improves performance, demonstrating the method's versatility.
- The paper presents a boosting approach, Boost-DD, which generates a nested dataset with high-performing subsets, enhancing the quality of distilled datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other methods such as RCIG and other parametrization methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental evidence to support the claims about the method's ability to handle larger datasets or its generalizability across different architectures.
- The paper does not discuss the potential societal impact of the work, which could be a significant limitation for some readers.
- The method's novelty is questioned as it combines existing techniques (truncated BPTT and random window) without significant innovation.
- The paper does not address the limitations of the method comprehensively, which could have provided a more balanced view of the research.

**Questions:**
- How does the RaT-BPTT method compare in terms of computational efficiency and performance with other state-of-the-art methods, especially when applied to larger datasets?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the distilled datasets are used in downstream tasks and how the method's performance is evaluated?
- How does the method handle the issue of intercorrelation in distilled datasets, and what are the implications of this for the practical application of the distilled datasets?
- Could the authors provide more experimental evidence to support the claims about the method's ability to handle larger datasets and different architectures?
- How does the method perform when combined with other data parametrization methods, and what are the potential benefits or drawbacks of such combinations?
