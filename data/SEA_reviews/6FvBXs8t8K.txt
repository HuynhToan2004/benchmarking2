 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel adversarial defense framework, LAST, which leverages historical states of target models as a proxy to enhance robustness against adversarial attacks. The framework employs a two-stage update rule and a self-distillation-based defense objective to address the unstable convergence behavior of adversarial training. The authors claim that this approach improves the robustness of models against various adversarial attacks, showing significant performance enhancements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the historical state usage, the clarity of the methodological presentation, and the generalization of the results across different datasets and architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using historical states of target models as a proxy to improve robustness against adversarial attacks, which is an innovative idea.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, with clear motivations and a well-structured presentation that makes the paper easy to follow.
- The paper demonstrates significant performance improvements over existing methods, showing that the proposed approach can enhance the robustness of models against various adversarial attacks.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are convincing, with detailed ablation studies and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper addresses the critical issue of unstable convergence behavior in adversarial training, which is a significant concern in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation from existing methods, particularly those that also use historical states to improve robustness. This makes it difficult to ascertain the novel contributions of the proposed method.
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in methodological presentation, with important details and definitions missing, which could hinder understanding.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the hyperparameters used and the random seeds, which could affect the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper's focus is primarily on the CIFAR10 and CIFAR100 datasets, with limited evaluation on other datasets like ImageNet, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's claims of improving stability and alleviating overfitting are not adequately supported by empirical evidence, and the results presented do not convincingly demonstrate these improvements.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of catastrophic overfitting, which is a significant concern in adversarial training.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method differs from existing methods that also use historical states to improve robustness?
- Why is the performance of the proposed method not better than that of the original SAT method on some datasets and attack scenarios?
- How do the authors address the issue of catastrophic overfitting, and what measures have been taken to ensure that the proposed method does not exacerbate this issue?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the hyperparameters used and the random seeds, to enhance the reproducibility of the results?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more empirical evidence to support the claims of improved stability and alleviation of overfitting?
