 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a semi-supervised prompt learning method for vision-language models (VLMs), specifically utilizing a cross-model framework that leverages unlabeled samples to enhance model performance. The approach involves using different augmentations on images and varying the lengths of visual and text prompts to create multiple views of unlabeled samples. This method is designed to make the learned prompts invariant to different views of the same sample, thereby improving the model's ability to generalize across various datasets. The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments demonstrating the method's effectiveness across various benchmark datasets, showing improvements over traditional supervised learning methods. However, the paper has been criticized for its lack of clarity in presentation, insufficient theoretical analysis, and limited comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel semi-supervised prompt learning method for vision-language models (VLMs) using a cross-model framework, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is simple yet effective, using augmentations on images and varying the lengths of visual and text prompts to create multiple views of unlabeled samples, making the learned prompts invariant to different views.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with comprehensive experiments conducted on 15 datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The idea of using a semi-supervised prompt learning method to enhance the performance of vision-language models is both interesting and practical, with the potential to reduce the need for extensive manual labeling.
- The methodology is sound, and the paper provides a detailed analysis of the results, which is beneficial for understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance metrics and methodological approaches.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis and detailed explanation of the method, which could limit the understanding of its effectiveness and applicability.
- The presentation of the paper is unclear in some sections, particularly in the introduction and methodology, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect the reliability and generalizability of the results.
- The experiments conducted are limited to specific datasets, and the paper does not explore the method's performance on larger, more diverse datasets, which could affect the robustness of the findings.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the scalability of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including a discussion on the performance metrics and methodological approaches?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger, more diverse datasets, such as ImageNet?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of the method, including the underlying principles and assumptions?
- How does the method handle different types of augmentations, and what are the implications for the performance and generalizability of the model?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including its scalability and applicability in different scenarios?
- How does the method handle the generation of pseudo-labels, and what are the implications for the performance and reliability of the model?
