 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a class of soft clipping schemes for stochastic gradient descent (SGD) in both convex and non-convex settings. These schemes aim to address the challenges of steep gradients and the sensitivity of learning rates, offering theoretical guarantees of convergence in expectation and almost sure convergence to stationary points. The authors provide a rigorous mathematical analysis, including proofs of convergence rates, and conduct numerical experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of these methods. Despite the paper's strengths in theoretical analysis and empirical validation, it has been critiqued for its lack of novelty and significant contributions, as the proposed algorithms closely resemble existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a large class of soft clipping schemes for stochastic optimization and provides a rigorous mathematical analysis of the convergence rates of these schemes.
- Theoretical results are supported by numerical experiments, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods in practical scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the theoretical results and the experimental setup, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work, which helps in contextualizing the research within the existing literature.
- The paper is significant in its contribution to the field of stochastic optimization, offering insights into the convergence rates of soft clipping schemes and their practical implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not clearly distinguish its contributions from existing literature, particularly from the works of Zhang et al. (2020a) and Mai & Johansson (2021), which might lead to questions about the novelty of the research.
- The numerical experiments are limited in scope, and the results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority or significant differences of the proposed methods compared to existing algorithms like SGD, SGD with momentum, and Adam.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which is crucial for understanding their practical efficiency.
- The theoretical analysis and proofs are not sufficiently novel or significant, as they closely follow established methods in the literature, which might limit the perceived impact of the research.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly how the convergence rates translate into real-world performance improvements.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the main differences between their proposed algorithms and those of Zhang et al. (2020a) and Mai & Johansson (2021)? How do these differences impact the convergence rates and practical applicability of the algorithms?
- How do the authors justify the choice of step sizes in their numerical experiments, particularly in light of the theoretical results and the empirical observations?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity of their proposed methods with existing algorithms, especially in terms of the number of iterations required to achieve a given level of accuracy?
- How do the authors address the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of improving the performance of neural networks in practical settings?
- Given the similarities in the proof techniques used, how do the authors differentiate their theoretical contributions from those in existing literature?
