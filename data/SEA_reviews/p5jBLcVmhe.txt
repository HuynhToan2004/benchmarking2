 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SoftTreeMax, a novel policy gradient method that integrates tree search with softmax policy gradients, aiming to reduce gradient variance and improve sample complexity in policy gradient methods. The methodology extends softmax logits with multi-step discounted cumulative rewards and the logits of future states, demonstrating exponential variance decay with the planning horizon. This approach is supported by a theoretical analysis and empirical results showing significant reductions in gradient variance and improvements in performance compared to distributed PPO. The paper also discusses the practical implementation of SoftTreeMax in Atari environments and provides a comprehensive theoretical framework for variance reduction in policy gradient methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by combining policy gradient methods with tree search, which is both innovative and well-articulated.
- The methodology is supported by a robust theoretical analysis, particularly in the area of variance reduction, which is crucial for improving sample complexity in policy gradient methods.
- The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements in performance and variance reduction, validating the theoretical claims.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The practical implementation of SoftTreeMax in Atari environments, utilizing a parallel GPU-based simulator for fast and efficient tree expansion, is highlighted as a significant contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to more complex environments and the scalability of the tree search method.
- There is a need for a more detailed comparison with existing methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and the practical implications of tree search in different environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, such as those mentioned in the related work section, to establish the superiority of the proposed method.
- The theoretical results, while interesting, are not sufficiently novel or surprising, and the empirical results are somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on Atari environments.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential issues of sample complexity and computational cost, which are critical for practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the method performs in more complex environments, particularly those with higher dimensional state and action spaces?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and sample complexity with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in more complex environments?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over other tree search-based methods, and how does it compare in terms of scalability and practical applicability?
- Could you provide more details on the choice of the behavior policy πb and its impact on the performance of the algorithm?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational cost and sample complexity in practical implementations, and what strategies are used to mitigate these issues?
