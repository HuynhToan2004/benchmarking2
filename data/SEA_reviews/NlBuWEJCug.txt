 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for learning a representation in latent space that models reachability between states, enhancing the performance of goal-conditioned planning in environments with rich observations and exogenous noise. The approach involves training an encoder network that maps observations to a latent space, followed by a decoder network that predicts the next state based on the current state and a noise vector. This is achieved by optimizing a contrastive loss to ensure that similar states are represented closely in the latent space. The paper also explores the use of this representation for hierarchical planning, demonstrating its effectiveness through a variety of experiments, including in maze navigation and robotic arm control.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear and effective presentation of the main ideas.
- The proposed method is novel and contributes to the field of learning latent representations for planning, particularly in environments with rich observations and exogenous noise.
- The method is evaluated on a variety of environments, including maze navigation and robotic arm control, demonstrating its effectiveness and versatility.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that validate the method's ability to learn useful latent representations and enhance planning performance.
- The use of a hierarchical planning approach, which includes a high-level planner for coarse planning and a low-level planner for detailed action selection, is highlighted as an effective method for handling complex tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its strengths and weaknesses.
- There are concerns about the clarity and detail in some sections of the paper, particularly in the descriptions of the experimental setups and the technical details of the method.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation, including a broader range of baselines and a more detailed comparison with existing methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational efficiency of the method, particularly in terms of the time and resources required for training and inference.
- The paper does not discuss the societal implications of the work, which could be a significant oversight for a paper of this scope.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the method, including the time and resources required for training and inference?
- How does the method perform in environments with different types and levels of exogenous noise?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a discussion of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method compared to these approaches?
- How does the method handle the clustering of states in the latent space, and what are the implications of this for the effectiveness of the planning algorithm?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the noise vector in the method, and how it affects the learning of the latent representations?
- How does the method perform when the goal is not explicitly defined in the observation space, and what are the implications of this for the planning algorithm?
