 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SSLMem, a novel framework for defining and measuring memorization in self-supervised learning (SSL) models. It proposes a methodology that compares the alignment of representations for data points and their augmented views, using a leave-one-out approach to measure memorization. The framework is tested across various datasets and architectures, showing that SSL models do memorize data, and this memorization can enhance downstream generalization. The paper also discusses the implications of memorization in SSL and provides empirical evidence to support its claims. However, it lacks theoretical justification and detailed comparative analysis with existing methods, which could strengthen its contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel definition of memorization in SSL, which is independent of the training method, its concrete training loss, and operates directly on the representations.
- The empirical evaluation of the definition is comprehensive, covering different architectures and training methods in SSL, and demonstrating the memorization of SSL models.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a logical structure that makes it easy to follow.
- The analysis of the impact of SSL memorization on downstream generalization is insightful and supports the claim that memorization can improve generalization.
- The proposed definition of memorization is simple and intuitive, making it accessible and easy to understand.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly the Deja Vu memorization method, which could provide a clearer understanding of the differences and similarities.
- The theoretical justification for the proposed definition is missing, which could enhance the paper's credibility and depth.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed definition and its potential drawbacks, which could provide a more balanced view of the research.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the impact of different SSL methods on memorization, which could have enriched the analysis.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples, especially in the introduction and methodology sections, to improve clarity and understanding.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with the Deja Vu memorization method, highlighting the main differences and similarities?
- How does the proposed definition of memorization compare to other existing definitions, especially in terms of its practical applicability and theoretical soundness?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their proposed definition and provide examples or scenarios where it might not be effective?
- How does the proposed definition of memorization influence the training and optimization of SSL models, and what are the potential implications for model generalization and performance?
- Could the authors provide more examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications and implications of their research findings?
