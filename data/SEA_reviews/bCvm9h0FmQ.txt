 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for black-box backdoor detection, focusing on the input-level scenario where only the input and output of a DNN are accessible. The approach utilizes a causal inference perspective, employing counterfactual samples to distinguish between clean and poisoned samples. The methodology involves adding noise to test images to identify when the model's output changes, indicating potential backdoor manipulation. The paper claims that this method is effective against various backdoor attacks, including sample-agnostic and sample-specific triggers, and has been validated through experiments on CIFAR10 and ImageNet datasets. However, concerns were raised regarding the clarity of the experimental setup, the robustness of the results, and the novelty of the approach compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel perspective on backdoor attacks from a causality-based standpoint, which is both intriguing and well-explained.
- The proposed method is straightforward, easy to implement, and does not require access to the model's weights, making it practical for real-world applications.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations and illustrative examples.
- The proposed method is validated on two datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper provides a comprehensive overview of the related work, which is helpful for readers unfamiliar with the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and detailed description of the experimental setup, particularly the training phase and the attack methods used.
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as it primarily relies on adding noise to test images, a technique that has been explored in previous works.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its effectiveness against different types of backdoor attacks and its scalability to larger datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing baselines, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance under various conditions.
- The paper's claims about the effectiveness of the method against different types of backdoor attacks are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the training phase and the attack methods used?
- How does the proposed method perform against different types of backdoor attacks, especially those that are not sample-specific or sample-agnostic?
- Can the authors clarify the distinction between their method and existing methods that use similar techniques, such as adding noise to test images?
- How does the proposed method scale to larger datasets, and what are the computational requirements for its implementation?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the effectiveness of the method under different conditions, such as varying levels of noise addition or different types of backdoor attacks?
