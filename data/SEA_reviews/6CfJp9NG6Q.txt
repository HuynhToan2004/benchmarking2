 **Summary:**
The paper introduces STUDY, a socially-aware, temporally causal decoder recommender system designed for educational content, specifically targeting students with dyslexia or reading difficulties. The system leverages social structures to enhance recommendations by jointly inferring over groups of students, using a modified transformer decoder network to model both within-user and cross-user interactions. The paper presents an ablation study to understand the components of the model and evaluates the system against several baselines, showing that it outperforms them in terms of hits@n metrics. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in the presentation of the methodology and experimental results, and lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and societal impact of the proposed system.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of education, focusing on improving recommendations for students with dyslexia or reading difficulties.
- The methodology is clearly presented, and the paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow.
- The paper includes an ablation study to understand the components of the model and their impact on performance, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The paper evaluates the proposed method against several baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a clear description of the dataset used.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and societal impact of the proposed system, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of the research.
- The clarity of the presentation could be improved, particularly in the methodology section, which is confusing and lacks detail.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the findings, as the experiments were conducted on a single dataset and the results are not validated across different educational contexts.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scalability and applicability in different educational settings.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art models, which could provide a better understanding of its relative performance.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the related work and existing models in the field, which could provide a better context for the proposed research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method handles the causality of interactions in the data, especially in the context of social networks?
- How does the proposed method ensure fairness and avoid bias in the recommendations, particularly in educational settings where students have diverse backgrounds and abilities?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method and discuss its scalability in different educational contexts?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other state-of-the-art models in the field, and what are the specific advantages of the proposed approach?
- Could the authors provide more details on the dataset used, including its size, diversity, and the characteristics of the students and items in the dataset?
- How does the proposed method handle the privacy and security concerns related to the use of educational data, particularly in the context of social networks?
