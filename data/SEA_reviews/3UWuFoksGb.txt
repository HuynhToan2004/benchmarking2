 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a framework called Planning Abstraction from Language (PARL), which leverages language-annotated demonstrations to automatically learn state and action abstractions. The framework consists of three stages: recovering object-level and action concepts, learning state abstractions, and applying low-level policies for abstract actions. During inference, PARL generates abstract action plans using latent transition and feasibility functions, which are then refined using low-level policies. The framework is evaluated in two environments, BabyAI and Kitchen Worlds, showing strong generalization capabilities across different object instances, concept compositions, and planning horizons. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its reliance on fully observable environments and the simplicity of the action space, which may not reflect real-world complexities.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to learning state and action abstractions in sequential decision-making domains using language-annotated demonstrations.
- The methodology is well-organized, clearly presented, and includes detailed explanations of the experimental setup and results.
- The proposed framework, PARL, demonstrates strong generalization capabilities across different object instances, concept compositions, and planning horizons.
- The use of pretrained large language models to parse natural language instructions into abstract actions is an innovative approach that enhances the efficiency and effectiveness of the model.
- The paper addresses the significant problem of learning action abstractions from language-annotated demonstrations, which is crucial for improving the efficiency and performance of learning policies in complex tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes fully observable environments, which may not reflect real-world complexities where agents often operate with partial observations.
- The action space is relatively simple, focusing on pick-and-place actions, which may not adequately represent more complex environments where agents need to perform a variety of actions.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of generalization capabilities and the complexity of the environments considered.
- The evaluation is limited to two environments, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method across a broader range of tasks.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the method to more complex environments or the integration of pretrained foundation models for object recognition.
- There is a lack of clarity in the description of the methodology, particularly in the definitions of key components such as the feasibility function and the low-level policy.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other approaches that learn action abstractions from language-annotated demonstrations, especially in terms of generalization capabilities and the complexity of the environments considered?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the feasibility function and the low-level policy, including their implementation details and the specific roles they play in the overall system?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the language instructions are not aligned with the low-level commands, which could lead to incorrect or ineffective actions?
- Can the authors clarify the definitions and roles of the different components in the method, such as the feasibility function and the low-level policy, to enhance the understanding of the proposed framework?
- How does the method perform in more complex environments, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in scaling up the method to such environments?
