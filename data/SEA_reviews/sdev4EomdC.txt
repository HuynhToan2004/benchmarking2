 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the performance of online and offline continual learning algorithms under similar memory and computational constraints. It introduces a unified framework, UCL, which treats online and offline continual learning as variants of the same algorithm, differing only in the allocation of memory to recent samples. Theoretical analysis is provided to support the framework, and empirical results demonstrate that online methods can sometimes outperform offline methods, especially when memory constraints are severe. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the field, suggesting that online methods might be more effective in practical scenarios where memory is limited.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a unified framework, UCL, which effectively treats online and offline continual learning as variants of the same algorithm, providing a novel perspective on the relationship between these two settings.
- The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, including a generalization bound that is well-explained, which supports the empirical findings.
- The empirical results are robust and demonstrate that online methods can outperform offline methods when memory constraints are severe, which is an interesting and surprising finding.
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions, figures, and explanations, making it accessible and easy to understand.
- The experiments are thorough and convincing, showing that online methods can be more effective than offline methods in certain scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claims about the superiority of online methods over offline methods are not entirely convincing, as the performance improvements are marginal and do not consistently outperform offline methods in all scenarios.
- The theoretical analysis is not entirely novel, as similar ideas have been explored in previous works, which may limit the perceived originality of the paper.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework, especially in terms of its applicability to other continual learning methods and scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the definitions and explanations of certain concepts and algorithms, which could confuse readers.
- The experimental setup and results are not always clearly explained or presented, which could make it difficult for readers to replicate or understand the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the conditions under which online methods are expected to outperform offline methods, and whether these conditions are realistic in practical scenarios?
- How does the proposed framework handle scenarios where the data distribution is non-stationary or where there are significant shifts in the data over time?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the data is split into tasks and how the memory allocation is managed across different phases of the experiment?
- How does the proposed framework compare to other methods that have been proposed for addressing catastrophic forgetting in continual learning?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical contributions of their work, particularly how the generalization bound relates to the practical performance of the algorithms?
