 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FuseLLM, a method that leverages the generative distributions of existing large language models (LLMs) to create a unified model, integrating their strengths and capabilities. By aligning tokenizations and fusing probability distributions, FuseLLM aims to enhance the performance of target LLMs across various benchmarks and tasks, such as reasoning, commonsense, and code generation. The methodology is validated through experiments that demonstrate the superiority of FuseLLM over individual source LLMs and established baselines. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive evaluation, and potential issues with the alignment of tokenizations and the generalization of the method to other LLMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology and experiments.
- The idea of knowledge fusion for LLMs is innovative and addresses a significant challenge in the field.
- The methodology is validated through experiments that demonstrate the superiority of FuseLLM over individual source LLMs and established baselines.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge fusion for LLMs, which is a promising avenue for future research.
- The experiments are conducted on a variety of benchmarks and tasks, showcasing the effectiveness of FuseLLM in improving the performance of target LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited as it primarily involves aligning tokenizations and fusing probability distributions, which are well-known techniques in the field.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation, with limited datasets and tasks used, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability and robustness of the proposed method.
- There is a potential issue with the alignment of tokenizations, particularly when the models have different tokenization methods, which could lead to misalignment and inaccurate results.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalization of the method to other LLMs, particularly those with different architectures or training methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and potential areas for future research.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the discrepancies in the performance of FuseLLM compared to the baseline models, particularly in the tasks where FuseLLM underperforms?
- How does the method perform when applied to LLMs with different architectures or training methods?
- Can you provide more details on the alignment process and its impact on the results, especially when tokenizations are misaligned?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting to the training corpus, and what steps are taken to mitigate this risk?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of benchmarks and tasks, and consider including more diverse datasets to better evaluate the generalizability of the method?
