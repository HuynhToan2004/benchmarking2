 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new dataset, CommitPack, derived from Git commits, and a new benchmark, HumanEvalPack, designed to evaluate instruction-tuned code models. The dataset, comprising 4TB of data across 350 programming languages, is used to train models like OctoCoder and OctoGeeX, which are evaluated on the benchmark alongside other models. The paper also presents a new evaluation methodology for code models, focusing on code repair, code explanation, and code synthesis, and demonstrates the effectiveness of these models on a variety of tasks. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty and insufficient comparison with other models, particularly those that use more extensive or diverse training datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- It introduces a large-scale dataset, CommitPack, which covers a wide range of programming languages, providing a valuable resource for the community.
- The dataset is used to train models like OctoCoder and OctoGeeX, which are shown to perform well on various tasks.
- The paper presents a new benchmark, HumanEvalPack, which evaluates models across different tasks such as code repair, code explanation, and code synthesis, offering a comprehensive evaluation framework.
- The paper provides detailed analysis and insights into the performance of different models, contributing to a better understanding of the capabilities and limitations of instruction-tuned code LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it primarily introduces a new dataset and benchmark without significant advancements in methodology or model design.
- There is insufficient comparison with other models, particularly those that use more extensive or diverse training datasets, which could affect the perceived effectiveness of the proposed models.
- The paper's claims about the importance of natural language targets and the generalization of models to unseen languages are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- The dataset, while large, may not be diverse enough in terms of code types and programming languages, which could limit the models' ability to handle a wide range of coding tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of contamination in the dataset, which could affect the reliability and validity of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition of "permissively licensed" models and how this term is used in the context of the paper?
- How does the paper address the issue of contamination in the dataset, particularly in the context of the benchmark tasks?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of 20 characters as the threshold for removing solution overlap? Is this threshold arbitrary, and could it affect the results?
- Could the authors provide more detailed statistics on the distribution of different types of code in the dataset, especially in relation to the performance of the models on different types of code?
- How does the paper ensure the quality and diversity of the dataset, particularly in terms of the representation of different programming languages and coding styles?
