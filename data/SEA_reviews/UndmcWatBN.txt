 **Summary:**
The paper explores the zero-shot visual reasoning capabilities of vision-language models (VLMs) and large language models (LLMs) using synthetic datasets. The study investigates how different representations of scene information, such as visual embeddings versus textual scene descriptions, affect performance. It also compares the effectiveness of chain-of-thought prompting versus standard prompting. The paper finds that LLMs, particularly those with large scales, outperform VLMs when provided with textual scene descriptions, and that chain-of-thought prompting is more effective for larger LLMs. However, the paper's scope is limited by its focus on synthetic datasets, which may not generalize well to real-world scenarios. Additionally, the paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the datasets and the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors conduct a thorough analysis of zero-shot visual reasoning capabilities of VLMs and LLMs using synthetic datasets, which is a novel approach.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the performance of VLMs and LLMs under various factors, such as the number of "reasoning steps," question types, and model scale.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are convincing, showing that LLMs outperform VLMs when provided with textual scene descriptions.
- The paper introduces a novel comparison between LLMs and VLMs, which is crucial for understanding the role of world knowledge in visual reasoning tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the datasets used, particularly in terms of their generalizability to real-world scenarios.
- The findings on the effectiveness of chain-of-thought prompting are not surprising and do not offer significant new insights.
- The paper's scope is limited by its focus on synthetic datasets, which may not generalize well to real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the datasets and the generalizability of the findings.
- The use of CLEVR and PTR datasets, which are known to be challenging for visual reasoning, might not be the best choice for evaluating the zero-shot visual reasoning capabilities of VLMs and LLMs.
- The paper could provide more insights into the potential reasons for the observed differences in performance between LLMs and VLMs, such as the complexity of tasks and the capabilities of the models.

**Questions:**
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of the datasets used in their study, particularly in terms of their generalizability to real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential reasons for the observed differences in performance between LLMs and VLMs, and how these might affect the generalizability of the findings?
- How do the authors justify the use of CLEVR and PTR datasets, which are known to be challenging for visual reasoning, for evaluating the zero-shot visual reasoning capabilities of VLMs and LLMs?
- What are the authors' thoughts on the potential impact of the findings on the design and development of future VLMs and LLMs for visual reasoning tasks?
