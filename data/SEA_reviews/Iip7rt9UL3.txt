 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Presto, a transformer-based model pre-trained on remote sensing pixel-timeseries data, which is designed to handle a variety of tasks in remote sensing. Presto leverages a self-supervised learning methodology and is shown to be competitive with state-of-the-art models while requiring less computational resources. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated across different geographical regions and datasets, showcasing its versatility and robustness. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive comparison with existing methods, and insufficient experimental validation in some areas.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the model architecture and pre-training strategy, making it accessible to readers.
- Presto, the proposed model, is lightweight, computationally efficient, and achieves state-of-the-art results in a variety of remote sensing tasks.
- The model demonstrates versatility by performing well across different geographical regions and datasets, highlighting its robustness and applicability.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the model's performance across various tasks and datasets, including both time-series and image-based tasks, which showcases the model's adaptability.
- The use of a transformer-based model for remote sensing data pre-training is innovative and addresses the unique challenges posed by this data modality.
- The paper is commendable for its practical application, as it focuses on creating a model that can be deployed in real-world scenarios with limited computational resources.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance metrics.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it primarily builds upon existing self-supervised learning approaches and transformer architectures.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed model, such as its inability to handle very high-resolution imagery or its performance on image-based tasks compared to pixel-timeseries tasks.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims made about the model's ability to perform well in the absence of specific input features.
- There is a lack of discussion on the model's limitations, particularly in terms of its generalization to other types of remote sensing data and its performance on image-based tasks.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, including computational efficiency and performance metrics?
- How does the model perform when trained on different types of remote sensing data, such as very high-resolution imagery?
- Could the authors provide more evidence to support the claims made about the model's ability to perform well in the absence of specific input features?
- How does the model handle missing input sensors or timesteps, and what is the impact of these on the model's performance?
- Can the authors clarify the model's performance on image-based tasks compared to pixel-timeseries tasks, and discuss the reasons for these differences?
- What are the specific limitations of the proposed method, and how do these affect its applicability and performance?
