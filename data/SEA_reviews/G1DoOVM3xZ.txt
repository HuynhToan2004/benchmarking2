 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MQL-UCB, a novel algorithm for reinforcement learning (RL) in general function approximation settings, designed to achieve low switching costs and near-optimal regret bounds. The algorithm employs a rare-switching policy based on the cumulative sensitivity of historical data, which is a novel approach in the context of RL. Theoretical results demonstrate that MQL-UCB achieves a regret bound of O(dHK) and a switching cost of O(dH), where d is the eluder dimension of the function class and H is the planning horizon. The paper also provides a matching lower bound for the switching cost, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel algorithm, MQL-UCB, which achieves a near-optimal regret bound and low switching cost in general function approximation settings, improving upon previous works.
- The algorithm is designed with a monotonic value function structure and a variance-weighted regression scheme, which are innovative approaches in the context of RL.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the algorithm, including the proof of a matching lower bound for the switching cost, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical results are well-supported by the paper, which includes a clear and concise explanation of the algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a paper in this field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the intuition behind the algorithm, particularly the policy-switching strategy based on the cumulative sensitivity of historical data.
- The paper assumes a high level of familiarity with the literature on RL, which might make it less accessible to a broader audience.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the completeness of the function class with respect to the second-order Bellman operator, are not clearly justified or explained.
- The paper's notation and definitions, particularly around the D2F uncertainty, could be more clearly defined and explained to aid in understanding the algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the algorithm, which could provide a more balanced view of its contributions and potential shortcomings.
