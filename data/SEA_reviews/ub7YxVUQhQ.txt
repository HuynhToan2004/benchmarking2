 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for discovering the governing equations of nonlinear dynamics of moving objects in 3D space using videos recorded by multiple calibrated cameras. The approach involves a target tracking module, a coordinate transformation module based on Rodrigues' rotation formula, and a spline-enhanced library-based sparse regressor. This framework is designed to handle challenges such as noise and missing data in the videos, which are common in real-world applications. The methodology is validated through experiments on synthetic videos, demonstrating its effectiveness in identifying the correct terms in the equations and its robustness against various perturbations. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods, and its applicability to real-world scenarios is limited by the assumptions of fixed-position cameras and the use of synthetic data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and challenging problem of discovering the dynamics of moving objects in 3D space from videos, which is a significant contribution to the field of physics-informed learning.
- The methodology is well-structured, with clear explanations and justifications, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and concise presentation that effectively communicates the main ideas and contributions.
- The proposed method is novel and interesting, combining existing techniques such as object tracking and physics-informed learning in a novel way to address the specific problem of discovering dynamics from videos.
- The experiments are robust and showcase the effectiveness of the proposed method, demonstrating its ability to handle noise and missing data in the videos.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure that facilitates understanding and reproducibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is somewhat limited due to its reliance on existing methods like SINDy and the use of similar techniques in previous works.
- The methodology assumes a fixed-position camera setup, which may not be practical in real-world scenarios where cameras are often moving.
- The paper primarily uses synthetic data for experiments, which may not fully represent real-world scenarios where data quality and quantity are often lower.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on related works, particularly in the area of physics-informed learning, which could have provided a better context for the research.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations and assumptions of the proposed method, as well as a more detailed comparison with existing methods.
- The paper could be improved by including more real-world data in the experiments to better demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the method would perform with a single camera setup, given the assumptions made about the camera positions?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the object's shape changes during motion, and what are the implications for the accuracy of the results?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method handles the assumption of a predefined coordinate system, and how this assumption affects the results?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applications in real-world scenarios?
- How does the method handle the assumption of a predefined reference coordinate system, and what are the implications if this system is not well-defined or if it changes during the experiment?
