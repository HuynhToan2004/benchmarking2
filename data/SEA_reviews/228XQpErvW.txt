 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ISMAQ, a novel method for offline-to-online reinforcement learning, which aims to enhance the performance of pre-trained offline models by fine-tuning them in online environments. ISMAQ employs a regularization term to maximize the average Q-value of the replay buffer, which helps in adapting to distribution drift and maintaining the quality of the learned policy. The method is evaluated on the D4RL benchmark and shows promising results, although it requires further experimental validation on more complex environments and a broader range of tasks. The paper also discusses the limitations of the method, including its dependence on the quality of the offline dataset and the potential for overfitting in online environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components.
- The idea of maximizing the average Q-value of the replay buffer is novel and interesting, providing a different approach from existing methods.
- The methodology is supported by a robust experimental setup, including extensive sensitivity analyses, which demonstrate the method's effectiveness and efficiency.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in offline-to-online reinforcement learning, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with a clear rationale for its design and implementation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of offline datasets and the potential for overfitting in online environments.
- The experimental results are limited to simple environments, and there is a need for more extensive testing on more complex domains to validate the method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of its advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper's presentation and structure could be improved to enhance clarity and coherence, particularly in the introduction and related work sections.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and memory requirements of the proposed method, which are crucial for practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the method performs on more complex environments, such as those with high-dimensional state spaces or stochastic transitions?
- How does the method handle distribution shifts in the online environment, and what measures are in place to prevent overfitting?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of different types of offline datasets on the performance of the method, and provide examples or case studies to illustrate these effects?
- How does the method compare in terms of computational efficiency and memory requirements with other state-of-the-art methods?
- In the context of offline-to-online reinforcement learning, what are the specific challenges and advantages of using a pre-trained offline model, and how does this approach compare to other methods that do not rely on such models?
