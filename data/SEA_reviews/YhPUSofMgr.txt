 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Text-Aware Diffusion Policies (TADPols), a novel approach to training reinforcement learning (RL) agents using text-to-image diffusion models as reward signals. The method involves corrupting rendered images with Gaussian noise and comparing the noise map to the model's output, which is then used to compute a reward signal. This approach is demonstrated to work effectively in simple environments like the DeepMind Control Suite, where it shows superior performance over CLIP-based methods. The method is evaluated against various baselines, including CLIP and video diffusion models, and the results suggest that it can generate more diverse and expressive actions compared to traditional methods. However, concerns are raised about the scalability of the approach and the limited scope of the experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and intuitive explanations that make the proposed method accessible and understandable.
- The idea of using a pretrained text-to-image diffusion model as a reward signal is novel and innovative, showing potential for significant impact in the field of reinforcement learning.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison of the proposed method against various baselines, including CLIP and video diffusion models, which helps to validate the effectiveness of the approach.
- The use of a diffusion model to predict noise added to rendered images, and the subsequent computation of a reward signal based on the similarity of predicted and actual noise, is a novel and interesting approach to reward design.
- The method demonstrates the ability to generate more diverse and expressive actions compared to traditional methods, which could be beneficial in various applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to simple environments like the DeepMind Control Suite, which may not fully demonstrate the scalability or effectiveness of the approach in more complex or realistic settings.
- The method's sensitivity to the noise level and the choice of diffusion models used is not adequately addressed, which could impact the reliability and generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the diffusion model is trained, which could be crucial for reproducibility and understanding the method's effectiveness.
- The method's reliance on pre-trained models and its performance in environments with complex dynamics or long-term dependencies is not thoroughly explored, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in certain applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform in more complex environments, such as those involving long-term dependencies or complex dynamics?
- Can the method be adapted to work with other types of diffusion models, such as video diffusion models?
- How sensitive is the method to the choice of diffusion model, and how does this impact the results?
- Could you provide more details on how the diffusion model is trained, including the dataset used and the specific training procedures?
- How does the method handle the sensitivity to the noise level, and what are the implications of using different noise levels in different environments?
- In light of the potential negative societal impacts, how does the method address ethical considerations, particularly in terms of fairness and bias in the generated actions?
