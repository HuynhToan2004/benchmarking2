 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of "benign oscillation" in neural network training using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with large learning rates. The authors propose a feature-noise data model to study how large learning rates can effectively learn weak features in the presence of strong features, while small learning rates primarily focus on strong features. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that oscillations during training can prevent over-greedy convergence, allowing for the discovery and learning of all useful components of the data. This is supported by experiments that show SGD with large learning rates can outperform SGD with small learning rates in terms of generalization performance. However, the paper's assumptions and experimental settings are criticized for being too specific and potentially misleading, and the theoretical results are considered overly simplistic.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel concept of "benign oscillation" in SGD training with large learning rates, which is an interesting and original contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis is well-structured, providing a clear and detailed explanation of the phenomenon, which is supported by rigorous proofs and well-designed experiments.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the ideas and results.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of large learning rates in learning weak features, which is a significant contribution to the understanding of SGD dynamics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental settings are too specific, focusing on a single training data point, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The theoretical results are overly simplistic, focusing on a single training data point and not considering more realistic scenarios with multiple data points.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the boundedness of the learning rate and the oscillation of the training trajectory, are not well-justified and could limit the applicability of the results.
- The paper's focus on a specific model and dataset (CIFAR10) may limit the broader applicability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the implications of the results for more complex models and datasets, which could be crucial for understanding the practical relevance of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the boundedness of the learning rate and the oscillation of the training trajectory?
- How do the findings of this paper relate to or differ from the results of other studies on large learning rates in SGD training?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to illustrate the conditions under which the assumptions of the paper hold?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the specific model and dataset used in the experiments, and what are the implications of these choices for the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors discuss the implications of their findings for more complex models and datasets, and suggest directions for future research to expand upon the current results?
