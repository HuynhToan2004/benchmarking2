 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for modeling Marked Temporal Point Processes (MTPPs) using Neural Ordinary Differential Equations (Neural ODEs). This approach decouples the estimation of the ground intensity and mark distribution into separate processes, which are then combined to form the conditional intensity. The method is claimed to provide better interpretability and computational efficiency compared to existing methods. The framework is evaluated on several datasets and shows competitive performance, although concerns are raised about the novelty and the depth of the evaluation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and the presentation is clear, making it easy to follow.
- The use of Neural ODEs for modeling MTPPs is innovative and provides a novel approach to handling marked temporal point processes.
- The proposed method is computationally efficient, with a parallel computing scheme that significantly reduces computation time.
- The method is capable of capturing complex temporal dynamics and provides a level of interpretability that is often lacking in existing methods.
- The paper includes a detailed comparison with existing methods and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through experiments on real-world datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it appears to be a combination of existing methods without significant new insights or contributions.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of interpretability, which is a critical aspect of the proposed method. The explanation of how interpretability is achieved is insufficient and lacks concrete examples.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a few datasets, which may not be sufficient to demonstrate its robustness and generalizability.
- There are concerns about the fairness of the comparison due to the use of different training schemes for different methods, which could potentially bias the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the scalability to large datasets or complex scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the influence of events is selectively considered in the proposed method?
- How does the method handle the potential issue of overfitting, especially considering the complexity of the model?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method handles the computation of the compensator and the influence of events in the past?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle different types of marks, such as categorical variables?
- How does the method handle the computational complexity, particularly in terms of scalability to larger datasets or more complex scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more examples or case studies to demonstrate the interpretability of the proposed method?
- How does the method compare in terms of computational efficiency and model performance when using different training schemes?
