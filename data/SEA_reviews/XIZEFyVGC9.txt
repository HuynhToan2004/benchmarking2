 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DAMA, a novel method aimed at mitigating gender bias in large language models (LLMs) by specifically targeting mid-upper feed-forward layers. This approach leverages a linear projection to adapt the model's weights, thereby reducing bias in language generation tasks without affecting the model's performance on other tasks. The methodology is supported by a causal analysis that identifies the most biased components in LLMs, and the paper includes empirical evidence showing that DAMA effectively reduces bias in various LLM tasks. However, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the findings to other types of biases and the practical implications of the method in real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue of reducing gender bias in large language models (LLMs), which is crucial for ensuring fairness and inclusion in AI applications.
- The methodology is clearly explained, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The experimental results are robust, showing that the proposed method, DAMA, effectively reduces gender bias in LLMs without significant performance loss.
- The paper introduces a novel approach by focusing on the mid-upper feed-forward layers, which is a new direction in bias mitigation research.
- The causal analysis conducted is thorough and informative, providing insights into the components of LLMs that store gender-biased representations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on gender bias alone may limit its applicability to other types of biases, such as racial or religious biases.
- The methodology of DAMA appears to be heuristic and lacks a rigorous theoretical foundation, which could limit its generalizability to other types of biases.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to gender bias, which may not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of DAMA in real-world scenarios where multiple biases co-exist.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method comprehensively, which could affect the reliability and practical applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other existing methods, which could help in understanding the relative advantages and disadvantages of DAMA.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the methodology of DAMA could be adapted to address other types of biases, such as racial or religious biases?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods in terms of effectiveness and practical applicability?
- In light of the potential limitations discussed, what are the specific scenarios or applications where DAMA is most effective in reducing gender bias in LLMs?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the linear projection method and how it was validated to ensure that it effectively mitigates gender bias?
- How does the method handle the potential for unintended biases or negative impacts on other aspects of the model's performance?
