 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel acquisition function for multi-objective Bayesian optimization (MOBO) based on the CDF indicator, which assigns multivariate ranks to solutions using the cumulative distribution function. This approach is claimed to be invariant to relative rescaling or monotonic transformations of objectives and provides a scalable solution to the high-dimensional integrals involved in traditional multi-objective acquisition functions. The CDF indicator is shown to be Pareto-compliant and robust to monotonic transformations of objectives, making it a potentially valuable tool for MOBO. The paper includes a series of synthetic and real-data experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, although concerns are raised about the novelty and the lack of a direct comparison with other methods like EHVI and entropy search.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method.
- The method is general and can be used in various scenarios, including discrete spaces.
- The proposed CDF indicator is Pareto-compliant and robust to monotonic transformations of objectives, which is a significant advantage over other methods like EHVI and entropy search.
- The paper includes a series of synthetic and real-data experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it performs on par with or better than existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- The method's invariance to relative rescaling or monotonic transformations of objectives is a notable strength, especially in the context of multi-objective optimization where such transformations can significantly affect the optimization process.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a direct comparison with other methods like EHVI and entropy search, which are also designed to be invariant to relative rescaling or monotonic transformations of objectives. This makes it difficult to assess the true advantages of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the computational costs of the proposed method with existing methods, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- The paper's claims of scalability and robustness are not adequately supported by empirical evidence, particularly in high-dimensional scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its strengths and weaknesses.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods, particularly in terms of the novelty and practical advantages over established methods like EHVI and entropy search.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a direct comparison of the proposed method with EHVI and entropy search, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- How does the proposed method perform in high-dimensional scenarios, and what are the computational costs associated with its implementation in such settings?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their proposed method and how these might impact its practical application?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues that are common in multi-objective optimization, particularly in high-dimensional spaces?
- Is there a specific reason why the proposed method was not compared directly with EHVI and entropy search in the experiments, and could this comparison be included in future work?
