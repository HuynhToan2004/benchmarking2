 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework called RL-CFR, which leverages a Markov Decision Process (MDP) to dynamically select the most effective action abstraction in Imperfect Information Extensive-Form Games (IIEFGs). This method aims to optimize performance by adjusting action abstractions based on the current state, which is a significant improvement over traditional fixed action abstractions. The approach is tested against existing methods like ReBeL and Slumbot, showing promising results in the Heads-up No-limit Texas Hold'em (HUNL) game. The paper's methodology involves a two-phase process where the first phase uses a MDP to determine the optimal action abstraction, and the second phase applies CFR to find the best strategy based on this abstraction. Despite the innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited scope to a single game and the lack of a comprehensive comparison with other action abstraction techniques.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a well-structured presentation that aids in understanding the complex concepts.
- The introduction of a novel MDP formulation for IIEFGs, which allows for dynamic adjustment of action abstractions based on public information, is a significant contribution.
- The proposed RL-CFR framework effectively combines deep reinforcement learning with CFR, offering a principled way to handle the challenges of IIEFGs.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of RL-CFR through empirical evaluations, showing significant improvements over existing methods like ReBeL and Slumbot.
- The paper provides a thorough background on IIEFGs, which is helpful for readers unfamiliar with the domain.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a single game, Heads-up No-limit Texas Hold'em, which may not generalize well to other games or scenarios.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other action abstraction techniques, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of RL-CFR, which is a critical aspect for understanding its practical applicability.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need correction, which could detract from the professionalism of the paper.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the proposed method handles the challenges of IIEFGs, such as the need for mixed strategies and the probability-dependent rewards.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on how the proposed method handles the challenges of IIEFGs, particularly the need for mixed strategies and probability-dependent rewards?
- How does the RL-CFR framework compare in terms of computational complexity to other action abstraction techniques?
- What are the specific computational resources required for training the RL-CFR model, and how do these compare to other methods like ReBeL and Slumbot?
- In the context of the MDP formulation, how does the public state (PS) capture the uncertainty and incomplete information inherent in IIEFGs?
- Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the action network and critic network used in the RL-CFR framework, and how they contribute to the overall performance of the model?
- The paper mentions that the action abstraction chosen by the action network outperforms the default action abstraction after a period of training. Could you provide more insights into this observation?
