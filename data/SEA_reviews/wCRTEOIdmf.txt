 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of graph kernels for subgraph isomorphism counting, proposing a novel approach that enhances the traditional Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL) kernel by incorporating neighborhood information. This modification allows for the calculation of kernel values between graphs, which can be used to estimate the number of subgraph isomorphisms. The approach is tested on various datasets, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods like RGIN, and its experimental results do not convincingly outperform state-of-the-art methods like CompGCN.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with a clear presentation of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The use of graph kernels to approximate subgraph isomorphism counting is an innovative approach, which could potentially lead to more efficient and effective methods for handling complex graph data.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of related work, including both traditional methods and recent advancements in graph kernels and subgraph counting.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method, while not outperforming state-of-the-art methods in all cases, does offer some improvements, particularly in the handling of heterogeneous data.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is limited as it builds upon existing methods like RGIN, with only minor modifications such as the incorporation of neighborhood information.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods like CompGCN, particularly in terms of accuracy and efficiency.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant drawback in practical applications.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of scalability and efficiency on larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous experimental validation, including a broader range of datasets and a more thorough analysis of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in relation to the size of the datasets used?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of scalability and efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly on larger, more complex datasets?
- Why was the decision made to exclude certain datasets from the experimental evaluation, and what impact does this have on the generalizability of the results?
- Can you provide more detailed results on the performance of the proposed method on homogeneous and heterogeneous datasets, and how these results compare to those of other methods?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of overfitting, particularly in cases where the training data is limited or noisy?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of datasets and the selection criteria for including or excluding datasets from the experimental evaluation?
