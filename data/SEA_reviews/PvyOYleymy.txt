 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CRATE-MAE, a novel autoencoder architecture that integrates white-box principles with diffusion models, aiming to learn structured representations through unsupervised pretext tasks such as masked autoencoding. CRATE-MAE leverages the connection between denoising and compression to optimize the sparsity and compression of the representation, which is crucial for efficient data processing. The architecture is designed to be mathematically interpretable, making it a significant advancement in the field of white-box models for representation learning. The paper also discusses the potential of this model to outperform existing methods like MAE while using fewer parameters, demonstrating its efficiency and effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel autoencoder architecture that integrates white-box principles with diffusion models, offering a unique approach to learning structured representations through unsupervised pretext tasks.
- The proposed CRATE-MAE model is mathematically interpretable, which is a significant advancement in the field of white-box models for representation learning.
- The paper provides a clear and comprehensive explanation of the theoretical underpinnings and the practical implementation of the CRATE-MAE model, making it accessible to readers.
- The empirical results show that CRATE-MAE outperforms existing methods like MAE while using fewer parameters, demonstrating its efficiency and effectiveness.
- The paper establishes a strong connection between denoising and compression, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of data in different representations and could lead to new insights and applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed model, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of data and its scalability to larger or more complex datasets.
- The empirical results are not sufficiently robust to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of CRATE-MAE over existing methods, especially in terms of the accuracy and generalization of the model across different datasets and scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and the training time of CRATE-MAE compared to other models like MAE, which is crucial for practical deployment.
- There is a lack of detailed comparison with other white-box models, which could help in understanding the unique advantages and disadvantages of CRATE-MAE.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed visualizations and explanations of the attention maps and the semantic segmentation properties of the model, which are crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the model.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific advantages of CRATE-MAE over other white-box models, especially in terms of its scalability and applicability to different types of data?
- How does CRATE-MAE perform in scenarios where the data distribution is more complex or the data volume is significantly larger? What are the potential limitations of the model in such scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more detailed visualizations and explanations of the attention maps and the semantic segmentation properties of the model? How do these properties contribute to the overall effectiveness of the model?
- In terms of computational complexity and training time, how does CRATE-MAE compare to other models like MAE? What are the potential trade-offs in terms of performance and efficiency?
- How does CRATE-MAE handle the issue of overfitting, especially in scenarios where the model is trained on smaller or less diverse datasets? What strategies can be employed to mitigate this risk?
