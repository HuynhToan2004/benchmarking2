 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ProteinWorkshop, a benchmark suite designed to evaluate protein structure representation learning methods. It curates datasets for pretraining and downstream tasks, including both experimental and predicted structures, and employs various GNN architectures for benchmarking. The authors explore different levels of structural granularity and the impact of pretraining and auxiliary tasks on model performance. Key findings include the effectiveness of sequence denoising and structure denoising in improving model performance, and the superiority of equivariant models over invariant models when pretrained on specific tasks. The paper also highlights the utility of the benchmark in reducing the barrier to entry for working with large protein structure datasets and facilitating systematic evaluations of protein structure representation learning methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive benchmark suite for evaluating protein structure representation learning methods, which is crucial for the machine learning and computational biology communities.
- It offers large-scale pre-training and downstream tasks using both experimental and predicted structures, ensuring a balanced challenge to representation learning algorithms.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, with clear figures and tables that aid in the presentation of complex data.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments, which are detailed and provide a comprehensive evaluation of the effectiveness of different GNN architectures for protein structure representation learning.
- The paper includes open-source code, which is a significant contribution to the field, reducing the barrier to entry for working with large protein structure datasets and facilitating systematic evaluations of protein structure representation learning methods.

**Weaknesses:**

- The experimental results are not comprehensive, with only a few GNN architectures being tested, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing benchmarks, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed methods.
