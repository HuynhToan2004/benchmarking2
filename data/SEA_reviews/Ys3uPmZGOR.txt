 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel leader-follower neural network (LFNN) architecture that replicates the behavior of biological neural networks, where neurons function as "workers" that can be either leaders or followers. The LFNN architecture is designed to mimic the collective behavior observed in nature, with leaders receiving global error signals and followers aligning with their neighbors. This approach allows for the training of networks without backpropagation, which is claimed to improve scalability and performance. The paper presents extensive experiments on various datasets including MNIST, CIFAR-10, and ImageNet, demonstrating that LFNNs can outperform traditional backpropagation-based methods in some scenarios. However, the paper also raises concerns about the novelty and practical implementation of the leader selection mechanism, which is crucial for the effectiveness of the LFNN.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel leader-follower neural network (LFNN) architecture that replicates the behavior of biological neural networks, which is a significant contribution to the field of deep learning.
- The LFNN architecture is designed to mimic the collective behavior observed in nature, which is a unique approach that differentiates it from traditional neural network architectures.
- The paper presents extensive experiments on various datasets including MNIST, CIFAR-10, and ImageNet, demonstrating that LFNNs can outperform traditional backpropagation-based methods in some scenarios.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors have made the code publicly available, which facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the leader-follower architecture, especially in relation to the biological plausibility of the leader selection mechanism.
- The leader selection mechanism is not well-explained, particularly how it is implemented and how it affects the performance of the network.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the LFNN with other BP-free methods, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its advantages and limitations.
- The novelty of the LFNN is questionable as similar approaches have been explored in the literature, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing work.
- The paper does not address the computational complexity or scalability of the LFNN architecture, which could limit its applicability in large-scale neural networks.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the LFNN architecture, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of neural networks and its impact on training times and resource usage.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify how the leader selection mechanism is implemented and how it affects the performance of the network?
- How does the LFNN compare to other BP-free methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- What are the specific advantages of the LFNN architecture over traditional neural networks, especially in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Could you provide more details on the practical implementation of the LFNN architecture, including the hardware and software requirements and the impact on training times and resource usage?
- How does the LFNN architecture perform in different types of neural networks, such as recurrent neural networks and transformer models?
- Can you provide more experimental results on larger datasets like JFT-300M and ImageNet, and compare the LFNN with more state-of-the-art methods to better establish its effectiveness?
