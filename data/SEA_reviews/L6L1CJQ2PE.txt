 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for massively editing large language models (LLMs) by aggregating parameter shifts using a least squares problem and the normal equation. This approach, termed MALMEN, enables the editing of multiple facts simultaneously, reducing the computational and memory requirements compared to existing methods like MEND. The method is evaluated across various tasks, including closed book fact verification and question answering, demonstrating its scalability and efficiency. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as it primarily adapts existing techniques to the context of massively editing LLMs, and for its reliance on specific model architectures and training setups.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and presentation of the problem.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with a clear algorithm that is easy to understand and implement.
- The experiments are comprehensive, including scalability studies, ablation studies, and comparisons to relevant baselines.
- The paper addresses an important problem of editing large language models (LLMs) to correct misconceptions, which is crucial for ensuring the accuracy and reliability of LLMs.
- The method demonstrates good scalability, enabling the editing of hundreds of facts simultaneously, which is a significant advancement over existing methods.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the limitations of the method, which aids in understanding its applicability and potential improvements.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison to existing works, particularly those that address similar issues in the context of massively editing LLMs.
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as it primarily adapts existing techniques to the context of massively editing LLMs without significant innovation.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to specific model architectures and training setups, which may not generalize well to other models or settings.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method beyond a certain number of edits, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The method's reliance on the normal equation for parameter shift aggregation and the use of a regularization term for numerical stability are not well justified or explained, which could confuse readers.
- The paper's experimental results and methodology, such as the use of a linear layer to map the final hidden state of the BOS token to a log-probability, are not clearly justified or explained, which could limit the reproducibility and understanding of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to existing works that address similar issues in the context of massively editing LLMs?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the method beyond a certain number of edits?
- Why is the normal equation used for parameter shift aggregation, and how does it compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness?
- How does the method ensure that the edits are consistent across different prompts, and what measures are taken to prevent the model from learning to reverse the prompts instead of editing them?
- Can the authors clarify the role and necessity of the regularization term in the method, and provide more details on its impact on the model's performance and stability?
- How does the method handle the potential for contradictory edits, and what measures are taken to prevent these from occurring?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the use of a linear layer to map the final hidden state of the BOS token to a log-probability, and the choice of training setups?
