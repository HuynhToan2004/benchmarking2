 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called odd-k-out (OKO) learning, aimed at improving model calibration and accuracy, particularly in scenarios with limited training data and class imbalances. OKO modifies the learning objective by presenting sets of examples rather than individual examples, which encourages the model to consider correlations between sets of examples. This approach leads to better calibration and uncertainty estimates compared to standard cross-entropy training, as demonstrated through extensive experiments. Theoretical analysis supports the effectiveness of OKO by showing how it prevents models from overfitting to low-entropy regions, thereby reducing confidence in predictions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method called odd-k-out (OKO) learning, which addresses the problem of model calibration, particularly in scenarios with limited training data and class imbalances.
- The method is simple, easy to implement, and requires minimal computational overhead, making it accessible for practical applications.
- The theoretical analysis provided supports the effectiveness of OKO by showing how it prevents models from overfitting to low-entropy regions, thereby reducing confidence in predictions.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed method and its implementation.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of OKO in improving model calibration and accuracy, particularly in scenarios with limited training data and class imbalances.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the OKO method, including its theoretical foundations and practical applications, which could be beneficial for researchers and practitioners in the field of machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the area of model calibration and class imbalanced learning.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets and model architectures, which may not generalize well to other datasets or architectures.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including its applicability to other types of datasets and its scalability to larger, more complex models.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational overhead introduced by the OKO method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- The theoretical analysis provided is limited to binary classification, and it is unclear how the method can be extended to multi-class classification scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a critical consideration for any new machine learning technology.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition of the odd-one-out task used in the paper and provide a clearer explanation of how it differs from other similar tasks?
- How does the OKO method compare to other methods that also aim to improve model calibration, such as temperature scaling or other post-processing techniques?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the OKO method can be adapted to handle multi-class classification scenarios?
- How does the OKO method handle class imbalances in the training data, and what are the potential impacts on model performance and calibration?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how these impacts might be mitigated or addressed?
- How does the OKO method perform in scenarios with very small or very large datasets, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in these scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational overhead introduced by the OKO method, and how this might affect the scalability and practical applicability of the method?
