 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for object-centric learning, named Competition Over Pixel features (COP), which leverages a non-iterative, fully-differentiable approach using only Convolution and MaxPool layers, alongside an attention layer. COP is designed to extract slots from images and has been evaluated on standard benchmarks, showing competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods. The methodology is based on competitive subnetworks and a soft-winner-takes-all attention layer, which aims to simplify the training process and improve scalability. Despite its strengths, the paper has been criticized for its limited evaluation on more complex datasets and its similarity to existing methods, particularly in its use of attention mechanisms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology.
- The method is simple, using only convolution, max-pooling, and attention layers, which makes it easy to implement and understand.
- The proposed method, COP, is scalable and non-iterative, which simplifies the training process and reduces the complexity of the model.
- COP achieves competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods on standard benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper includes an ablation study that helps in understanding the impact of different components of the method.
- The use of competition mechanisms like MaxPooling and SWTA-Attention for object-centric learning is innovative and provides a fresh perspective on the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other recent methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of COP's relative performance.
- The evaluation is limited to simpler datasets, and the method's performance on more complex, real-world datasets is not explored.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate COP from existing methods, particularly in its use of attention mechanisms, which are similar to those in other methods.
- There is a lack of clarity in some figures and descriptions, making it difficult to fully grasp the method's operation.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the method, especially in terms of the number of slots, which is a critical parameter for object-centric learning.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential improvements.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between COP and other methods, especially in terms of the attention mechanisms used?
- How does the method perform on more complex datasets, such as those with more objects or more varied shapes and colors?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, particularly how the number of slots is determined and how it affects performance?
- How does the method handle occlusion in objects, and what are the implications of this for the quality of the extracted slots?
- Is there a possibility to adapt the method to handle different numbers of objects in the test data, or does it require a fixed number of slots?
- Can the authors provide more visualizations or detailed explanations of the attention mechanisms to better understand how they contribute to the model's performance?
