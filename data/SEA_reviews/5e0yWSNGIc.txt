 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of adversarial training on the accuracy and consistency of state-action value functions in reinforcement learning. The authors provide both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support their claims, demonstrating that adversarially trained models can suffer from overestimation of Q-values and inconsistencies in action rankings. The paper argues that these issues arise due to the adversarial training's focus on ensuring robustness to perturbations rather than accurate estimation of state-action values. The empirical evidence is supported by experiments conducted in the Arcade Learning Environment, showing that vanilla trained models perform better in certain scenarios. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope, lack of comprehensive theoretical justification, and unclear presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of adversarial training for reinforcement learning, which is an important and emerging area of research.
- The authors provide both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support their claims, enhancing the credibility of their findings.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed analysis of the potential issues with adversarial training.
- The empirical results are supported by a thorough analysis of the action gap and the ranking of actions, providing a robust foundation for the conclusions drawn.
- The paper is significant as it challenges the assumptions and methodologies commonly used in adversarial training, potentially leading to new insights and improvements in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical analysis is limited and does not fully explain the empirical results, particularly the overestimation of Q-values and inconsistencies in action rankings.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of adversarial training, which could have provided a more balanced view of the topic.
- The experimental setup is limited, focusing only on a few environments, which may not generalize well to other settings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the methodologies used in the experiments, including hyperparameters and training details.
- The presentation of the paper is unclear in some sections, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key terms and concepts.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of the findings, which could be significant in the context of adversarial training.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the theoretical underpinnings of the empirical observations, particularly the overestimation of Q-values and inconsistencies in action rankings?
- How do the findings of this study relate to other types of robust training methods beyond adversarial training, such as those that use natural perturbations?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the hyperparameters used and the specifics of the training procedures?
- How do the findings of this study impact the design and implementation of adversarial training in practical applications, particularly in safety-critical systems?
- Can the authors address the potential limitations of their study, such as the scope of the experiments and the generalizability of the findings?
