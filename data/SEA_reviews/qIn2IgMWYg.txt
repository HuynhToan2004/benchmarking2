 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for attribution in deep neural networks, focusing on the integration of gradient ascent and descent to enhance the interpretability of model outputs. The Iterative Search Attribution (ISA) method aims to address the limitations of existing attribution methods by providing a more comprehensive and accurate attribution of feature importance. It uses an iterative approach to identify the most significant features, employing both gradient ascent and descent to determine the importance of different features. The method is evaluated against several baselines and demonstrates superior performance in certain metrics, such as the Insertion score. However, concerns are raised about the clarity and completeness of the paper, with issues noted in the experimental setup, presentation, and theoretical contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed method and its application to various models.
- The method introduces a novel approach by integrating gradient ascent and descent, which is a significant improvement over existing methods like IG and AGI.
- The methodology is straightforward and effective, with a focus on improving the Insertion score, which is a critical metric in attribution tasks.
- The paper is structured well, with a clear explanation of the method and its application, and includes a detailed evaluation of the proposed method against various baselines.
- The method's integration of gradient ascent and descent is a novel approach that enhances the interpretability of model outputs, which is a crucial aspect of deep learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough evaluation of the method's performance across different models and datasets, which limits the generalizability of the results.
- The experimental setup is not clearly described, particularly the parameters used and their impact on the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the method, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the method, with several sections requiring revision for better comprehension.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing methods in terms of computational complexity and time efficiency, which are crucial for practical deployment.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, particularly the scalability and applicability to different types of neural networks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed evaluation of the method's performance across different models and datasets, particularly in terms of its applicability to different architectures and its robustness to noise and adversarial attacks?
- How does the method perform in terms of computational complexity and time efficiency compared to existing methods?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and the parameters used in the experiments, particularly how these parameters were chosen and their impact on the results?
- How does the method handle the issue of gradient saturation, which is a common problem in gradient-based attribution methods?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing methods, particularly in terms of its theoretical contributions and practical implications?
- How does the method perform in terms of its interpretability and the accuracy of the attributions it provides?
