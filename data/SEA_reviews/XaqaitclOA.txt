 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of Physics Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) in solving the Burgers' equation, focusing on the stability and generalization bounds in the presence of finite-time blow-ups. It introduces new theoretical bounds for PINNs, demonstrating their correlation with the L2-distance of the neurally found surrogate from the true blow-up solution. The paper also includes experiments to validate these theoretical findings, showing that the bounds are non-trivial and correlate well with the L2-distance of the derived solution from the true solution. Despite the novelty of the approach, the paper is critiqued for its limited scope to the Burgers' equation and the lack of broader experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces novel generalization bounds for PINNs in the context of the Burgers' equation, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of the theoretical results, making the paper accessible to readers.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the correlation between the theoretical bounds and the true risk, enhancing the credibility of the theoretical findings.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear structure and logical flow, making it easy to follow.
- The paper's approach is innovative, using PINNs to solve PDEs that exhibit finite-time blow-ups, which is a novel application of the technology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to the Burgers' equation, which may not generalize well to other types of PDEs, particularly those that do not exhibit finite-time blow-ups.
- The experimental validation is limited to the Burgers' equation, which does not convincingly demonstrate the applicability of the proposed generalization bounds to other types of PDEs.
- The paper lacks a broader discussion on the practical implications of the findings, such as how the theoretical results could be applied to real-world problems.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing methods and a more comprehensive discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these limitations affect the applicability of the findings to other types of PDEs?
- How do the authors plan to extend the theoretical results beyond the Burgers' equation to other types of PDEs, particularly those that do not exhibit finite-time blow-ups?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of the findings, such as how the theoretical results could be applied to real-world problems?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability with other existing methods for solving PDEs using PINNs?
- The paper mentions that the theoretical results are not vacuous, but it is not clear what this means in the context of the results. Could the authors clarify this point?
