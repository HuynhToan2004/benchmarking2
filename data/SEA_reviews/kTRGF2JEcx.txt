 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, I3C, designed to enhance the performance of large language models (LLMs) in solving math word problems (MWPs) by identifying and ignoring irrelevant conditions. This method involves a two-step process: first, using a pre-trained language model to identify irrelevant conditions, and second, instructing the LLM to ignore these conditions. The approach is tested across various MWP datasets and demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods, particularly in few-shot settings. The paper also proposes a demonstration selection strategy, I3C-Select, which selects confusing problems to enhance the effectiveness of the method. However, the method's generalizability to other types of problems and its reliance on specific LLMs and datasets are noted as limitations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and well-defined problem.
- The proposed method, I3C, is simple, effective, and easy to implement, with potential broad applicability.
- The method demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods, particularly in few-shot settings, as shown in extensive experiments across multiple MWP datasets.
- The use of semantic similarity to identify irrelevant conditions and the instruction for the LLM to ignore these conditions is novel and effective.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's effectiveness, including ablation studies and efficiency evaluations, which support the robustness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's effectiveness might be limited to math problems, as it heavily relies on the SimCSE model which is trained on a corpus of math problems.
- The method's reliance on specific LLMs and datasets, such as GPT-3 and the selected MWP datasets, could limit its generalizability.
- The method's performance on more complex or varied problems, such as those with multiple irrelevant conditions or more complex logical relationships, is not thoroughly tested.
- The method's sensitivity to hyperparameters, such as the threshold for identifying irrelevant conditions, is not thoroughly analyzed, which could impact its reliability and applicability.
- The paper's experimental design and results presentation could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and detail in the experimental setup and results analysis.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform on other types of problems, such as those in the MathQA dataset, which might not be as dependent on specific problem formats?
- Can the method be adapted to other types of problems, such as natural language reasoning problems, and how might this affect its performance?
- How does the method handle more complex or varied problems, such as those with multiple irrelevant conditions or more complex logical relationships?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate how the method handles different types of irrelevant conditions and how it impacts the final answer?
- How does the method perform when different hyperparameters are used, and what are the implications of these choices on the method's effectiveness and reliability?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results presentation, particularly in terms of the methodology used and the statistical significance of the reported results?
