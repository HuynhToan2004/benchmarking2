 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MediTab, a framework designed to enhance the scalability of predictive models for medical tabular data. MediTab employs a three-step process: data consolidation, enrichment, and refinement, using large language models (LLMs) to align out-of-domain data with target tasks. This approach allows for the handling of diverse datasets with varying features and schema, enhancing the generalization of predictive models across different medical tabular datasets. The framework is evaluated on seven patient outcome prediction datasets and three trial outcome prediction datasets, demonstrating significant improvements over supervised baselines and the ability to perform zero-shot and few-shot learning. However, concerns about the novelty of the approach, the complexity of the method, and the clarity of the experimental results have been raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and understudied problem in the medical domain, focusing on the engineering of tabular data for predictive models, which is crucial for advancing healthcare applications.
- The proposed method, MediTab, is innovative in its approach to consolidating and enriching tabular data, which is a novel concept in the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The methodology is comprehensive, covering data consolidation, enrichment, and refinement, which is a robust approach to handling the complexities of medical data.
- The experiments demonstrate that MediTab outperforms supervised baselines, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited, as similar methods have been previously proposed, such as the use of LLMs for data consolidation and enrichment.
- The methodology is complex and not well-explained, particularly in sections 2.3 and 2.4, which could benefit from clearer descriptions and more detailed explanations.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The experimental results are not convincingly presented, with a lack of detailed comparisons and baselines that could undermine the claims of the paper.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of data quality, which is critical for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific contributions of MediTab compared to similar methods proposed in the past, such as those mentioned in the review?
- How does MediTab handle the challenge of data quality, particularly in ensuring that the data used for training is reliable and accurate?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of MediTab and how it scales with the size of the datasets used?
- In the context of the experimental results, could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods and include more comprehensive baselines to strengthen the claims of the paper?
- How does MediTab address the issue of noisy data, which is a significant challenge in medical data analysis?
- Could the authors elaborate on the methodology used in the experiments, particularly in sections 2.3 and 2.4, to improve the clarity and understanding of the proposed method?
