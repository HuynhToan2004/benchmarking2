 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for repairing Vision Transformers (ViTs) by focusing on the last layer's parameters, aiming to achieve provable correctness guarantees without modifying the model's architecture. The approach, named PRoViT, is designed to scale well and efficiently repair state-of-the-art ViTs, showing promising results in terms of generalization and efficiency. The method is evaluated against several baselines and existing methods, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency. However, concerns about the scalability of the method to larger models and the practicality of the repair set's size are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for repairing Vision Transformers (ViTs) by focusing on the last layer's parameters, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is efficient and scalable, as shown by its ability to repair large ViTs with thousands of images and achieve good generalization results.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results are promising, showing that the proposed method outperforms several baselines and existing methods in terms of accuracy and efficiency.
- The paper is the first to provide a provable repair method for ViTs, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the practical implications of the repair set's size and the scalability of the method to larger models, such as ViT-H/192.
- There is a lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art methods like MEMIT, which could provide a better understanding of the method's effectiveness and efficiency.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the drawdown and generalization results, which are crucial for evaluating the method's effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed experimental results, including the accuracy of the repaired models and the computational resources required.
- The method's reliance on the linear programming (LP) formulation for repairing the last layer may limit its applicability to other types of neural networks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the practical implications of the repair set's size and the scalability of the method to larger models?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods like MEMIT, particularly in terms of efficiency and accuracy?
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the drawdown and generalization results, including the computational resources required for these experiments?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to other types of neural networks, such as CNNs or recurrent neural networks (RNNs)?
- How does the method handle the trade-off between drawdown and generalization, and what are the implications of this trade-off for the practical applicability of the method?
