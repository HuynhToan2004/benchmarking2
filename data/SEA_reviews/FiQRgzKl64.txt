 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Mixture-of-Supernets (MoS) to enhance the performance of weight-sharing supernets in Neural Architecture Search (NAS) by reducing the performance gap between supernet and standalone models. This is achieved through a mixture-of-experts (MoE) mechanism that dynamically generates weights for different subnetworks, which are then used to train the final models. The methodology is applied to tasks like machine translation and pre-trained language models, showing improvements in both training efficiency and model performance. The paper also explores the use of MoS in different network architectures and discusses its effectiveness in reducing training time and improving model quality.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant issue of performance degradation in subnetworks extracted from supernets, which is a critical challenge in Neural Architecture Search (NAS).
- The proposed method, Mixture-of-Supernets (MoS), is well-motivated and effectively improves the performance of supernets, as demonstrated by extensive experiments.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly explained, contributing to its readability and comprehensibility.
- The proposed method is flexible and can be applied to various network architectures, which enhances its applicability and versatility.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in reducing training time and improving model performance.
- The use of Mixture of Experts (MoE) in supernet training is innovative and shows potential for improving the efficiency and effectiveness of NAS.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods like HAT and other state-of-the-art NAS techniques, which could have provided a clearer picture of the proposed method's advantages and limitations.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of the proposed method on the training time for standalone models, which could be a significant concern given the computational resources required.
- The paper's experimental setup and the choice of hyperparameters are not thoroughly explained, which could limit the reproducibility and understanding of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in large-scale NAS scenarios where the computational cost could be prohibitive.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost and memory requirements of the proposed method, which could be crucial for practical implementation.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its applicability in different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art NAS techniques, particularly in terms of computational cost and model performance?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the search space is significantly larger or smaller than those used in the experiments?
- Can the authors discuss the potential of using different routing strategies or other MoE techniques to further improve the performance of the supernets?
- How does the proposed method perform in different types of NLP tasks beyond machine translation and pre-trained language models?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the hyperparameters used and the computational resources required?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost and memory requirements of the proposed method, particularly in large-scale NAS scenarios?
