 **Summary:**
The paper explores the transferability of continuous prompts across different language models, proposing a method that encodes source prompts into a relative space and searches for corresponding target prompts. This approach aims to generalize task semantics across models, showing effectiveness in zero-shot settings. The methodology involves using a set of common tokens as anchors to encode the relative space, which is then used to search for target prompt embeddings. The paper includes experiments that demonstrate the method's effectiveness in transferring task semantics from one model to another, although the novelty of the approach is questioned due to its similarity to previous works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach to continuous prompt transfer, which is both original and well-executed.
- The methodology is clearly articulated, making it easy to understand and implement.
- The experimental setup is robust, with comprehensive results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of prompt transfer, which is crucial for advancing the application of large language models (LLMs).
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a well-structured presentation that aids in understanding the complex concepts.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it closely resembles previous works, particularly in the use of relative space for prompt transfer.
- The paper lacks a broader evaluation of the method's effectiveness across different types of tasks beyond factual probing, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The method's reliance on a shared vocabulary between source and target models might limit its applicability in scenarios where such overlap does not exist.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include its applicability to different model architectures and the potential for overfitting due to the transfer of task-specific prompts.
- The paper's evaluation primarily focuses on factual probing, which might not adequately reflect the method's performance in other types of tasks.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when the source and target models are trained on different corpora, and what are the implications of this on the transferability of the prompts?
- Can you provide more details on the optimization of the transfer accuracy function and how it affects the final performance?
- How does the method handle the transfer of task-specific prompts to models with different architectures or training paradigms?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of using relative space for prompt transfer and how it compares to other methods like direct tuning or discretization?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting when transferring task-specific prompts to models with different architectures or training paradigms?
