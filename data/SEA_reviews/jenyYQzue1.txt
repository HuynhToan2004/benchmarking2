 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Multi-Step Soft Reasoning (MuSR) dataset, designed to evaluate the reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) in complex, natural language narratives. The dataset is synthetically generated using GPT-4 and is structured into three domains: murder mysteries, object placement, and team allocation. The authors evaluate various LLMs, including GPT-4, GPT-3.5, and others, using a chain-of-thought prompting strategy. The dataset is claimed to be challenging and realistic, requiring multiple steps of reasoning and commonsense understanding. However, concerns were raised about the dataset's complexity and the generalizability of the results due to the synthetic nature of the data and the potential for overfitting to the training data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel dataset, MuSR, which is specifically designed to challenge LLMs in complex reasoning tasks, particularly in long and complex narratives.
- The dataset is well-constructed, with clear methodology and detailed descriptions of the dataset generation process, including the use of GPT-4 for generating narratives and reasoning trees.
- The paper includes a variety of experiments and evaluations, including human evaluations, which provide valuable insights into the dataset's effectiveness and the performance of different models.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the dataset and the experimental setup, making it accessible and easy to understand.
- The dataset is innovative in its approach to generating complex reasoning problems, using a neurosymbolic method that involves generating a reasoning tree and then a narrative based on that tree.

**Weaknesses:**
- The dataset is primarily synthetic and not based on real-world data, which could limit its generalizability and applicability to real-world scenarios.
- The dataset is relatively small, with only 250 instances across three domains, which may not be sufficient to draw robust conclusions about the performance of different models.
- The evaluation of the dataset is limited to a few models, primarily GPT-4, which may not fully demonstrate the dataset's capabilities across a broader range of models.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting due to the synthetic nature of the data, which could limit the dataset's usefulness in real-world applications.
- The dataset's complexity and the need for multiple steps of reasoning make it difficult for humans to solve, which could limit its practical utility as a benchmark for evaluating model performance.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the generation process for the dataset, which could be crucial for understanding the dataset's quality and reliability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the gold facts F are generated and whether they are generated by GPT-4 or another method?
- How does the dataset ensure that the generated narratives are consistent with the facts and the reasoning tree, especially given the potential for overfitting?
- What are the specific reasons for using GPT-4 to generate the narratives, and how does this affect the dataset's quality and reliability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the generation process for the dataset, including the prompt used and the steps involved in generating the narratives?
- How does the dataset ensure that the reasoning tree is valid and does not contain shortcuts or vacuous transitions?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of overfitting on the dataset's results and how this was addressed in the evaluation?
