 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method to detect distribution shifts in model-based optimization (MBO) by training a binary classifier to distinguish between training data and data generated by an optimization algorithm. The method is validated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in detecting distribution shifts and potentially improving the quality of designed samples. The approach uses a binary classifier to predict whether a sample is in-distribution or out-of-distribution, which can be used as a proxy for distribution shift. The method's practical utility is highlighted by its application in real-world scenarios, such as protein engineering, where it can potentially enhance the reliability of model predictions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in machine learning-guided design, specifically the issue of distribution shift in model-based optimization (MBO).
- The proposed method is straightforward and effective, particularly in real-world applications such as protein engineering, where it can be applied to improve the reliability of model predictions.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The methodology is supported by empirical results that demonstrate its effectiveness in real-world scenarios, showing a reduction in regret when selecting designs based on the OOD score.
- The paper provides a novel approach to detecting distribution shifts in MBO, which is a crucial aspect of model-based optimization.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods for detecting distribution shifts in MBO, which could help establish the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The methodology section is insufficiently detailed, particularly in explaining how the binary classifier is trained and how it distinguishes between in-distribution and out-of-distribution samples.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include issues such as the sensitivity of the OOD score to the choice of threshold and the potential for misclassification of in-distribution samples as out-of-distribution.
- There is a lack of discussion on the generalizability of the method to other types of MBO problems beyond protein design.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the binary classifier is trained and how it distinguishes between in-distribution and out-of-distribution samples?
- How does the method perform when applied to other types of MBO problems beyond protein design?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical application?
- Could the authors elaborate on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it might impact its feasibility in real-world applications?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for detecting distribution shifts in MBO, and what are the advantages and disadvantages of each approach?
