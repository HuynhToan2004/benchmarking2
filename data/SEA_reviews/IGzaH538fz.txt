 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GraphGuard, a certified defense mechanism against adversarial attacks on graph classification tasks. GraphGuard employs a novel approach by dividing graphs into sub-graphs using a hash function, which allows for the construction of an ensemble classifier that predicts labels for each sub-graph. This method ensures robustness against both graph structure and node feature perturbations, offering deterministic guarantees against adversarial attacks. The paper claims that GraphGuard outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods in terms of robustness and efficiency, as demonstrated through experiments on eight benchmark datasets. However, concerns are raised about the clarity and depth of the threat model, the practicality of the hash function approach, and the fairness of the comparison with other methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel certified defense mechanism, GraphGuard, which effectively addresses the limitations of existing certified defenses by providing robustness guarantees for both graph structure and node feature perturbations.
- The proposed method is straightforward, efficient, and easy to implement, making it practical for real-world applications.
- The paper is well-structured, providing clear explanations and detailed experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of GraphGuard in improving robustness and efficiency over existing methods.
- The use of a hash function to divide graphs into sub-graphs is innovative and offers a new approach to defending against adversarial attacks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The threat model is not clearly defined, particularly concerning the adversary's knowledge and capabilities, which could affect the practical applicability of the proposed defense.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scope and applicability.
- There are concerns about the fairness of the comparison with other methods, particularly in terms of the computational resources and training data used.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant omission in ethical considerations.
- The practicality of the hash function approach is questionable, especially in real-world scenarios where node IDs might not be known or consistent.
- The paper's claims of deterministic robustness are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which could lead to misleading conclusions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the adversary's knowledge and capabilities in the threat model, and how this affects the practical applicability of the proposed defense?
- How does the proposed method perform against adaptive attacks, where the attacker is aware of the defense mechanism?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their proposed method, including potential scenarios where GraphGuard may not be effective?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational resources and training data with other methods, especially when these factors are not controlled?
- Could the authors address the potential negative societal impacts of their proposed method, and how do they ensure that the method is used ethically and responsibly?
- How does the proposed method perform against different types of attacks, such as black-box attacks, and what are the implications of these attacks on the robustness guarantees provided by GraphGuard?
