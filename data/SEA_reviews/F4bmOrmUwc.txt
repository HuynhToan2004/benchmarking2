 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel classifier, the Fixed Non-negative Orthogonal (FNO) classifier, designed to induce a zero-mean neural collapse phenomenon in non-negative Euclidean space. This classifier is proposed to address challenges in continual learning and imbalanced learning by employing feature dimension separation (FDS) and arc-mixup. The FNO classifier, which is orthogonal and non-negative, aims to improve classification accuracy by ensuring that last-layer features are orthogonal and non-negative. The paper also explores the theoretical underpinnings of this classifier and provides empirical evidence of its effectiveness through experiments on various datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- It introduces a novel classifier, the FNO classifier, which is orthogonal and non-negative, and demonstrates its effectiveness in inducing a zero-mean neural collapse phenomenon.
- The theoretical analysis of the FNO classifier is robust, providing a strong foundation for understanding its behavior and performance.
- The paper is original in its approach, offering a fresh perspective on neural collapse and its application in continual learning and imbalanced learning.
- The empirical results are convincing, showing that the proposed method can outperform existing methods in certain scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed classifier.
- The experimental results are not robustly supported, with a limited number of trials and a lack of detailed experimental setups.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the requirement that the number of features be greater than the number of classes.
- There are inconsistencies in the notation and definitions used throughout the paper, which could confuse readers and detract from the clarity of the presentation.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work and a more rigorous analysis of the experimental results to enhance its credibility.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific advantages of the FNO classifier over other existing classifiers, particularly in terms of its ability to induce a zero-mean neural collapse?
- How does the FNO classifier compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability to other fixed classifiers?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental setups and results, including a broader range of datasets and more rigorous statistical analysis?
- What are the potential applications of the FNO classifier beyond continual learning and imbalanced learning?
- How does the FNO classifier handle the challenge of requiring a larger number of features than classes, and what are the implications of this requirement for practical deployment?
