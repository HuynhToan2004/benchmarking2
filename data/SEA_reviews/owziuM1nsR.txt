 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Recursive Generalization Transformer (RGT) for image super-resolution, which utilizes recursive-generalization self-attention (RG-SA) to aggregate global features and local self-attention for local context. The RG-SA is designed to reduce computational complexity by recursively aggregating features into smaller maps, which are then used for cross-attention. This method aims to enhance the global context exploitation while maintaining computational efficiency. The RGT is evaluated on various datasets and shows improvements over existing methods. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods like XCiT, and the paper lacks detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed RG-SA is simple and effective, reducing computational complexity by recursively aggregating features into smaller maps.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive ablation study that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) results on standard datasets like Set5, Set14, B100, Urban100, and Manga109.
- The use of a hybrid adaptive integration (HAI) module allows for direct fusion between features at different levels, enhancing the integration of global and local contexts.
- The paper provides a detailed complexity analysis, which is crucial for understanding the computational efficiency of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and model size.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it bears strong similarities to existing methods like XCiT, especially in the recursive feature aggregation and channel dimension scaling.
- The paper does not provide sufficient quantitative comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, such as CAT-A and SwinIR, which are crucial for validating the claimed improvements.
- The paper lacks visual results on high-resolution datasets like Kodak, which are essential for evaluating the performance of the method on high-resolution images.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed visual comparisons and a broader range of experimental setups to validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods across different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the RG-SA module differs from existing methods like XCiT, particularly in terms of feature aggregation and channel dimension scaling?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and model size with state-of-the-art methods like CAT-A and SwinIR?
- Could the authors provide visual results on high-resolution datasets like Kodak to better evaluate the performance of the method on high-resolution images?
- How does the proposed method handle the misalignment between global and local features, especially in scenarios where this misalignment could lead to suboptimal results?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and potential areas for future research?
- Could the authors provide more detailed visual comparisons with state-of-the-art methods to better demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods?
