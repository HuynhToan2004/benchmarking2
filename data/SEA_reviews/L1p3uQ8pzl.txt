 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Pokemon-Py, a Python library designed to simulate Pokemon battles, which is used to evaluate the performance of large language models (LLMs) in a new world setting, specifically the Pokemon universe. The authors demonstrate that existing LLMs struggle with out-of-distribution knowledge and logical reasoning in this context. They propose a novel self-supervised learning method called "Self-Training with Self-Competition" to adapt LLMs to new world settings. The method involves training models to compete against each other, allowing the winning model to evolve and learn new strategies. The approach is evaluated on two downstream tasks, showing that the adapted LLMs can perform better than baseline models. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to the similarity of the self-training method to existing approaches, and the evaluation is considered limited as it only includes a few LLMs and does not compare the method across diverse datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem of how large language models (LLMs) perform in new and unfamiliar domains, specifically focusing on the Pokemon universe.
- The proposed self-training method, "Self-Training with Self-Competition," is a novel approach to adapting LLMs to new world settings, showing potential for broader applications.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and includes a comprehensive evaluation of LLMs' performance in the Pokemon world, providing valuable insights into their limitations.
- The authors have released the Pokemon-Py library, which can be used by the community to further research in this area.
- The proposed method shows promise in improving LLMs' performance in specific domains, which could be beneficial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on a single domain (Pokemon) limits the generalizability of the findings and the applicability of the proposed method to other domains.
- The evaluation of the method is limited, focusing only on a few LLMs (ChatGPT, LLaMA2-70b, and Alpaca-7b) and not comparing the method across a diverse range of datasets or models.
- The novelty of the self-training method is questioned as it closely resembles existing approaches, such as those by Vinyals et al. (2019).
- The paper does not adequately address how the method could be adapted to other domains or how it might perform in more complex or diverse environments.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its potential societal impacts, which could be critical for understanding its broader implications.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the proposed method could be adapted to other domains beyond the Pokemon universe?
- How does the method compare to other self-training methods, such as those proposed by Vinyals et al. (2019)?
- What are the potential societal impacts of using LLMs in new and unfamiliar domains, and how might the proposed method address or exacerbate these issues?
- How does the method perform in more complex or diverse environments, and what are the limitations of its applicability?
- Could you provide more details on the selection of the LLMs used in the study, and how representative they are of the broader LLM landscape?
- How does the method handle the issue of hallucination in LLMs, and what steps are taken to mitigate this issue in your experiments?
