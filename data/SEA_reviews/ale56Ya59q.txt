 **Summary:**
The paper introduces VQScore, a self-supervised metric for evaluating speech quality using a vector-quantized variational autoencoder (VQ-VAE). The methodology includes a self-supervised speech enhancement (SE) model that trains on clean speech, utilizing a novel self-distillation mechanism combined with adversarial training for robustness. The paper claims that VQScore can be used to predict speech quality without requiring clean speech references, which is a significant advantage over traditional methods. The authors also propose a speech enhancement model that does not require noisy speech for training, which is a departure from conventional methods. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, unclear methodological explanations, and insufficient experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel self-supervised speech quality estimator, VQScore, which is trained on clean speech and does not require paired data, making it more scalable than existing methods.
- The proposed method is well-motivated and clearly presented, with a clear structure that makes it easy to follow.
- The methodology is supported by comprehensive experiments, including subjective evaluations, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, which is crucial for scientific communication.
- The paper provides code, which is a valuable resource for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in the context of its applicability and generalizability.
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as similar approaches using VQ-VAE for speech quality estimation and enhancement have been previously explored.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive experiments to validate the proposed method across different datasets and conditions, especially in terms of its robustness and scalability.
- There are some unclear explanations and potential inconsistencies in the methodology, such as the use of different distance metrics for speech quality estimation and the rationale behind certain experimental setups.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with state-of-the-art approaches, which could undermine the perceived effectiveness and novelty of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the computational efficiency and resource requirements of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the rationale behind using different distance metrics (cosine similarity vs. L2 distance) for speech quality estimation? What are the specific advantages of using cosine similarity?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of noise or distortions in speech, and what are the implications for its performance in real-world scenarios?
- Can you elaborate on the role of the adversarial training in the speech enhancement model, and how it contributes to the overall effectiveness of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art approaches in terms of performance, scalability, and resource requirements?
- Could you provide more insights into the design choices of the VQ-VAE, particularly how it contributes to the effectiveness of the speech quality estimation and enhancement?
