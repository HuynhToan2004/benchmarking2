 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SPDER, a novel neural network architecture designed to address the spectral bias in conventional implicit neural representation networks. SPDER utilizes a sinusoidal multiplied by a sublinear function as its activation function, which effectively captures positional encoding and maintains the value of the input coordinate. This architecture is demonstrated to speed up training by 10x and achieve state-of-the-art performance in image and audio representation tasks, showing significant improvements in performance and efficiency. The methodology is supported by extensive experiments, demonstrating SPDER's superiority over existing methods in various applications such as super-resolution and frame interpolation.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed SPDER architecture is simple yet effective, with a novel activation function that effectively addresses the spectral bias issue in conventional implicit neural representation networks.
- SPDER demonstrates superior performance in image and audio representation tasks, showing a significant improvement over existing methods in terms of performance and efficiency.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with extensive experiments and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of SPDER in various applications.
- SPDER is versatile and can be applied to various domains such as image super-resolution, edge detection, video frame interpolation, and audio representation, demonstrating its general applicability.
- The paper provides a detailed discussion on the spectral bias issue and the role of the proposed activation function in mitigating it, enhancing the understanding of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of SPDER, particularly its performance in novel view synthesis and its scalability to larger datasets.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage, especially in large-scale applications.
- The paper could benefit from more theoretical insights into the behavior of SPDER's activation function and its impact on the learning process.
- The paper's claims about SPDER's ability to learn positional information automatically and its impact on spectral bias are not sufficiently substantiated with empirical evidence.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the size and type of datasets used, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the societal impact of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in applications involving neural representations of images and audio.

**Questions:**
- How does SPDER perform in novel view synthesis tasks, and what are the specific challenges it faces in this application?
- Can SPDER be scaled to handle larger datasets, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in doing so?
- How does SPDER compare in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in large-scale applications?
- Could you provide more theoretical insights into the behavior of SPDER's activation function and its impact on the learning process?
- How does SPDER handle the spectral bias issue, and what specific mechanisms does it employ to mitigate this bias?
- Can SPDER be adapted to other types of neural representations, such as those used in 3D scene representations or other domains?
- How does SPDER perform in different types of image and audio data, and are there specific types of data where it might not perform as well?
