 **Summary:**
The paper explores asynchronous multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) by introducing value factorization for macro-actions, aiming to enhance scalability and performance in complex coordination tasks. It extends the Individual Global Max (IGM) principle to macro-actions, allowing agents to execute asynchronous actions, which is critical for real-world applications where actions can take varying durations. The authors propose a novel Asynchronous Value Factorization (AVF) algorithm that integrates macro-states into the mixer to maintain temporal consistency, which is crucial for accurate value estimation. Extensive experiments are conducted across various benchmark tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods over existing baselines. However, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity in defining the asynchronous setting and the novelty of its contributions, particularly in relation to existing works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces value factorization for macro-action based multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), which is a novel approach that extends the IGM principle to macro-actions, allowing for asynchronous execution.
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the methodology, theoretical contributions, and experimental results, making it accessible and understandable.
- Extensive experiments are conducted across various benchmark tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods in handling complex coordination tasks where primitive factorization methods fail.
- The paper is the first to formally define the IGM principle for macro-actions, which is a significant theoretical contribution to the field.
- The introduction of asynchronous value factorization (AVF) algorithms is a significant technical contribution that enables the use of existing factorization architectures to support macro-actions, enhancing scalability and performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining the asynchronous setting, particularly how agents execute asynchronous macro-actions and how this affects the joint action selection process.
- The paper does not provide a clear motivation for the need for asynchronous execution in MARL, especially considering that existing methods like QPLEX can handle synchronous execution effectively.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed methods, such as the computational cost and scalability issues, which could be critical in real-world applications.
- There is a lack of novelty in the proposed methods, as the use of macro-actions and asynchronous execution have been explored in previous works.
- The paper's theoretical contributions, while significant, are not sufficiently novel as they build on existing works like QPLEX and do not significantly advance the state of the art.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper does not sufficiently compare its methods with other state-of-the-art CTDE methods.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify how the asynchronous setting is defined in your paper, particularly how agents execute macro-actions asynchronously and how this affects the joint action selection process?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational cost and scalability issues, especially in large-scale real-world applications?
- Could you provide more details on how the macro-state is defined and how it is used in the AVF algorithms?
- In the context of the AVF algorithms, how does the use of joint macro-states affect the learning process, and what are the implications of using different mixing strategies?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art CTDE methods, especially in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can you provide more detailed experimental results, such as the number of training steps and the average reward over multiple runs, to better assess the effectiveness of your methods?
