 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DISK, a method designed to infer domain labels from training and validation datasets without relying on additional information. It employs a KL-divergence-based objective to maximize the difference between spurious correlations in the training and validation sets, thereby potentially improving generalization on test data. The method is evaluated across various datasets, including both synthetic and real-world image datasets, and shows promise in enhancing model generalization in the presence of subpopulation shifts. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of theoretical rigor, particularly in the estimation of mutual information and the handling of spurious correlations. Furthermore, the experimental setup and the clarity of the method's advantages over existing approaches have been questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the experiments are comprehensive, including both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The proposed method, DISK, is novel and well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the approach and its theoretical foundations.
- The method's ability to infer domain labels from training and validation datasets without additional information is a significant advantage, potentially reducing the need for extensive data collection and preprocessing.
- The paper provides a new metric for evaluating domain partitioning, which could be useful for assessing the effectiveness of other domain generalization methods.
- The method's potential to improve model generalization in the presence of subpopulation shifts is demonstrated through experiments, showing promising results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed method, particularly in the estimation of mutual information and the handling of spurious correlations.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse datasets and a broader range of baseline models to better evaluate the method's effectiveness across different scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity in the method's advantages over existing approaches, particularly in the context of domain generalization where the method's effectiveness is not consistently superior to existing methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the context of domain generalization and spurious correlations.
- The presentation of results, such as the use of color in figures and the clarity of the results in tables, could be improved for better comprehension.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the estimation of mutual information and how it affects the performance of the method?
- How does the method handle spurious correlations, particularly in scenarios where they may not be as straightforward as those depicted in the examples provided?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the specific model architectures and hyperparameters used, to better understand the method's performance across different datasets and scenarios?
- How does the method perform when the validation set is not available, and what are the implications of this for the method's effectiveness?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the method and how these might affect its practical application in real-world scenarios?
