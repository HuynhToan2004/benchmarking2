 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the performance of Large Language Models (LLMs) in graph reasoning tasks, specifically focusing on how different graph encoding methods and prompting techniques affect performance. It introduces a new benchmark, GraphQA, which evaluates LLMs on various graph-related tasks. The study explores the impact of graph structures, encoding methods, and prompting strategies on LLM performance, showing that the choice of graph encoding method and prompting strategy can significantly influence performance. The paper also discusses the limitations of LLMs in handling complex graph structures and proposes a set of best practices for encoding graphs as text for LLMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive analysis of the impact of graph encoding methods and prompting techniques on Large Language Model (LLM) performance in graph reasoning tasks.
- The introduction of a new benchmark, GraphQA, for evaluating LLMs on graph reasoning tasks is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the influence of different graph structures on LLM performance, which is crucial for understanding the capabilities and limitations of LLMs in graph reasoning.
- The study on the influence of model capacity on graph reasoning tasks is insightful and helps in understanding the scalability of LLMs for more complex graph reasoning tasks.
- The paper provides a set of best practices for encoding graphs as text for LLMs, which can be valuable for practitioners and researchers working in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on basic graph reasoning tasks, which might not fully demonstrate the capabilities of LLMs in more complex graph reasoning tasks.
- The novelty of the study is limited as it mainly evaluates existing graph encoding methods and prompting techniques, which have been previously explored.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of LLMs in handling complex graph structures and does not propose any new graph encoding methods or prompting strategies.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more complex graph structures and a broader range of graph generators to better represent real-world graphs.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of LLMs in handling implicit information in graphs, which could significantly impact their performance.
- There is a need for more comprehensive experiments to validate the claims made in the paper, especially concerning the impact of graph structures on LLM performance.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the impact of graph structures on LLM performance, particularly in terms of how different graph structures affect the model's ability to reason effectively?
- How do you ensure that the LLM's performance on graph reasoning tasks is not influenced by the complexity of the graph structure but rather by the task itself?
- Have you considered using more complex graph structures in your experiments to better represent real-world graphs? If so, what were the results?
- How do you address the issue of LLMs' inability to handle implicit information in graphs effectively? What strategies could be employed to improve their performance in this area?
- Could you discuss the potential of using graph-to-text models to encode graphs for LLMs, and how this might compare to the methods used in your study?
- How do you envision the integration of graph-to-text models with LLMs to improve graph reasoning capabilities, and what are the potential challenges in implementing such a system?
