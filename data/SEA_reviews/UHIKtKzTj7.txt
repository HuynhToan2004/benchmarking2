 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel physics-aware proxy model (PAPM) designed to address challenges in process systems, particularly those involving complex temporal-spatial dynamics. PAPM integrates partial prior mechanistic knowledge, including conservation and constitutive relations, and introduces a holistic temporal and spatial stepping method (TSSM) to enhance generalization capabilities. The model's performance is evaluated against state-of-the-art pure data-driven models and physics-aware models across five two-dimensional non-trivial benchmarks in nine generalization tasks. Despite the innovative approach and the significant performance improvements demonstrated, concerns about the novelty and the scope of the methodology are highlighted. The paper's contribution is seen as an improvement over existing methods, but its impact on broader process modeling applications remains questionable.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel physics-aware proxy model (PAPM) that effectively integrates partial prior mechanistic knowledge, including conservation and constitutive relations, which enhances the generalization capabilities of the model.
- The proposed TSSM method aligns with the distinct equation characteristics of different process systems, ensuring better applicability across various scenarios.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to evaluate the performance of the proposed model, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness against state-of-the-art pure data-driven models and physics-aware models.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The proposed method achieves significant performance improvements, requiring fewer FLOPs and significantly fewer parameters compared to the prior leading method, PPNN.
- The paper provides a systematic comparison of state-of-the-art pure data-driven models and physics-aware models, which aids in understanding the relative strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as it appears to be an extension of existing methods like PPNN, with similar model structures and learning procedures.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and challenges of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The scope of the methodology is somewhat limited, as it primarily focuses on 2D process systems, which may not generalize well to more complex or higher-dimensional systems.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- There is a need for more detailed explanations and justifications for the design choices made in the proposed method, particularly in the areas of boundary conditions and the use of different operators.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for any technological advancement.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the novelty of the proposed method compared to existing methods like PPNN?
- How does the proposed method perform in higher-dimensional systems, such as 3D or 4D process systems?
- Could the authors elaborate on the design choices made in the proposed method, particularly in the areas of boundary conditions and the use of different operators?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations and challenges of the proposed method, and how these might affect its practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method handle the incorporation of physical constraints, and what are the implications of these constraints on the model's performance?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the societal impacts of the proposed method, and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
