 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the capability of deep video understanding models to simulate the dynamics of the visual cortex, focusing on the differences between image and video processing models. It employs a regression model to predict fMRI responses from brain areas, comparing various deep learning models like ViT and MViT, and their ability to mimic the neural responses. The study includes both real and simulated environments, and the results suggest that video models perform better in predicting neural responses, particularly in early and mid-level visual areas. The paper also explores the impact of different model architectures, including transformer-based and convolutional models, and discusses the implications of these findings for the understanding of neural processing in the visual system.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely question regarding the ability of deep video understanding models to simulate the dynamics of the visual cortex, which is a critical aspect of understanding neural processing.
- The study includes a large-scale comparison of different deep learning models, providing a comprehensive analysis of their capabilities in predicting neural responses.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The inclusion of both real and simulated environments adds depth to the analysis and provides a more robust evaluation of the models.
- The paper includes a variety of models, including convolutional and transformer-based models, which enhances the comprehensiveness of the study.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental setup and the rationale behind certain design choices are not adequately explained, which could lead to confusion or misunderstanding of the results.
- The use of Pearson's correlation coefficient as the primary metric for evaluating model performance is questionable, as it does not provide a clear indication of the model's ability to predict neural responses accurately.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the study, which could help in better understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of video models over image models in predicting neural responses are not convincingly supported by the data, particularly in the late stages of visual processing.
- The paper's conclusions are not sufficiently novel or insightful, as many of the findings are expected given the existing literature on deep learning and neural processing.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the rationale behind the choice of Pearson's correlation coefficient as the primary metric for evaluating model performance?
- How do the authors justify the use of a single activation value for each voxel in the brain, particularly in light of the complex neural processing that occurs in different brain regions?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, especially the choice of models and the criteria for their selection?
- How do the authors address the potential confounding effects of the model architecture on the results, particularly in terms of the hierarchical nature of the models and the complexity of the neural processing they simulate?
- Could the authors elaborate on the implications of their findings for the broader field of neural processing and the development of more accurate models of neural dynamics?
