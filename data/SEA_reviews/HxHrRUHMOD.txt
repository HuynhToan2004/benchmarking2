 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the issue of inaccurate derivatives in hybrid neural fields, which are crucial for various downstream applications such as rendering, collision simulation, and solving PDEs. The authors propose a two-stage method: first, they use a local polynomial-fitting operator to obtain more accurate derivatives from pre-trained hybrid neural fields. Second, they propose a self-supervised fine-tuning approach to refine the neural field directly, ensuring the accuracy of the derivatives. The methodology aims to improve the quality of autodiff derivatives by reducing high-frequency noise, which is a significant issue in hybrid neural fields. The paper presents experimental results demonstrating improvements in derivative accuracy and reduced artifacts in downstream applications. However, concerns about the practical applicability of the method due to its reliance on specific hyperparameters and the computational cost of the fine-tuning approach are highlighted.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant issue in hybrid neural fields, specifically the inaccuracy of derivatives, which is crucial for various downstream applications.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with a clear motivation and presentation that makes it easy to follow.
- The method is shown to be effective in improving derivative accuracy and reducing artifacts in downstream applications, as demonstrated through experimental results.
- The paper is well-written and provides clear explanations, making the methodology and results accessible to readers.
- The problem of inaccurate derivatives in neural fields is a significant issue that has been effectively addressed, with potential for broad impact in various fields.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The method's reliance on specific hyperparameters (k and σ) for optimal performance could limit its applicability without a systematic approach to selecting these parameters.
- The paper's methodology is not clearly explained in certain sections, particularly in the description of the fine-tuning approach and the role of different operators in the method.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, and the paper could benefit from additional experiments to validate the method's effectiveness across different datasets and scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the proposed method, such as the computational cost and the practicality of hyperparameter selection.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the neural field?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to more complex datasets or scenarios, such as those involving high-frequency details or noisy data?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the different operators (L_con and L_grad) in the fine-tuning approach?
- Is there a systematic approach to selecting the hyperparameters (k and σ) for optimal performance, or is this a manual process?
- Could the authors provide additional experiments to validate the method's effectiveness across different datasets and scenarios, and to compare it with other baseline methods?
