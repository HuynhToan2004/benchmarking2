 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new dataset, LRV-Instruction, which includes both positive and negative instructions to address hallucination issues in large multi-modal models (LMMs). The dataset is generated using GPT4 and covers a wide range of vision-and-language tasks. The authors propose a new evaluation method, GAVIE, which leverages GPT4 to assess the accuracy and relevance of model outputs without requiring human-annotated ground truth. The paper also explores the impact of negative instructions on model performance and demonstrates that a balanced ratio of positive and negative instances can lead to more robust models. Despite the innovative approach and the creation of a valuable dataset, concerns were raised about the novelty and the clarity of the contributions, particularly in the methodology and the presentation of results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel dataset, LRV-Instruction, which includes both positive and negative instructions, a departure from existing datasets that primarily focus on positive samples. This dataset is valuable for training large multi-modal models (LMMs) and can be used to improve the performance of current models.
- The GAVIE evaluation method is innovative and does not require human-annotated ground truth, which is a significant advancement in evaluating model outputs.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive experimental setup that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed dataset and evaluation method.
- The authors have created a valuable dataset that covers a wide range of vision-and-language tasks, which can be used to train LMMs to address hallucination issues.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of current LMMs and demonstrates that a balanced ratio of positive and negative instances in the training data leads to a more robust model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty in its contributions, particularly in the methodology and the evaluation metrics used. The dataset, while valuable, is not entirely novel as similar datasets have been proposed in previous works.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of results and the methodology used, which could confuse readers and undermine the credibility of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the results, particularly the effectiveness of the proposed method in real-world scenarios or with different types of models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the results, particularly in terms of the impact of different instruction types and the effectiveness of the negative instructions in addressing hallucination issues.
- There is a need for more detailed information on the generation of the dataset, including the prompt used and the selection criteria for the negative instructions.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the definition of "hallucination" and how it is measured in your study?
- How do the negative instructions in your dataset differ from those in previous works, and what are the specific challenges introduced by these instructions?
- What are the specific prompts used to generate the dataset, and how were the negative instructions selected?
- How do the results of your study generalize to other models or real-world scenarios, and what are the implications of these findings for the broader community?
- Could you provide more details on the evaluation methodology, particularly how GAVIE was designed and how it compares to other evaluation methods?
- How do the authors ensure the quality and diversity of the negative instructions generated by GPT4, and what steps were taken to verify their effectiveness in addressing hallucination issues?
