 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LegoMT2, a federated learning framework designed for multilingual machine translation (MT) with 435 languages. It utilizes a novel grouping strategy to optimize the training process, employing a mixture of expert (MoE) model architecture to manage parameter interference. The methodology involves asynchronous parameter updates and non-blocking federated learning to enhance training efficiency. Extensive experiments demonstrate that LegoMT2 outperforms existing models like NLLB-200-1.3B and NLLB-200-54.5B on various benchmarks, including the Flores-101 dataset. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive analysis, and inadequate comparison with more recent and relevant models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to multilingual machine translation (MT) using federated learning, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method, LegoMT2, demonstrates a 16.2x speedup compared to traditional distributed training methods, which is a substantial improvement in training efficiency.
- The paper presents a large-scale dataset of 25 billion sentence pairs, which is a valuable resource for future research in MT.
- The results show that LegoMT2 outperforms existing models like NLLB-200-1.3B and NLLB-200-54.5B on various benchmarks, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and provides detailed experimental results and analysis, which enhance the understanding and reproducibility of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive analysis of the training process, particularly in terms of parameter interference and its impact on performance.
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with more recent and relevant models like Llama, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The grouping strategy used in the study is not adequately justified, and the paper lacks detailed information on the grouping process and its impact on performance.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other multilingual MT models, which could have helped in understanding the relative performance of the proposed model.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experiments, including evaluations on more diverse datasets and a broader range of languages to demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the training process, specifically addressing how parameter interference is managed and its impact on performance?
- How does the grouping strategy affect the performance of the model, and could you provide more details on the grouping process and its impact on the model's effectiveness?
- Why was the decision made to use a federated learning approach for multilingual MT, and how does it compare to other approaches like using a single model for all languages?
- Could you clarify the grouping strategy used in the study, particularly how it was determined and what factors influenced the grouping decisions?
- How does the proposed method compare to other multilingual MT models, and what are the key advantages of using LegoMT2 over these models?
- Can you provide more details on the dataset used in the experiments, particularly how it was collected and processed, and what steps were taken to ensure the quality and diversity of the data?
