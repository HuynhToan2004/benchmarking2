 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a retrieval-based video language model (R-VLM) designed to address the challenges of long video question answering (QA). R-VLM identifies and selects the most relevant video chunks based on questions, thereby reducing computational costs and enhancing system performance. This approach is contrasted with traditional methods that either use global spatial and temporal pooling or aggregate video tokens, which can lead to loss of visual details and noise interference. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on various datasets, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper's reliance on a single baseline and limited novelty in its approach has been noted as significant drawbacks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods.
- The motivation behind the research is clear, and the paper addresses a significant problem in the field of long video QA.
- The proposed method of retrieving relevant video chunks and using them as context for LLM inference is innovative and potentially effective in reducing computational costs and enhancing system performance.
- The paper includes thorough ablation studies that validate the effectiveness of the proposed retrieval mechanism and chunk-wise design.
- The method is simple and efficient, with a focus on reducing computational costs by selecting only the most relevant video chunks for inference.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other methods in the field, particularly those that use different strategies for video QA, such as those mentioned in the related work section.
- The novelty of the approach is limited, as similar methods have been proposed in the past, such as those that use retrieval-based methods for video summarization and video QA.
- The method's reliance on the quality of video encoders and LLMs raises concerns about its effectiveness, especially in scenarios where these components are not optimally tuned or when the video content is complex or varied.
- The paper's experimental setup is limited, with a focus on a single baseline and a small set of datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability or robustness of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method and potential negative societal impacts, which could be critical for understanding the broader implications of the research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to existing methods, particularly those mentioned in the related work section?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness when applied to different video encoders and LLMs?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the datasets used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the video content is complex or varied, and what are the potential impacts on the accuracy and reliability of the QA system?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of their method and the broader implications of their research, particularly in terms of ethical considerations and potential societal impacts?
