 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to conformal prediction for language models, focusing on generating prediction sets with performance guarantees. It proposes a method that samples responses from a predicted distribution over the output space, using a stopping rule to ensure the set covers at least one acceptable response. The method also includes a rejection rule to remove candidates that do not meet certain criteria, thereby improving the quality of the output set. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this method through experiments on various tasks, including radiology report generation and open-domain question answering, using different language model variants. The approach offers a rigorous coverage guarantee by constructing prediction sets that contain at least one acceptable response with high probability, while ensuring that the output sets are not only accurate but also precise.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to conformal prediction for language models, which bridges the gap between conformal prediction and language models by calibrating the sampling of output sets.
- The method is rigorous and offers a rigorous coverage guarantee by constructing prediction sets that contain at least one acceptable response with high probability.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a solid theoretical foundation.
- The experiments demonstrate that the method achieves many desired coverage levels within a limited number of total samples, showing that it is efficient and precise compared to baselines.
- The paper provides a rigorous coverage guarantee, which is crucial for applications where correctness is essential, such as medical diagnosis and financial forecasting.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not compare its method with existing baselines for conformal prediction in language models, which could provide insights into the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the method performs in scenarios where the language model's responses are not diverse or where the diversity of responses is a critical factor.
- The paper does not discuss the computational efficiency of the method, which is a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the method with respect to the size of the calibration dataset and the length of the generated text.

**Questions:**
