 **Summary:**
The paper introduces several methods to extend the capabilities of motion diffusion models, particularly focusing on generating longer sequences and coordinating motions between multiple subjects. The authors propose a method called DoubleTake, which refines the transitions between generated motifs by refining the noise in the transition region. Additionally, they introduce a communication model for multi-person motion generation and a diffusion blending method for fine-grained motion control. The paper also discusses the limitations of these methods, including the need for more data and the potential for overfitting to specific datasets. Despite these limitations, the paper presents a comprehensive evaluation and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods in generating high-quality motion sequences.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the proposed methods are well-motivated and technically sound.
- The evaluation is comprehensive, with strong results demonstrated across various tasks, including long-sequence generation, multi-person motion generation, and fine-grained motion control.
- The use of diffusion models for motion generation is innovative and the paper effectively demonstrates how these models can be extended to handle longer sequences and more complex motions.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation, including both quantitative and qualitative results, which support the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the methods extend the capabilities of the motion diffusion model beyond what is already possible with the model itself.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, particularly in terms of motion quality and diversity.
- There are several instances of unclear writing and potential errors in the text, such as missing references and unclear definitions of terms.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly the potential for overfitting and the need for more data to generalize the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its methods with state-of-the-art techniques, which could undermine the claims of novelty and effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the proposed methods extend the capabilities of the motion diffusion model beyond what is already possible with the model itself?
- How do the authors ensure that the refined transitions in the DoubleTake method are not simply an average of the previous and next intervals?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of parameters such as the handshake length and the number of layers in the communication model?
- How do the authors address the potential for overfitting in their models, particularly in the context of long-sequence generation?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of motion quality and diversity?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for unrealistic motions, especially in the context of long-sequence generation?
