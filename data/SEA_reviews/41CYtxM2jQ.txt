 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel linear diffusion model, LinDiff, for speech synthesis, leveraging an ordinary differential equation (ODE) to model the diffusion process. This model significantly reduces sampling steps, making it faster than traditional diffusion models, and introduces a Transformer-based architecture for audio denoising. The model's training involves adversarial loss to enhance sample quality and reduce computational complexity. Despite its innovative approach, the paper suffers from several issues including unclear explanations, potential misleading statements, and lack of comprehensive ablation studies. The method's effectiveness is questioned, with concerns about its applicability to multi-speaker datasets and the generalizability of its results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel linear diffusion model for speech synthesis, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The use of an ordinary differential equation (ODE) to model the diffusion process is innovative and potentially more efficient than traditional methods.
- The Transformer-based architecture for audio denoising is a novel approach that could be beneficial for future research in this area.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed model, comparing it against various benchmarks and demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The methodology is well-articulated, and the paper is generally well-written, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper contains several unclear or misleading statements that could confuse readers. For example, the claim that the proposed method reduces computational complexity is not substantiated.
- The explanation of the Transformer-based architecture is insufficient, and it is unclear how it contributes to the overall performance of the model.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study, which is crucial for understanding the impact of different components of the model on its performance.
- There are concerns about the generalizability of the results, particularly in multi-speaker datasets, where the model's performance is subpar compared to existing methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and real-time factor (RTF).
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of diversity in the generated speech, which is a significant drawback compared to other models like WaveNet.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the computational complexity of their model compared to existing methods?
- How does the Transformer-based architecture contribute to the performance of the model, and what specific benefits does it provide over traditional methods?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, particularly the use of adversarial loss and its impact on the model's performance?
- How does the model handle multi-speaker datasets, and what are the implications of its subpar performance in this context?
- Could the authors conduct a more comprehensive ablation study to better understand the impact of different components of their model on its performance?
- Is there a possibility to extend the model to handle multi-speaker datasets more effectively?
