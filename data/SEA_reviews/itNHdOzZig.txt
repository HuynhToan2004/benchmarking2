 **Summary:**
The paper explores the role of encoding methods in Neural Architecture Search (NAS) by introducing a novel hybrid encoding method, FLAN, which integrates graph neural networks (GNNs) with operation embeddings. The authors evaluate this method across various NAS benchmarks, demonstrating improved performance and sample efficiency. The study also investigates the transferability of predictors across different NAS search spaces, showcasing significant improvements in predictor training efficiency. Despite the innovative approach and comprehensive evaluation, concerns are raised regarding the novelty of the method and the clarity of the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise sections that effectively communicate the research.
- It provides a comprehensive evaluation of different encoding methods for NAS, including structural, score-based, and learned encodings, which is valuable for understanding the impact of different encodings on NAS performance.
- The introduction of a unified encoding that allows for the transfer of predictors to new search spaces with minimal new accuracy samples is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors have made the encodings for 1.5 million neural network architectures open-source, which is a valuable resource for the NAS community.
- The paper is original in its approach, combining GNNs with operation embeddings to enhance the performance of NAS encoders, and it is the first to evaluate the impact of NAS encoders across multiple NAS search spaces.
- The study includes a large number of experiments, which helps to validate the claims and provides a robust foundation for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how the unified encoding is constructed, which could hinder the reproducibility and understanding of the results.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it seems to be a combination of existing techniques such as DGF and GAT, without significant innovation.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art NAS methods, which could undermine the perceived effectiveness of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity in the experimental setup, particularly in the description of the search space and the training and evaluation of the predictor, which could lead to confusion about the results.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the unified encoding is constructed and how it affects the performance of the predictor?
- How does the proposed method compare with state-of-the-art NAS methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the search space and the training and evaluation of the predictor, to enhance the reproducibility and understanding of the results?
- How does the proposed method handle the transfer of predictors across different NAS search spaces, and what are the implications of this transfer in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
- What are the computational costs associated with the proposed method, and how do these compare with existing NAS methods?
