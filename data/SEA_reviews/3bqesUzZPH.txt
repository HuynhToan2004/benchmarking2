 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel backdoor attack method, FTA, specifically designed for federated learning environments. This method employs a generative model to produce custom triggers for each client, ensuring stealthiness and adaptability to different client models. The attack is evaluated against several existing defenses, demonstrating high attack success rates and stealthiness. The method's effectiveness is shown to be robust against various defenses, including those based on norm clipping and cluster-based filtering. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty and potential overclaiming of stealthiness, as the method still relies on certain assumptions about the defender's knowledge and capabilities.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue of backdoor attacks in federated learning, proposing a novel method that effectively evades detection by existing defenses.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, with a clear presentation of the challenges in backdoor attacks in federated learning and the design of a novel trigger generator to address these challenges.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness and stealthiness of the proposed method across various datasets and defense mechanisms.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a thorough evaluation against multiple baselines, showcasing the superior performance of the proposed method.
- The use of a generative model to learn flexible triggers for each sample in each round is a novel approach that enhances the adaptability and stealthiness of the attack.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the explanation of how the method is stealthy, particularly in relation to the defender's knowledge and capabilities.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it builds upon existing works, particularly those that use similar techniques for learning trigger functions.
- The experimental results, while showing some effectiveness, do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods, especially in terms of stealthiness and effectiveness under different conditions.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its dependency on certain assumptions about the defender's knowledge and the potential impact of different training datasets.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in the clarity of the threat model and the explanation of the experimental setup.

**Questions:**
- How does the method ensure stealthiness when the defender has access to the training data and can detect anomalies based on the behavior of the attacker's updates?
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their method and how it differs from existing works, particularly those that also use similar trigger generation techniques?
- How does the proposed method perform under different conditions, such as varying poison fractions and different training datasets?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the stealthiness of the attack, possibly using metrics like SSIM and LPIPS to quantify the perceptibility of the triggers?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the attacker does not have full access to the local training processes and datasets, which is a common assumption in many federated learning settings?
