 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to sampling from causal effect estimands using generative models trained on observational data. This method, referred to as ID-DAG, is designed to handle complex causal graphs with latent confounders, which are typically challenging for existing methods. The approach is based on the identification algorithm from Pearl, adapted to a sampling algorithm that utilizes diffusion models to approximate conditional distributions. The paper presents two datasets, one synthetic and one real, to demonstrate the effectiveness of the method. However, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity, particularly in the presentation of the algorithm and its assumptions, and for the lack of comprehensive evaluation against existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in causal inference, specifically the identification and sampling from interventional distributions, which is a novel application of diffusion models.
- The proposed ID-DAG algorithm is sound and complete, providing a theoretical framework for sampling from interventional distributions.
- The methodology is general and applicable to various causal graphs, including those with latent confounders, which is a significant advancement over previous methods.
- The paper is well-written and provides clear explanations of the methodology and its application, making it accessible to readers.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can be applied to real-world datasets, such as the COVID-19 dataset.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the assumptions made, particularly regarding the complexity of the causal graph and the availability of sufficient data.
- The presentation of the algorithm is confusing, with some steps not clearly explained or justified, and the overall structure of the paper could be improved for better clarity.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, which makes it difficult to assess the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical challenges of applying the method to high-dimensional data, such as images, and does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method.
- The evaluation of the method is limited, with insufficient detail on the experimental setup and results, particularly in terms of statistical significance and the choice of baselines.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the assumptions made about the complexity of the causal graph and the availability of sufficient data for effective sampling?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness in handling high-dimensional data?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the choice of datasets, the training and testing data splits, and the statistical significance of the results?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of latent confounding, and what are the implications of this approach for causal inference in general?
- Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the ID-DAG algorithm, particularly the recursive steps and the handling of latent variables?
- Is there a possibility to include more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, such as those mentioned in related works, to better establish the novelty and effectiveness of your approach?
