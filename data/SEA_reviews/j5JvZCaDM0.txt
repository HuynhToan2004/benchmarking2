 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FISOR, a novel safe offline reinforcement learning (RL) algorithm that addresses the challenge of ensuring safety constraints in offline settings. It proposes a feasibility-dependent objective and a diffusion model to maximize rewards within feasible regions and minimize safety risks in infeasible regions. The algorithm is evaluated on multiple benchmarks and reportedly outperforms existing methods. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the approach, including the use of Hamilton-Jacobi reachability and the reformulation of safety constraints. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and the potential for over-conservative policy outcomes due to the use of hard constraints.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed FISOR algorithm is innovative, particularly its use of HJ reachability to determine the feasible region, which is a novel approach in offline RL.
- The method is evaluated on a large number of tasks and reportedly outperforms existing methods, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including a detailed derivation of the optimal policy and the use of a diffusion model for policy learning.
- The experimental results are robust and support the claims made by the authors, showing the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it builds on existing methods such as RCRL and IQL, with the primary contribution being the use of HJ reachability for offline RL.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the conservative nature of the policies it produces.
- There is a significant computational overhead due to the need to train three networks for each task, which could limit the scalability of the method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of distributional shift in offline RL, which is crucial for ensuring the robustness of the learned policies.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the related work, particularly in the context of safe RL and offline RL, to better position its contributions within the existing literature.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the proposed method addresses the issue of distributional shift in offline RL?
- How does the method ensure that the learned policy is robust to out-of-distribution (OOD) actions, especially in safety-critical applications?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in comparison to other existing methods?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle more complex scenarios, such as continuous action spaces or more complex safety constraints?
- How does the method handle the trade-off between safety and reward, especially in scenarios where the two objectives conflict?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for using different types of diffusion models, such as those that use classifiers, to improve the performance of the method?
