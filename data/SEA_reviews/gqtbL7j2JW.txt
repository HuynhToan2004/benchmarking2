 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel problem called Generative Model Identification (GMI), aimed at identifying the most suitable generative model for a given user requirement using a single example image. The proposed method employs a weighted Reduced Kernel Mean Embedding (RKME) framework, a pre-trained vision-language model, and an image interrogator to address dimensionality challenges and cross-modality issues. The methodology is evaluated on a benchmark platform consisting of 16 tasks using stable diffusion models, demonstrating an average top-4 identification accuracy of over 80%. The paper also discusses the challenges of capturing the relationship between images and prompts, which is crucial for accurately identifying generative models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem of identifying the most suitable generative model for a given user requirement using a single example image, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method is well-motivated and clearly explained, with a comprehensive evaluation that demonstrates its effectiveness and efficiency.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes detailed visualizations that aid in understanding the methodology.
- The use of a weighted Reduced Kernel Mean Embedding (RKME) framework, a pre-trained vision-language model, and an image interrogator is innovative and addresses the challenges of dimensionality and cross-modality effectively.
- The methodology is evaluated on a benchmark platform consisting of 16 tasks using stable diffusion models, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the challenges of capturing the relationship between images and prompts, which is crucial for accurately identifying generative models.
- The evaluation is limited to 16 tasks, which might not adequately represent the diversity and complexity of real-world scenarios.
- There is a need for more comprehensive experiments, including different model categories and a larger number of tasks, to validate the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with other existing methods, such as contrastive learning, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed approach.
- The method's reliance on a single example image for identification might limit its applicability in scenarios where multiple images or more complex prompts are required.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where multiple images or more complex prompts are required for accurate identification?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the image interrogator is trained and how it handles different types of prompts?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other existing methods, such as contrastive learning or other model identification techniques?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their approach and suggest potential avenues for future research to address these limitations?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how can these be mitigated?
