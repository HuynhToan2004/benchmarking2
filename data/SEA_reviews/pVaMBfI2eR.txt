 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Federated Dual Prompt Tuning (Fed-DPT) for domain-aware federated learning, leveraging a pre-trained vision-language model and applying prompt tuning for both visual and textual inputs. The method is designed to adapt to decentralized data and address the challenges of domain shift and communication efficiency. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of Fed-DPT, showing significant improvements over existing methods on various benchmarks. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the method, the clarity of the presentation, and the practicality of the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method called Federated Dual Prompt Tuning (Fed-DPT) that leverages prompt tuning for both visual and textual inputs, addressing the challenges of domain shift and communication efficiency in federated learning.
- The proposed Fed-DPT is well-motivated, well-designed, and achieves impressive results, as demonstrated by extensive experiments that show significant improvements over existing methods on various benchmarks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive ablation study that dissects the performance gains of the proposed method.
- The methodology is well-explained, with a clear description of the problem setting, local training, and parameter aggregation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as it seems to be a combination of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The experimental setup is not practical as it assumes a centralized parameter server and clients, which is not typical in federated learning scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the privacy implications of sharing textual prompts across clients.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method to large-scale datasets or the potential for overfitting due to the use of prompt tokens.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect the generalizability and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the prompt tokens are initialized and whether they are randomly initialized or initialized with the same values across all clients?
- What are the specific advantages of using the CLIP model for your method, and how does it compare to other vision-language models?
- How does the proposed method ensure privacy when sharing textual prompts across clients?
- Can you provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method to large-scale datasets and the potential for overfitting due to the use of prompt tokens?
- What are the specific challenges addressed by the proposed method that are unique to the federated learning setting?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where clients have varying amounts of data, and what are the implications for performance and privacy?
