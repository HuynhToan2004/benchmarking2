 **Summary:**
The paper explores the robustness of Large Language Models (LLMs) against prompt injection attacks, specifically focusing on the vulnerability of LLMs to adversarial instructions embedded in web search results. It introduces a benchmark to evaluate the robustness of instruction-following LLMs and examines the performance of leading models across four datasets. The study reveals significant vulnerabilities, particularly in models that are overly tuned to follow embedded instructions, and suggests that model size does not directly correlate with robustness. The paper proposes a new evaluation framework and contributes to the understanding of LLM robustness against prompt injection attacks, providing insights into potential defense mechanisms and the need for improved prompt comprehension.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new benchmark for evaluating the robustness of instruction-following Large Language Models (LLMs) against prompt injection attacks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments are extensive and include a variety of models and datasets, providing a comprehensive analysis of the robustness of LLMs.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear explanations of the experimental setup and results.
- The findings are original and provide new insights into the vulnerabilities of LLMs to prompt injection attacks, which is an important area of research given the increasing use of LLMs in various applications.
- The paper includes a human evaluation which adds depth to the analysis and provides valuable insights into the performance of LLMs in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the defense mechanisms against prompt injection attacks, which is a significant limitation given the focus on robustness.
- The evaluation metrics used, such as Performance Drop Rate (PDR) and Instruction Discrimination Rate (IDR), are not sufficiently explained, and their relevance and effectiveness in evaluating LLM robustness are not convincingly demonstrated.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the hypothesis that LLMs should be able to understand the structure of the prompt and discern its various components, which is a critical aspect of the study.
- The paper's evaluation setup and methodology are not sufficiently robust, with potential biases introduced by the use of GPT-4 for generating adversarial questions and the limited diversity in the generated questions.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the study, such as the potential for overfitting to specific datasets and the generalizability of the findings to other types of LLMs or attack scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the implications of the findings and potential directions for future research, particularly in terms of developing more robust LLMs.

**Questions:**
- How does the paper address the issue of potential overfitting to specific datasets, and what steps were taken to ensure the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors provide more details on the defense mechanisms used against prompt injection attacks, and how effective were these mechanisms in mitigating the vulnerabilities identified?
- How were the adversarial questions generated using GPT-4, and what steps were taken to ensure the diversity and relevance of these questions to the evaluation task?
- What are the potential reasons for the observed differences in robustness between models of similar sizes, and how do these findings relate to the model's instruction-following capabilities?
- How do the findings of this study compare to other evaluations of LLM robustness, particularly in terms of the evaluation metrics and methodologies used?
- What are the potential implications of these findings for the development of more robust LLMs, and what directions should future research take to address the identified vulnerabilities?

**Paper Decision:**
- Decision: Reject

However, the reviewers have raised concerns about the clarity and depth of the evaluation metrics and the experimental setup. Specifically, the use of GPT-4 for generating adversarial questions and the potential biases introduced by this methodology were not adequately addressed. Additionally, the paper lacks a detailed discussion on defense mechanisms and does not sufficiently explore the implications of its findings for the development of more robust LLMs. These shortcomings, coupled with the need for a more comprehensive evaluation of LLM robustness, lead to the decision to reject the paper.
