 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the challenge of optimizing tree placement in urban areas to mitigate heat stress, using a U-Net model to predict mean radiant temperature (Tmrt) and an iterated local search algorithm to optimize tree placement. The approach employs a genetic algorithm and hill climbing to iteratively refine tree positions, aiming to minimize Tmrt over time. The model is trained on a dataset from a single city and evaluated against various baselines, showing promising results in reducing Tmrt across different time scales. However, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the model to other cities and the robustness of the optimization method, particularly in scenarios where the Tmrt model may not accurately predict temperature changes.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in urban planning, focusing on optimizing tree placement to reduce mean radiant temperature (Tmrt) and improve urban thermal comfort.
- The use of a U-Net model to predict Tmrt from spatial and temporal inputs is innovative and allows for fast and efficient optimization of tree placement.
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the problem, methodology, and results, with a comprehensive evaluation of the optimization method against various baselines.
- The iterative local search algorithm, including genetic algorithms and hill climbing, effectively optimizes tree placements, leading to substantial reductions in Tmrt.
- The approach is scalable and applicable to large-scale urban planning, with potential for significant impact on urban design and planning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the Tmrt model and how it might affect the accuracy of temperature predictions, which could impact the effectiveness of the tree placement optimization.
- The generalizability of the model to other cities is questionable, as the training data is from a single city, raising concerns about the model's applicability in diverse urban environments.
- The paper does not adequately address the real-world challenges of tree placement, such as urban planning constraints and ecological feasibility, which could limit the practical application of the optimized tree placements.
- There is a lack of comparison with other optimization methods or different aggregation functions, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, particularly in relation to the computational resources required for optimization.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the Tmrt model handles different tree species or urban environments, and how this might affect the accuracy of temperature predictions?
- How do you ensure that the optimized tree placements are ecologically feasible and comply with urban planning regulations?
- Could you discuss the potential of using other aggregation functions, such as the top 5% of extreme heat events, to evaluate the robustness of your method?
- How does the optimization method perform when applied to different urban environments, and what are the implications for the scalability and applicability of your approach?
- Can you provide more details on the computational resources required for the optimization process and discuss potential strategies for reducing computational costs while maintaining accuracy?
