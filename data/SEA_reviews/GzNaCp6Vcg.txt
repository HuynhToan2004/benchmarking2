 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PINNACLE, a novel algorithm for Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) that optimizes the selection of training points, including collocation and experimental points. It utilizes a criterion based on the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) eigenvalues to determine the optimal point selection, aiming to improve generalization error and convergence speed. The methodology is supported by theoretical insights and empirical results, demonstrating its effectiveness in various PDE problems such as forward, inverse, and transfer learning. Despite its theoretical contributions, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty, insufficient experimental validation, and potential issues with clarity and presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to point selection in PINNs, which is a significant problem in the field.
- It introduces a novel criterion based on the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) eigenvalues, which is both innovative and theoretically sound.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The proposed method shows promise in improving the training of PINNs, which could lead to significant advancements in the field.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed theoretical analysis, which is supported by empirical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and generalization performance.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some figures (e.g., Figure 4) being difficult to interpret or not sufficiently detailed.
- The paper's novelty is questioned, as similar methods have been previously proposed, such as using the NTK to determine the importance of different points.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive experimental validation, particularly in more complex and realistic scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of their method with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and generalization performance?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the PDEs are nonlinear or more complex?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the Nystrom approximation in their method, particularly how it affects the accuracy of the results?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the PDEs are time-dependent, and what are the implications for the training process?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to support the claims made in the paper, particularly in the experimental results section?
