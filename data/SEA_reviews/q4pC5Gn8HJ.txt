 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SimXIR, a novel non-contrastive self-supervised learning method that utilizes neighbor-averaging dynamics to optimize the online network by alternatively taking the online network of the last round as the target network. This approach is claimed to exhibit two intriguing properties: contraction of neighbor-connected groups and alienation between disjoint groups, which prevent collapsed solutions and enhance the performance of SSL models. Theoretical proofs and empirical results demonstrate that SimXIR can improve the performance of SSL models on downstream classification tasks. However, the paper's theoretical analysis is limited to linear representation functions, and the experimental results are not comprehensive enough to fully validate the claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The introduction of the neighbor-averaging dynamics and the concept of neighbor-connected groups is innovative and provides a novel perspective on non-contrastive learning.
- The theoretical analysis, particularly the proof of the contraction and alienation properties, is rigorous and provides a strong foundation for the proposed method.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in various settings.
- The paper provides a novel perspective on non-contrastive learning by focusing on the implicit bias of the method, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough literature review, missing out on related works that could provide a more robust context for the research.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the bounded representation function and the linear representation function, are not sufficiently justified or explained.
- The experimental results are limited to specific datasets and do not include comparisons with a broader range of SSL methods or other datasets like ImageNet.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive analysis of the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in various scenarios.
- The paper's claims about the prevention of collapsed solutions and the effectiveness of the method are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence, particularly in scenarios where the method does not perform as well as expected.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the bounded representation function and the linear representation function, are justified in practical scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data is not naturally grouped, and what are the implications of this for the effectiveness of the method?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons with a broader range of SSL methods and other datasets like ImageNet?
- How does the proposed method compare to other non-contrastive methods that do not use asymmetric tricks, such as SimCLR?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup and the results, particularly in scenarios where the method does not perform as well as expected?
