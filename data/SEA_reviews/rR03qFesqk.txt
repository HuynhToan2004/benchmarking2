 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FIRE (Functional Interpolation for Relative Positional Encoding), a novel method aimed at enhancing the generalization of Transformer models to longer input sequences. FIRE utilizes a learnable MLP to map input positions to biases, employing progressive interpolation to ensure bounded inputs across all sequence lengths. This approach is validated through extensive experiments across various datasets, including C4, SCROLLS, and NarrativeQA, demonstrating improvements over existing methods like RoPE and T5's RPE. Theoretical proofs are provided to support the representational equivalence of FIRE with other popular relative position encoding methods. Despite its strengths, concerns are raised about the clarity of the presentation, the need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, and the potential impact of FIRE on long-range dependencies in transformers.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces FIRE, a novel positional encoding method that significantly improves the generalization of Transformers to longer contexts, which is a significant challenge in the field.
- FIRE's design is simple yet effective, and the experimental results are impressive, showing a substantial improvement over existing methods like RoPE and T5's RPE.
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, including a proof that FIRE can represent popular position encodings, and empirical evidence that FIRE learns both local and anti-local position biases.
- FIRE's use of a learnable function to map input positions to biases and a progressive interpolation technique ensures bounded inputs for the position encoding function, enhancing the model's ability to generalize to longer contexts.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are extensive, covering various datasets and benchmarks, which demonstrates the robustness and applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other relative position encoding methods, particularly those that also use learnable MLPs, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of FIRE's effectiveness.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with some sections being difficult to understand, such as the explanation of the progressive interpolation technique and the choice of normalizer.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of FIRE on long-range dependencies in transformers, which is a critical aspect of the model's performance on long sequences.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of FIRE, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and training time of FIRE compared to other methods, especially considering its potential impact on the practical deployment of the model.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of FIRE with other relative position encoding methods, particularly those that also use learnable MLPs?
- How does FIRE handle the trade-off between learning long-range dependencies and the risk of overfitting to the training data, especially in the context of long sequences?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the design choices of FIRE, particularly the use of progressive interpolation and the choice of normalizer?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of FIRE and how these might impact its practical deployment?
- How does FIRE perform in terms of computational complexity and training time compared to other methods, and what are the implications of these factors for the practical deployment of the model?
