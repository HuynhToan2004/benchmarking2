 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the robustness of Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) algorithms to misspecifications in behavioral models, specifically focusing on three common models: optimality, Boltzmann-rationality, and causal entropy maximization. The authors analyze the sensitivity of IRL to misspecifications in reward functions and behavioral models, providing theoretical insights into the conditions under which IRL can recover the true reward function despite misspecifications. They introduce a novel metric, STARC, to measure the distance between reward functions, and explore the implications of misspecifications on IRL performance. The paper also discusses the limitations of the current approach and suggests directions for future research.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the robustness of Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) to misspecifications in reward functions and behavioral models, which is a significant and timely topic.
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions and theoretical results that are presented in a clear and concise manner, making the content accessible and understandable.
- The authors introduce a novel metric, STARC, which is well-motivated and useful for measuring the distance between reward functions, contributing to the field by offering a new tool for evaluating IRL robustness.
- The paper addresses a critical issue in IRL by analyzing the sensitivity of IRL to misspecifications, which is a crucial aspect of IRL that has not been thoroughly explored before.
- The theoretical results are sound, and the paper provides a comprehensive framework for understanding the robustness of IRL to misspecifications, which is valuable for both theoretical and practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical validation of the theoretical results, which could have strengthened the paper by demonstrating the practical implications of the theoretical findings.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the availability of an oracle that can generate data from the true reward function, may not hold in real-world applications, which could limit the applicability of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the current approach, which could include the assumptions made and the potential for future research to address these limitations.
- The paper's focus on specific types of misspecifications, such as perturbations of the observed policy and misspecification of parameters, may not fully capture the complexity of real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the assumptions made and the implications of these assumptions on the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the implications of the assumptions made in the paper, particularly how these assumptions affect the applicability of the results in real-world scenarios?
- How does the paper's approach compare to other metrics for measuring the distance between reward functions, such as those proposed in other papers?
- Can the authors provide more examples or case studies to illustrate the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of how misspecifications affect the performance of IRL algorithms?
- How does the paper's analysis relate to other works on robustness in IRL, such as those by Skalse and Abate (2023)?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential directions for future research, particularly in terms of extending the current analysis to more complex scenarios or developing more robust IRL algorithms?
