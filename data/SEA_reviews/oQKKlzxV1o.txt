 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the mechanism design problem in a multi-agent information acquisition setting where a principal hires multiple agents to gather information about a state of nature. The agents report their information to the principal, who makes a decision based on this information and pays the agents according to a scoring rule. The authors propose a polynomial-time algorithm to find an optimal incentive compatible mechanism and design a no-regret algorithm for the online problem. The paper also discusses the computation of optimal mechanisms and their implications in different settings. Despite its technical contributions, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and lack of significant real-world examples.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the problem is clearly motivated and well-defined.
- The algorithmic design is sound, and the paper provides a polynomial-time algorithm to find an optimal incentive compatible mechanism, which is a significant contribution.
- The analysis of the online learning problem is thorough, and the paper is clear in its presentation, which aids in understanding the complex concepts.
- The paper introduces a novel model where the principal can privately recommend actions to agents, which is a departure from traditional models where agents choose their actions independently.
- The paper provides a linear programming relaxation for computing optimal mechanisms, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as it primarily combines existing techniques without introducing significant new insights or methodologies.
- The paper lacks real-world examples or applications, which could enhance its relevance and impact.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the assumption that the joint probability distribution is known, are not realistic in many real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of its assumptions and the potential impact of these limitations on the applicability of the results.
- There are some minor issues with clarity and notation, such as the use of the same symbol for different concepts and the lack of clear definitions for certain terms.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the novelty of their work compared to existing literature?
- Is there a possibility to extend the results of this paper to scenarios where the joint probability distribution is unknown?
- How does the paper handle the assumption that the principal can privately recommend actions to agents, and what are the implications of this assumption in practical scenarios?
- Can the authors clarify the definitions and notations used in the paper, particularly in sections where the notation is confusing or inconsistent?
- How do the authors justify the assumptions made in their model, and what are the implications of these assumptions on the practical applicability of their results?
