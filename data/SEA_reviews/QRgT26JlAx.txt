 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the issue of temporal label noise in time series classification, where labels are assumed to change over time. It proposes a novel method called Temporal Noise Robust (TENOR) to estimate the temporal noise function directly from data, aiming to improve classification accuracy. The paper provides theoretical justifications and experimental validations, comparing TENOR with other methods like VolMinNet and Anchor. Despite the novel approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental evaluation and lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods. The paper also faces criticism regarding the clarity of some theoretical proofs and the practical implications of the assumptions made.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to the problem of temporal label noise.
- The proposed method is theoretically justified and experimentally validated, demonstrating improvements over existing methods in specific scenarios.
- The paper addresses a significant and under-explored problem in the field of learning from noisy labels, focusing on the temporal nature of label noise which is often overlooked.
- The paper is the first to study temporal label noise in the context of time series classification, a critical area for many applications in healthcare, finance, and other domains.
- The methodology is innovative, combining existing techniques with new approaches to tackle the problem of temporal label noise effectively.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly those that address temporal label noise, which could undermine the claimed novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, with only a few datasets used, which may not sufficiently validate the method's performance across different scenarios and data types.
- The paper assumes that the temporal label noise function is known, which might not be practical in many real-world scenarios.
- The theoretical proofs and assumptions, such as the identifiability of the noise function and the use of the backward sequence loss, are not convincingly justified or practical.
- The paper's claims of novelty and significance are overstated, as similar problems have been addressed in other works, which are not adequately acknowledged or compared.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, especially in the introduction and related work sections.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the method performs when the temporal label noise function is not known, as this is a more practical scenario?
- How does the method handle different types of temporal label noise, such as those that are periodic or non-periodic?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical proofs, particularly regarding the identifiability of the noise function and the use of the backward sequence loss?
- How does the method compare to other existing methods, particularly those that address similar problems in the field of learning from noisy labels?
- Can the authors provide more comprehensive experimental results, including comparisons with a broader range of datasets and scenarios?
- How does the method perform when the noise function is not known, and what are the implications of this on the practical applicability of the method?
