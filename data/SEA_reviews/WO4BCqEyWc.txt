 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach called Conditional Augmentation-aware Self-supervised Learning (CASSLE), which modifies the projector network in self-supervised learning architectures to retain information about data augmentations. This method allows the feature extractor to learn representations that are sensitive to these augmentations, which is crucial for maintaining information about characteristics affected by augmentations. The method is tested across various downstream tasks, showing improved performance over existing methods. The paper is well-structured, with clear experimental results and a detailed analysis of the method's effectiveness. However, it is noted that the method is limited to specific augmentations and may not generalize well to other types of data augmentations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed method.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and effective, with good experimental results that demonstrate its utility in various downstream tasks.
- The analysis of the sensitivity of the feature extractor to augmentations is insightful and provides a clear understanding of the method's operation.
- The paper includes detailed ablation studies and analyses, which help in understanding the method's components and their impact on performance.
- The method is straightforward and can be applied to different self-supervised learning (SSL) methods, showing improved performance on various datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method is limited to specific types of data augmentations and may not generalize well to other types of augmentations, which could limit its practical application.
- The method requires the use of a hypernetwork, which could introduce additional computational overhead.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other methods that also utilize augmentation information, such as AugSelf and AugLearn, which could provide a better understanding of the method's advantages.
- The method's sensitivity to augmentations might not be the only factor contributing to its performance, as other factors such as the strength of augmentations could also play a role.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the method's performance under different augmentation conditions and a comparison with other methods that use similar approaches.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when applied to other types of data augmentations, such as random erasing or random cutting?
- Can the method be adapted to work with different types of augmentations, such as those used in other SSL methods like MoCo and SimCLR?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over other methods that also utilize augmentation information, such as AugSelf and AugLearn?
- How does the method's sensitivity to augmentations affect its performance on different datasets, and what are the implications of this sensitivity for the method's practical application?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational overhead introduced by the use of the hypernetwork, and how this compares to the computational efficiency of other SSL methods?
