 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DIFAIR, a novel method for open-set recognition aimed at enhancing the interpretability of neural network classifiers. DIFAIR employs a loss function that optimizes the model to differentiate between known and unknown classes by ensuring class separability and interpretability in the feature space. The approach uses class anchors in the feature space to assign specific dimensions to classes, which should ideally represent distinct features for each class. However, the paper suffers from significant issues in clarity, presentation, and experimental validation. The experimental results are not convincing, and the method's interpretability claims are not substantiated with clear evidence. Furthermore, the paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods and fails to provide sufficient details on the implementation and training of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to open-set recognition by associating each dimension of the representation with a class, enhancing the interpretability of the model's predictions.
- The proposed method is well-motivated and the idea of having a feature space representation that aligns with class labels is innovative.
- The paper is generally well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed description of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is simple and easy to implement, which could make it accessible for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the methodology and the role of certain components, such as the class anchor clustering and the use of the Euclidean distance thresholded by the hypersphere radius.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with the proposed method not outperforming existing methods like CAC and DCHS.
- There is a lack of detailed implementation and training information, making it difficult to replicate the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for shared features across classes and the impact of the method on closed-set accuracy.
- The paper suffers from poor presentation, with figures and diagrams not clearly illustrating the proposed method or its results.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the OSR score and the use of different datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and implementation details of the class anchor clustering and the use of the Euclidean distance thresholded by the hypersphere radius?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of shared features across classes, and what are the implications for the model's interpretability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and implementation of the DIFAIR method, including the specifics of the loss function and the experimental setup?
- How does the proposed method compare to other open-set recognition methods, particularly in terms of the OSR score and the use of different datasets?
- Can the authors explain the discrepancies in the results shown in Figure 3 and provide more evidence to support the claims of interpretability?
