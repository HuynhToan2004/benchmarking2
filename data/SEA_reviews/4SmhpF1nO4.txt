 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel oversampling technique, Tabular Deep-SMOTE (TD-SMOTE), which integrates metric learning loss, importance oversampling, and baseline classifier filtering to address class imbalance in tabular datasets. TD-SMOTE utilizes an autoencoder to learn a latent space, prioritizing minority samples, and employs a filtering mechanism to discard synthetic samples that do not align with the original data. The methodology is evaluated across 36 datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving classification performance compared to existing methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty and the lack of comprehensive experimental validation, particularly in terms of scalability and robustness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the methodology and experimental results.
- The proposed method, Tabular Deep-SMOTE (TD-SMOTE), is innovative, integrating metric learning loss, importance oversampling, and baseline classifier filtering, which are not commonly used together.
- TD-SMOTE shows promising results, particularly in terms of its ability to improve classification performance on 36 datasets.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including a detailed comparison with existing methods and an ablation study.
- The use of an autoencoder to learn a latent space for oversampling is novel and interesting, as it allows for the prioritization of minority samples and the discarding of synthetic samples that do not align with the original data.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning scalability and the computational complexity of the KNN algorithm used in the importance sampling stage.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, with only a few datasets used for evaluation, and the results are not consistently superior to existing methods across all datasets.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned, as the use of metric learning loss, importance oversampling, and baseline classifier filtering are not entirely novel.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the importance-oversampling component, which could limit its applicability to larger datasets.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive experimental setup, including a broader range of datasets and a comparison with more recent and advanced methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the importance-oversampling component and the computational complexity of the KNN algorithm used in this stage?
- How does the proposed method perform on datasets with a high degree of class imbalance, and what are the potential implications for model performance and generalization?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the Normalized Softmax loss over other metric learning losses, and how this choice impacts the overall effectiveness of the method?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to multi-class settings, and if so, what are the potential challenges and solutions?
- Could the authors clarify the use of "y" in Algorithm 1 and explain its role in the training process?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of synthetic samples crossing into the majority domain, and what measures are in place to prevent this?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger datasets?
