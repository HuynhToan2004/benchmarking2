 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel hierarchical concept discovery model that leverages recent advances in image-text models to enhance the interpretability of deep learning algorithms. The model, named Concept Pyramid Models (CPMs), utilizes a coarse-to-fine approach to discover and exploit granular concept information residing in patch-specific regions of images. The CPMs are evaluated against state-of-the-art methods and demonstrate superior performance in classification tasks. The model's interpretability is assessed using a new metric based on the Jaccard index, which measures the similarity between ground truth concepts and learned data-driven binary indicators. The paper also proposes a new paradigm for concept discovery, which includes a concept hierarchy and a coarse-to-fine concept selection process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and intuitive presentation that makes it easy to follow.
- The concept hierarchy and coarse-to-fine concept selection are novel and interesting, providing a new perspective on interpretability in deep learning models.
- The proposed framework outperforms other state-of-the-art approaches in classification tasks while substantially improving interpretation capacity.
- The paper is well-motivated and addresses a significant problem in deep learning, namely the interpretability of deep neural networks.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the paper provides a thorough quantitative and qualitative analysis, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other existing methods, particularly in terms of the interpretability of the discovered concepts.
- The evaluation of the model's interpretability is limited, and the paper does not provide a comprehensive analysis of the discovered concepts, such as their semantic similarity to the original concepts.
- The paper's dependence on pre-trained image and text encoders and the computational cost of training the model are not adequately discussed.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework and the challenges in implementing it.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the discovered concepts, including their semantic similarity to the original concepts and their distribution across different classes.

**Questions:**
- How do the discovered concepts compare to the original concepts in terms of semantic similarity?
- What are the limitations of the proposed framework, and how do these limitations affect the interpretability of the model?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost of training the model and the scalability of the framework to larger datasets?
- How does the proposed framework handle the trade-off between interpretability and performance?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the discovered concepts, including their distribution across different classes and their semantic similarity to the original concepts?
