 **Summary:**
The paper introduces URIAL, a novel in-context learning (ICL) method aimed at aligning untuned large language models (LLMs) with human preferences. It proposes a system-level prompt that rephrases and restructures the instruction and output, aiming to improve the LLM's performance by making it more human-aligned. The methodology includes a revised evaluation protocol using a new dataset, which is evaluated using human annotators. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its limited scope, lack of comprehensive evaluation, and potential over-reliance on human evaluators. The reviewers suggest expanding the scope, including more diverse models and datasets, and providing a more rigorous evaluation to substantiate the claims made.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for aligning untuned LLMs with human preferences, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is clearly presented, making it easy to understand and replicate.
- The use of human evaluators to assess the effectiveness of the proposed method adds a valuable perspective to the evaluation process.
- The paper is well-written and the results are clear, providing a compelling case for the proposed method.
- The method is simple and effective, with good performance demonstrated across various LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on a single model (Llama-2-7b) limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The evaluation protocol is limited to a small dataset, which may not adequately represent the diversity of tasks and domains.
- The reliance on human evaluators for assessment introduces potential biases and subjectivity, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other alignment methods and does not include a detailed analysis of the method's effectiveness across different types of tasks and datasets.
- The method's performance on larger models and its scalability are not thoroughly explored, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper's claims regarding the method's ability to outperform fine-tuned models are not convincingly supported by the data presented.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the methodology used in the human evaluation, particularly how the annotators were trained and the criteria used for scoring?
- How does the proposed method perform on other LLMs, such as GPT-3.5 and GPT-4, and what are the implications for its scalability and applicability to larger models?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the effectiveness of the method across different types of tasks and datasets, including a broader range of LLMs?
- How does the method compare to other alignment methods in terms of computational cost, scalability, and practical applicability?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations and ethical considerations of using human evaluators in assessing the effectiveness of LLMs, particularly in terms of bias and subjectivity?
