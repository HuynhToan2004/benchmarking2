 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Sum-of-Parts (SOP) models, a novel approach to feature attribution in machine learning models, focusing on group-wise feature attribution. The authors demonstrate how traditional feature attribution methods can incur exponentially large errors in faithfulness tests, particularly in high-dimensional settings. To address this, SOP models group features and assign scores to these groups, which can be faithful by construction and compatible with various backbone architectures. The methodology is evaluated on standard benchmarks and real-world applications, showing potential improvements over existing methods. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in methodological details, particularly in the selection of groups and the assignment of scores, and the absence of a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to feature attribution, specifically focusing on group-wise feature attribution, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, demonstrating the exponential error incurred by standard feature attribution methods in high-dimensional settings, which is an interesting and relevant observation.
- The methodology is well-presented, making it accessible and understandable, which is crucial for a paper aimed at a broad audience.
- The paper provides a valuable case study in cosmology, demonstrating the practical application of the proposed methodology in a real-world context.
- The experimental results are promising, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in terms of accuracy and faithfulness metrics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the methodology, particularly in the selection of groups and the assignment of scores, which are critical components of the proposed method.
- There is a significant gap in the comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overloading human interpreters with too many scores, which is a significant concern in the field of feature attribution.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed or comprehensive, which limits the ability to fully evaluate the effectiveness and reliability of the proposed method.
- The paper suffers from some inconsistencies and potential errors in the presentation of results and methodological details, which could mislead or confuse readers.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the groups are selected and how scores are assigned, perhaps with examples or additional figures to illustrate the process?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of overloading human interpreters with too many scores? Are there strategies or limitations in place to manage this?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies and potential errors noted in the presentation of results and methodological details, particularly in the figures and equations?
- In light of the concerns about the faithfulness of feature attribution methods, how does the proposed method ensure that the attributions are truly faithful to the model's decision-making process?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of accuracy, faithfulness, and other relevant metrics? Could the authors provide a more comprehensive comparison to better establish the method's position in the field?
