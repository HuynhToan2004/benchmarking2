 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel hierarchical certification method for semantic segmentation, aiming to reduce the abstain rate and improve the certified information gain by relaxing the label granularity within a semantic hierarchy. This method, termed AdaptiveCertify, operates by certifying pixels at different levels of granularity based on their stability, thereby improving the robustness of the segmentation model. The paper provides a theoretical foundation for this approach and empirical evidence through experiments on Cityscapes and ACDC datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling noisy data and improving the accuracy of segmentation results. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in presentation, insufficient comparative analysis, and potential limitations in its practical applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel hierarchical certification method for semantic segmentation, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method, AdaptiveCertify, is well-motivated and provides a reasonable approach to handling noisy data in semantic segmentation.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and includes a detailed description of the proposed method and its implementation, making it accessible and understandable.
- The methodology is supported by a thorough theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.
- The paper is original in its approach to hierarchical certification for semantic segmentation, which is a promising direction for improving the reliability of segmentation models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the description of the hierarchical label space and the definition of the certified information gain metric.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods like SegCertify, and the improvements are marginal in some cases.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with other hierarchical segmentation methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed approach.
- The method's practical applicability is limited due to the need for a pre-defined class hierarchy, which may not be feasible in all scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not explore its potential impact on downstream tasks such as object detection or instance segmentation.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational cost of the proposed method, which could be a significant limitation for real-time applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and calculation of the certified information gain metric, especially how it relates to the level of granularity in the hierarchical label space?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other datasets or scenarios beyond those tested?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other hierarchical segmentation methods to establish the superiority of their approach?
- What are the specific challenges and limitations of applying the proposed method in practical scenarios, and how can these be addressed?
- How does the proposed method impact the performance of downstream tasks such as object detection or instance segmentation, and what are the potential benefits or drawbacks of using a hierarchical certification approach in these contexts?
