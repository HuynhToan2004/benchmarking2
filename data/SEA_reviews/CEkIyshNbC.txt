 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the double descent phenomenon in deep learning models, specifically focusing on the influence of noisy data on this behavior. The authors propose that the double descent is a result of models first learning noisy data until interpolation and then acquiring the capability to separate information from noise through over-parameterization. The study is supported by empirical evidence, including experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10 datasets, which show that the double descent effect is more pronounced in models with higher noise levels. Despite the novel approach, the paper's contributions are seen as incremental and not sufficiently distinct from existing literature. The methodology, while interesting, lacks clarity in its experimental setup and theoretical justification, making it difficult for readers to fully grasp the implications of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the double descent phenomenon, which is an important problem in the field of deep learning.
- The authors propose that the double descent phenomenon is caused by the model's ability to isolate noise from information in the training dataset, which is a novel perspective.
- The experiments conducted on MNIST and CIFAR-10 datasets are well-designed and support the hypothesis that the double descent effect is more pronounced in models with higher noise levels.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, which enhances its accessibility to a broader audience.
- The authors provide a detailed description of their experiments, including the neural architectures and training details, which facilitates reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
