 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the role of background and body information in action recognition, comparing human performance with that of deep neural networks. It introduces a brain-inspired architecture, DomainNet, which separates body and background processing, demonstrating superior performance in body-only scenarios. The study uses the Human Atomic Actions 500 dataset to train and evaluate models, showing that DomainNet outperforms baseline models in recognizing actions from body-only inputs. However, the paper is criticized for its limited dataset and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, which affects the generalizability and robustness of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel brain-inspired architecture, DomainNet, which processes body and background information separately, enhancing the understanding of how humans and machines process action recognition.
- The experimental results are compelling, showing that DomainNet outperforms baseline models in recognizing actions from body-only inputs, aligning more closely with human performance.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed architecture and its implementation.
- The research addresses an important question about how deep neural networks utilize body and background information, which is a significant gap in current understanding.
- The paper's focus on action recognition, a crucial aspect of human cognition, is highlighted, and the findings could inspire future research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited by its use of a small and potentially biased dataset, which may not generalize well to other datasets or real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, such as foundation models like BLIP2 and CLIP, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed architecture.
- The paper's methodology and results are not sufficiently novel or surprising, as the use of separate body and background streams for action recognition is a well-established approach.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed architecture, particularly in terms of its scalability and applicability to other domains or tasks.
- The dataset used in the study is not sufficiently detailed, raising concerns about its quality and the representativeness of the results.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the work and the implications of the findings, which is crucial for understanding the broader impact and applicability of the research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the dataset used, including its size, diversity, and representativeness?
- How does the DomainNet architecture perform when applied to other datasets or tasks beyond action recognition?
- What are the potential applications of the DomainNet architecture in real-world scenarios, and how does it compare to existing methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors elaborate on the limitations of the study and suggest areas for future research?
- Could the authors discuss the potential implications of their findings for the development of more human-like AI systems, and how these systems might be integrated into practical applications?
