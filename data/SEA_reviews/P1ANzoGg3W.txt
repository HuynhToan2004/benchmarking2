 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel two-phase learning approach, H2O-SDF, for 3D indoor scene reconstruction using Object Surface Fields (OSF) to address the vanishing gradient problem in high-frequency regions. The first phase focuses on the global scene geometry, while the second phase refines the object details. The method leverages Signed Distance Functions (SDF) and Object Surface Fields (OSF) to capture both the room layout and object details with high accuracy. The approach is evaluated on the ScanNet dataset and shows state-of-the-art performance in terms of both accuracy and smoothness. However, the method's novelty and its performance in more challenging scenes with complex lighting conditions are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel two-phase learning approach, H2O-SDF, which effectively addresses the vanishing gradient problem in high-frequency regions, particularly in indoor scenes.
- The method leverages Object Surface Fields (OSF) to guide the sampling process, which enhances the capture of object details without sacrificing the integrity of the overall scene.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, and includes comprehensive experiments that validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The introduction of a novel OSF concept to enhance SDF in capturing object geometries and surfaces is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper presents a solid technical foundation and achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in terms of both accuracy and smoothness on the ScanNet dataset.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its generalizability to different datasets and the scalability of the OSF-guided sampling strategy.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as it heavily relies on existing techniques such as OSF and re-weighting normal and color loss, which have been previously explored.
- The performance of the method in challenging scenes with complex lighting conditions is not adequately addressed, and the paper does not provide a comprehensive analysis of how the method handles such conditions.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the OSF-guided sampling strategy, which is a crucial component of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of quantitative and qualitative comparisons with state-of-the-art methods in terms of object reconstruction, which is a significant contribution of the paper.
- The paper does not provide a clear discussion on the convergence of the proposed method and the implications of the two-phase learning approach.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the OSF-guided sampling strategy is scaled to handle more complex scenes or datasets?
- How does the proposed method perform in more challenging scenes with complex lighting conditions?
- Could the authors clarify the convergence of the proposed method and the implications of the two-phase learning approach?
- Is there a plan to release the code for the proposed method to facilitate further research and reproducibility?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in terms of object reconstruction, both qualitatively and quantitatively?
- How does the method handle the vanishing gradient problem in more complex scenes, and what are the specific challenges in such scenarios?
