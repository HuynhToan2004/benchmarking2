 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LoraHub, a method for combining multiple LoRA modules to achieve cross-task generalization in few-shot scenarios. LoraHub is designed to optimize the weighted combination of existing LoRA modules without additional training, which can adapt to new tasks using a gradient-free optimization technique. The approach is evaluated on the BBH benchmark and shows comparable performance to in-context learning with fewer tokens per example, demonstrating potential in reducing computational overhead. However, the paper's novelty is questioned as it primarily combines existing methods without significant innovation, and the performance improvements over existing methods like LoRA are not substantial.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to utilizing existing LoRA modules for generalizing to new tasks, which is a promising direction for reducing computational costs.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the LoraHub learning process and the use of gradient-free optimization techniques.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method achieves competitive performance compared to in-context learning, with fewer tokens per example, which is a significant advantage in terms of computational efficiency.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including the impact of different LoRA modules and the performance across various tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it primarily combines existing methods without significant innovation.
- The performance of the proposed method is not consistently superior to existing methods like LoRA, and the improvements are sometimes marginal.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The method's applicability to different types of LLMs and its scalability to larger models are not adequately addressed.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that the proposed method can generalize to new tasks effectively, and the results are sometimes inconsistent across different tasks.
- The method's reliance on a large pool of LoRA modules and the computational cost of the optimization process are not adequately addressed.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more evidence to support the claim that the proposed method can effectively generalize to new tasks?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of computational cost and scalability to larger models?
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies in performance across different tasks and explain why the method does not consistently outperform existing methods?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of LLMs, such as decoder-only models like GPT?
- Could the authors provide more details on the optimization process and the computational cost associated with it?
- How does the proposed method handle the selection of LoRA modules for different tasks, and what are the implications of using different numbers of modules?
