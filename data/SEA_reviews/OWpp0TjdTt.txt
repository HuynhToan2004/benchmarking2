 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the challenge of learning a policy from logged data, where some rewards are missing, by proposing a semi-supervised batch learning method. The authors introduce a regularization approach using KL divergence or reverse KL divergence between the logging policy and a parameterized learned policy, which is reward-independent. This approach allows for the evaluation of the learned policy using missing-reward data, enhancing the learning process even when rewards are not available for all samples. The paper presents an upper bound for the true risk under inverse propensity score estimation and proposes a method for handling missing rewards in offline policy learning. The methodology is tested on benchmark datasets, showing that it can lead to better policy performance compared to logging policies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an interesting and relevant problem in the field of offline policy learning, focusing on handling missing rewards in logged data.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and does not require additional reward data, making it applicable in various settings.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The theoretical analysis is solid, with a rigorous proof of the upper bound for the true risk and a novel regularization approach based on KL divergence or reverse KL divergence.
- The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can lead to better policy performance compared to logging policies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the assumptions and limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the estimation of the propensity score.
- There is a lack of clarity in the experimental setup, including the definition and handling of missing rewards, and the choice of baselines.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other relevant baselines, particularly in terms of variance reduction and handling of missing rewards.
- The theoretical analysis and experimental results are not convincingly linked, and the paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the implications of the theoretical findings for practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the scalability and computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in larger datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the assumptions and limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the estimation of the propensity score?
- How does the paper handle the missing rewards in the experimental setup, and could you provide more details on the methodology used?
- Can you provide a more detailed comparison with other relevant baselines, particularly in terms of variance reduction and handling of missing rewards?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of baselines and the rationale behind their selection?
- In the experimental results, what does the "Logging Policy" refer to, and how does it compare with the proposed method in terms of policy performance?
