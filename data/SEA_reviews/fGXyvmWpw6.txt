 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for federated learning (FL) named FedLGD, which addresses the challenges of heterogeneity and data privacy by utilizing virtual data generated through local and global dataset distillation. This approach aims to improve consistency between local and global features, thereby enhancing the efficiency and effectiveness of FL. The methodology involves iterative distribution matching and federated gradient matching to align local and global data distributions, and it is evaluated on various datasets, including MNIST and CIFAR-10C. The paper claims that FedLGD outperforms existing state-of-the-art heterogeneous FL algorithms, although it has been critiqued for its incremental novelty and lack of comprehensive comparisons with recent works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, FedLGD, for federated learning that leverages virtual data created through a combination of local and global dataset distillation. This method addresses the heterogeneity issue by harmonizing domain shifts.
- The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a detailed algorithm presented in the appendix, which enhances understanding and reproducibility.
- The methodology is evaluated on both benchmark and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and applicability in practical scenarios.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, comparing it with multiple baseline methods and demonstrating its superior performance.
- The authors have addressed the issue of data heterogeneity in federated learning, which is a significant challenge in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the potential negative societal impacts.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with recent works, which could affect the assessment of its effectiveness and relevance.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, particularly in scenarios with a large number of clients.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation on larger datasets and a broader range of models to better demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of data privacy, which is a significant concern in federated learning.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the incremental nature of their contributions and how they differentiate from existing methods?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with a large number of clients, and what are the implications for communication and computation costs?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the privacy aspects of the proposed method, particularly in terms of data leakage and inference attack vulnerability?
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent works that address similar challenges in federated learning, such as FedFA and FedFM?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the number of clients and the specifics of the data used?
- How does the proposed method handle class imbalance in federated learning, and what are the implications for model performance and training efficiency?
