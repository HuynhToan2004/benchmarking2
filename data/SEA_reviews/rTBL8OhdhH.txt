 **Summary:**
The paper explores the challenges in dataset distillation by focusing on the alignment of training trajectories with the size of the synthetic dataset. It proposes a Difficulty-Aligned Trajectory Matching (DATM) method that matches the training trajectories of surrogate models trained on synthetic and real datasets. The method aims to enhance the effectiveness of dataset distillation by ensuring that the synthetic dataset closely mirrors the training dynamics of the real dataset. The paper provides a detailed analysis of the impact of trajectory matching on dataset distillation and presents empirical results demonstrating the method's effectiveness across various datasets and network architectures. However, concerns are raised about the novelty and depth of the theoretical analysis, the clarity of the paper's structure, and the limited scope of the experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to dataset distillation by aligning the training trajectories of surrogate models trained on synthetic and real datasets, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the impact of trajectory matching on dataset distillation, highlighting the importance of matching late trajectories for larger datasets.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and network architectures.
- The proposed method, Difficulty-Aligned Trajectory Matching (DATM), is innovative and provides a new perspective on dataset distillation, particularly in the context of larger synthetic datasets.
- The paper includes detailed ablation studies and visualizations that support the claims made about the effectiveness of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear theoretical analysis of why aligning trajectories is beneficial for dataset distillation, which could strengthen the argument for the proposed method.
- The structure of the paper is not well-organized, with some sections and figures (e.g., Figure 2) being confusing or inadequately explained.
- The experiments are limited to small-scale datasets and do not include larger datasets such as ImageNet, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts, which are important considerations for the application of dataset distillation.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the novelty of the method compared to existing approaches, particularly in the context of trajectory matching-based methods.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the computational complexity of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, which could affect the practical applicability of the method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancy between the results in Figure 2 and the analysis in Section 3.1, particularly the performance of matching all trajectories?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger datasets such as ImageNet, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, including the cost of training the trajectory models?
- How does the proposed method handle the mislabeling problem in dataset distillation, and what are the potential impacts of this issue on the performance and generalizability of the distilled datasets?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent approaches in dataset distillation, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
