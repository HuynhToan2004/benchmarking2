 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework called ILS-CSL, which integrates Large Language Models (LLMs) with Causal Structure Learning (CSL) to enhance the accuracy and efficiency of causal discovery from observational data. The framework iteratively refines the causal Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) based on LLM feedback, addressing the challenges of data sparsity and the expansive DAG space. The approach is evaluated against eight real-world datasets, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is critiqued for its lack of theoretical analysis, limited comparison to other LLM-based methods, and potential over-reliance on LLMs which could lead to biased or inaccurate causal inferences.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) into Causal Structure Learning (CSL) is a novel and innovative approach, offering a promising solution to the challenges of data sparsity and the expansive DAG space.
- The proposed framework, ILS-CSL, is evaluated against eight real-world datasets, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods.
- The iterative approach of ILS-CSL allows for refinement of causal DAGs based on LLM feedback, potentially leading to more accurate and robust causal inferences.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results, which enhances understanding and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough theoretical analysis, which could strengthen the claims made about the effectiveness of the proposed framework.
- The comparison to other LLM-based methods is limited, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed approach.
- There is a potential over-reliance on LLMs, which could lead to biased or inaccurate causal inferences if the LLM's understanding of causality is not aligned with the true causal relationships in the data.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed approach, such as the potential for incorrect causal inferences due to the imperfect nature of LLMs.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, which could hinder the reproducibility and validation of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison to other LLM-based methods to better understand the relative strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach?
- How does the proposed approach handle scenarios where the LLM's understanding of causality is not aligned with the true causal relationships in the data?
- What are the specific contributions of the LLM in the iterative process, and how do these contributions compare to a baseline where the LLM is not used?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the experimental results, including a comparison of the SHD metrics across different datasets and a discussion of the implications of these results?
- How does the proposed approach ensure that the LLM's inferences are not biased by the data used to train the LLM?
- Could the authors discuss the potential ethical implications of the proposed approach, particularly in terms of data privacy and the potential for misuse of the causal inferences generated?
