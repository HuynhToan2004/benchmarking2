 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DORAEMON, a method for domain randomization in reinforcement learning that optimizes the training distribution to maximize entropy while maintaining a success rate. This approach is aimed at improving generalization in sim-to-real transfer, as demonstrated through experiments on a simulated panda pushing task and real-world applications. The method involves a constrained optimization problem that adjusts the dynamics distribution to maximize entropy, ensuring that the policy remains successful. The paper also discusses the challenges of balancing generalization and robustness, and the potential for overfitting to easy modes of the optimization landscape.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the methodology and results.
- The approach of maximizing entropy in the training distribution is novel and effective, as demonstrated by the experiments.
- The experiments are comprehensive and convincing, showing the method's ability to improve generalization and achieve successful sim-to-real transfer.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of domain randomization, which is crucial for sim-to-real transfer.
- The methodology is simple yet effective, and the paper provides a thorough analysis of the results, including the limitations of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to other types of randomization and its sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- There is a lack of comparison with other methods, such as those using history-dependent policies, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's performance.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive experimental validation, particularly in more complex and varied environments.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of overfitting to easy modes of the optimization landscape, which could limit the practical applicability of the method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential societal impacts of the work, which is an important consideration for any technology that could be deployed in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when the randomization is not only in dynamics but also in other aspects, such as object shapes and textures?
- How does the method compare with other approaches that use history-dependent policies?
- Can the method be applied to more complex and varied environments, such as those with a larger number of dynamics parameters?
- How sensitive is the method to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the success rate threshold and the entropy target?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the policy's performance drops below the success rate threshold?
- Could the method be adapted to handle different types of randomization, such as randomization in the reward function or the initial state distribution?
- How does the method ensure that the policies learned are not overly reliant on specific dynamics parameters, which could limit their generalizability?
