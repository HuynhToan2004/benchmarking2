 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to estimating the trace of the Laplacian regularization term in the context of optimal transport for domain adaptation, using Hutchinson's trace estimator. This method significantly reduces computational complexity, improving time efficiency from O(n^3) to O(n^2), where n is the sample size. The proposed method is validated through experiments on MNIST and USPS datasets, demonstrating comparable accuracy to existing methods while reducing computational time. The paper also discusses the potential of the method in large-scale data scenarios and its effectiveness in mitigating overfitting. However, concerns are raised regarding the generalizability of the method to more complex or larger datasets, and the paper's claims about overfitting mitigation are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its applications.
- The paper introduces a novel use of Hutchinson's trace estimator for approximating the trace of a matrix in the Laplacian regularization term, which significantly refines computational complexity.
- The methodology is simple yet effective, as demonstrated by the experiments, which show that the proposed method can achieve competitive accuracy with a much lower computational requirement.
- The paper provides a thorough empirical analysis, including a detailed study of the number of queries and their impact on accuracy and computational time.
- The paper is well-motivated, addressing the computational challenges posed by optimal transport methods in domain adaptation, particularly in large-scale data scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its generalizability and applicability to more complex or larger datasets.
- The experimental validation is limited to small-scale datasets (MNIST and USPS), which might not adequately demonstrate the method's effectiveness in more challenging scenarios.
- The paper does not convincingly demonstrate the mitigation of overfitting by the proposed method, as the experimental results do not show a significant reduction in variance or overfitting.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with other existing methods, especially those that also employ Hutchinson's trace estimator, to establish the superiority of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational cost and complexity of the proposed method, which is critical for understanding its practical applicability in large-scale data scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be applied to larger and more complex datasets, such as those used in image or text classification tasks? If so, how does the method perform in these scenarios?
- How does the computational cost and complexity of the proposed method compare to other existing methods, especially those that also employ Hutchinson's trace estimator?
- In the experiments, why was the accuracy of the proposed method not significantly higher than the baseline method, given the reduction in computational time?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency with other methods that use Hutchinson's trace estimator?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons with other methods and a broader range of datasets, to better demonstrate the effectiveness and applicability of the proposed method?
