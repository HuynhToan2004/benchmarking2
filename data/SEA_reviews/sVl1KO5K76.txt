 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel optimization algorithm, Momentum-SAM (MSAM), which leverages the momentum vector to perturb parameters, aiming to reduce the computational overhead of Sharpness Aware Minimization (SAM). MSAM approximates the sharpness of the loss landscape by utilizing the momentum vector, which is updated before the gradient calculation, thereby eliminating the need for additional forward and backward passes. The algorithm is tested across various image classification benchmarks, demonstrating improvements in training speed without compromising generalization capabilities. Despite its theoretical and empirical contributions, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty and the marginal performance improvements over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive analysis of the proposed method.
- The introduction of MSAM, which reduces the computational overhead of SAM, is a significant contribution to the field, especially for resource-constrained environments.
- The empirical results demonstrate that MSAM outperforms SAM in terms of training speed, without compromising generalization capabilities.
- The paper includes a detailed comparison of MSAM with other sharpness-aware methods, highlighting its efficiency and effectiveness.
- The theoretical framework and empirical results are robust, supported by comprehensive experiments across various datasets and models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other sharpness-aware methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and generalization capabilities.
- The performance improvements of MSAM over SAM are marginal, and the theoretical underpinnings of MSAM's performance enhancements are not convincingly explained.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of MSAM, such as its potential impact on training time and the generalization of the results across different datasets and models.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as the use of momentum in optimization is not a new concept, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate MSAM from existing methods like NAG.
- The paper's experimental setup and results, particularly the comparison with SAM and other methods, are not clearly presented or justified, which could mislead the reader about the effectiveness of MSAM.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of MSAM with other sharpness-aware methods, including a discussion on the computational efficiency and generalization capabilities?
- How does the performance of MSAM compare to SAM in terms of generalization, particularly in scenarios where SAM has been shown to outperform MSAM?
- Can the authors clarify the theoretical underpinnings of MSAM's performance enhancements and explain why MSAM performs better than SAM in some scenarios?
- How does the paper address the concerns regarding the novelty of the approach, particularly in light of existing methods that use similar techniques?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the standard deviation of the results and a more comprehensive comparison with other methods to better establish the effectiveness of MSAM?
