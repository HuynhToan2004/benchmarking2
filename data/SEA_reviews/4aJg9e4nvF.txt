 **Summary:**
The paper explores the inner workings of Vision Transformers (ViTs) by employing optimization-based feature visualization techniques, focusing on the visualization of high-dimensional features from the feed-forward layer. This approach reveals that ViTs preserve spatial information of patches across layers, except the last, which behaves like average pooling. The authors also compare ViTs with Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and discuss the effects of natural language supervision on ViTs. The paper further investigates how ViTs trained with CLIP supervision exhibit features related to semantic concepts. However, the paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of its methods and the generalizability of its findings across different datasets and architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for visualizing the inner workings of Vision Transformers (ViTs) by focusing on the high-dimensional features from the feed-forward layer, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that focus on low-dimensional components like keys, queries, or values.
- The authors conduct a comprehensive visualization study across a wide range of ViT variants, including DeiT, CoaT, ConViT, PiT, Swin, and Twin, which provides a robust validation of the effectiveness of their method.
- The paper offers insights into the behavior of ViTs, particularly their ability to make use of background information and their reliance on high-frequency, textural attributes, which are crucial for understanding the architecture's functionality.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making the complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide a detailed comparison between ViTs and CNNs, highlighting their similarities and differences, which is crucial for understanding the relative strengths and weaknesses of these architectures.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of its methods, which could help in understanding the generalizability and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide sufficient quantitative evidence to support some of its claims, such as the sudden disappearance of localization information in the last attention layer.
- The paper's visualizations are primarily based on ImageNet, which might limit the generalizability of the findings to other datasets.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and time efficiency of the proposed visualization methods, which could be critical for practical applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of its findings, which could be significant given the increasing use of ViTs in various applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the findings might apply to other datasets or architectures, and how the visualization methods might be adapted for different use cases.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and time efficiency of their visualization methods?
- How do the findings of this paper apply to other datasets, such as those used in the training of models like CLIP?
- Can the authors elaborate on the societal impacts of their findings, particularly in terms of the potential applications of ViTs in areas like facial recognition or medical imaging?
- How might the visualization methods be adapted for use with different architectures, such as those that use different attention mechanisms or different layers for feature extraction?
- Could the authors provide more quantitative evidence to support the claims made in the paper, particularly those related to the functionality of ViTs?
