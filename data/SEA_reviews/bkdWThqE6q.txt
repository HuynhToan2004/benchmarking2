 **Summary:**
The paper introduces INTR, a novel transformer-based approach for interpretable image classification. INTR utilizes a transformer encoder-decoder architecture, where class-specific queries are used as inputs to the decoder, allowing for the identification of distinct attributes per class. This method is evaluated on eight fine-grained datasets and demonstrates the ability to localize attributes effectively, though it shows some performance degradation compared to baseline methods. The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the attention mechanism and its interpretability, highlighting the model's potential for fine-grained classification and interpretability. However, it faces criticism for its limited novelty and the lack of extensive comparative analysis with other interpretable models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach by utilizing a transformer-based model for interpretable image classification, which is a novel application of transformers in this context.
- The methodology is clearly explained, making the paper accessible and understandable to readers.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through experiments on eight fine-grained datasets, showcasing the model's ability to localize attributes and provide interpretability.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the attention mechanism, which is crucial for understanding the model's interpretability.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of interpretability and performance.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as similar ideas have been explored in previous works.
- The performance of the proposed method is significantly lower than baseline methods, which raises concerns about its practical applicability.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method when dealing with a large number of classes, which could lead to increased training and inference times.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed experimental settings and comparisons, including the number of attention heads and the number of decoder layers used in different models.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between their method and the DETR method, especially in terms of the application of cross-attention and the use of class-specific queries?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issue when dealing with a large number of classes?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other interpretable models, particularly in terms of interpretability and performance?
- How does the method perform when applied to datasets with a large number of classes, such as ImageNet?
- Can the authors discuss the potential of their method in other applications beyond image classification, such as image generation or segmentation?
