 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MLLM-Guided Image Editing (MGIE), a method that integrates Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) with a diffusion model to enhance instruction-based image editing. MGIE leverages MLLMs to generate concise and expressive instructions, which are then transformed into visual guidance using a diffusion model. The method aims to improve image editing by providing more precise and detailed instructions, which are evaluated against various benchmarks and compared to existing methods like InsPix2Pix and LGIE. The results show that MGIE outperforms these methods in terms of automatic metrics and human evaluations, indicating its effectiveness in image editing.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components.
- The use of a summarizer to obtain concise instructions from the MLLM is a novel approach that enhances the efficiency and effectiveness of the method.
- The introduction of visual tokens in the form of [IMG] to represent visual-related guidance is a creative solution that bridges the gap between language and vision modalities.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method, showing improvements in automatic metrics and human evaluations.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the method and its components, making it accessible for replication and further study.
- The use of MLLMs to guide image editing is a promising approach that could inspire future research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the generalizability of the approach to different types of images or editing tasks.
- The use of MLLMs as a text-to-image model could be seen as a limitation, as it may not provide the same level of control or precision as a text-to-image model specifically designed for image editing.
- The method's reliance on the quality of the pre-training dataset, which is based on synthetic data, could limit its effectiveness in real-world applications where images may not meet the same quality standards.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods in the field, which could help establish its position more clearly.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process, particularly the training time and the number of training iterations, which could affect the reproducibility and scalability of the method.
- There are concerns about the scalability of the method, especially when applied to larger or more complex datasets, as it may not generalize well to new or different types of images.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, particularly the training time and the number of training iterations?
- How does the method perform when applied to images that are not of the same quality as those in the pre-training dataset?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the visual tokens [IMG] in the method, especially in terms of their contribution to the image editing process?
- How does the method handle the trade-off between image fidelity and editing accuracy, particularly in scenarios where the image quality may affect the editing outcomes?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the method and how these might affect its practical application in real-world scenarios?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance, particularly in scenarios where the images are not of the same quality as those in the pre-training dataset?
