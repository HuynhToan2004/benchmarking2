 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the ability of large language models (LLMs) to self-correct their reasoning, a crucial aspect for improving the reliability and accuracy of their outputs. The study employs various datasets and models to evaluate the effectiveness of self-correction mechanisms, particularly focusing on intrinsic self-correction without external feedback. The results show that LLMs struggle with self-correction, often leading to incorrect answers or no changes in their initial responses. The paper also discusses the limitations of self-correction in reasoning tasks and suggests that the effectiveness of self-correction may depend on the initial prompting and the task at hand.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The study is comprehensive, covering various datasets and models, which enhances the reliability of the findings.
- The paper provides valuable insights into the limitations of self-correction in reasoning tasks, which is a significant and relevant topic in the field of large language models (LLMs).
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of self-correction in LLMs, including the effects of different prompts and the comparison with other methods like multi-agent debate and self-consistency.
- The paper offers a novel perspective on self-correction as a form of post-hoc prompting, which is an interesting and less explored area in LLM research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's main claim that LLMs struggle with self-correction is not new and has been previously discussed in other works. More recent studies, such as those using GPT-4, have shown that self-correction can be effective, which contradicts the paper's findings.
- The experimental setup and results are limited to specific models (GPT-3.5 and GPT-4) and datasets, which might not generalize well to other models or tasks.
- The paper's claims about the ineffectiveness of self-correction are not convincingly supported by the experiments, especially when compared to other methods like multi-agent debate and self-consistency.
- There is a lack of discussion on the broader implications and applications of self-correction, particularly in real-world scenarios where LLMs might benefit from this capability.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with other methods and a more detailed analysis of the experimental results to strengthen its claims.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancy between their findings and those of more recent studies that show self-correction can be effective?
- How do the authors address the concerns about the generalizability of their findings to other models and tasks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, especially the prompts used and the conditions under which self-correction was applied?
- How does the paper's definition of "self-correction" align with other definitions in the literature, and why was the decision made to focus on intrinsic self-correction without external feedback?
- Given the limitations of self-correction, what are the potential applications or scenarios where this capability might still be beneficial?
