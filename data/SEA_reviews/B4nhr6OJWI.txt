 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Subtask Induction, aimed at instilling inductive biases in neural networks by localizing and transferring subnetworks from trained models to newly initialized networks. This approach is demonstrated to enhance the model's ability to perform specific tasks, such as arithmetic and image classification, with reduced training data. The methodology involves discovering subnetworks that encode specific subtasks within a trained model, which are then transferred to a new model initialized with random weights. This process is shown to improve sample efficiency and reduce the need for extensive training data. The paper also introduces a new dataset, Mean-pooled ImageNet, which is used to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Subtask Induction, which is a promising approach for instilling inductive biases in neural networks.
- The method is supported by strong experimental results, particularly in reducing the amount of training data required for models to adopt specific solutions to modular arithmetic tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the authors have released their code, enhancing reproducibility.
- The method demonstrates improved sample efficiency and the ability to reduce the amount of training data required for models to adopt specific solutions to modular arithmetic tasks.
- The introduction of the Mean-pooled ImageNet dataset is a valuable contribution to the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the method across different tasks and datasets, which could strengthen the claims of its general applicability and effectiveness.
- There is a need for a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly concerning the scalability and applicability to different architectures and tasks.
- The paper does not sufficiently address how the method might be applied in scenarios where the task decomposition is not explicitly known or where the subtasks are not well-defined.
- The method's reliance on supervised training of a binary mask for subnetwork discovery could limit its practical applicability, especially in scenarios where labeled data for subtask identification is not available.
- The method's effectiveness and applicability are not clearly demonstrated in the experiments, particularly in the image classification task, where the results are not as convincing as in the arithmetic task.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when applied to different tasks, such as language tasks or other types of image classification?
- Can the method be adapted to scenarios where the task decomposition is not explicitly known, or where the subtasks are not well-defined?
- How does the method handle the scalability issues, particularly in larger models or more complex tasks?
- Could you clarify the results in Figure 3, particularly the discrepancy between the left and right panels, and the performance of the randomly sampled subnetwork?
- In the arithmetic task, how does the method perform when the training dataset is not limited to a single subtask, but includes examples from multiple subtasks?
- How does the method compare to other methods for instilling inductive biases, such as data augmentation or other forms of pretraining?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the specifics of the arithmetic task and the image classification task, to better understand the method's effectiveness and limitations?
