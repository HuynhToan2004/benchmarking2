 **Summary:**
The paper explores the problem of forecasting time series governed by ordinary differential equations (ODEs) by viewing the space of trajectories as a finite-dimensional Riemannian manifold. This perspective enables the application of statistical estimation methods directly on the manifold, potentially improving forecasting accuracy. The authors demonstrate that this approach can be used to optimize various statistical objectives such as maximum likelihood (ML), maximum a posteriori (MAP), and minimum mean squared error (MMSE) estimation. Theoretical insights are supported by numerical simulations, although the paper's presentation and the clarity of its contributions are criticized.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel perspective on forecasting ODE trajectories, viewing the space of trajectories as a finite-dimensional Riemannian manifold.
- The theoretical results are robust, particularly the proof of Theorem 1, which is crucial for the paper's claims.
- The paper is well-written, with clear exposition and a comprehensive proof of the main theorem.
- The application of statistical estimation methods directly on the manifold of feasible ODE trajectories is a significant contribution, potentially improving forecasting accuracy.
- The paper is significant in its approach to ODE forecasting, which is a crucial aspect of time series forecasting.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with existing methods, particularly neural ODEs, which could have strengthened the argument for the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The presentation of the paper is unclear in some sections, particularly in the discussion of the manifold and the application of statistical estimation methods.
- The paper's contributions are not clearly articulated, making it difficult to understand the novelty and significance of the work.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental validation, particularly in terms of the practical applicability of the proposed method in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which could limit its applicability in more complex or larger-scale problems.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach, particularly the assumptions and constraints that may limit its applicability in certain scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the proposed method and existing methods like neural ODEs, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and scalability with other state-of-the-art methods?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including real-world applications, to demonstrate the practical utility of their method?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the ODE is not Lipschitz continuous or where the data is noisy?
- Can the authors elaborate on the choice of the Riemannian metric and its impact on the results?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to clarify the computational and theoretical aspects of their method, particularly in the application of statistical estimation methods on the manifold?
