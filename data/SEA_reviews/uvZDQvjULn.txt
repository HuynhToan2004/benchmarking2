 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the optimization of Reinforced Language Models (RLMs) from a bi-objective perspective, focusing on the trade-off between reward and likelihood objectives. The authors propose a method called Reward Dropout, which selectively sets rewards to zero based on their rank, aiming to improve the optimization process. Theoretical analysis supports the Pareto optimality of RLMs, and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of Reward Dropout across various datasets and models. Despite the novel approach and the theoretical insights, concerns were raised about the clarity of the experimental setup and the need for a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to analyzing Reinforced Language Models (RLMs) from a bi-objective perspective, which is both original and insightful.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, providing a solid foundation for the proposed methodologies.
- The empirical results are convincing, showing that the proposed methods, particularly Reward Dropout, are effective across various datasets and models.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the main ideas and a good balance between theoretical and empirical evidence.
- The proposed method, Reward Dropout, is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, making it a practical contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods in the field of RLMs, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- The experimental setup is not clearly defined, and the details of the experiments, such as the specifics of the reward models and the parameters used, are not adequately explained.
- The paper's focus on the bi-objective nature of RLMs and the use of Pareto optimization is somewhat limited, as these concepts are not new and have been previously explored in other contexts.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and a clearer explanation of how the results generalize to other scenarios or models.
- The paper's claims regarding the Pareto optimality and the effectiveness of the proposed methods are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence, particularly in terms of the practical application of the methods.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger models, such as those with billions of parameters?
- Could you clarify the experimental setup, particularly the details of the reward models and the parameters used?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of the quality of the generated text, and what metrics were used to evaluate this?
- Can you provide more examples of the generated text and discuss how the proposed method affects the generation process?
- How do the authors address the issue of Pareto optimality in practical scenarios, and what are the implications of this for the practical application of the methods?
