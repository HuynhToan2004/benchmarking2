 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called CALM (Composition to Augment Language Models) for enhancing the capabilities of existing language models by integrating them with smaller, task-specific models. The method involves the addition of cross-attention layers between the models to facilitate information exchange, without modifying the weights of the base models. This approach is demonstrated to improve performance on various tasks such as translation, arithmetic reasoning, and code generation, particularly in low-resource settings. The method is shown to outperform fine-tuning baselines and other parameter-efficient methods like LoRA. The paper also addresses the challenges of adapting models to new domains and tasks without extensive training, thus expanding the applicability of large language models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, CALM, which enables the augmentation of existing language models with task-specific models without modifying their weights, allowing for efficient and effective adaptation to new tasks and domains.
- The method is shown to outperform fine-tuning baselines and other parameter-efficient methods like LoRA, demonstrating its effectiveness in enhancing model performance.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the method and its implementation, and the experiments are well-designed, showing significant improvements in performance across various tasks.
- The method is applicable to a variety of settings, including low-resource languages and code generation, and is flexible enough to be used with different types of models, including those trained on proprietary data.
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in the field of language models by enabling the adaptation of models to new tasks and domains without extensive training, which is a critical issue in the deployment of large language models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other parameter-efficient methods like LoRA, which could provide a clearer picture of CALM's relative performance.
- The method's effectiveness in more complex or diverse settings, such as handling multiple augmenting models or different types of tasks, is not explored, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational costs or scalability of the method, which could be crucial for understanding its practical implementation in real-world scenarios.
- The method's reliance on the availability of task-specific models and the quality of the data used to train these models could limit its applicability in certain scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the potential limitations or societal impacts of the method, which could be important considerations for its practical deployment.

**Questions:**
- Can CALM be used with more than one augmenting model, and if so, how does the method handle the integration of multiple models?
- How does the method perform when applied to other types of tasks, such as those not directly related to translation or code generation?
- What are the computational costs and scalability of the method, and how do these compare to other parameter-efficient methods like LoRA?
- How does the method handle the integration of task-specific models that may not be as effective or high-quality as those used in the experiments?
- Could you provide more details on the training data used for the augmenting models and how this data was selected or validated?
- How does the method compare to other methods that use different types of models or approaches for task-specific adaptation?
