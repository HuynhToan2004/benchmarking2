 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for controlling text generation using successor features, which are applied to adjust the probability of selecting tokens based on the likelihood that the text will become undesirable. This method is aimed at enhancing the controllability of text generation by leveraging successor features to estimate the probabilities of undesired final discourse under different token selections. The authors claim that their method is more efficient and effective than existing methods, as it allows for dynamic steering of text generation without altering the LLM's parameters. The paper is evaluated on various tasks, including sentiment control and detoxification, and shows promising results compared to baseline models. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the paper, the complexity of the method, and the comparability of results with existing works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel application of successor features in text generation, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the key concepts and their implementation.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are promising, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the theoretical framework is well-explained, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.
- The method's efficiency and effectiveness are highlighted, with potential for significant impact on the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in certain sections, particularly in the introduction and methodology, which could be improved with better organization and clearer explanations.
- The complexity of the method, particularly the use of successor features, might be a barrier to understanding for readers not familiar with this concept.
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the experimental setup and results, such as the use of different datasets and models, which could affect the validity of the comparisons made.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help readers better understand its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in terms of the formatting and organization of tables and figures, which could enhance the readability and comprehensibility of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of the successor feature in the context of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage, particularly in scenarios involving multiple subjects?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, especially in terms of the experimental setup and results, to better establish the novelty and effectiveness of their approach?
- How does the method perform when using different models or datasets, and what are the implications of these choices for the generalizability and applicability of the findings?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of their method and discuss potential avenues for future research to address these limitations?
