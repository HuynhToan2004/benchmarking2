 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CLAM-TTS, a novel approach to zero-shot text-to-speech synthesis that leverages a probabilistic residual vector quantization method to encode mel-spectrograms into discrete latent codes. This method allows for the efficient generation of multiple tokens at once, eliminating the need for cascaded modeling. The model utilizes a pre-trained large language model to generate these latent codes, which are then converted into speech using a vocoder. The system is evaluated on a variety of datasets and tasks, demonstrating improvements in naturalness, intelligibility, speaker similarity, and inference speed compared to existing baselines. The paper also explores the impact of pretraining extent and text tokenization strategies on performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and detailed methodology.
- The proposed method is novel, combining probabilistic residual vector quantization with large language models, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The experiments are thorough, including both quantitative and qualitative evaluations, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the impact of pretraining extent and text tokenization strategies on performance, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The results show that the proposed method can achieve better naturalness, intelligibility, speaker similarity, and inference speed compared to existing baselines.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other zero-shot TTS systems, particularly in terms of inference time and computational efficiency.
- There is a lack of discussion on the impact of different pretraining strategies and the choice of models, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and inference of the vocoder, which is a critical component of the system.
- The evaluation is limited to specific datasets and does not include a broader range of languages or speakers, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address potential negative societal impacts, such as the potential for misuse or the ethical implications of the technology.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the training and inference of the vocoder, including the specifics of the model architecture and the training dataset?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of inference time and computational efficiency with other zero-shot TTS systems, particularly those that use different architectures or training strategies?
- Can you elaborate on the choice of models and pretraining strategies, and how these choices affect the performance of the system?
- How does the proposed method handle different languages or accents, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the results?
- Could you provide more details on the ethical considerations and potential negative societal impacts of the technology, and how these were addressed in the development of the system?
