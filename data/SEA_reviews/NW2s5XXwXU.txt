 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for enhancing the generation of tail classes in long-tailed datasets using diffusion models. It proposes a Bayesian framework that utilizes a weighted score-matching technique to facilitate knowledge transfer between head and tail classes, enhancing the diversity and quality of generated samples. The method employs a gating mechanism to control the transfer process, supported by extensive experiments on various benchmark datasets. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its unclear presentation, limited experimental validation, and potential over-reliance on certain theoretical assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for enhancing the generation of tail classes in long-tailed datasets using diffusion models, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is supported by a theoretical framework that is well-articulated, providing a solid foundation for the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for understanding complex theoretical concepts.
- Extensive experiments are conducted on various benchmark datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in enhancing the diversity and quality of generated samples.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology, which is beneficial for readers seeking a deeper understanding of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from poor presentation, with numerous typos, unclear notations, and inconsistencies in terminology and equations, which can confuse readers and undermine the credibility of the research.
- The experimental validation is limited, with insufficient comparisons to state-of-the-art methods and a lack of comprehensive performance metrics, such as visual comparisons of generated images and statistical analysis of generated samples.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous analysis of the impact of different parameters on the performance of the method, particularly the hyperparameters used in the experiments.
- The paper's theoretical claims are not always clearly supported by empirical evidence, which could weaken the persuasiveness of the arguments.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the notation and terminology used in the paper to ensure consistency and clarity?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more detailed visual comparisons of the generated images to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the class imbalance is more severe, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in such scenarios?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect the practical applicability of the findings?
- How does the method handle the transfer of knowledge between head and tail classes, and what are the implications of this transfer for the overall performance and diversity of the generated samples?
