 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel hybrid X-shaped Transformer, named Xformer, designed for image denoising tasks. It integrates both spatial-wise and channel-wise Transformer blocks within a concurrent network structure, facilitating the capture of multi-scale features. The Bidirectional Connection Unit (BCU) is proposed to bridge the learned representations from these two branches, enhancing information fusion. Extensive experiments demonstrate that Xformer achieves state-of-the-art performance on synthetic and real-world image denoising tasks, outperforming existing methods in terms of both quantitative and visual results. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty in network design and the lack of comparisons with some recent methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear motivation and clear methodology.
- The proposed Xformer achieves state-of-the-art performance on both synthetic and real-world image denoising tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The use of a concurrent network structure with two branches, each utilizing spatial-wise and channel-wise Transformer blocks, allows for the capture of multi-scale features, enhancing the model's performance.
- The Bidirectional Connection Unit (BCU) is an innovative approach that effectively bridges the learned representations from the two branches, enhancing information fusion and improving the model's ability to model global information.
- The paper includes extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed Xformer in image denoising tasks, supported by strong visual results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the network design is limited, as similar approaches have been previously explored, such as the use of concurrent networks for image restoration tasks.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with some recent methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the Xformer's performance relative to the state-of-the-art.
- The paper's experimental results do not convincingly support the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the FLOPs and model size, which are not significantly reduced compared to existing methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed components, such as the BCU and the spatial-wise and channel-wise Transformer blocks.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and a broader comparison with other methods to better position the Xformer within the existing landscape of image denoising models.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other recent methods to better understand the Xformer's performance relative to the state-of-the-art?
- How does the Xformer perform when compared to other methods in terms of computational efficiency, specifically in terms of FLOPs and model size?
- Can the authors provide more ablation studies to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed components, such as the BCU and the spatial-wise and channel-wise Transformer blocks?
- What are the specific contributions of the Xformer that differentiate it from existing methods, particularly in terms of its design and performance?
- How does the Xformer handle different types of noise beyond Gaussian noise, and what are its limitations in terms of noise types and image qualities?
