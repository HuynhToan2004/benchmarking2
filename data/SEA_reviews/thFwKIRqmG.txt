 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel on-policy actor-critic reinforcement learning algorithm named VSOP, which integrates three primary modifications: applying a ReLU function to advantage estimates, spectral normalization of actor-critic weights, and the use of dropout for Bayesian approximation. VSOP's theoretical underpinnings are grounded in a lower bound on the value function, and its empirical evaluations demonstrate significant improvements over existing on-policy algorithms like A3C, PPO, SAC, and TD3 across various benchmarks. Despite its innovative approach, concerns about the novelty of the modifications and the theoretical soundness of the proposed methodologies were raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to on-policy actor-critic reinforcement learning by integrating three simple modifications to the A3C algorithm: applying a ReLU function to advantage estimates, spectral normalization of actor-critic weights, and incorporating dropout as a Bayesian approximation.
- The theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method are robust, grounded in a lower bound on the value function, and demonstrate significant improvements over existing on-policy algorithms in empirical evaluations.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a thorough empirical evaluation of the proposed method across various benchmarks, demonstrating significant improvements over existing on-policy algorithms.
- The use of dropout as a Bayesian approximation is innovative and adds a novel perspective to the application of Bayesian inference in reinforcement learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning its applicability in scenarios with non-stationary environments.
- The novelty of the modifications to the A3C algorithm is questionable, as similar approaches have been previously explored, such as using ReLU activations and dropout in actor-critic networks.
- There are concerns about the theoretical soundness of the proposed methodologies, particularly the use of dropout for Bayesian approximation and the application of spectral normalization.
- The paper's experimental results are not comprehensive enough, and the results in some environments, such as ProcGen, are not convincingly superior to existing methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of sample inefficiency, which is a known challenge in reinforcement learning.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning its applicability in scenarios with non-stationary environments?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent works that have also explored similar modifications to the A3C algorithm, such as those mentioned in the paper?
- Could the authors clarify the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed methodologies, particularly the use of dropout for Bayesian approximation and the application of spectral normalization?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of sample inefficiency, which is a significant challenge in reinforcement learning?
- In light of the concerns about the novelty of the modifications to the A3C algorithm, can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the unique contributions of their approach compared to existing methods?
