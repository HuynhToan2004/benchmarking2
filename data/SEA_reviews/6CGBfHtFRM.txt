 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for training deep reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms using a mean-field Langevin TD learning method for the critic and a mean-field Langevin policy gradient for the actor. This approach is supported by theoretical guarantees of global convergence to the optimal policy and linear convergence of the value function. The method is analyzed using tools from mean-field Langevin dynamics, providing a new perspective on how neural networks learn features in the RL setting. The paper also includes numerical experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in the CartPole-v1 environment.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research.
- The introduction of a new method for training deep RL algorithms using a mean-field Langevin TD learning method for the critic and a mean-field Langevin policy gradient for the actor is a significant contribution.
- The paper provides theoretical guarantees of global convergence to the optimal policy and linear convergence of the value function, which is a strong theoretical result.
- The paper includes numerical experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in the CartPole-v1 environment, showing that it can learn features from the input data.
- The use of mean-field Langevin dynamics for policy gradient and TD learning in neural networks is a novel approach that extends the convergence analysis of MFLD with general over-parameterized neural networks to both TD learning and policy gradient methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and applicability to more complex environments.
- The empirical evaluation of the method is limited to the CartPole-v1 environment, which might not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- The theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions, such as the boundedness of rewards and the stationarity of the Markov Decision Process (MDP), which might not hold in practical scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and practical implementation.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the practical implications of the theoretical results, such as the convergence rates and the computational cost of the proposed method.

**Questions:**

- Can the authors clarify the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of convergence rates and computational complexity?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, particularly in terms of feature learning and computational efficiency?
- How does the paper address the practical challenges of implementing the theoretical results in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly in relation to the boundedness of rewards and the stationarity of the MDP?
