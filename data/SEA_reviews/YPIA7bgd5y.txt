 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of in-context labels on the predictions of large language models (LLMs) through a series of experiments. It explores how LLMs utilize label information, their ability to learn novel tasks, and the influence of in-context label relationships. The study also examines how LLMs treat all information provided in-context equally and whether they can overcome prediction preferences from pre-training. The experiments are designed to falsify null hypotheses about how ICL incorporates label information, and the results suggest that ICL does indeed consider in-context label relationships, but it struggles to fully overcome prediction preferences acquired from pre-training data. The paper also discusses the limitations of ICL, such as its inability to treat all in-context information equally and its reliance on labels that are closer to the query.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear and concise figures and tables that aid in the comprehension of the results.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are convincing, providing new insights into how in-context labels affect the predictions of large language models (LLMs).
- The study is thorough, covering a range of tasks and models, and the conclusions are well-supported by the data presented.
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic, which is the in-context learning (ICL) of LLMs, and provides novel insights into how ICL leverages label information.
- The experiments are conducted in a principled way, with the use of probabilistic metrics which better highlight the resulting ICL dynamics, and the study shows that ICL predictions depend on in-context labels and that ICL can learn truly novel tasks in-context.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing literature, particularly in terms of novelty and depth of insights.
- The paper's claims about ICL's ability to overcome pre-training preferences and its treatment of all in-context information equally are not convincingly supported by the data presented.
- The paper's conclusions are somewhat limited by the scope of the experiments, which primarily focus on classification tasks and do not adequately address the generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential limitations of the study, such as the generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks and the potential impact of different model sizes and training methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the study and how these might affect the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- How does the paper's findings on the ability of ICL to overcome pre-training preferences align with the results of other studies, such as those by Zhao et al. (2021)?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies in the results between their study and those of other studies, particularly in terms of the impact of label randomization on ICL performance?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of the study, such as the generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks and the impact of different model sizes and training methods?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations for the observed differences in ICL performance between different model sizes and training methods?
- How do the authors justify the use of probabilistic metrics in their experiments, and could they provide more detailed justifications for why these metrics are more effective than accuracy metrics in highlighting the dynamics of ICL?
