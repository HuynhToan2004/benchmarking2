 **Summary:**
The paper explores the impact of layer-selective rank reduction on the performance of large language models (LLMs), specifically focusing on transformer-based architectures. The authors propose a method called LASER, which involves removing higher-order components of learned weight matrices in specific layers to improve model performance. The experiments conducted on various datasets, including question-answering tasks and non-text domains, demonstrate that LASER can enhance model performance, particularly by improving accuracy and robustness to paraphrases. The paper also investigates the effect of training data on the performance of the models, suggesting that LASER acts as a denoising technique that makes weakly learned facts more accessible.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The proposed method, LASER, is simple and effective, requiring no additional parameters or training data, making it straightforward to apply to existing models.
- The experiments are well-designed, showing that LASER can improve model performance on various tasks such as question-answering, decision-making, and computer vision tasks.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the impact of LASER on different layers and components of the model, offering valuable insights into how different components of the model contribute to performance improvements.
- The paper includes a variety of experiments, including on non-text domains, which demonstrate the generalizability of the findings across different domains.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation for why LASER works, especially why removing higher-order components improves performance. More theoretical insights or hypotheses would strengthen the paper.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential negative impacts on model performance or generalizability.
- The experimental results are not consistently strong across all datasets and models, with some showing only marginal improvements or no improvement.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review, particularly in the area of model pruning and low-rank approximations, to better contextualize the current work within existing research.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed visualizations and explanations, particularly in sections where the methodology and results are not clearly explained.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more insights into why LASER works, particularly why removing higher-order components improves performance?
- Is there a risk that LASER could make the model over-confident on less frequently occurring data, leading to overfitting?
- How does LASER compare to other pruning techniques, such as those that focus on removing weights with the smallest magnitudes?
- Can the authors clarify the impact of LASER on the model's calibration, particularly in terms of temperature tuning?
- How does LASER affect the model's performance on out-of-distribution (OOD) data, and could this method be used for OOD detection?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative impacts of LASER, such as increased training time or potential negative effects on model performance?
