 **Summary:**
The paper introduces RenEmb, a novel neural network architecture designed to learn structural embeddings from raw file content, specifically focusing on tabular data. The architecture integrates a transformer module and a convolutional module, trained in a self-supervised manner on a large dataset of public data files. RenEmb is demonstrated to perform comparably with state-of-the-art baselines on six real-world datasets, improving upon them when combined with the structural embeddings provided by the approach. The authors aim to address data preparation tasks by encoding the structure of tabular text data files, which can be fine-tuned for specific tasks like dialect detection, table understanding, and column type annotation. The paper also discusses the challenges of data preparation, such as the lack of labeled datasets and the general-purpose nature of existing models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach for learning structural embeddings, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is well-motivated and addresses a critical problem in data preparation, which is often overlooked in the field of machine learning.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broader audience.
- The proposed method is evaluated on six real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.
- The use of a transformer module and a convolutional module in the neural network architecture is innovative and contributes to the novelty of the approach.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the RenEmb architecture, including its pattern tokenization strategy and two novel pre-training tasks, which are essential for understanding the method's functionality and effectiveness.
- The authors have shared all datasets with their annotations, the trained models with their weights, and the source code, which enhances the reproducibility and transparency of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There is a need for more detailed explanations of the training process and the specifics of the datasets used, particularly in the appendix.
- The paper does not adequately address how the method would perform on data that is not standardized or follows different formats, which could limit its practical application.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods, such as those using transformer models, which might lead to questions about its novelty.
- The paper's experimental setup could be improved by including more datasets and a broader range of tasks to better demonstrate the method's versatility and effectiveness.
- The paper's focus on pre-training models on tabular data might limit its applicability to other types of data, such as image or text data.

**Questions:**
- How does the method handle data that is not standardized or follows different formats?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process, such as the specifics of the datasets used and the hyperparameters tuned?
- How does the method compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Could the authors elaborate on how the method might be adapted or extended to handle different types of data, such as image or text data?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting to specific dialects or formats, especially in datasets with limited diversity?
- What are the potential applications of the method beyond data preparation, such as in data integration or discovery?
- How does the method perform on datasets with different dialects or formats, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
