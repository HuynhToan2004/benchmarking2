 **Summary:**
The paper explores the collaboration mechanisms among large language model (LLM) agents, focusing on how they mirror human-like social behaviors in a multi-agent society. It introduces two agent traits (easy-going and overconfident) and two thinking patterns (debate and reflection) to evaluate the effectiveness of different collaborative strategies. The authors conduct experiments across three datasets, showing that the order of thinking patterns significantly influences performance. They also discuss the implications of these findings for the design of more socially-aware AI systems. However, the paper is criticized for its limited exploration of different agent traits and thinking patterns, the lack of novelty in its approach, and the need for more comprehensive experiments to validate its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an interesting concept of modeling LLM agents as a society, which is a novel approach in the field of AI.
- It provides a comprehensive empirical analysis across three datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of different collaborative strategies.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, with clear explanations of the experiments and results.
- The authors have made significant efforts to ensure reproducibility by providing code and datasets, which is commendable.
- The paper raises important questions about the collaboration mechanisms among LLM agents and how they mirror human-like social behaviors, which is a significant area of interest in AI research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution to the field is limited as it primarily focuses on a single trait and thinking pattern, which may not adequately capture the complexity of human social dynamics.
- The experiments are not extensive enough, with a limited number of agents and rounds of collaboration, which may not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed strategies.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as similar studies have been conducted previously, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from existing works.
- The paper lacks a deeper exploration into the dynamics of collaboration between LLM agents, particularly in terms of how different agent traits and thinking patterns influence performance.
- The paper's claims about the LLM agents' mirroring of human-like social behaviors are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the findings could be applied to improve AI systems, which is a significant gap in the paper's contribution.

**Questions:**
- Can you elaborate on how the proposed collaborative strategies could be applied in real-world scenarios to improve AI systems?
- How does the paper address the limitations in the exploration of different agent traits and thinking patterns?
- What are the implications of the findings for the design of more socially-aware AI systems?
- Could you provide more details on how the experiments were conducted, particularly in terms of the methodology and the selection of datasets?
- How do the authors respond to the concerns regarding the novelty and depth of the experimental results?
