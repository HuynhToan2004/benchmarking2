 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an adaptive method for training Softmax Trees (ST) to address the limitations of fixed tree structures in Softmax Tree (ST) models. The proposed method, named Adaptive Softmax Trees (AST), optimizes the tree structure by interleaving expansion steps that turn wide softmaxes into narrower ones, followed by global optimization of the entire tree. This approach allows for more efficient and accurate classification in scenarios with a large number of classes. The method is demonstrated to improve predictive accuracy and reduce inference time and model size, although it may require significant computational resources. The paper includes experimental results showing the method's effectiveness, although concerns are raised about the novelty and the clarity of the presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel adaptive approach to train Softmax Trees (ST) by interleaving expansion steps that turn wide softmaxes into narrower ones, followed by global optimization of the entire tree, which is a significant improvement over traditional fixed tree structures.
- The methodology is clearly explained, with detailed experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving predictive accuracy and reducing inference time and model size.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear and comprehensive description of the methodology, making it accessible to readers.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing baselines in terms of accuracy, inference time, and model size.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the motivation behind using the ST model over other classification models like SVM or kNN, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy.
- There is a significant increase in computational resources required by the proposed method compared to traditional ST models, which might limit its practical applicability in resource-constrained environments.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the scalability of the proposed method with the number of classes, which is crucial for understanding its applicability in large-scale classification tasks.
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the computational complexity of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, which is necessary to assess its practical efficiency.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which detract from its professional presentation.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed method scale with the number of classes, and what are the practical implications of using this method in large-scale classification tasks?

- Why were certain baselines not included in the experimental results, and can the authors provide a justification for this omission?
