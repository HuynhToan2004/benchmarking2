 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SoundStorm, a novel approach to audio generation that leverages a hierarchical RVQ-based codec and a MaskGIT-inspired decoding strategy to achieve fast and efficient audio synthesis. SoundStorm is designed to handle long sequences of audio data by predicting masked tokens in a coarse-to-fine manner, which is claimed to improve performance and reduce computational costs compared to existing methods like AudioLM. The model's architecture includes a bidirectional attention-based Conformer, which is trained to predict masked audio tokens from a residual vector-quantized neural audio codec, using a coarse, low-bitrate conditioning signal. The paper presents several experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the model, although concerns are raised about the novelty and the depth of the experiments, particularly in terms of the variety of datasets used and the comparative analysis with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the proposed method is clear and easy to understand.
- The use of a hierarchical RVQ-based codec and a MaskGIT-inspired decoding strategy is novel and effective for efficient audio synthesis.
- The experiments are well-designed, including ablation studies on different aspects of the model, and the results are convincing.
- The model is shown to outperform AudioLM in terms of speech intelligibility, voice preservation, and acoustic consistency.
- The paper introduces a new audio generation method that is faster than previous methods and demonstrates improved performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited as it builds upon existing works like AudioLM and MaskGIT, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from these prior works.
- The experiments are limited to a single dataset (LibriSpeech), which may not generalize well to other datasets.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other related works, such as AudioGen, MusicGen, and VALL-E, which could provide a clearer picture of the model's advantages and limitations.
- There is a lack of detailed information on the training and inference setup, such as the specifics of the training corpus, the training and inference speed, and the hardware used.
- The paper does not include subjective listening tests, which are crucial for evaluating the perceived quality of the generated audio.
- The paper does not address how the model handles variations in speaker voice or acoustic conditions, which could be significant for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific contributions of their work compared to existing methods like AudioLM and MaskGIT?
- How does the model handle variations in speaker voice and acoustic conditions, especially in long-form audio generation?
- What are the training and inference speeds of the proposed model, and how do they compare to other models like AudioLM?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and inference setup, such as the specifics of the training corpus and the hardware used?
- How does the model perform on other datasets, and what are the implications of using a different dataset for training and testing?
- Could the authors elaborate on the decision to use only a single dataset for evaluation and consider including subjective listening tests to assess the perceived quality of the generated audio?
