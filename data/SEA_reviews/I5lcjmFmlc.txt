 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Robust Diffusion Classifier (RDC), a novel method that leverages diffusion models to enhance adversarial robustness in image classification tasks. RDC operates by first maximizing the data likelihood of an input image and then predicting class probabilities using the conditional likelihood estimated by the diffusion model. This approach is supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results that demonstrate improved robustness against various adversarial attacks. The methodology includes a multi-head diffusion model and an efficient sampling strategy to reduce computational complexity. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the complexity of the method, and the clarity of the presentation, which may limit its accessibility and applicability to broader audiences.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by leveraging diffusion models to enhance adversarial robustness in image classification tasks, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-structured, with clear explanations of the theoretical underpinnings and empirical results that support the claims made.
- The paper presents a detailed analysis of the robustness of the diffusion classifier, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The experiments conducted are robust and comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods across various threat models and adversarial attacks.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable to readers, and the use of illustrations and figures enhances the clarity of the content.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the computational complexity and memory requirements of the proposed methods, which are crucial for practical implementation.
- There is a significant gap between the theoretical analysis and the empirical results, particularly concerning the robustness of the diffusion classifier under optimal settings.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed methods, such as the dependency on the quality of the pre-trained diffusion model and the scalability of the method to larger datasets.
- The paper suffers from several typographical and formatting errors, which could detract from its professionalism and readability.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as it appears to be a simple application of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the proposed methods to larger datasets and more complex models, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the computational complexity and memory requirements of the proposed methods, and discuss the feasibility of scaling these methods to larger datasets and more complex models?
- How does the proposed method compare to other adversarial training techniques, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and robustness against different types of adversarial attacks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the pre-trained diffusion model used in the experiments, including its size and performance metrics?
- Is there a possibility to include more recent datasets and attack methods in the experimental validation to ensure the relevance and applicability of the findings?
- Can the authors address the typographical and formatting errors in the manuscript to enhance its professionalism and readability?
