 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel meta-learning framework, MeDe, aimed at learning with noisy labels across multiple target domains. MeDe operates by training a reusable meta denoiser on meta features extracted from a source dataset, which can then be applied to various target datasets to identify and correct mislabeled data. The meta denoiser is trained using a binary classifier to distinguish between clean and mislabeled data based on meta features, which are characterized by their transferability across different datasets. The framework's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on benchmark datasets, showcasing its ability to outperform existing methods in many scenarios. Despite its innovative approach, concerns were raised about the novelty of the meta-learning framework and the clarity of the experimental setup, particularly regarding the selection of the source dataset and the generalization of the meta denoiser across different noise levels.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel meta-learning framework, MeDe, which is designed to learn with noisy labels on multiple target domains, focusing on the transferability of meta features across datasets.
- The methodology of using meta features to train a meta denoiser is innovative, allowing for the identification and correction of mislabeled data, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers and facilitating understanding of the proposed methods.
- Comprehensive experiments are conducted, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in achieving state-of-the-art performance.
- Theoretical analysis is provided, enhancing the understanding of the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the meta-learning framework is questioned as it primarily relies on the transferability of meta features, which is a common approach in meta-learning.
- The paper lacks clarity in the experimental setup, particularly in the selection of the source dataset and the training process for the meta denoiser.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some results showing marginal improvements over existing methods, and the generalization of the meta denoiser across different noise levels is not thoroughly explored.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The comparison with existing methods is insufficient, particularly in terms of the selection of baselines and the fairness of experimental comparisons.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and selection process of the source dataset in the MeDe framework?
- How does the meta denoiser generalize to different noise levels, and what are the implications of this generalization for the performance of the method?
- Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the meta denoiser is trained, including the specifics of the training process and the selection of hyperparameters?
- How do the meta features, particularly the loss trajectory and confidence mismatches, contribute to the effectiveness of the meta denoiser?
- Can you elaborate on the theoretical analysis provided and how it supports the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the MeDe framework compare with other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how do you address these concerns?
