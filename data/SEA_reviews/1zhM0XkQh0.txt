 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Projected Feature Adversarial Training (ProFeAT), a novel method for enhancing the robustness of deep neural networks against adversarial attacks through a combination of self-supervised learning and adversarial training. The method employs a projection layer to align the training objectives of the teacher and student models, and uses a distillation loss to enhance the robustness of the student model. The paper also proposes a combination of weak and strong augmentations for the teacher and student respectively, to improve generalization without increasing training complexity. The approach is validated on several benchmark datasets and models, demonstrating significant improvements in performance compared to existing SSL methods and being on par with TRADES.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and well-defined research questions.
- The proposed approach is novel, using a projection layer to align the training objectives of the teacher and student models, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper includes extensive experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method and its robustness-accuracy trade-offs.
- The use of weak and strong augmentations for the teacher and student respectively is an innovative approach that enhances the generalization of the model without increasing training complexity.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results, which is supported by a thorough literature review and a detailed explanation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on image classification tasks and does not explore the application of the proposed method in other domains such as object detection or segmentation.
- The performance of the proposed method is not consistently superior across all datasets and model architectures, and there is a noticeable variance in performance as indicated by the standard deviation in the results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of training instability caused by enforcing losses at different layers, which could affect the reproducibility and reliability of the results.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant works, such as DynACL, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the intuition behind the proposed method, particularly how the projector helps in aligning the training objectives and preventing overfitting.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the projector helps in aligning the training objectives and preventing overfitting, and how this relates to the distillation loss used in the method?
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets and model architectures, particularly those not explored in the current study?
- What are the specific benefits of using a projector layer over other methods for aligning the training objectives, and how does this compare to other methods such as those mentioned in the literature?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the projection layer in the training process, particularly in terms of its impact on the model's performance and the overall training dynamics?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of training instability, and what steps have been taken to ensure the reproducibility and reliability of the results?
