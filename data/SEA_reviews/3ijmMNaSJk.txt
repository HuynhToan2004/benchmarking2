 **Summary:**
The paper explores the effectiveness of Masked Distillation (MD) in mitigating overfitting and enhancing performance in pre-trained models, focusing on the attention mechanisms and loss landscape of these models. It presents a comprehensive analysis of MD's impact on various pre-trained models, including CLIP, DINO, and DeiT, and demonstrates that MD can improve model performance by addressing issues such as attention homogenization and representation folding. The paper also discusses the influence of Masked Distillation on the loss landscape and the decision-making biases of models, suggesting that it can lead to more balanced and less biased predictions. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty and the absence of a new method, with some reviewers questioning its contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the Masked Distillation (MD) method, including its effects on attention mechanisms and the loss landscape, which is beneficial for understanding the inner workings of MD.
- The visualization of attention maps and singular value spectrum is well-executed, offering valuable insights into the MD process.
- The paper presents a thorough analysis of the MD method, covering various aspects such as attention homogenization, representation folding, and the smoothness of the loss landscape, which is useful for understanding the MD process.
- The writing is clear, making it easy to follow, and the paper is well-structured, contributing to its readability and comprehensibility.
- The experiments conducted are robust, providing a solid foundation for the conclusions drawn about the MD method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and differentiation of the MD method from other similar methods like masked image modeling (MIM), which could lead to confusion among readers.
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as the MD method has been previously studied, and the results presented are not significantly different from existing literature.
- The paper does not introduce a new method or approach, which could limit its impact on the field.
- The experiments are primarily conducted on CLIP, DINO, and DeiT, which are not the most recent or widely used models, raising questions about the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the MD method and its potential negative impacts, which could be crucial for a complete understanding of the technology.
- There are concerns about the novelty and the depth of the analysis, as the paper seems to rehash existing results without providing significant new insights or contributions.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the differences between the MD method and other similar methods like MIM, especially in terms of how they operate and what they aim to achieve?
- How do the authors justify the use of older models like CLIP, DINO, and DeiT for their experiments, and what implications does this have for the generalizability of the findings?
- What are the specific advantages of the MD method over other pre-training methods, and how do these advantages translate into practical applications?
- Could you provide more details on the experiments conducted, particularly how they were designed and what they aimed to demonstrate?
- In light of the concerns about the novelty and depth of the analysis, how do the authors respond to the criticisms and what steps are being taken to address these issues in future work?
