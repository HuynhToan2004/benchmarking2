 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for fair empirical risk minimization using f-divergence measures, aiming to address the challenges of fairness and distribution shifts in machine learning. It proposes a unified stochastic optimization framework that extends to scenarios with distribution shifts, utilizing distributionally robust optimization reformulation under ℓp norms as uncertainty sets. The framework is designed to be scalable and computationally efficient, with theoretical guarantees for convergence and empirical validation showing its effectiveness across various batch sizes. The paper also discusses the application of the framework in real-world scenarios, demonstrating its robustness against distribution shifts. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the depth of experimental validation, and the clarity of the theoretical underpinnings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem of fairness in machine learning, which is crucial for ensuring fairness in real-world applications.
- It introduces a novel framework for fairness-accuracy tradeoffs using f-divergence measures, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed algorithm is scalable, efficient, and effective, as demonstrated through extensive experiments on multiple datasets.
- The paper provides a theoretical guarantee for the convergence of the proposed algorithm, enhancing its credibility and practical applicability.
- The framework is robust to distribution shifts, which is crucial for applications where data distributions may change over time or across different domains.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the f-divergence measures used, which could limit the understanding of their effectiveness in promoting fairness.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as it seems to resemble existing methods, particularly those involving f-divergence in fairness-preserving training.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing methods, which could help in understanding the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed framework.
- The experiments are limited in scope, particularly in terms of the datasets used and the fairness metrics employed, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer explanation of the theoretical assumptions and their implications.
- There are concerns about the computational complexity and the scalability of the proposed algorithm, particularly in large-scale applications.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed proofs and explanations of the theoretical results, especially those related to the convergence guarantees.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the theoretical justification for using f-divergence measures to promote fairness and discuss the specific properties of f that make it effective for this purpose?
- How does the proposed framework compare with existing methods in terms of computational efficiency, scalability, and practical applicability?
- Could the authors provide more detailed proofs or references for the theoretical results, especially those related to the convergence guarantees?
- How does the proposed algorithm handle the computational complexity and scalability issues, particularly in large-scale applications?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of datasets and fairness metrics used in the experiments, and discuss the implications of these choices for the generalizability of the results?
- What are the specific assumptions required for the theoretical results to hold, and how do these assumptions affect the practical applicability of the framework?
- How does the framework perform when the training and test distributions are significantly different, and what are the implications of this for the fairness-accuracy tradeoff?
