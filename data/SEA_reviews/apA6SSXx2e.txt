 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel metric, Topological Concentration (TC), to measure the local topology of nodes in graph neural networks (GNNs) for link prediction tasks. The metric is based on the intersection of the local subgraph of each node with those of its neighbors, aiming to correlate better with link prediction performance than traditional node-level topological metrics like degree and subgraph density. The paper also discusses a topological distribution shift issue and proposes an Approximated Topological Concentration (ATC) to address computational complexity. The authors claim that TC can identify low-performing nodes more effectively and demonstrate its utility through empirical results on real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel metric, Topological Concentration (TC), which is a significant contribution to the field of link prediction in graph neural networks (GNNs).
- The authors provide a clear and comprehensive empirical analysis of the proposed metric, including its correlation with link prediction performance and its ability to identify low-performing nodes.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with detailed experimental setups and results.
- The authors address the issue of topological distribution shift and propose an approximated topological concentration to address computational complexity.
- The paper includes a variety of datasets and experiments, which support the claims made about the effectiveness of the proposed metric.
- The theoretical justification provided for the metric and the proposed approximated topological concentration adds depth to the paper.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed metric, particularly how it might not apply to all types of link prediction tasks or datasets.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed metric with existing metrics such as degree and subgraph density, which could have strengthened the argument for its superiority.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed metric and how it scales with the size of the graph.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of cold-start nodes, which are known to have poor performance in link prediction tasks.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that the proposed metric can generalize well to other link prediction tasks or datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition of TCVal and TCTest as shown in Figure 5(b)?
- How does the proposed metric compare to other existing metrics in terms of computational complexity and scalability?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the metric might be applied to different types of link prediction tasks, such as homophilous or heterophilous networks?
- How does the proposed metric address the issue of cold-start nodes, and what are the implications for the performance of the link prediction task in such scenarios?
- The paper mentions the use of Gaussian random projections for approximating the metric. Could the authors elaborate on how this approximation affects the accuracy and reliability of the metric?
- How does the proposed metric perform in real-world applications, and what are the implications for practical use cases?
