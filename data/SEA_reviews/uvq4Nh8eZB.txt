 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel federated learning approach called federated co-training (FedCT), which allows clients to share predictions on unlabeled data, forming a consensus label for local training. This approach enhances privacy by not sharing model parameters and is theoretically analyzed for convergence and differential privacy. The method is tested across various datasets, including healthcare and non-iid data, demonstrating its effectiveness and privacy benefits compared to existing methods like federated averaging and distributed distillation. The paper also explores the use of differentially private mechanisms and discusses the scalability and interpretability of models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel federated co-training (FedCT) approach that allows clients to share predictions on unlabeled data, enhancing privacy and potentially improving model quality.
- Theoretical analysis is provided for the convergence of the proposed method, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable.
- The methodology is tested on various datasets, including healthcare and non-iid data, demonstrating its effectiveness and privacy benefits compared to existing methods like federated averaging and distributed distillation.
- The paper explores the use of differentially private mechanisms to enhance privacy, which is a significant concern in federated learning.
- The proposed method allows the use of various models, including interpretable models like decision trees, which can be beneficial for understanding the model's behavior.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the method and its theoretical underpinnings, which is helpful for understanding the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the communication complexity between FedCT and other methods, which could be crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed approach.
- The method's scalability is not thoroughly discussed, which is a significant concern for federated learning applications where the number of clients can be large.
- The paper's theoretical analysis primarily focuses on convergence, and there is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method.
- The paper's focus on binary data might limit its applicability to other types of data, such as image data, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to evaluate the effectiveness and reliability of the proposed method.
- The paper's claims about the method's privacy benefits are not adequately supported by empirical evidence, which could undermine the credibility of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the communication complexity between FedCT and other methods, including a discussion on how the size of the unlabeled dataset might affect this complexity?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with a large number of clients, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in such settings?
- Can you discuss the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of how it might compare to other federated learning methods?
- How does the proposed method handle non-iid data, and what are the potential implications of this for the model's performance and generalizability?
- Could you clarify the definition and role of the "public unlabeled dataset" in the proposed method, and how it is collected and utilized in the training process?
- How does the paper ensure the privacy and security of the unlabeled dataset, especially in the context of potential membership inference attacks?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the datasets used, the models trained, and the training parameters?
- How does the paper address the potential for data leakage or information leakage from the unlabeled dataset, especially in the context of federated learning?
