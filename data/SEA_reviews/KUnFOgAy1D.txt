 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a differentiable approximation of the generalized Low-Rank Regularization (LRR) to address the non-differentiability of the rank function, which is typically solved using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The authors propose an iterative method for approximating the matrix pseudo-inverse and matrix square root, which are used to compute the rank and nuclear norm differentiably. These approximations are then applied to various LRR functions, including the nuclear norm and other nonconvex relaxations, to optimize the loss function. The paper claims that these approximations are computationally efficient and can be applied to a broad class of LRR functions, although the experimental results are somewhat limited and do not convincingly demonstrate superiority over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a differentiable approximation of the generalized Low-Rank Regularization (LRR) that can be applied to a broad class of LRR functions, including nuclear norm and other nonconvex relaxations.
- The proposed method can be conveniently appended to a loss function in a plug-and-play fashion and optimized by existing machine learning libraries, making it versatile and easy to implement.
- The approximation solely depends on matrix multiplication, which enables highly efficient parallel implementation, making it computationally efficient.
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the problem and the proposed method, with a clear structure that makes it easy to follow.
- The experiments conducted on matrix completion, video fore-background separation, and image denoising demonstrate the versatility and efficiency of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the convergence of the iterative methods used to approximate the matrix pseudo-inverse and matrix square root, which are crucial for the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The experimental results presented are somewhat limited and do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods. The paper could benefit from more extensive comparisons and results to validate its claims.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis on the approximation error of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability, especially in large-scale applications.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other existing methods, such as those mentioned in the related work section, which could provide a better understanding of the proposed method's advantages and limitations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the convergence of the iterative methods used to approximate the matrix pseudo-inverse and matrix square root?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational complexity and practical applicability, especially in large-scale applications?
- Could the authors provide more extensive experimental results to validate the claims made in the paper?
- How does the proposed method handle the approximation error introduced by the iterative methods used to approximate the matrix pseudo-inverse and matrix square root?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the proofs, particularly in Proposition 3 and Lemma 1, to ensure their validity and applicability?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other existing methods in terms of accuracy and efficiency, especially in the experiments presented?
