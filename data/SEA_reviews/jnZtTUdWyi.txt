 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for domain generalization in non-stationary environments, focusing on invariant representation learning to adapt to evolving distributions. The method involves a representation learning network that maps data from source domains to a common representation space, and a classifier network that learns from these representations to predict class labels. Theoretical analysis is provided to support the proposed approach, and empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness across various datasets. The paper is structured well, with a clear presentation of the methodology and experimental results. However, there are concerns regarding the clarity of some theoretical concepts, the practical applicability of the method in real-world scenarios, and the scalability of the proposed solution.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in domain generalization, focusing on non-stationary environments which are relevant to real-world applications.
- The methodology is well-structured, with a clear presentation of the theoretical analysis and empirical results, supported by a detailed explanation of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of related work, effectively positioning the proposed method within the existing literature.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the theoretical and empirical results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the non-stationary consistency of hypothesis mechanism, which is crucial for understanding the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method.
- There is a need for more detailed experimental validation, particularly in scenarios where the method's performance is not as expected, such as in the Circle-Hard dataset.
- The paper does not adequately address the practicality of the method, particularly in scenarios where the method requires access to historical data during inference, which may not be feasible in real-world applications.
- The scalability of the proposed solution is a concern, especially in terms of the computational resources required for training and the potential for overfitting in large datasets.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced perspective on its applicability and effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition and role of the non-stationary consistency of hypothesis mechanism in the context of your method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data distribution shifts are not consistent or predictable, such as in the Circle-Hard dataset?
- Is there a possibility to adapt the method for scenarios where the data distribution shifts are not known in advance, or where the shifts are non-stationary?
- Can you provide more details on the computational resources required for training the proposed method, and discuss potential strategies for reducing the computational overhead?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the data distribution shifts are not aligned with the sequence of source domains, such as in the Yearbook dataset?
- Can you discuss the limitations of the proposed method and provide examples or scenarios where it may not be effective?
