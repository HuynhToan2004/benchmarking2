 **Summary:**
The paper presents a comprehensive empirical study on enhancing the performance of Large Language Models (LLMs) in solving math problems. It explores three fine-tuning strategies: solution fine-tuning, solution-cluster re-ranking, and multi-task sequential fine-tuning. The experiments are conducted using the PaLM 2 models on the MATH dataset, demonstrating that these methods can significantly improve the model's performance. Key findings include the importance of solution quality in fine-tuning, the effectiveness of solution re-ranking and majority voting, and the superiority of multi-task sequential fine-tuning over solution fine-tuning. The paper also introduces a novel solution re-ranking method that combines the benefits of majority voting and re-ranking, achieving better performance and computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear and detailed explanation of the proposed methods, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The empirical study conducted is thorough and comprehensive, covering various aspects of the proposed methods, which enhances the credibility of the findings.
- The proposed solution re-ranking method is novel and effective, combining the benefits of majority voting and re-ranking, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the results, including the impact of different solution styles and the performance of various re-ranking strategies, which offers valuable insights into the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The experiments conducted are extensive, covering different training strategies, loss functions, and re-ranking methods, which provides a robust evaluation of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the term "style" used in the context of step-by-step solutions, which could lead to confusion among readers.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance metrics like Pass@1, Maj1@64, and MajK@64, which could undermine the perceived effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational efficiency of the proposed methods, especially in comparison to baseline methods.
- There is a lack of discussion on the generalizability of the proposed methods to other types of math problems or other LLMs, which could limit the practical applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the term "style" as used in the context of step-by-step solutions and provide a clear definition or explanation?
- How do the proposed methods perform when compared to existing methods in terms of performance metrics like Pass@1, Maj1@64, and MajK@64?
- What are the limitations of the proposed methods, and under what conditions might they not be effective?
- Could you discuss the computational efficiency of the proposed methods in more detail, perhaps by comparing the training and inference times to baseline methods?
- How do the proposed methods perform when applied to other types of math problems or other LLMs? Are there any specific conditions or limitations that need to be considered?
- Could you provide more details on the data collection process for the step-by-step solutions, especially whether they were collected from an external source or generated by the model itself?
