 **Summary:**
The paper explores the relationship between finite-time Lyapunov exponents (FTLEs) and the gradient of the loss function in recurrent neural networks (RNNs), specifically focusing on the alignment of dominant gradient directions with FTLEs. It derives a novel expression for the loss gradient in terms of FTLEs and Q vectors, which are crucial for understanding the dynamics of RNNs. The authors conduct experiments on the MNIST dataset to demonstrate the alignment between FTLEs and gradient directions, suggesting that the network's sensitivity to perturbations is influenced by the FTLEs. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the training and optimization of RNNs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to analyzing the gradient of the loss function in RNNs using finite-time Lyapunov exponents (FTLEs), which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The theoretical derivations and empirical validations are well-supported, providing a robust foundation for the claims made.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of complex concepts and a logical flow that aids in understanding the content.
- The experimental setup and results are convincingly presented, demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed methods.
- The paper's contribution to the understanding of RNNs and their dynamics is substantial, potentially leading to new insights and methodologies for training and optimizing RNNs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on vanilla RNNs, which might limit the generalizability of the findings to more complex or modern RNN architectures.
- The experimental setup and results are somewhat limited, focusing on simple tasks and small datasets, which might not fully demonstrate the effectiveness or applicability of the proposed methods in more complex or varied scenarios.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are not clearly distinguished from existing works, such as those by Vogt et al. (2022a), which might lead to confusion about the novelty and originality of the contributions.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the implications and potential applications of the findings, particularly in terms of practical improvements in RNN training and optimization.
- The paper's claims regarding the significance of FTLEs in RNN training and optimization are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence, which could weaken the paper's argument.

**Questions:**

- How do the findings of this paper apply to more complex or modern RNN architectures, such as those based on attention mechanisms or transformers?
- What are the potential practical implications of the findings for improving the training and optimization of RNNs?
- Could the authors provide more detailed empirical evidence to support the claims regarding the significance of FTLEs in RNN training and optimization?
