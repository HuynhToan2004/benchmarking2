 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel ensemble method for unsupervised constituency parsing, leveraging a CYK-like algorithm to aggregate the predictions of multiple unsupervised parsers into a single, averaged parse tree. This method, termed "tree averaging," aims to improve performance by combining the strengths of different parsers. The ensemble is then distilled into a student model, enhancing inference efficiency and mitigating over-smoothing issues. The paper is evaluated on the Penn Treebank and demonstrates superior performance compared to existing methods. However, the paper's technical novelty is questioned, as it primarily combines existing methods without significant innovation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method shows promising results, particularly in the unsupervised setting, and demonstrates effectiveness in both in-domain and out-of-domain scenarios.
- The ensemble-then-distill approach is innovative and effective, as shown by the results.
- The method's ability to leverage the strengths of different parsers and its robustness to variance in teacher models are notable.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation and analysis, including an ablation study on the number of teachers and the effect of different expertise.
- The use of CYK-like algorithm for tree averaging is an interesting approach that simplifies the computation of the F1 score.

**Weaknesses:**
- The technical novelty of the paper is limited, as it primarily combines existing methods without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other ensemble methods, such as those using weighted voting or Bayesian model averaging.
- There is a potential issue with the selection of the best tree among multiple runs, which could lead to misleading results if the best trees are not representative of the ensemble's performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed methods.
- The results on the SUSANNE dataset are not convincing, and there is a need for a more comprehensive evaluation on different datasets to validate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, such as the sensitivity to the choice of teacher models and the potential for over-smoothing in the distillation process.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to other ensemble methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- In the ensemble-then-distill approach, how is the best tree selected from the ensemble of runs, and what are the implications of this selection on the overall performance?
- Can you clarify the selection process for the best tree and discuss how this affects the results, especially in the context of the SUSANNE dataset?
- How does the paper address the potential for over-smoothing in the distillation process, and what measures are in place to mitigate this issue?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of datasets for evaluation and discuss the implications of using different datasets for training and testing?
