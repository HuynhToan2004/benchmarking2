 **Summary:**
The paper introduces OpenACL, a novel method for open-world continual learning that utilizes unlabeled out-of-distribution (OOD) data to enhance model performance. OpenACL employs a prototype-based approach to adapt to new tasks and mitigate catastrophic forgetting by leveraging unlabeled data. The method is evaluated across various datasets, showing consistent improvements over existing methods. However, concerns were raised regarding the clarity of the problem formulation, the realism of the experimental settings, and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant and challenging problem of open-world continual learning, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The proposed method, OpenACL, is innovative and well-articulated, utilizing a prototype-based approach that effectively mitigates catastrophic forgetting and adapts to new tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experiments that demonstrate the method's effectiveness across various datasets.
- The use of unlabeled out-of-distribution (OOD) data in continual learning is a novel and interesting approach that has been validated through empirical results.
- The method is well-motivated and technically sound, providing a clear and logical structure that enhances understanding.

**Weaknesses:**
- The problem formulation could be clearer, particularly in terms of the definitions and distinctions between open-world and open-set continual learning.
- The experimental settings are not realistic, as they do not reflect real-world scenarios where labeled and unlabeled data are typically accessed separately.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could better establish the method's effectiveness and novelty.
- There are concerns about the method's scalability and computational efficiency, particularly in handling large datasets or high-dimensional data.
- The paper does not adequately address how the method handles the issue of out-of-distribution (OOD) data, which is a significant challenge in continual learning.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation and a broader range of datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definitions and distinctions between open-world and open-set continual learning?
- How does the method ensure that the unlabeled data used is truly out-of-distribution, and what measures are in place to prevent the model from learning biased representations?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method handles the scalability and computational efficiency issues, particularly in handling large datasets or high-dimensional data?
- How does the method perform in more complex or varied data scenarios, such as those found in natural language processing or other domains?
- Can the authors discuss the potential implications of using different prototype initialization methods or varying the number of prototypes used?
- In the experimental results, why does OpenACL sometimes underperform compared to other methods, and how do these results align with the method's theoretical advantages?
