 **Summary:**
The paper explores the alignment between bias and variance in the context of deep learning models, particularly focusing on the ensemble of deep learning based classification models. It presents both empirical and theoretical evidence to support the hypothesis that the bias and variance of correctly classified points are approximately equal. The empirical results are supported by experiments across various datasets and model architectures, including ResNet-50 on ImageNet, and the theoretical analysis is grounded in the concepts of calibration and neural collapse. The paper argues that this alignment is crucial for understanding the generalization capabilities of deep learning models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a significant and intriguing phenomenon of bias-variance alignment in deep learning models, which is a novel contribution to the field.
- The authors provide a comprehensive empirical analysis across various datasets, architectures, and model sizes, which demonstrates the robustness and generalizability of the observed phenomenon.
- The theoretical analysis is well-grounded in the concepts of calibration and neural collapse, providing a solid foundation for understanding the observed phenomena.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations and detailed experiments that support the claims made.
- The paper provides a unified definition for a variety of definitions of calibration, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and discussion on the sample-wise bias-variance alignment, which is a central claim of the study.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to specific model architectures and training scenarios, which may not generalize well to other models or training methods.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the study, which could affect the reliability and applicability of the findings.
- The assumption of perfect calibration and the neural collapse assumption used in the theoretical analysis are not well justified or discussed, which could limit the credibility of the theoretical results.
- The empirical analysis does not sufficiently address the potential impact of different sources of randomness in the model's performance, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the implications of the findings for practical applications, which could limit the relevance of the study for practitioners.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a clear definition and discussion on the sample-wise bias-variance alignment, and clarify how it relates to the bias-variance decomposition?
- How does the assumption of perfect calibration affect the theoretical results, and is there a possibility to relax this assumption?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the neural collapse assumption, and how these assumptions impact the validity of the results?
- Is there a possibility to extend the theoretical analysis to other model architectures or training scenarios, and how might this affect the results?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the implications of the findings for practical applications, and how might these insights be used to improve the performance of deep learning models in real-world scenarios?
