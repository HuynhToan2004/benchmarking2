 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LUMEN-PRO, a framework designed for multi-task learning in Diffractive Optical Neural Networks (DONNs). The framework leverages the rotatability of the physical system to replace task-specific layers with rotated shared layers, thereby reducing memory requirements and enhancing model efficiency. The authors claim that LUMEN-PRO achieves significant improvements in accuracy and efficiency compared to existing methods, as demonstrated through experiments on datasets such as MNIST and CelebA. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the approach, the limited scope of experiments, and the lack of a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The idea of using rotatability in the physical system to replace task-specific layers with rotated shared layers is innovative and interesting.
- The proposed method demonstrates better accuracy and model efficiency compared to existing multi-task learning (MTL) baselines and single-task models.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including experiments on MNIST and CelebA datasets, which show significant improvements in accuracy and efficiency.
- The framework is well-motivated, and the experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it primarily builds upon the work of RubikONN, with the main difference being the use of a gradient-based architecture search algorithm.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of energy efficiency and throughput, which are crucial for evaluating the practicality and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The experiments are limited in scope, focusing only on a few datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other related works, such as FINN, which also addresses energy efficiency in neural networks.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method to more complex datasets or tasks, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to other types of neural networks or its scalability to more complex tasks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of energy efficiency and throughput?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods that address energy efficiency in neural networks, such as FINN?
- Could the authors provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including experiments on more complex datasets or tasks?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, especially in terms of its applicability to more complex datasets or tasks?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and suggest areas for future research?
