 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Multivariate Projection Unit (MPU) as an activation function in neural networks, inspired by the structural similarity between a single layer of a Feed-forward Neural Network (FNN) and a single iteration of the Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) algorithm. This MPU is designed to project inputs onto convex cones, which is claimed to enhance the expressive power of neural networks. Theoretical proofs and empirical evaluations on various architectures, including convolutional neural networks and vision transformers, are provided to support the effectiveness of the proposed MPU. However, the paper faces criticisms regarding the clarity of the experimental setup, the theoretical contributions, and the practical implications of the proposed activation function.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations and illustrations that aid in understanding the concepts presented.
- The theoretical proofs provided in the paper are solid, supporting the claims made about the expressive power of the Multivariate Projection Unit (MPU) and its relationship to the Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) algorithm.
- The paper introduces a novel activation function, the MPU, which is a significant contribution to the field of neural network activations, potentially enhancing the expressive power of neural networks.
- The empirical results presented in the paper demonstrate the effectiveness of the MPU in various architectures, showing improvements in accuracy and computational efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for why the proposed MPU is superior to existing activation functions, especially in terms of its practical applications and theoretical contributions.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with issues such as missing details in the experimental setup, unclear figures, and a lack of comparisons with a sufficient number of baseline models.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect the understanding of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the MPU compared to other activation functions, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of the MPU over existing activation functions are not convincingly supported by the empirical results, which show only marginal improvements or no significant difference in performance.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the motivation behind the design of the MPU and how it addresses the limitations of existing activation functions?
- How does the MPU perform in more complex architectures, such as those involving multiple layers or different types of neural networks?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity of the MPU with other activation functions, especially in terms of the number of operations required?
- How does the MPU handle different types of input data, such as categorical or continuous variables, and what are the implications for its performance in such scenarios?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the MPU and how these might affect its practical applicability?
- In light of the marginal improvements observed in the empirical results, can the authors explain why the MPU does not show a more significant advantage over existing activation functions?
