 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an offline tracking model designed to handle occluded object tracking in autonomous driving scenarios. The model is composed of three components: a standard online tracker, a re-identification module, and a track completion module. The re-identification module uses a lane map as a prior to associate tracklets before and after occlusion, while the track completion module refines the trajectories using a vectorized map as input. The model is evaluated on the nuScenes dataset, showing improvements over existing methods in terms of tracking accuracy and completeness. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the limited comparative analysis, and the clarity of the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in autonomous driving by focusing on the handling of occluded object tracking, which is crucial for accurate and efficient tracking in real-world scenarios.
- The proposed method is well-structured and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the components and their roles in the tracking process.
- The use of a lane map as a prior for re-identification and track completion is innovative and adds a novel aspect to the methodology.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and detailed explanations of the methodology, making it accessible and understandable.
- The method shows improved performance over existing methods, particularly in scenarios involving occlusion, as demonstrated by the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited, as similar methods using a lane map as a prior have been previously explored. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's advantages and disadvantages.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with some results showing marginal improvements and others being questionable due to potential data leakage.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of identity switches (IDS) in the results, which is a critical aspect of tracking performance.
- The methodology and experimental setup are not clearly explained in some sections, which could lead to confusion about the method's implementation and effectiveness.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential shortcomings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of their approach and how it differs from existing methods that also use a lane map as a prior?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where there are multiple vehicles on the same lane, and how does it differentiate between them?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including a discussion on how their method compares in terms of computational efficiency and tracking accuracy?
- How does the method handle scenarios where there are no tracklets after occlusion?
- In the experimental setup, how does the method perform when there are no ground truth tracks, and how does it handle the potential for data leakage from the training set to the test set?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results and explanations for the experimental setups, particularly the pseudo-occlusion setup and the results presented in Tables 1, 2, and 3?
