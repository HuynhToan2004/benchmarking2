 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the internal mechanisms of GPT models, specifically focusing on the translation task, by analyzing how different layers contribute to in-context machine translation. The study employs layer-wise context-masking experiments to identify critical layers for translation tasks and explores the redundancy in layers following task recognition. It also introduces a method for identifying task-specific layers and pruning attention heads to enhance inference efficiency. The findings suggest that task recognition occurs around layer 20 for GPTNeo and layer 25 for Bloom, with later layers showing minimal impact on translation performance. The paper also proposes a method to identify task-specific layers and prunes attention heads to enhance inference efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important question regarding the internal mechanisms of GPT models, specifically focusing on the translation task, which is a significant area of interest in the community.
- The experiments conducted are well-designed and demonstrate a clear methodology, with the results showing a clear pattern of task recognition and redundancy in different layers of the model.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and easy to understand, which is crucial for disseminating the findings to a broader audience.
- The proposed method for identifying task-specific layers and pruning attention heads is a novel approach that could potentially enhance inference efficiency and reduce computational costs.
- The findings provide valuable insights into how GPT models perform in-context machine translation, which could be useful for future research and practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the implications of the findings for practical applications, such as how the identified critical layers could be used to improve translation performance or inference efficiency.
- The focus on BLEU score as the primary evaluation metric is questioned, as it may not fully capture the nuances of translation quality, especially in terms of fluency and naturalness.
- The paper's methodology and results are limited to specific models (GPTNeo and Bloom), raising concerns about the generalizability of the findings to other models or datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently explore the impact of different instruction-tuning strategies on the translation performance, which could provide valuable insights into how the models learn from different types of prompts.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential biases in the experimental setup, such as the choice of datasets and the generalizability of the findings across different models and datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into how the identified critical layers could be used to improve translation performance or inference efficiency in practical applications?
- How does the paper's methodology and findings compare to other models or datasets, especially those that might be more representative of real-world translation tasks?
- What are the implications of the findings for the broader community, particularly in terms of how they could be applied to improve translation systems or enhance our understanding of GPT models?
- How does the paper address the potential biases or limitations in the experimental setup, such as the choice of datasets or the generalizability of the findings to other models or datasets?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential impact of different instruction-tuning strategies on the translation performance, and how this might affect the model's ability to learn from different types of prompts?
