 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for computing path gradients in normalizing flows, enhancing the training efficiency by reducing the number of forward passes required. The authors propose a recursive method to compute these gradients, which significantly reduces the computational overhead compared to existing methods. This approach is applicable to both forward and reverse KL training, improving the efficiency of maximum likelihood training. The method is demonstrated to be faster and more efficient than previous methods, with numerical experiments showing its effectiveness across various normalizing flow architectures. The paper also discusses the application of these gradients in forward KL training, which is typically more challenging due to the need for reweighting.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the method and its application.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with a focus on reducing the number of forward passes needed for path gradient estimation, which is crucial for improving training efficiency.
- The paper demonstrates the method's applicability across various normalizing flow architectures, including both implicitly and explicitly invertible flows, and shows that it can be applied to forward KL training, which is typically more challenging.
- The numerical experiments provided are robust, showing the method's effectiveness in reducing variance and improving training efficiency compared to standard gradient estimators.
- The paper provides a comprehensive overview of normalizing flows and their training methods, which is beneficial for readers unfamiliar with the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational overhead of the proposed method, particularly the time and memory complexity, which could be crucial for practical implementation.
- There is a potential issue with the accuracy of the method when applied to implicitly invertible flows, as the numerical inversion used in these cases might not be as accurate as closed-form inversion.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the practical implications of the reduced number of forward passes.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in scenarios where the method might not be as effective or where it could potentially introduce new challenges or issues.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed experimental setup and results, including a broader range of benchmarks and a more detailed analysis of the method's performance under different conditions.
