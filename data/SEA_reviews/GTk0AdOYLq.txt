 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DiffAR, a novel autoregressive diffusion model for generating raw speech waveforms, conditioned on linguistic and phonological units. It extends the DiffWave model by incorporating an autoregressive approach, allowing for the generation of arbitrary length waveforms. The model's architecture includes a duration predictor and energy predictor, which are trained on the LJ Speech dataset. The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments, including ablation studies, and compares the model with existing models like WaveGrad 2 and FastSpeech 2. Despite the innovative approach and the potential for generating high-quality, diverse speech, the paper is criticized for its limited evaluation scope, lack of comparison with state-of-the-art models, and inadequate discussion on the novelty and impact of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the model's architecture and training details.
- The proposed autoregressive diffusion model for generating raw audio waveforms is innovative and allows for the generation of arbitrary length waveforms, which is a significant advantage over previous models.
- The model is conditioned on linguistic and phonological units, which is a novel approach in the field of text-to-speech (TTS) synthesis.
- The paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments, including ablation studies, which help in understanding the model's components and their impact on performance.
- The model's ability to generate high-quality, diverse speech is highlighted, with examples demonstrating the model's potential to generate natural-sounding speech with vocal fry phonation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation of the model is limited to a single dataset (LJ Speech), which may not adequately demonstrate the model's generalization capabilities across different datasets and speakers.
- There is a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art models such as Voicebox, VALL-E, and other recent diffusion-based TTS models, which makes it difficult to assess the model's relative performance.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the novelty and impact of the proposed method, particularly in relation to existing models like DiffWave and other autoregressive TTS models.
- The model's performance is not evaluated in terms of synthesis time, which is a critical metric for practical applications.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as the long synthesis time and the lack of comparison with other autoregressive TTS models.
- The paper's claims regarding the model's ability to generate "unlimited" waveform durations are misleading, as the model still has practical limits on the duration of the generated waveforms.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and inference procedures, particularly how the model handles long-range dependencies in the autoregressive approach?
- How does the model perform when conditioned on different types of linguistic and phonological units, such as grapheme-to-phoneme (G2P) units or raw text?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the energy predictor in the model, particularly how it affects the model's performance and the generated speech's naturalness?
- How does the model handle the variability in the generated speech, and what are the implications of this variability for the model's performance and practical applications?
- Could the authors provide more details on the model's performance on longer audio sequences and how it compares to other models in terms of synthesis time and resource usage?
