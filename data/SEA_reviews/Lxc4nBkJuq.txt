 **Summary:**
The paper explores the robustness of diffusion models against adversarial perturbations, focusing on the impact of randomness in the forward and reverse processes. It introduces a theoretical framework that explains how randomness in diffusion models can lead to gradient masking, which might mislead about the model's robustness. The authors propose a method called "randomness replay" to mitigate this issue, demonstrating its effectiveness through experiments on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet datasets. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the design of more robust diffusion models and the evaluation of adversarial robustness in general.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem in the field of adversarial robustness, focusing on the impact of randomness in diffusion models.
- The theoretical analysis is well-presented, providing a clear explanation of the mechanisms behind gradient masking and its impact on robustness evaluations.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and support the theoretical claims, showing that the proposed "randomness replay" method can improve robustness estimates.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The findings have implications for the broader research community, as they could inform the design of more robust diffusion models and the evaluation of adversarial robustness in general.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implementation of "randomness replay" and its implications for the computational cost and efficiency of adversarial robustness evaluations.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the specifics of the adversarial attack used in the experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The paper's theoretical framework and experimental results are somewhat disconnected, which might lead to confusion about the practical applicability of the theoretical insights.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets (CIFAR-10 and ImageNet), which might not generalize well to other datasets or models, limiting the scope of the findings.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of adversarial attacks, such as those that are not gradient-based?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the adversarial perturbations were generated and applied?
- Given the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, how robust are the findings to different model architectures and dataset characteristics?
- Could you discuss the potential implications of your findings for the broader research community, particularly in terms of how they might inform the design of more robust diffusion models?
