 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FaRULi, a novel framework for unsupervised lifelong learning aimed at maintaining fairness across a sequence of tasks. FaRULi employs an over-complete elastic model to optimize task order, mitigating negative model reuse and ensuring fairness. It leverages a shared latent space to map instances from both learned and incoming tasks, allowing for the transfer of knowledge from one task to another. The framework is supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results, demonstrating its effectiveness in various real-world datasets. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticisms regarding the complexity of the model, the lack of clarity in certain methodological descriptions, and the need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to unsupervised lifelong learning, focusing on fairness across a sequence of tasks, which is a significant and under-explored area in machine learning.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust and supports the proposed method, enhancing the paper's credibility.
- The empirical results are strong, showing that the FaRULi method outperforms other baselines in terms of both accuracy and fairness.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the inclusion of a detailed theoretical analysis adds depth to the research.
- The authors provide code for reproducibility, which is a commendable effort towards transparency and replicability in research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in certain methodological descriptions, particularly in Section 2.2, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.
- The complexity of the proposed model is high, which might make it difficult to implement and understand for practitioners.
- The evaluation section could be improved by including more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, especially those that address similar issues in fair representation learning.
- There is a need for more detailed discussions on the limitations of the proposed method, including scenarios where it might not perform optimally or where it might introduce biases.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the trade-offs between fairness and accuracy, especially in relation to the hyperparameters used.
- The assumption of similarity in fair representation across tasks is strong and not adequately justified, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between the shared latent space and the fairness constraints, particularly how these constraints are enforced in the model?
- How does the FaRULi method handle the trade-off between fairness and accuracy, especially in scenarios where these goals conflict?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the model's complexity and its implications for practical implementation?
- How does the FaRULi method ensure that the fairness constraints are not overly restrictive, potentially leading to a model that is too conservative in its predictions?
- Could the authors discuss the implications of the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the assumption of similar fair representation across tasks?
- How does the FaRULi method compare to other methods that address similar issues in fair representation learning, especially in terms of fairness metrics and model performance?
