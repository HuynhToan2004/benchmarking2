 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the trade-off between uncertainty and perception in image restoration, employing information-theoretic tools to quantify this relationship. The authors introduce a new uncertainty-perception (UP) function, which is shown to be a quasi-linear function of the perceptual quality. The paper also explores the implications of this relationship on the distortion-perception trade-off, suggesting that high perceptual quality is inversely proportional to low uncertainty. This relationship is supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results, demonstrating the practical relevance of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the proposed uncertainty-perception (UP) function and its implications.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the UP function, including both local and global bounds, which helps in understanding the feasible region of the tradeoff.
- The theoretical results are supported by empirical findings, demonstrating the practical relevance of the theoretical insights.
- The paper introduces a novel perspective on the distortion-perception tradeoff by integrating uncertainty, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The use of information-theoretic tools to quantify uncertainty in image restoration tasks is a novel and interesting approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of restoration tasks and the generalizability of the results.
- The choice of Renyi divergence as the measure of perception is not justified, and the paper does not explore how the results might vary with different choices of divergence measures.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing works on distortion-uncertainty tradeoffs, particularly in terms of the novelty and depth of the contributions.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to specific assumptions and divergence measures, which may not generalize well to other types of restoration tasks or divergence measures.
- There is a lack of experimental validation for some of the theoretical claims, particularly in high-dimensional settings, which could undermine the robustness of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on the choice of Renyi divergence as the measure of perception and discuss how the results might vary with different choices of divergence measures?
- How do the assumptions and divergence measures used in the theoretical analysis affect the generalizability of the findings to other types of restoration tasks?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the Kozachenko-Leonenko estimator was applied in the context of image restoration?
- How does the paper's analysis of uncertainty in image restoration tasks compare to similar studies in other domains, such as time-series analysis?
- Could you clarify the discrepancy between the theoretical results and the empirical observations in Figure 5, particularly the apparent lack of correlation between perception and uncertainty in certain models?
- How do the authors address the limitations of their method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of restoration tasks and the generalizability of the results?
