 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to solving multi-player general-sum games by utilizing diversity measures to enhance the exploration of policy space, leading to the development of a new equilibrium concept called Diverse (Coarse) Correlated Equilibrium (D(C)CE). The paper proposes a policy training algorithm, Diverse Correlated Oracle (DCO), which integrates this concept into a new equilibrium solving method. The approach is tested against existing methods such as JPSRO and α-Rank, demonstrating improvements in performance. The paper also discusses the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications in large-scale games.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear structure and well-defined notations.
- The concept of diversity is well-explained, and the authors effectively leverage this concept to enhance the exploration of policy space, leading to the development of a new equilibrium concept, D(C)CE.
- The proposed DCO algorithm is novel and computationally efficient, which is crucial for practical applications in large-scale games.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the existence and uniqueness of the equilibrium, and the derivation of a closed-form solution.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it outperforms existing methods such as JPSRO and α-Rank.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of scalability and computational efficiency.
- The definition and use of diversity measures are not clearly defined or justified, and the choice of diversity measures seems arbitrary.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some figures showing significant gaps in performance between different methods, and the absence of error bars which could affect the interpretation of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other existing methods, such as those based on Nash equilibrium or other equilibrium concepts.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the number of players and policies.
- There are some typographical errors and inconsistencies in the paper that need to be corrected.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of scalability and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods, such as those based on Nash equilibrium or other equilibrium concepts?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of diversity measures and provide a more detailed justification for their use?
- In terms of the experimental results, can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the performance gaps observed between different methods, and discuss the impact of different hyperparameters on the results?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed method scale with the number of players and policies, and what are the practical implications of this complexity in terms of runtime and memory usage?
