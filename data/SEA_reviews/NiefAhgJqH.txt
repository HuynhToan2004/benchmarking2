 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Bayesian Exploration Networks (BEN), a novel model-free Bayesian reinforcement learning (BRL) approach that addresses the challenges of Bayes-optimality and computational complexity in high-dimensional state spaces. BEN models both aleatoric and epistemic uncertainties using normalizing flows, allowing for the estimation of Bayes-optimal policies in complex environments. Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate that BEN can learn Bayes-optimal policies, outperforming existing model-free BRL methods in a challenging search and rescue task. The approach is supported by a rigorous theoretical foundation and empirical evidence, showing that BEN can handle complex environments and is computationally efficient.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for Bayesian exploration networks (BEN), which is a novel approach in Bayesian reinforcement learning (BRL).
- BEN effectively models both aleatoric and epistemic uncertainties using normalizing flows, allowing for the estimation of Bayes-optimal policies in complex environments.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear structure and well-defined notations.
- The empirical results are strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in a challenging search and rescue task, showing that BEN can learn Bayes-optimal policies.
- The theoretical analysis is thorough, highlighting the limitations of existing model-free BRL approaches and providing a comprehensive theoretical framework for BEN.
- The paper addresses significant issues in BRL, such as computational complexity and Bayes-optimality, which are crucial for practical applications in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of BEN, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially in high-dimensional environments.
- There is a need for more extensive empirical evaluations to validate the effectiveness of BEN in a broader range of tasks and environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the assumption of a Gaussian posterior in the variational inference process.
- The experimental setup and results could be improved by including more detailed descriptions of the experimental conditions and comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper could provide more insights into the practical implementation of BEN, including the choice of hyperparameters and the training process.
- There is a need for a clearer explanation of the theoretical underpinnings and the practical implications of the assumptions made in the model.

**Questions:**
- Can BEN be applied to other types of reinforcement learning problems, such as stochastic MDPs?
- How does BEN handle scenarios where the environment is not fully observable, such as in partially observable MDPs (POMDPs)?
- Can BEN be extended to handle continuous action spaces?
- How does BEN compare to other Bayesian exploration methods, such as those based on variational inference or other Bayesian model-based methods?
- What are the computational and memory requirements of BEN, and how do they scale with the dimensionality of the environment?
- How does the performance of BEN compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of sample efficiency and computational efficiency?
- What are the implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, and how do they affect the practical implementation of BEN?
