 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Chain-of-Symbol (CoS) prompting, which represents spatial relationships using symbols in the intermediate steps of the Chain-of-Thought (CoT) process. This approach is designed to enhance the performance of Large Language Models (LLMs) in spatial planning and understanding tasks, particularly in complex virtual environments. The methodology involves manually converting CoT demonstrations into CoS, which is then used to guide LLMs in a few-shot manner. The paper claims that CoS outperforms CoT in various spatial reasoning tasks and reduces the number of tokens required, thus potentially reducing inference costs. However, concerns are raised about the manual nature of the conversion process, the generalizability of the method across different models and tasks, and the limited evaluation scope.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Chain-of-Symbol (CoS) prompting, which converts spatial relationships described in natural languages into condensed symbolic representations, potentially improving the performance of Large Language Models (LLMs) in spatial planning and understanding tasks.
- The method is easy to use and does not require additional training on LLMs, making it accessible and practical for various applications.
- The paper provides extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the CoS method across different tasks and models, showing improvements in performance and a reduction in the number of tokens used.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear examples and detailed descriptions that facilitate understanding and replication of the results.
- The paper addresses an important and understudied problem in the field of spatial reasoning, which is crucial for advancing AI capabilities in complex environments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The methodology relies on a manual conversion process from CoT to CoS, which could limit its scalability and applicability to more complex or varied scenarios.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a few tasks and models, which raises concerns about its generalizability and robustness across different domains and LLM architectures.
- The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the impact of different symbols on the performance of the CoS method, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed approach.
- There is a noticeable absence of discussion on the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant given the increasing use of AI in various domains.
- The paper does not compare the CoS method with other symbolic reasoning methods, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its advantages and limitations.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the manual conversion process from CoT to CoS, including the time and resources required for this process?
- How do you ensure the consistency and reliability of the CoS demonstrations when they are manually converted from CoT?
- How does the CoS method perform when applied to more complex or varied scenarios, such as those involving multiple objects or more intricate spatial relationships?
- Have you considered using automated methods to generate CoS demonstrations from CoT, and if so, how effective have these methods been in reducing the manual effort required?
- Could you discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its application in areas like gaming or virtual environments?
- How does the CoS method compare with other symbolic reasoning methods, and what are the specific advantages or disadvantages of using symbols versus natural language in LLM prompting?
