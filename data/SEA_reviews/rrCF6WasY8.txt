 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel non-interactive blind averaging method for differentially private distributed learning, focusing on blind averaging of models trained on local data by different parties. This method allows for a single round of communication between parties, which is significant in reducing communication overhead. The paper provides theoretical analysis and experimental results demonstrating the method's effectiveness in preserving privacy and achieving utility-privacy trade-offs. The methodology is applied to SVMs and Softmax-activated single layer perception (Softmax-SLP), showing that these models can converge to the centralized learned model in the limit. Despite the novelty and practical implications, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation scope and the need for more extensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel non-interactive blind averaging method for differentially private distributed learning, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method achieves strong utility-privacy tradeoffs, demonstrating a good balance between privacy protection and model utility.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings, making it accessible to readers.
- The experimental results provided in the paper show that the proposed method can achieve competitive utility-privacy tradeoffs compared to centralized learning.
- The paper presents a theoretical analysis that is both solid and insightful, providing a deep understanding of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The authors have demonstrated that the convergence of SVMs to the centralized learned model in the limit is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the proposed method's advantages and limitations.
- The evaluation scope is limited, focusing only on SVMs and Softmax-SLP, which might not adequately demonstrate the method's applicability across different models.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The practicality of the method is questionable, as the assumptions about the number of honest users and the availability of strong pre-training models might not hold in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's experimental results are not convincing, particularly in demonstrating the method's effectiveness across different models and privacy settings.
- The paper's claims about the method's scalability and convergence in the limit are not adequately supported by empirical evidence.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of communication overhead and computational complexity?
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of privacy-utility trade-offs?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how do the authors address these concerns?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different models beyond SVMs and Softmax-SLP?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the performance of the method under different privacy settings and model architectures?
- How does the proposed method ensure convergence in practical scenarios, and what are the implications of this convergence in terms of model accuracy and privacy protection?
