 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a zeroth-order algorithm for private non-convex optimization, specifically focusing on the Renyi Differential Privacy (RDP) setting. It adapts the Online-to-Nonconvex Conversion (O2NC) framework from Cutkosky et al. (2023) to create a zeroth-order algorithm that converts an Online Convex Optimization (OCO) algorithm into a non-convex one, finding a stationary point in non-smooth non-convex optimization. The algorithm leverages a variance-reduced gradient oracle and a tree mechanism to ensure differential privacy, achieving a data complexity rate of O(dδ−1ε−3). The paper also discusses the privacy implications and provides theoretical proofs of the algorithm's effectiveness and privacy guarantees.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of private non-convex optimization, which is crucial for practical applications in machine learning.
- The authors effectively combine existing techniques such as the O2NC framework and the tree mechanism to create a new algorithm that optimizes non-smooth non-convex functions.
- The paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow, and the proofs and theoretical analysis are provided, which are essential for understanding the algorithm's effectiveness and privacy guarantees.
- The paper introduces a new variance-reduced gradient oracle and a tree mechanism to enhance privacy in the zeroth-order optimization setting, which is a novel approach.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed analysis of the algorithm, including the convergence rate and privacy guarantees, which are crucial for the practical application of the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper heavily relies on the O2NC framework and the tree mechanism, which might limit its novelty and the depth of its contribution to the field.
- The paper's focus on zeroth-order optimization, while interesting, might be seen as a limitation due to the potential computational inefficiencies compared to first-order methods.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications for certain technical aspects, such as the use of the tree mechanism and the choice of parameters in the algorithm.
- The paper does not provide experimental results, which could help in validating the theoretical claims and demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the algorithm.
- The paper's notation and definitions, such as the use of the term "zeroth-order" and the definition of the Goldstein δ-subdifferential, could be clearer and more standardized to improve understanding.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition of the Goldstein δ-subdifferential and its relation to the standard subdifferential?
- How does the paper justify the choice of parameters, such as the number of samples used in the algorithm?
- Is there a possibility to apply the algorithm to other privacy models, such as (ε, δ)-DP? If so, how would the results compare?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples for the use of the tree mechanism and its impact on the privacy guarantees?
- How does the paper ensure the privacy of the algorithm when it involves the aggregation of noisy gradients from multiple iterations?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the variance-reduced gradient oracle and its impact on the convergence rate and privacy guarantees?
- Is there a plan to include experimental results in future versions of the paper to validate the theoretical claims?
