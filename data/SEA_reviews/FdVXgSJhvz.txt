 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for automatically filtering instruction-tuning datasets using a prompt-based evaluation by a large language model (LLM). The approach involves rating the quality of each data point based on prompts, which are then used to filter out low-quality data. This method is tested on various datasets including Alpaca, Dolly, and GPT4LLM, demonstrating that the filtered data can lead to better performance in instruction-following tasks. The paper also explores the impact of different filtering thresholds and model sizes, showing that the method can reduce the number of training examples while maintaining or improving performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and methodology that is well-explained.
- The proposed method for automatically filtering instruction-tuning datasets using a prompt-based evaluation by a large language model (LLM) is novel and practical, demonstrating significant computational savings and potentially improving the quality of instruction-following models.
- The experimental results are robust and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can reduce the number of training examples while maintaining or improving performance.
- The paper includes comprehensive evaluations on multiple datasets and benchmarks, as well as human studies and benchmark evaluations, which support the findings and the method's generalizability.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of instruction tuning, focusing on the quality of training data, which is a critical factor in the performance of instruction-tuned models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other existing methods for evaluating instruction-tuning datasets, which could provide a better understanding of the advantages and limitations of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential biases introduced by the LLM used for data filtering, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the threshold for filtering data is determined, which could affect the reproducibility and applicability of the method.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant given the potential for biased data filtering.
- The paper does not include a detailed analysis of the quality of the filtered data, which could help in understanding the effectiveness of the method.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
