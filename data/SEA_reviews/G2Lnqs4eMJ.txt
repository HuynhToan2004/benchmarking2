 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the universal approximation property of neural networks using a combination of super-expressive activation functions and EUAF. It provides a construction of a neural network with O(d) neurons that can approximate any d-variate continuous function on [0,1]^d within arbitrary error in the Euclidean norm. The paper also explores the optimal width of the network and demonstrates that the number of neurons required is proportional to the input dimension d. It relies on the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem and the super-expressive activation functions to achieve these results. The paper is critiqued for its lack of novelty and clear exposition, with reviewers pointing out several typographical and logical errors.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations and examples that aid in understanding the theoretical results.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant question in the field of neural network approximation, specifically focusing on the minimum width of a neural network for universal approximation.
- The proofs provided are simple and elegant, making the paper accessible and understandable.
- The paper includes a comprehensive review of related work, which helps to contextualize the research within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks originality as it primarily relies on existing results from the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem and previous works by Yarotsky and Shen et al. The contributions are seen as incremental rather than groundbreaking.
- There are several typographical and logical errors throughout the paper, which could mislead or confuse readers.
- The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from prior works, particularly in the proofs and theorems presented.
- The paper's main results are not clearly stated or presented in a manner that enhances their impact or novelty.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with existing results and a clearer delineation of its contributions to the field.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences in proofs and results compared to previous works, especially in the context of Theorem 1 and the proofs of theorems 2 and 3?
- What is the significance of Theorem 4, and how does it contribute to the understanding of the paper's main results?
- How does the paper's construction of a neural network with O(d) neurons compare to other known constructions, such as those using Cantor-type functions or other activation functions?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of their results, particularly in terms of the activation functions and network architectures that could achieve the claimed results?
- How do the authors address the concerns regarding the novelty and originality of their contributions, especially in light of the existing literature on neural network approximations?
