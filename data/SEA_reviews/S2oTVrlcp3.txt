 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SmartPlay, a benchmark designed to evaluate the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) in various agent-environment interactions, including games like Tower of Hanoi, Minecraft, and Rock-Paper-Scissors. The benchmark includes a suite of six games, each with unique challenges, and aims to assess the performance of LLMs in tasks such as long-term planning, understanding randomness, and spatial reasoning. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation using different LLMs and compare their performance, highlighting the potential for further research in enhancing LLMs' capabilities as agents.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the performance of various LLMs in different games, which helps in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of these models.
- The benchmark is designed to assess a wide range of skills necessary for intelligent agents, including reasoning, instruction following, and spatial reasoning.
- The paper is well-written and clearly explains the evaluation methodology and the results, making it accessible to readers.
- The inclusion of a benchmark specifically for LLMs as agents is a significant contribution to the field, as it addresses a gap in existing benchmarks.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the results, highlighting the performance of different LLMs and the challenges they face in various games.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the evaluation methodology, particularly how the LLMs were prompted and the specifics of the evaluation process.
- The benchmark's design seems to focus primarily on text-based games, which might not fully represent the capabilities of LLMs in more complex, visually rich environments like Minecraft.
- The paper lacks a clear definition of the capabilities being evaluated and how they are measured, which could lead to confusion about the benchmark's effectiveness.
- There is a noticeable absence of a human baseline in the evaluation, which makes it difficult to compare the LLMs' performance against a standard of human intelligence.
- The benchmark's design might be seen as somewhat limited, as it only includes a few games and does not fully represent the capabilities of LLMs in more complex, real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How were the LLMs prompted to perform in the games, and what specific instructions were given?
- How were the games chosen for the benchmark, and what criteria were used to select them?
- Can you provide more details on how the evaluation metrics were derived and how they were applied in the benchmark?
- How does the benchmark ensure that the LLMs are not using their knowledge of the games from training data to perform well?
- Could you clarify how the benchmark's capabilities are defined and measured, and how these capabilities translate into real-world applications?
- How does the benchmark address the issue of dataset contamination, and what steps are taken to ensure that the LLMs are not simply memorizing solutions from their training data?
