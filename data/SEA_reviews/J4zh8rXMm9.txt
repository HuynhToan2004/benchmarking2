 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the issue of forgetting in federated learning (FL) by introducing a novel metric to measure both local and global forgetting at each round. It proposes a new algorithm called Flashback, which integrates dynamic distillation to mitigate forgetting at both local and global update phases. The algorithm aims to retain essential knowledge across rounds while also assimilating new knowledge, leading to faster convergence to target accuracy compared to existing methods. The experiments are conducted on various datasets, including CIFAR-10, CINIC-10, and FEMNIST, to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the choice of baselines, and the experimental settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and important problem in federated learning (FL) by focusing on the issue of forgetting, which is crucial for improving the efficiency and effectiveness of FL algorithms.
- The proposed method of using dynamic distillation at both the local and global update phases is innovative and could potentially lead to significant improvements in FL systems.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of a new metric for measuring forgetting at each round is a valuable contribution to the field, providing a more granular and nuanced understanding of the forgetting phenomenon in FL.
- The paper demonstrates that the proposed method can achieve faster convergence to target accuracy compared to existing methods, which is a significant advantage in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods such as FedNTD and FedReg, which also address forgetting in FL. The proposed method's novelty and distinct advantages over these methods are not clearly articulated.
- The choice of baselines and experimental settings is not adequately justified, raising concerns about the fairness and relevance of the comparisons made.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets and do not include a broader range of datasets, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational cost and communication overhead of the proposed method, which are critical factors in FL systems.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive discussion on the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the proposed method, which is essential for practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method differentiates from existing methods such as FedNTD and FedReg, and what specific advantages does it offer?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of forgetting in FL, especially in scenarios with high data heterogeneity?
- What are the implications of the hyperparameters used in the proposed method, and how do they affect the performance of the FL system?
- Could the authors provide more detailed discussions on the computational cost and communication overhead of the proposed method, and how these compare to other FL methods?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with different levels of data heterogeneity, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in such scenarios?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other methods, especially those that address forgetting in FL, and discuss the impact of the proposed method in terms of convergence speed and forgetting mitigation?
