 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel activation function, HOSC, which is a hyperbolic oscillation function that incorporates a controllable sharpness parameter, allowing for the modeling of both smooth and sharp features in implicit neural representations (INRs). This activation function, which is a modification of the SIREN architecture, is designed to improve the capture of high-frequency details and is shown to outperform SIREN and ReLU in various tasks such as image and SDF fitting. The authors also propose an adaptive version of the function, AdaHOSC, which optimizes the sharpness parameter during training. Despite the promising results, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental scope, focusing primarily on 2D images and 2D SDFs, and lacking comprehensive comparisons with other activation functions and more complex datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the experiments are well-conducted.
- The proposed activation function, HOSC, is simple, elegant, and effectively models sharp features.
- The adaptive version of the activation function, AdaHOSC, is a notable contribution that optimizes the sharpness parameter during training, enhancing the model's ability to capture high-frequency details.
- The paper provides a clear mathematical motivation for the efficacy of HOSC and discusses its limitations.
- The proposed HOSC is a simple yet effective activation function that can be easily incorporated into any existing method employing a neural network as a way of implicitly representing a signal.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks comprehensive comparisons with other activation functions, particularly those designed to capture high-frequency details such as Gaussian, Swish, and SReLU.
- The experimental results are not convincing enough, and the paper could benefit from more extensive comparisons on different types of data, including gigapixel images, SDFs, and potentially 3D shapes.
- The paper primarily focuses on 2D images and 2D SDFs, which limits the generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the HOSC activation function compares to other recent works like Factor Fields and TensoRF.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the training time or the number of parameters used in the models, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more experimental results and comparisons with other activation functions, especially Gaussian, Swish, and SReLU, to demonstrate the effectiveness of HOSC in capturing high-frequency details?
- How does the HOSC activation function compare to other recent works like Factor Fields and TensoRF in terms of capturing high-frequency details and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of using HOSC in hybrid representations and other architectures to further enhance its capabilities?
- What is the training time and the number of parameters used in the HOSC model compared to other models like SIREN and ReLU?
- How does the HOSC model perform on more complex datasets, such as gigapixel images, SDFs, and potentially 3D shapes?
