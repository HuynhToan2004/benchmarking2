 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a visibility prediction network aimed at enhancing the rendering quality of NeRFs by predicting the visibility of points in the scene from different camera viewpoints. This network is integrated into the NeRF training process, providing a visibility score that can be used to filter out low-visibility points, thus improving rendering quality. The method is validated through experiments on the ObjectScans dataset, showing improvements in rendering quality by skipping unreliable near-range points and selecting additional training images. The paper also explores the application of this network in selecting additional views for NeRF training, which can potentially enhance the model's performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method is simple and effective, with the potential to be applied in various downstream tasks.
- The introduction of a visibility prediction network is novel and addresses a significant issue in NeRFs, namely the prediction of visibility from any point in space from any of the input cameras.
- The method is computationally efficient, reducing the computational burden associated with calculating the visibility of points in the scene.
- The paper provides a clear and intuitive explanation of the proposed method, and the results are promising, showing a significant improvement in rendering quality.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to state-of-the-art techniques.
- The experimental setup and results are not comprehensive enough, with a limited number of datasets used for validation.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability in scenarios with sparse input views or challenging materials like non-Lambertian surfaces.
- The paper does not provide sufficient visual results, which could help in better understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison with existing methods to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to NeRFs trained with a larger number of views, and how does it compare to NeRFs trained with fewer views?
- Can the authors provide more visual results to better illustrate the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios with sparse input views or challenging materials like non-Lambertian surfaces?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential directions for future research?
