 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DAME, a novel model-agnostic, gradient-free method for local model-agnostic explainability, specifically designed to address the limitations of existing methods like LIME and RISE. DAME operates by distilling a black-box model into a student network, generating saliency maps that indicate the importance of different image regions, and then combining these with the black-box model's predictions to generate explanations. The method is evaluated across various tasks including image and audio classification, showing promising results in terms of qualitative and quantitative metrics. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive baseline comparisons, and potential issues with computational efficiency and scalability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed method.
- The methodology is innovative, particularly in its application of distillation-based explainability, which is a novel approach compared to traditional linear approximation methods.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation across multiple tasks, including image and audio classification, demonstrating the versatility of the proposed method.
- The use of a saliency map to explain the importance of different image regions is an intuitive and effective way to provide explanations, which is well-received.
- The method is model-agnostic and gradient-free, making it applicable to a wide range of models without requiring access to their internal workings.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear descriptions of the methodology and results, and includes a detailed appendix that provides additional insights into the method's operation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing baselines, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, which are critical for practical applications.
- There is a significant computational cost associated with the method, which may limit its practicality in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, including its scalability and potential for overfitting to the training data.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed visualizations and explanations of the saliency maps and other outputs, as well as a more comprehensive evaluation of the method's performance on a wider range of datasets and tasks.
- The paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods like LIME, and the lack of a clear differentiation in its approach.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation of the method's effectiveness, particularly in terms of its ability to identify the correct regions of the input that influence the model's predictions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational efficiency and scalability of their method compared to existing baselines?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the black-box model is trained on a different distribution than the test data, and what are the implications of this for the method's effectiveness?
- Could the authors provide more detailed visualizations and explanations of the saliency maps and other outputs to help clarify the method's operation?
- How does the method ensure that the explanations generated are not simply a reflection of the training data, and what steps are taken to prevent overfitting?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their method, particularly in terms of its application in fields like healthcare or finance?
- How does the method perform on other types of data, such as text or time series data, and what are the implications of this for its broader applicability?
