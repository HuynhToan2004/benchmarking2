 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, DIRE, for estimating individual treatment effects in scenarios where the covariate distribution varies significantly between treatment groups. This method leverages a disentangled latent representation to estimate a balanced prognostic score, which is crucial for handling limited overlap issues. Theoretical analysis and empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of DIRE in outperforming existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, the clarity of presentation, and the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of causal inference, focusing on the estimation of individual treatment effects in scenarios with limited overlap.
- The proposed method, DIRE, is well-motivated and supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results, showing superior performance over existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a logical flow that aids in understanding the complex concepts and methodologies.
- The use of a disentangled representation to address the limited overlap problem is innovative and potentially impactful, as it allows for the estimation of treatment effects in settings where traditional methods may struggle.
- The experimental setup is robust, with comprehensive evaluations across different datasets and settings, which helps to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly Assumption 4.4, are not clearly justified or explained, which could limit the generalizability and applicability of the results.
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key terms and concepts, which could hinder the reader's understanding.
- There are several minor typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected to enhance the professional presentation of the paper.
- The paper's contribution is somewhat limited by its reliance on existing methodologies and its focus on specific types of data, which may not generalize well to more diverse or complex settings.
- The paper's experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse datasets and scenarios, and by providing more detailed information on the data generation processes and model configurations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly Assumption 4.4, and provide more detailed justification or examples to support these assumptions?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to datasets with different types of covariates or treatment structures? Are there specific scenarios where the method might not be effective?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the key terms and concepts used in the paper, particularly those related to causal inference and latent variable models?
- How do the authors address the issue of identifiability in their model, especially in light of the assumptions made about the injectivity of certain mappings?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results and comparisons for the IHDP dataset, including both within-sample and out-of-sample evaluations?
- What are the specific advantages of using a balanced prognostic score over other types of scores, and how does this approach compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
