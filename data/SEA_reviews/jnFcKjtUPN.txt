 **Summary:**
The paper introduces COPlanner, a novel model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) framework designed to address the issue of inaccurately learned dynamics models by employing a conservative model rollout strategy and an optimistic environment exploration. COPlanner leverages an uncertainty-aware policy-guided model predictive control (UP-MPC) component to plan for multi-step uncertainty estimation, which is used as a penalty during model rollouts and as a bonus during real environment exploration. This approach aims to avoid model uncertain regions through conservative model rollouts and explore high-reward model uncertain regions to reduce model error. The paper presents experiments across various continuous control tasks, demonstrating improved sample efficiency and performance compared to existing MBRL methods. However, concerns were raised regarding the clarity of the paper's structure, the presentation of results, and the novelty of the approach compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear and concise explanation of the proposed framework and its components.
- The framework is novel, particularly the combination of conservative model rollouts and optimistic environment exploration, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework across various continuous control tasks, showing significant improvements in sample efficiency and performance.
- The use of uncertainty-aware policy-guided model predictive control (UP-MPC) is innovative and helps in planning for multi-step uncertainty estimation, which is crucial for model-based reinforcement learning.
- The paper is structured well, with clear explanations of the framework and its components, and includes detailed explanations of the experiments and their results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework, which could help in understanding the scenarios where it might not perform optimally.
- The results presented in some figures (e.g., Figure 1) are not clearly explained, making it difficult to understand the implications of the results.
- There is a significant computational cost associated with the framework, which could limit its practical application in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed framework with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and sample efficiency.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant new insights or contributions.
- The paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in the presentation of figures and the explanation of certain components and experiments.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework and scenarios where it might not perform optimally?
- How does the proposed framework compare in terms of computational efficiency and sample efficiency with other existing methods, particularly in more complex environments?
- Can the authors clarify the computational cost of the framework and discuss potential methods to reduce this cost without significantly impacting performance?
- How does the framework handle different types of model errors, and what are the implications of these errors on the performance of the framework?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the results presented in Figure 1, particularly the differences in performance between different tasks?
- How does the framework handle the trade-off between exploration and exploitation, especially in environments where the model uncertainty is high?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending the framework to include other types of exploration strategies, such as intrinsic rewards or direct exploration policies?
