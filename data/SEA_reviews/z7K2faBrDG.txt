 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the perceptual scaling of stimuli using a probabilistic framework, specifically focusing on the relationship between the Fisher information of stimuli and their perceived similarity. The authors employ a difference scaling method to measure perceptual scales, comparing them to the Fisher information of stimuli. They propose a novel method for estimating perceptual scales, particularly in the context of interpolating between different textures, which is tested against empirical data. The paper also discusses the theoretical implications of these findings, suggesting that the Fisher information of stimuli can predict perceptual scales. However, the paper is critiqued for its lack of clarity in presentation, insufficient experimental validation, and unclear theoretical contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents an interesting and novel approach to measuring perceptual scales using a difference scaling method, which is supported by theoretical analysis.
- The integration of Fisher information into the study of perceptual scales provides a new perspective on how stimuli are perceived.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and precise mathematical definitions and proofs, making the content accessible and understandable.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in estimating perceptual scales.
- The study is significant as it contributes to the understanding of how stimuli are perceived, potentially enhancing the design of perceptual distances and image quality metrics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and concise presentation, making it difficult for readers to follow the main ideas and contributions.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods and models, which limits the evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness and novelty.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, with only a few participants tested, which may not adequately represent the population.
- The theoretical contributions are not clearly articulated, with some sections appearing more like a literature review than a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the study, such as the generalizability of the findings to other types of stimuli or the assumptions made in the theoretical models.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that detract from the professional quality of the manuscript.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the main contributions of the paper and provide a clearer presentation of the content to enhance reader comprehension?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of accuracy and efficiency in estimating perceptual scales?
- What are the implications of the findings for the design of perceptual distances and image quality metrics?
- Can the authors elaborate on the theoretical contributions and how they advance the understanding of perceptual scales and their relationship to Fisher information?
- How do the assumptions made in the theoretical models affect the validity of the findings, and what are the limitations of these assumptions?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the number of participants and the statistical analysis used to ensure the robustness of the results?
