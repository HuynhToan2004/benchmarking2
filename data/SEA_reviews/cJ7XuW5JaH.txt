 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel label recovery attack in federated learning (FL) by utilizing the posterior probabilities of a target batch, derived from an auxiliary dataset, to recover labels from gradients. The authors leverage the focal loss function to establish a relationship between gradients, labels, and posterior probabilities, explaining the essence of label leakage from gradients. The attack's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on various datasets and models, showcasing high class-level and instance-level label accuracy. However, concerns were raised regarding the practicality of the attack, particularly its reliance on an auxiliary dataset with the same distribution as the training data, which might not be readily available in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to label recovery in federated learning (FL) by estimating the posterior probabilities of the target batch from an auxiliary dataset, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is evaluated on a variety of datasets, models, and activation functions, demonstrating its effectiveness and versatility.
- The authors provide a theoretical foundation for their approach, which is crucial for understanding the underlying principles and potential applications of the method.
- The paper includes a comprehensive discussion on the related work, providing a thorough review of existing literature and positioning the proposed method within the context of current research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practicality of the attack, particularly the availability and accessibility of an auxiliary dataset with the same distribution as the training data.
- The paper's experimental setup is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on small-scale datasets and models, which might not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in more complex or realistic scenarios.
- There is a need for more rigorous evaluation metrics, such as statistical significance testing, to support the claims of superior performance over existing methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity or scalability of the proposed method, which could limit its applicability in larger or more complex FL settings.
- The paper's reliance on the focal loss function might limit its applicability to other loss functions, which could be a significant limitation in broader FL applications.
- The paper's experimental setup and results could benefit from a more detailed analysis, particularly concerning the impact of different hyperparameters and model configurations on the attack's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the practicality of the attack, specifically how an auxiliary dataset with the same distribution as the training data might be obtained in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform in larger or more complex FL settings, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in scaling the method to such environments?
- Could the authors discuss the computational complexity of their method and compare it with existing methods to assess its efficiency and scalability?
- How does the proposed method perform with other loss functions beyond focal loss, and what are the implications of this limitation on the broader applicability of the method?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including statistical significance testing and a more thorough analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters and model configurations on the attack's effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of efficiency and effectiveness with other state-of-the-art methods in FL, particularly in terms of computational resources and scalability?
