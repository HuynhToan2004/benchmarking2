 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for active domain adaptation in medical imaging, focusing on the extraction of domain-specific and task-specific features using a feature disentanglement network. The method employs an autoencoder to separate features and selects informative samples based on criteria such as uncertainty, domainness, density, and novelty. The experiments were conducted on two medical imaging datasets, showing some improvements over baseline methods. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of novelty, as it resembles existing methods and does not sufficiently compare with state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods. The method's effectiveness is also questioned due to the limited datasets used and the absence of comprehensive ablation studies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem of active domain adaptation in medical imaging, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the proposed feature disentanglement approach and the selection criteria for informative samples.
- The experiments are conducted on two medical imaging datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in identifying informative samples across different domains.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodologies and their applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it primarily combines existing methods without significant innovation.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with limited datasets used and insufficient comparisons with state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There is a lack of comprehensive ablation studies, which are necessary to validate the effectiveness of the proposed components.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the hyperparameter settings and their impact on the results.
- The method's generalizability to other medical imaging tasks and its scalability to larger datasets are not adequately addressed.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods, particularly in terms of performance and methodological contributions?
- How do the authors address the generalizability of the proposed method to other medical imaging tasks and its scalability to larger datasets?
- What are the specific advantages of using the proposed method over existing domain adaptation methods, especially in terms of performance and practical applicability?
- Could the authors elaborate on the hyperparameter settings and their impact on the results, possibly including a sensitivity analysis or a more detailed discussion on the selection process?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for domain-specific features to be less informative, especially in scenarios where the source and target domains differ significantly?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the selection criteria for the source and target datasets, and the specifics of the active learning process?
