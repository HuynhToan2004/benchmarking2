 **Summary:**
The paper explores the capability of large language models (LLMs) in backward reasoning tasks, specifically in math word problems (MWPs). The authors introduce a novel task where given a question and an answer, the goal is to infer the missing numerical quantity in the question. To address the challenge of LLMs in backward reasoning, the paper proposes several methods: rephrasing, using program-aided language models (PALMs) with external solvers, and iterative prompting with verification. The research demonstrates that these methods can enhance LLM performance, though the improvements are modest. The paper also discusses the limitations and potential future research directions, including the need for more diverse and realistic datasets and a deeper exploration of the fundamental causes of LLM's backward reasoning difficulties.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic of improving the reasoning capability of large language models (LLMs) in math word problems (MWPs), which is a significant challenge in the field.
- The authors propose several innovative techniques such as rephrasing, using program-aided language models (PALMs) with external solvers, and iterative prompting with verification, which are well-motivated and demonstrated to enhance LLM performance.
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the problem, proposed solutions, and experimental results, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.
- The experimental design is thorough, including the creation of new datasets, and the results are supported by a robust analysis, providing valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of the proposed methods.
- The paper introduces a novel task of backward reasoning in math word problems, which is a significant contribution to the field, and provides a detailed analysis of the performance of LLMs on this task, showing that it is challenging for current models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more diverse and realistic datasets to better reflect real-world scenarios and to test the generalizability of the proposed methods.
- The performance gains from the proposed methods are modest, and the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the improvements are statistically significant.
- There is a lack of discussion on the fundamental causes of LLM's backward reasoning difficulties, which could limit the depth of understanding and the development of more effective solutions.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation, including a broader range of models and datasets, to validate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's focus on elementary arithmetic problems might limit the applicability of the findings to more complex or varied mathematical reasoning tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of the proposed methods, which could be significant in educational or assessment contexts.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples of how the proposed techniques, such as rephrasing and PALMs, specifically enhance LLM performance in backward reasoning tasks?
- How do the authors ensure that the improvements in performance are statistically significant, and what steps could be taken to validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods more robustly?
- Given the modest performance gains observed, what are the key challenges that remain in enabling LLMs to perform well in backward reasoning tasks, and how might these be addressed in future research?
- How do the authors envision the application of their findings in real-world scenarios, such as educational or assessment contexts, and what are the potential implications and limitations of using LLMs for math reasoning in these contexts?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the proposed methods to other types of mathematical reasoning tasks, such as geometry or algebra, and how these might be adapted or modified to address the unique challenges posed by these domains?
