 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TrendDiff, a novel method aimed at identifying and distinguishing between intrinsic and measurement trends in time series data, which is crucial for causal discovery. The methodology involves a two-step process: first, identifying variables with trends, and then differentiating between intrinsic and measurement trends using structural characteristics. The paper presents both synthetic and real-world data experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of TrendDiff. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in definitions and assumptions, especially concerning the distinction between intrinsic and measurement trends, and the absence of a theoretical guarantee for the method's correctness. The paper also lacks comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods and a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of time series data analysis, specifically the identification of intrinsic and measurement trends, which is crucial for causal discovery.
- The proposed method, TrendDiff, is innovative and provides a novel approach to the problem, supported by both synthetic and real-world data experiments.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the problem and the proposed solution.
- The methodology is supported by theoretical analysis, and the paper includes a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, comparing it with existing methods.
- The paper discusses the limitations of the proposed method, which is commendable for transparency and credibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and differentiation between intrinsic and measurement trends, which are crucial for the validity of the method.
- The assumption of pseudo causal sufficiency is not well-justified, and its impact on the correctness of the method is not discussed.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed approach, which is essential for practical applications.
- There is a lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which limits the evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness and efficiency.
- The paper lacks a theoretical guarantee for the method's correctness, which is crucial for the reliability of the results.
- The method's assumptions, such as the independence of error terms, are not adequately justified, which could limit the applicability of the method in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the method, such as the specific algorithms used and their computational requirements.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and differentiation between intrinsic and measurement trends, and provide a theoretical justification for their distinction?
- How does the proposed method handle variables with both intrinsic and measurement trends?
- What is the computational complexity of the proposed method, and how does it scale with the size of the dataset and the number of variables?
- How does the method handle nonlinear trends, and what are the assumptions made regarding the nature of the trends?
- Can the authors provide a detailed discussion on the assumptions made in the method, particularly the assumption of pseudo causal sufficiency and the independence of error terms?
- How does the method perform in scenarios with multiple variables, and what are the implications for scalability and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide a theoretical guarantee for the correctness of the method, and discuss the implications of violating the assumptions made in the method?
