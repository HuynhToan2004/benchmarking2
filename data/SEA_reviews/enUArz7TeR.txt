 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Offline Decoupled Prioritized Resampling (ODPR), a method designed to enhance the performance of offline reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms by prioritizing transitions based on their action quality. ODPR utilizes two priority functions, ODPR-A and ODPR-R, which differ in their computational requirements and data availability. ODPR-A requires a value network to estimate advantages, while ODPR-R uses trajectory returns for priority calculation. The method is evaluated across various offline RL algorithms and datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving performance. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as the concept of prioritized resampling is not new, and the theoretical justification for the method's effectiveness is questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined problem.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and easy to understand, with a clear explanation of the algorithm and its implementation.
- The method is evaluated on a wide range of tasks and algorithms, demonstrating its effectiveness and general applicability.
- The paper includes a thorough ablation study that explores the impact of various components of the method, providing valuable insights into its performance.
- The use of decoupled resampling in the method is highlighted as a key innovation that allows for efficient handling of data prioritization.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited as it primarily builds on existing ideas such as prioritized experience replay and advantage weighting, which have been extensively studied.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other offline RL algorithms, particularly those that address the distributional shift problem effectively.
- The theoretical justification for the method's effectiveness is insufficient, with no clear proof or empirical evidence provided to support the claims made.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational costs associated with the method, which could be significant due to the need for a value network in ODPR-A.
- The method's generalizability is questionable, as it may not be applicable to all offline RL scenarios, especially those involving complex data distributions or where trajectory data is not available.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other offline RL algorithms that address the distributional shift problem, such as CQL and IQL?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational costs associated with the method, including the time required for training and the impact of hyperparameters on performance?
- Is there a theoretical or empirical justification for the choice of the priority function used in the method?
- How does the method handle the potential for biased estimation of priority weights, especially in scenarios where the behavior policy is not well-defined or varies significantly across different data points?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the behavior policy in the method and how it is estimated or defined in practical applications?
