 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ODEFormer, a novel transformer-based model designed to infer multidimensional ordinary differential equations (ODEs) from noisy, irregularly sampled observations. It leverages a large-scale pre-training dataset, ODEBench, to enhance its performance and robustness. The model's transformer architecture processes trajectory data and generates symbolic expressions for ODEs, outperforming existing methods in terms of accuracy and inference speed. Despite its innovative approach, the paper acknowledges limitations such as the handling of higher-order ODEs and the lack of identifiability guarantees. The method's scalability and practical applicability, particularly in real-world scenarios, remain open questions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive review of related work that effectively positions the study within the current research landscape.
- The introduction of a novel dataset, ODEBench, for training and evaluating the proposed model, ODEFormer, is a significant contribution to the field.
- The use of a transformer-based model for dynamical system identification from noisy, irregularly sampled trajectory data is innovative and shows promise in handling complex systems.
- The paper presents a detailed description of the data generation process, which aids in understanding the methodology and the model's performance.
- The experimental results demonstrate the superiority of ODEFormer over existing methods in terms of accuracy and inference speed, highlighting its potential impact on the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning its scalability and practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the model's identifiability, which is crucial for understanding its reliability and accuracy in inferring ODEs.
- There is a notable absence of a discussion on the computational complexity of the model, which could limit its scalability to more complex systems.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of chaotic systems, which are known to be challenging for dynamical system identification methods.
- The paper's reliance on a pre-training dataset raises concerns about the model's generalizability to unseen systems, especially in terms of its ability to handle different initial conditions and system parameters.
- The paper does not discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant given its potential applications in various fields.

**Questions:**
- How does the model handle higher-order ODEs, and what are the implications for its scalability and performance?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the identifiability of the model, particularly in terms of its ability to uniquely identify ODEs from noisy data?
- What are the computational costs associated with training and inference using ODEFormer, and how do these compare to other methods in terms of efficiency?
- How does the model perform when applied to chaotic systems, and what are the implications for its practical applicability in such scenarios?
- Can the authors elaborate on the decision-making process behind the choice of hyperparameters, such as the number of binary operators and the depth of unary operators?
- How does the model handle the issue of masked variables, and what are the implications for its ability to infer ODEs accurately when some variables are unobserved?
