 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Subspace Grid-sweep, designed to evaluate the robustness of machine learning models against adversarial attacks. This method operates by exhaustively searching for adversarial regions in a lower-dimensional subspace, which is less computationally intensive than traditional methods. The authors demonstrate that deterministic defenses can be as effective as randomized ones, suggesting that randomness might not be crucial for robustness. They also show that some defenses, such as k-Winners-Take-All, may not be robust due to gradient obfuscation. The paper's contributions include a new evaluation tool and insights into the effectiveness of deterministic defenses, which could potentially reduce the reliance on randomness in defense mechanisms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for evaluating the robustness of machine learning models against adversarial attacks, which is both original and innovative.
- The method is well-explained, easy to follow, and the experiments are clearly described, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.
- The paper provides a new perspective on the role of randomness in machine learning defenses, showing that deterministic defenses can be as effective as randomized ones.
- The paper's findings challenge the traditional understanding of the need for randomness in machine learning defenses, potentially reducing the computational complexity and improving the reliability of defense mechanisms.
- The paper is well-written and the experiments are well-conducted, with a clear demonstration of the effectiveness of the proposed method in identifying obfuscated gradients.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claims about the effectiveness of deterministic defenses are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which are limited in scope and depth.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method against a variety of attacks and defenses, which could undermine the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's focus on a single attack (PGD) and a limited set of defenses (k-Winners-Take-All and a few others) may not adequately demonstrate the robustness of the proposed method across different scenarios.
- The paper's experimental setup and results, such as the effectiveness of grid-sweep against k-Winners-Take-All and the robustness of deterministic defenses, are not convincingly argued or supported by the data presented.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough theoretical analysis and a broader range of experimental validations to strengthen its claims and enhance its impact.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies in the results between the proposed method and existing defenses, particularly in terms of the robustness against different types of attacks?
- How does the proposed method perform against other types of attacks beyond PGD, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the findings?
- What are the implications of using different attack algorithms, such as AutoAttack, in the evaluation of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness in identifying obfuscated gradients?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results and comparisons, especially in the appendix, to support the claims made in the paper?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of randomness in machine learning defenses, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the findings?
