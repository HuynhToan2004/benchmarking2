 **Summary:**
The paper explores the federated domain generalization problem, focusing on the challenges of training models that perform well across different domains in a federated learning setting. It introduces a new benchmark methodology that allows for the control of client heterogeneity and evaluates various federated domain generalization algorithms on diverse datasets. The authors propose a new data partitioning method and a benchmark that includes 7 datasets, comparing it with other federated learning methods. The study aims to provide insights into the current state of federated domain generalization and identify gaps and opportunities for future research.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with a clear structure and detailed analysis, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The introduction of a new benchmark methodology for federated domain generalization (FDG) is a significant contribution to the field, as it allows for the evaluation of various FDG algorithms under controlled conditions.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of 13 federated domain generalization methods on 7 diverse datasets, offering a robust comparison of existing methods and identifying gaps in current research.
- The proposed data partition method is innovative and provides a novel approach to distributing data across clients, which is crucial for federated learning applications.
- The paper is reproducible, with code provided for replication of the experiments, ensuring the reliability and transparency of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the privacy implications of the data partition method, which is crucial for federated learning applications.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it primarily builds upon existing data partitioning methods without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other benchmarks, particularly those that consider federated learning challenges, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The methodology's complexity, particularly in the data partitioning process, may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where data heterogeneity is not as controlled.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method, which could limit its applicability in larger, more complex federated learning environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and a clearer explanation of the experimental setup, including the choice of datasets and hyperparameters.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the privacy implications of the data partition method and discuss potential mitigation strategies?
- How does the proposed method compare with other benchmarks that consider both domain generalization and federated learning challenges?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing data partitioning methods, particularly in terms of performance and applicability?
- How does the method handle data heterogeneity in real-world scenarios where data distributions may not be as controlled?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with other existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and scalability?
- How does the method perform under different hyperparameter settings, and what are the optimal settings for achieving the best results?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of the proposed method on the convergence and generalization capabilities of federated learning models?
