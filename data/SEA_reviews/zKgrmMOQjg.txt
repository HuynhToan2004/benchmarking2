 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel text image change detection (TCD) model designed for multilingual document comparison. The model operates at the character level, utilizing a unit-level text image-to-image comparison without relying on OCR, which simplifies the process by not requiring language-specific models. The model employs a Siamese network architecture with cross-attention and self-attention mechanisms to enhance feature extraction and alignment, alongside a correlation map and marginalization to reduce computational complexity. The TCD model is evaluated against various benchmarks and existing methods, demonstrating competitive performance. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and insufficient justification for certain design choices.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to text image change detection, focusing on character-level segmentation without the need for text recognition, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The use of a Siamese network architecture with cross-attention and self-attention mechanisms effectively enhances feature extraction and alignment, contributing to the model's effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive ablation study that demonstrates the effectiveness of each component of the proposed method.
- The model is capable of handling multilingual document comparison, which is a valuable feature for global applications.
- The paper includes a new text image change detection test dataset, which is a significant contribution to the field and enhances the reproducibility of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods in terms of efficiency, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The justification for the use of a Siamese network architecture over other potential architectures is insufficient, and the paper does not adequately explain why this specific architecture was chosen.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the training process, particularly how the model is trained with synthetic data, which could be a critical aspect for reproducibility and understanding the model's effectiveness.
- There are issues with the clarity and organization of the paper, including unclear explanations of key terms and a lack of cohesion in the presentation of the methodology and results.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the model to handle larger documents or different types of text images, which could limit its practical application.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the choice of a Siamese network architecture and provide a more detailed justification for its use?
- How does the proposed model handle the issue of text rotation, and what are the implications of this limitation on the model's performance?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process, particularly how the synthetic data is generated and used to train the model?
- How does the proposed model perform when compared to other methods in terms of efficiency, and what are the specific metrics used to measure efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the dataset used for training and testing, including the number of samples and the specifics of how the data is generated and labeled?
- How does the proposed model handle different types of text images, such as those with varying font sizes or different text styles, and what are the implications of these variations on the model's performance?
