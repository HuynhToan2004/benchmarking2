 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for extracting sensitive information from large language models (LLMs) in federated learning (FL) environments. It employs a two-layer neural network to reconstruct intermediate features from the pooler layer of LLMs, which are then used to enhance the effectiveness of gradient inversion attacks. The method leverages the pooler layer's output to recover the input tokens, which is a departure from traditional methods that rely solely on gradients. The paper is evaluated on various datasets and shows improvements over existing methods. However, it faces criticism for its limited evaluation scope, lack of theoretical analysis, and potential over-reliance on specific model architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for extracting sensitive information from large language models (LLMs) in federated learning (FL) environments, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is clearly described, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable.
- The proposed method is evaluated on various datasets, showing improvements over existing methods, and the experimental results are robust.
- The use of pooler layer outputs for recovering tokens is a novel approach that could potentially enhance the effectiveness of attacks.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the methodology, including the use of a two-layer neural network to reconstruct intermediate features, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation, particularly in terms of the diversity of datasets and models used. It primarily focuses on BERT-based models and a limited set of datasets, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis, which is crucial for understanding the proposed method's efficacy and limitations.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the two-layer neural network used and the hyperparameters employed, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The method's reliance on the pooler layer output may limit its applicability to other models that do not use this architecture, such as RoBERTa.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the potential for detection by the server due to the modified model architecture and the use of additional loss functions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the two-layer neural network used and the hyperparameters employed?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use different model architectures, such as RoBERTa?
- Could the authors provide more theoretical analysis to support the proposed method's efficacy and limitations?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of datasets, such as those with longer sequences or different token distributions?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for detection by the server due to the modified model architecture and the use of additional loss functions?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of tasks, such as those that do not involve text classification?
