 **Summary:**
The paper explores the problem of learning low-dimensional representations of data under the assumption that the downstream prediction task is known. It introduces a game-theoretic framework where one player chooses a representation, and another player selects a prediction task, with the goal of minimizing the regret of the representation player and maximizing the regret of the prediction task player. Theoretical analysis is provided for linear MSE loss and linear representation, and a minimax solution is derived. The paper also proposes an algorithm for general settings and provides numerical experiments to validate the theoretical findings. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues, with complex notation and a lack of detailed explanation in some sections, making it difficult to follow.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in machine learning, focusing on learning feature representations that are efficient for a variety of prediction tasks.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing a comprehensive understanding of the problem, and the paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow.
- The algorithm proposed is innovative, using a minimax framework that is not commonly seen in similar problems, which could potentially lead to significant improvements in learning feature representations.
- The paper includes detailed examples and numerical experiments that support the theoretical findings, enhancing the credibility of the results.
- The introduction of a game-theoretic framework for learning feature representations is a novel approach that could inspire future research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is hard to follow due to complex notation and lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the introduction and algorithm descriptions.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the computational complexity and practical implementation of the proposed algorithm, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately compare its results with existing methods, which could undermine the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper suffers from some inconsistencies and typographical errors that need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.
- The paper's contribution to the field is not clearly articulated, particularly in terms of its novelty and the practical implications of the theoretical results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the notation and provide more detailed explanations in the introduction to make the paper more accessible to readers?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare in terms of computational complexity and practical applicability with existing methods for learning feature representations?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications of the theoretical results?
- How does the paper address the issue of computational complexity, particularly in high-dimensional settings?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, such as those mentioned in related works, to better establish the novelty and effectiveness of their approach?
- Could the authors address the typographical errors and inconsistencies noted in the paper to enhance its professional quality?
