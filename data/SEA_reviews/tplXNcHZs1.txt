 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to Bayesian posterior sampling for linear inverse problems using diffusion models. It establishes a connection between Bayesian filtering and Bayesian posterior sampling, leveraging particle filtering to enhance the method's accuracy and efficiency. The proposed method, termed FPS, is shown to outperform existing methods in various computer vision tasks such as inpainting, super-resolution, and deblurring. Despite its theoretical and empirical strengths, the paper is critiqued for its clarity in explaining the method's connections to existing works and for its computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to Bayesian posterior sampling using diffusion models, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is well-motivated and clearly explained, with a clear connection established between Bayesian filtering and Bayesian posterior sampling.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed method, including a proof of consistency under certain assumptions.
- Empirical results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in various computer vision tasks, demonstrating its practical applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed experimental setup and results, which are convincing and well-documented.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a clearer exposition of the method's connections to existing works, particularly in terms of how it builds upon or diverges from prior research.
- There are some minor issues with clarity and notation, which could be improved to enhance the readability and comprehension of the paper.
- The computational efficiency of the proposed method is a concern, particularly in high-dimensional spaces where particle sizes need to be large to achieve reasonable performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential drawbacks of the proposed method, especially in scenarios where it may not perform as well or where it may not be applicable.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method with increasing particle sizes, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the proposed method builds upon or diverges from existing works, particularly in terms of its theoretical contributions?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the linear inverse problem is non-linear?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential drawbacks of the proposed method, especially in scenarios where it may not perform as well or where it may not be applicable?
- How does the computational efficiency of the proposed method compare to existing methods, particularly in terms of runtime and resource requirements?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the scalability of the method with increasing particle sizes, and how this affects the practical applicability of the method?
