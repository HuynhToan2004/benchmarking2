 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to video-to-video transfer learning using synthetic paired video datasets, which eliminates the need for per-video-per-model fine-tuning. This method adapts the Instruct Pix2Pix paradigm to the video domain, generating paired samples and utilizing a long video sampling correction technique to ensure consistent long videos across batches. The proposed method has been evaluated on the TGVE dataset, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance and outperforming existing methods. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, as it closely resembles previous works, and the method's reliance on synthetic data for training.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to video-to-video transfer learning using synthetic paired video datasets, which eliminates the need for per-video-per-model fine-tuning.
- The proposed method is technically sound, with a clear motivation and effective implementation.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on the TGVE dataset, and the user study results are convincing, showing the method's effectiveness in real-world applications.
- The authors provide a detailed description of the method, including the synthetic paired video dataset, which is a valuable resource for the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it closely resembles previous works, particularly in the use of synthetic data for training and the adaptation of the Instruct Pix2Pix paradigm to the video domain.
- The method's reliance on synthetic data for training might limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where paired training data is not readily available.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where paired training data is not available.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods, such as those that use synthetic data for training, which could undermine the perceived effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method's performance is highly dependent on the quality of the synthetic data, and there is a lack of discussion on how the method would perform with low-quality or noisy synthetic data.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the synthetic paired video dataset, including the process of generating the paired videos and the quality control measures used to ensure the dataset's effectiveness?
- How does the method perform when synthetic data is not available, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in such scenarios?
- Could the authors compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods that use synthetic data for training, such as those mentioned in the review, to better establish the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the synthetic data is of low quality or noisy? What are the potential limitations or challenges in such scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more details on the method's performance with different types of synthetic data, such as those generated by different models or with different training parameters?
