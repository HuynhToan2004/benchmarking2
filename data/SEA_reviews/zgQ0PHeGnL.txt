 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ElliDock, a novel method for rigid protein-protein docking that predicts elliptic paraboloid interfaces for two input proteins and performs rigid docking by aligning these interfaces. The method is designed to be equivariant to rotations and translations, ensuring generalization. ElliDock employs a global level, pairwise SE(3)-equivariant message passing network called EPIT to predict the interface, and it is evaluated against existing methods on the Docking benchmark version 5.5 and the Structural Antibody Database, showing competitive performance and faster inference times. The methodology includes a graph transformer layer and a rotation refinement step to enhance the accuracy and robustness of the predictions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for rigid protein-protein docking, ElliDock, which is based on the prediction of elliptic paraboloid interfaces and is strongly competitive with state-of-the-art learning-based methods for antibody-antigen docking.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear and detailed explanations of the model's design and operation, including the use of graph transformer layers and the EPIT network for predicting SE(3)-equivariant features.
- The paper includes rigorous evaluations on the Docking benchmark version 5.5 and the Structural Antibody Database, demonstrating the method's effectiveness and its ability to outperform or match existing methods in terms of performance and inference time.
- The authors provide a thorough analysis of the limitations and potential future directions for the research, which shows a commitment to transparency and ongoing improvement.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other baseline methods, particularly in terms of inference time and computational efficiency. This makes it difficult to fully assess the practical advantages of ElliDock over existing methods.
- The methodology relies heavily on pre-trained models and complex computational processes, which may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios or in environments without access to these resources.
- There is a noted absence of a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its strengths and weaknesses.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the model across different protein structures or the potential impact of sequence similarity on the model's performance.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the computational efficiency of ElliDock compared to other baseline methods, especially in terms of inference time and resource requirements?
- How does the model handle variations in protein structures or sequences, and what are the implications for the model's generalizability?
- Could you provide more details on the training process and the role of pre-trained models in the performance of ElliDock?
- How does the model handle cases where the protein structures are not well-defined or where there is significant sequence similarity between the proteins being docked?
- In the context of the elliptic paraboloid interface prediction, how does the model ensure that the predicted interfaces accurately reflect the physical interactions between the proteins?
