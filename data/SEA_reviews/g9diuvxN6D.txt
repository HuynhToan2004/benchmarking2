 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the robustness of instruction-tuned language models (LLMs) against variations in instruction phrasings. It demonstrates that performance degradation occurs when LLMs are presented with unseen instructions, even when they are semantically equivalent to those seen during training. The authors propose a method using "soft prompt" embedding parameters to align representations of semantically equivalent instructions, aiming to enhance model robustness. The study involves a dataset of 319 English instructions manually written by NLP practitioners for over 80 unique tasks and evaluates the performance of LLMs like Flan-T5, Alpaca, and T0. The findings suggest that instruction-tuned models are not robust to instruction rephrasings, but the proposed method can improve robustness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive and in-depth analysis of the robustness of instruction-tuned LLMs.
- The proposed method of introducing "soft prompt" embedding parameters to align representations of semantically equivalent instructions is simple and effective, showing significant improvements in model robustness.
- The experimental results are solid, demonstrating the degradation of performance when using unseen instructions, even though they are semantically equivalent to those seen during training.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the robustness of instruction-tuned LLMs, which is crucial for the development of more robust and generalizable models.
- The dataset of 319 English instructions manually written by NLP practitioners for over 80 unique tasks is valuable and will be beneficial for future research on instruction-tuned LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of why the proposed method works, and the analysis of why the model is sensitive to instruction phrasings is insufficient.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for overfitting to the paraphrased instructions or the scalability of the method to larger models.
- The dataset used in the study is small, and the authors do not adequately address how the model's performance varies with the size of the dataset.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the model's performance is affected by the size and diversity of the instruction-tuning dataset.
- The paper does not explore the impact of different instruction phrasings on model performance, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model's robustness.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method work, and what is the intuition behind its effectiveness?
- What is the impact of the proposed method on the model's performance when using in-context learning (ICL)?
- How does the model's performance vary with the size of the dataset, and how does the model's performance on the test set compare to the performance on the held-out set?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into why the model's performance is sensitive to instruction phrasings?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger models, and is there a risk of overfitting to the paraphrased instructions?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for using different instruction phrasings to improve model performance, and how this might affect the model's generalization capabilities?
