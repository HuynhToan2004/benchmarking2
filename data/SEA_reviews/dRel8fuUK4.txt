 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel membership inference attack (RMIA) that leverages both reference models and reference data to enhance the distinction between population data and training data. The methodology involves a likelihood ratio test that calculates the relative probability of a data point being in the training set compared to a random sample from the population. The attack's effectiveness is evaluated across various datasets, showing improvements over existing methods in terms of test power and false-positive rates. However, concerns are raised about the clarity and depth of the methodological explanations, the novelty of the approach, and the empirical justification for its superiority.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and well-organized content, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed method is novel, providing a new perspective on the membership inference attack (MIA) problem, and is well-motivated with a clear rationale behind its design.
- The experiments are comprehensive, including multiple datasets and a variety of settings, which support the claims made in the paper.
- The paper is original in its approach, particularly in the use of relative MIA, which distinguishes it from existing methods.
- The paper includes a detailed appendix with additional results and explanations, which enhances the understanding of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology, particularly in sections 2.1 and 2.2, which could confuse readers unfamiliar with the topic.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing attacks, such as LiRA and Attack-R, which could undermine the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper's reliance on multiple reference models for improved performance might not be practical or scalable, especially in scenarios where data distribution shifts are significant.
- The paper's empirical results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the improvements in test power and false-positive rates are not consistently significant across different datasets and settings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's contribution to the field is questionable, as the methodological advancements over existing methods are not clearly articulated, and the empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate superior performance.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the methodology, particularly in sections 2.1 and 2.2, to make it more accessible to readers unfamiliar with the topic?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing attacks, such as LiRA and Attack-R, in terms of performance and practical applicability?
- Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the proposed method handles data distribution shifts, especially in scenarios where reference models are not available?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational complexity and scalability, and what are the practical implications of these limitations?
- In light of the concerns about the novelty and empirical justification of the method, could you provide additional evidence or arguments to support the claimed improvements over existing methods?
