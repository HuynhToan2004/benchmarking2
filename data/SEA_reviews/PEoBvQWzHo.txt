 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to personalized federated learning by integrating Dirichlet-based uncertainty quantification into the model selection process. The proposed method, FedPN, uses both local and global models to make predictions, with the choice between them based on uncertainty scores. The paper addresses issues in the loss function of existing models, such as NatPN, and provides a new loss function to better handle aleatoric and epistemic uncertainties. Experiments on various datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in handling out-of-distribution data. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as it heavily relies on existing works, and its empirical evaluation, which is seen as insufficient and potentially misleading.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the important and relevant problem of personalized federated learning, focusing on uncertainty quantification.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and practically applicable, with a clear structure and logical flow.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the limitations of existing methods and proposes a novel solution to rectify the loss function, which is both simple and effective.
- The experimental results show the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios with out-of-distribution data.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper heavily relies on existing works, particularly the NatPN model, and does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from these prior works.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it primarily adapts existing methods to the federated learning setting without significant innovation.
- The empirical evaluation is insufficient and potentially misleading, with results that are not convincingly superior to existing methods and may not accurately reflect the performance of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other methods, especially those that also use Dirichlet-based models, which could provide a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and practical implementation of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the training and inference times.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the computational overhead introduced by the proposed method, particularly in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of local ambiguous data, and what are the implications for the performance of the model in such scenarios?
- In the experimental setup, why was the decision made to split the validation dataset into calibration and testing sets, and what impact does this have on the reported results?
- Can you provide more details on the selection of the uncertainty threshold and how it affects the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare to other Dirichlet-based models in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of datasets and the rationale behind the selection of specific datasets for your experiments?
