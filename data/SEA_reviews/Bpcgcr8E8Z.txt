 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Fast-DetectGPT, a zero-shot machine-generated text detector that utilizes a sampling model to generate alternative samples and computes the conditional probability of each token, thereby reducing the computational overhead compared to DetectGPT. The method leverages the observation that machine-generated text exhibits a positive curvature in the conditional probability function, while human-written text does not. Fast-DetectGPT demonstrates superior performance in both white-box and black-box settings, achieving significant speedup and accuracy improvements over DetectGPT. The method is evaluated on various datasets and language models, showcasing its effectiveness and efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel hypothesis that human and machine select words differently given a context, which is supported by empirical evidence.
- The proposed method, Fast-DetectGPT, is simple yet effective, utilizing conditional probability curvature to distinguish between machine-generated and human-authored text, achieving significant speedup and accuracy improvements over DetectGPT.
- The method is well-motivated and well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- The experiments are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and settings.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including an ablation study and a comparison with other baseline methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different text lengths and the potential for false positives in long texts.
- The choice of using the same model for sampling and scoring raises questions about the independence of the sampling process and the potential for overfitting.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the ethical implications of the proposed method, particularly concerning the potential misuse of the technology.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the robustness of the method against paraphrasing and other types of text manipulation.
- There are some typographical and formatting errors that need correction.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when the text length is not fixed, and how does it handle different text lengths?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the method performs in the black-box setting, especially concerning the use of surrogate models?
- How does the method compare with other zero-shot classifiers in terms of computational cost and accuracy?
- What are the implications of using the same model for sampling and scoring, and how does this affect the independence and generalization of the sampling process?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the text is generated using different decoding strategies, such as top-k or top-p sampling?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of using the same model for sampling and scoring, and discuss potential alternatives or improvements?
