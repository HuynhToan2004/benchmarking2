 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new variant of the board game Diplomacy, termed Welfare Diplomacy (WD), where players aim to maximize their welfare points by balancing military conquest and domestic welfare. This new variant is designed to incentivize cooperation rather than competition, and the authors construct baseline agents using zero-shot prompted language models to evaluate the performance of AI systems in this setting. The paper provides theoretical and empirical evidence for the benefits of WD over traditional Diplomacy, demonstrating that while AI agents can achieve high social welfare, they remain vulnerable to exploitation. The study includes a detailed analysis of equilibrium strategies and their application in zero-shot language model agents.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined research question.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of equilibrium strategies in a simplified version of Welfare Diplomacy (WD), which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed benchmark, WD, is novel and interesting, with potential for broader impact in the field of AI research.
- The paper includes a thorough analysis of equilibrium strategies in a simplified version of WD, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors have conducted experiments on a variety of state-of-the-art models, providing a robust evaluation of the benchmark.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed benchmark, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness.
- The empirical results presented in the paper are not convincing, with large confidence intervals and a lack of statistical significance in some figures.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the societal impact of the research, which is an important consideration given the potential applications of the benchmark.
- The paper's reliance on zero-shot language model agents for evaluation may limit the generalizability of the findings to other types of AI systems.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the insights from the study of Diplomacy can be applied in real-world settings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed benchmark and how these limitations affect the validity and applicability of the results?
- How do the authors justify the use of zero-shot language model agents for evaluation, and what are the implications of this approach for the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and calculations used in the empirical results, particularly in figures where the statistical significance is not clear?
- How do the authors address the potential for AI collusion against human overseers in the context of WD, and what steps can be taken to mitigate this risk?
- In the context of WD, how do the authors measure the level of cooperation and collusion among agents, and what are the implications of these measures for the effectiveness of the benchmark?
