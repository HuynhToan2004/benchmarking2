 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Self-supervised Open-world Hierarchical Entity Segmentation (SOHES), a novel method for open-world entity segmentation without the need for human annotations. SOHES employs a three-phase approach: self-exploration, self-instruction, and self-correction, leveraging a pre-trained self-supervised representation from DINO to generate pseudo-labels through visual feature clustering. These pseudo-labels are then refined through a teacher-student mutual-learning procedure, which enhances the model's ability to segment entities and their constituent parts. The paper demonstrates significant improvements in performance compared to existing self-supervised methods and reduces the gap between self-supervised and supervised methods. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the computational complexity, and the lack of detailed analysis on the hierarchical segmentation and the impact of different pre-training strategies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and challenging task of open-world entity segmentation, which is crucial for various applications such as image editing and robotics.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, well-designed, and well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The method achieves good performance in open-world entity segmentation and significantly reduces the gap between self-supervised and supervised methods.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, and includes a thorough ablation study that demonstrates the effectiveness of each component.
- The methodology is innovative, combining self-supervised learning with hierarchical segmentation, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and tasks, including part segmentation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it heavily relies on existing techniques such as DINO for self-supervised representation and hierarchical segmentation methods.
- The computational complexity of the method is high, particularly in the self-exploration phase which involves multiple clustering and merging operations, which could be time-consuming and resource-intensive.
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis on the hierarchical segmentation, especially in terms of how the hierarchical structure is learned and how it impacts the segmentation results.
- The method's performance is not significantly better than existing methods like CutLER, and it does not achieve state-of-the-art performance on some datasets.
- There are concerns about the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of its ability to handle large-scale datasets like the full SA-1B dataset.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational resources and training time.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the hierarchical segmentation process, particularly how the hierarchical structure is learned and how it impacts the segmentation results?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger datasets like the full SA-1B dataset?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational resources and training time?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and efficiency with other self-supervised methods like FreeSolo and CutLER?
- Can the authors provide more visual examples and detailed analysis to support the claims of hierarchical segmentation and the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- Can the authors clarify the computational complexity and the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of its ability to handle large-scale datasets and complex scenes?
