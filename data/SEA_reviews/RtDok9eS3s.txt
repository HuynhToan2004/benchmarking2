 **Summary:**
The paper explores simplifying the Transformer architecture by removing various components such as skip connections, projection parameters, and normalization layers, aiming to reduce complexity and enhance training efficiency. The authors propose a new block design, the Simplified Attention Sub-block (SAS), which eliminates these components while maintaining performance and even improving training speed by up to 15%. The study also investigates the impact of these changes on different Transformer architectures, including autoregressive decoder-only and encoder-only models, demonstrating that the proposed simplifications do not affect performance. However, the paper's scope is limited to small-scale models, and the generalizability of the findings to larger models remains unclear.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical flow that makes it accessible to readers.
- The proposed simplified transformer block is a significant contribution to the field, potentially reducing computational complexity and improving training efficiency.
- The experiments conducted are thorough and demonstrate that the simplified transformer block matches the performance of standard transformers while reducing parameters and improving training speed.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the impact of different components of the transformer block, which is beneficial for understanding the role of these components in training dynamics.
- The paper's theoretical grounding in signal propagation theory is interesting and provides a solid foundation for the proposed simplifications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on small-scale models, which limits the generalizability of the findings to larger models like those used in large-scale applications.
- The motivation for removing certain components, such as normalization layers and projection parameters, is not convincingly explained, especially considering their potential impact on training stability and performance.
- The paper's scope is limited to specific model architectures, and it is unclear whether the findings can be applied to other types of transformer architectures.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the simplified transformer block in various real-world applications, which could have provided a better understanding of its practical implications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed simplifications, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of the research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify why the performance of the simplified transformer block does not match that of the standard transformer block in some cases, as shown in Figure 5?
- How do the authors justify the removal of normalization layers, especially considering their potential impact on training stability and performance?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations for the observations in Figure 4 regarding the convergence of residual-skip ratios to zero?
- Is there a possibility to extend the simplified transformer block to other types of transformer architectures, such as those used in vision or graph transformers?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed simplifications, particularly in terms of fairness and bias in transformer models?
- How do the authors address the potential issues of overfitting or underfitting that could arise from the simplifications, especially in larger models?
