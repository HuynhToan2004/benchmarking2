 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the long-tailed recognition problem in a semi-supervised setting, utilizing an alternate sampling strategy to tackle the scarcity and imbalance of data. The methodology involves training a feature embedding using random sampling and a classifier with class-balanced sampling. This approach is iterative, with each stage focusing on different aspects of data handling. The paper presents a semi-supervised long-tailed recognition benchmark and claims to outperform existing methods on two datasets. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, insufficient experimental validation, and lack of comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and presents a clear motivation for the study.
- The problem setting is interesting and relevant, focusing on the long-tailed recognition problem in a semi-supervised setting, which is not commonly addressed in existing literature.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with a clear description of the training pipeline and a comprehensive ablation study.
- The paper introduces a semi-supervised long-tailed recognition benchmark, which could be beneficial for future research in this area.
- The methodology is straightforward and the results show improvements over baseline methods, although the improvements are not significant.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, with only two datasets used and no comparison with state-of-the-art methods in long-tailed learning or semi-supervised learning.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the novelty of the method, as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental details, such as the specifics of the datasets used and the hyperparameters of the baseline methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method to larger datasets like ImageNet, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the time and space complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the datasets used, such as the specific classes included in the tail classes and the overall class distribution?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets like ImageNet, and what are the computational costs associated with training on such datasets?
- Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed method, and how these limitations might affect its practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method compare to state-of-the-art methods in long-tailed learning and semi-supervised learning, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could you clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of hyperparameters and the details of the baseline methods used in your experiments?
