 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Protein Structure Transformer (PST), a novel approach that integrates structural information into existing protein language models (PLMs) to enhance their performance in various downstream tasks. The PST leverages a graph neural network (GNN) to encode structural information into each self-attention layer of the PLM, which is pretrained on a protein structure database. The model is shown to outperform the baseline ESM-2 model on several benchmarks, including EC, GO, and ProteinShake, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling protein structural information. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the effectiveness of the structural information integration, and the clarity of the methodological presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach by integrating structural information into existing PLMs, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is well-motivated, and the experiments are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method over existing baselines.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The methodology is simple yet effective, and the results show that the proposed model outperforms the baseline ESM-2 on various benchmarks.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the model's performance across different tasks and model sizes, which is useful for understanding the model's behavior and potential improvements.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it primarily involves the integration of structural information into existing PLMs, which has been explored in previous works.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential negative effects of incorporating structural information into the model, such as the loss of generalizability and the potential for overfitting.
- The effectiveness of the structural information integration is not convincingly demonstrated, as the performance gains are marginal in some cases.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the model's relative performance.
- The methodological presentation could be improved, particularly in the description of the PST model and the integration of the GNN module.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, especially in terms of performance and generalizability?
- How does the model handle the integration of structural information, and what are the potential implications for the model's performance and generalizability?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods to better understand the model's relative performance?
- How does the model perform on other downstream tasks, and what are the potential applications of the model in practical scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these could be addressed in future work?
