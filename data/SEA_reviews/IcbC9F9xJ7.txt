 **Summary:**
The paper introduces scDiff, a general framework for single-cell analysis that unifies various tasks into a posterior modeling problem. This framework leverages conditional diffusion generative models (DGMs) to model the posterior distribution of cell expression given specific conditions. The model is evaluated on multiple datasets, demonstrating competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods. The authors also explore the use of large language models and graph neural networks to incorporate prior knowledge, enhancing the model's capabilities in few-shot and zero-shot settings. The paper provides a comprehensive review of related work and presents extensive experimental results, although some reviewers noted the lack of detailed comparative analysis and the need for more rigorous justification of the model's components.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a general framework for single-cell analysis that unifies various tasks into a posterior modeling problem, demonstrating a novel approach to integrating different computational tasks.
- The proposed scDiff model is competitive with state-of-the-art methods, showing strong performance on multiple datasets.
- The model's ability to incorporate prior knowledge from large language models (LLMs) and graph neural networks (GNNs) is highlighted, providing a flexible and adaptable approach to single-cell analysis.
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the model's architecture and the rationale behind its design choices, making it accessible to readers unfamiliar with the field.
- The experimental results are extensive, demonstrating the model's effectiveness in various settings, including few-shot and zero-shot scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other generative models such as GANs, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the model's capabilities.
- The integration of LLMs and GNNs into the model is described as a post-hoc modification rather than a fundamental component, which might limit the model's effectiveness in certain tasks.
- The paper does not provide sufficient justification for the choice of the predict-x0 parameterization over the predict-ε parameterization, which could affect the model's ability to recover the original expression from noise.
- There is a lack of clarity in the experimental setup, particularly in the one-shot cell type annotation experiments, which might lead to misinterpretations of the model's performance.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed framework, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The model's performance is not consistently superior across all datasets, and the reasons for these discrepancies are not explored.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the rationale behind the choice of the predict-x0 parameterization over the predict-ε parameterization, and provide evidence supporting its effectiveness?
- How does the model handle the integration of LLMs and GNNs into the model, and what are the implications for the model's performance in different tasks?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly in the one-shot cell type annotation experiments, to ensure the results are not misinterpreted?
- How does the model perform in multi-omics or multi-modality tasks, and could the authors expand on the potential applications of the framework in these areas?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other generative models, particularly GANs, to better understand the model's relative strengths and weaknesses?
