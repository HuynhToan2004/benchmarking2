 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called ShapeConv, which integrates the concept of shapelets from traditional machine learning with the convolutional layers of CNNs. This integration aims to enhance the interpretability of CNNs in time-series modeling, a significant challenge in deep learning. The methodology involves the use of squared norms and pooling to equate CNN kernels to shapelets, thereby improving the model's ability to learn interpretable features from time series data. The paper presents several regularizers to ensure the diversity and discriminability of the learned kernels, and demonstrates the effectiveness of ShapeConv through experiments on various datasets, showing superior performance compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations and a comprehensive related work section that effectively positions the research within the current literature.
- The introduction of the ShapeConv layer, which uses CNN kernels as shapelets, is a novel approach that enhances the interpretability of CNNs in time series modeling.
- The paper provides a theoretical justification for the equivalence between CNN kernels and shapelets, which is supported by the experimental results that show the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The inclusion of regularizers such as the diversity regularizer and the shape regularizer is a significant contribution that helps maintain the diversity and discriminability of the learned kernels.
- The paper is structured to be self-contained, making it accessible to readers without requiring extensive background knowledge on shapelets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the ShapeConv layer, which could be a significant concern for large datasets.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of the statistical significance of the results and the comparison with other state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential issues with the scalability and generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the impact of the initialization method on the performance of the ShapeConv layer, which is crucial for practical applications.
- There are several minor typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of the ShapeConv layer, especially in comparison to other shapelet-based methods?
- How do you ensure that the learned kernels remain interpretable throughout the training process, and what steps are taken to prevent overfitting?
- Could you clarify the role of the diversity regularizer and the shape regularizer in the learning process, and how they influence the performance of the ShapeConv layer?
- How do the authors address the issue of scalability in their method, particularly in terms of handling large datasets and high-dimensional time series?
- Is there a possibility to extend the ShapeConv layer to handle multivariate time series data, and if so, what modifications would be necessary?
- Can you provide more detailed experimental results, including statistical significance tests and comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods?
