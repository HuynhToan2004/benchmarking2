 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called DeepROCK, aimed at detecting feature interactions in deep neural networks (DNNs) while controlling the false discovery rate (FDR). DeepROCK employs a plugin pairwise-coupling layer and a knockoff-based methodology to assess the importance of feature interactions. The methodology involves generating knockoff features that mimic the empirical dependence structure of original features but are conditionally independent of the response. These knockoffs are used to compute feature importance scores, which are then used to control the FDR. The paper presents experiments on both simulated and real datasets, showing that DeepROCK effectively detects feature interactions while maintaining FDR control. However, the method's reliance on a pairwise-coupling layer and its generalizability to different types of interactions (e.g., higher-order) remain concerns.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and problem statement.
- The proposed method, DeepROCK, is innovative and provides a novel approach to detecting feature interactions in DNNs while controlling the false discovery rate (FDR).
- The methodology is supported by theoretical insights and practical applications, demonstrating its effectiveness in both simulated and real-world datasets.
- The use of knockoffs to control FDR is a novel approach that has not been explored in previous literature.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations and a structured presentation of results, which aids in understanding the methodology and its applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The methodology heavily relies on the pairwise-coupling layer, which may limit its applicability to other types of feature interactions, such as higher-order interactions.
- The experimental setup primarily focuses on pairwise interactions, which may not generalize well to higher-order interactions.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of how DeepROCK performs against other feature interaction detection methods.
- There is a lack of theoretical guarantees for the effectiveness of DeepROCK in controlling FDR, which could be addressed by providing more rigorous theoretical proofs or empirical evidence.
- The paper does not discuss the scalability of the proposed method with respect to the number of features or the complexity of the neural network, which could affect its practical applicability in larger, more complex models.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its reliance on specific types of feature interactions and the assumptions made in the model.

**Questions:**
- Can DeepROCK be extended to detect higher-order interactions beyond pairwise interactions? If so, how would this be achieved?
- How does the method perform when compared to other feature interaction detection methods, particularly in terms of FDR control and statistical power?
- Is there a theoretical justification for the effectiveness of DeepROCK in controlling FDR, or is this primarily based on empirical evidence?
- How does the method perform when applied to other types of neural network architectures, such as convolutional neural networks (CNNs) or recurrent neural networks (RNNs)?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the method with respect to the number of features and the complexity of the neural network?
- How does the method handle the issue of feature selection, particularly in scenarios where the number of features is large?
- Could the authors provide more details on the calibration procedure used to control FDR, possibly with additional experimental results or theoretical insights?
