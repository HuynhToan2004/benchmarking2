 **Summary:**
The paper explores the generalization bounds of Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) with skip connections and graph sampling. It introduces a novel sparse effective adjacency matrix, A*, which is used to characterize the influence of graph sampling in GCNs with skip connections. The authors demonstrate that the generalization accuracy of the learned model closely approximates the highest achievable accuracy within a broad class of target functions. The paper also highlights that the sampling requirements vary across layers, with the first layer being more affected by sampling deviations from A*. Theoretical analyses are provided, supported by empirical experiments on both synthetic and real datasets. However, the paper's scope is limited to two-hidden-layer GCNs, and it does not consider more general or deeper architectures, which could affect the applicability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is the first to analyze the generalization of GCNs with skip connections using graph sampling, providing a novel theoretical framework for understanding the impact of skip connections on graph sampling.
- The theoretical results are supported by empirical experiments on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating the practical relevance and applicability of the findings.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and understandable, with clear and well-structured proofs that are easy to follow.
- The analysis is original and offers a fresh perspective on the generalization of GCNs with skip connections, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of GCNs, specifically the generalization of models with skip connections and graph sampling, which is a significant challenge in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to two-hidden-layer GCNs, which may not generalize well to more complex or deeper architectures.
- The sampling strategy used in the experiments is not realistic or practical, as it does not consider the actual sampling methods used in GCNs, which could limit the applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the impact of graph sparsity on the generalization of GCNs, which is a significant factor in real-world applications.
- The paper's assumptions about the graph structure and node features are not realistic or practical, which could limit the applicability of the findings.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincing, with some figures (e.g., Figure 1(c) and 2(a)) not clearly demonstrating the claimed effects of skip connections on generalization.
- The paper's discussion on the limitations of the proposed method is insufficient, which could limit the understanding of the practical implications and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle the impact of graph sparsity on the generalization of GCNs?
- Can the proposed method be extended to more complex or deeper GCN architectures?
- How does the sampling strategy used in the experiments compare to actual sampling methods used in GCNs?
- What are the specific implications of the findings for practical applications of GCNs in real-world scenarios?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made about the graph structure and node features in the theoretical analysis, and discuss how these assumptions affect the applicability of the findings?
- How do the authors address the potential issues with the experimental setup, particularly in the interpretation of the results?
