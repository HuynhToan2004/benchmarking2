 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the use of warm-up in Large Batch Learning (LBL) and proposes a new algorithm, Time-Varying LARS (TVLARS), to address the challenges associated with sharp minimizers and the need for warm-up. The authors argue that the warm-up process can be detrimental due to the reduction in gradients, which might prevent the model from escaping sharp minimizers early on. To overcome this, TVLARS uses a sigmoid-like function to replace the warm-up process, allowing for gradient exploration in the early phase and robust training in the latter stages. Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of TVLARS compared to LARS and LAMB, particularly in scenarios with large batch sizes. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of theoretical support and the absence of a detailed comparison with other optimizers like Adam.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed TVLARS algorithm is simple and effective, with a straightforward implementation that enhances its applicability.
- Extensive experiments have been conducted, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm in large-batch training scenarios.
- The analysis of LARS and LAMB with and without warm-up strategies provides valuable insights into the behaviors of these optimizers.
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the LARS optimizer, which is crucial for understanding the dynamics of large-batch training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough theoretical analysis, particularly in the area of sharp minimizers and their impact on optimization.
- There is a significant absence of comparisons with other optimizers, such as Adam, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of TVLARS.
- The paper's claims about the necessity of a warm-up technique in LBL and the failure of LARS and LAMB in many cases are not sufficiently supported by experimental evidence.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of TVLARS over other optimizers, and the improvements are marginal in some cases.
- There is a lack of discussion on the relationship between TVLARS and other existing optimizers, which could have enriched the paper's contribution.
- The paper's structure and content could benefit from reorganization to better align with the main contributions and to provide more detailed comparisons and theoretical discussions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of sharp minimizers and their impact on optimization?
- How does TVLARS compare to other optimizers like Adam, particularly in terms of convergence speed and stability?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, especially the use of different datasets and batch sizes, to ensure the validity of the results?
- What are the specific advantages of TVLARS over other optimizers, particularly in terms of generalization and robustness?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of TVLARS on the training of transformer models, given their different training dynamics compared to CNNs?
- How does TVLARS address the issue of gradient reduction during warm-up, and what are the implications for the model's training stability?
