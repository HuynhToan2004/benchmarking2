 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Gandalf, a novel approach for Extreme Multi-Label Classification (XMC) that utilizes a label correlation graph to enhance training data with label features, acting as additional data points. This method leverages the characteristics of short-text XMC to capture label-label correlations, which are then used to generate soft-label targets. Gandalf is demonstrated to improve the performance of existing XMC algorithms by up to 30% on benchmark datasets. The approach is simple yet effective, and the paper provides extensive experiments to validate its efficacy. The method's novelty lies in its data-centric augmentation, which is orthogonal to recent algorithmic advancements, and it does not add computational overhead during inference.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and good presentation of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and orthogonal to recent algorithmic advancements, making it a plug-and-play approach that can be applied to various existing XMC algorithms.
- The method significantly improves the performance of existing XMC algorithms by up to 30% on benchmark datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper provides extensive experiments, including ablation studies and qualitative results, which help in understanding the method's impact on tail labels and its sensitivity to different values of delta.
- The approach does not add computational overhead during inference, making it practical for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to long-text XMC and its potential impact on model performance.
- There is a lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's relative performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of the data used in the experiments, including details on the label distribution and the impact of different label distributions on the method's performance.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a critical consideration for ethical AI research.
- The method's effectiveness in scenarios with a large number of labels or when the label distribution is skewed is not thoroughly explored, which could limit its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the data used in the experiments, particularly the distribution of labels and the impact of different label distributions on the method's performance?
- How does the proposed method compare with recent state-of-the-art methods, and what are the potential advantages or disadvantages compared to these methods?
- Can you elaborate on the potential limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to long-text XMC and its impact on model performance?
- How does the method handle the potential negative societal impacts, and what steps are being taken to mitigate these risks?
- Could you provide more details on the computational overhead during training and how it compares to the computational savings during inference?
