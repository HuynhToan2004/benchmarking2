 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to dataset distillation, focusing on self-supervised learning (SSL) objectives to optimize a small set of synthetic samples for efficient pre-training. The method addresses the instability in bilevel optimization caused by data augmentations and proposes a kernel ridge regression method to minimize the mean squared error between a model's representations and target representations. The approach is evaluated across various datasets and shows improvements over existing methods. The paper also discusses the computational efficiency and the reduction in computational cost through the use of a closed-form solution for the linear head.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel problem of self-supervised dataset distillation for transfer learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method, KRR-ST, is innovative and provides a closed-form solution for the linear head, which simplifies the optimization process.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The empirical results demonstrate that KRR-ST outperforms existing supervised dataset distillation methods in transfer learning, indicating its effectiveness.
- The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis and proof of the biased gradient of SSL objectives, which is a valuable contribution to the understanding of dataset distillation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in dataset distillation, such as those mentioned in the review.
- The experimental setup and results, particularly in tables and figures, are not clearly presented, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its scalability to larger datasets.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the computational efficiency and the reduction in computational cost achieved by the proposed method.
- The paper could provide more details on the initialization of the model pool and the choice of hyperparameters, which are crucial for reproducibility and understanding the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the initialization of the model pool and the choice of hyperparameters, such as the learning rate and the number of models in the pool?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger datasets, and what are the scalability limitations?
- Can you clarify the experimental setup and the results presented in the tables and figures, particularly in Table 1 and Figure 2?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in dataset distillation, especially in terms of computational efficiency and the reduction in computational cost?
- Could you discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical application?
