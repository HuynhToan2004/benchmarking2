 **Summary:**
The paper introduces EFFL, a novel approach to federated learning (FL) that addresses the Matthew Effect by focusing on egalitarian fairness across clients. EFFL is formulated as a multi-constrained multi-objective optimization (MCMOO) problem, aiming to minimize empirical risk losses while ensuring egalitarian fairness in both accuracy and decision bias. The paper proposes a three-stage gradient-based algorithm to achieve Pareto optimal solutions, which are evaluated against several state-of-the-art FL algorithms. The experiments demonstrate that EFFL can achieve high-performance global models with enhanced fairness among clients, although there are concerns about the clarity of the paper's presentation and the novelty of the approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, EFFL, which addresses the Matthew Effect in federated learning by focusing on egalitarian fairness, a critical issue in real-world applications.
- The authors have formulated EFFL as a multi-constrained multi-objective optimization (MCMOO) problem, which is a novel approach to tackling the challenges of fairness in federated learning.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the problem, solution, and evaluation, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed three-stage gradient-based algorithm is innovative and shows potential for achieving Pareto optimal solutions, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper includes comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in achieving high-performance global models with enhanced fairness among clients.
- The authors have provided theoretical proof of convergence, which adds credibility to the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as it seems to resemble existing methods like FedMDFG, with similar formulations and methodologies.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of computational complexity and memory requirements, which could limit its applicability in large-scale federated learning scenarios.
- There are concerns about the clarity of the paper's presentation, with some sections requiring more detailed explanations and proofs to enhance understanding.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other relevant works, such as those that address client-level fairness in federated learning, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive experiments, including different datasets and a broader range of client conditions, to more thoroughly evaluate the proposed method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method address the scalability issues, particularly in terms of computational complexity and memory requirements?
- Can the authors provide more details on the convergence rate of the proposed method and how it compares to other existing methods?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the data distribution is imbalanced or the client participation is non-uniform?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its applicability in different scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods that address fairness in federated learning, particularly in terms of fairness metrics and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the mathematical proofs and assumptions used in the paper to enhance understanding and reproducibility?
