 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the synaptic geometry in neural networks, proposing a novel approach using mirror descent to model synaptic weight changes. It argues that the choice of distance metric, specifically the Bregman divergence, influences the implicit bias and the resulting synaptic weight distributions. The authors demonstrate that the weight distributions converge to a Gaussian in the limit of large network sizes, suggesting a Gaussian change in the dual space. This finding is supported by experiments on both synthetic and real neural data, including a deep neural network trained on a subset of ImageNet. The paper also explores the implications of these findings for understanding synaptic plasticity in the brain, suggesting that the synaptic geometry may be a critical factor in learning dynamics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the results and their implications.
- The theoretical framework is robust, with a solid mathematical foundation and clear statements of results.
- The experiments are well-designed and convincingly support the theoretical claims, demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed methods.
- The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by considering the synaptic geometry in neural networks, which is an important and often overlooked aspect of neural network dynamics.
- The results are novel and interesting, providing a new perspective on the synaptic geometry and its implications for learning dynamics in neural networks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions about the synaptic geometry and its implications for learning dynamics are not thoroughly explored, leaving some questions about the practical applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential misapplications of the proposed methods.
- The experiments are somewhat limited in scope, focusing mainly on synthetic data and simple network architectures, which might not fully capture the complexity of real neural data or more complex network structures.
- There is a lack of direct comparison with existing models or experimental data, which could help to better contextualize the findings and assess their novelty and significance.
- The paper's focus on mirror descent as a specific method for modeling synaptic geometry might limit its broader applicability to other learning dynamics or network architectures.
