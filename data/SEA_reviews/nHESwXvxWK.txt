 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called MCGdiff, designed to solve Bayesian linear inverse problems using a Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) algorithm. This method leverages the structure of both the linear inverse problem and the denoising diffusion generative model to create an efficient SMC sampler. The authors establish the theoretical convergence of the empirical distribution of samples produced by MCGdiff to the target posterior when the number of particles approaches infinity. The paper provides numerical simulations demonstrating the superiority of MCGdiff over competing methods in handling ill-posed inverse problems in a Bayesian setting. However, the paper lacks detailed implementation and computational complexity discussions, and the experimental evaluations are limited to specific datasets and inverse problems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined problem setting.
- The proposed method, MCGdiff, is novel and provides a theoretical analysis of its convergence, supported by numerical simulations that demonstrate its superiority over existing methods.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the methodology, assumptions, and theoretical results.
- The authors have made significant efforts to ensure reproducibility by providing code and detailed experimental setups.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and potentially applicable to a wide range of problems, making it accessible and practical for other researchers to build upon.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the implementation and computational complexity of MCGdiff, which could be crucial for understanding its practical applicability and efficiency.
- The experimental evaluations are limited to specific datasets and inverse problems, which might not fully demonstrate the generalizability or robustness of the method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of MCGdiff, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper does not compare MCGdiff with the latest diffusion-based methods, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its performance.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved by adding more figures to aid in understanding complex concepts, and by including a more detailed discussion on the theoretical results and their implications.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the implementation and computational complexity of MCGdiff, including a discussion on the scalability of the method with increasing problem dimensions?
- How does MCGdiff compare to the latest diffusion-based methods, and what are the main differences in terms of performance and applicability?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specific datasets and inverse problems used, and the parameters of the diffusion model used in the experiments?
- How does MCGdiff perform when the assumptions of the linear Gaussian inverse problem are not met, and what are the implications for its applicability in more complex or non-linear inverse problems?
- Could you provide more details on the theoretical results and their implications, including a discussion on the assumptions and limitations of the theoretical analysis?
