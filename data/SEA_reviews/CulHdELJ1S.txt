 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel hybrid optimization strategy, HUB, which integrates hand-designed and learned optimizers to enhance the generalization capabilities of learned optimizers. The HUB methodology utilizes a softmax function to determine the weighting between the two optimizers based on gradient magnitudes, aiming to balance the strengths of both approaches. The methodology is evaluated across various tasks and datasets, including CIFAR, Tiny-ImageNet, and Imagenet1K, demonstrating improvements over traditional methods like Adam. However, concerns are raised regarding the fairness of the comparisons, the clarity of the methodological description, and the lack of detailed experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel hybrid optimization strategy, HUB, which effectively integrates hand-designed and learned optimizers, offering a more refined blending mechanism that significantly reduces computational overhead.
- The methodology is evaluated across a range of mainstream neural network architectures, including MLP, CNN, RNN, Neural ODE, and Transformer, demonstrating its versatility and applicability.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear description of the methodology and its implementation.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering various datasets and tasks, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method in handling out-of-distribution tasks and its robustness.
- The theoretical analysis of the hybrid strategy's impact on the behaviors and inherent traits of learned optimizers provides deeper insights into their functionalities and interactions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the HUB methodology, particularly how it integrates hand-designed and learned optimizers and the specifics of the hybridization process.
- The experimental setup and results are not clearly presented, making it difficult to understand the impact of the HUB methodology on the performance of the learned optimizer.
- The paper does not provide a clear comparison of the computational overhead of the HUB methodology compared to other methods, which is crucial for assessing its practicality.
- The paper does not adequately address the fairness of comparisons, particularly in the evaluation of the learned optimizer's performance against other optimizers that may not have undergone similar training processes.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts, which are crucial for a comprehensive understanding of the technology.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the HUB methodology, which is crucial for assessing its practicality in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the specifics of the HUB methodology, particularly how it integrates hand-designed and learned optimizers and the details of the hybridization process?
- How does the HUB methodology handle the computational overhead compared to other methods, and what are the practical implications of this overhead in real-world applications?
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost of the HUB methodology against other methods to better assess its practicality?
- How does the HUB methodology ensure fair comparisons in the evaluation of the learned optimizer's performance against other optimizers that may not have undergone similar training processes?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the training processes and the evaluation metrics used?
- How does the HUB methodology address the limitations of learned optimizers, such as the difficulty in handling out-of-distribution tasks and uncontrollable behaviors?
