 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of consistency models as a policy representation in reinforcement learning (RL), aiming to balance computational efficiency and expressiveness. Consistency models are proposed as an alternative to diffusion models, particularly in settings like offline RL, offline-to-online RL, and online RL. The authors argue that consistency models offer faster sampling speeds and potentially fewer computational resources, though they may sacrifice some expressiveness compared to diffusion models. The paper presents empirical results demonstrating that consistency models can match or outperform diffusion models in some settings, particularly in online RL where computational efficiency is critical. However, concerns about the fairness of comparisons due to different training durations and the lack of significant performance improvements over existing methods persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces the use of consistency models as a policy representation in reinforcement learning, which is a novel approach that could potentially reduce computational costs and enhance performance.
- The method is evaluated across various settings including offline, offline-to-online, and online RL, providing a comprehensive evaluation of its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The empirical results are robust, showing that the consistency model can achieve comparable performance to diffusion models in some settings and significantly outperform them in others.
- The use of consistency models in RL is a significant contribution, as it addresses the computational efficiency issue often associated with diffusion models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of using consistency models as policy representations, particularly in terms of their expressiveness and generalization capabilities.
- The paper does not provide sufficient empirical evidence to support claims of faster sampling speeds or significant computational savings compared to diffusion models.
- The experimental setup and comparisons are not always fair, particularly in the online RL setting where the consistency model is trained for significantly shorter durations than the diffusion model.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of its work.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it appears to be a direct application of existing consistency models without significant modifications or innovations.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more detailed comparisons between consistency models and diffusion models in terms of computational efficiency and expressiveness?
- How do the authors justify the choice of using consistency models in online RL settings where diffusion models may perform better?
- Can you clarify the training duration differences between consistency and diffusion models in the online RL experiments?
- How does the consistency model handle the generation of multi-modal actions, particularly in settings where multiple actions may be valid in a given state?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the offline datasets used and the training parameters for the consistency and diffusion models?
