 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel quantization method for continual learning, termed Hadamard Domain Quantized Training (HDQT), which utilizes Hadamard transforms to optimize the quantization of tensors in the backward pass and employs tiled matrix multiplication for the forward pass. The method aims to maintain performance levels comparable to non-quantized models while reducing computational demands by quantizing matrices to 4-bit integers with 8-bit accumulators. The paper evaluates HDQT across various datasets, including CIFAR100 and human activity recognition datasets, showing a minimal degradation in accuracy. However, concerns are raised regarding the generalizability of the method across different continual learning algorithms and its impact on computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel quantization method for continual learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is well-explained, with clear figures and detailed explanations, making it easy to understand.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method across multiple datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing computational demands.
- The use of Hadamard transforms in the backward pass and tiled matrix multiplication in the forward pass is a novel approach that enhances the efficiency of the training process.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and detailed explanations, which aids in understanding the proposed method and its implementation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other quantization methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods, particularly in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential impact of the proposed method on the learning process, particularly in scenarios where the model may be overfitting to the training data.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to specific continual learning algorithms, raising concerns about its generalizability across different types of continual learning scenarios.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the Hadamard transform, which is a critical component of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation, including a broader range of datasets and continual learning algorithms to better demonstrate the robustness and effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other quantization methods in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the Hadamard transform, including the specific algorithm used and any assumptions made during its implementation?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different continual learning algorithms, and what are the implications for the learning process in each scenario?
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the Hadamard transform and the continual learning problem, particularly how the transform addresses the challenges posed by the dynamic nature of continual learning?
- How does the proposed method impact the training and inference times, and what are the implications for the practical deployment of the method on edge devices?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specific hardware and software used, and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
