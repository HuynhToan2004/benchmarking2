 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel unsupervised outlier detection method, Noise-guided unsupervised Outlier Detector (NOD), which leverages a binary classifier trained on both inliers and uniform noise to detect anomalies. Theoretical analysis and extensive experiments on 22 datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of NOD, showing improvements in ROC_AUC compared to existing methods. The method's simplicity and efficiency are highlighted, with the use of a simple MLP and a binary classifier to learn the difference between samples and uniform noise. However, concerns are raised about the theoretical soundness, particularly the assumptions made and the practical implications of these assumptions in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear introduction to the problem of unsupervised outlier detection.
- The proposed method is simple, efficient, and achieves good performance on various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The theoretical analysis provided is sound, and the experiments conducted are extensive, covering a wide range of datasets.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to unsupervised outlier detection using a simple MLP and binary classifier, which is a departure from traditional methods that rely on complex models or large datasets.
- The method's ability to handle high-dimensional data and its robustness to hyperparameters are notable strengths.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies heavily on the assumption that the outliers are sparser distributed than the inliers, which may not hold true in all real-world scenarios.
- The method's performance is not robust to hyperparameters, particularly the number of negative samples, which could affect its effectiveness.
- The theoretical analysis, while sound, is not well-integrated with the practical implementation of the method, and the assumptions made are not thoroughly justified or supported by empirical evidence.
- The method's scalability and performance on large-scale datasets are not adequately addressed, and the computational complexity is not clearly defined.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the method, particularly its assumptions and the potential negative societal impacts.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and how these assumptions are justified in practical scenarios?
- How does the method perform when the assumption of outliers being sparser than inliers does not hold true?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the method, especially in large-scale datasets?
- What are the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments, and how were these chosen?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the method and how these could be mitigated?
