 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Fleet-Merge, a method for merging policies learned by different robots, addressing the challenge of distributing learning tasks across a fleet of robots. The approach leverages the symmetries in neural network parameters to merge policies effectively, which is validated through experiments on a new benchmark, Fleet-Tools, and existing datasets like Meta-World. The method is designed to optimize communication costs by merging weights directly, which is contrasted with traditional methods that involve sharing raw data. The paper also discusses the challenges of handling permutation symmetries in RNNs and proposes a method to address these. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and the lack of a clear distinction from existing methods, particularly in the use of permutation symmetries in RNNs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a logical flow that makes it easy to follow.
- The problem addressed is significant, particularly the communication costs associated with merging learned policies, which is a critical issue in distributed learning.
- The proposed method, Fleet-Merge, is well-explained and appears to be effective in its application, showing improvements over baseline methods in various experiments.
- The introduction of a new benchmark, Fleet-Tools, is a valuable contribution to the field, offering a new standard for evaluating policy merging in robotics.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions and explanations that aid in understanding the complex concepts presented.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear distinction from existing methods, particularly in the use of permutation symmetries in RNNs, which are not new and have been previously explored in similar contexts.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it heavily relies on existing methods and does not sufficiently differentiate itself.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples, particularly in sections that are currently dense or confusing.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently robust or convincing, with some results being misleading or inadequately explained.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need correction.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the distinction between the proposed method and existing methods, particularly in the use of permutation symmetries in RNNs?
- How does the method handle the identification of tasks by observations, especially when the observations are not distinct enough?
- Could you provide more detailed explanations or examples for the mathematical formulations and algorithms used in the paper, particularly those in Section 3?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- In the context of the experiments, how do the results compare when using different network architectures or when varying the communication epochs?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of metrics and their relevance to the tasks being evaluated?
