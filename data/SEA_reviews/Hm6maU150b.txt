 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel federated learning method called Nested Federated Learning (NeFL), which divides a global model into submodels through depthwise and widthwise scaling. This allows for efficient handling of heterogeneous devices in federated learning, where clients can train submodels based on their resource constraints. The method uses learnable step sizes and batch normalization layers as inconsistent parameters, which are averaged separately from consistent parameters. The experiments conducted on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets demonstrate that NeFL outperforms existing methods in terms of model performance and training efficiency. However, the paper lacks comprehensive theoretical analysis and experiments on larger, more complex datasets, which could strengthen its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method of dividing a global model into submodels through depthwise and widthwise scaling, which is a significant advancement in federated learning.
- The use of learnable step sizes and batch normalization layers as inconsistent parameters is a novel approach that enhances the flexibility and adaptability of the model.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results show that NeFL outperforms existing methods, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency in handling heterogeneous devices in federated learning.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in federated learning, which is the handling of heterogeneous devices and the scalability of models to different clients.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks comprehensive theoretical analysis, particularly in the areas of model accuracy and convergence, which could strengthen its claims.
- The experiments are limited to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, which are relatively small and simple. Expanding the experiments to larger, more complex datasets such as ImageNet could provide a more robust evaluation.
- There is a lack of clarity and detail in the presentation of some experimental results and methodologies, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with other existing methods, particularly those that address similar issues in federated learning, such as model pruning and quantization.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its approach, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis, especially in the areas of model accuracy and convergence, to support their claims?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the client selection and the training parameters used?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of inconsistency in model training, and what are the implications of this inconsistency on the overall performance of the model?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of learnable step sizes and batch normalization layers in the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger, more complex datasets, and what are the potential challenges and limitations in such scenarios?
