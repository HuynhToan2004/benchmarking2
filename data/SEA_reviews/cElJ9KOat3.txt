 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) method for coordinating agents under directed acyclic graph (DAG) constraints, utilizing a leader agent and a reward generator and distributor (RGD) to guide follower agents. The methodology involves a new synthetic reward function based on the MARL model with synthetic rewards (MARLM-SR), which is proven to serve as a lower bound of the optimal value function. The paper also presents a practical training algorithm and empirical results from four DAG environments, including a real-world scheduling scenario, demonstrating the method's effectiveness. Despite the theoretical and empirical contributions, the paper has been critiqued for its complexity, lack of clarity in certain sections, and potential issues with the scalability and generalizability of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) method to learn multiple coordinated agents under directed acyclic graph (DAG) constraints, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is supported by a theoretical foundation, including a novel surrogate value function based on a MARL model with synthetic rewards (MARLM-SR) and a lower bound of the optimal value function.
- The paper is well-written and provides a comprehensive literature review, effectively positioning the proposed work within the existing body of knowledge.
- The empirical results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various DAG environments, including a real-world scheduling scenario.
- The paper introduces a novel leader agent and reward generator and distributor (RGD) to guide the decomposed follower agents, which is a novel approach in the field of MARL.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is complex and difficult to follow, particularly in the methodology and algorithm sections, which could benefit from clearer explanations and potentially more detailed examples or illustrations.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the assumption that the policy for the entire system can be decomposed into policies for individual subtasks, which may not hold in complex or dynamic environments.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability and generalizability of the proposed methods, particularly in environments where the DAG structure is complex or not well-defined.
- The empirical results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, as the paper does not compare the proposed method with a broader range of baselines, and the results are not convincingly superior in all scenarios.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which could detract from its professionalism and readability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and discuss how these assumptions might affect the applicability of the proposed methods in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform when the DAG structure is not well-defined or when there are multiple potential DAGs?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or illustrations to help clarify the methodology and algorithm, particularly in the sections where the complexity makes the content difficult to follow?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of scalability and generalizability, especially in environments where the DAG structure is complex or not well-defined?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of their approach and how these might impact the practical application of their methods in real-world scenarios?
