 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the expressive power of Graph Transformers (GTs) by critically examining the use of activation functions in attention mechanisms. It highlights the limitations of the softmax function, which is commonly used in GTs, and proposes the use of sigmoid activation to enhance the expressive capacity of GTs, potentially reaching the expressivity of the Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL) test. The paper also introduces the Expressive Graph Transformer (EGT), which incorporates sigmoid activation and other improvements to the feed-forward layer, aiming to address the expressivity issues in GTs. Extensive experiments across graph classification and node classification tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of EGT, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper's theoretical contributions are seen as limited, and the experimental results do not convincingly outperform state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue in Graph Transformers (GTs) by critically examining the use of activation functions in attention mechanisms, which is crucial for the expressive power of GTs.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the limitations of existing GTs and proposes a novel approach using the Expressive Graph Transformer (EGT) with sigmoid activation, which enhances the expressive capacity of GTs.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on various graph classification and node classification tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper includes extensive experiments and ablation studies, which provide valuable insights into the impact of different activation functions on the performance of GTs.
- The paper is significant as it explores the expressive power of GTs, which is a critical aspect of their utility in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical contributions are limited, with the proofs of theorems relying heavily on existing works without substantial novelty.
- The experimental results do not convincingly outperform state-of-the-art methods, particularly in node classification tasks, where the performance gains are marginal.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing methods like GAT, which could have provided a clearer picture of its advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper's claims about the expressive power of GTs are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which raises questions about the validity of the results.
- The paper's focus on small datasets might limit its applicability to larger, more complex datasets, which are more representative of real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these limitations might affect its applicability in different scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods like GAT, particularly in terms of expressive power and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more experimental results on larger datasets to validate the scalability of the proposed method?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how might these impacts be mitigated?
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies between the theoretical claims and the experimental results, particularly in the node classification tasks?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of expressivity in GTs, and what are the implications of this approach for the broader field of GT research?
