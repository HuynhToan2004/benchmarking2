 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GADAM, a novel method for unsupervised graph anomaly detection (UGAD) that addresses the challenge of local inconsistency mining in graph neural networks (GNNs). GADAM employs a two-stage approach: first, using a message-passing-free MLP-based local inconsistency mining (LIM) to calculate local anomaly scores. In the second stage, these scores are used to construct positive and negative instance pairs for a binary classification task, enhancing the effectiveness of anomaly detection. The methodology is supported by extensive experiments on nine datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper, the lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, and the potential for over-reliance on synthetic anomalies which might not adequately represent real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of graph anomaly detection, focusing on the challenge of local inconsistency mining in graph neural networks.
- The proposed method is novel and well-explained, with a clear two-stage approach that enhances the effectiveness of anomaly detection by utilizing local anomaly scores as pseudo-labels.
- Extensive experiments are conducted on nine benchmark datasets, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method over existing state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear and logical flow, and includes detailed analyses of the method's performance and efficiency.
- The hybrid attention mechanism is innovative and effectively addresses the conflict between local inconsistency mining and message passing, improving the accuracy of anomaly detection.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in several sections, particularly in the explanation of key concepts such as local inconsistency mining and the role of message passing in anomaly detection.
- The related work section is incomplete and does not adequately compare the proposed method with recent state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the approach.
- The paper primarily focuses on synthetic anomalies, which may not adequately represent real-world scenarios, and the impact of this on the method's performance is not discussed.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed method, particularly in relation to the time and space requirements.
- There are several grammatical and typographical errors throughout the paper which detract from its professionalism and clarity.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role of message passing in anomaly detection and how it affects the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of over-smoothing in GNNs, which could potentially reduce the effectiveness of anomaly detection?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods to better establish the novelty and effectiveness of their approach?
- How does the proposed method perform when using real-world datasets with organic anomalies, and what are the implications of this for the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors discuss the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed method in more detail, including a comparison with other methods in terms of time and space requirements?
