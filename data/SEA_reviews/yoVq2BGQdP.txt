 **Summary:**
The paper explores the multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) framework, focusing on achieving fairness in MDPs by optimizing a fairness function that ensures equitable rewards across agents. The authors propose a novel algorithm that achieves sub-linear regret in terms of the number of episodes, providing theoretical guarantees for both online and offline settings. The paper also introduces a PAC guarantee based on the obtained regret bound, demonstrating the efficacy of the approach through simulation experiments. Despite the theoretical contributions, concerns were raised about the practicality of the proposed methods, particularly in the context of large state spaces and the clarity of the paper's presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to achieving fairness in multi-agent MDPs using a reinforcement learning (RL) framework, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- It provides a theoretical guarantee for the proposed algorithm, demonstrating sub-linear regret in terms of the number of episodes, which is a notable achievement.
- The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The authors have provided a PAC guarantee based on the obtained regret bound, which is a valuable addition to the theoretical contributions of the paper.
- The proposed algorithm achieves sub-linear regret with respect to the α-fairness function in MDP, which is a first for this problem.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of regret bounds and computational efficiency, which could have strengthened the paper's position in the field.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in the clarity of the main text and the organization of the proofs in the appendix.
- The paper assumes that the agents in the MDP are part of the environment, which may not be realistic in many practical scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the practicality of the proposed methods, especially in large state spaces, which could limit the applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, which could be a significant limitation in practical implementations.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other fairness metrics, such as max-min fairness, to better understand the trade-offs and advantages of the proposed α-fairness metric.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the assumption of known state transition probabilities, and discuss how these assumptions impact the practical applicability of the proposed methods?
- How does the paper's algorithm compare in terms of computational efficiency and regret bounds with other existing methods in the field?
- Could the authors provide more details on the practical implications of the fairness metrics used in the paper, particularly how they compare to other fairness metrics like max-min fairness?
- In the context of large state spaces, how do the proposed algorithms perform, and what are the computational complexities involved?
- Can the authors elaborate on the choice of the α-fairness metric and discuss any potential drawbacks or limitations of this metric compared to other fairness metrics?
