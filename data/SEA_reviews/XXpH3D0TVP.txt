 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for data attribution in diffusion models, aiming to understand how individual training data points influence the generation of specific images. It employs a method called TRAK to estimate the attribution of each training image to a generated image, which is evaluated using metrics like LDS and FID scores. The framework is demonstrated to be effective in identifying influential training images, particularly in scenarios where these images significantly alter the generated image. The methodology is applied to various datasets including CIFAR-10 and MS COCO, showing that it can be used to remove influential images and generate new images from scratch. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation on diverse datasets and its reliance on a single metric, which might not fully capture the complexity of data attribution.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework for data attribution in diffusion models, which is a significant contribution to the field of generative models.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering various aspects of data attribution, and the results are clearly presented.
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem in the community, focusing on the influence of training data on the generation process.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with a focus on improving the understanding of how data influences the generation process.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough evaluation on diverse datasets, particularly large-scale datasets like LAION-400M, which could better demonstrate the robustness and applicability of the proposed method.
- The reliance on a single metric (FID) for evaluation might not fully capture the complexity of data attribution, and additional metrics could provide a more comprehensive assessment.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include potential biases introduced by the method or its applicability to different types of diffusion models.
- The method's applicability to different types of diffusion models, such as text-to-image models, is not explored, which could limit the practical utility of the findings.
- The paper's claims about the method's ability to "forget" an image and generate a new one from scratch are not convincingly supported, and the method's effectiveness in such scenarios is not convincingly demonstrated.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations on how the proposed method can be applied to different types of diffusion models, particularly text-to-image models?
- How does the proposed method handle potential biases that might be introduced by the data attribution process?
- Can the authors provide more comprehensive evaluations using additional metrics beyond FID to better assess the effectiveness of the method?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of diffusion models, and what are the implications of these differences in performance?
- Could the authors clarify the method's ability to "forget" an image and generate a new one from scratch, and provide more convincing evidence to support these claims?
