 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ProteinINR, a method for pre-training protein representations using Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) that encode surface features of proteins. The authors propose a multi-modal pre-training approach that integrates sequence, structure, and surface features, demonstrating improved performance on downstream tasks such as protein classification and function prediction. The method is based on a combination of existing techniques, including GearNet and DSPoint, and employs a novel approach by integrating INRs with protein surface features. The paper is well-written and presents a clear methodology, although it lacks a comprehensive comparison with other INR-based methods and does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup and results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear descriptions of the methodology and experimental setup.
- The introduction of ProteinINR, a novel method for pre-training protein representations using Implicit Neural Representations (INRs), represents an important step towards incorporating protein surfaces, which play a crucial role in protein functions.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method on downstream tasks, showing improved performance compared to existing methods.
- The paper provides a comprehensive comparison with other INR-based methods, highlighting the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The authors conduct a series of experiments to verify the effectiveness of the proposed method, demonstrating that the model can effectively capture the surface features of proteins.
- The paper is significant as it is the first to use INRs to pre-train the protein surface, introducing a method that can effectively reconstruct protein surfaces and enhance performance in various downstream tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the INR-based method and the architecture of ProteinINR, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the methodology.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup and results, such as the number of proteins used in the experiments and the specifics of the experimental conditions.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other INR-based methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the dependency on protein structure and the potential impact of predicted structures on performance.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be crucial for understanding the practicality and scalability of the approach.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a standard requirement in scientific publications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the INR-based method and the architecture of ProteinINR, including the specifics of how the INRs are trained and the dimensions of the input and output features?
- How does the proposed method compare with other INR-based methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, including the number of proteins used and the specifics of the experimental conditions?
- How does the proposed method perform on proteins without known structures, and what are the implications of using predicted structures?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how these impacts might be mitigated or addressed?
