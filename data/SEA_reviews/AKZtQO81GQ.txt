 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel metric, SkewSize, designed to evaluate model bias by focusing on the distribution of model errors across subgroups. This metric, based on Cramer's V statistic, aggregates effect sizes of associations between model predictions and subgroup variables to provide a scalar measure of bias. The metric is tested across various datasets including synthetic, image classification, and VLMs, demonstrating its ability to capture biases not detected by standard metrics like accuracy or worst-group accuracy. The paper also discusses the limitations and practical considerations of the metric, including its applicability to continuous variables and its potential impact on model development and fairness evaluations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel metric, SkewSize, which effectively captures biases in model predictions that are not detected by standard metrics like accuracy or worst-group accuracy.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear definitions and formulations that aid in understanding the proposed metric and its application.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a logical flow that guides the reader through the concepts and experiments.
- The experimental results are robust and support the effectiveness of the proposed metric, demonstrating its ability to capture biases in model predictions across various datasets.
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue in machine learning, focusing on the evaluation of model bias, which is crucial for improving the fairness and reliability of AI systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed metric, particularly in terms of its applicability to continuous variables and its potential impact on model development and fairness evaluations.
- There is a need for more extensive experimentation to validate the effectiveness of the metric across a broader range of models and datasets, especially those that are not specifically designed to test for biases.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough literature review and comparison with existing metrics to better position its contributions within the current research landscape.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implications of using the metric, such as how it could be used to improve model fairness or mitigate biases in real-world applications.
- The paper's focus on a specific metric (SkewSize) might limit its applicability to other types of biases or datasets, and the methodology could be extended to include other metrics or approaches.

**Questions:**
- How does the metric perform when applied to models that are not trained on biased data? Could it potentially mislead or over-emphasize biases that are not present in the model's predictions?
- Can the metric be adapted to evaluate biases in models that use continuous variables as inputs or outputs? How would this adaptation affect the metric's effectiveness and applicability?
- How does the metric compare to other metrics for evaluating model fairness or bias, such as demographic parity, equalized odds, or statistical parity difference?
- Can the metric be used to evaluate the impact of different model architectures or training strategies on bias reduction? For example, how does it compare the performance of different models trained using the same dataset but with different training strategies?
- How does the metric perform in real-world applications, especially in domains where model biases are known to exist, such as image classification or natural language processing?
- Could the authors provide more details on the synthetic datasets used in the experiments, particularly how they were generated and how they represent real-world biases?
- How does the metric handle cases where the bias variable is not systematically related to the data-generating process, such as in cases of implicit or unconscious bias?
