 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to combining deep reinforcement learning (DRL) with classical planning to address sparse-reward goal-directed tasks. It employs a two-stage process: first, it extracts critical action schemata from demonstrations using mutual information and symbolic regression, and then, it infers the critical action network based on these schemata. This method is evaluated in several gridworld environments, demonstrating sample efficiency and task performance. However, concerns were raised about the generalizability of the method to more complex environments and its reliance on predefined subtasks. The paper also lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods and a detailed discussion on the limitations of the approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to integrating planning and deep reinforcement learning (DRL) by automatically inducing task structures and substructures from a few demonstrations, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is evaluated across multiple domains, demonstrating sample efficiency and task performance, and includes an ablation study to justify the design of the induction module.
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation and detailed explanations of the methodology, making it accessible to readers.
- The authors have provided code and detailed experimental setups, enhancing the reproducibility and transparency of their research.
- The proposed method shows promising results in handling sparse-reward tasks, which are typically challenging for DRL algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of generalization performance and sample efficiency.
- The generalization ability of the proposed method is questionable, as it relies heavily on predefined subtasks and may not perform well in more complex environments.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and scope.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the design choices of the induction module and the genetic programming procedure, including how these components were chosen and their impact on the results.
- The paper's reliance on demonstrations for initializing the induction module could limit its applicability in scenarios where such data is not available.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in environments with a large state space.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the generalization performance of their method in more complex environments, such as those involving continuous input or stochastic environments?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of sample efficiency and task performance to other state-of-the-art methods, especially those that do not rely on predefined subtasks?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their method and how these might affect its applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform when the induction module fails to produce rules, and what are the implications of this failure mode?
- Could the authors provide more details on the design choices of the induction module and the genetic programming procedure, including the impact of these components on the results?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of generalizing from one task to another, especially when the preconditions and effects vary?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the baselines were chosen and how the results were validated?
