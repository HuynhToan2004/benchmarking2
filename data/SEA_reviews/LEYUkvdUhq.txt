 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ZipIt!, a method for merging models trained on different tasks without retraining. It proposes a novel approach by merging features within each model and only merging the output features of the last layer, allowing for partial zipping up to a specified layer. This method aims to improve performance over previous methods that only merge the output features of the last layer. The paper provides extensive experiments and ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method. It also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the method, including the concept of mode connectivity and its application to merging models trained on disjoint tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures that aid in understanding the methodology.
- The proposed method is novel and shows promising results in various settings, including merging models trained on different tasks without retraining.
- The paper includes extensive experiments and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method is supported by theoretical analysis, which provides a strong foundation for its application.
- The approach is simple yet effective, and the paper provides a detailed explanation of the method, including the challenges and solutions in model merging.
- The paper includes a comprehensive review of related work, which positions the research well within the current literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods for merging models trained on different tasks, which could strengthen the argument for the superiority of the proposed method.
- The method's effectiveness might be limited by the similarity of the models being merged, as the method relies on finding similar features across models, which might not always be present.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed descriptions of the experimental setup, including the datasets used and the specifics of the model training and merging processes.
- The paper's reliance on the assumption that models are trained from the same checkpoint might limit its applicability in scenarios where models are trained from different checkpoints.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when merging models trained from different checkpoints, and what are the implications of this on the model's performance?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods for merging models trained on different tasks?
- How does the method perform when merging models trained on different datasets, such as ImageNet and CIFAR?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and provide examples or scenarios where the method might not be effective?
- How does the method handle models with different architectures or different numbers of layers?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the model training and merging processes?
