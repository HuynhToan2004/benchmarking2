 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Representation-Aligned Preference-based Learning (RAPL), a novel method for aligning visual representations in robot learning using human feedback. RAPL leverages a triplet-based representation space to optimize visual representation alignment, which is crucial for learning visual rewards through optimal transport. The methodology involves fine-tuning a pre-trained visual encoder with human feedback and using this alignment to guide reward learning. The approach is tested in both simulation and real-world scenarios, demonstrating improved sample efficiency and generalization capabilities. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its experimental scope and the clarity of its methodological presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and its implementation.
- The use of human feedback to align visual representations is a novel and intuitive approach, which is supported by a robust theoretical framework.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various scenarios.
- The paper introduces a novel method for visual representation alignment using optimal transport, which is a significant contribution to the field of robot learning.
- The methodology is supported by extensive ablation studies and comparisons to relevant baselines, providing a robust validation of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with related works, particularly those that use similar methodologies such as optimal transport for visual reward learning.
- The experimental setup is limited to simple environments and does not include real-world or more complex scenarios, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- There are inconsistencies in the experimental results and the presentation of the methodology, which could confuse readers and detract from the paper's impact.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which could limit its applicability in more complex or larger-scale scenarios.
- The methodology section is not clearly presented, and there are several typographical errors and unclear descriptions that need to be addressed for better clarity and comprehension.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the optimal transport method in the alignment of visual representations?
- How does the method perform in more complex environments, such as those with multiple objects or more diverse visual stimuli?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of its applicability to larger or more complex scenarios?
- What are the implications of using different visual representation models, such as those based on different pre-trained models or different training methods?
- How does the method handle noisy or inconsistent human feedback, and what measures are in place to ensure the reliability and accuracy of the feedback used?
