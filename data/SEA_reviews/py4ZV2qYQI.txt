 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HybridTree, a novel federated learning algorithm designed for hybrid data settings, where data from different parties may differ in both features and samples. HybridTree leverages the existence of consistent split rules in trees to incorporate knowledge from different parties into the lower layers of the tree, thereby reducing communication traffic. The paper presents theoretical analysis and extensive experiments, demonstrating that HybridTree achieves comparable accuracy to centralized settings with lower computational and communication overhead. The methodology is supported by a layer-level solution that does not require frequent communication, thereby enhancing efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel federated learning approach, HybridTree, which enables federated tree learning on hybrid data, a significant advancement in the field.
- The existence of meta-rules in trees is observed, providing a new perspective on tree learning.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method, including comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and results that show improvements in efficiency.
- The paper addresses a realistic and practical problem of federated learning on hybrid data, which is not commonly explored in existing literature.
- The proposed method is intuitive and easy to understand, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough review of related works, particularly in the area of federated learning on hybrid data. This omission weakens the paper's contribution to the field.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable as it appears to be a straightforward extension of existing federated learning algorithms to hybrid data settings.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of the number of clients and the size of the data.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other federated learning methods, particularly those that use cryptographic techniques, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The privacy protection aspects of the proposed method are not adequately discussed, which is a critical concern in federated learning.
- The paper's assumptions about the data distribution and the presence of meta-rules in trees are not convincingly supported, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other federated learning methods, particularly those that use cryptographic techniques?
- Can the proposed method be extended to other types of data beyond tabular data?
- How does the method handle the privacy concerns in federated learning, especially in the context of hybrid data settings?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the meta-rules are extracted from the data and how they are used to improve the efficiency of tree learning?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, especially in terms of the number of clients and the size of the data?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made about the data distribution and the presence of meta-rules in trees, and discuss how these assumptions affect the generalizability of the findings?
