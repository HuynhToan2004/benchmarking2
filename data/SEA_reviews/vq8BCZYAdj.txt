 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity approach to deep symbolic optimization, aiming to address the high computational costs associated with evaluating large numbers of candidate solutions in complex real-world domains. The proposed methods, Multi-Fidelity Markov Decision Processes (MF-MDPs), are designed to handle multiple fidelities and their associated costs, enhancing the efficiency of symbolic optimization processes. The paper presents two algorithms, RSEP and PGEP, which are evaluated on two challenging domains: symbolic regression and antibody optimization. The experimental results show that these methods outperform fidelity-agnostic and fidelity-aware baselines, indicating their effectiveness in handling complex, computationally intensive problems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to multi-fidelity symbolic optimization, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis and experimental validation of their proposed algorithms, enhancing the credibility and applicability of the proposed methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The proposed algorithms are evaluated on two challenging domains, demonstrating their effectiveness and robustness across different scenarios.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of symbolic optimization, which is of great interest and relevance to the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing multi-fidelity optimization methods, which could provide a better understanding of the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approaches.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed or convincing, particularly in terms of the choice of hyperparameters and the comparison with other algorithms.
- There is a noticeable absence of a discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implications of the computational costs associated with high-fidelity simulations, which could limit the scalability and applicability of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, which could affect the reliability and generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing multi-fidelity optimization methods, including a discussion on how their proposed methods differ and potentially outperform these existing approaches?
- How do the authors justify the choice of hyperparameters in their experiments, and what impact do these choices have on the results?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of the computational costs associated with high-fidelity simulations, and discuss potential strategies for mitigating these costs?
- How do the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis affect the reliability and generalizability of the results, and what steps could be taken to ensure that these assumptions are valid in a broader range of scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and results, particularly in terms of the choice of benchmarks and the comparison with other algorithms?
- How do the proposed methods compare in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness with other symbolic optimization methods, especially in scenarios where high-fidelity simulations are not feasible?
