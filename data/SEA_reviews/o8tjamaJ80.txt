 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AdAutoMixup, an adversarial augmentation method aimed at enhancing image classification robustness by generating diverse mixup samples. It utilizes an attention-based generator to create a mixup policy, which is then used to generate multiple mixed samples from a set of original images. The method is designed to improve the classifier's robustness by introducing adversarial training, which alternatively optimizes the classifier and the mixup sample generator. The approach is evaluated on several datasets, including CIFAR100, TinyImageNet, and ImageNet-1K, showing improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the incremental nature of the improvements and the computational cost of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components.
- The use of an attention-based generator to learn a mixup policy and the inclusion of an adversarial training approach to optimize both the classifier and mixup sample generator are innovative and well-explained.
- The paper conducts extensive experiments, including ablation studies, and the results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves better performance than previous methods on several benchmarks.
- The paper is well-motivated, with a clear description of the problem and the proposed solution, and it includes a comprehensive literature review.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other data augmentation methods, particularly those that generate adversarial samples, which could provide a clearer understanding of the proposed method's advantages.
- There is a lack of clarity in the experimental setup, particularly concerning the number of training epochs and the hyperparameters used, which could affect the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the proposed method, which is critical for practical implementation.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be an incremental improvement over existing methods like AutoMix, with only minor differences in the generator design.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims of overfitting in previous methods, which could undermine the credibility of the proposed solution.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the computational cost of the proposed method and compare it with other methods to assess its practicality?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different datasets, especially those with varying label distributions or class imbalances?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other data augmentation methods, particularly those that generate adversarial samples, to better understand the advantages of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of training time and computational resources required?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the hyperparameters used and the training conditions, to ensure reproducibility and transparency?
