 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel synthetic dataset and a controlled environment for evaluating feature attribution methods in neural networks. The dataset, designed with specific properties such as sensitivity and symmetry, allows for the testing of attribution methods under controlled conditions, ensuring that the neural network uses the intended ground truth features. The paper employs various evaluation metrics and compares the results with ground truth attributions, providing insights into the faithfulness and effectiveness of different attribution methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticisms for its limited scope, lack of novelty in the dataset design, and the generalizability of its findings to real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel synthetic dataset specifically designed to test feature attribution methods, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments on this dataset, providing a thorough evaluation of various attribution methods.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and detailed experimental setups, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The inclusion of a controlled environment for testing faithfulness is a novel approach that allows for a more rigorous evaluation of attribution methods.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of feature attribution, which is crucial for understanding and improving the interpretability of neural networks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a specific dataset and set of attribution methods, which may not generalize well to other datasets or methods.
- The dataset design, while innovative in some aspects, lacks novelty as it primarily uses pre-existing techniques and does not significantly advance the state of the art.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other synthetic datasets or a broader range of attribution methods, which could have strengthened its claims.
- The paper's reliance on manual weight setting and dataset design may limit its applicability in more complex or varied scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of its findings to real-world scenarios, which is a critical concern for the practical application of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the dataset design and the manual weight setting contribute to the evaluation of faithfulness?
- How do the authors address the issue of generalizability to real-world scenarios, especially given the controlled nature of the experiments?
- Would it be possible to include a broader range of attribution methods in future experiments to validate the findings more comprehensively?
- How does the paper's evaluation method compare to other existing methods for evaluating faithfulness, such as those based on post-hoc solutions or other synthetic datasets?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their approach and how these might affect the practical applicability of their findings?
