 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to transferring preferences across tasks in preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL) using the Gromov-Wasserstein (GW) distance for optimal transport. This method aligns trajectory distributions between source and target tasks, enabling the transfer of preference labels without extensive human labeling. The approach is validated through experiments on robotic manipulation tasks from Meta-World and Robomimic, showing that it outperforms existing methods in limited-data scenarios. The paper also proposes a robust preference transformer to handle noisy labels and demonstrates the method's effectiveness in zero-shot and few-shot settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear motivation for the research.
- The proposed method, using the Gromov-Wasserstein distance for optimal transport to align trajectory distributions between source and target tasks, is innovative and effectively addresses the problem of transferring preferences across tasks.
- The method is demonstrated to be effective in zero-shot and few-shot settings, showing significant performance improvements over existing methods.
- The paper includes a robust preference transformer to handle noisy labels, which enhances the method's applicability and robustness.
- The experiments are well-designed, with results showing that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in limited-data scenarios.
- The paper is well-organized and includes clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the proposed method and its results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of tasks and its scalability.
- The method's reliance on the Gromov-Wasserstein distance for optimal transport may not be suitable for high-dimensional inputs due to potential slower processing speeds.
- The method's efficiency relies on the same action space between source and target tasks, limiting its applicability to tasks with different action spaces.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with a broader range of baseline methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of its effectiveness.
- The method's performance in scenarios with varying levels of noise and the robustness of the preference transformer under different noise conditions are not thoroughly discussed.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the Gromov-Wasserstein distance for optimal transport, which could affect the method's performance and applicability.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when the source and target tasks have different action spaces?
- Can the authors provide more details on the efficiency of the method in terms of computational resources and training time?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the preference labels are noisy or inconsistent?
- Could the authors elaborate on the method's scalability and its applicability to tasks with high-dimensional inputs?
- What are the specific advantages of using the Gromov-Wasserstein distance for optimal transport over other distance metrics?
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent works in the field of PbRL, particularly those that also address the transfer of preferences across tasks?
- Could the authors provide more insights into the design choices of the reward distribution modeling and the regularization loss, and discuss their impact on the method's performance?
