 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework called "template-content" to explain the reasoning abilities of large language models (LLMs). This framework decomposes complex tasks into template and content components, where the template acts as a flow indicator for task decomposition, and the content provides specific details for each question. The authors extend the Universal Approximation Theorem (UAT) to causal Transformers and demonstrate that well-trained models can solve complex tasks by borrowing knowledge from their training data. The framework's hierarchical structure allows for the modeling of complex tasks with multiple levels of sub-tasks. The paper also presents experiments that show practical models exhibit different behaviors for "template" and "content," supporting the modeling. However, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity and the depth of its empirical evidence, with concerns about the generalizability of the template-content structure and the clarity of the theoretical contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel framework called "template-content" that decomposes complex tasks into template and content components, providing a new perspective on how large language models (LLMs) solve complex tasks.
- The framework is well-supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence, demonstrating the different behaviors of LLMs for "template" and "content."
- The paper is well-written, making the complex concepts accessible and easy to understand, with clear definitions and explanations of key concepts such as template, content, and T/C classification.
- The extension of the Universal Approximation Theorem (UAT) to causal Transformers is a significant contribution, providing a theoretical foundation for understanding the reasoning abilities of LLMs.
- The hierarchical structure of the template-content framework allows for the modeling of complex tasks with multiple levels of sub-tasks, enhancing the understanding of how LLMs perform complex reasoning tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's clarity could be improved, particularly in the theoretical sections where the contributions of the paper are not clearly articulated.
- The empirical evidence provided is limited, with only one experiment conducted on a single dataset, which may not be sufficient to validate the generalizability of the template-content structure.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the requirement that the model must remember all training examples and the assumption that the model's behavior is always correct, are not well-justified or supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper does not adequately address how the template-content structure can be applied to different types of tasks or how it can be generalized to other models or datasets.
- The paper's discussion on the limitations of the work is not detailed enough, particularly in the areas of alignment, sampling, and the generalizability of the template-content structure.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the template-content structure can be applied to different types of tasks, and provide examples or experiments to support this?
- How does the template-content structure generalize to other models or datasets, and what are the implications of this generalization for the applicability of the framework?
- What are the specific contributions of this paper to the field, particularly in terms of the theoretical contributions and the empirical evidence provided?
- Can you elaborate on the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the assumption that the model's behavior is always correct, and how this assumption is supported by empirical evidence?
- How does the template-content structure handle the alignment of templates and contents, especially in scenarios where the alignment might not be perfect?
- Could you provide more details on the experiments conducted, such as the dataset used and the methodology employed, to better understand the results presented in the paper?
