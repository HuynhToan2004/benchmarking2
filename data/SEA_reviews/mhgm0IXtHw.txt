 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for inverting images using noise maps, which are derived from the diffusion model's noise schedule. This approach is aimed at improving the spatial context in image editing, particularly in real images. The method involves a dual-conditioning strategy that integrates both noise maps and text embeddings, which is claimed to enhance the quality of image editing without compromising on speed. The paper presents a series of experiments and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to existing techniques. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the writing, the experimental design, and the depth of the theoretical justification for the use of noise maps.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to inverting images using noise maps, which is a significant advancement in the field of image editing.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and fast, with clear explanations and good presentation of the method and results.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with existing methods, including an ablation study that helps in understanding the impact of different components of the proposed method.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various tasks such as local editing, global editing, and zero-shot image-to-image translation.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed method and its implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other inversion-based methods, particularly those that do not use noise maps, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- There are several typographical and grammatical errors throughout the paper, which detract from its professional quality.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the theoretical aspects of using noise maps for image inversion, including the implications of the noise map's size and the computational complexity of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of the number of images used and the statistical significance of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of images and the scalability of the method to larger or more complex images.
- The paper does not include a user study, which could provide valuable insights into the practical utility and user experience of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the number of images used and the statistical significance of the results?
- How does the proposed method compare with other inversion-based methods that do not use noise maps, such as those mentioned in related works?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in terms of the size of the noise map and the computational resources required?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the image quality is low or the noise map is of low quality?
- Could the authors provide more details on the theoretical justification for using noise maps for image inversion, particularly in terms of the noise map's role in preserving spatial context?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of images, such as those with complex or low-quality textures?
- Can the authors provide more examples or case studies to demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method in real-world applications?
