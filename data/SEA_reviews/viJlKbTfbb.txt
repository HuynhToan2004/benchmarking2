 **Summary:**
The paper explores the causal awareness of learned representations in multi-agent interactions, particularly focusing on the CausalAgents benchmark. It critiques the benchmark's labeling and evaluation protocols, proposing a diagnostic dataset to enhance causal awareness in multi-agent forecasting models. The paper introduces a causally-aware regularization method that leverages causal annotations to improve model robustness and generalization. The method is tested across various scenarios, including sim-to-real transfer, demonstrating its effectiveness in out-of-distribution settings. However, concerns are raised about the method's scalability, its evaluation against state-of-the-art methods, and the clarity of its contributions to the field of causal inference.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the causal awareness of learned representations, questioning the notions of non-causal robustness in the CausalAgents benchmark and proposing a diagnostic dataset to enhance causal awareness in multi-agent forecasting models.
- The proposed causally-aware regularization method is simple yet effective, leveraging causal annotations of varying granularity to improve causal awareness and out-of-distribution robustness.
- The paper introduces a sim-to-real causal transfer framework, which extends the strengths of causal regularization from simulation to real-world contexts, enhancing generalization in challenging settings.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method can significantly improve causal awareness and out-of-distribution robustness, providing insights into the challenges and opportunities of learning causally-aware representations in multi-agent contexts.
- The paper is well-written, making the ideas and experiments clear and easy to follow, and the code is provided, which is beneficial for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of scalability and its generalization to other domains.
- The method's evaluation against state-of-the-art methods is insufficient, which could affect the understanding of its effectiveness and superiority.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of its applicability to larger or more complex scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently clarify the relationship between the proposed method and existing works, particularly in terms of its contributions to the field of causal inference.
- The paper's methodology and results are not sufficiently robust to changes in the training data, which could limit its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method scale to larger or more complex scenarios, and what are the potential limitations in such settings?
- Can the authors provide more details on the evaluation protocol used in the CausalAgents benchmark and how it affects the robustness evaluation?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of causal awareness and out-of-distribution robustness?
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between their work and existing works, particularly in terms of the contributions to the field of causal inference?
- Could the authors elaborate on the method's scalability and applicability to other domains, and discuss any potential limitations or challenges in extending the method to these areas?
