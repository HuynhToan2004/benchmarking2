 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training objective for auto-regressive language models (LMs) aimed at addressing the limitations of Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) in optimizing the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) between the model's distribution and the data distribution. This is achieved by optimizing a tractable upper bound of EMD, which is differentiable and enables end-to-end training. The proposed method, EMO, is evaluated across various language modeling tasks and is shown to outperform MLE and other baselines. The paper also discusses the potential of EMO in improving the diversity and recall properties of LMs, though it faces criticisms for its complexity and potential computational inefficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in language modeling by critiquing the limitations of Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) and proposing a novel approach using Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) to enhance model training.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and structured, with detailed explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, covering various aspects of language modeling and demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed method's properties and its theoretical advantages over MLE, which is well-supported by empirical results.
- The method is straightforward and easy to understand, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a direct comparison with other recent works such as PANG, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The complexity of the proposed method, including the computation of the EMD matrix and the use of an external solver, could potentially lead to computational inefficiencies and increased training time.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of negative diversity, which is a significant limitation of the MLE approach.
- The evaluation of the proposed method is limited to specific datasets and models, which may not generalize well to other datasets or models.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for increased training time and the computational complexity.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed method over MLE are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which are sometimes not statistically significant.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with PANG and discuss how their method addresses the limitations of MLE more effectively?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational efficiency and training time compared to MLE?
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method addresses the issue of negative diversity in language modeling?
- How does the choice of the transport cost function affect the performance of the proposed method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and training time of the proposed method compared to MLE?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger models, and what are the implications for the scalability of the method?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the training dynamics and the convergence properties of the proposed method?
