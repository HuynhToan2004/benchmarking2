 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Hyper-Dimensional Function Encoding (HDFE) for encoding continuous objects into fixed-length vectors, which can be used for various downstream tasks such as classification and regression. HDFE does not require training and is designed to be sample distribution and size invariant, which is crucial for handling continuous data. The method is evaluated on tasks like function-to-function mapping and point cloud surface normal estimation, showing promising results. The authors also provide theoretical foundations and extensive experimental validations to support the claims of sample invariance, decodability, and distance-preservation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides extensive experimental validations, which support the claims of sample invariance, decodability, and distance-preservation.
- The method does not require training and can be applied to various downstream tasks, making it versatile.
- The method is evaluated on two different domains, showing its effectiveness in both mesh-grid and sparse data.
- Theoretical foundations are provided to support the claims of the method.
- The paper is original and fills a gap in the literature by proposing a method that can encode Lipschitz functions while upholding all the four properties.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other methods like Vector Function Architecture (VFA), which might limit the understanding of the method's effectiveness.
- The method's application is currently limited to point cloud data, and it is unclear how it would perform with other types of continuous data.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential improvements.
- The experimental setup and results could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and depth of presentation.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and scalability of the method, especially in high-dimensional or large-scale datasets.

**Questions:**
