 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Dynamic Neighborhood Construction (DNC), designed to handle structured large discrete action spaces (SLDAS) in reinforcement learning. DNC leverages a simulated annealing search to find the optimal discrete action from a continuous output, which is more scalable and efficient compared to existing methods like k-nearest neighbors and min-max mapping. The method is evaluated across various environments, including maze navigation, inventory replenishment, and job shop scheduling, demonstrating its effectiveness and scalability. The authors claim that DNC can handle action spaces with up to 10^73 actions, a significant improvement over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper, particularly in the presentation of the methodology and the empirical results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Dynamic Neighborhood Construction (DNC), which effectively handles structured large discrete action spaces (SLDAS) in reinforcement learning.
- DNC is scalable and efficient, with the ability to handle action spaces up to 10^73, which is a significant improvement over existing methods.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the DNC method, including a detailed explanation of the methodology and empirical results.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a comprehensive evaluation across multiple environments.
- The authors provide a detailed explanation of the DNC method, including a clear explanation of the methodology and empirical results.
- The paper presents a novel approach to handling structured large discrete action spaces, which is a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in the presentation of the methodology and the empirical results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other existing methods, such as hierarchical reinforcement learning and multi-agent reinforcement learning.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scalability and efficiency.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive empirical evaluations, including comparisons with more baseline methods and different action space sizes.
- The paper's claims of scalability to action spaces of size 10^73 are not substantiated with empirical results, and the practical applicability of such large action spaces is questionable.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of action space enumeration, which is a critical limitation of many existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and use of the term "action space" in the context of DNC?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods, such as hierarchical reinforcement learning and multi-agent reinforcement learning?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the action space?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of action space enumeration, which is a critical limitation of many existing methods?
- Can the authors provide more empirical results to support the claim of scalability to action spaces of size 10^73?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of local optima in the discrete action space, and what measures are in place to ensure that the method does not get stuck in these optima?
- Can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning and its impact on the performance of the proposed method?
