 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for saliency map computation, focusing on the path method for attribution in deep neural networks (DNNs). The proposed method, SAMP, utilizes a concentration principle to optimize the path selection, aiming to enhance the clarity and interpretability of neural network outputs. It employs a model-agnostic interpreter that efficiently searches for near-optimal paths, incorporating infinitesimal constraint and momentum strategy to ensure rigorousness and optimality. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through various experiments on datasets like MNIST, CIFAR-10, and ImageNet, showing superior performance compared to existing methods. However, concerns are raised regarding the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the presentation, and the empirical justification of the method's superiority.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and challenging problem in the field of neural network interpretability, specifically focusing on the ambiguity of attribution methods.
- The proposed method, SAMP, is novel and provides a unique approach to the problem, introducing the concentration principle to enhance the clarity of attributions.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a technical audience.
- Extensive experiments are conducted on various datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR-10, and ImageNet, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method is model-agnostic, which is a significant advantage as it can be applied across different model architectures without modification.
- The paper includes both qualitative and quantitative results, providing a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation from existing methods such as Guided IG, particularly in the use of the concentration principle, which might be seen as a minor modification rather than a significant innovation.
- There is a lack of empirical justification for the superiority of the SAMP method over existing methods, with some results showing marginal improvements or even worse performance in certain scenarios.
- The paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in the explanation of the concentration principle and the role of the infinitesimal constraint in the method.
- The method's scalability and computational efficiency are not adequately discussed, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper does not provide sufficient visual examples or detailed comparisons with other methods, which could help in better understanding the method's effectiveness and limitations.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support the claims made about the superiority of the SAMP method, especially in terms of its ability to pinpoint salient areas accurately.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the SAMP method differs from existing methods like Guided IG, particularly in the use of the concentration principle?
- How does the infinitesimal constraint affect the computational efficiency and scalability of the SAMP method?
- Can you provide more detailed comparisons and visual examples to better illustrate the method's effectiveness and limitations?
- How does the SAMP method handle different types of inputs, such as natural images versus synthetic images, and how does this affect the method's performance?
- Could you elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of the SAMP method, particularly how it ensures the pinpointing of salient areas accurately?
- How does the SAMP method perform in real-world applications, and what are the practical implications of its use in safety-critical systems like autonomous driving and healthcare?
