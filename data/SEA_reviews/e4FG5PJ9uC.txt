 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for constructing perceptual embeddings at inference time without training data or deep neural network features. This method, referred to as the Linear Autoregressive Similarity Index (LASI), employs a weighted least squares problem to capture both global and local image characteristics. The authors demonstrate that LASI can perform competitively with learned methods and outperform traditional untrained methods on benchmark psychophysical datasets. The method is shown to be data-free, differentiable, and scalable, offering an alternative to existing methods like LPIPS and MS-SSIM. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, the limited scope of experimental validation, and the lack of discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for constructing perceptual embeddings at inference time without training data or deep neural network features, which is a significant advancement in the field of image quality assessment (IQA).
- The proposed method, LASI, is data-free, differentiable, and scalable, making it a promising alternative to existing methods like LPIPS and MS-SSIM.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the method and its implications.
- The authors demonstrate that LASI can perform competitively with learned methods and outperform other untrained methods on benchmark psychophysical datasets, indicating its effectiveness in real-world applications.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the method, including a discussion on the correlation between the prediction loss and performance on downstream 2-AFC tasks, which adds depth to the understanding of the method's capabilities.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the LASI method, which could enhance the understanding of its effectiveness and limitations.
- The experimental validation is limited to a single dataset (BAPPS), which might not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the method across different types of images or tasks.
- There is a noticeable absence of comparisons with other data-free IQA methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method's performance.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational complexity and resource requirements of the LASI method, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to small image patches, which might not generalize well to larger or more complex images, such as those used in high-resolution image processing.
- The paper does not discuss the potential societal impacts of the LASI method, which could be a significant concern in applications involving human subjects or privacy.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the LASI method and how it aligns with the human visual system?
- How does the LASI method compare with other data-free IQA methods, and what are the advantages or disadvantages of using LASI over these methods?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and resource requirements of the LASI method, especially in comparison to other methods like LPIPS and MS-SSIM?
- How does the LASI method perform on larger or more complex images, such as those used in high-resolution image processing?
- Can the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of the LASI method, especially in applications involving human subjects or privacy?
- How does the LASI method handle variations in image content and distortions, and what are the implications of these variations on the method's performance?
