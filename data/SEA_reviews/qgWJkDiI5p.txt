 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the Fast Equilibrium Conjecture of Li et al. (2020) by removing non-generic assumptions such as the uniqueness of the basin and analyticity of the minima. It extends the proof of the conjecture to more general settings, demonstrating that the equilibrium is reached in polynomial time. The paper introduces new techniques, including the use of the large deviation principle, to analyze the behavior of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) in over-parametrized neural networks. It also explores the implications of these findings for the training dynamics of neural networks, particularly in the context of stochastic weight averaging.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a rigorous proof of the Fast Equilibrium Conjecture in a more general setting, removing non-generic assumptions such as the uniqueness of the basin and analyticity of the minima.
- The authors introduce new techniques, such as the use of the large deviation principle, to analyze the behavior of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) in over-parametrized neural networks.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and examples, and the proofs are detailed and rigorous, making the paper accessible to readers with a strong background in the field.
- The results of the paper are significant and contribute to a better understanding of the training dynamics of neural networks, particularly in the context of stochastic weight averaging.
- The paper addresses an important question in the field of neural network training dynamics and provides a theoretical foundation for understanding the behavior of SGD in practical scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the minima form a cone, which may not hold in general for neural networks with ReLU activations.
- There is a lack of empirical evidence to support the theoretical claims, particularly in terms of the convergence rates predicted by the theory.
- The paper heavily relies on previous works, with the main technical contribution being the removal of assumptions rather than introducing new techniques or insights.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of how they can be applied to improve the training of neural networks.
- The paper is dense and requires a strong background in the field to fully understand, making it less accessible to a broader audience.

**Questions:**
- How does the paper handle the assumption that the minima form a cone, particularly in the context of neural networks with ReLU activations?
- Can the authors provide empirical evidence to support the theoretical claims, such as experiments demonstrating the convergence rates predicted by the theory?
- What are the practical implications of the theoretical results, and how can they be applied to improve the training of neural networks?
- Could the authors clarify the practical implications of the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the assumptions about the minima and the noise structure?
- How does the paper's theoretical analysis compare to existing empirical evidence, such as the observations of multiple equilibria in practical training scenarios?
