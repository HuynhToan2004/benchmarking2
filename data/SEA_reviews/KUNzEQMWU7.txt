 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MathVista, a benchmark designed to evaluate mathematical reasoning in visual contexts, integrating 28 existing multimodal datasets and three newly created datasets. It evaluates 12 foundation models, including proprietary and open-source models, using a comprehensive evaluation protocol. The results show that GPT-4V outperforms other models, but still falls short of human performance. The paper also discusses the potential of GPT-4V's capabilities like self-verification and interactive chatbot capabilities. However, concerns are raised about the dataset's diversity and the need for more rigorous quantitative analysis and comparative studies with other models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to understand, and the dataset is diverse, encompassing various visual contexts and mathematical reasoning abilities.
- The benchmark is comprehensive, integrating 28 existing multimodal datasets and three newly created datasets, providing a robust evaluation of mathematical reasoning in visual contexts.
- The evaluation protocol is thorough, including both qualitative and quantitative studies, and the results are clearly presented, aiding in understanding the performance of models like GPT-4V.
- The paper introduces a novel benchmarking strategy for MathVista, which is distinct from traditional methods, and provides a detailed analysis of the results, including a discussion on the limitations of the benchmark.
- The dataset is well-documented, with clear definitions of mathematical reasoning types and primary tasks, and the data collection and annotation processes are detailed and rigorous.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the datasets were selected and curated, particularly concerning the process of filtering and selecting examples from existing datasets.
- There is a need for more quantitative analysis, such as precision, recall, and F1 scores, to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the models.
- The dataset's diversity is questioned, as it primarily focuses on math-related visual questions, which may not fully represent the capabilities of models in broader visual reasoning tasks.
- The paper could benefit from more comparative studies with other models, such as LLaVA, to better understand the performance of different models in similar tasks.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the benchmark, particularly concerning the scalability and generalizability of the findings to other domains.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential biases in the dataset, which could affect the fairness and accuracy of the evaluations.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on how the datasets were selected and curated, including the criteria used for filtering and the rationale behind the selection of examples?
- How does the dataset ensure that it covers a wide range of visual contexts and mathematical reasoning abilities, and how does it address the potential biases in the data?
- What are the specific challenges posed by the MathVista dataset, and how do these challenges differ from those posed by other visual reasoning datasets?
- Could you elaborate on the decision to focus on math-related visual questions and how this choice aligns with the broader goals of evaluating visual reasoning capabilities?
- How do you address the concerns about the generalizability of the findings from MathVista to other domains or types of visual reasoning tasks?
- In the evaluation of GPT-4V, how does the paper ensure that the results are not biased by the model's ability to generate plausible-sounding but incorrect answers?
