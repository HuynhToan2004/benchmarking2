 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel perspective on denoising diffusion models by interpreting them as gradient descent on the Euclidean distance function, which is approximately equivalent to denoising under the manifold hypothesis. The authors propose a new sampler that leverages this insight to improve the performance of diffusion models, particularly in the context of the DDIM sampler. This new approach is supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results, demonstrating state-of-the-art FID scores on datasets like CIFAR-10 and CelebA. The paper also discusses the implications of this perspective for the broader field of diffusion models, suggesting potential applications in other areas of machine learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel perspective on denoising diffusion models by interpreting them as gradient descent on the Euclidean distance function, which is both insightful and theoretically sound.
- The proposed sampler based on this insight achieves state-of-the-art FID scores on CIFAR-10 and CelebA datasets, demonstrating its practical utility.
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the theoretical results and empirical findings, making it accessible and understandable.
- The analysis and experiments conducted in the paper are rigorous and well-explained, contributing to the understanding of diffusion models and potentially advancing the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies heavily on the manifold hypothesis, which may not hold in practical scenarios, particularly when the dataset is not well-represented by a low-dimensional manifold.
- The improvement over existing methods like DDIM is marginal, and the empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive experimental validation, including a broader range of datasets and a comparison with a wider variety of state-of-the-art methods.
- The novelty of the proposed sampler is questioned, as it appears to be a minor modification of existing methods, and the theoretical contributions may not be sufficiently novel or distinct from prior works.
- There are concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed sampler, particularly in terms of its computational complexity and the need for specific conditions (e.g., low-dimensional manifolds) that may not be met in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed sampler perform when applied to high-resolution datasets like those used in Stable Diffusion?
- Can the authors provide more details on the specific conditions under which the manifold hypothesis holds, and discuss scenarios where it might not be valid?
- How does the proposed sampler compare in terms of computational complexity and practical applicability to other state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that do not rely on the manifold hypothesis?
- In light of the marginal improvements over existing methods, how does the proposed sampler offer a significant advancement in the field of diffusion models?
- Could the authors discuss the potential implications of their findings for the broader field of machine learning, particularly in areas such as optimization and constraint handling?
