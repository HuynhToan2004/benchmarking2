 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MemStranding, a novel adversarial attack framework designed for temporal graph neural networks (TGNNs). This framework leverages node memories to inject persistent and contagious noise into dynamic graphs, aiming to degrade TGNN performance in various tasks. The authors address challenges such as noise decay and knowledge missing by strategically introducing fake nodes and edges at arbitrary timestamps. MemStranding's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on multiple TGNN models and datasets, showing significant performance drops. However, the paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing baselines, which is crucial for validating its contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear motivation for the study of adversarial attacks on temporal graph neural networks (TGNNs).
- The proposed MemStranding framework is novel and effective, utilizing node memories to inject persistent and contagious noise into dynamic graphs, which is a significant advancement in the field of TGNNs.
- The experimental results demonstrate that MemStranding can significantly decrease the TGNN models' performances in various tasks, highlighting its practical applicability and impact.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the challenges in attacking TGNNs and proposes a practical and effective attack framework that addresses these challenges.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing baselines, particularly in terms of the number of nodes used and the performance metrics. This makes it difficult to assess the effectiveness of MemStranding against other state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the attack budget is utilized, which could affect the understanding of the attack's impact and efficiency.
- The paper primarily focuses on node classification and edge prediction tasks, limiting the generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks in TGNNs.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed attack framework.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing baselines, including the number of nodes used and the performance metrics under different attack budgets?
- How does the proposed attack framework perform against different types of TGNNs, such as those that use different memory update mechanisms or different types of node embeddings?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed attack framework and provide recommendations for mitigating these risks?
- How does the proposed attack framework perform against different types of defenses, such as adversarial training and regularization under empirical Lipschitz?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and efficiency of the attack framework, particularly in terms of the scalability with larger graphs and more nodes?
