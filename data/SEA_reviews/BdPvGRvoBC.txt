 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of gradient clipping in federated learning, specifically focusing on per-sample and per-update clipping strategies. It extends the analysis from Koloskova et al. (2023) to the federated learning setting, providing convergence guarantees for these clipping methods under standard assumptions like bounded variance and gradient dissimilarity. The paper also discusses the theoretical implications of these clipping strategies in differentially private settings, showing how they can be adjusted to achieve optimal privacy-utility trade-offs. Theoretical results are supported by empirical validations, demonstrating the practical relevance of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper extends the convergence analysis of clipped SGD to the federated learning setting, providing a rigorous theoretical analysis of the impact of gradient clipping on the convergence of the federated averaging (FedAvg) algorithm.
- The authors introduce a novel distributed (L0,L1)-smoothness assumption, which is tailored to the distributed setup and facilitates the proof of convergence.
- The paper provides a thorough privacy analysis, including the impact of stochastic noise and gradient dissimilarity on the clipped FedAvg, and offers insights into the appropriate stepsize and clipping threshold for optimal privacy-utility trade-offs.
- Theoretical results are supported by empirical validations, demonstrating the practical relevance and applicability of the findings.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear contributions and a comprehensive related work section.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a clearer distinction and justification for the choice between per-sample and per-update clipping strategies, as both are shown to have similar convergence behaviors.
- The practical implications of the findings are not thoroughly explored, particularly in terms of how the theoretical results translate to practical scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the work, which is a significant omission in the ethical considerations section.
- There are minor typographical errors and inconsistencies in the notation and terminology used throughout the paper, which could confuse readers.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the assumptions made and the potential impact of these assumptions on the applicability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the distinction and justification for choosing between per-sample and per-update clipping strategies, especially when both seem to have similar convergence behaviors?
- How do the theoretical results translate to practical scenarios, and what are the implications for federated learning applications?
- Can you provide a more detailed comparison with existing works, especially in terms of the convergence rates and the assumptions made?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the work, and how are these addressed or mitigated in your research?
- Could you elaborate on the practical implications of the findings, particularly in terms of improving the performance of federated learning algorithms in real-world scenarios?
