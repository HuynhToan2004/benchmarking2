 **Summary:**
The paper explores the phenomenon of oversmoothing in Transformer models, challenging the common belief that Transformers act as low-pass filters. It presents a theoretical analysis showing that the eigenvalues of the weight matrices in Transformers determine whether oversmoothing occurs. The authors propose a novel parameterization of the weight matrices to avoid oversmoothing, supported by empirical evidence. This parameterization is shown to improve robustness and data efficiency in vision Transformers. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the design of Transformer architectures and the understanding of feature convergence in deep networks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel analysis of the phenomenon of oversmoothing in Transformers, challenging the common belief that Transformers act as low-pass filters.
- The authors propose a new parameterization of the weight matrices in Transformers to avoid oversmoothing, which is supported by empirical evidence showing improvements in robustness and data efficiency.
- The paper is well-written and clearly presents the theoretical analysis and empirical results, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The findings have implications for the design of Transformer architectures, suggesting that feature convergence in deep networks can be controlled by adjusting the eigenvalues of the weight matrices.
- The paper includes a comprehensive literature review, situating the current work within the context of existing research on Transformer architectures.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the implications of the proposed parameterization for the expressiveness of Transformers, particularly how it affects the capacity of the model to learn complex features.
- The empirical results are limited to vision Transformers, and it is unclear whether the findings generalize to other types of Transformers such as those used in natural language processing (NLP).
- The paper does not include experiments on NLP models, which could provide insights into the applicability of the proposed parameterization across different domains.
- The theoretical analysis assumes fixed attention and weight matrices, which may not reflect the practical scenarios where these matrices are learned during training.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed parameterization, such as potential issues with training stability or the computational cost of additional parameters.
- The empirical results are not comprehensive, and the paper does not include experiments on larger or more complex models, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed parameterization affects the expressiveness of Transformers and whether it could lead to a loss of capacity to learn complex features?
- How does the proposed parameterization perform in other types of Transformers, such as those used in NLP?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training stability of the models with the proposed parameterization, especially concerning the convergence of the eigenvalues of the weight matrices?
- How does the proposed parameterization impact the computational cost of training and inference, and what are the implications for practical deployment?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the eigendecomposition parameterization and discuss how it affects the training and inference times compared to other parameterizations?
- Is there a possibility to extend the theoretical analysis to include the effects of multi-head attention and the linear layer following the residual connection?
