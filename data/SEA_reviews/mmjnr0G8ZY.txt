 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multi-resolution diffusion model (mr-Diff) for time series forecasting, which leverages seasonal-trend decomposition to extract trends from time series data and denoises them in a non-autoregressive manner. This model is designed to handle the unique challenges of time series data by utilizing a multi-resolution approach that decomposes the data into coarser and finer trends, allowing for a more accurate prediction of time series. The model is evaluated on multiple datasets and shows competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art models, with a focus on improving inference efficiency. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as it primarily extends existing methods to the time series domain without significant innovation, and for its lack of detailed theoretical analysis and comprehensive empirical evaluation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the motivation is clear.
- The idea of using a multi-resolution approach to handle time series data is reasonable and innovative.
- The proposed model achieves good performance and shows a good trade-off between performance and inference speed.
- The paper includes a comprehensive review of related work and provides a detailed description of the proposed model.
- The experimental results are solid, and the paper includes a detailed comparison with previous works.
- The use of multi-resolution diffusion models for time series forecasting is a novel and interesting approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the limitations and failure cases of the proposed model.
- The theoretical analysis of the proposed model is not sufficient, and the paper could benefit from a more in-depth exploration of the model's capabilities and limitations.
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily extends existing methods to the time series domain without significant innovation.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive set of experiments, particularly missing experiments on multivariate time series forecasting.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the model's performance.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with the latest state-of-the-art models, which could affect the assessment of its competitiveness.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the relationship between the proposed model and existing models, such as ScaleFormer.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the model's performance?
- Why was the decision made to use a diffusion model for time series forecasting, and how does it compare to other models like ScaleFormer?
- How does the proposed model handle non-stationary time series, and what are the implications for its performance in such scenarios?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with the latest state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of inference efficiency and performance on multivariate time series forecasting?
- How does the proposed model perform in scenarios where the training data and test data have different lengths, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the standard deviation of the results and a comparison with the latest state-of-the-art models?
