 **Summary:**
The paper explores the transferability of adversarial examples across different prompts in Vision-Language Models (VLMs). Specifically, it introduces the Cross-Prompt Attack (CroPA) method, which optimizes both visual and textual perturbations to enhance the transferability of adversarial examples across various prompts. The methodology is evaluated on several VLMs, including Flamingo, BLIP-2, and InstructBLIP, across tasks such as VQA, classification, and captioning. The experiments demonstrate that CroPA outperforms existing methods, showcasing its effectiveness in manipulating VLMs across different prompts. However, the paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation on a broader range of models and tasks, and the practical implications of the findings are not thoroughly discussed.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel perspective on adversarial transferability by focusing on the cross-prompt adversarial transferability, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method, Cross-Prompt Attack (CroPA), is innovative and effectively improves the transferability of adversarial examples across prompts, as demonstrated through extensive experiments.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering various tasks and models, and the results are convincing, highlighting the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The research addresses an important and timely problem of adversarial robustness in Vision-Language Models (VLMs), which is crucial for understanding the security and reliability of these models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a broader range of models and tasks in its evaluation to more comprehensively demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The practical implications of the findings are not thoroughly discussed, which is essential for understanding the real-world applications and limitations of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the conditions under which it may not be effective.
- The experiments are limited to only three models, and it would be beneficial to include more models to validate the robustness and generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the effectiveness of different optimization strategies and the impact of different perturbation types.
- The paper's focus on the transferability across prompts could be seen as somewhat limited, as it does not address the broader issue of transferability across different models or datasets.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of transferability across different models and datasets?
- Could you provide a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the findings and the potential limitations of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other optimization strategies, such as query-based methods?
- What are the specific conditions under which the proposed method may not be effective, and how do these conditions affect the transferability of adversarial examples?
- How does the proposed method perform on different datasets, such as those with varying image and prompt distributions?
- Could you discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its application in real-world scenarios?
