 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to learning belief updates in Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes (POMDPs) by leveraging a latent space model learned using the Wasserstein Auto-Encoded MDP (WAE-MDP). This model is trained to mimic the dynamics of the original POMDP, and the belief encoder is optimized to minimize the Wasserstein distance to the true belief update rule. The method is supported by theoretical guarantees and demonstrated through empirical results on the POMDPs from POMDPGym and a custom environment. The paper also discusses the challenges in training the belief encoder and proposes using KL divergence as a tractable alternative. Despite the innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its clarity in explaining the methodology, the choice of baselines, and the practical applicability of the assumptions made.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning belief updates in POMDPs using a latent space model, which is both innovative and theoretically sound.
- The use of Wasserstein Auto-Encoded MDPs (WAE-MDPs) for training the belief encoder is a novel application, and the method's theoretical guarantees are a significant contribution to the field.
- The empirical results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in certain environments, indicating its practical effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written and the methodology is clearly explained, making it accessible to readers with a background in reinforcement learning.
- Theoretical guarantees provided by the paper are robust, which adds to the credibility of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the methodology, particularly in how the belief encoder is trained and the role of the KL divergence in this process.
- The choice of baselines is not convincing, as the paper does not compare against the most recent or relevant methods, such as those that use similar approaches like DVRL.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the availability of the true state during training, are not well-justified or practical in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's empirical results are not convincing enough to demonstrate the method's effectiveness across a variety of environments.
- The scalability of the proposed method to more complex environments, such as those involving high-dimensional observations, is not discussed, which raises concerns about its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's theoretical analysis and experimental results do not convincingly support the claims made about the method's ability to learn optimal value functions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and training of the belief encoder, particularly how it is optimized using the Wasserstein distance?
- Why was the KL divergence chosen as an alternative to the Wasserstein distance, and how does this affect the theoretical guarantees provided by the method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent works in the field, such as those using similar approaches like DVRL?
- In terms of practical applicability, how does the method handle scenarios where the true state is not available during training?
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability of the method to more complex environments, such as those involving high-dimensional observations?
- How does the method handle the practical challenges of learning the belief encoder, and what are the implications of these challenges for the method's effectiveness?
