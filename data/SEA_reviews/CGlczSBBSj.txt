 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SEAL, a framework designed to evaluate real-world super-resolution (SR) methods by clustering degradation cases and proposing new evaluation metrics such as acceptance rate (AR) and relative performance ratio (RPR). The framework is aimed at providing a more systematic and comprehensive evaluation of SR methods, which is crucial given the vast degradation space in real-world applications. The paper presents a coarse-to-fine evaluation protocol, new metrics, and benchmarks existing real-SR methods, offering valuable insights into their performance. The framework's approach is seen as a significant step towards creating a comprehensive real-SR evaluation platform, which could potentially enhance the development of real-SR methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant issue of evaluating real-world super-resolution (SR) methods, which is crucial for advancing the field.
- The proposed framework, SEAL, is innovative and provides a systematic way to evaluate the performance of SR methods across various degradation cases, which is more comprehensive and reliable compared to traditional methods.
- The introduction of new metrics such as acceptance rate (AR) and relative performance ratio (RPR) offers a more nuanced evaluation of SR methods, which can lead to more accurate comparisons and insights.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed framework in evaluating SR methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how the clustering process works, which is crucial for understanding the framework's effectiveness.
- The choice of the number of clusters (K) and its impact on the evaluation results is not thoroughly discussed, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential bias introduced by the clustering process, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation of different SR methods, including those not evaluated, to provide a more robust benchmarking.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed framework, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of the rationale behind the choice of specific evaluation metrics, such as AR and RPR, which could affect the perceived fairness and effectiveness of the evaluation.

**Questions:**
- How does the clustering process work in detail, and what are the specific parameters or methods used?
- How is the number of clusters (K) determined, and what is the impact of this choice on the evaluation results?
- Can you provide more details on how the acceptance line and excellence line are determined, and how they are chosen from the existing SR methods?
- How does the proposed framework handle different types of degradation, such as blurring, resizing, and noise, which may require different evaluation strategies?
- Could you elaborate on the potential biases introduced by the clustering process and how these might affect the evaluation results?
- How does the proposed framework compare to other existing methods in terms of fairness and effectiveness in evaluating SR methods?
- Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the choice of specific evaluation metrics, such as AR and RPR?
