 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Free-form Injective Flows (FIF), a novel approach to training normalizing flows that addresses the limitations of existing methods such as rectangular flows. FIF replaces the need for conjugate gradient iterations to estimate the gradient of the log-determinant term with a more efficient method, thereby improving computational efficiency and potentially reducing training times. The method also introduces a new loss function that helps stabilize training by avoiding divergent solutions. Experiments are conducted on various datasets including tabular, MNIST, and CelebA, demonstrating the practicality and scalability of the proposed method. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the method and provides a detailed comparison with existing works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and efficient estimator for the maximum likelihood loss, which is compatible with free-form bottleneck architectures.
- The authors have identified pathological behavior in naive maximum likelihood training in the presence of a bottleneck and have proposed a solution to avoid this behavior while maintaining computational efficiency.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their theoretical justifications.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the practicality and scalability of the proposed method, showing that it outperforms existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with existing works, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of different methods, and discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other recent works such as conformal embedding flows, which also address the issue of low-dimensional latent spaces in normalizing flows.
- The theoretical contributions of the paper are not clearly distinguished from those of existing works like rectangular flows, which might lead to confusion about the novelty of the proposed method.
- There are concerns about the practical applicability of the method due to the large number of parameters required for training, which could limit its scalability to larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its generalizability and applicability to different types of data distributions.
- The experimental results are not consistently superior to existing methods, particularly in terms of FID scores, which are sometimes lower for other methods.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experiments, including additional datasets and a broader range of model architectures to better demonstrate the effectiveness and scalability of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with conformal embedding flows, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the ability to model different types of data distributions?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability with other recent works that address similar issues in normalizing flows?
- Could the authors clarify the theoretical contributions of their work, particularly in relation to existing methods like rectangular flows?
- What are the practical implications of the large number of parameters required for training the proposed method, and how does this affect its scalability to larger, more complex datasets?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of data distributions, and what are the limitations of its applicability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly in terms of the hardware and software used, to ensure a fair comparison with other methods?
