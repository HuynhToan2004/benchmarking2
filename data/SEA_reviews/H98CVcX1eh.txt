 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the compositional generalization of modular neural networks through the use of hypernetworks, which serve as a teacher-student setup. The authors demonstrate that under certain conditions, such as compositional and connected support, the student network can learn the teacher's parameters up to a linear transformation, thereby achieving compositional generalization. This is supported by theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, showing that modular architectures can outperform monolithic ones in tasks with compositional structure. The paper also discusses the limitations of these architectures, such as overfitting and the requirement for specific data distributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to compositional generalization using modular architectures, specifically hypernetworks, which is a promising direction for future research.
- The theoretical analysis is well-articulated and supported by empirical evidence, showing that modular architectures can outperform monolithic ones in tasks with compositional structure.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear and intuitive explanations of the theoretical concepts.
- The experiments are well-designed, with clear and convincing results that support the theoretical claims.
- The paper includes a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the approach, which is appreciated for its transparency and honesty.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the teacher network is linear, which may not hold in practical scenarios where non-linear networks are more commonly used.
- The requirement for the student network to have the same dimensions as the teacher network may limit its applicability in real-world settings where different network architectures are used.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in large-scale datasets, which could affect its practical utility.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to linear hypernetworks, which may not generalize well to more complex, non-linear scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the approach, particularly how it might not generalize to more complex or varied data distributions.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the results might be affected by the specifics of the hypernetwork architecture, such as the number of modules and their interactions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the student network learns the latent task variable in the experiments, and whether this is a fair comparison given the different learning processes involved?
- How does the paper address the issue of overfitting, especially in scenarios where the student network has more capacity than the teacher network?
- Could the authors elaborate on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the smoothness assumption and the non-degeneracy of parameters?
- How does the paper handle the identification of modules when the number of modules is large, and what are the implications of this for the practical applicability of the findings?
- In the context of the experimental results, how does the paper ensure that the tasks are not too similar, and what are the implications of this for the generalizability of the findings?
