 **Summary:**
The paper presents a novel approach to vehicle routing problems (VRPs) by treating them as different combinations of a set of shared underlying attributes, enabling the application of multi-task learning. The authors propose a unified model that leverages reinforcement learning to train on multiple VRPs with varying attributes, demonstrating zero-shot generalization capabilities on unseen VRPs. The model's effectiveness is validated through experiments on eleven VRPs, showcasing competitive results compared to single-task models and promising zero-shot generalization capabilities. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, insufficient comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, and the need for more comprehensive performance metrics and baseline comparisons.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to solving vehicle routing problems (VRPs) by treating them as different combinations of a set of shared underlying attributes, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The authors propose a unified model with a novel attribute composition block, which shows a promising zero-shot generalization ability to handle any combination of basic attributes.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear and concise presentation that effectively communicates the main ideas and contributions.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that the unified model performs comparably to single-task models and exhibits zero-shot generalization capabilities on unseen VRPs.
- The paper is innovative in its application of multi-task learning to solve VRPs, which is a relatively new and under-explored area in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is somewhat limited as it primarily applies existing techniques such as reinforcement learning and multi-task learning to a new problem domain.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance metrics and baseline comparisons.
- The experimental results, while promising, are not sufficiently robust or comprehensive, with a limited number of instances used in the experiments and a lack of comparison with more recent or advanced methods.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, especially in the description of the attribute composition block and the experimental setup.
- There is a notable absence of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts, which could be critical for understanding the broader implications of the work.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the training process, particularly how the model is trained on multiple VRPs simultaneously and how the training instances are generated?
- How does the proposed method compare with other advanced multi-task learning techniques and approaches, such as prompt learning, in terms of zero-shot generalization performance?
- Can you provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance metrics and baseline comparisons?
- How does the model perform when trained on a larger set of attributes, and how does this affect the generalization capabilities across different VRP variants?
- Could you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of your work and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
