 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the challenge of using uni-modal data to learn cross-modal tasks, focusing on the modality gap in multi-modal contrastive learning. The authors propose a three-step method called C3, which includes Connect, Collapse, and Corrupt, to enhance the interchangeability of embeddings from different modalities. This method is validated through experiments on various tasks such as image/audio/video captioning and text-to-image generation, demonstrating state-of-the-art results. The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the modality gap and proposes a method to address it, which could potentially reduce the need for paired multi-modal data in cross-modal learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on various tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper provides a theoretical explanation of the modality gap and proposes a method to address it, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments are well-designed and comprehensive, including ablation studies that validate the proposed method.
- The paper is original in its approach to addressing the modality gap in multi-modal contrastive learning, which is a significant challenge in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed explanations for certain claims, such as the effectiveness of the collapse operation and the necessity of adding Gaussian noise.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where it may not be effective.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its results with existing methods, such as those mentioned in the related works section.
- The paper's methodology is somewhat ad-hoc and does not provide a comprehensive explanation for why certain components of the method are effective.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations for the effectiveness of the collapse operation and the necessity of adding Gaussian noise?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods in terms of computational complexity and practical application?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and roles of certain variables used in the paper, such as $f_x$ and $f_y$?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the modality gap is not present or is significantly reduced?
- Is there a possibility to extend the proposed method to other types of multi-modal tasks, such as audio-text or video-text tasks?
