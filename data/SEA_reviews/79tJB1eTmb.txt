 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Meta-CoT, a novel chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting method designed for mixed-task scenarios where the type of input questions is unknown. Meta-CoT categorizes scenarios based on the input question and constructs diverse demonstrations from the corresponding data pool using an automatic pattern. It achieves remarkable performance on ten public benchmark reasoning tasks and superior generalization capabilities. The methodology involves a three-step process: first, the scenario is identified; second, demonstrations are selected; and third, the answer is derived. The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across various datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of Meta-CoT in out-of-distribution scenarios. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, unclear methodological descriptions, and potential over-reliance on specific datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the motivation, methodology, and results.
- The proposed method, Meta-CoT, is straightforward and easy to implement, requiring only a few hyperparameters to tune.
- Meta-CoT shows superior generalization capabilities and achieves state-of-the-art results on SVAMP and GSM8K.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across various datasets, including both in-distribution and out-of-distribution scenarios, demonstrating the robustness of the proposed method.
- The authors conduct a thorough analysis of the method's performance, including the impact of scenario identification and the effectiveness of different demonstration construction methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the "demonstration selection" phase, which is crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness.
- There is a noticeable overlap in content with prior works, particularly in the use of in-context learning and the categorization of reasoning tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to diverse types of questions or its scalability to more complex scenarios.
- The methodology, particularly the scenario identification and demonstration selection phases, seems overly reliant on specific datasets and may not generalize well to other types of questions.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the "demonstration selection" phase, particularly how the questions are selected and how this impacts the method's effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the question types are not clearly defined or overlap?
- Could the authors provide more details on the methodology used for scenario identification and demonstration selection, especially how these processes are adapted for different types of questions?
- How does the paper address the potential limitations of the proposed method, such as its scalability and applicability to diverse types of questions?
- What are the specific negative societal impacts that the proposed method might have, and how does the paper address these concerns?
