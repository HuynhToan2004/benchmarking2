 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Reinforcement Learning Contemplation (RLC) for language model self-improvement, which leverages the observation that evaluating a sentence is simpler than generating it. The method uses a reinforcement learning algorithm to update the language model parameters based on self-generated answers, aiming to improve performance on tasks such as reasoning and summarization. The approach is tested on various datasets and tasks, showing promising results compared to existing methods like RLAIF. The paper argues that RLC's effectiveness stems from the simplicity of evaluation tasks compared to generation tasks, which is substantiated by experiments. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach and the need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the experiments are clearly described, making the methodology accessible.
- The proposed method, Reinforcement Learning Contemplation (RLC), is novel and addresses an important problem in language model self-improvement.
- The paper provides extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of RLC, showing its ability to improve language model performance without external supervision.
- The authors have presented a novel method for unsupervised training of LLMs, which eliminates the need for training a reward model and is more straightforward than previous methods.
- The proposed method is evaluated on multiple datasets and tasks, showing its effectiveness and broad applicability.
- The paper introduces a novel LMSI method for unsupervised training of LLMs, which is more straightforward and eliminates the need for training a reward model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods like RLAIF and other self-improvement methods, which could have strengthened the argument for the superiority of RLC.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as it appears similar to existing methods like RLAIF, and the paper does not adequately distinguish its contributions.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational cost of RLC compared to other methods, which is a critical aspect for practical implementation.
- The paper's reliance on the heuristic assumption that AI models can provide effective feedback and correct errors is not adequately justified, and the method's performance may depend on the quality of the feedback provided.
- The paper does not address the potential limitations of RLC, such as its effectiveness on larger language models or its generalizability to different domains.
- The paper's focus on using a single model for both evaluation and improvement raises concerns about the reliability of the evaluation results and the potential for overfitting.

**Questions:**
- How does RLC compare to RLAIF in terms of computational cost and performance?
- Can you clarify the differences between RLC and RLAIF, particularly in how they approach the self-improvement of language models?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting when using a single model for both evaluation and improvement?
- Could you provide more details on the computational cost of RLC compared to other methods?
- How does the method perform on larger language models, and what are the potential limitations of RLC in different domains or tasks?
- Can you discuss the potential for using RLC in more invasive ways, such as updating the LLM parameters directly?
- How does the method ensure that the feedback provided by the AI model is effective and reliable, especially in scenarios where the AI model may not be perfect?
