 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedAIoT, a federated learning benchmark tailored for Artificial Intelligence of Things (AIoT) applications. This benchmark comprises eight datasets sourced from various IoT devices, each with distinct characteristics such as data types and sizes. The authors propose a unified end-to-end federated learning framework, which includes non-IID data partitioning, IoT-specific data preprocessing, IoT-friendly models, federated learning hyperparameters, and an IoT-factor emulator. The benchmark is designed to address the challenges of federated learning in AIoT scenarios, including non-IID data, noisy labels, and quantized training. The paper provides a detailed analysis of the benchmark's performance under different conditions and discusses the implications for future research in this area.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmark for federated learning in AIoT scenarios, which is the first of its kind and fills a significant gap in the field.
- The benchmark includes eight datasets sourced from various IoT devices, each with unique characteristics, which provides a comprehensive evaluation of federated learning algorithms in real-world AIoT applications.
- The authors propose a unified end-to-end federated learning framework for AIoT, which covers the complete federated learning pipeline and includes non-IID data partitioning, IoT-specific data preprocessing, IoT-friendly models, federated learning hyperparameters, and an IoT-factor emulator.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the benchmark's performance under different conditions, including the impact of varying degrees of non-IID data distributions, federated optimizers, and client sampling ratios on the performance of federated learning.
- The introduction of a novel way to design noisy labels and the consideration of quantized training during client-side training are significant contributions to the field of federated learning in AIoT.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and description of the IoT-factor emulator, which is a crucial component of the proposed framework.
- There is a lack of detail regarding the non-IID data partitioning schemes used in the benchmark, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the specific IoT devices used to collect the data, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the benchmark.
- The paper does not include a comparison with other federated learning benchmarks, which could help to establish the superiority or uniqueness of the proposed benchmark.
- The selection of datasets is limited, and the paper does not discuss the generalizability of the results across different datasets or the impact of varying data distributions on the performance of federated learning algorithms.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework, which could help to identify potential areas for improvement or further research.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed description of the IoT-factor emulator, including its design and implementation details?
- How were the non-IID data partitioning schemes selected, and what criteria were used to determine the number of samples allocated to each client?
- What specific IoT devices were used to collect the data, and how do these devices affect the data collection and processing processes?
- How does the proposed benchmark compare to other federated learning benchmarks, particularly in terms of performance and applicability to real-world AIoT scenarios?
- Could you discuss the generalizability of the results across different datasets and the impact of varying data distributions on the performance of federated learning algorithms?
- What are the main limitations of the proposed framework, and how do these limitations affect the practical applicability and scalability of the benchmark in real-world AIoT scenarios?
