 **Summary:**
The paper explores the local representation capabilities of self-supervised Vision Transformers (ViTs) through a series of experiments and comparisons. It evaluates the performance of various models like DINO, DINOv2, MAE, and SimMIM, focusing on their ability to perform few-shot tasks such as patch classification, fine-grained retrieval, and object association in video frames. The study reveals that DINO and DINOv2 perform well in these tasks, with DINOv2 showing robustness against image degradations. The paper also highlights the poor performance of masked image modeling methods like MAE and SimMIM in k-NN patch classification, suggesting that high-variance features in these models are not informative for downstream tasks. The experiments are conducted across different datasets and tasks, providing insights into the strengths and limitations of different self-supervised learning methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the local representation capabilities of self-supervised ViTs, comparing them to supervised and masked image modeling methods.
- It evaluates the performance of different models across various downstream tasks, including patch classification, fine-grained retrieval, and object association in video frames, using both k-NN and linear probing methods.
- The study identifies the causes of poor performance in k-NN methods, such as high-variance features in masked image modeling methods, and proposes a method to remove these features to improve performance.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation framework and datasets to analyze the inherent power of pretrained Visual Transformers for locality-aware tasks.
- The findings, such as the superior performance of DINO and DINOv2 in certain tasks and the poor performance of MAE in k-NN patch classification, are significant and contribute to a better understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of different self-supervised learning methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the findings.
- The experiments are limited to a few datasets and tasks, which might not generalize well to other scenarios or domains.
- There is a lack of comparison with other self-supervised learning methods, such as those based on contrastive learning, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the models.
- The paper does not discuss the scalability of the findings with larger datasets or more complex models, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The analysis is primarily based on the DINO series of models, which might not fully represent the capabilities of other self-supervised learning methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed methods, especially in large-scale applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations on why DINOv2 performs worse in some tasks despite being trained on more data?
- How do the findings generalize to other datasets and tasks beyond those used in the study?
- What are the implications of the findings for the design of future self-supervised learning methods?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies in the performance of different models, such as the poor performance of MAE in k-NN patch classification?
- How do the findings relate to the broader landscape of self-supervised learning methods, and what are the potential applications of the insights gained from this study?
