 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MUSE, a method designed for multimodal patient representation learning that addresses the challenges of missing modalities and labels in healthcare data. MUSE employs a bipartite graph to model the patient-modality relationship and a mutual-consistent contrastive loss to learn modality-agnostic and label-decisive features. The method is evaluated on three public datasets and shows superior performance over existing baselines. However, the paper's novelty is questioned as it resembles existing methods, and the theoretical underpinnings and the robustness of the results against hyperparameter choices are not adequately addressed.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and practical issue in healthcare data analysis, focusing on the handling of missing modalities and labels, which is crucial for accurate patient representation.
- The proposed method, MUSE, is well-articulated and supported by a comprehensive evaluation on three public datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, including an ablation study and runtime analysis, which provide a comprehensive view of MUSE's performance and efficiency.
- The proposed method shows superior performance over existing baselines, indicating its effectiveness in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it builds upon existing methods like GRAPE and HGMF, with only minor modifications.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of MUSE, particularly how it learns modality-agnostic and label-decisive features.
- There is a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods in the field, which could have provided a more robust validation of MUSE's effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impact of the proposed method, which could be significant in healthcare applications.
- The results presented in the paper are not robust against hyperparameter choices, which could affect the reliability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of MUSE, particularly how it learns modality-agnostic and label-decisive features?
- How does MUSE handle the imbalance in the number of patients with complete versus incomplete data, and what are the implications for the model's performance?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in the field to validate MUSE's effectiveness more robustly?
- What are the specific challenges in applying MUSE to other types of data beyond healthcare data, and how might these applications affect the model's performance?
- How does MUSE handle the issue of missing labels, particularly in scenarios where labels are not available for a significant portion of the data?
- Could the authors provide more details on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the results, including a sensitivity analysis if possible?
