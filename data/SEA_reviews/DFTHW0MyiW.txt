 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for adversarial robustness in reinforcement learning (RL), focusing on the test-time robustness of RL policies against non-worst-case attacks. It proposes a method that iteratively discovers a set of non-dominated policies during training, which are then adapted during test time to minimize regret. This approach allows for robustness against a variety of attack scenarios, including periodic and probabilistic attacks, and maintains natural performance. The paper also explores the theoretical implications of this method, providing guarantees on the optimality of the discovered policy set and the convergence of the algorithm. However, the paper faces criticism for its high computational overhead, lack of clarity in some theoretical proofs, and limited empirical evaluation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise language that facilitates understanding.
- The framework introduced in the paper is novel and innovative, addressing the challenge of ensuring robustness of RL policies against non-worst-case attacks, a significant issue in real-world applications.
- The paper presents a comprehensive set of theoretical results, including theorems and propositions, which provide a strong foundation for the proposed methods.
- The empirical results are convincing, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing baselines in terms of both natural performance and robustness.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to online adaptation, which is a significant departure from the traditional approach of solving a minimax problem for robustness.
- The use of adversarial bandit subroutines for efficient adaptation within a finite policy class is an interesting and potentially practical solution for achieving robustness in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some theoretical proofs, particularly in the proof of Theorem 4.10, which is crucial for understanding the optimality of the discovered policy set.
- The computational overhead of the proposed method is high, which could limit its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The empirical evaluation is limited, focusing only on a few environments and not including a broader range of baselines, which could undermine the generalizability of the results.
- The paper's assumptions about the attacker's policy space and the transition dynamics being Lipschitz are not well-justified, which could limit the applicability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the computational resources required to implement it in more complex environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these could be addressed in future work.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the proof of Theorem 4.10 and provide more detailed explanations on how the optimality of the discovered policy set is guaranteed?
- How does the proposed method perform in environments with more complex state and action spaces, such as those found in robotic manipulation tasks?
- Could the authors provide more detailed implementation and hyperparameter details for the baselines used in the experiments?
- How does the proposed method compare to other robust training methods, such as ATLA-PPO and PA-ATLA-PPO, in terms of computational overhead and practical applicability?
- Can the authors discuss the potential implications of the assumptions about the attacker's policy space and the transition dynamics being Lipschitz on the robustness of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between natural performance and robustness, particularly in scenarios where the attacker's policy space is unknown or dynamic?
