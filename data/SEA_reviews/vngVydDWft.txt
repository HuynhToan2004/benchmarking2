 **Summary:**
The paper explores the problem of aligning latent spaces across different neural networks trained on the same data. It introduces a method for merging latent spaces by learning a set of invariances using different aggregation methods. The proposed method is tested on various modalities including vision, text, and graphs, demonstrating improvements in zero-shot stitching performance. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of novelty, as it heavily relies on existing methods like the relative representation framework and does not sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work. The experimental evaluations are also seen as insufficient, with concerns about the fairness of comparisons and the robustness of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors have conducted comprehensive experiments across various domains, including vision, text, and graphs, which demonstrate the versatility of their method.
- The proposed method is intuitive and simple, making it easy to understand and implement.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the method, including detailed ablation studies and analysis of different architectures.
- The method's application is broad, as demonstrated by its effectiveness across different domains.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it heavily relies on existing methods like the relative representation framework, which have been previously proposed in other works.
- The experimental evaluations are insufficient and not convincing, with a lack of detailed comparisons to prior work and an inadequate discussion of the results.
- The method's performance is not consistently superior to existing methods, as shown in some experiments where it underperforms or shows no significant improvement.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of scalability and the impact of different invariances on performance.
- The writing could be improved in some sections to enhance clarity and understanding.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of their work beyond the existing methods, particularly in terms of the experimental setup and results?
- How does the method compare to other existing methods, especially in terms of performance and scalability?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons and discussions of the results, especially in the appendix where additional experiments and discussions are presented?
- What are the implications of using different invariances in the method, and how do these affect the performance and scalability of the method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the architectures used and the training procedures?
- How does the method handle different types of transformations, and what are the implications of using different types of invariances in the method?
