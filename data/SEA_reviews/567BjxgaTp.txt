 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the problem of lie detection in large language models (LLMs) by proposing a method that involves asking unrelated yes/no questions to elicit responses from the LLM, which are then used to train a logistic regression classifier. The approach is tested across various LLMs and settings, including factual and ambiguous questions, and shows promising results in detecting lies. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments that show the model's ability to generalize across different LLMs and scenarios, including real-world applications. However, concerns about the practicality of the method in real-world settings and the potential for spurious correlations are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a timely and important problem of lie detection in LLMs, which is crucial for AI safety and trustworthiness.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and does not require access to the LLM's activations, making it practical for real-world applications.
- The experiments are extensive, covering various LLMs, settings, and scenarios, demonstrating the method's effectiveness and generalizability.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed experimental setups and analyses that support the claims made.
- The method shows promise in detecting lies in LLMs, even in real-world scenarios, which could be a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's effectiveness relies on the assumption that LLMs will behave differently when lying versus telling the truth, which may not always hold true.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in real-world applications where the method may not be as effective due to the complexity of real-world scenarios.
- The method's reliance on a fixed set of elicitation questions may limit its adaptability to different LLMs and scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of spurious correlations, which could affect the method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's experimental design and results could benefit from more rigorous testing and validation, particularly in scenarios where the method's assumptions may not hold true.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the method's assumptions about LLM behavior when lying versus telling the truth, and how these assumptions were tested?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the LLM's lying behavior deviates from the assumptions made by the method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the dataset used in the experiments, particularly how it was collected and validated?
- How does the method's effectiveness vary across different LLMs and scenarios, and what are the implications of these variations for the practical application of the method?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for spurious correlations in the method's results and how these were addressed in the experimental design?
- How does the method's performance compare to other methods for lie detection in LLMs, and what are the advantages or disadvantages of the proposed method compared to these alternatives?
