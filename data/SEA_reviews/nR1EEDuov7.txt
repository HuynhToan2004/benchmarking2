 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for detecting generated images by injecting imperceptible signatures into a pre-trained generative model. This signature is then used to train a detector that can classify both original and generated images, enhancing the model's robustness against various attacks. The methodology involves a two-stage training process where the signature injector and the generator are fine-tuned to ensure the signature's imperceptibility and robustness. The paper provides empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness through experiments on datasets like FFHQ and ImageNet, comparing it with existing methods. However, concerns were raised about the method's practicality, scalability, and the depth of experimental validation, particularly in terms of its ability to generalize across different datasets and generative models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for detecting generated images, which is a significant contribution to the field of digital forensics.
- The proposed method is technically sound and well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The use of adversarial signatures to enhance the robustness of generative models against various attacks is a novel approach.
- The paper provides empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness through experiments on datasets like FFHQ and ImageNet, which demonstrates the method's practical applicability.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly presents the methodology, making it accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance metrics like FID, which could undermine the claimed effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method's practicality is questionable, especially in scenarios where the signature injector and the detector need to be trained together, which may not be feasible in real-world applications.
- The scalability of the method is a concern, particularly in terms of its applicability to different datasets and generative models, as the method's effectiveness might vary depending on the specifics of the models and datasets used.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for malicious users to adapt to the proposed method by removing the signatures, which could limit the method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity in the experimental setup and results, particularly in the description of the datasets used and the details of the experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and reliability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the adversarial signature in the context of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its ability to enhance the robustness of the generative model against various attacks?
- How does the method perform when applied to different datasets and generative models, and what are the specific conditions under which it might not be effective?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, including a discussion on the performance metrics used and the specific advantages of their method over others?
- In terms of practical implementation, how feasible is it to train the signature injector and the detector together, and what are the implications of this approach for the scalability and adaptability of the method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly in terms of the datasets used, the parameters of the models, and the specifics of the experiments, to enhance the reproducibility and reliability of the results?
