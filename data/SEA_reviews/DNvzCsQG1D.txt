 **Summary:**
The paper introduces InstructionGPT-4, a novel approach to fine-tuning multimodal large language models (MLLMs) using a smaller, high-quality dataset. The authors propose a data selector module trained on multiple quality metrics to automatically filter low-quality data, enabling the model to outperform the baseline MiniGPT-4 on various benchmarks. The methodology involves selecting a subset of high-quality instructions from a larger dataset, which is then used to fine-tune the model. The paper also introduces a new metric for evaluating data quality and demonstrates that a smaller dataset can be more effective than a larger one with lower-quality data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive set of experiments and ablations that support the claims made.
- The introduction of a learnable data selector to automatically identify and filter low-quality vision-language data is a novel and interesting approach.
- The paper demonstrates that a smaller dataset of high-quality instructions can be more effective than a larger dataset with lower-quality data, which is a significant finding.
- The proposed method is shown to be effective across various benchmarks, outperforming the baseline MiniGPT-4 in most cases.
- The use of multiple metrics to measure the quality of multimodal instruction data is a valuable contribution to the field, as it helps in the selection of high-quality data for fine-tuning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other existing methods for data selection and data pruning, which could have strengthened the claims made.
- The selection of the 200-instruction subset seems arbitrary and not well-justified, which raises questions about the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the potential for overfitting due to the limited data used for training the data selector.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a single model (MiniGPT-4), which does not provide a comprehensive view of its effectiveness across different models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the data selector architecture and the rationale behind the selection of specific metrics for measuring data quality.

**Questions:**
- How is the number of high-quality instructions (200) determined, and is it consistent across different datasets?
- Can the authors provide more details on the data selector architecture, particularly how it operates and why it was chosen over other potential architectures?
- What is the impact of the data selector on the model's performance when the number of high-quality instructions is increased?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other models or datasets, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
- Could the authors clarify the process of selecting the genuine quality labels and how they are used to train the data selector?
- Is there a plan to release the code and trained data selector to facilitate reproducibility and further research in this area?
