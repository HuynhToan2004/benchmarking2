 **Summary:**
The paper introduces EquiPocket, a novel E(3)-equivariant Graph Neural Network (GNN) designed for ligand binding site prediction. The model integrates three key modules: a local geometric module, a global structure module, and a surface message passing module, which are evaluated on various datasets, demonstrating superior performance over existing methods. The authors address issues such as irregular protein structures, sensitivity to rotations, insufficient protein surface characterization, and protein size shift, which are critical in drug discovery. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods. The experimental setup and the clarity of some figures and methodological descriptions also require improvement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to binding site prediction using E(3)-equivariant Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), which effectively addresses issues such as irregular protein structures, sensitivity to rotations, insufficient protein surface characterization, and protein size shift.
- The proposed framework, EquiPocket, is evaluated on various datasets, demonstrating superior performance over existing methods, and the experimental results are well-presented.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed ablation study that supports the effectiveness of each module.
- The authors provide detailed explanations of the model's components and the experimental setup, enhancing the paper's reproducibility.
- The methodology is original, combining local geometric modeling, global structure modeling, and surface message passing, which is a novel approach to binding site prediction.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the number of parameters.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as similar methods have been previously explored, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from existing works.
- There are issues with the clarity of some figures and the methodological descriptions, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of rotation invariance, which is crucial for binding site prediction.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for a comprehensive understanding of its applicability and potential shortcomings.
- The experimental setup and data processing are not sufficiently detailed, which could hinder reproducibility and understanding of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to existing methods, particularly in terms of the use of E(3)-equivariant GNNs and the integration of different modules?
- How does the proposed method handle rotation invariance, and what steps are taken to ensure that the model remains invariant to rotations?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, including computational efficiency and the number of parameters?
- How does the model perform when applied to different protein structures, and what are the implications of using different protein structures for training and testing?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the methodology, particularly in sections where the figures and descriptions are unclear?
- How does the model handle the issue of protein size shift, and what are the implications of using different protein sizes for training and testing?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations of their approach and potential future directions for research?
