 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of magnetoencephalography (MEG) for decoding visual perceptions, focusing on the temporal resolution of MEG signals which is superior to functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI). The authors propose a three-stage pipeline that utilizes a pretrained image encoder, an MEG decoder trained with both contrastive and regression losses, and a pretrained image generator. This pipeline is evaluated using the THINGS-MEG dataset and shows improved image retrieval performance compared to linear decoders. The study also investigates the decoding of high-level visual features from MEG signals and their application in image generation. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation, lack of comparison with fMRI, and unclear presentation of results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an interesting and important problem of decoding brain activity with high temporal resolution using MEG, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed methodology is well-explained, and the paper is generally well-written, making it accessible and understandable.
- The use of MEG for decoding visual perceptions is a novel and significant contribution, particularly in terms of improving the temporal resolution over traditional methods like fMRI.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the methodology, including the training objectives, brain module, image modules, and generation module, which are crucial for understanding the proposed method.
- The inclusion of a variety of image embeddings and the evaluation of retrieval performance on sliding windows of different durations adds depth to the study.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough evaluation of the decoding performance of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its ability to decode high-level visual features from MEG signals.
- The comparison with fMRI is insufficient, which is crucial for understanding the advantages and limitations of MEG over fMRI in decoding visual perceptions.
- The paper suffers from poor writing and presentation, making it difficult to follow and understand the results and methodologies described.
- The paper does not adequately address the ethical implications of the research, which is a significant concern given the potential misuse of such technology.
- The paper's novelty is questionable as it appears to be a combination of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The paper does not provide enough detail on the preprocessing of MEG data, which is crucial for understanding the robustness and reliability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the preprocessing of MEG data, particularly how the raw data is processed to obtain the MEG time window?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of decoding performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the temporal aggregation layer in the brain module on the decoding performance?
- Why was the decision made to use a combination of CLIP and MSE losses, and how does this affect the decoding performance?
- How does the paper address the ethical implications of using MEG for decoding visual perceptions, particularly in terms of mental privacy and potential misuse?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the decoding performance of the proposed method, especially in terms of high-level visual features and their correlation with brain activity?
