 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedSKU, a defense mechanism against backdoor attacks in federated learning (FL) that utilizes a selective knowledge unlearning approach to separate malicious triggers from useful knowledge in model updates. This method aims to mitigate the impact of malicious clients by unlearning the triggers and aggregating the clean knowledge, thereby enhancing the performance of the global model. The paper claims that this approach is more efficient and effective than existing methods, as it allows for the recovery of useful information from malicious clients. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of novelty, unclear motivation, and insufficient experimental validation, particularly in terms of the practicality of the assumptions and the scalability of the proposed method in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and practical problem of defending against backdoor attacks in federated learning, which is crucial for ensuring the security and reliability of machine learning systems.
- The proposed approach is innovative, focusing on selective unlearning of malicious triggers while preserving useful knowledge, which could potentially enhance the performance of the global model.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The methodology of FedSKU is novel and well-explained, providing a detailed overview of the framework and its components.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method against various attacks and baseline methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and compelling motivation for why the proposed method, which primarily involves unlearning malicious triggers, would be more effective than existing methods that simply remove or obfuscate malicious models.
- The assumption that malicious clients are easily identifiable and that their contributions can be selectively unlearned is not practical and may not hold in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's experimental validation is insufficient, with a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant works, such as FedPrune and FedRewind.
- The scalability of the proposed method is questionable, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger datasets and models, and its computational efficiency.
- The paper's claims of superiority over existing methods are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which show only marginal improvements in some cases.
- The paper suffers from several grammatical and typographical errors, which detract from its professionalism and clarity.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the practical implications of the assumption that malicious clients can be easily identified and their contributions selectively unlearned?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability with existing methods, particularly in scenarios with larger datasets and more complex models?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the datasets used and the hyperparameter settings?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the malicious clients are not easily identifiable, and what are the potential implications for the overall performance of the system?
- Could you elaborate on the experimental results, particularly the marginal improvements observed in some cases, and how these improvements translate into real-world performance enhancements?
- The paper mentions several unclear statements and potential errors in the text (e.g., the use of "ASR" and "GACC" instead of "ASR" and "GACC"). Could you clarify these points?
