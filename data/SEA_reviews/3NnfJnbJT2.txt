 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for data selection in machine learning models, focusing on minimizing KL divergence between a target distribution and a selected subset of data. The authors propose a gradient-based optimization method that uses K-means clustering to efficiently select data points. The method is demonstrated to be effective across various domains, including machine translation and spelling correction, showing improvements over existing methods. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the method's application in different settings and the robustness of the results, particularly in image classification tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the motivation, methodology, and results, making it accessible and understandable.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, with a straightforward approach that can be applied across various domains.
- The methodology is backed by thorough experimental validation, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing the size of training data while maintaining performance.
- The use of KL divergence as a metric for data selection is novel and provides a theoretical foundation for the method.
- The paper is self-contained, with clear explanations of the method and its components, including the use of k-nearest neighbors and k-means clustering.
- The results are robust and consistent, showing that the method outperforms random sampling and other baseline methods in most cases.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the assumptions made by the method, particularly concerning the uniformity and representativeness of the target distribution (X).
- There is a potential issue with the method's sensitivity to the choice of the initial dataset (D), which could significantly affect the results.
- The experimental results, while promising, are not consistently superior across all settings. For instance, in image classification tasks, the method does not show significant improvements over random sampling.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the method, which could be a concern for large datasets.
- The method's applicability is limited to datasets where the target distribution (X) is known or can be accurately estimated, which might not be feasible in all real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the method and how these might impact its practical application.
