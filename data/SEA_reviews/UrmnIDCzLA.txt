 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the issue of bias towards base sessions in few-shot class-incremental learning (FSCIL), where performance on base classes is significantly higher than on newly introduced classes. To mitigate this, the authors propose a pipeline that integrates pretrained representations, classification modules, and prediction aggregation. They also introduce new evaluation metrics and benchmark datasets to assess the generalization of FSCIL models. The paper claims to achieve state-of-the-art performance and introduces a new methodology to address the performance bias in FSCIL. However, the novelty of the proposed pipeline is questioned, and the method's effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated across different datasets and architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and realistic problem in Few-shot Class-Incremental Learning (FSCIL) by proposing a pipeline that mitigates the bias towards base sessions.
- The authors provide a comprehensive empirical study and introduce new evaluation metrics and benchmark datasets to assess the generalization of FSCIL models, which are valuable for the research community.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in terms of both conventional and proposed session-level metrics.
- The introduction of new benchmark datasets and evaluation metrics is a significant contribution to the field, providing a more nuanced understanding of FSCIL model performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed pipeline is limited as it mainly integrates existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks clarity in the problem formulation, particularly in the motivation and justification for the proposed method.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets and architectures, which may not generalize well to other datasets or architectures.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods, which could undermine the claimed improvements.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of base session bias, as the proposed method still shows performance differences between base and incremental sessions.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis and deeper insights into why the proposed method works, which could enhance the understanding and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the problem formulation and the motivation behind the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets and architectures, and can the authors provide results on these configurations?
- Could the authors compare the proposed method with more SOTA methods to validate its effectiveness more robustly?
- What are the specific reasons for the observed performance differences between base and incremental sessions, and how does the proposed method address these differences?
- How do the authors ensure the reproducibility of their results, and can they provide more details on the experimental setup and data preprocessing to facilitate replication of their study?
