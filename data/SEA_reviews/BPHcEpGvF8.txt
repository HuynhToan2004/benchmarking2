 **Summary:**
The paper explores the theoretical underpinnings of backdoor attacks, focusing on the factors that influence the success of these attacks. It introduces the notions of statistical risks and derives finite-sample bounds for these risks, which are then applied to real-world scenarios. The theoretical framework is supported by experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the practical implications of the theoretical insights. The paper discusses the determinants of a backdoor attack's success, including the ratio of poisoned data, the direction and magnitude of the trigger, and the clean data distribution. It also explores the optimal trigger direction and the minimum trigger magnitude required for a successful attack. The paper's contributions are significant as they provide a comprehensive understanding of backdoor attacks, offering insights into the factors that influence their success and practical implications for designing more effective backdoor attacks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of backdoor attacks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical results are supported by experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets, enhancing the credibility of the findings.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The results are significant and have practical implications, providing insights into the design of more effective backdoor attacks.
- The paper is the first to provide theoretical insights into backdoor attacks in generative models, which is a novel and valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on attacks where the attacker can only modify the training data, which may not be the most practical or realistic scenario.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the monotonicity of the conditional density and the Holder continuity of the loss function, are quite strong and may not hold in all practical scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the theoretical analysis, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The experimental results are somewhat limited, focusing primarily on the MNIST dataset and not comparing the theoretical results with other datasets or more recent backdoor attack methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results and how they can be applied in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the theoretical results be extended to more general scenarios where the attacker can modify the training process?
- Is there a way to apply the theoretical results to design new backdoor attacks?
- How do the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis affect the validity of the results in practical scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to clarify the assumptions and their implications?
- How do the results compare with other recent backdoor attack methods, especially those that are more sophisticated or use different attack strategies?
- Can the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their findings and how they might be mitigated?
