 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HiC Bridge, a novel method for enhancing the resolution of Hi-C data by learning transformations from low-resolution to high-resolution data using a direct diffusion bridge (DDB). This approach is claimed to outperform existing deep learning-based models and provides a significant advancement in the field by improving the reproducibility of Hi-C data. The methodology involves a diffusion process that directly bridges low- and high-resolution Hi-C data, and the model is trained using real-world data to prevent overfitting. The paper is well-written and provides extensive experimental results, although it could benefit from more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art models and a broader range of cell types.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method is novel and innovative, particularly in utilizing a diffusion process to directly bridge low- and high-resolution Hi-C data, which is not commonly seen in previous works.
- The methodology is simple and effective, with clear motivation and well-designed experiments.
- The paper provides extensive experimental results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving the reproducibility of Hi-C data.
- The use of diffusion models to generate new low-resolution data from high-resolution data is an interesting approach that could potentially enhance the generalization performance of the HiC Bridge model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the ability to handle different cell types.
- There is a need for more quantitative and qualitative comparisons with other methods, especially in terms of performance metrics like PCC, SCC, and PSNR.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experimental validation across different cell types to demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for overfitting due to the use of real-world data for training.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity of the method and the scalability of the approach to larger datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential societal impacts of the research, which is a critical aspect of ethical research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the ability to handle different cell types?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance metrics like PCC, SCC, and PSNR with other existing methods?
- Can the authors provide more quantitative and qualitative comparisons between the reconstructed Hi-C data and the ground truth data, especially in terms of the accuracy of the TAD boundaries and the recovery of chromatin loop positions?
- How does the proposed method handle different cell types, and can it generalize to other cell types beyond those tested?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method, such as the risk of overfitting due to the use of real-world data for training?
- What are the potential societal impacts of the research, and how does the paper address these impacts?
