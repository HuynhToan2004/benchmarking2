 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the contextual bandits problem using neural networks, focusing on the reduction from contextual bandits to online regression under realizability assumptions. It introduces novel regret bounds for online regression with almost convex losses that satisfy the Quadratic Growth (QG) condition, which is a generalization of the PL condition. The paper also discusses the use of neural networks to achieve these bounds, showing that adding a random perturbation to the network predictions can make the loss satisfy the QG condition with a unique minimum. This leads to improved regret bounds for both square loss and KL loss in the neural contextual bandits setting. The paper also provides empirical evidence supporting the theoretical results, comparing the performance of the proposed algorithms against existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a lower bound for NeuCB, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical results are sound and the proofs are correct, with clear explanations of the ideas and techniques used.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed proofs and explanations of the techniques used.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, showing consistent performance across various datasets.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to achieving O(logT) regret for online regression with almost convex losses that satisfy the QG condition, which is a significant improvement over previous results.
- The paper discusses the limitations of previous methods and provides a clear and concise summary of the contributions and limitations in the introduction.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution appears limited as it mainly builds on previous works, such as Foster and Rakhlin (2020) and Foster and Krishnamurthy (2021), with minor improvements in regret bounds.
- The paper's dependence on the number of parameters in the network is not addressed, which could limit its applicability in certain scenarios.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, with a lack of comparison with some relevant baselines such as EE-Net and a lack of discussion on the variance of the results.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the realizability assumption and the assumption of unique minima, are not thoroughly discussed or justified, particularly in the context of neural networks.
- There are some inconsistencies and potential errors in the proofs and assumptions, such as the proof of Theorem 4.1 and the handling of the QG condition in certain scenarios.
- The paper's contribution to the field of bandits is not clearly articulated, and it could benefit from a more focused discussion on how the results relate to the specific challenges of bandit problems.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the contribution of their work in comparison to previous studies, particularly in terms of the novelty and significance of the improvements in regret bounds?
- How does the paper handle the assumption of unique minima in the context of neural networks, and what are the implications if this assumption is not met?
- In the experimental evaluation, how does the paper address the variance in the results, and what steps were taken to ensure the reliability of the findings?
- Can the authors provide more details on the assumptions used in the proofs, particularly Assumption 5 and the handling of the QG condition in the proof of Theorem 4.1?
- How does the paper's approach to handling the QG condition with a random perturbation compare to other methods, such as those using the NTK?
- Could the authors elaborate on the implications of the assumptions and results in the context of the neural network model, particularly in terms of the realizability and interpolation assumptions?
