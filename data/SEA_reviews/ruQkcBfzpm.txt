 **Summary:**
The paper introduces mBLIP, a multilingual vision-language model that leverages a pretrained multilingual large language model (LLM) to re-align an image encoder to a multilingual LLM, achieving comparable or better performance than state-of-the-art models on various benchmarks. The model is trained on a relatively small dataset, demonstrating efficiency in terms of data and computational resources. It also explores the use of machine translation to adapt English data to various languages, showcasing its potential in multilingual applications. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods like BLIP-2, and there are concerns about the depth of the ablation study and the clarity of the experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the methodology, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed method of re-aligning a pretrained image encoder to a multilingual LLM is innovative and shows potential for efficient training of multilingual vision-language models.
- The model achieves comparable or better performance on various benchmarks compared to state-of-the-art models, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The use of machine translation to adapt English data to various languages is a practical and efficient approach, which is highlighted in the paper.
- The paper is the first to propose a multilingual vision-language model, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it closely resembles existing methods like BLIP-2, with only minor modifications.
- The paper lacks a thorough ablation study, particularly concerning the impact of different training strategies and the effectiveness of the LoRA method.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing models, and the paper does not adequately compare its results with state-of-the-art models.
- The paper does not release the code, which limits the reproducibility of the results.
- There are concerns about the method's effectiveness in low-resource languages and its generalizability across different language families.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential biases in the training data, which could impact the model's performance and fairness in multilingual applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and BLIP-2, especially in terms of the novel contributions?
- How does the model perform in low-resource languages, and what are the potential strategies to improve its effectiveness in such scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the impact of different training strategies, such as the use of LoRA and the warm-start approach?
- How does the model handle different language families, and what are the potential challenges in adapting it to various linguistic contexts?
- Could the authors address the concerns about the model's fairness and generalizability across different language groups, particularly in light of the potential biases in the training data?
- Why was the decision made to not release the code, and what are the implications for the reproducibility and further development of the model?
