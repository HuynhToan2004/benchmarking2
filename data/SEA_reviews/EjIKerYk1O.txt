 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a real-time deep learning approach for estimating the distance-to-touchdown (DTD) of approaching aircraft using multi-view video feeds from digital towers. It combines YOLOv7 for object detection and an LSTM model for feature extraction and prediction. The methodology is tested on simulated and real video data from Singapore Changi Airport, demonstrating low predictive errors (MAPE = 0.18% for simulated data and MAPE = 0.33% for real data) up to 10 nautical miles under visual meteorological conditions. The approach aims to improve airport efficiency and safety by providing accurate DTD estimates, which can aid in runway sequencing and final approach spacing.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem of estimating the distance-to-touchdown (DTD) of approaching aircraft, which is crucial for enhancing airport operations and safety.
- The methodology is clearly explained, making it accessible and understandable, which is essential for practical applications.
- The use of a multi-view approach and advanced deep learning techniques, such as YOLOv7 for object detection and LSTM for feature extraction, is innovative and effective.
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation and detailed descriptions of the experimental setup and results, which support the claims made.
- The proposed method is evaluated using both simulated and real-world video data, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness in various scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and challenges of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability in different weather and lighting conditions, and its generalizability to other airports or camera configurations.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, which makes it difficult to assess the novelty and superiority of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not provide sufficient implementation details, such as the number of cameras used in real-world experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and scalability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential biases or limitations in the training data, which could affect the performance and generalizability of the model.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive evaluations, including comparisons with different object detection models and a broader range of weather and lighting conditions.
