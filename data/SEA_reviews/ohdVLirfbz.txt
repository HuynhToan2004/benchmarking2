 **Summary:**
The paper explores the generalization bounds of gradient descent (GD) in two-layer and three-layer neural networks under different scaling factors, termed as c. It extends prior work by providing excess risk bounds for GD in these networks, demonstrating the impact of scaling on over-parameterization and generalization error rates. The authors derive generalization bounds that are dimension-independent, offering insights into the relationship between network width and generalization performance. The paper also discusses the implications of these results for understanding the generalization capabilities of neural networks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of generalization bounds for gradient descent (GD) in two-layer and three-layer neural networks, which is a significant extension of previous work.
- The authors demonstrate that the generalization bounds are dimension-independent, which is a notable contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The results provide insights into the relationship between network width and generalization performance, which is an important aspect of neural network research.
- The analysis is thorough, with clear explanations and proofs that support the claims made in the paper.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is not clearly distinguished from existing literature, particularly the works by Lei et al. and Richards & Kuzborskij. The novelty of the results needs to be more clearly articulated.
- The paper is dense and could benefit from better organization and more intuitive explanations, especially in the introduction and the presentation of results.
- There are several technical contributions that are not adequately highlighted, which could lead to confusion about the paper's originality and depth of analysis.
- The paper lacks experimental validation, which could have strengthened the claims made about the generalization capabilities of the neural networks studied.
- The paper's assumptions and the implications of certain results (e.g., Theorem 3) are not adequately discussed, which could limit the understanding and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the technical novelties of their work compared to previous studies, particularly in terms of the scaling parameter c and its implications on the network's behavior?
- How do the authors justify the assumptions made in their analysis, particularly those related to the network's scaling and the initialization of weights?
- In light of the results presented, how do the authors interpret the role of the scaling parameter c in influencing the network's behavior and generalization capabilities?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to illustrate the practical implications of the results, especially in terms of how they might guide the design or training of neural networks?
- How do the authors address the concerns regarding the clarity and organization of the paper, particularly in the introduction and presentation of results?
