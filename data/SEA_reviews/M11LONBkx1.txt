 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel diffusion-based method for feature imputation in graphs with missing features, focusing on low-variance channels that significantly impact graph learning tasks. The method involves two stages: a pre-diffusion stage that imputes missing features using a diffusion process, and a diffusion with synthetic features stage that generates synthetic features for low-variance channels. These synthetic features are then diffused to enhance the variance of features and improve graph learning tasks like semi-supervised node classification and link prediction. The paper presents a theoretical framework and empirical results on several datasets, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in handling missing features. However, the paper has been criticized for its limited scope, lack of comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and potential over-reliance on synthetic features which could affect the accuracy of downstream tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of graph learning with missing features, which is of great importance in real-world applications.
- The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the problem and the proposed method, making it easy for readers to follow.
- The method is well-motivated and supported by theoretical proofs, which enhances its credibility and applicability.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the proposed method and its results.
- The proposed method, Feature Imputation with Synthetic Features (FISF), is novel and addresses a gap in the current literature by focusing on low-variance channels, which are often overlooked in previous studies.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of FISF in improving graph learning tasks, such as semi-supervised node classification and link prediction, compared to existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance metrics and methodological approaches.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the generation of synthetic features and the selection of nodes for feature injection, which could affect the reliability and reproducibility of the results.
- The paper's focus on low-variance channels might limit its applicability to other types of missing features, such as those caused by noise or measurement errors.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough evaluation of the impact of hyperparameters on the performance of FISF, especially in scenarios where synthetic features are not available.
- The paper's experimental setup and results lack depth, particularly in terms of the variety of datasets used and the comparative analysis with other methods.
- The paper's theoretical proofs and empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate that the synthetic features significantly enhance performance on graph learning tasks, which could limit the credibility of the claims.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the method used for generating synthetic features and the selection of nodes for feature injection?
- How does the proposed method perform when synthetic features are not available?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the number of iterations in the diffusion process and the selection of hyperparameters?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance metrics and methodological approaches?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and provide examples or scenarios where the method might not be effective?
- How does the method handle the issue of over-reliance on synthetic features, and what steps have been taken to mitigate this risk?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of hyperparameters on the performance of FISF, and how do these parameters affect the accuracy of downstream tasks?
