 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MiniLLM, a knowledge distillation approach that distills large language models (LLMs) into smaller models by replacing the forward KL divergence objective with reverse KL divergence. This approach aims to prevent the student model from overestimating low-probability regions of the teacher distribution, thereby improving the precision and quality of responses generated by the student model. The paper also proposes optimization strategies to address issues like variance and reward hacking, and demonstrates the effectiveness of MiniLLM through experiments on various instruction-following datasets, showing improvements over baseline methods. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the method and the lack of comprehensive evaluation metrics beyond ROUGE scores.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and concise explanations of the proposed method.
- The introduction of reverse KL divergence as a loss function for distillation is a novel approach, and the optimization strategies proposed are well-motivated and supported by empirical results.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, covering various model sizes and instruction-following datasets, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including its scalability and the potential for distilling larger models into smaller ones.
- The use of human evaluations and a variety of automatic metrics such as ROUGE, human evaluation, and calibration metrics adds depth to the evaluation of the distilled models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods in terms of automatic metrics, particularly in the generation of long texts and complex responses.
- The use of only ROUGE scores for evaluation may not be sufficient to fully capture the quality of the generated text, especially in terms of fluency and coherence.
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as similar approaches have been explored in previous works.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the potential negative societal impacts.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training hyperparameters, such as learning rates and batch sizes, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the training data and the potential biases that might affect the performance of the distilled models.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the use of ROUGE scores in the evaluation and discuss how these scores were computed?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of automatic metrics, particularly in the generation of long texts and complex responses?
- Can you provide more details on the training hyperparameters and how these were selected?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for distilled models to learn from biased training data?
- Could you elaborate on the scalability of the method and how it performs when distilling larger models into smaller ones?
- How does the method perform when the teacher model is not well-calibrated or when it exhibits exposure bias?
- Can you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated?
