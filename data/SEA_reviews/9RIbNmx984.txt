 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the performance of the regularized LSTD algorithm in the context of reinforcement learning, focusing on the double descent phenomenon in the regime of over-parameterization. It employs a double asymptotic approach, considering the number of parameters and distinct visited states to infinity with a constant ratio. The paper derives deterministic limits for both empirical and true Mean-Square Bellman Error (MSBE), highlighting the role of correction terms in the double descent phenomenon. Theoretical predictions are validated against numerical experiments on synthetic and small real-world environments, showing a good match between theoretical and empirical results. However, the paper is criticized for its limited applicability to deep neural networks and the lack of comparison with other RL algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the influence of network size and L2 regularization on the performance of TD algorithms in RL, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of RL algorithms in practical scenarios.
- The authors have derived deterministic limits for both empirical and true Mean-Square Bellman Error (MSBE), which are significant for the theoretical understanding of RL algorithms.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the results are supported by both theoretical predictions and numerical experiments on synthetic and small real-world environments.
- The introduction of a double asymptotic regime where the number of parameters and distinct visited states go to infinity while maintaining a constant ratio is a novel approach that provides a precise assessment of the performance in both over-parameterized and under-parameterized regimes.
- The paper closely matches empirical results with theoretical predictions, demonstrating the robustness and applicability of the theoretical framework.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's applicability is limited to regularized LSTD with random features, which may not generalize well to other RL algorithms or deep neural networks.
- The theoretical results are not directly applicable to deep neural networks, which are commonly used in practical RL applications.
- The paper does not compare the performance of the regularized LSTD algorithm with other RL algorithms, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its relative effectiveness.
- The paper's assumptions and theorems are not well-explained, making it difficult for readers to understand the implications and assumptions behind the results.
- The paper's relevance to deep RL is questionable, as it primarily focuses on linear TD methods and does not address the challenges posed by deep neural networks in RL.
- The paper's conclusions are limited by its focus on linear TD methods and its assumptions about the number of distinct states visited, which may not hold in more complex or non-stationary environments.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the theoretical results can be extended to other RL algorithms or deep neural networks?
- How does the paper's analysis compare with other RL algorithms in terms of performance and applicability?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the assumptions and theorems used in their analysis?
- How do the correction terms in the double descent phenomenon affect the performance of the regularized LSTD algorithm in practical scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the practical implications of their findings, especially in terms of improving the performance of RL algorithms in real-world applications?
