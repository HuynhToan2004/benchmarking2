 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new class of pseudometrics, called STARC metrics, for quantifying the difference between reward functions in reinforcement learning. The STARC metrics are designed to induce both upper and lower bounds on worst-case regret, ensuring that small distances between reward functions correspond to similar policy orderings. These metrics are evaluated through experiments in both synthetic and real-world environments, showing that they outperform existing metrics like EPIC and DARD. The theoretical underpinnings of STARC metrics are discussed, including their soundness and completeness, which are critical for ensuring the robustness and reliability of reward learning algorithms. The paper also critiques existing reward metrics and provides a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential extensions of the proposed metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the proposed pseudometrics, including the steps involved in their computation, which enhances their comprehensibility.
- The theoretical results are well-presented and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the advantages of the proposed metrics over existing ones.
- The experiments are well-conducted and show that the proposed metrics outperform existing ones, providing empirical evidence of their effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and concise presentation of the main results and a thorough discussion of related work.
- The proposed metrics are shown to be sound and complete, which is crucial for ensuring the reliability and robustness of reward learning algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed metrics, which could be critical for understanding their applicability and potential pitfalls.
- The theoretical results are primarily presented for finite state/action spaces, which may limit their applicability in more complex, continuous environments.
- The experiments are conducted primarily in synthetic environments, which may not fully capture the complexities of real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational efficiency of the proposed metrics, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the proposed metrics compare to other existing metrics, particularly those that also aim to quantify the difference between reward functions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed metrics and how they might affect their applicability in different scenarios?
- How do the proposed metrics perform in continuous environments, and what modifications, if any, are necessary for their application in such settings?
- Could the authors elaborate on the computational efficiency of the proposed metrics and discuss potential strategies for reducing computational costs?
- How do the proposed metrics compare to other existing metrics that aim to quantify the difference between reward functions, and what are the key advantages of the proposed metrics over these alternatives?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the theoretical results presented in the paper, particularly the assumptions and implications of these results in practical applications?
