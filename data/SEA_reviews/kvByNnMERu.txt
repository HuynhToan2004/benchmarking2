 **Summary:**
The paper explores the estimation of shape distances between high-dimensional neural representations, focusing on the Procrustes size-and-shape distance and Riemannian shape distance. The authors introduce a method-of-moments estimator to address the challenges of high-dimensional data and limited samples, providing theoretical bounds on the convergence of plug-in estimators. This method is tested on both simulated and real neural data, showing its effectiveness in estimating shape distances. The paper also discusses the theoretical and practical implications of these estimators, including the trade-off between bias and variance, and the impact of data quality on the accuracy of the estimations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of neural representations, focusing on the estimation of shape distances in high-dimensional settings, which is a critical area of interest.
- The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of plug-in estimators, establishing upper and lower bounds on their performance, and introduce a novel method-of-moments estimator to address the challenges of high-dimensional data and limited samples.
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the problem, methodology, and results, and includes a thorough discussion of the theoretical and practical implications of the proposed estimators.
- The paper includes both theoretical analysis and empirical validation, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed estimators through experiments on simulated and real neural data.
- The paper contributes to the understanding of shape distances in neural representations, which is a crucial aspect of neural network research, and provides a practical method for estimating these distances with controlled bias and variance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed estimators, particularly in terms of their practical applicability and the assumptions required for their effectiveness.
- There is a lack of discussion on the statistical efficiency of the proposed estimators compared to other methods, which could be crucial for understanding their performance in different scenarios.
- The paper could be improved by including more comprehensive experimental results, such as additional experiments on neural data and a more detailed comparison of the proposed estimators with other methods.
- The paper assumes that the neural representations are mean-centered and bounded, which may not hold in all practical scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide a clear comparison of the proposed estimators with other methods, which could help in understanding their relative advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions and limitations of the theoretical results, particularly the assumptions required for the validity of the theoretical bounds.
