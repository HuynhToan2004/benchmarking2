 **Summary:**
The paper explores the phenomenon of feature redundancy in pretrained models when applied to few-shot tasks, focusing on linear probing. It demonstrates that under extreme few-shot settings, using only a small percentage of the most important feature dimensions can recover performance comparable to using the full representation. The authors also discuss how this redundancy affects the performance of different models and datasets, and propose methods to mitigate its impact. Theoretical analysis and empirical results are provided to support the findings, which could be beneficial for future research in few-shot learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of few-shot learning, specifically the feature redundancy in pretrained models.
- It provides a clear and intuitive explanation of the feature redundancy phenomenon, which is supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and easy to understand, with a clear structure and logical flow.
- The experimental results are comprehensive, covering various datasets and models, and the findings are supported by a variety of experiments and ablation studies.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to identifying and mitigating feature redundancy, which could be valuable for future research in few-shot learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on the ResNet-50 model and does not explore how the findings might apply to other architectures such as ViT or Swin.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the findings relate to other works in the field, which could limit the understanding of the broader implications of the research.
- The paper's approach to feature selection and the use of feature importance metrics are not clearly differentiated from existing methods, which could lead to confusion about the novelty of the contributions.
- The paper's focus on linear probing and its implications for other methods like nearest centroid classifier (NCC) is not adequately addressed, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed or explained, which could hinder the reproducibility and understanding of the results.

**Questions:**
- How do the findings of this paper relate to the work of Luo et al. (2022) on feature importance? Are there significant differences in the methods or results?
- Can the feature redundancy phenomenon be observed in other pretrained models, such as ViT or Swin, and how might this affect the findings?
- How does the paper's method for identifying and mitigating feature redundancy compare to existing methods, and what are the advantages or disadvantages of the proposed approach?
- What are the implications of the findings for other methods beyond linear probing, such as NCC? How might the feature redundancy phenomenon affect the performance of these methods?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and results, particularly how the feature importance is estimated and how this affects the performance of the models?
