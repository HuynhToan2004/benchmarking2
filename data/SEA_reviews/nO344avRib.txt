 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph representation named GEEL (Gap Encoded Edge List) for scalable graph generation, which encodes the difference between node indices instead of the indices themselves. This approach reduces the vocabulary size and improves scalability by using a simple LSTM model for autoregressive generation. The GEEL representation is evaluated on various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and scalability. The method is also extended to handle attributed graphs, and the paper includes comprehensive experimental results and ablation studies. The authors claim that GEEL outperforms existing methods in terms of scalability and efficiency, and the results are supported by extensive evaluations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel graph representation named GEEL, which encodes the difference between node indices instead of the indices themselves, significantly reducing the vocabulary size and improving scalability.
- The use of LSTM for autoregressive graph generation with O(M) complexity is highlighted, and the method is shown to be effective in generating graphs with high quality and scalability.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experimental results and ablation studies that support the effectiveness and scalability of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is straightforward and can be applied to both attributed and unattributed graphs, which is a significant advantage in handling different types of graph data.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, comparing it to various baselines and demonstrating its effectiveness in both general and molecular graph generation tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental results are primarily based on MMD, which may not fully capture the graph generation quality, and additional metrics such as FID could be beneficial.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for ethical implications.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the proposed method handles self-loops and the representation of them in the GEEL format.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the scalability of the method, especially in terms of its applicability to very large graphs.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how self-loops are represented in the GEEL format and discuss any specific challenges or considerations in their representation?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other graph generation methods, especially in terms of quality and scalability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss any potential negative societal impacts?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, particularly in terms of scalability and efficiency?
- How does the proposed method compare to other edge-based representations, and what are the specific advantages of the GEEL representation?
