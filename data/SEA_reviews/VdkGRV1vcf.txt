 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for efficiently computing the gradient of the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) using Riesz kernels and sliced MMD, which is shown to have a computational complexity of O((M+N)log(M+N)) for two measures with M and N support points. This is a significant improvement over the naive O(MN) complexity. The authors propose a generative model based on MMD flows, which can be trained using neural networks, and demonstrate its effectiveness on image generation tasks such as MNIST, FashionMNIST, and CIFAR10. The paper also discusses the theoretical aspects of the Riesz kernel and its relation to the Wasserstein-1 distance, and provides a detailed algorithm for computing the gradient of the MMD.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to computing the gradient of MMD using Riesz kernels, which is shown to have a computational complexity of O((M+N)log(M+N)), significantly improving upon the naive O(MN) complexity.
- The method is applied to generative modeling, particularly in image generation tasks, and shows promising results in terms of computational efficiency and model performance.
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the problem, methodology, and results, and provides a detailed algorithm for computing the gradient of the MMD.
- The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through experiments on various datasets, showing a significant improvement in computational efficiency and model performance compared to existing methods.
- The paper discusses the theoretical aspects of the Riesz kernel and its relation to the Wasserstein-1 distance, providing a deeper understanding of the underlying mathematical principles.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on Riesz kernels, which may limit its applicability to other types of kernels.
- The method's reliance on the Riesz kernel may limit its adaptability to different datasets or scenarios where other kernels might be more appropriate.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other generative models, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and model performance.
- The theoretical analysis of the method's convergence and its applicability to different types of kernels is not thoroughly discussed.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its methodology, which could be crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**

- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and efficiency of their method, particularly in comparison to other existing methods?
