 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for encoding medical diagnostic codes using Holographic Reduced Representations (HRR) to enhance the performance of Transformer models in clinical tasks. The approach leverages the SNOMED CT ontology to generate structured embeddings for medical codes, which are then integrated into a BERT model for training and fine-tuning on various tasks. The authors demonstrate that their method improves the model's ability to handle rare codes and maintains semantic similarity between related medical concepts. The method is validated on the MIMIC-IV dataset, showing modest improvements over baseline models with unstructured embeddings. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper, the novelty of the approach, and the limited scope of the experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to encoding medical codes using Holographic Reduced Representations (HRR), which is a promising method for handling rare codes and maintaining semantic similarity between related medical concepts.
- The integration of HRR with the BERT model is a novel application that enhances the model's ability to process structured medical data, which is crucial for clinical decision-making and research.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed description of the methodology, including the use of the SNOMED CT ontology and the construction of vector-symbolic embeddings.
- The experiments conducted on the MIMIC-IV dataset demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms baseline models with unstructured embeddings, particularly in handling rare codes and maintaining semantic similarity.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the methodology and includes supplementary materials that aid in reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the HRR and its application, which could be improved by including more detailed examples or additional figures.
- The novelty of the approach is limited as similar methods have been previously explored, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing works.
- The scope of the experiments is limited, focusing only on the MIMIC-IV dataset and not including broader benchmarks or more diverse datasets, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and computational efficiency of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for larger, more complex datasets.
- There is a lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art models, which could provide a better understanding of the method's effectiveness and limitations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or additional figures to clarify the application of HRR in the context of medical coding?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art models in terms of performance, scalability, and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing methods that use ontologies or other structured data in medical coding?
- How does the method handle the scalability issue, especially when applied to larger, more complex datasets?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the method's ability to handle different types of medical data, such as free text or image data, and how it performs in these contexts?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of the method on clinical decision-making and patient outcomes, and provide examples or case studies to illustrate these impacts?
