 **Summary:**
The paper introduces BaTex, a novel method for personalized text-to-image generation, which optimizes text embeddings in a low-dimensional textual subspace to enhance the text-image alignment. The method leverages a rank-based selection strategy to determine the basis vectors of the textual subspace, aiming to improve the textual similarity and reduce the training time compared to traditional methods. The approach is evaluated against Textual Inversion and other state-of-the-art methods, showing improvements in text-image alignment and training efficiency. However, the method's novelty is questioned, as it seems to be a simple extension of existing methods like Textual Inversion, and its effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated across a broader range of datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for personalized text-to-image generation, which is both efficient and effective in terms of time-efficiency and text-image alignment.
- The proposed BaTex method is well-motivated and theoretically sound, with a clear algorithm design and a well-structured presentation.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the method significantly improves text-image alignment compared to existing methods, as shown in both quantitative and qualitative evaluations.
- The method is easy to implement and can be applied to any text-to-image model, making it a practical solution for personalized image generation.
- The paper provides a comprehensive discussion of the method's limitations and potential negative societal impacts, which is commendable for its transparency and ethical considerations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods like Textual Inversion, which uses a similar approach to optimize text embeddings in a low-dimensional space.
- The effectiveness of the method is not convincingly demonstrated across a broader range of datasets, and the method's performance on different datasets varies significantly, raising concerns about its generalizability.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of training time and image-image alignment, which are crucial metrics for evaluating the method's effectiveness.
- The selection of hyperparameters, such as the dimension of the textual subspace and the training steps, is not well-justified, and the paper does not provide a clear guideline for choosing these parameters in practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly concerning the robustness of the results to different initial embeddings and the method's ability to handle complex textual prompts.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform on different datasets, and what are the specific challenges in applying the method to diverse datasets?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of training time and image-image alignment to better understand the method's performance?
- How does the method handle complex textual prompts, and what are the potential limitations in this regard?
- Could the authors clarify the selection of hyperparameters, particularly the dimension of the textual subspace and the training steps, and provide guidelines for choosing these parameters in practical applications?
- How robust is the method to different initial embeddings, and what are the implications of using different initial embeddings on the method's effectiveness?
- In the context of the method's claims of reducing training time, can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the training time savings and how this translates into practical benefits for users?
