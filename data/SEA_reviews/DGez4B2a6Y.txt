 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Plug-and-Play Image Registration Network (PIRATE) and its enhanced version PIRATE+, which integrates a pre-trained AWGN denoiser as a regularizer within an iterative optimization framework. The method leverages a DEQ-based fine-tuning mechanism to enhance the network's performance. PIRATE and PIRATE+ were evaluated on datasets such as OASIS and CANDI, showing state-of-the-art results. The methodology aims to address the issue of data fidelity in deep learning-based image registration by incorporating a data-fidelity penalty and a CNN prior. Despite the promising results, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of comprehensive experimental validation, particularly in terms of its generalizability and scalability across different imaging modalities and anatomies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel application of plug-and-play priors for image registration, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method is well-motivated and presents a novel approach by integrating a data-fidelity penalty and a CNN prior to address the issue of data fidelity in deep learning-based image registration.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear and logical presentation of the proposed method.
- The experiments are conducted on two datasets, demonstrating the method's effectiveness and its potential to achieve state-of-the-art performance.
- The use of a pre-trained AWGN denoiser as a regularizer within an iterative optimization framework is a novel approach that enhances the method's performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the method's performance across various imaging modalities and anatomies, which limits its generalizability.
- The method's scalability is not thoroughly discussed, which is crucial for understanding its applicability in practical scenarios.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis and explanation of the method's components, such as the DEQ-based fine-tuning mechanism and the choice of penalty functions.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and runtime of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, particularly in terms of quantitative results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related works and a clearer presentation of the method's contributions in relation to existing literature.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different imaging modalities and anatomies?
- How does the choice of penalty functions affect the method's performance, and could the authors provide more insights into this decision?
- Could the authors clarify the computational complexity and runtime of the proposed method, and discuss any potential strategies for reducing these costs?
- In terms of the method's evaluation, could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a discussion of the method's advantages and disadvantages?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of generalizability and scalability with other recent deep learning-based image registration methods?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the method to other imaging modalities or anatomies, and provide any preliminary results or plans for future work in this area?
