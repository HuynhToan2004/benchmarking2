 **Summary:**
The paper discusses the potential of using differential privacy (DP) and Near Access Freeness (NAF) as a measure of copyright infringement, focusing on the limitations of these approaches in accurately reflecting copyright law. It argues that these methods, while useful for privacy, do not adequately address the complexities of copyright law, such as the distinction between protected expressions and unprotected ideas, and the fair use doctrine. The paper highlights that these methods may either overly restrict lawful content or fail to detect unlawful content, thus not providing a robust solution for copyright infringement. The authors suggest that a more nuanced approach, possibly involving a combination of computational and legal analysis, is needed to address the complexities of copyright law.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear and detailed discussion on the differences between copyright and privacy, which is crucial for understanding the unique challenges of copyright law.
- The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of copyright law, particularly the fair use doctrine, and provide a comprehensive overview of the legal concepts involved.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of complex legal and technical concepts.
- The inclusion of real-world examples and case studies enhances the practical relevance and applicability of the discussion.
- The paper provides a valuable contribution to the ongoing discourse on the intersection of copyright law and AI-generated content, highlighting the need for a more nuanced approach that considers both computational and legal perspectives.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear, actionable solution or recommendation for how to address the identified gaps between copyright law and computational methods.
- The discussion on differential privacy and its application to copyright law is not particularly novel or insightful, as these concepts have been previously explored in other works.
- The paper does not sufficiently engage with the technical aspects of differential privacy and Near Access Freeness, which could limit its appeal to readers primarily interested in these aspects.
- The paper could benefit from a more structured presentation, particularly in the section discussing the limitations of computational methods, to better highlight the key takeaways and implications for future research.
- There is a need for a more rigorous evaluation of the proposed methods against real-world copyright infringement cases to validate their effectiveness and practical utility.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on how their proposed approach could be applied in practice, particularly in terms of operationalizing the legal concepts discussed?
- How do the authors envision the integration of computational methods with legal analysis to address the complexities of copyright law?
- In light of the paper's focus on the limitations of computational methods, could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on how these limitations could be addressed or mitigated?
- Could the authors provide more specific examples or case studies that illustrate how the computational methods discussed in the paper could be applied in real-world scenarios?
- How do the authors address the potential for misuse or misinterpretation of the computational methods by copyright holders or other interested parties?
