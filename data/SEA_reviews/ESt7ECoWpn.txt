 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to per-instance differential privacy (pDP) by proposing a game-theoretic framework called the Noise Variance Optimization (NVO) game. This framework aims to optimize the variance of per-instance noise to ensure privacy and utility, with a focus on achieving the Nash Equilibrium (NE) in the game. The authors demonstrate the utility of this approach through empirical experiments on real-world datasets, showing improvements in statistical utility compared to traditional methods like the Laplace mechanism. However, the paper faces criticisms regarding its theoretical rigor, particularly in the proof of Theorem 4.1, and the practical applicability of the game-theoretic approach in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to per-instance differential privacy (pDP) by optimizing noise variance, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The empirical results show that the proposed method improves statistical utility compared to traditional methods like the Laplace mechanism.
- The use of a game-theoretic approach to ensure pDP is innovative and well-explained, providing a clear framework for understanding the privacy implications of data handling.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear introduction and a good explanation of the methodology, making it accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its practical applicability and scalability.
- There is a significant gap in the theoretical rigor of the paper, particularly in the proof of Theorem 4.1, which is crucial for the validity of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the game-theoretic approach, which could be a significant barrier to practical implementation.
- The experimental setup is limited, with only one dataset used, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the implications of the game-theoretic approach for real-world privacy concerns, particularly in terms of potential biases in the data.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition of the utility function U(D,M(D)) in the context of the game-theoretic approach?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of the Nash Equilibrium (NE) in the game-theoretic framework, and how does it contribute to the overall privacy and utility outcomes?
- How do the authors address the computational complexity of the game-theoretic approach, particularly in terms of scalability to larger datasets?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the datasets used and the parameters of the Laplace mechanism compared to the proposed method?
- How does the paper address the potential biases that could arise from the game-theoretic approach, particularly in terms of data privacy and fairness?
