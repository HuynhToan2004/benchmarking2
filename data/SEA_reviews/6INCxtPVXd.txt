 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel discriminator-based mode affinity score (dMAS) for continual learning in conditional generative adversarial networks (cGANs). This score is used to quantify the similarity between modes and identify relevant modes for learning new tasks while preserving previous knowledge. The approach is validated through experiments on various datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and Oxford Flower, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in continual learning scenarios. The paper also includes a theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation to demonstrate the robustness and stability of the dMAS.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel discriminator-based mode affinity score (dMAS) to quantify the similarity between modes in conditional generative adversarial networks (cGANs), which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method demonstrates its effectiveness in continual learning scenarios, particularly in image generation tasks, and outperforms existing methods in terms of performance metrics such as FID.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed method and its implementation.
- The theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation provided in the paper demonstrate the robustness and stability of the dMAS, which is crucial for its practical application.
- The paper is the first to consider mode-awareness in continual learning for cGANs, which is a novel approach that could potentially advance the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on related works, particularly in the area of transfer learning and knowledge distillation, which could have provided a more robust context for the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not convincing, especially in terms of the comparison with existing methods and the generalizability of the results across different datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.
- There are several minor issues with the presentation of the paper, such as typographical errors and inconsistencies in the use of symbols and terminology.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications for certain methodological choices, such as the use of Hessian matrices and the selection of modes for learning new tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or justifications for the choice of Hessian matrices in the calculation of the dMAS?
- How does the proposed method compare with other transfer learning and knowledge distillation methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- In the context of continual learning, how does the proposed method ensure that the model does not forget previously learned tasks?
- How does the paper define and measure the "mode" in the context of cGANs, and what are the implications of this definition for the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- Can the authors clarify the computational complexity of the proposed method and discuss potential strategies for reducing this complexity in practical applications?
- How does the paper ensure that the results are generalizable across different datasets and tasks, especially given the specific nature of the datasets used in the experiments?
