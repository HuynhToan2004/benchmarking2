 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Inference-time Dynamic Prompting (IDP), a novel method aimed at enhancing the performance of compressed Large Language Models (LLMs) by dynamically selecting appropriate prompts during inference. The approach addresses the challenges of post-compression performance degradation by leveraging dynamic prompting strategies, which are claimed to outperform traditional methods like LoRA and prefix-tuning in certain scenarios. The methodology involves the use of distinct prompts for different input types and a dynamic selection process based on input context, aiming to restore performance without requiring additional training parameters. The paper presents empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of IDP across various tasks, though the method's generalizability and the extent of its performance improvements are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the content.
- The proposed method, Inference-time Dynamic Prompting (IDP), is novel and effective, particularly in improving the performance of compressed LLMs without requiring additional training parameters.
- The experiments conducted are extensive and convincing, demonstrating the practical utility of IDP in various scenarios.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of IDP across multiple tasks and knowledge domains, showing its effectiveness in real-world applications.
- The motivation behind the research is clear, and the paper presents a compelling argument for the use of IDP as a lightweight adaptation tool to restore performance in compressed LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other dynamic prompting methods, which could provide a more robust evaluation of IDP's effectiveness.
- The evaluation tasks used in the study are limited to multiple-choice format, which might not fully capture the capabilities of LLMs in more complex, open-ended tasks.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of IDP during inference, which could be a significant concern for real-world applications.
- The generalizability of IDP to different types of compression methods and models is not thoroughly explored, raising concerns about its applicability beyond the specific models tested.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential impact of IDP on the interpretability of LLMs, which is a critical aspect for understanding and trusting the model's outputs.
- There are some typographical errors and inconsistencies in the paper, which could detract from its professionalism and credibility.

**Questions:**
- How does IDP compare with other dynamic prompting methods, such as those mentioned in related works?
- Can IDP be adapted to open-ended tasks, and if so, how does it perform in these scenarios?
- How does the computational cost of IDP during inference compare with other methods, and what are the implications for practical deployment?
- Is IDP applicable to other types of compression methods beyond those tested in the study?
- How does IDP affect the interpretability of LLMs, and what steps are being taken to ensure that the model's outputs remain understandable and trustworthy?
- Could the authors provide more details on the selection and optimization of prompts in IDP, particularly in scenarios where the performance gains are marginal?
- The paper mentions a potential trade-off between performance and prompt length. How does IDP balance these factors, and what are the implications for real-world applications?
