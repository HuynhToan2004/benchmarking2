 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GROOT, a transformer-based agent designed to learn from video demonstrations and execute tasks in Minecraft. The agent's architecture includes a video encoder and a decoder that uses goal embeddings to guide its actions. GROOT's performance is evaluated against human players and existing agents using a novel benchmark called Minecraft SkillForge. The paper demonstrates that GROOT can follow instructions effectively, showing a significant improvement over existing methods in certain tasks. However, concerns are raised about the generalization of the model to other environments and the clarity of its performance in different scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The proposed GROOT agent is innovative, utilizing a video encoder that learns from demonstrations to generate goal embeddings, which are then used by a decoder to generate actions.
- The benchmark introduced, Minecraft SkillForge, is novel and comprehensive, covering a variety of tasks in Minecraft, and the evaluation methodology is well-designed.
- The paper presents a significant improvement in performance compared to previous works, as demonstrated in the results.
- The use of a video encoder and a goal-conditioned policy is novel and well-explained, providing a clear understanding of the methodology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the goal space and how it is learned, which is crucial for understanding the model's capabilities.
- The methodology of using a video encoder to learn from demonstrations is not novel and similar approaches have been previously used in other works.
- The generalization of the model to other environments beyond Minecraft is not clearly addressed, raising concerns about the model's applicability in different contexts.
- The paper does not adequately compare the performance of GROOT with other baselines, particularly in terms of the success rate and the number of tasks evaluated.
- The model's reliance on a pre-trained inverse dynamic model and the quality of the trajectories used for training are not thoroughly discussed, which could affect the model's performance.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training data and the training process, which could limit the reproducibility and understanding of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the goal space is learned and how it relates to the performance of the model?
- How does the model perform when trained on different types of trajectories, and what are the implications for its generalization to other environments?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, such as the length of trajectories used and the quality of the trajectories?
- How does the model handle tasks that are not included in the training set, and what are the implications for its performance in such scenarios?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other baselines, including the success rate and the number of tasks evaluated?
- How does the model handle the trade-off between the quality of the goal space and the performance of the policy, and what are the implications for the model's overall effectiveness?
