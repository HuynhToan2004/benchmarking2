 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training method, Certifiable Robust Multi-modal Training (CRMT), aimed at enhancing the robustness of multi-modal models against uni-modal attacks and missing modalities. The authors analyze the theoretical aspects of multi-modal robustness and propose a two-step training procedure that adjusts the uni-modal representation margin and the integration of modalities. This approach is supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results, demonstrating improvements over existing methods. The paper also discusses the limitations of multi-modal models and how these limitations can be addressed through the proposed training method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear and detailed analysis of multi-modal robustness, including the theoretical analysis of the vulnerability of multi-modal models to uni-modal attacks and the influence of modality preference on robustness.
- The proposed two-step training procedure, CRMT, is simple yet effective, and the paper includes both theoretical analysis and experimental results to support its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methodologies and their implications for improving multi-modal robustness.
- The analysis of modality preference and its impact on multi-modal robustness is a significant contribution to the field, as it addresses a critical issue in multi-modal model training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and applicability to larger multi-modal models.
- The experimental results, while showing improvements, do not consistently outperform existing methods, and the improvements are sometimes marginal.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the findings to other multi-modal models or datasets beyond those used in the experiments.
- The theoretical analysis and proofs are not fully convincing, particularly in the proof of Theorem 3.5 and the assumptions made about the model's behavior under different conditions.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method against a broader range of attacks and a more rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the proof of Theorem 3.5 and how these assumptions affect the generalizability of the findings?
- How does the proposed method perform against more complex and diverse attacks, such as multi-modal attacks?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the experimental results, particularly in terms of the statistical significance of the improvements observed?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method to larger and more complex multi-modal models?
