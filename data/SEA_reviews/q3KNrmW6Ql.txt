 **Summary:**
The paper introduces G-FairAttack, a method for adversarial attacks on fairness in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). The authors propose a novel surrogate loss function to mimic various fairness-aware GNNs, which is crucial for the success of the attack. The paper also presents a non-gradient optimization method to solve the constrained optimization problem and a fast computation strategy to improve scalability. The methodology includes a sequential attack algorithm and a fast computation approach to reduce time complexity. Experiments are conducted on real-world datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of G-FairAttack in corrupting the fairness of GNNs with minimal impact on prediction utility. However, the paper's novelty and the practical implications of the attacks are questioned, with concerns about the real-world applicability and the necessity of the attacks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel surrogate loss function that can mimic different fairness loss functions, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed attack method is innovative, using a non-gradient optimization method and a fast computation strategy to improve scalability.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear problem formulation and a thorough discussion of the limitations.
- The experimental evaluation is extensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed G-FairAttack in corrupting the fairness of GNNs while minimally impacting prediction utility.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear problem definition and a well-motivated approach to addressing the challenges of attacking fairness in GNNs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implications of the attacks, particularly in scenarios where the attacker cannot control the training process of the victim model.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it primarily extends existing attack methods to fairness-aware GNNs without significant innovation.
- The paper does not adequately address the real-world applicability of the attacks, which is crucial for evaluating the practical impact of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis on the effectiveness of the proposed attack method, which could strengthen the paper's claims.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with other existing attack methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach.
- The paper's contribution to the field is questioned, as the proposed method does not introduce new types of attacks and the problem formulation is considered a direct extension of existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the practical implications of the attacks, particularly in scenarios where the attacker cannot control the training process of the victim model?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing attack methods, especially in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- What is the rationale behind choosing the total variation loss as the surrogate loss, and how does it compare to other potential surrogate loss functions?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of fairness loss functions, and what are the implications of using different loss functions in the attack process?
- Could you provide more insights into the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed attack method, particularly how it ensures the effectiveness of the attacks?
- How does the proposed method perform under different attack budgets, and what are the implications of varying the attack budget on the attack's effectiveness?
