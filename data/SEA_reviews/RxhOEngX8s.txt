 **Summary:**
The paper introduces BROAD, a novel benchmark for evaluating out-of-distribution (OOD) detection across various types of distribution shifts. It includes a comprehensive evaluation of existing OOD detection methods and proposes a new ensemble method based on Gaussian mixture models to enhance OOD detection capabilities. The benchmark encompasses five distinct types of distribution shifts, each represented by multiple datasets, and includes a diverse set of OOD detection methods. The paper also presents a new method that combines existing detection scores using a Gaussian mixture model to achieve more consistent and comprehensive OOD detection. Despite its strengths in benchmarking and methodological innovation, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty and potential lack of generalizability due to its focus on specific datasets and model architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmark, BROAD, which is a significant contribution to the field of out-of-distribution (OOD) detection.
- It provides a comprehensive evaluation of existing OOD detection methods, highlighting the inconsistencies in their performance across different types of distribution shifts.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes a detailed analysis of the limitations of current methods, which is crucial for advancing the field.
- The introduction of a new method based on Gaussian mixture models for OOD detection is innovative and shows potential for improving detection capabilities.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the limitations of current methods, which is crucial for advancing the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on specific datasets and model architectures (ResNet-50 and ViT) may limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it primarily combines existing methods without significant innovation.
- The performance of the proposed method is not significantly better than existing methods, which raises concerns about its effectiveness.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the benchmark, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and scope.
- There are issues with the presentation of results, such as inconsistencies in the reported results and a lack of clarity in the presentation of the results for different models and datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other datasets and model architectures beyond those tested?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it compares to existing methods?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of distribution shifts that are not represented in the benchmark?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the selection of datasets and the rationale behind the choice of specific models for testing?
- How does the proposed method address the limitations of current methods, particularly in terms of generalizability and applicability to real-world scenarios?
