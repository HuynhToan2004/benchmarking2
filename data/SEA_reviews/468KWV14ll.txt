 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DRND, a variant of the Random Network Distillation (RND) algorithm designed to address the "bonus inconsistency" issue in RND by distilling a distribution of random networks and incorporating pseudo counts to improve bonus allocation precision. DRND's approach aims to enhance exploration in both online and offline reinforcement learning settings, showing superior performance in various benchmarks compared to RND. The paper also explores the application of DRND in offline RL, combining it with SAC to create SAC-DRND, which outperforms other baselines. Despite its theoretical contributions and experimental validations, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach and the clarity of its presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, DRND, effectively addresses the "bonus inconsistency" issue in RND, enhancing bonus allocation precision and exploration efficiency.
- Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of DRND in both online and offline reinforcement learning settings, showing superior performance in various benchmarks.
- The method's ability to improve exploration in challenging environments and its application in offline RL are notable contributions.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the bonus inconsistency issue and how DRND addresses it, with a well-structured presentation of the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited, as DRND appears to be a direct extension of RND with pseudo counts, which may not significantly advance the field beyond existing methods.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the computational overhead and memory requirements of DRND compared to RND, which could be crucial for practical deployment.
- The experimental setup and results are not thoroughly validated, with some results showing inconsistencies or lack of clarity, such as the performance of DRND in certain environments and the comparison with other methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of DRND, which could include its applicability to different types of environments or its sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of DRND, particularly the assumptions made and their implications.

**Questions:**
- How does DRND compare to other exploration methods like curiosity-driven approaches and count-based methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- Can you clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly the performance of DRND in different environments and the comparison with other methods?
- How does DRND handle the computational overhead and memory requirements compared to RND, and what are the practical implications of these costs?
- Is there a specific reason why DRND was not tested in more complex or varied environments, such as the AntMaze dataset, and how might this affect the generalizability of the results?
- Could you provide more details on the theoretical assumptions made in DRND and how they influence the performance and applicability of the method?
