 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to address class imbalance in binary and multiclass classification problems by regrouping the majority class into smaller pseudo-classes, turning the imbalanced classification problem into a balanced multi-class classification. This method leverages pretrained models like CLIP to encode input images into a lower-dimensional feature space, which is then used to cluster the majority class into pseudo-classes. The proposed method, termed RG, is evaluated on several datasets, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and HAM10000, and shows promising results in terms of average precision (AP) and balanced accuracy (BA). However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods, and the empirical evaluation is seen as insufficient.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is novel and innovative, particularly in its application to image classification using pretrained models like CLIP, which can be extended to other modalities like text and audio.
- The method demonstrates good performance on several datasets, showing a significant improvement over baseline methods.
- The paper provides a clear motivation for the proposed method, which is supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence.
- The experiments are well-designed, with appropriate baselines and metrics used to evaluate the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There is a notable absence of comparisons with other clustering algorithms, which could provide insights into the effectiveness of different clustering methods in the context of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of computational complexity and memory requirements, which could limit its applicability to larger datasets or more complex models.
- The empirical evaluation of the method is seen as insufficient, with a limited number of datasets and a lack of detailed analysis of the results, especially in terms of statistical significance and reproducibility.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the generalizability of the method to other datasets or domains, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant in certain applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be applied to datasets with more classes, such as CIFAR-100 or ImageNet, and how would it perform in such scenarios?
- How does the method perform when applied to other datasets, such as those in the long-tailed learning domain, which are known to be challenging for imbalanced learning?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to other modalities, such as text or audio, and how would this affect the performance or applicability of the method?
- How does the method handle the issue of asynchronous learning, particularly in terms of the learning pace of different classes?
- Could the method be adapted to handle more complex scenarios, such as those involving multiple minority classes, and how would this affect the performance or applicability of the method?
- How does the method compare to other methods in terms of computational complexity and memory requirements, and what are the implications of these factors for the scalability of the method?
- Can the method be adapted to handle different types of class imbalance, such as imbalance in the number of samples per class or imbalance in the distribution of features across classes?
