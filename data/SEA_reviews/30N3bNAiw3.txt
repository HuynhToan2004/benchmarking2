 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for contrastive analysis, leveraging contrastive learning to separate common from salient patterns across different datasets. This approach maximizes mutual information between the common and salient spaces, using alignment and uniformity terms to achieve this goal. The methodology is evaluated on various datasets, including medical and natural images, demonstrating its effectiveness in distinguishing between common and salient factors. The paper also addresses the issue of information leakage between common and salient spaces by minimizing mutual information between them. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive theoretical analysis, and insufficient experimental validation, particularly in terms of scalability and generalizability to larger datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for contrastive analysis that separates common from salient patterns using a contrastive learning framework, which is both innovative and effective.
- The method is theoretically grounded, with a clear and detailed derivation of the objective function, which enhances understanding and reproducibility.
- The paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments across various datasets, including both medical and natural images, which demonstrates the versatility and applicability of the proposed method.
- The use of mutual information terms to capture common and salient information is a novel approach that is well-motivated and theoretically sound.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear exposition of the methodology and its implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the scalability and generalizability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger, more complex datasets.
- There is a lack of comprehensive theoretical analysis, particularly concerning the identifiability of the model and the conditions under which the model can recover the true latent factors of the underlying data-generating process.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of information leakage between common and salient spaces, which is a critical aspect of the proposed method.
- The paper suffers from a lack of novelty, as it builds upon existing methods such as SepVAE and does not sufficiently differentiate itself from these prior works.
- The experimental setup is limited, and the paper could benefit from additional experiments, such as those involving multi-view learning and a broader range of datasets, to further validate the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the choice of hyperparameters and the architecture of the encoder, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancy in the number of attributes used in the experiments, as mentioned in the appendix and in the main text?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to datasets with more complex patterns, such as those involving multiple salient factors?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the standard deviation of the experiments and the performance of the method on larger datasets?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Could the authors provide more insights into the identifiability of the model and the conditions under which the model can recover the true latent factors of the underlying data-generating process?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of information leakage between common and salient spaces, and what are the implications of this on the effectiveness of the method?
