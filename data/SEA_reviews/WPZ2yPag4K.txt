 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FactTune, a method for enhancing the factuality of text generated by large language models (LLMs) without requiring human labeling. It leverages two approaches for estimating truthfulness: reference-based and reference-free. The reference-based approach uses a FactScore method to measure the consistency of generated text with external knowledge, while the reference-free approach relies on the model's confidence in its generated claims. These methods are used to construct preference datasets, which are then used to fine-tune LLMs using the Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) algorithm. The paper presents experiments on two datasets, showing that FactTune improves the factuality of LLMs compared to standard RLHF and decoding strategies. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach and the limited scope of the experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces FactTune, a method for enhancing the factuality of text generated by LLMs without requiring human labeling, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is well-motivated and clearly explained, with a focus on reducing the need for human intervention in model training.
- The paper presents a straightforward approach that is easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The method is applicable to a wide range of LLMs and tasks, and does not require a large number of examples or extensive tuning, making it practical for real-world applications.
- The paper includes a thorough analysis of the impact of different design decisions, which adds depth to the understanding of the method's effectiveness.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing baselines in terms of factuality.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the method's impact on the fluency of generated text, which is a significant limitation given the trade-off between factuality and fluency in LLM outputs.
- The experimental setup is limited to specific datasets and tasks, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the method across different domains and applications.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as similar methods have been previously proposed, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from existing work.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for overfitting in the LLMs due to the use of the same models for both training and evaluation, which could lead to biased results.
- The method's reliance on the accuracy of the LLMs used for claim extraction and question rephrasing could limit its effectiveness if these models are not highly accurate.
- The paper does not discuss the potential societal impacts of the method, which could be significant given the widespread use of LLMs in various applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how they ensure that the generated text is not only factual but also fluent?
- How does the method perform when applied to different domains and tasks beyond those tested in the paper?
- What are the specific advantages of the reference-free approach over the reference-based approach, and how do these trade-offs affect the overall effectiveness of the method?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting due to the use of the same models for training and evaluation?
- Could the authors provide more details on the methodology used for claim extraction and question rephrasing, particularly how these processes affect the overall performance of the method?
- How does the method perform when applied to larger models, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in scaling up the method?
