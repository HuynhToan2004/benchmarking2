 **Summary:**
The paper explores the class-wise generalization error in supervised learning, focusing on the discrepancies in generalization performance across different classes. It introduces novel information-theoretic bounds using KL divergence and conditional mutual information (CMI) to quantify these variations. The authors theoretically derive and empirically validate these bounds using ResNet-50 on CIFAR10 and its noisy variant. They demonstrate that these bounds can provide insights into generalization behavior, particularly in scenarios where data is noisy or class imbalanced. The paper also discusses how these theoretical tools can be applied to other contexts, such as sub-task generalization and generalization in the presence of sensitive attributes.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and interesting concept of class-wise generalization error, which is a significant contribution to the field of machine learning.
- The theoretical derivations are well-presented, and the paper is generally easy to follow, making the complex mathematical concepts accessible.
- The empirical validation using ResNet-50 on CIFAR10 and its noisy variant supports the theoretical findings, enhancing the credibility of the results.
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions and notations that aid in understanding the complex mathematical formulations.
- The theoretical tools developed in the paper can be applied to various scenarios beyond the class-generalization error, showcasing their versatility and potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the results would generalize to other types of models or datasets, which could limit the practical applicability of the findings.
- The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on ResNet-50 on CIFAR10, which might not fully demonstrate the robustness or applicability of the proposed bounds across different model architectures and datasets.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing bounds, which could help in understanding the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed bounds.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the assumptions made in the theoretical derivations, particularly the assumptions around the conditional mutual information.
- The paper could be improved by including a more detailed literature review and a clearer discussion on the implications and limitations of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on how the results would generalize to other types of models or datasets?
- How do the proposed bounds compare to existing bounds in terms of tightness and applicability?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of their bounds with the bounds proposed by Harutyunyan et al. (2021) for standard generalization error?
- How do the bounds perform when applied to other models or datasets, and what are the implications of these applications?
- Can the authors discuss the implications of their findings for practical applications, such as improving generalization performance in real-world scenarios?
- How do the authors address the assumptions made in the theoretical derivations, particularly the assumptions around the conditional mutual information?
