 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LUM-ViT, a novel approach to under-sampling in hyperspectral imaging using a learnable mask for pre-acquisition modulation. The method employs a Vision Transformer variant and a kernel-level weight binarization technique to optimize optical calculations. It is evaluated on both simulated and real-world data, demonstrating a reduction in data volume and maintaining accuracy. However, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the method beyond the specific dataset used and the practicality of the under-sampling rates in real-world applications. The paper also faces criticism for its limited comparative analysis and the lack of detailed hardware specifications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to under-sampling in hyperspectral imaging, using a learnable mask for pre-acquisition modulation, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear figures and detailed explanations, making the paper accessible and easy to understand.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results, which enhances its readability and comprehensibility.
- The use of a learnable mask for under-sampling is innovative and has been validated through experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper provides a detailed explanation of the hardware setup and experimental results, which adds credibility to the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer understanding of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The hardware specifications, such as the resolution and spectral range of the spectrometer, are not clearly stated, which could limit the reproducibility and applicability of the results.
- The paper's focus on under-sampling rates that are too low for practical applications (e.g., 2%) raises concerns about the practicality and real-world applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a single dataset, which may not generalize well to other datasets or real-world applications.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the practical implementation of the under-sampling mask in real-world scenarios, particularly how it is determined before the target object is detected.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost and efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for real-time applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the hardware setup, such as the resolution and spectral range of the spectrometer, to enhance the reproducibility and applicability of the results?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other datasets or real-world applications beyond the specific dataset used in the study?
- Can the authors clarify the practical implementation of the under-sampling mask in real-world scenarios, particularly how it is determined before the target object is detected?
- What are the computational costs and efficiency of the proposed method, and how do they compare to other state-of-the-art methods?
- Could the authors provide more comparative analysis with other state-of-the-art methods to better understand the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method?
