 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Sorting Krylov Recycling (SKR), aimed at enhancing the efficiency of data generation for neural operators in solving partial differential equations (PDEs). SKR utilizes a sorting algorithm to sequence the systems of linear equations, thereby reducing computational redundancy and improving the convergence rate of Krylov subspace methods. The method is evaluated against traditional GMRES algorithms across various PDE datasets, demonstrating significant improvements in computational efficiency and accuracy. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty and insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and effectively demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through extensive experiments.
- The proposed Sorting Krylov Recycling (SKR) method is innovative and provides a novel approach to solving systems of linear equations in the context of neural operators for PDEs.
- SKR is shown to reduce computational redundancy and improve the convergence rate of Krylov subspace methods, which is crucial for data generation in neural operators.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies, which provide a detailed analysis of the method's performance and its impact on computational efficiency and accuracy.
- The method's application in reducing computational costs for neural operators, which is a significant challenge in the field, is highlighted as a key contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not adequately compare it with other state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of its effectiveness.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as it appears to be a simple combination of existing techniques without significant new insights or contributions.
- The paper does not include a detailed comparison with other methods for reducing computational costs in neural operators, such as those that use physical priors or other data-efficient methods.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the description of the sorting algorithm and its impact on the method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the method, such as its applicability to nonlinear problems or its scalability to larger, more complex datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the sorting algorithm and its impact on the method's effectiveness? How does the sorting algorithm contribute to the reduction of computational redundancy?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods for reducing computational costs in neural operators, particularly in terms of accuracy and efficiency?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle nonlinear problems, and if so, how might this be achieved?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and potential areas for future research?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of neural operators, such as Fourier Neural Operators (FNOs) or other types of neural operators?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational costs and efficiency of their method with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of the number of iterations required and the computational time?
