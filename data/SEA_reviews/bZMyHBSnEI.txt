 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel multimodal fusion approach using a deep equilibrium (DEQ) model, aiming to balance intra- and inter-modal interactions. The DEQ model iteratively projects modality-specific and fused features to an equilibrium state, integrating both static and dynamic fusion strategies. The authors claim that this method achieves state-of-the-art performance on several benchmarks, although the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in methodological details and the absence of comparisons with recent, more effective fusion methods. The paper also lacks a comprehensive comparison with other multimodal learning methods and does not adequately address the computational efficiency and scalability of the DEQ model.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to multimodal fusion using a deep equilibrium (DEQ) model, which iteratively projects modality-specific and fused features to an equilibrium state.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on several benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a thorough experimental evaluation of the proposed method.
- The DEQ model is a flexible architecture that can be adapted to various multimodal tasks and frameworks, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
- The paper includes extensive ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the DEQ model and its components, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the method.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with recent, more effective fusion methods, which could have provided a better context for the evaluation of the DEQ model.
- There is a lack of comprehensive comparison with other multimodal learning methods, such as large pre-trained models, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the DEQ model's effectiveness.
- The computational efficiency and scalability of the DEQ model are not discussed, which is a significant concern given the potential computational overhead of the method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the DEQ model's training and inference, which could affect the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the DEQ model, such as its potential to become computationally expensive with the addition of more modalities.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the DEQ model is trained and inferred, including the specifics of the root solver used and the computational resources required?
- How does the DEQ model perform with a larger number of modalities, and what are the computational costs associated with this?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the gating function in the DEQ model, particularly in terms of its ability to dynamically perceive modality importance?
- Why was the DEQ model chosen over other multimodal learning methods, such as large pre-trained models, and how does it compare in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the DEQ model's training and inference, to improve the reproducibility of the results?
- How does the DEQ model handle the potential for feature collapse or overfitting, particularly in scenarios where it may not find an equilibrium state?
