 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the hypothesis that "Junk DNA" in pre-trained large language models (LLMs) plays a crucial role in solving more complex tasks. It challenges the notion that small-magnitude weights are redundant and can be safely removed, arguing that they encode vital knowledge necessary for handling more challenging downstream tasks. The study employs sparsity techniques to quantify the significance of low-magnitude weights across various tasks and datasets, demonstrating that these weights are essential for maintaining performance on difficult tasks. The paper also proposes a novel methodology for assessing task difficulty, using metrics such as data adequacy, multi-domain learning, and external information availability. Despite its novel approach and extensive experiments, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in defining "junk DNA" and the absence of a comprehensive literature review, which could undermine its credibility and originality.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel hypothesis regarding the importance of small-magnitude weights in pre-trained LLMs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- Extensive experiments are conducted across various datasets, models, and pruning methods, providing a robust validation of the proposed hypothesis.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear figures that aid in understanding the results.
- The Junk DNA Hypothesis is supported by extensive experiments and the results are consistent, showing that small-magnitude weights are essential for solving more complex tasks.
- The paper addresses the challenge of finding a quantifiable definition of "task difficulty," which is crucial for understanding the role of small-magnitude weights in LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive literature review, which is crucial for establishing the novelty and originality of the work.
- The definition of "junk DNA" is unclear and inconsistent throughout the paper, which could lead to confusion and misinterpretation of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the research, which is a significant omission.
- The methodology for measuring task difficulty is not well-explained, and the results are not convincingly linked to the hypothesis, which weakens the paper's argument.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the limitations of the study, which is essential for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- There is a lack of comparison with other pruning methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed hypothesis.

**Questions:**
- How do you define "junk DNA" in the context of LLMs, and how does this definition influence the interpretation of the results?
- Could you clarify the methodology for measuring task difficulty and how this relates to the hypothesis being tested?
- How does the paper address the potential negative societal impacts of the research, and what steps have been taken to mitigate these risks?
- In light of the findings, what are the implications for the development of LLMs, and how can this research be applied to improve model design and training strategies?
- Can you provide more details on the limitations of the study and how these might affect the generalizability of the findings?
