 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Dec-Options, a method for decomposing neural networks into sub-policies that can be used to synthesize temporally extended actions, or options, in reinforcement learning. This decomposition is achieved by mapping neural networks with piecewise linear activation functions to oblique decision trees, allowing for the creation of options by wrapping sub-policies in while-loops. The method involves a selection mechanism based on minimizing the Levin loss for a uniform policy on these options, aiming to identify useful options that accelerate learning on similar but different tasks. The paper evaluates Dec-Options in two grid-world domains and compares it with various baselines, showing that it can outperform other methods in some scenarios. However, the method's assumptions about the sequence of tasks and the generalizability of options across different tasks are not thoroughly explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The problem addressed is significant and the proposed solution is novel, introducing a method for learning options from neural networks.
- The paper provides a clear description of the algorithm and its components, including the decomposition process and the selection mechanism based on minimizing the Levin loss.
- The experimental setup is well-designed, with a clear description of the tasks and the evaluation metrics used.
- The method's ability to learn options from neural networks is highlighted, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental setup is limited, focusing on only two grid-world domains, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the method across different tasks or environments.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, such as those mentioned in the related work section, which could have provided a clearer context for the proposed method's contributions.
- The assumptions made about the sequence of tasks and the generalizability of options across different tasks are not thoroughly explored or justified.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the method, particularly the high time complexity of the greedy approximation used for option selection.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with some sections being difficult to follow due to unclear explanations and notations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the notations and definitions used in the paper, particularly in sections where they are not well-explained?
- How does the method handle the issue of generalizability of options across different tasks, especially when the tasks are not similar?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that learn options directly from neural networks?
- How does the method scale with the complexity of the tasks and the size of the neural networks used?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the method and potential areas for future research, such as extending the method to continuous action spaces or exploring different activation functions?
- In the context of the experiments, can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameters used and their impact on the results?
