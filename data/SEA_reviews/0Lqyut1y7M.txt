 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the optimality of activations in Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) from a theoretical perspective, specifically focusing on the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem. It explores how different activations, such as sinc, Gaussian, wavelet, and ReLU, affect the reconstruction of signals and their approximation properties. The authors propose that sinc activations, which form a Riesz basis, are theoretically optimal for signal encoding in shallow INRs. The paper also connects INRs to dynamical systems and uses sampling theory to bridge these two paradigms. However, the paper's clarity and accessibility to readers unfamiliar with sampling theory are noted as significant issues.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by connecting implicit neural representations (INRs) to sampling theory, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, with clear definitions, propositions, and theorems that effectively explain the properties of different activations.
- The paper demonstrates the practical applicability of these theoretical insights through experiments, showcasing the effectiveness of sinc activations in image reconstruction and dynamical systems modeling.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear introduction and logical progression of ideas, making it easy to follow.
- The use of sinc activation in INRs is highlighted as a novel contribution, which could potentially lead to new insights and applications in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's writing is not accessible to readers without a strong background in sampling theory, making it difficult for a broader audience to understand the content fully.
- The experimental results are somewhat underwhelming, with the performance of sinc activation not consistently outperforming other activations in all scenarios.
- The paper's claims about the optimality of sinc activation and its theoretical advantages are not convincingly demonstrated, particularly in terms of practical applicability and empirical evidence.
- The paper's focus on shallow INRs and its theoretical approach might limit its relevance to more complex or deeper INRs, which are commonly used in practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, such as how to optimally use sinc activation in INRs and its potential limitations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the practical implications of using sinc activation in INRs and how it can be optimally applied in different scenarios?
- How does the choice of activation function in INRs affect the model's performance, and what are the empirical results that support the theoretical claims made in the paper?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the theoretical concepts and their practical applications in INRs?
- How do the results of this study apply to deeper INRs, which are more commonly used in practical applications?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of their approach and discuss potential future directions for research in this area?
