 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the emergence of symbolic concepts in well-trained deep neural networks (DNNs) by focusing on the sparsity of interactions between input variables. It proposes that under specific conditions, such as the network's output having high-order derivatives that are zero, the network's performance on occluded samples, and the network's confidence not significantly degrading on occluded samples, the DNN can encode a small number of sparse interactions. These interactions are treated as symbolic primitive inference patterns. The paper also provides theoretical proofs and empirical evidence to support these claims, albeit with some limitations in the scope of experiments and assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-understand language, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The topic of the paper is significant, focusing on the emergence of symbolic concepts in deep neural networks (DNNs), which is a crucial area of research in explainable AI.
- The paper introduces novel theoretical results on the sparsity of interactions in DNNs, which could potentially be applicable to a wide range of networks trained for various tasks.
- The authors provide empirical evidence to support their claims, including detailed experimental results and a thorough discussion on the emergence of sparse interactions in DNNs.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear and detailed proofs of the main theorems, which are supported by extensive experiments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on image classification tasks, which might limit the generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks such as natural language processing.
- The assumptions made in the paper, particularly Assumption 3, are not well justified, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion or empirical evidence to support these assumptions.
- The experiments are limited to a small number of samples, which might not be sufficient to draw robust conclusions.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the work in detail, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The proofs and assumptions in the paper are not clearly explained, which could hinder the understanding of the theoretical contributions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly Assumption 3, and provide more detailed justification or empirical evidence to support these assumptions?
- How does the paper's approach to interaction primitives differ from previous works that have also explored similar concepts?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their work and the implications of these limitations for the generalizability of the findings?
- In the proof of Theorem 2, how does the proof handle the assumption that the sum of effects of all k-order interactions is zero?
- Could the authors elaborate on the experimental setup and results, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions might not hold, such as in natural language processing tasks?
