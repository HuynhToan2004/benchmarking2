 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Readout-Decomposition of Activation Change (RDAC) framework, which aims to address the stability-plasticity dilemma in continual learning by analyzing the decomposition of activation changes into range and null space components. The RDAC framework is applied to various continual learning algorithms, including SI, EWC, LwF, GEM, and data replay, to assess their stability-plasticity trade-offs. The authors demonstrate that the proposed method can maintain stability without significant plasticity loss, providing valuable insights into the behavior of existing CL algorithms and potentially guiding the development of new approaches. The framework is tested on one-hidden-layer linear neural networks, showing that it can restrict activation change only in the range of prior readouts to maintain high stability while not sacrificing plasticity.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined problem.
- The proposed framework, RDAC, is simple, intuitive, and provides a novel perspective on the stability-plasticity dilemma in continual learning.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the stability-plasticity trade-offs of popular continual learning algorithms, comparing their behavior to the learning-induced activation changes in the range and null space of the prior readouts.
- The RDAC framework offers a diagnostic tool to assess the behavior of existing continual learning algorithms and provides insights into the development of new approaches.
- The paper is well-organized and the results are clearly presented, with a comprehensive discussion of the implications of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on the stability-plasticity dilemma in task-incremental learning, which may not be generalizable to other continual learning scenarios, such as unbounded continual learning.
- The analysis of the RDAC framework is limited to one-hidden-layer linear neural networks, and it is unclear how the framework can be extended to more complex network architectures like CNNs.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing continual learning algorithms, particularly in terms of their performance on previous tasks and their ability to learn new tasks.
- The empirical results are limited to a few datasets and architectures, and the paper lacks comprehensive experiments to support the claims made.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not include a discussion on the societal impact of the work.

**Questions:**
- Can the RDAC framework be extended to other continual learning scenarios, such as unbounded continual learning?
- How can the RDAC framework be applied to more complex network architectures like CNNs?
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the RDAC framework with existing continual learning algorithms, particularly in terms of their performance on previous tasks and their ability to learn new tasks?
- How can the insights from the RDAC framework be used to develop new continual learning algorithms?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how might these limitations affect the practical application of the RDAC framework?
- Can the authors discuss the societal impact of their work and any potential ethical considerations?
