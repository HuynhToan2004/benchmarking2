 **Summary:**
The paper introduces NL2ProGPT, a novel framework for macromolecular protein sequence generation that bridges the domain gap between natural and protein languages. It employs a pre-trained protein language model to generate textual descriptions of protein sequences, which are then fine-tuned using a reinforcement learning algorithm to ensure adherence to structural constraints. The model is evaluated against existing methods and shows promising results in generating high-quality protein sequences. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, as similar methods have been previously used, and the generalizability of the results across different datasets. The paper's experimental setup and the clarity of its presentation were also criticized, with suggestions for more rigorous comparisons and clearer descriptions of the methodology.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, NL2ProGPT, which effectively bridges the domain gap between natural and protein languages, enabling conversational protein design.
- The framework incorporates structural information from protein representation models, enhancing the informativeness and generalizability of protein descriptions.
- The use of reinforcement learning from AI feedback for fine-tuning the model under the constraints of structural information is a significant contribution.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The methodology is straightforward and innovative, utilizing a pre-trained protein language model to generate textual descriptions of protein sequences.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which limits the evaluation of its performance against the current best practices.
- The use of a small dataset (Pfam) for training the model raises concerns about the model's generalizability to other, more diverse datasets.
- The experimental setup is not rigorous, with the use of small datasets and limited evaluation metrics, which may not provide a comprehensive assessment of the model's performance.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential shortcomings.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected for better clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods to better establish the performance of their model?
- How does the model perform when trained on other, more diverse datasets, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the reward model in the overall performance of the model?
- How does the model handle the generation of protein sequences with specific functional domains, and what are the implications for its practical applications?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their proposed method and how these might affect its applicability in real-world scenarios?
