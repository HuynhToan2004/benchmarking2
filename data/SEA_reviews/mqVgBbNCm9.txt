 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach called Skeleton-of-Thought (SoT) to enhance the efficiency of large language models (LLMs) in generating responses. This method involves first generating a skeleton of the response, which is then expanded in parallel, potentially reducing response generation latency. The SoT approach is tested across various LLMs, including open-source and proprietary models, showing significant speedups and in some cases, improved answer quality. The paper also explores the use of a router to adaptively trigger SoT, enhancing its applicability across different question types. Despite these advancements, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation on a small dataset, the potential bias in the evaluation methodology, and the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, making it accessible for a wide range of applications.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the method across multiple models, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach in reducing latency and improving answer quality.
- The method's application is not limited to specific models or settings, making it a versatile tool for various applications.
- The authors have conducted a comprehensive evaluation, including a detailed analysis of the method's performance across different models and question categories.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a small dataset, which may not adequately represent the method's performance in real-world scenarios with a larger and more diverse set of questions.
- There is a potential bias in the evaluation methodology, as the answer quality is evaluated using a model (GPT-4) that might not be independent of the method being evaluated.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for some applications.
- The method's generalizability is limited by its focus on specific question types that require a long answer whose structure can be planned ahead, which may not be applicable to all types of questions.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the dataset used for evaluation, particularly its size and diversity?
- How does the method perform when evaluated using different models, especially those not used in the evaluation?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated?
- How does the method compare to other data-level approaches for improving the efficiency of LLMs, and what are the key differences?
- Could the authors provide more details on the implementation of the method, particularly how it is adapted for different models and question types?
