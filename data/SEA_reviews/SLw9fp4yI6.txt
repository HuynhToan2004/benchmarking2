 **Summary:**
The paper introduces "model arithmetic," a novel framework for controlled text generation that combines multiple LLMs and attributes into a single, precisely controlled model. This method allows for fine-grained control over generated text, extending the capabilities of speculative sampling for efficient inference. The framework is evaluated on toxicity reduction and other controlled text generation tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency. It also introduces a new operator, the union operator, which enhances the framework's expressiveness and control. The paper is well-written, providing clear explanations and extensive empirical results, although it could benefit from more detailed discussions on the limitations and potential misuse of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and extensive empirical results.
- The proposed method is intuitive, flexible, and efficient, with the ability to express many prior CTG approaches.
- The introduction of the union operator enhances the expressiveness and control of the framework, enabling more precise generation of text with desired attributes.
- The speculative sampling extension is novel and helpful, reducing the number of model calls and improving efficiency.
- The method is orthogonal to other techniques like prompting, fine-tuning, and simple CTG techniques, allowing for a variety of controlled text generation tasks.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method, particularly in reducing toxicity in generated text.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, including potential misuse scenarios and the computational overhead of the union operator.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational cost of the union operator, which could be a significant factor in practical applications.
- There is a need for more comprehensive experiments to validate the claims of fine-grained control and efficiency, especially in terms of the impact on fluency and the practical applicability of the method.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with other recent works that use different methods to achieve similar results, such as those using gradient-based methods or other optimization techniques.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the method, such as the scalability and applicability in different settings.

**Questions:**
- How does the computational cost of the union operator scale with the size and complexity of the models used?
- Is there a possibility to use the union operator in a more efficient way, such as by combining it with other operators or optimizing the sampling process?
- Can the method be applied to other types of LLMs, such as those using different architectures or training methods?
- How does the method perform in terms of perplexity and other quality metrics compared to other CTG methods?
- Could you provide more detailed results on the practical applicability of the method, such as the time required for inference and the impact on fluency and coherence of the generated text?
- How does the method handle different types of attributes, such as those that are not easily expressible in natural language?
