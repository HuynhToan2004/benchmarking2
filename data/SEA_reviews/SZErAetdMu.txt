 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TOTEM, a universal approach for scalable time series modeling across various tasks and domains, leveraging a task-agnostic embedding derived from a self-supervised objective using a convolution-based vector quantized variational autoencoder. This embedding is then utilized to learn downstream models using the Transformer architecture. TOTEM demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on forecasting, classification, and translation tasks across diverse domains, including neuroscience, seismology, meteorology, power grids, and urban traffic. It also showcases scalability by introducing new datasets and evaluating on larger benchmarks. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its reliance on pre-existing methodologies, lack of novelty in model design, and limited experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the scalability and universality of time series modeling across various tasks and domains, which is a significant challenge in the field.
- It introduces a novel approach using a self-supervised objective formulated as a task-independent convolution-based vector quantized variational autoencoder (VQ-VAE) for learning task-agnostic embeddings.
- The paper is well-organized, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed descriptions of the proposed method and its application to different tasks and domains.
- The methodology is supported by extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showing superior performance over existing state-of-the-art methods in various applications.
- The paper introduces new datasets and benchmarks, which are valuable resources for the community and enhance the reproducibility and applicability of the research.
- The zero-shot generalization capabilities of the method are highlighted, demonstrating its ability to adapt to new, unseen data without retraining, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's reliance on pre-existing methodologies, such as VQ-VAE and transformers, limits the novelty of the proposed approach.
- The experimental validation is limited, with only a few datasets used for evaluation, which may not sufficiently demonstrate the generalizability of the method across diverse domains.
- The methodology's scalability and applicability in real-world scenarios with large, complex, and diverse datasets are not thoroughly tested or discussed.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and advanced methods in time series forecasting and analysis, which could provide a clearer picture of the method's performance and relevance.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational and memory requirements of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical implementation in resource-constrained environments.
- The paper's claims of universality and scalability are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which could benefit from more comprehensive testing and validation.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle large, complex, and diverse datasets that are common in real-world applications?
- Can the method be adapted to handle missing data in time series, which is a common issue in practical applications?
- How does the method perform when applied to time series data from different domains that have distinct characteristics, such as different data distributions or sampling frequencies?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational and memory requirements of the proposed method, especially in comparison to other state-of-the-art methods?
- How does the method handle the scalability and applicability across different domains and tasks, especially when the data distributions and characteristics vary significantly?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results and comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods in time series forecasting and analysis, especially those that use similar or more advanced models?
- How does the method handle the zero-shot generalization in practical applications, where the data distributions and characteristics may not be as distinct as in controlled experiments?
