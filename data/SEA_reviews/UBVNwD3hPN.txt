 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CivRealm, a novel environment designed to evaluate decision-making agents in complex, open-ended scenarios inspired by the Civilization game. The environment offers both tensor-based and language-based APIs, allowing for diverse agent implementations. It includes mini-games to assess agent capabilities and a full game for comprehensive evaluation. The authors present initial results using RL and LLM-based agents, highlighting the challenges in managing complex environments with sparse rewards and long-term planning. The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of existing methods and proposes new approaches to enhance agent reasoning and learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new environment, CivRealm, which is open-source and offers both tensor-based and language-based APIs, providing a versatile platform for agent evaluations.
- The environment is designed to be complex and challenging, with a focus on long-term planning and reasoning, which is well-suited for evaluating agents' capabilities in strategic decision-making.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the environment and its features, including the types of tasks and the mini-games used for evaluation.
- The authors present a comprehensive evaluation of existing methods, including RL and LLM-based agents, and provide insights into the strengths and limitations of these approaches.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for why the Civilization game was chosen as the basis for the environment, and how it specifically enhances the evaluation of agents compared to other games or environments.
- There is a lack of novelty in the agent architectures presented, with the RL and LLM-based agents using standard approaches that have been previously explored.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation and training of the RL agent, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The evaluation of the agents is limited to a few instances, which might not be sufficient to draw robust conclusions about their performance.
- The paper does not discuss the ethical implications of the work, which is a significant omission given the potential societal impact of the research.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify why the Civilization game was chosen as the basis for the environment, and how it specifically enhances the evaluation of agents compared to other games or environments?
- How does the RL agent perform in the full game compared to the mini-games, and what are the main challenges in scaling up from mini-games to the full game?
- How does the LLM-based agent perform in the full game, and what are the main challenges in scaling up from mini-games to the full game?
- Could you provide more details on the implementation and training of the RL agent, including the specific hyperparameters used and the training data and environment setup?
- How does the environment handle multi-agent interactions, and what are the specific challenges and benefits of these interactions in the context of the environment?
- Could you discuss the ethical implications of the work and how they were addressed in the design and implementation of the environment and the agents?
