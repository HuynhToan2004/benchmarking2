 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Global and Local Embedding Network (GLEN), a novel method for temporal graph representation learning that integrates both global and local perspectives. GLEN leverages a GCN-TCN-based global embedding module, a local embedding module based on time interval weighting, and a cross-perspective fusion module to effectively model temporal graphs. Extensive experiments are conducted on multiple real-world datasets, demonstrating GLEN's effectiveness in link prediction and dynamic node classification tasks. The paper also includes a detailed analysis of hyper-parameters and provides a comprehensive ablation study to validate the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined problem statement.
- The proposed method, GLEN, is innovative and effective, integrating both global and local perspectives to model temporal graphs, which is a novel approach.
- The paper includes extensive experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of GLEN across various datasets and tasks.
- The methodology is well-explained, and the paper provides a detailed analysis of the impact of hyper-parameters, contributing to the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper includes a comprehensive review of related work and a detailed description of the proposed method, which aids in understanding the contributions and the context of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, which could undermine the claimed advantages of GLEN.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definition and implementation of the global and local perspectives, which could lead to confusion about the actual contributions of each perspective.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The experimental setup and the choice of baselines are not adequately justified, which could raise questions about the fairness and robustness of the comparisons.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the cross-perspective fusion module and the rationale behind the choice of hyper-parameters.
- There is a need for more diverse datasets and tasks to validate the generalizability of the findings, as the current set of experiments is somewhat limited.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- How does GLEN handle the integration of global and local perspectives, and what are the specific advantages of this approach over other methods?
- Can the authors clarify the definition and implementation of the global and local perspectives, and how these are integrated in practice?
- How sensitive is GLEN to the choice of hyper-parameters, and what are the recommended settings for different types of datasets?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect the applicability and generalizability of the findings?
- How does GLEN compare to other methods in terms of handling noisy or irrelevant edges, and what are the implications of this for the accuracy and robustness of the predictions?
- Could the authors provide more detailed justifications for the experimental setup and the choice of baselines, particularly in terms of fairness and comparability?
