 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for data poisoning that enables an attacker to manipulate any class in a model by poisoning only a small fraction of the training data. This method, termed "universal backdoor attacks," leverages inter-class poison transferability to create a single set of triggers applicable across multiple classes, thus reducing the number of poisoned samples required. The paper evaluates this method on various datasets and models, including ImageNet, and demonstrates its effectiveness against existing defenses. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the attack in real-world scenarios, the robustness of the results, and the clarity of the presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for data poisoning that enables manipulation of any class with a small increase in poison samples, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method is evaluated on various datasets and models, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness against a comprehensive set of defenses.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue in the field of deep learning security, highlighting the potential vulnerabilities of models trained on large datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, particularly in the definition and explanation of key terms and concepts such as "inter-class poison transferability" and the use of Linear Discriminate Analysis (LDA).
- The practicality of the attack is questionable, as the method requires a surrogate model with similar accuracy to the target model, which might not be feasible in many real-world scenarios.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a few datasets and models, which might not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- The method's reliance on a surrogate model raises concerns about its robustness, especially if the surrogate model is less accurate or different from the target model.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of trigger detectability, which is crucial for the practical application of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition and role of "inter-class poison transferability" in the context of your method?
- How does the paper address the issue of trigger detectability, especially considering the potential for detection by data sanitation defenses?
- Can you provide more details on the practicality of the attack, particularly how the surrogate model's accuracy affects the effectiveness of the attack?
- How does the method perform when the surrogate model is less accurate or different from the target model?
- Could you elaborate on the method's scalability and applicability to different types of models and datasets, particularly those that are not as large or diverse as those used in your experiments?
