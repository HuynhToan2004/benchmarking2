 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PAC-PFL, a novel federated learning (FL) algorithm that utilizes a PAC-Bayesian approach to personalize models for clients with heterogeneous data. It employs a hyper-posterior distribution to personalize the model for each client, ensuring differential privacy and handling the overfitting issue in low-data scenarios. The algorithm is designed to minimize the generalization bound on the average true risk of clients, thus improving the accuracy and calibration of predictions. Theoretical analysis and empirical evaluations on datasets like photovoltaic panel power generation and FEMNIST demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental validation on small datasets, lack of comparison with existing methods, and potential issues with differential privacy due to SVGD.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the problem of personalized federated learning (PFL) with a focus on probabilistic models, which is an important and relevant area of research.
- The PAC-PFL algorithm introduced is novel and provides a principled approach to regularizing the hyper-posterior, enhancing flexibility in capturing heterogeneous patterns.
- The theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results are promising, showing that the algorithm can produce accurate and well-calibrated predictions.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The use of a hyper-posterior distribution to personalize the model for each client is a significant contribution, as it allows for better handling of heterogeneous data in federated learning scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks comprehensive experimental validation, particularly on larger, more diverse datasets, which could better demonstrate the generalizability and scalability of the proposed algorithm.
- The comparison with existing methods is insufficient, which makes it difficult to gauge the algorithm's performance relative to state-of-the-art techniques.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed algorithm, which is crucial for practical implementation in real-world scenarios.
- There is a potential issue with differential privacy due to the use of SVGD, which may not fully preserve the privacy of the data.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential negative societal impacts and ethical considerations.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper lacks a detailed comparison of computational costs and training time.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed algorithm perform in scenarios with a large number of clients, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in such settings?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed algorithm, especially in terms of the number of clients and the size of the datasets?
- How does the algorithm handle the privacy concerns, particularly in light of the use of SVGD which may not fully preserve differential privacy?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the hyper-posterior distribution in the federated learning process, and how it contributes to the overall effectiveness of the algorithm?
- How does the algorithm ensure that the hyper-posterior distribution is independent of observations, as required for the theoretical analysis?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of experimental settings, particularly the small dataset sizes used, and discuss the implications of these choices for the generalizability of the results?
