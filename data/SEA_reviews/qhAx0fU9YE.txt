 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the persistence of biases from pre-trained models in transfer learning settings, specifically focusing on how biases from the source dataset can influence the performance on the target dataset. The authors demonstrate this through experiments involving backdoor attacks and naturally occurring biases, showing that these biases can transfer even when the target dataset is debiased. They also explore the impact of full-network fine-tuning and weight decay on mitigating these biases. The paper is well-written and provides a comprehensive literature review, although it is criticized for its limited scope in terms of the types of biases studied and the lack of detailed experimental setups.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear definitions and examples that enhance understanding.
- The problem of bias transfer from the source dataset to the target dataset is addressed, which is an important issue in transfer learning.
- The paper provides a comprehensive literature review and presents a variety of experiments, including both synthetic and natural datasets, which demonstrate the transfer of biases.
- The paper offers a novel perspective on the transfer of biases in transfer learning, which is not commonly discussed in existing literature.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are convincing, showing that biases can persist even when the target dataset is debiased.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the types of biases studied and how they relate to real-world scenarios.
- The experimental setup and methodology are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to replicate the results or understand the experimental conditions.
- The paper does not provide a clear definition or operationalization of the term "bias," which could lead to confusion about the nature of the biases being studied.
- The paper primarily focuses on image classification tasks and does not explore other types of tasks or datasets, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of quantitative metrics to measure the extent of bias transfer, which could make it difficult to compare or evaluate the results across different datasets or models.
- The paper does not discuss the potential impact of different transfer learning strategies on the transfer of biases, which could be a significant factor in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the biases studied in the paper relate to real-world scenarios and how they might affect the performance of models in practical applications?
- How do the authors operationalize the term "bias" in their experiments, and what specific biases were studied in the experiments described in the paper?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific datasets used, the pre-trained models, and the training procedures?
- How do the authors measure the extent of bias transfer in their experiments, and what metrics were used to quantify this?
- In the experiments involving backdoor attacks, how do the authors ensure that the bias introduced by the attack is not due to the attack itself rather than the transfer of biases from the source dataset?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of different transfer learning strategies on the transfer of biases, and how these strategies might be optimized to mitigate bias transfer?
