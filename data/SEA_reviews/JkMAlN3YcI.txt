 **Summary:**
The paper presents a comprehensive empirical study on the use of unrolling techniques in neural network-augmented physics simulators, comparing different variants of training neural networks on discrete ground truth trajectories. It evaluates three distinct methods: fully differentiable unrolling, unrolling without temporal gradients, and one-step setups. The study benchmarks these methods across various physical systems, network sizes, architectures, and training setups to understand the effects of training distribution shifts and long-term gradients. The paper concludes by providing recommendations for training autoregressive neural simulators via unrolling, aiming to improve inference accuracy. However, the paper has been criticized for its lack of novelty, the limited scope of physical systems, and the absence of a theoretical framework to support the empirical findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a detailed benchmark across various physical systems, network sizes, network architectures, training setups, and test scenarios, which is crucial for understanding the effects of unrolling on neural network-augmented physics simulators.
- The authors have evaluated three variants of training neural networks on discrete ground truth trajectories, which helps in disentangling the effects of training distribution shifts and long-term gradients.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The empirical study is comprehensive, covering a wide range of experiments and providing insights into the behavior of unrolling techniques across different setups.
- The paper is significant as it addresses the practical challenges of training neural networks for physics simulators, providing valuable insights for researchers and practitioners in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as the concept of unrolling training trajectories over time is not new, and the results are not surprising, which might limit its impact.
- The empirical study is limited to a specific set of physical systems (primarily chaotic and turbulent systems), which may not generalize well to other types of systems or domains.
- The paper does not provide a theoretical framework to support the empirical findings, which could strengthen the paper's claims and provide a deeper understanding of the phenomena observed.
- The paper's scope is limited to the effects of unrolling on neural network-augmented physics simulators, and it does not explore the broader implications or potential applications of these findings.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the study, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the specific challenges encountered when integrating non-differentiable numerical simulators into training setups, even if full differentiability is unavailable?
- How do the findings of this study apply to other types of physical systems or domains beyond those studied?
- What are the potential applications or implications of the findings beyond improving the accuracy of neural network-augmented physics simulators?
- Could the authors provide more details on the physical systems used in the study, particularly those that were not described in the paper?
- Why were the results of the KS system not included in the paper, and how do the results of the other systems compare to those of the KS system?
