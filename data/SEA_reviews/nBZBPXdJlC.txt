 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel model named LTU, which integrates a pre-trained audio encoder with a large language model (LLM) to handle audio question-answering tasks. The model, trained on a new dataset called OpenAQA-5M, demonstrates strong performance in both closed-ended and open-ended audio tasks. The authors have conducted extensive experiments, including human evaluations, to validate the model's effectiveness. The paper also introduces a new curriculum learning strategy to train the model, which is highlighted for its innovative approach. Despite some concerns about the model's generalization and the need for more robust benchmarks, the paper is well-received for its technical contributions and potential impact on the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel model that combines a pre-trained audio encoder with a large language model (LLM) to handle audio question-answering tasks, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The model demonstrates strong performance on both closed-ended and open-ended audio tasks, showcasing its versatility and effectiveness.
- The introduction of the OpenAQA-5M dataset, which is the largest and most diverse audio question-answering dataset to date, is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides extensive experiments, including human evaluations, which add credibility to the model's performance claims.
- The proposed curriculum learning strategy is innovative and could be of interest to the community, demonstrating a thoughtful approach to training the model.
- The model's ability to handle open-ended questions and its potential to reduce hallucination by learning from unanswerable questions generated by GPT-3.5 is a notable feature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing models, particularly in terms of the model's architecture and its performance on various audio tasks.
- There is a need for more rigorous benchmarking to validate the model's performance claims, especially in scenarios where it may not perform as well as expected.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalization of the model to different types of audio data or its ability to handle noisy or low-quality audio recordings.
- The model's performance on open-ended questions is not consistently high, and the paper does not provide sufficient analysis to explain these discrepancies.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the model, particularly in terms of its applicability to real-world scenarios and its potential impact on society.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the model's architecture and how it compares to existing models in terms of performance and efficiency?
- How does the model handle noisy or low-quality audio recordings? What are the implications of this for the model's performance in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the model's performance on open-ended questions, particularly in scenarios where it may not perform as well as expected?
- How does the model's performance compare when trained on different subsets of the OpenAQA-5M dataset?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their model, particularly in terms of privacy concerns and the model's ability to understand and respond appropriately to sensitive or offensive content?
