 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new benchmark, I-PHYRE, designed to evaluate the capabilities of agents in interactive physical reasoning, particularly focusing on intuitive physical reasoning, multi-step planning, and in-situ interventions. The benchmark involves four game splits that challenge agents' learning and generalization of essential principles of interactive physical reasoning. The experiments involve various planning strategies and agents, including supervised and reinforcement learning, and demonstrate a significant gap between human performance and that of current learning algorithms. The benchmark is designed to address the limitations of existing evaluation protocols by allowing agents to interact with dynamic events in real time, thereby bridging the gap between human-like reasoning and machine capabilities.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new benchmark, I-PHYRE, which is the first to evaluate agents' interactive physical reasoning, focusing on intuitive physical reasoning, multi-step planning, and in-situ interventions.
- The benchmark is well-designed, with clear goals and a variety of games that challenge agents' learning and generalization of essential principles of interactive physical reasoning.
- The paper includes a human baseline for comparison, which is crucial for understanding the performance of current learning algorithms in relation to human capabilities.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise language, and the experiments are well-designed and executed, providing a thorough evaluation of various planning strategies and agents.
- The introduction of the I-PHYRE benchmark fills a gap in evaluating agents' abilities to interact with dynamic events, which is a significant advancement in the field of physical reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The benchmark is currently limited to 2D environments, which might not fully capture the complexity and challenges of real-world 3D environments.
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the performance of the agents on the benchmark, particularly why certain agents perform better or worse than others.
- The generalization ability of the agents is not thoroughly evaluated, with most experiments conducted on the basic split and then generalized to other splits without further fine-tuning.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the limitations of the current approach, which could help in identifying potential areas for future research.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the benchmark could be extended to include more complex physical phenomena or how it could be adapted for use in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- How could the benchmark be extended to include more complex physical phenomena, such as fluid dynamics or soft-body dynamics?
- Could the benchmark be adapted for use in real-world applications, such as robotics or virtual environments?
- How does the benchmark compare to other existing benchmarks in terms of complexity and realism?
- What are the specific challenges that agents face in the I-PHYRE benchmark, and how do these challenges differ from those in other benchmarks?
- Could the authors provide more detailed analysis on the performance of the agents on the benchmark, particularly why certain agents perform better or worse than others?
- How does the benchmark handle scenarios where agents need to reason about physical phenomena beyond Newtonian mechanics, such as electromagnetism or thermodynamics?
