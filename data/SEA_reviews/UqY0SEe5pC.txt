 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the theoretical foundations of score-based diffusion models using a two-layer neural network, focusing on the score matching and denoising score matching objectives. It establishes the global optimality of the score matching objective through a convex program, demonstrating that the diffusion model converges to a Gaussian or Gaussian-Laplace distribution for univariate data. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for high-dimensional data and provides convergence guarantees for the Langevin sampling algorithm. Despite its theoretical depth, the paper is criticized for its limited practical applicability due to its focus on univariate data and its lack of novelty in extending existing convex formulations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and proofs, making the content accessible to readers.
- It provides a novel theoretical analysis of the score matching objective in diffusion models, which is significant given the importance of these models in the field.
- The paper demonstrates the global optimality of the score matching objective through a convex program, which is a notable contribution.
- The authors establish convergence results for the Langevin sampling algorithm, which are crucial for understanding the behavior of diffusion models.
- The paper is original in its approach, presenting a novel framework for analyzing diffusion models using convex optimization, which is a significant advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contributions are somewhat limited by the restricted focus on univariate data and the lack of extension to more complex network architectures like CNNs.
- The paper's relevance to the ICLR community is questionable, as the theoretical results are not particularly novel and the practical applications are not clearly demonstrated.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper's results are not generalizable to high-dimensional data, which is a significant limitation given the prevalence of high-dimensional data in practical applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its results with existing literature, which could help in establishing the novelty and significance of the contributions.
