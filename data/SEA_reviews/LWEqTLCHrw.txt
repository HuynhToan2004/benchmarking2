 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel anomaly detection method for federated learning systems aimed at detecting and mitigating malicious client submissions. It utilizes a two-stage process: first, a cross-round check compares local models to reference models from previous rounds to identify potential attacks. Second, a cross-client detection phase employs the three sigma rule to identify anomalous clients. This method is further enhanced by a zero-knowledge proof mechanism to ensure the honest execution of defense mechanisms. The paper also includes a comprehensive experimental evaluation across various datasets and models, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method against different types of attacks. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, the lack of theoretical guarantees, and the limited scope of experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear introduction and a logical structure that effectively presents the problem and the proposed solution.
- The proposed method is innovative, combining the use of reference models and the three sigma rule to detect and remove anomalous client submissions, which is not commonly seen in previous works.
- The paper includes a comprehensive experimental evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and models, including CNN, RNN, and ResNet56.
- The paper addresses the critical issue of malicious client submissions in federated learning systems, which is a significant and timely topic.
- The use of zero-knowledge proofs adds an additional layer of security and trust to the system, enhancing the overall robustness of the defense mechanisms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the rationale behind the choice of the three sigma rule and how it is applied in the context of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not provide theoretical guarantees or proofs to support the effectiveness of the proposed method, which is a significant gap in the validation of the proposed approach.
- The experimental evaluation is limited in scope, focusing on a small number of clients and datasets, which may not adequately represent real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational overhead and the impact of the proposed method on the overall training process, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing state-of-the-art defense mechanisms, which is essential for establishing the superiority or at least the competitiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the choice of the three sigma rule and how it is applied in the context of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method ensure that the detection of anomalous clients does not lead to false positives, potentially removing benign clients from the system?
- Could the authors elaborate on the computational overhead introduced by the proposed method and discuss its impact on the overall training process?
- In the context of the experimental evaluation, how do the results compare when the number of clients and datasets is increased?
- How does the proposed method perform against different types of attacks, such as gradient attacks and backdoor attacks?
- Could the authors provide a theoretical analysis or proof to support the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- The paper mentions the use of zero-knowledge proofs; could the authors provide more details on how these proofs are integrated into the system and what specific benefits they provide?
