 **Summary:**
The paper explores the fusion of outputs from multiple expert models, each specialized in a specific domain, to enhance performance on out-of-distribution (OOD) test data. The approach involves training a fuser model that combines the outputs of these experts, either through a supervised learning method or a graph shortest path problem. The method is evaluated across various tasks including image and text classification, summarization, and multiple-choice QA, showing improvements over individual experts and other baselines. The paper also introduces a frugal variant that selectively queries experts to reduce computational costs. Despite its innovative approach and potential utility, the paper faces criticisms for its lack of novelty and insufficient experimental validation, particularly in terms of the generalization of the method to unseen domains and the comparison with other state-of-the-art models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The methodology is simple and intuitive, making it straightforward to implement and understand.
- The proposed approach demonstrates significant improvements over individual expert models and other baselines, showcasing its effectiveness.
- The experiments are extensive, covering various tasks and demonstrating the method's versatility and applicability.
- The paper provides a clear and concise description of the method, which aids in understanding and replication of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art models, which could help establish the method's position within the current research landscape.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalization of the method to unseen domains, which is crucial for practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to other methods, particularly in terms of expert selection and the performance of the fuser model.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational costs and scalability of the method, which could limit its practical implementation in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the method performs in scenarios with a large number of experts or in domains with significant domain shifts.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method performs in scenarios with a large number of experts, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and expert selection accuracy?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the domain shift is significant, and what are the implications for the performance of the fuser model?
- Could the authors elaborate on the computational costs and scalability of the method, particularly in comparison to other ensemble methods?
- In the text generation evaluation task, how does the method perform when using expert outputs as features for the fuser model, and what are the implications for the performance of the fuser model in such scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art models in terms of performance and computational efficiency, particularly in the context of the MMLU task?
