 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the class imbalance issue in graph learning, focusing on the root causes of bias in class imbalanced graphs. It introduces two phenomena: Ambivalent Message Passing (AMP) and Distant Message Passing (DMP), which significantly affect the predictive bias in class imbalanced graphs. The authors propose a novel method, ToBE, to mitigate these biases by dynamically augmenting the topology of critical nodes. Theoretical insights are provided, supported by extensive experimental results, demonstrating the effectiveness of ToBE in improving classification performance and reducing predictive bias. The paper is well-structured, with clear theoretical explanations and practical demonstrations, and includes comprehensive experimental validations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and practical problem of class imbalance in graph learning, providing a novel perspective on the root causes of class imbalance bias through the lens of graph topology.
- The proposed method is model-agnostic and can be easily integrated with existing class rebalancing techniques, enhancing its versatility and applicability.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with comprehensive experimental validations that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving classification performance and reducing predictive bias.
- Theoretical insights are provided to support the findings, which are crucial for understanding the underlying mechanisms of class imbalance in graph learning.
- The paper includes a comprehensive experimental section that validates the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets, demonstrating its robustness and scalability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on related works, particularly those that address similar issues in graph learning, to position its contributions more clearly within the existing literature.
- The theoretical analysis and proofs are limited to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), which may not fully capture the complexity of real-world graphs. A more general theoretical analysis could strengthen the paper's claims.
- The paper's methodology is somewhat limited to node classification tasks and does not explore its applicability to other graph learning tasks such as link prediction or graph classification.
- The paper's experimental results, while robust, are limited to specific datasets, and it would be beneficial to see how the proposed method performs on a more diverse set of datasets to validate its generalizability.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and implications of the AMP coefficient, especially in relation to the definition provided in the paper?
- How does the proposed method perform on datasets with natural imbalance, where the class distribution is not artificially manipulated?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in relation to the number of classes and the size of the graph?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially in large-scale graph learning scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the proposed method to other graph learning tasks, such as link prediction or graph classification?
