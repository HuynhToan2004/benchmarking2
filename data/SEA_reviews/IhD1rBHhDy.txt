 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the CheF dataset, a collection of 100,000 molecules and their associated functional labels derived from patent descriptions. The dataset is created using a large language model (LLM) to extract functional labels from patent text, which are then clustered and summarized to reduce the number of labels. The dataset is evaluated using various machine learning models to predict functional labels from molecular fingerprints, demonstrating its utility for drug discovery. The paper also includes a visualization of the dataset using t-SNE plots and a discussion on the alignment of text-based functional relationships with structural relationships. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited technical contribution, lack of detailed methodology, and potential issues with the dataset's quality and novelty.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel dataset, CheF, which is a significant contribution to the field, providing a valuable resource for drug discovery.
- The dataset is evaluated using several machine learning models, demonstrating its utility and potential for further research.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The use of large language models (LLMs) to extract functional labels from patent descriptions is a novel approach that could potentially enhance drug discovery.
- The paper includes interesting visualizations and discussions on the alignment of text-based functional relationships with structural relationships, which could be beneficial for understanding the dataset's utility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed methodology, particularly in the extraction and clustering of functional labels, which could benefit from more detailed explanations.
- The dataset's quality and novelty are questioned, as it primarily extracts labels from patent descriptions without ensuring their accuracy or relevance to the molecules described.
- The paper's evaluation is limited, focusing primarily on a logistic regression model and not exploring other potential models or methods for evaluating the dataset's utility.
- There is a lack of comparison with other datasets or benchmarks, which could help contextualize the CheF dataset's contributions and limitations.
- The paper's technical contribution is limited, as it primarily uses existing tools and methods without significant innovation or advancement.
- The dataset's utility and the effectiveness of the LLM in extracting functional labels are not thoroughly validated, which could undermine the paper's claims about the dataset's utility.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed information on how the functional labels are extracted and clustered, including the specific algorithms and parameters used?
- How do the authors ensure the accuracy and relevance of the functional labels extracted from patent descriptions, especially given the potential for inaccuracies or biases in the patent data?
- Could the authors explore the use of different machine learning models or methods to evaluate the dataset's utility, and compare the results to other datasets or benchmarks to better contextualize the CheF dataset's contributions?
- How does the CheF dataset compare to other existing datasets in terms of its utility for drug discovery and its potential to advance the field?
- Could the authors provide more details on the LLM's performance in extracting functional labels, including metrics such as accuracy, precision, and recall?
- How does the dataset handle the potential for false functional relationships arising from prophetic claims in patents, and what measures are in place to mitigate these issues?
