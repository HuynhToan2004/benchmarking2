 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CODAE, a novel encoder-decoder architecture designed to learn independent factors of variation, specifically focusing on translational and rotational equivariance and invariance. CODAE integrates a dual-branch network, one for learning equivariant features and the other for invariant features, leveraging image moments to capture centroids and orientations. The model is evaluated against five state-of-the-art baseline models across multiple datasets, demonstrating superior disentangling and reconstruction performance, along with enhanced training and inference efficiency. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its incremental nature, lack of clarity in certain sections, and insufficient discussion on the limitations of its methodology.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and introduces an innovative approach to learning independent factors of variation, specifically focusing on translational and rotational equivariance and invariance.
- The proposed CODAE model is evaluated across a range of datasets, demonstrating superior disentangling and reconstruction performance compared to existing methods.
- The paper provides extensive experimental results, which are supported by a clear and detailed presentation, and includes a thorough evaluation of the model's performance against multiple baselines.
- The use of image moments to capture centroids and orientations is a novel and effective approach, and the method's ability to achieve disentanglement is well-demonstrated.
- The paper is the first to introduce the concept of image moments into the field of learning centroids and orientations of objects and shapes in images, offering a new perspective on feature learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the generalization of the method to more complex scenarios and its applicability to real-world datasets.
- There is a notable absence of experimental results on real-world datasets, which could have strengthened the paper's claims by demonstrating the method's effectiveness in practical scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of disentanglement in the latent space, which is crucial for the method's claimed contributions.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive review of related work, particularly in the context of disentangled representation learning and the use of image moments in image processing.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, especially in the introduction and related work sections.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational efficiency of the proposed method compared to existing methods, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the image moments in the method's performance, particularly in terms of their influence on the model's ability to capture and represent centroids and orientations accurately?
- How does the proposed method ensure that the latent space is effectively disentangled, and what are the implications of this disentanglement for the model's performance on downstream tasks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the image moments used and the parameters of the network architecture?
- In terms of the method's applicability, can the authors discuss how the method might be adapted or extended to handle more complex scenarios, such as images with multiple objects or more varied orientations?
- How does the method compare in terms of computational efficiency and training time to existing methods, particularly in scenarios where computational resources are limited?
