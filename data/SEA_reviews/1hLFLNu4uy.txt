 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PORTIA, a novel method designed to mitigate position bias in large language models (LLMs) used for automated evaluation of AI-generated text. PORTIA operates by segmenting answers into smaller parts, aligning similar content across answers, and then merging them back into a single prompt for evaluation. This approach aims to enhance the consistency and reliability of LLM-based evaluators, as demonstrated through extensive experiments across six LLMs and 11,520 answer pairs. The method is evaluated against GPT-4 and other models, showing improved consistency rates and reduced position bias. However, the paper's reliance on proprietary models and its potential for over-reliance on LLMs for evaluation have been noted as significant limitations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-structured presentation of the method and results.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and effective, with extensive experiments demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing position bias in LLM-based evaluators.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the performance of the proposed method across different LLMs and comparison forms, showing that it can achieve consistent results even with less powerful models like GPT-3.5.
- The approach of splitting and merging answers is innovative and well-explained, with a clear discussion on the design choices and the impact of different parameters on the method's performance.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation using both automatic and human evaluations, demonstrating the method's ability to improve the consistency of LLM evaluators and its potential for improving the quality of LLM-generated text.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on related work, particularly in the area of LLM-based evaluation and LLM debating, which are closely related to the proposed method.
- There is a concern about the potential for over-reliance on LLMs for evaluation, as the method heavily relies on LLMs for the evaluation process.
- The method's effectiveness may be limited by the performance of the LLM evaluator, as shown in the results where less powerful models like GPT-3.5 still exhibit significant position bias.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to other types of LLMs or its potential impact on the quality of LLM-generated text.
- The method's performance may be affected by the quality of the LLM's reasoning and the accuracy of its evaluation, which could introduce additional biases or errors.
- The paper does not address how the method could be adapted to handle more complex or nuanced tasks, such as evaluating the quality of LLM-generated text in a more realistic setting.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the performance of the method on more powerful LLMs like GPT-4, and how it compares to other LLMs in terms of consistency and performance?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of LLMs, such as those based on different architectures or trained on different datasets?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential directions for future research to address these limitations?
- How does the method handle cases where the LLM evaluator's reasoning is incorrect or biased, and what steps can be taken to mitigate these issues?
- Can the authors provide more details on the method's computational cost and the impact of different parameters on the method's performance?
- How does the method perform in more realistic settings, such as evaluating the quality of LLM-generated text in a more complex or nuanced task?
