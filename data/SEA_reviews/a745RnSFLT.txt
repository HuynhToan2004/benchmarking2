 **Summary:**
The paper explores the generalization capabilities of prompts in vision-language models, specifically focusing on the zero-shot learning setting. It argues that the discrete nature of prompts and the prior given by a language model enable tight generalization bounds, which are empirically validated across multiple datasets. The authors demonstrate that these bounds are surprisingly close to the actual test error, indicating that prompt engineering can effectively avoid overfitting. The paper also discusses the potential of using these bounds for model selection and the generalization of prompt engineering to various tasks. Despite the innovative approach and the theoretical insights provided, the paper has been critiqued for its limited scope to image classification tasks, lack of a comprehensive discussion on prior works, and insufficient experimental validation on larger, more complex datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of prompt engineering, focusing on the generalization of prompts in vision-language models, which is a significant and under-explored area.
- The proposed generalization bound based on PAC-Bayes is novel and offers a tighter bound than existing methods, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which enhances its readability and comprehensibility.
- The empirical results are strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets, and the theoretical analysis is robust, providing a solid foundation for the findings.
- The paper introduces a new perspective on prompt engineering, highlighting its potential as a generalizable approach that can be applied to various tasks, not just image classification.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on image classification tasks, which limits its applicability to other vision-language tasks such as image captioning or image-text retrieval.
- The discussion on prior works is insufficient, particularly in terms of how the paper differentiates itself from existing literature on generalization bounds in deep learning.
- The paper's experimental validation is limited to smaller datasets like CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, and does not include larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet, which could provide more robust evidence of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper's focus on prompt engineering and its generalization properties might be misleading, as it primarily discusses the generalization of the image encoder rather than the prompt engineering itself.
- The paper's assumptions about the prior knowledge of the data or domain are not clearly articulated, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's claims about the tightness of the bounds and the generalization of prompt engineering are not adequately supported by empirical evidence, particularly in scenarios involving large datasets or complex models.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing works on generalization bounds in deep learning, particularly highlighting the differences and advantages of their approach?
- How does the paper address the potential for data leakage or contamination in the datasets used, especially given the close relationship between the image encoder and the training data?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the image encoder in the generalization properties discussed in the paper, particularly in scenarios where the encoder is trained on the same data as the test set?
- How does the paper's methodology ensure that the generalization bounds are not overly influenced by the specific characteristics of the datasets used, especially given the limited scope of the experimental validation?
- Could the authors provide more evidence or theoretical support for the generalization of prompt engineering to other vision-language tasks beyond image classification?
