 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to neural network training called Unified Likelihood Ratio (ULR) method, which aims to replace the backpropagation algorithm by perturbing the output of each layer to estimate gradients using the likelihood ratio. This method does not require the chain rule and can be applied to various neural network architectures including CNNs, RNNs, and GNNs. The ULR method is shown to perform comparably to backpropagation in terms of accuracy on clean data and can improve robustness to adversarial attacks. The paper also discusses the potential of the ULR method to improve training efficiency and reduce variance by using variance reduction techniques. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, as similar methods have been previously explored, and the paper does not adequately address these prior works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to neural network training, the ULR method, which is an alternative to traditional backpropagation and is shown to be effective across various neural network architectures.
- The method is simple, intuitive, and well-explained, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The paper demonstrates the potential of the ULR method to improve robustness to adversarial attacks, which is a significant advantage over traditional methods.
- The authors have conducted thorough experiments and ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of the ULR method, showing that it can perform comparably to backpropagation on clean data and improve robustness.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-structured presentation of the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the novelty of the ULR method, as similar methods have been previously explored, and the differences between these methods and the ULR method are not clearly articulated.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and training time, which is crucial for assessing the practicality and scalability of the ULR method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the ULR method, particularly how the noise is generated and how it affects the training process.
- There is a lack of clarity in the experimental results, particularly in the presentation of the data, which could make it difficult for readers to interpret the results and draw meaningful conclusions.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the ULR method, particularly in terms of its scalability and applicability to more complex neural network architectures.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the novelty of the ULR method in comparison to similar methods that have been previously explored?
- How does the ULR method compare in terms of computational efficiency and training time to existing methods, particularly in large-scale applications?
- Can you provide more details on the implementation of the ULR method, particularly how the noise is generated and how it affects the training process?
- How does the ULR method perform on more complex neural network architectures, such as transformers, and what are the potential limitations of its application in such settings?
