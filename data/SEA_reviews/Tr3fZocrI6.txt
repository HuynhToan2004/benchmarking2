 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the challenges in linear representation learning across multiple non-iid and non-isotropic tasks, addressing issues such as bias in representation updates and sample complexity. It introduces a new algorithm, De-bias & Feature-Whitened (DFW) Alternating Minimization-Descent, which is shown to converge to optimal representations with noise levels scaling with the total source data size. Theoretical results are supported by numerical simulations, demonstrating the effectiveness of the DFW algorithm in handling non-iid and non-isotropic data. However, the paper's theoretical contributions are questioned due to the lack of novelty and clarity in the presentation of assumptions and proofs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the challenges in linear representation learning under non-iid and non-isotropic data, addressing issues like bias in representation updates and sample complexity.
- It introduces a novel algorithm, De-bias & Feature-Whitened (DFW) Alternating Minimization-Descent, which addresses these challenges and converges to optimal representations with noise levels scaling with the total source data size.
- The paper is well-written and clearly presents the main results and intuition behind the proposed algorithm, making it accessible to readers.
- Numerical simulations are provided to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm, supporting the theoretical results.
- The paper provides a general framework for linear representation learning, which can be applied to various tasks such as linear system identification and imitation learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the assumptions made and the specific contributions of the proposed algorithm.
- The theoretical contributions, such as Theorem 3.1, are not clearly explained, and the assumptions and proofs are not adequately detailed, which limits the understanding of the theoretical underpinnings.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive numerical experiments to validate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm across different scenarios and data sets.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, particularly in scenarios where the number of tasks and data points per task is large.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in Theorem 3.1 and provide a more detailed explanation of the proof?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and practical performance?
- Could the authors provide more detailed numerical experiments to validate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm across different scenarios and data sets?
- What are the specific contributions of the proposed algorithm compared to existing methods, and how does it address the identified challenges in linear representation learning?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and outline strategies for mitigating these risks?
