 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SASANet, a novel neural network architecture designed to provide Shapley Additive Self-Attribution (SASA) for neural networks. SASANet is a Shapley ASA model that integrates a marginal contribution-based sequential module, a Shapley Value module, and a positional Shapley Value distillation module to ensure permutation-invariance and convergence to Shapley value constraints. The network is trained using a set-based modeling approach, which allows it to handle variable-size inputs. The paper provides theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence supporting the convergence of SASANet to Shapley values. Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the model's performance, interpretability, and efficiency, showing that it outperforms other self-attributing models and is competitive with black-box models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear definitions and notations.
- The introduction of a new self-attribution model, SASANet, which is theoretically proven to provide additive self-attribution converging to the Shapley value of its own output.
- The model's innovative use of Shapley Value for self-attribution is a significant contribution to the field of explainable AI.
- The paper provides extensive theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, including experiments that show SASANet's performance, interpretability, and efficiency.
- The model's ability to handle variable-size inputs and its theoretical guarantees for the self-attribution value equal to the output's Shapley values are notable strengths.
- The experimental results indicate that SASANet surpasses existing self-attributing models in performance and rivals black-box models, making it a valuable tool for understanding model predictions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the use of Shapley Value for self-attribution, and the benefits of using Shapley Value over other methods are not convincingly argued.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable as it appears to be a combination of existing methods like SENN and SHAPNet.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to small datasets and does not compare the model's performance on larger, more complex datasets such as MNIST, CIFAR-10, or ImageNet.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could help in understanding the model's relative performance.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the motivation behind using Shapley Value for self-attribution and how it enhances the model's interpretability compared to other methods?
- How does the model handle missing values in the input data, and what are the implications of this for the model's performance and interpretability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the input data?
- How does the model handle different types of input data, such as continuous and categorical variables, and what are the implications of this for the model's performance and interpretability?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and provide examples or scenarios where the model might not be applicable or might not provide accurate interpretations?
- How does the model compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance, interpretability, and computational efficiency?
