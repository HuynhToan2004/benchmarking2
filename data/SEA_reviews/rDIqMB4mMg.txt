 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new benchmark, PostRainBench, designed for post-processing precipitation forecasts from Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) models using deep learning techniques. The benchmark includes three datasets from South Korea, Germany, and China, which are used to evaluate the performance of the proposed model, CAMT, against traditional NWP models and other deep learning methods. CAMT employs a channel attention mechanism and a multi-task learning approach to improve the forecasting of precipitation, particularly focusing on heavy rain conditions. The model's performance is evaluated using metrics like Critical Success Index (CSI) and Heidke Skill Score (HSS), demonstrating improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method and the lack of comparison with more recent deep learning models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmark, PostRainBench, which is a significant contribution to the field of precipitation forecasting.
- The proposed method, CAMT, is well-motivated and supported by a comprehensive ablation study, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving precipitation forecasting.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and the experimental results.
- The introduction of a new dataset, particularly the China dataset, adds value to the research by providing a more diverse set of data for comparison.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the datasets, highlighting the challenges of imbalanced precipitation data and the importance of variable selection.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited, as the use of channel attention and multi-task learning is not new in the field.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with more recent deep learning models, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The performance of the proposed method, particularly CAMT, is not consistently superior across all datasets and metrics, raising questions about its generalizability.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities.
- The paper does not adequately address the societal impact of the research, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of the work.
- The use of the term "post-processing" might be misleading, as it suggests a final step in the forecasting process, while in reality, it is an intermediate step.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the selection of variables for the China dataset, particularly how they were chosen and why?
- How does the proposed method compare to more recent deep learning models, such as those mentioned in recent studies?
- Can the authors elaborate on the discrepancies in performance observed across different datasets and metrics, especially in terms of the generalizability of the method?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of imbalanced precipitation data, especially in terms of class weights and their impact on the model's performance?
- Could the authors clarify the terms and notations used in the paper, particularly those mentioned in the abstract and the methodology sections?
- Is there a plan to make the datasets and code publicly available to facilitate reproducibility and further research in the field?
