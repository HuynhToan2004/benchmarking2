 **Summary:**
The paper investigates continual learning through a dual-domain approach, leveraging Lagrangian duality to manage the stability-plasticity trade-off. It introduces a novel methodology that partitions memory buffers based on task difficulty, as indicated by dual variables, and selects impactful samples to optimize learning. Theoretical analysis and empirical validation demonstrate the method's effectiveness in reducing forgetting and improving performance in various continual learning benchmarks. Despite its theoretical contributions and empirical validations, the paper faces criticism for its complexity, limited novelty, and potential lack of practical applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and presents a clear theoretical analysis of continual learning from a dual domain perspective.
- The proposed methodology is simple and effective, with good empirical performance demonstrated across multiple continual learning benchmarks.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to continual learning by viewing it as a constrained learning problem and using Lagrangian duality to solve it.
- The theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results are convincing, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing baselines in most cases.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical framework for continual learning, including a novel perspective on the stability-plasticity trade-off.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is quite complex, making it difficult for readers to fully grasp the contributions and the implications of the theoretical results.
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as it builds on existing methods and does not introduce significant new techniques or insights.
- The experimental results are not robust, with large variances and sometimes inconsistent performance across different datasets and settings.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of its competitive advantages.
- The computational cost of the proposed method is high, which could limit its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's assumptions and the implications of these assumptions are not clearly explained, which could affect the understanding and the applicability of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly how they affect the practical applicability of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational cost and practical performance?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations and examples to illustrate the theoretical results, particularly how they are applied in practical scenarios?
- How does the paper address the issue of outliers in sample selection, and what are the implications of this for the overall performance of the method?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their approach and how these could be addressed in future research?
