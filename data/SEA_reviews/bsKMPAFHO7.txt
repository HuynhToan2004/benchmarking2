 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for computing linear correlated equilibria (LCE) in extensive-form games, focusing on a new deviation set called untimed communication (UTC) deviations. This set is shown to be equivalent to the set of linear deviations, which are crucial for polynomial time computation of LCE. The paper proposes a no-regret algorithm based on counterfactual regret minimization (CFR) to minimize UTC deviation, demonstrating a significant improvement in computational efficiency over previous methods. The theoretical contributions are supported by empirical results showing the algorithm's superior performance in terms of both theoretical bounds and practical runtime.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the main ideas and results.
- The proposed algorithm is novel and shows significant practical improvements over previous methods, as evidenced by experimental results.
- The paper introduces a new deviation set, UTC deviations, which is equivalent to the set of linear deviations, providing a new perspective on the computation of LCE.
- The theoretical results are strong, particularly the connection between UTC deviations and linear deviations, which is crucial for the application of the CFR algorithm in extensive-form games.
- The paper is original in its approach, providing a new interpretation of linear deviations and a new algorithm for computing linear correlated equilibria, which is both theoretically sound and practically efficient.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the concept of UTC deviations and how they relate to the concept of communication deviations.
- The paper might be seen as a minor extension of previous work by Farina and Pipis (2023), with similar theoretical results and a similar approach to solving the LCE problem.
- There is a lack of discussion on the implications of the results for other notions of equilibria, such as communication equilibrium, which could be a missed opportunity to broaden the application of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the proofs and the technical contributions of the work, particularly in the context of the UTC deviations and their relation to linear deviations.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the proof of Theorem 4.1, specifically the use of the linearity of expectation and the choice of distribution π in the proof?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare to other recent approaches for computing LCE, such as those mentioned in the paper by Anagnostides et al. (2023)?
- Is there a possibility to extend the results of this paper to other notions of equilibria, such as communication equilibrium?
- Could you clarify the definition and role of UTC deviations in the context of the paper, particularly in relation to the concept of communication deviations?
- How does the paper address the computational complexity of the CFR algorithm when applied to the UTC deviations, and what are the implications for the practical applicability of the results?
