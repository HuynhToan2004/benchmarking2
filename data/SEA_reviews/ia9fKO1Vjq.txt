 **Summary:**
The paper discusses the identifiability of latent causal variables in nonlinear polynomial models with noise from the exponential family. It extends the work of Liu et al. (2022) by relaxing assumptions on the noise distribution and the number of environments needed. The authors provide theoretical results for complete and partial identifiability under specific conditions and demonstrate these through experiments on synthetic and real-world data. The paper introduces a method for learning polynomial causal representations and discusses the implications of these results for practical applications. However, the paper is criticized for its presentation, with concerns about the clarity of the writing and the structure of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important problem in causal representation learning.
- The authors provide a novel approach to the problem of identifiability in latent causal variables, extending the work of Liu et al. (2022) to nonlinear polynomial models with noise from the exponential family.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the main results and a detailed discussion on the identifiability of latent causal variables.
- The authors provide a general framework for learning causal representations, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is complemented by extensive experimental results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the main results and the implications of the theoretical findings, particularly in the context of practical applications.
- The presentation of the paper needs significant improvement, with issues such as unclear writing, typos, and inconsistencies in the use of notation.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the requirement that all coefficients change for identifiability, are not well-justified or explored in depth.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions may not hold.
- The experimental setup and the results presented are not convincing, with some figures and results being difficult to interpret or understand.
- There is a lack of discussion on the broader implications of the work, such as its impact on related fields or potential societal applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the main results and the implications of the theoretical findings, particularly in practical applications?
- How do the authors justify the assumptions made in the paper, especially those related to the identifiability of latent causal variables?
- In the experimental results, how do the authors ensure that the assumptions of the model are met, and what are the implications if these assumptions do not hold?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the methodology used in the experiments, particularly in the synthetic data experiments?
- How does the paper's approach compare to other existing methods for learning causal representations, and what are the advantages or disadvantages of the proposed method?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest directions for future research to address these limitations?
