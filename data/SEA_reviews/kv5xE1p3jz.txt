 **Summary:**
The paper introduces JointNet, a novel neural network architecture that models the joint distribution of images and dense modalities, such as depth maps. JointNet extends from a pre-trained text-to-image diffusion model by integrating a copy of the original network for the new dense modality branch, which is densely connected with the RGB branch. The model is trained using the COYO-700M dataset and is tested on various downstream tasks, including joint RGBD generation, depth-conditioned image generation, and coherent tile-based 3D panorama generation. The paper claims that JointNet maintains the strong generalization ability of the pre-trained diffusion model while effectively learning the new modality distribution. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the methodology, and the effectiveness of the model in handling diverse data distributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant challenge of extending text-to-image diffusion models to handle dense modality, which is crucial for various computer vision applications.
- The methodology is straightforward and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the challenges and solutions in extending the model to handle diverse data distributions.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and concise presentation that effectively communicates the challenges and solutions related to extending the model.
- The proposed method achieves comparable or better performance in various downstream tasks compared to existing methods, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper provides extensive experimental results, including visualizations and quantitative evaluations, which support the claims made about the model's performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of model architecture and training strategies. This limits the understanding of the unique contributions of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable as it primarily extends the existing ControlNet method to handle additional modalities, which may not represent a significant advancement.
- The methodology section is unclear and could benefit from more detailed explanations, particularly regarding the training and inference processes, which are critical for understanding the model's operation.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational efficiency of the model, which is a significant concern given the additional computational demands of handling dense modalities.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the model, such as the potential for overfitting or the impact of different data distributions on the model's performance.
- The paper's reliance on a pre-trained diffusion model raises concerns about the generalizability of the model to other data distributions and the potential for catastrophic forgetting during fine-tuning.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method extends the existing ControlNet method to handle additional modalities, and what specific advantages does this extension offer?
- How does the model handle the potential for catastrophic forgetting during fine-tuning, especially given the reliance on a pre-trained diffusion model?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and inference processes, including the training data, the training loss function, and the inference pipeline?
- How does the model perform when trained on different data distributions, and what are the potential impacts on the model's performance and generalizability?
- Can the authors discuss the potential for using the model in other modalities beyond RGBD, such as RGB-M, and how this might affect the model's performance and training requirements?
- How does the model handle the potential for overfitting, especially given the limited availability of labeled dense images compared to pure text-image pairs?
