 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the transfer learning problem in reinforcement learning (RL) where multiple tasks have different reward functions but share the same underlying transition dynamics. It proposes a successor feature (SF) and generalized policy improvement (GPI) framework to address this problem, providing theoretical guarantees for SF learning in the context of deep neural network (DNN) approximation. The authors claim that SF-DQN with GPI outperforms traditional RL methods such as DQN, supported by numerical experiments on both synthetic and real RL tasks. However, the paper is criticized for its limited empirical evaluation, lack of comparison with other recent RL algorithms, and unclear presentation of theoretical results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear problem formulation and theoretical results.
- The proposed method is innovative and interesting, focusing on the transfer learning problem in RL where multiple tasks have different reward functions but share the same underlying transition dynamics.
- The convergence analysis of SF-DQN to the optimal Q-function with generalization guarantees is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper introduces a new theoretical analysis for successor feature learning with deep neural network approximation, which is a first in the literature.
- The theoretical results are well-presented and supported by numerical experiments, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other recent RL algorithms, such as PPO, A2C, and TRPO, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The empirical evaluation is limited, with insufficient experimental details and a lack of comparison with other methods in the same environment.
- The theoretical results are not well-presented, with some key theoretical results missing from the main paper and relegated to the appendix.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of RL problems and its performance under varying conditions.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions and their implications on the theoretical results, particularly how they affect the convergence and generalization guarantees?
- How does the proposed method perform when the reward function is not differentiable, and what are the implications for the theoretical analysis?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the number of runs and the statistical significance of the results?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how might these be mitigated or addressed?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art RL algorithms in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
