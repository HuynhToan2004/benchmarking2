 **Summary:**
The paper explores the uncertainty in deepfake detection by evaluating the performance of various deepfake detectors against different generative models. It introduces uncertainty maps to visualize the relationship between generative artifacts and detector responses, and conducts extensive experiments to compare the uncertainty of detectors across different datasets and models. The study also includes an analysis of the robustness of detectors against adversarial attacks and their generalization capabilities. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the methodology, and the depth of the experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue of deepfake detection, focusing on the uncertainty aspect which is often overlooked.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the experiments, making it accessible and understandable.
- The use of uncertainty maps is innovative and provides a new perspective on understanding the relationship between generative artifacts and detector responses.
- The experiments are extensive, covering various deepfake detectors, generative models, and datasets, which helps in understanding the performance and behavior of different models.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and logical flow, which aids in comprehension and follow-up of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for why uncertainty is important in deepfake detection, and the relevance of the uncertainty analysis to practical applications is not convincingly demonstrated.
- The methodology used in the experiments is not sufficiently detailed, with some crucial aspects such as the training procedure and the specifics of the adversarial attacks not adequately explained.
- The paper does not provide a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could hinder the understanding of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The novelty of the approach is limited, as the use of Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) and Monte-Carlo Dropout for uncertainty estimation is well-established in the literature.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the findings, as the experiments are limited to specific datasets and models, which may not reflect the performance on more diverse or realistic scenarios.
- The paper suffers from some formatting issues, such as unclear references and inconsistent citation styles, which detract from its professional presentation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific adversarial attack used in the experiments and how it affects the performance of the BNN-based detectors?
- How does the uncertainty map correlate with the performance of the detectors, and can the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to illustrate this relationship?
- What is the motivation behind the choice of using uncertainty maps in deepfake detection, and how does it contribute to the overall understanding or improvement of the detection process?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training procedure for the BNN-based detectors, including the training data and the specifics of the Bayesian inference process?
- How does the paper address the potential biases in the deepfake datasets used, and what steps were taken to ensure the fairness and reliability of the results?
- Given the limitations in the generalizability of the findings, how do the authors plan to extend their research to more diverse and realistic scenarios?
