 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the task of procedure planning in instructional videos, focusing on the role of state changes in enhancing the learning process. The authors propose a novel method that represents each step as a state change and tracks these changes through a combination of LLM-generated state descriptions and cross-modal contrastive learning. The method aims to establish a structured state space by aligning visual observations with language descriptions, which is crucial for effective state representation and action prediction. The paper demonstrates its effectiveness through extensive experiments on various benchmark datasets, showcasing improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper's structure, the fairness of comparisons, and the need for more robust ablation studies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by representing each step as a state change, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that focus solely on action information.
- The proposed method aligns visual observations with language descriptions using cross-modal contrastive learning, which is a novel application in the context of instructional videos.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of LLMs to generate state descriptions and the integration of these descriptions with visual observations provide a more comprehensive and structured approach to procedure planning.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on multiple benchmark datasets, demonstrating its practical utility and potential for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in its structure, particularly in the description of the mid-state prediction and step prediction components. More detailed explanations and visual aids would enhance understanding.
- There is a need for more robust ablation studies to better understand the contributions of different components of the proposed method, such as the state space learning and mid-state prediction modules.
- The comparison with other methods, such as PDPP, is not entirely fair due to differences in dataset handling and evaluation settings. The paper should either adjust the comparison settings or clearly differentiate the results to avoid misleading conclusions.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential improvements.
- There is a potential issue with the fairness of comparisons due to differences in dataset handling and evaluation settings. The paper should either adjust the comparison settings or clearly differentiate the results to avoid misleading conclusions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the mid-state prediction and step prediction components, possibly with additional visual aids?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the state changes are not clearly defined or where the visual observations are ambiguous?
- Could the authors clarify the comparative analysis with other methods, particularly PDPP, to ensure a fair and accurate evaluation of the proposed method?
- Is there a possibility to include more diverse datasets in future experiments to test the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and suggest areas for future research to address these limitations?
