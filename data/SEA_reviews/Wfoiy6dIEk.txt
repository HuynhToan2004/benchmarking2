 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for anomaly detection in sequence data, focusing on credit card transactions. It utilizes a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) framework to detect anomalies in user transactions and payment history, leveraging both transaction and payment information. The methodology is evaluated using the Credit Card Fraud Detection dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness in detecting anomalies in credit card transactions. Despite its innovative approach, the paper suffers from significant issues in clarity, organization, and experimental validation. The experimental setup and data preprocessing are not adequately detailed, and the paper lacks comparisons with relevant baseline methods, which undermines the credibility of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the financial industry, focusing on the detection of anomalous transactions in credit card transactions.
- The proposed method is novel, utilizing a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) framework to detect anomalies in user transactions and payment history, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-structured, providing a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method and its application to a real-world dataset.
- The use of attention mechanisms in the MIL framework is innovative and potentially effective in identifying key samples that contribute to anomalous behavior in transaction sequences.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and comprehensive description of the proposed method, particularly in the algorithmic descriptions and mathematical formulations, which makes it difficult to fully understand the methodology.
- The experimental setup and validation are not convincingly robust, with insufficient details on data preprocessing, dataset characteristics, and comparisons with relevant baseline methods.
- The paper suffers from significant issues in clarity and organization, with numerous typos and unclear explanations, which detract from its professional quality and credibility.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications in the financial industry where data volumes can be large.
- The paper's claims of novelty and superiority over existing methods are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which are limited to a single dataset and lack comparisons with relevant baseline methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed and clearer description of the proposed method, including the mathematical formulations and algorithmic descriptions?
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets, especially those with different characteristics and sizes?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, including the data preprocessing steps and the dataset characteristics?
- How does the proposed method compare with other baseline methods, particularly in terms of performance metrics such as precision, recall, and F1-score?
- Can the authors discuss the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to large datasets and its computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of imbalanced data, which is a significant challenge in many real-world applications?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations for the choices made in the experimental setup, such as the data sampling and the use of specific datasets?
