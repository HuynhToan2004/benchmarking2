 **Summary:**
The paper explores a novel backdoor attack, termed DIFF2, specifically designed for security-centric diffusion models. DIFF2 manipulates the diffusion process by introducing a malicious diffusion-denoising process that guides inputs with specific triggers towards an adversary-defined distribution, while preserving the normal process for other inputs. This attack is evaluated across various benchmark datasets and diffusion models, demonstrating its effectiveness in diminishing both post-purification and certified accuracy. The paper also discusses potential countermeasures, suggesting promising avenues for future research. However, concerns about the novelty of the attack and its practical implications are raised, particularly in terms of its applicability to state-of-the-art diffusion models and its transferability across different datasets and models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The motivation behind the research is well-articulated, focusing on the potential risks of utilizing pre-trained diffusion models as defensive tools.
- The authors present a novel backdoor attack tailored to security-centric diffusion models, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper evaluates the proposed attack across various benchmark datasets and diffusion models, demonstrating its effectiveness in diminishing both post-purification and certified accuracy.
- The paper explores potential countermeasures, suggesting promising avenues for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the attack is questionable, as the concept of backdoor attacks on diffusion models has been previously explored.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art diffusion models, which could have strengthened the results.
- The trigger pattern used in the study is not adequately justified, and its impact on the effectiveness of the attack is not thoroughly analyzed.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the transferability of the attack across different datasets and models, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The attack's effectiveness in real-world scenarios is not convincingly demonstrated, particularly in terms of its applicability to state-of-the-art diffusion models.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for a complete understanding of its applicability and effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their attack compared to existing works on backdoor attacks on diffusion models?
- How does the proposed attack compare in terms of effectiveness and efficiency with other state-of-the-art backdoor attacks on diffusion models?
- Could the authors provide more detailed justification for the choice of the trigger pattern and its impact on the attack's effectiveness?
- How transferable is the attack across different datasets and models, and what are the implications of this transferability for practical applications?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their proposed method and suggest potential avenues for future research to address these limitations?
