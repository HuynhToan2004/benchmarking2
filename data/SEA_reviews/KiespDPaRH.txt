 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel regularization technique for NeRFs to improve the convergence of dynamic NeRFs by leveraging statistical regularities of rendered frames. It proposes a regularization loss that minimizes the difference between the pixel intensity distributions of two consecutive time steps, approximated using the sliced-Wasserstein distance. This approach aims to enhance the convergence of NeRFs by treating the dynamics of a time-varying scene as a low-frequency change of a probability distribution of the light intensity. The method is computationally efficient, architecture-agnostic, and has been tested across multiple dynamic NeRF datasets, demonstrating improved performance over baseline models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to regularizing the convergence of NeRFs for dynamic scenes, which is a significant and understudied problem.
- The proposed method is architecture-agnostic, meaning it can be applied to any NeRF architecture, enhancing its applicability.
- The use of sliced-Wasserstein distance for regularization is innovative and computationally efficient, which is crucial for real-time applications.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, with a straightforward implementation that can be integrated into any dynamic NeRF architecture.
- The paper includes a theoretical analysis of the convergence rate of the proposed regularization loss, which adds a solid theoretical foundation to the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the intuition behind using sliced-Wasserstein distance as a regularization loss, which could be improved with additional ablation studies.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other regularization methods, such as those using L2 regularization or MMD, to better understand the advantages of the proposed method.
- The paper's assumption of smooth temporal dynamics might not hold for all dynamic scenes, particularly those with abrupt changes or complex motions.
- There is a need for more qualitative results, such as video comparisons, to better demonstrate the practical impact of the proposed method.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could be valuable for understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper's reliance on a fixed camera angle for rendering pixel values might limit its applicability in more complex scenarios where multiple camera angles are involved.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be applied to NeRFs with more than three cameras, and how might this affect the performance?
- What is the impact of the sampling size (n) on the performance of the proposed method, and how does it compare to other methods like MMD or Sinkhorn?
- Is the proposed method applicable to static NeRFs, and if so, how does it compare to existing methods in terms of performance?
- How does the proposed method handle scenes with complex motions or abrupt changes, and what are the implications for the performance in such scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more qualitative results, such as video comparisons, to better demonstrate the practical impact of the proposed method?
- What are the specific advantages of using sliced-Wasserstein distance over other regularization methods, and how does it compare in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness?
