 **Summary:**
The paper explores the representation complexity of reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms by comparing the circuit complexity of the transition function, reward function, and optimal Q-function in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs). The authors introduce a new class of MDPs called Majority MDPs, which are characterized by a low circuit complexity for the transition and reward functions, contrasting the exponential complexity of the optimal Q-function. Theoretical results are supported by empirical studies using MuJoCo environments, demonstrating that the approximation errors for the optimal Q-function are significantly higher than those for the transition and reward functions. This study contributes to understanding the sample complexity of RL algorithms by highlighting the complexity differences between various functions in RL, though the paper's practical implications and the generalizability of its findings are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by studying the representation complexity of reinforcement learning (RL) through the lens of circuit complexity, which is a rigorous and fundamental metric.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis and empirical validation, demonstrating that the optimal Q-function in certain MDPs has higher complexity than the transition and reward functions.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a logical structure that enhances understanding.
- The study of approximation error is highlighted as an important aspect of RL algorithms, which is often overlooked in favor of sample complexity.
- The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by focusing on the complexity of representing the optimal Q-function, which is a crucial aspect of RL algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical results are limited to specific MDPs, which might not generalize well to more complex or realistic MDPs.
- The practical implications of the findings are unclear, particularly how the theoretical results translate into practical improvements in RL algorithms.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the bounded model misspecification error, are not adequately justified, which could limit the applicability of the results.
- The empirical validation is limited to MuJoCo environments, which might not fully represent the complexity of real-world RL applications.
- The paper's focus on the Majority MDPs might not adequately address the complexity of more general MDPs, which could limit the applicability of the results.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in the introduction and related work sections, to better contextualize the study within the existing literature.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the theoretical results could be applied to practical RL algorithms to improve their performance?
- How do the authors justify the assumptions made in their theoretical analysis, particularly the bounded model misspecification error?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of their findings, especially in terms of improving sample complexity in RL algorithms?
- How do the results generalize to more complex or realistic MDPs, and what are the implications for the broader field of RL?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and roles of the functions used in their theoretical analysis, such as the control function and the condition function?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of their study, such as the focus on specific MDPs and the assumptions made in their analysis?
