 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the reliability of simplified representations of Transformer models in predicting out-of-distribution (OOD) behaviors. It demonstrates that while these simplified models can accurately approximate the full model on in-distribution data, they may not capture the model's behavior on OOD data, leading to misleading interpretations. The study employs dimensionality reduction and clustering to simplify the model, and tests the models on various OOD datasets, including Dyck balanced-parenthesis languages and computer code. The findings suggest that these simplified models can underestimate or overestimate the generalization performance of the original models, depending on the data distribution and the type of simplification used.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue in the field of deep learning, focusing on the reliability of simplified representations for understanding model behavior beyond the training set.
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the problem and providing a comprehensive analysis of the effects of simplification on model performance.
- The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating a clear methodology and logical progression, supported by visual aids and detailed explanations.
- The paper presents a novel perspective on the limitations of simplified models in predicting out-of-distribution behaviors, which is a critical aspect of model interpretability and generalization.
- The paper contributes to the understanding of how simplified models can fail to generalize effectively, which is a significant finding with implications for the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on simplified representations, which may not accurately reflect the complexity of real-world datasets, potentially limiting the generalizability of the findings.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, focusing on a specific type of model (Transformer) and a narrow range of tasks (Dyck language modeling), which might not fully represent the complexity and variability of real-world applications.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the implications of the findings, particularly in terms of practical applications and broader societal impacts.
- There is a noticeable absence of comparative analysis with other types of models or datasets, which could have provided a more robust validation of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the simplification methods used, such as PCA, which could introduce biases or inaccuracies in the simplified representations.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors elaborate on how the findings of this study could be applied to other types of models or datasets, and what implications this might have for the broader field of model interpretability?
- How does the simplification process affect the generalization performance of the model, especially in terms of the accuracy of predictions on out-of-distribution data?
- Are there any specific scenarios where the simplified models might be more effective in predicting out-of-distribution behaviors, and if so, what are the conditions that favor this outcome?
- Could the authors discuss the implications of their findings for the design of more effective model simplification techniques, and how these might address the limitations identified in the study?
- How does the paper's approach to simplification compare to other methods, such as those that use feature extraction or other dimensionality reduction techniques?
- Are there any potential applications or practical uses for the insights gained from the simplification process, particularly in terms of improving model interpretability or generalization?
