 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for 3D molecule-text interpretation, termed 3D-MoLM, which integrates a 3D molecular encoder with a language model (LM) through a 3D molecule-text projector. This integration facilitates the alignment of 3D molecular structures with textual concepts, enhancing the LM's ability to understand and interpret molecular structures. The framework is supported by a dataset, 3D-MoIT, which is used to fine-tune the model and evaluate its performance in various tasks such as molecule-text retrieval, molecule captioning, and open-text molecular QA. The experiments demonstrate that 3D-MoLM outperforms existing baselines, highlighting its effectiveness in interpreting 3D molecular structures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, 3D-MoLM, for 3D molecule-text interpretation, which effectively integrates a 3D molecular encoder with a language model (LM) using a 3D molecule-text projector.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with a comprehensive literature review and a detailed explanation of the methodology.
- The proposed dataset, 3D-MoIT, is well-designed and extensive, providing a valuable resource for future research in the field.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through extensive experiments, showing that 3D-MoLM outperforms existing baselines in various tasks.
- The integration of a 3D molecular encoder into an LM is a significant advancement in the field, enabling the LM to perceive and analyze 3D molecular structures.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the choice of the 3D molecular encoder and the 3D molecule-text projector, which could benefit from further discussion and justification.
- There is a lack of clarity on the training details, such as the specifics of the training data and the hyperparameters used.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially concerning the size of the dataset and the computational resources required.
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as similar approaches have been explored in prior works, and the main contributions seem to be the integration of existing models rather than significant innovations.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process, such as the specifics of the training data and the hyperparameters used, which could affect the reproducibility and understanding of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for data leakage and the generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the motivation behind the choice of the 3D molecular encoder and the 3D molecule-text projector, and how they contribute to the overall effectiveness of the model?
- How does the model handle different 3D conformations of the same molecule, and what implications does this have for the model's performance and interpretability?
- What are the specifics of the training data used in each stage of the training process, and how were the hyperparameters selected?
- How does the model handle different types of molecular structures, such as those with high rotational symmetry or those that are highly flexible?
- Could you provide more details on the training process, including the size and characteristics of the training data, and the computational resources required?
- How does the model ensure that the results are not biased towards specific molecular structures or properties?
