 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of diffusion models in contextual bandits, specifically focusing on the use of linear diffusion models to enhance exploration in large action spaces. The authors introduce a Thompson sampling algorithm that leverages these models to estimate action parameters, demonstrating theoretical and empirical improvements over existing methods. Theoretical analysis provides regret bounds and discusses the computational and statistical efficiency of the proposed method. The paper also discusses the limitations and potential extensions, including the use of non-linear diffusion models and more complex bandit models. Despite some concerns regarding the novelty and depth of theoretical contributions, the paper's empirical results and methodological approach are seen as valuable contributions to the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise descriptions of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The idea of using diffusion models in contextual bandits is novel and provides a fresh perspective on handling large action spaces.
- The paper provides theoretical guarantees and empirical validation, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results are convincing, showing improvements over existing methods.
- The paper introduces a novel approach by combining diffusion models with Thompson sampling, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow and understand the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and intuitions for the theoretical results, particularly the regret bounds and the role of the mixing matrices.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it seems to be a straightforward application of existing diffusion models to contextual bandits without significant theoretical advancements.
- The empirical results are limited to synthetic datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method are not adequately addressed, particularly in terms of the number of actions and the dimensionality of the context.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer differentiation from related works.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and significance of the mixing matrices in the theoretical analysis?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and scalability to other existing methods, especially in scenarios with a large number of actions and high-dimensional contexts?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of using diffusion models in contextual bandits, and how are these addressed in the paper?
- Could the authors provide more detailed intuitions or examples to help understand the theoretical results and the implications of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the reward distribution is not linear or Gaussian?
- What are the specific advantages of using diffusion models over other types of models in contextual bandits, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
