 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method named Chain of Images (CoI) aimed at enhancing the reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) by integrating image generation and recognition. This approach, which involves generating images from textual prompts and using them to solve complex problems, is evaluated on various datasets including geometric, chess, topological, and temporal reasoning tasks. The authors propose a symbolic multimodal language model (SyMLLM) to generate images based on textual instructions and use these images as intermediate representations for reasoning. The methodology is validated through experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the CoI approach over traditional text-based reasoning methods. However, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the method and the lack of comparative analysis with other multimodal models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, the Chain of Images (CoI), which integrates image generation and recognition to enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs, offering a fresh perspective on multimodal reasoning.
- The proposed CoI method is evaluated on various datasets including geometric, chess, topological, and temporal reasoning tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and versatility.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, involving multiple models and datasets, which strengthens the validity of the results.
- The idea of using images as intermediate representations in reasoning tasks is innovative and could inspire further research in multimodal reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comparative analysis with other multimodal models, making it difficult to assess the relative performance of the proposed method against existing solutions.
- The dataset used in the experiments is limited in size and diversity, raising concerns about the generalizability of the results to more complex or varied reasoning tasks.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scope and applicability.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, particularly in the geometric reasoning task where the performance of the proposed method is not significantly better than baseline models.
- The method's dependence on specific image generation models and its integration with other LLMs are not thoroughly explored, which could limit its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed CoI method compare to other multimodal models in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art multimodal models to better establish the effectiveness of the CoI approach?
- What are the specific challenges encountered in integrating the CoI method with other LLMs, and how can these be addressed?
- How does the CoI method perform when applied to more complex or varied reasoning tasks beyond those included in the current experiments?
- Can the authors elaborate on the method's limitations and discuss potential future research directions to address these limitations?
