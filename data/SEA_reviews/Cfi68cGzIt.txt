 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel regularization technique for Q-learning algorithms, specifically targeting the Q-value estimation in double Q-learning. The proposed method, referred to as SQT, involves subtracting the standard deviation of Q-value estimates from the target Q-values, aiming to enhance the accuracy and stability of Q-value estimation. This method is tested across various environments including MuJoCo and D4RL, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing the number of training iterations required for convergence. The paper is commended for its clarity and the novelty of its approach, though concerns are raised about the empirical evidence and the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear and concise presentation of the method and its application.
- The proposed method is straightforward, intuitive, and simple, making it easy to implement and understand.
- The paper provides code for reproducibility, enhancing the transparency and reliability of the results.
- The method's application to existing algorithms like TD3, TD7, MaxMin, and REDQ demonstrates its versatility and potential for broader applicability.
- The empirical results show a reduction in the number of training iterations required for convergence, indicating the method's effectiveness in reducing variance and improving stability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed method, particularly in explaining the reduction of variance and the convergence to the optimal policy.
- The empirical evidence provided is limited to specific environments and does not include a comprehensive ablation study, which could strengthen the claims of the paper.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational overhead introduced by the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical implementation.
- The paper's claims about the necessity of reducing Q-value estimation variance and the convergence to the optimal policy are not well-supported by existing literature or rigorous theoretical analysis.
- The paper's structure and presentation could be improved, particularly in the related work section and the organization of the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed method, particularly explaining how it reduces variance and leads to convergence to the optimal policy?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing variance reduction techniques, such as those discussed in [1] and [2]?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of environments for testing and consider including more diverse environments to strengthen the claims of the paper?
- Is there a possibility to automate the tuning of hyperparameters like α to make the method more practical for real-world applications?
- Can the authors elaborate on the computational overhead introduced by the proposed method and discuss potential strategies for mitigating these costs?
- How does the proposed method affect the performance of the baseline algorithms, especially in terms of convergence speed and final performance?
