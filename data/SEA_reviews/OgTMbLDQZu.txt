 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework named LAMDA, designed for unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) in both semantic segmentation and object detection tasks. LAMDA leverages a pre-trained vision-language model (CLIP) to adapt to various unlabeled target domains, even those with distinct vocabularies. It integrates a unified proposal decoder, language-guided masked consistency, and hierarchical visual-language alignment to address the challenges of different vocabularies and task-specific network architectures. The framework's effectiveness is validated through extensive experiments on various segmentation and detection datasets, demonstrating its superiority over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and detailed methodology.
- The proposed method, LAMDA, introduces a unified language-driven multi-task domain adaptation, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The use of a pre-trained vision-language model (CLIP) for domain adaptation is innovative and effective, enabling the transfer of knowledge from source domains to unlabeled target domains.
- The paper presents comprehensive experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of LAMDA in both semantic segmentation and object detection tasks, outperforming existing methods.
- The methodology is well-organized, with clear explanations of each component and their roles in the overall framework.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed ablation studies on each component of the proposed method, particularly the effectiveness of the unified proposal decoder and the hierarchical visual-language alignment.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as similar approaches using CLIP for domain adaptation have been previously explored.
- The paper could benefit from more visual results, such as visualizations of masked target images and segmentation maps, to better illustrate the method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the need for multiple source domains and the potential impact of different pre-trained models on performance.
- There are inconsistencies and unclear explanations in the paper, such as the discrepancy between the number of categories in the source domain and the target domain, and the role of the text encoder in the unified proposal decoder.

**Questions:**
- How does the performance of LAMDA compare when using different pre-trained models, such as DINOv2 or R3M?
- Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the unified proposal decoder and the hierarchical visual-language alignment, including the specific roles of each component?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the target domain has a different number of categories compared to the source domain?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancy between the number of categories in the source and target domains, and explain the role of the text encoder in the unified proposal decoder?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and practicality with other existing methods, particularly in scenarios where the target domain has a distinct vocabulary?
