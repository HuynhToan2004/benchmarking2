 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel probabilistic graph representation learning framework called Graph-Induced Sum-Product Networks (GSPNs), which leverages the computational trees induced by vertices in message-passing neural networks to build hierarchies of sum-product networks. This framework allows for tractable computation of joint, marginal, and conditional distributions, which is crucial for handling missing data in graphs. The authors demonstrate the efficiency and effectiveness of GSPNs through experiments, showcasing their competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art deep graph networks in scenarios with scarce supervision and graph classification. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces challenges in clarity and depth in explaining the computational details and the rationale behind certain design choices.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is novel, providing a new probabilistic framework for graph representation learning that can tractably answer probabilistic queries.
- The paper is well-written, clearly outlining the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental setup.
- The method is efficient, with the complexity being linear in the number of edges, which is a significant advantage over other methods.
- The paper is technically sound, with a clear exposition of the method and its advantages over existing methods.
- The experimental results are promising, showing that the proposed method can outperform state-of-the-art methods in certain scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how the method works, particularly the computational details and the rationale behind certain design choices.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some results showing marginal improvements or no significant difference from existing methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its inability to model edge types and the potential for bias in the pseudo-likelihood.
- The paper's claims regarding the model's ability to capture structural dependencies and multimodality are not substantiated with sufficient evidence.
- The paper's comparison with other methods, such as GMM, is not fair due to the different assumptions made by each method.
- The paper's writing and presentation could be improved to better convey the method's advantages and to avoid confusion.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the method works, including the computational details and the rationale behind certain design choices?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of edges in graphs, and what are the implications of this limitation?
- In the experiments, why was the choice made to use a naive Bayes model as the base SPN, and how does this affect the model's performance compared to other models?
- How does the proposed method compare to other probabilistic graphical models in terms of expressiveness and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the meaning and implications of the "multimodality" term used in the paper, and provide evidence to support the claim that the method can capture this property?
- How does the method handle the bias introduced by the use of the same realization for different observable variables in the tree?
