 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel backdoor attack method, Influencer Backdoor Attack (IBA), designed for semantic segmentation models. The IBA method includes two techniques: Nearest Neighbor Injection (NNI) and Pixel Random Labeling (PRL), aimed at improving the attack's effectiveness by manipulating the contextual information of segmentation models. The authors demonstrate that these techniques can achieve high attack success rates while maintaining performance on benign samples. The paper presents experimental results on various datasets and segmentation models, showing the effectiveness of the proposed methods. However, concerns are raised regarding the novelty of the approach, the generalization of the results, and the practicality of the attack in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed Influencer Backdoor Attack (IBA) method is innovative and addresses a significant gap in the literature by focusing on segmentation-specific backdoor attacks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed techniques, including extensive experiments on multiple datasets and models, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the IBA method.
- The inclusion of real-world experiments and visualizations enhances the credibility of the results and provides practical insights into the application of the IBA method.
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem of backdoor attacks on semantic segmentation models, which is a growing area of concern with the increasing use of such models in critical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed techniques, which is crucial for understanding the practical applicability and potential drawbacks of the IBA method.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned as similar techniques have been previously explored in other works, such as those by Li et al. and Mao et al.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed methods with state-of-the-art backdoor attacks on classification tasks, which could provide a better understanding of the relative performance of the IBA method.
- The paper's evaluation metrics and methodology are not clearly defined or justified, which could lead to confusion about the effectiveness and reliability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the practicality of the attack in real-world scenarios, especially concerning the placement and size of the trigger, which could affect the attack's success rate.
- The paper does not discuss potential defense mechanisms against the IBA method, which is crucial for understanding the robustness of the proposed techniques.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the trigger size and positioning affect the attack's success rate, especially in real-world scenarios?
- How does the IBA method compare to other state-of-the-art backdoor attacks on classification tasks in terms of effectiveness and practicality?
- What are the specific defense mechanisms that the IBA method is most vulnerable to, and how can these be mitigated?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the hyperparameters were chosen and their impact on the results?
- How does the IBA method perform when combined with other existing backdoor attack techniques, and what are the potential advantages or disadvantages of such combinations?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of trigger patterns and their impact on the attack's success rate and generalization across different datasets?
