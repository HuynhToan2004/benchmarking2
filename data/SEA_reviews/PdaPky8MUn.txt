 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the performance of Transformers and State Space Models (SSMs) in modeling long-range dependencies, particularly in the context of the Long Range Arena benchmark. It challenges the notion that Transformers are less effective than SSMs, arguing that the disparity arises from inadequate training and evaluation practices. The authors propose a self-pretraining approach using denoising objectives, which can significantly improve the performance of Transformers and SSMs when initialized from downstream task data. The study also demonstrates that S4, a specific SSM, can achieve competitive performance when initialized using self-pretraining. The paper provides empirical evidence for these claims and suggests that self-pretraining can enhance the evaluation of model architectures by providing a more accurate comparison.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear structure that makes it accessible to readers.
- It challenges the notion that Transformers are less effective than State Space Models (SSMs) in modeling long-range dependencies, providing a new perspective on the role of training and evaluation practices.
- The empirical results are strong, showing that self-pretraining can improve the performance of both Transformers and SSMs significantly, especially when initialized from downstream task data.
- The paper demonstrates that S4, a specific SSM, can achieve competitive performance when initialized using self-pretraining, which challenges the conventional wisdom that SSMs are superior to Transformers.
- The findings suggest that self-pretraining can enhance the evaluation of model architectures by providing a more accurate comparison, which is crucial for understanding the relative strengths and weaknesses of different architectures.
- The paper is the first to report strong results on the PathX-256 task, which is challenging due to its large sequence length, and shows that self-pretraining can improve the performance of models on this task.
- The study provides a comprehensive evaluation of different architectures, including S4, SPADE, MEGA, and Pythia, which helps in understanding the relative performance of these models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on the Long Range Arena benchmark and does not explore the impact of self-pretraining on other tasks or datasets, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the study, such as the potential impact of different pre-training objectives on the results or the scalability of the findings to other tasks or datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the differences between self-pretraining and other forms of pre-training, such as pre-training on large corpora, and how these affect the results.
- The paper could provide more insights into the specifics of the self-pretraining process, such as the choice of pre-training objectives and the impact of different initialization methods on the performance of the models.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational costs associated with self-pretraining, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison of the computational costs of self-pretraining versus other methods, which could help in understanding the practical feasibility of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the computational costs of self-pretraining versus other methods, such as training from scratch or pre-training on large corpora?
- How do the findings of this study generalize to other tasks or datasets beyond the Long Range Arena benchmark?
- What are the specific pre-training objectives used in this study, and how do they compare to other pre-training objectives in terms of their impact on the results?
- Could the authors provide more details on the self-pretraining process, such as the choice of pre-training objectives and the impact of different initialization methods on the performance of the models?
- How does the self-pretraining approach perform in terms of data efficiency, especially in scenarios where data is scarce?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of different initialization methods on the performance of the models, especially in the context of self-pretraining?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the convolution kernels learned via self-pretraining, especially in comparison to HiPPO-based kernels?
- How do the findings of this study relate to other recent works that have also explored the impact of self-pretraining on model performance?
