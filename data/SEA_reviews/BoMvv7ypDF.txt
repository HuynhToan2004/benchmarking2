 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for sampling from general target distributions using a recursive score estimation technique, aiming to improve upon the high gradient complexity of the original Diffusion-based Monte Carlo (DMC) algorithm. The proposed method, named Recursive Score DMC (RS-DMC), divides the diffusion process into multiple segments, allowing for the estimation of the score function using a sequence of interconnected mean estimation and sampling subproblems. This approach is shown to have a quasi-polynomial dependency on the error tolerance, a significant improvement over the exponential dependency in the original DMC algorithm. Theoretical analysis supports the convergence of the RS-DMC algorithm, and the paper provides a detailed proof sketch to illustrate the method's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel recursive score estimation method that significantly improves the gradient complexity of the original Diffusion-based Monte Carlo (DMC) algorithm.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, intuitive, and easy to understand, with a clear presentation that aids in understanding the algorithm's workings.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to support the convergence of the proposed algorithm, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The authors demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed method by showing how it can be used to sample from a wide range of distributions, including those with complex sampling requirements.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks experimental results, which could have strengthened the claims made about the practical applicability and efficiency of the proposed method.
- There is a concern about the practical implementation of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the computational complexity and the need for multiple recursive calls.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing approaches, which could have provided a clearer picture of its advantages and limitations.
- The paper assumes a certain level of familiarity with the Diffusion-based Monte Carlo (DMC) algorithm, which might limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
- There are some minor issues with the clarity and presentation of the paper, such as inconsistent notation and unclear explanations in certain sections.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in practice, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the quality of the samples generated?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and results, including comparisons with existing methods and a discussion of the computational resources required?
- Is there a specific reason why the method is not compared with other existing approaches, such as the ULA or other diffusion-based sampling methods?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions and theoretical underpinnings of the method, particularly in terms of the log-smoothness and bounded second moment assumptions?
- How does the method handle the recursive calls and the estimation of the score function at each step, and what are the implications of these assumptions for the practical application of the method?
