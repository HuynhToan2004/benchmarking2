 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Polynormer, a novel graph transformer (GT) model designed to balance expressivity and scalability. Polynormer employs a polynomial-expressive attention mechanism, integrating both local and global attention to enhance its ability to capture complex interactions between nodes. The model is evaluated on various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency compared to existing GT models. It achieves high polynomial expressivity while maintaining linear complexity, which is crucial for handling large graphs. The paper also discusses the limitations of the model, including its potential over-smoothing and the need for further theoretical analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by combining polynomial functions with graph transformers, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The experimental results show that the proposed model outperforms existing graph transformers, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the model.
- The use of polynomial functions to enhance the expressivity of graph transformers is a novel and interesting approach.
- The model is scalable due to its linear complexity, which is crucial for handling large graphs.
- The paper includes a thorough ablation study that provides insights into the model's components and their contributions to performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing models, especially in terms of polynomial expressivity and complexity.
- The scalability of the model is not adequately demonstrated, particularly in terms of handling very large graphs.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the model in detail, which could have strengthened the discussion section.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential over-smoothing of node representations, which is a known issue with polynomial functions.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing models, especially in heterophilic datasets.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the model, particularly the polynomial expressivity and its implications.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the choice of activation functions and their impact on the model's performance.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison of the proposed model with existing models in terms of polynomial expressivity and complexity?
- How does the model perform on very large graphs, and what are the limitations in scaling up the model?
- Can the authors discuss the potential over-smoothing of node representations in more detail, and how this affects the model's performance?
- How does the choice of activation function impact the model's performance, and could alternative activations be explored?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including standard deviations and statistical significance tests, to strengthen the claims of the model's superiority?
- How does the model handle the issue of over-smoothing, and what measures can be taken to mitigate this effect?
