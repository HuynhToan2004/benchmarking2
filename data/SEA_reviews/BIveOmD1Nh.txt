 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel machine learning approach for molecular docking, leveraging a cross-correlation of scalar fields parameterized by equivariant graph neural networks. This method uses Fast Fourier Transforms to optimize the scoring function, which is claimed to be more efficient than traditional methods. The approach is evaluated against Vina and Gnina, showing competitive results in terms of accuracy and significant improvements in runtime. The paper also explores the use of amortization techniques to further enhance the efficiency of the method. Despite the promising results, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the method to more complex datasets and the clarity of the presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to molecular docking by using cross-correlation of scalar fields parameterized by equivariant graph neural networks, which is a unique contribution to the field.
- The use of Fast Fourier Transforms (FFTs) for rapid evaluation of the scoring function is a significant advantage, especially for large-scale applications.
- The method shows promise in terms of computational efficiency, with significant speedups over existing methods, which could be beneficial for high-throughput virtual screening tasks.
- The paper is well-written and provides a clear and detailed description of the methodology, making it accessible to readers.
- The experimental results demonstrate the method's effectiveness in terms of accuracy and efficiency, which is supported by a thorough evaluation against existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The generalizability of the method to more complex datasets, such as those with larger ligands or more complex protein structures, is not adequately addressed.
- There are concerns about the clarity of the paper, particularly in the explanation of certain equations and methodological details, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a better understanding of the method's relative performance.
- The paper does not address the impact of the method on the interpretability of the scoring function, which is a critical aspect for understanding the model's decision-making process.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the method perform when applied to more complex datasets, such as those with larger ligands or more complex protein structures?
- Could the authors clarify the equations and methodological details that were unclear in the review process?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- Can the authors discuss the potential implications of the method's reliance on Fast Fourier Transforms for computational efficiency, especially in terms of potential limitations or trade-offs?
- How does the method handle the interpretability of the scoring function, and what steps have been taken to ensure that the model's decisions are explainable?
