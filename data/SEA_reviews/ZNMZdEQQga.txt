 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method termed "transplant" for enhancing the performance of neural networks by periodically replacing inactive neurons with those exhibiting higher activity. This approach, inspired by biological neuron transplantation, is evaluated through theoretical analysis and experiments on various datasets, including MNIST and CIFAR-10. The authors propose using variance of output values as a measure of neuron activity, which is then utilized to select neurons for replacement. Despite the innovative concept and some promising experimental results, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental scope, insufficient theoretical justification, and lack of comparison with other methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to neural network training by incorporating biological insights, specifically the concept of neuronal transplantation from neuroscience.
- The proposed method is simple, easy to implement, and can be applied to any neural network architecture, including those with perceptron layers.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to support the proposed method, enhancing the paper's credibility and depth.
- The method is evaluated on several datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR-10, and a mushroom dataset, demonstrating its applicability and potential effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, which is crucial for disseminating complex ideas.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other methods, which is essential for establishing the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, focusing primarily on simple datasets and not sufficiently demonstrating the method's effectiveness on more complex or diverse datasets.
- The theoretical analysis and justification for using variance of output values as a measure of neuron activity are not convincingly argued, raising questions about the method's robustness and applicability.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a critical aspect of ethical considerations in AI research.
- The paper's claims regarding the improvement of neural network performance are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which are sometimes contradictory or insufficiently detailed.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancy between the theoretical analysis and the experimental results, particularly in terms of how the variance of output values affects the performance of the neural network?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including error bars and standard deviations, to better support their claims?
- How does the method perform on more complex datasets, such as those from the ImageNet dataset, and what are the implications of these results for the generalizability of the method?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how do the authors address these concerns?
