 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Proxy Match Transform (PMT) as a computationally efficient alternative to high-order convolution layers, aiming to address the computational challenges in geometric shape assembly tasks. PMT leverages the relationship between multi-head self-attention and convolution to approximate high-order convolutions with sub-quadratic complexity. This approach is demonstrated to outperform state-of-the-art methods on the BreakingBad dataset, showing significant improvements in accuracy and computational efficiency. The methodology is validated through extensive experiments, including ablation studies, and is shown to be effective in both pairwise and multi-part assembly scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The use of multi-head self-attention to approximate high-order convolution is a novel and effective approach.
- The proposed method significantly reduces computational complexity, making it practical for real-world applications.
- The paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is the first to tackle the problem of shape assembly without semantic annotations, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on the BreakingBad dataset, which might not fully validate the method's effectiveness across different datasets.
- The comparison with other baselines, particularly recent works, is not comprehensive, which could limit the understanding of the method's performance relative to the state-of-the-art.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to fully assess the method's effectiveness and limitations.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The method's applicability to other types of 3D shapes, such as point clouds, is not clearly addressed.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when applied to other datasets, and can the authors provide results on other datasets to validate its generalizability?
- Can the authors provide a comparison with other recent works, particularly those that address similar problems, to better position their method within the current research landscape?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of 3D shapes, such as point clouds?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the baseline methods used and the conditions under which the ablation studies were conducted?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the quality of input shapes is low, and what are the implications for the accuracy and reliability of the results?
