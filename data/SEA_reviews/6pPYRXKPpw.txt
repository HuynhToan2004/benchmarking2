 **Summary:**
The paper introduces D3IL, a new benchmark for evaluating imitation learning algorithms using diverse human demonstrations. It focuses on five tasks in Mujoco environments, designed to test the capacity of models to capture and reproduce diverse behaviors. The benchmark includes a variety of sub-tasks and multiple objects, requiring closed-loop feedback, and uses metrics like behavior entropy and conditional behavior entropy to quantify diversity. The paper also presents a comprehensive evaluation of state-of-the-art methods, comparing their performance across different architectures and data types. Despite its strengths in task design and evaluation, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, lack of diversity in task design, and potential issues with the fairness and comprehensiveness of the experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmark, D3IL, which focuses on imitation learning with diverse human demonstrations, addressing a significant gap in the field.
- The benchmark is well-designed, with a variety of sub-tasks and multiple objects, requiring closed-loop feedback, which enhances the evaluation of models' ability to capture and reproduce diverse behaviors.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the evaluation is comprehensive, covering a range of state-of-the-art methods and architectures.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the performance of different methods, including both state-based and image-based policies, and explores the impact of historical information and prediction horizons.
- The benchmark is designed to be challenging, requiring policies that heavily rely on closed-loop feedback, and includes metrics to quantify diversity, which is crucial for evaluating the effectiveness of imitation learning algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as it primarily builds on existing works like Robomimic and T-Shape Pushing, with minor modifications.
- The diversity of tasks is limited, with all tasks being object manipulation in 2D environments, which may not fully represent the complexity and diversity of real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the task design principles, making it difficult to understand the rationale behind the task selection and the criteria for diversity.
- There is a concern about the fairness and comprehensiveness of the experimental results, as the paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other benchmarks like D4RL or Robomimic, and the results are not comprehensive across all tasks and models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed description of the task design principles and a broader range of tasks to better represent the diversity of real-world scenarios.
- The paper's evaluation could be more comprehensive by including more recent and diverse imitation learning algorithms and by providing a more detailed comparison with other benchmarks.

**Questions:**
- How does the paper ensure that the benchmark tasks are representative of real-world scenarios and that the diversity of behaviors captured in the dataset is sufficient for evaluating the models' ability to generalize to new, unseen tasks?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed description of the task design principles and the criteria used for selecting the tasks?
- How does the paper address the issue of fairness and comprehensiveness in the experimental results, especially in comparison with other benchmarks like D4RL or Robomimic?
- Could the authors consider including more recent and diverse imitation learning algorithms in their evaluation to provide a more comprehensive comparison?
- How does the paper ensure that the models trained on the benchmark can generalize to new, unseen tasks, and what steps are taken to mitigate the risk of overfitting to the specific tasks in the benchmark?
