 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Multi-Vision Multi-Prompt (MVMP), a method designed to enhance the performance of few-shot learning in Vision-Language models, specifically CLIP, by employing multiple prompts and self-augmentation strategies. MVMP utilizes a prompt memory bank to store and average prompts from different stages, mixed self-augmentation to increase image diversity, and text distillation to improve alignment between image and text features. The method is evaluated on 11 datasets and shows significant improvements over existing methods, although the novelty and the clarity of the method's advantages over existing techniques are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to few-shot learning by utilizing multiple prompts and self-augmentation, which is both innovative and effective in enhancing the performance of Vision-Language models.
- The method is well-explained and easy to follow, with clear figures and a logical flow that aids in understanding the proposed techniques.
- The experimental results are strong, showing significant improvements over existing methods, and the paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets.
- The use of a prompt memory bank and Gaussian weights for high-layer prompts is an interesting and potentially useful approach to managing the complexity of prompt learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and convincing explanation of why using multiple prompts and self-augmentation specifically benefits few-shot learning. The advantages of these techniques over existing methods are not well-articulated.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as similar approaches have been previously explored, such as the use of multiple prompts and self-augmentation in other works.
- The paper suffers from some unclear writing and lacks detail in certain areas, such as the specifics of the mixed self-augmentation process and the rationale behind certain design choices.
- There are inconsistencies in the results reported, particularly in the performance of the baseline methods and the claimed improvements of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of its work.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the mixed self-augmentation process is implemented and its impact on the performance of the model?
- How does the use of multiple prompts and self-augmentation specifically benefit few-shot learning, and what are the advantages of these techniques over existing methods?
- In the ablation study, why does the performance of the baseline methods vary significantly across different datasets, and how does this affect the interpretation of the results?
- Could the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the Gaussian weights in the prompt memory bank, and how they contribute to the overall performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of training time and computational efficiency with other methods, especially those that use similar techniques like PLOT?
