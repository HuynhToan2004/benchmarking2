 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PromptDiff, a novel prompt-based 3D molecular diffusion model designed for structure-based drug design (SBDD). PromptDiff leverages a pretrained PMINet to select high-affinity ligands from a database and uses these as prompts to guide the diffusion model's generation process. This approach is aimed at improving the binding affinity of generated molecules, which is a critical challenge in SBDD. The paper presents a method that integrates prompting mechanisms such as self-prompting and exemplar prompting to enhance the model's effectiveness. Experiments conducted on the CrossDocked2020 dataset demonstrate the model's ability to generate molecules with higher binding affinity compared to existing methods. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation scope, potential data leakage issues, and lack of clarity in some sections.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel prompt-based 3D molecular diffusion model named PromptDiff, which is a significant advancement in the field of structure-based drug design (SBDD).
- The proposed prompting mechanisms, including exemplar prompting and self prompting, are innovative and well-explained, providing a clear methodological approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive experimental section that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of a pre-trained PMINet to select target-aware ligand prompts is a novel and effective approach that enhances the model's performance.
- The paper addresses an important problem in SBDD by leveraging a small set of target-aware ligand prompts to steer the diffusion model towards generating ligands that satisfy design criteria.
- The inclusion of a retrieval database for molecule retrieval enhances the model's ability to generate molecules with high binding affinity.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a single dataset, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- There is a potential data leakage issue due to the use of the same dataset for both pretraining PMINet and evaluating PromptDiff, which could affect the fairness and validity of the results.
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the description of the diffusion model and the methodology used in the experiments.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the work, which is a critical consideration in scientific research.
- The computational complexity of the model is not discussed, which could be a significant limitation for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the retrieval database is selected and its impact on the model's performance?
- How does the model handle the potential data leakage issue, and can this be mitigated in future work?
- Could the authors clarify the methodology used in the experiments, particularly in the diffusion model and the evaluation process?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the negative societal impacts of the work, and how these have been addressed or mitigated?
- In the context of the diffusion model, can the authors clarify the role of the diffusion process in the generation of molecules, and how it impacts the model's performance?
