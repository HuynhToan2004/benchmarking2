 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method named SePT (Self-influence-based Post-Training) to address bias in machine learning models, specifically focusing on the detection and removal of bias-conflicting samples. This method leverages the self-influence score to filter out such samples, thereby improving the robustness and fairness of the models. The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of SePT in enhancing model performance in both highly and mildly biased scenarios. However, the method's reliance on specific model architectures and its generalizability across different types of biases remain concerns.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets and tasks.
- The proposed method, SePT, is simple, intuitive, and effective, particularly in detecting bias-conflicting samples and enhancing model performance.
- The method is innovative, combining self-influence and loss functions to identify bias-conflicting samples, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with existing methods and demonstrates the effectiveness of SePT in both highly and mildly biased scenarios.
- The experiments are well-designed, with a thorough analysis of the results, and the paper includes a comprehensive set of ablation studies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the method across different types of biases, particularly those that are not related to label-irrelevant attributes.
- The method's reliance on the accuracy of the model's predictions and the quality of the pivotal set raises concerns about its effectiveness in scenarios where these assumptions are not met.
- The method's computational efficiency is not discussed, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with the latest state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a more robust evaluation of its effectiveness.
- The method's effectiveness is not clearly demonstrated in scenarios where the bias is not severe or where the dataset is not highly biased.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and scope.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the method performs when the bias is not severe or when the dataset is not highly biased?
- How does the method handle different types of biases, particularly those that are not related to label-irrelevant attributes?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the method, especially in terms of training time and resource requirements?
- How does the method ensure the accuracy of the model's predictions, and what are the implications if the predictions are not accurate?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and under what conditions is it not effective?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the bias is not known, and how can it be adapted to such settings?
- Could the authors discuss the potential ethical implications of their work, particularly in terms of data privacy and fairness?
