 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework named NeurRev, aimed at addressing the dormant neuron issue in Dynamic Sparse Training (DST) by focusing on pruning rather than growing. This approach is designed to reduce the frequency of updates and enhance the training efficiency, particularly on resource-constrained mobile devices. The framework is evaluated against various state-of-the-art methods and shows promising results in terms of accuracy and efficiency. The experimental results demonstrate that NeurRev outperforms existing methods, reducing the number of dormant neurons and improving overall system performance. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the limited scope of experiments, and the potential for broader applicability beyond mobile devices.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a good explanation of the proposed method.
- The proposed method, NeurRev, is novel and addresses an important issue in Dynamic Sparse Training (DST) by focusing on pruning rather than growing, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The paper includes comprehensive experimental results and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method and providing insights into the impact of different parameters.
- The paper is structured well, with a clear introduction and a logical flow of content, making it accessible to readers with varying backgrounds in the field.
- The methodology is innovative and could potentially have a significant impact on the field of DST by reducing the number of dormant neurons and improving overall system performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader comparison with other activation functions beyond ReLU, which could limit the understanding of the method's effectiveness across different architectures.
- The experiments are limited to specific devices and models, which might not generalize well to other platforms or models.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable as it appears to be a minor modification of existing methods, such as RigL, with similar performance metrics.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the method, such as its impact on different hardware platforms and its scalability to larger, more complex models.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the specifics of the experiments, such as the exact models used and the training parameters, which could affect the reproducibility and applicability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific hardware platform used for the experiments and how the results might vary on different platforms?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of neural networks, such as recurrent networks or attention-based models?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific models used and the training parameters, to enhance the reproducibility and applicability of the results?
- How does the method handle the pruning of negative weights, and what criteria are used to determine which weights are pruned?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other activation functions beyond ReLU, and discuss the implications of using different activation functions on the performance of the model?
