 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to causal inference using a self-attention mechanism, which is theoretically linked to optimal covariate balancing. This method, termed Causal Inference with Attention (CInA), is designed to generalize across multiple datasets and perform zero-shot causal inference on unseen data. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing that it can outperform traditional per-dataset methods. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the method due to its computational complexity and the need for specific data availability, which might limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to causal inference using a self-attention mechanism, which is both theoretically and empirically sound.
- The theoretical connection between self-attention and optimal covariate balancing is clearly established, providing a strong foundation for the proposed method.
- The method is shown to generalize well across multiple datasets and can perform zero-shot causal inference on unseen data, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method, outperforming traditional per-dataset methods in most cases.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The practicality of the method is questionable due to its high computational complexity, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The method requires specific data availability, such as the treatment indicator and potential outcomes, which might not be readily available in many real-world datasets.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability.
- The experimental results do not consistently show superior performance over existing methods, and the improvements are sometimes marginal or not statistically significant.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization, which is crucial for the practical application of the method.
- The method's scalability and efficiency are not thoroughly discussed, which could be a significant concern for its practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the practical implications of the method's high computational complexity and the specific data requirements?
- How does the method perform in real-world scenarios where data availability is limited or where the causal graph is unknown?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- How does the method handle OOD generalization, and what steps have been taken to ensure that it performs well in such scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the method to handle different types of causal effects, such as individual treatment effects?
- How does the method scale with the size of the datasets used, and what are the potential limitations in terms of computational resources and data complexity?
