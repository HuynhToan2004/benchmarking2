 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) based summarization model, termed MoeSumm, which integrates a main expert for general summarization capabilities and deputy experts for domain-specific adaptability. The model leverages a max-margin loss to optimize the separation of these abilities, demonstrating superior performance across 11 datasets compared to recent baselines and large language models. The approach allows for efficient parameter usage and adaptability to out-of-domain tasks. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the MoE approach, the clarity of the dataset descriptions, and the depth of experimental validation, particularly in terms of statistical significance and the robustness of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to summarization by combining a main expert for general summarization abilities with domain-specific deputy experts, which enhances the model's flexibility and adaptability.
- The use of a max-margin loss for separating the abilities of the main expert and deputy experts is innovative and well-executed, contributing to the model's effectiveness in different domains.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides extensive experimental results, including a detailed analysis of the model's performance on various datasets and scenarios.
- The introduction of a dataset-aware gating mechanism to select deputy experts based on input data is a significant contribution, improving the model's adaptability to different contexts.
- The paper demonstrates the model's ability to handle out-of-domain scenarios, which is crucial for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and description of the datasets used, making it difficult for readers to understand the specifics of the experiments and their implications.
- The novelty of the MoE approach is questioned as similar methods have been previously explored, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from existing literature.
- The experimental setup and results are not robust enough, with some results showing marginal improvements over baselines and a lack of statistical significance in the reported improvements.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational overhead and efficiency of the model, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The analysis of the model's performance is somewhat limited, with a need for more detailed insights into the model's behavior and the factors influencing its performance.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant summarization models, which could have provided a clearer picture of the model's advantages and limitations.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and characteristics of the datasets used in the experiments, particularly the "mix dataset" and the "mix dataset scale"?
- How does the model ensure the selection of the correct deputy experts, especially in scenarios where the dataset-aware gating mechanism might not be sufficient?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other summarization models, including recent state-of-the-art models, to better establish the model's position in the field?
- How does the model handle the potential for over-reliance on the main expert, especially in scenarios where the deputy experts might be more specialized for the task at hand?
- Can the authors elaborate on the computational efficiency and resource requirements of the model, especially in comparison to other summarization models?
- How does the model's performance vary with different numbers of deputy experts, and what are the implications of using a different number of experts for different datasets?
