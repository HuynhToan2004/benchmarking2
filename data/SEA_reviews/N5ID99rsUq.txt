 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the generalization performance of adversarial training (AT) methods using the algorithmic stability framework, focusing on the simultaneous optimization of perturbations and model parameters. The authors propose that this approach, termed "free adversarial training," could lead to reduced generalization errors and improved robustness against various adversarial attacks. Theoretical bounds are derived to support these claims, and empirical results are presented to substantiate the theoretical findings. The paper also discusses the practical implications of these theoretical results, such as the potential for better adversarial robustness and generalization in real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive literature review on adversarial training, generalization, and algorithmic stability.
- The authors effectively analyze the generalization error of adversarial training using the algorithmic stability framework, which is a novel approach.
- Theoretical results are provided to support the claim that free adversarial training (AT) can reduce the generalization gap, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- Empirical results demonstrate that free AT can lead to reduced generalization errors and improved robustness against various adversarial attacks.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the generalization behavior of adversarial training, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of why simultaneous optimization of perturbations and model parameters leads to reduced generalization errors, which is a critical aspect of the theoretical analysis.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other adversarial training methods such as TRADES and ALP, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the generalization properties of adversarial training.
- The theoretical results are limited to the nonconvex-nonconcave regime, which might not be practical in real-world applications where the objective function is typically non-convex.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its work, which is a crucial aspect for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly how they can be applied in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a clearer explanation of why simultaneous optimization of perturbations and model parameters leads to reduced generalization errors?
- How does the paper's theoretical analysis compare with other adversarial training methods such as TRADES and ALP?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of their theoretical results, particularly how they can be applied in real-world scenarios?
- What are the limitations of the paper's work, and how do these limitations affect the practical applicability of the findings?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the choice of parameters such as the learning rate and step size in the adversarial training algorithms?
- How does the paper's theoretical analysis hold up under different attack scenarios, such as transfer attacks or black-box attacks?
