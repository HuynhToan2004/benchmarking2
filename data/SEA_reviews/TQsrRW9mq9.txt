 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DeCUR, a novel method for multimodal self-supervised learning that distinguishes inter- and intra-modal embeddings. It aims to integrate complementary information across different modalities by decoupling common and unique representations. The method is evaluated on three multimodal scenarios, showing consistent benefits in scene classification and semantic segmentation downstream tasks. Despite its simplicity, DeCUR is shown to outperform existing methods without requiring extensive hyperparameter tuning. The paper also includes an explainability analysis to shed light on the interpretation of common and unique features in multimodal approaches.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for multimodal self-supervised learning, which decouples common and unique representations, a concept that is both intuitive and effective.
- The methodology is simple and straightforward, making it easy to understand and implement.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including a thorough ablation study that shows the impact of different components of the proposed method.
- The results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms existing methods without requiring extensive hyperparameter tuning, which is a significant advantage for practical applications.
- The paper includes an explainability analysis, which is a valuable contribution to the field of multimodal self-supervised learning, providing insights into the interpretation of common and unique features.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the benefits of the proposed method compared to existing methods, particularly in terms of the advantages of decoupling common and unique representations.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with a sufficient number of existing methods, which limits the ability to assess its effectiveness against the state-of-the-art.
- The methodology is limited to two modalities, which may not generalize well to more complex multimodal scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper lacks a broader evaluation across a variety of datasets and tasks, which could strengthen the claims of the method's effectiveness and generalizability.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the method can be adapted to handle more complex multimodal scenarios with a larger number of modalities?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods in multimodal self-supervised learning?
- Can you provide more insights into how the method can be applied to other multimodal tasks beyond scene classification and semantic segmentation?
- In the explainability analysis, how do the common and unique representations contribute to the model's performance, and what implications do these findings have for the practical application of the model?
- How does the proposed method handle the imbalance in information distribution across different modalities, and what strategies can be employed to address this issue?
