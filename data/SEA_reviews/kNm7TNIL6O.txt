 **Summary:**
The paper introduces UPAR, a prompting framework designed to enhance the performance of Large Language Models (LLMs) by emulating human cognitive structures. Inspired by Kant's philosophy of a priori knowledge, UPAR is divided into four phases: Understand, Plan, Act, and Reflect. Each phase corresponds to a distinct cognitive process, aiming to guide LLMs in understanding, planning, executing, and reflecting on their actions. The framework is evaluated using GPT-4 and shows promising results, particularly in complex reasoning tasks. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the experimental setup and the novelty of the approach, as it largely builds upon existing prompting methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and coherent presentation that effectively connects Kant's philosophy with the proposed prompting framework.
- The experimental results are promising, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed UPAR framework in enhancing the performance of LLMs.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experiments that validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The integration of Kant's philosophy into the prompting framework is a novel and interesting approach, offering a unique perspective on the application of philosophical concepts in AI research.
- The ablation study provides valuable insights into the contribution of each component of the UPAR framework, helping to understand its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the experimental setup, particularly the number of instances used for testing and the specifics of the dataset used.
- There is a lack of novelty in the proposed method, as it primarily builds upon existing prompting methods without sufficiently distinguishing itself from them.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of LLMs and the potential for overfitting to specific models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the UPAR framework compares to other existing methods, particularly in terms of its theoretical contributions and practical applications.
- There is a need for more comprehensive experimental validation, including a broader range of datasets and models to ensure the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the number of instances used for testing and the specifics of the dataset used?
- How does the UPAR framework compare to other existing methods in terms of its theoretical contributions and practical applications?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the effectiveness of the UPAR framework in real-world scenarios?
- How does the UPAR framework address the limitations mentioned, such as its applicability to different types of LLMs and the potential for overfitting?
- Could the authors expand on the theoretical foundations of the UPAR framework, particularly how it aligns with or diverges from existing philosophical and cognitive theories?
