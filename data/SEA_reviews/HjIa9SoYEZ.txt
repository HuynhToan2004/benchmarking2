 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, CTRL, for graph dataset distillation, which utilizes a combination of cosine similarity and Euclidean distance to optimize gradient matching. This approach aims to address the limitations of existing methods that solely rely on cosine similarity, which can lead to disparities in gradient directions and magnitudes. The paper presents extensive experimental results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and tasks. The authors argue that their method improves upon existing techniques by providing a more comprehensive matching criterion and a more effective initialization strategy. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty, as it primarily builds upon existing works, and for its lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the gradient matching process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear motivation and a comprehensive literature review.
- The proposed method, CTRL, is straightforward, intuitive, and well-explained, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The methodology is supported by extensive experimental results, which demonstrate the effectiveness of CTRL in graph dataset distillation tasks.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with existing methods, highlighting the advantages of CTRL over traditional approaches.
- The method is generalizable and can be integrated into other graph distillation methods based on gradient matching, enhancing its practical applicability.
- The paper is well-organized and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method, making it easy for readers to understand and apply the concepts presented.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily builds upon existing works by Jin et al. (2022a,b), with minor modifications.
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the gradient matching process and the initialization strategy.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential loss of informative properties during condensation.
- The experimental results are not consistently superior to existing methods, with some datasets showing no significant improvement or even a decline in performance.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to existing methods, which could affect its scalability in large-scale applications.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in large-scale applications.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed method perform on large-scale datasets, and what are the computational resources required for its implementation?
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to existing methods, and how this affects its scalability in large-scale applications?
- How does the proposed method compare to other gradient-based dataset condensation methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- Can you explain the discrepancies in the experimental results, particularly the decline in performance on some datasets, and how these results were validated?
- How does the proposed method handle the loss of informative properties during condensation, and what steps are taken to mitigate this issue?
