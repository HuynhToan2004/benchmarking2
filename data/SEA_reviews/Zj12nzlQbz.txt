 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for detecting knowledge hallucinations in large language models (LLMs) by leveraging the internal states of the models. Specifically, the authors propose the use of an EigenScore metric to evaluate the semantic consistency of responses across multiple generations, and a feature clipping technique to truncate extreme activations in the internal states, reducing overconfident generations. The methodology is tested on various question-answering benchmarks, demonstrating improved performance over existing metrics like perplexity and lexical similarity. The paper also explores the use of the covariance matrix of sentence embeddings to measure the semantic consistency in the embedding space, which is shown to be effective in detecting hallucinations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed methodology.
- The use of internal states of LLMs to detect knowledge hallucination is a novel approach that has shown effectiveness in experiments.
- The EigenScore metric is a simple yet effective method for measuring semantic consistency in the embedding space, which is crucial for detecting hallucinations.
- The paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The proposed test-time feature clipping approach is a simple yet effective technique for reducing overconfident generations, which is crucial for detecting hallucinations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the feature clipping technique, particularly how it impacts the performance and the number of clipped tokens.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to question-answering tasks, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the approach to other tasks such as summarization or translation.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other methods for detecting knowledge hallucination, such as those mentioned in related works.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of correctness measures and their impact on the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the number of clipped tokens affect the performance of the method?
- Is there a way to measure the number of clipped tokens in the model to ensure the effectiveness of the feature clipping technique?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other tasks such as summarization or translation?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method, including the time required for each step of the method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods for detecting knowledge hallucination, particularly those mentioned in related works?
- What are the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how might these affect its applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the choice of correctness measures affect the results of the method, and what are the implications of using different measures?
