 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel evaluation metric, counterfactual simulatability, to assess the quality of explanations provided by large language models (LLMs) in generating mental models. This metric is used to measure the precision and generality of explanations, which are crucial for understanding how LLMs process inputs and generate outputs. The authors propose two metrics, simulation generality and simulation precision, to assess these aspects. They evaluate these metrics on two tasks, multi-hop factual reasoning and reward modeling, using state-of-the-art LLMs like GPT-3.5 and GPT-4. The paper also includes a detailed discussion on the correlation between these metrics and the plausibility of explanations, which are not found to be correlated. The evaluation process involves human annotation to ensure the validity of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research and a well-defined evaluation metric.
- The proposed metrics, simulation generality and simulation precision, are novel and innovative, providing a new perspective on evaluating the quality of explanations generated by LLMs.
- The study addresses a significant and timely issue in the field of AI, focusing on the evaluation of LLM explanations and their impact on human understanding.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation across various tasks and models, providing a robust analysis of the results and their implications.
- The authors have provided detailed explanations of the evaluation process, including the use of human annotation and the rationale behind the choice of evaluation metrics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the correlation between the proposed metrics and the plausibility of explanations, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the explanations.
- The evaluation tasks used in the study, such as multi-hop factual reasoning and reward modeling, may not adequately reflect the complexity and diversity of real-world applications, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that LLMs can generate externalized reasoning that doesn't correspond to their internal reasoning, which is a significant aspect of the study.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed metrics and the potential biases introduced by the use of human annotation.
- There is a need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing metrics and a deeper exploration of the implications of the results, particularly in terms of the practical applications of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on the correlation between the proposed metrics and the plausibility of explanations? How does this correlation impact the effectiveness of the explanations in helping humans build mental models?
- How do the results of this study compare with other metrics that evaluate the quality of explanations provided by LLMs?
- What are the implications of the findings for the development of LLMs that can generate more precise and generalizable explanations?
- How do the authors address the potential biases introduced by the use of human annotation in the evaluation process?
- Could you provide more detailed evidence or examples to support the claim that LLMs can generate externalized reasoning that doesn't correspond to their internal reasoning?
- How do the results of this study influence the design and development of LLMs, particularly in terms of improving the quality of explanations and mental models?
