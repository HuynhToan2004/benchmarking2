 **Summary:**
The paper introduces EditVal, a benchmark designed for evaluating text-guided image editing methods. It employs a curated dataset of 92 images and 13 different types of edits, along with automated and human evaluations. The benchmark aims to assess the performance of various methods across different types of edits, providing a standardized evaluation framework. The results indicate that while some methods excel in specific types of edits, none can consistently perform well across all edits. The paper also discusses the limitations of existing metrics like CLIP score and introduces a new evaluation method based on human preferences. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its limited dataset size, potential bias in human evaluations, and the generalizability of its findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel evaluation benchmark, EditVal, which is a significant contribution to the field of image editing.
- The evaluation methodology is well-designed, combining both automated and human evaluations, which provides a robust assessment of the performance of different text-guided image editing methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a detailed analysis of the results, which helps in understanding the performance of different methods across various edit types.
- The introduction of a new benchmark, EditVal, is a valuable resource for the research community, providing a standardized evaluation framework that can be used to compare different methods.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of different methods, which helps in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of each method, and identifies areas where further research is needed.

**Weaknesses:**
- The dataset used in the study is relatively small (92 images), which might limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the human study, which is crucial for understanding the reliability and validity of the results.
- There is a need for more diverse and detailed examples in the paper to illustrate the types of edits and their effects, which would help in better understanding the methodology and results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the evaluation methodology, such as the potential bias in human evaluations and the generalizability of the results to other datasets or image editing tasks.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work and a clearer explanation of the methodology used in the human study.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the dataset used, such as its size and the criteria for selection?
- How were the images selected for the dataset, and were there any specific criteria used to ensure diversity and representativeness?
- Could the authors elaborate on the methodology used in the human study, particularly how the images were presented and how the annotators were trained?
- How do the authors address the potential bias in the human evaluations, and what steps were taken to ensure the reliability and validity of the results?
- Could the authors discuss the generalizability of the findings to other datasets or image editing tasks, and how might the results change if the evaluation was conducted on a different dataset?
- How do the authors plan to address the limitations of the evaluation methodology, such as the potential bias in human evaluations and the generalizability of the results?
