 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel dataset, FairSeg, specifically designed for fairness learning in medical image segmentation. This dataset focuses on optic disc and cup segmentation from SLO fundus images, aiming to address fairness issues in medical segmentation tasks. The paper also proposes a fair error-bound scaling approach to adjust the loss function and improve segmentation performance equity. The dataset is evaluated using two state-of-the-art medical segmentation models, and the proposed method shows superior or comparable fairness performance. The paper also introduces a new equity-scaled segmentation performance metric to facilitate fair comparisons. Despite its strengths, the paper has been criticized for its limited scope in terms of segmentation tasks and the lack of a broader evaluation across different medical segmentation tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue by focusing on fairness in medical image segmentation, a critical area that has been under-explored.
- It introduces a novel dataset, FairSeg, specifically designed for fairness learning in medical segmentation, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The paper proposes a new fair error-bound scaling approach and a new equity-scaled segmentation performance metric, which are innovative and potentially impactful.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving fairness performance, supported by comprehensive evaluations using two state-of-the-art medical segmentation models.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of a new dataset and evaluation metrics for fairness in medical segmentation is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is limited in its scope as it only focuses on one segmentation task, which may not generalize well to other medical segmentation tasks.
- The proposed method's effectiveness is limited to the specific dataset used in the study, which raises concerns about its applicability to other datasets or medical conditions.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method across different medical segmentation tasks, which is crucial for demonstrating its robustness and generalizability.
- There is a lack of comparison with other fairness learning algorithms, which is essential for establishing the superiority of the proposed method.
- The paper's focus on a specific medical condition (glaucoma) and a limited set of sensitive attributes (race, gender, ethnicity, language, and marital status) may limit its relevance to broader fairness issues in medical imaging.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how it might be adapted or improved for other medical imaging tasks or datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the registration process used to align the SLO fundus images with the OCT-derived fundus images?
- How were the six sensitive attributes included in the dataset selected, and what considerations were made in their selection?
- In the evaluation section, the results for the TransUNet model seem inconsistent. Could the authors clarify this?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other medical segmentation tasks, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in such applications?
- Could the authors provide more details on the generalizability of the proposed method to other medical imaging tasks or datasets?
- What are the potential ethical implications of the proposed method, and how do the authors address these concerns?
