 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for Hybrid Domain Adaptation (HDA), which aims to adapt a pre-trained generator to multiple target domains, preserving the source domain's characteristics while integrating attributes from the target domains. This is achieved through a discriminator-free framework that encodes images from different domains into separate embedding subspaces, leveraging a directional subspace loss to guide adaptation. The method is evaluated against existing domain adaptation methods and shows superior performance in terms of semantic similarity, image fidelity, and cross-domain consistency. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios with limited target domain images and the scalability of the approach to more complex or diverse domains.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and can be applied to various domains, as demonstrated by qualitative and quantitative results that showcase the method's superior performance compared to existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed experimental settings and a clear description of the proposed method.
- The idea of using a discriminator-free framework and a directional subspace loss to guide the adaptation is innovative and well-explained, showing the ability to preserve the source domain's characteristics while adapting to multiple target domains.
- The method is evaluated against a variety of baselines, including both domain adaptation and hybrid domain adaptation methods, and the results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in terms of semantic similarity, image fidelity, and cross-domain consistency.
- The paper provides a clear motivation for the hybrid domain adaptation problem and demonstrates the method's ability to adapt a pre-trained generator to a hybrid target domain, which is a significant advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's reliance on the availability of a pre-trained image encoder that can encode the target domains into separate embedding subspaces may limit its practicality in scenarios where such encoders are not readily available.
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the method's limitations and potential failure cases, which could help in understanding the method's robustness and applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The method's scalability to more complex or diverse domains is not thoroughly explored, which could limit its applicability in more challenging domain adaptation scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed experimental results and a more comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons with more recent and relevant methods in the field.
- There are some minor issues with the clarity and consistency of the paper, such as typographical errors and inconsistencies in the use of terminology and notation.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be adapted to scenarios where the target domains are not well-separated or where the domains overlap significantly?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the target domains are not well-represented by the source domain, such as in cases of domain shift?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the method's performance in more complex or diverse domains, and discuss any potential challenges or limitations in scaling the method to these scenarios?
- Is there a plan to release the code for the proposed method to facilitate reproducibility and further research in the field?
- How does the method handle the integration of different domain attributes, particularly in scenarios where the attributes are not easily separable or where there is significant overlap between domains?
