 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel denoising-assisted (DA) classifier for score-based models, which utilizes both noisy and denoised images as inputs to enhance the performance of classifiers. This methodology is supported by theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, showing improvements in classification accuracy and generalization to test data. The authors also propose a semi-supervised framework for training diffusion classifiers, which extends the application of DA classifiers in semi-supervised learning settings. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on CIFAR10 and ImageNet datasets, showcasing improvements over traditional classifier-guided diffusion models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and concise presentation of ideas, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed method, which utilizes both noisy and denoised images as inputs, is a novel approach that enhances the generalization and perceptual alignment of classifier gradients.
- The theoretical analysis provided supports the empirical observations, which is a strong point for the paper.
- The method is technically sound, with a simple and effective solution that enhances the performance of classifiers.
- The proposed denoising-assisted (DA) classifier and the semi-supervised framework for training diffusion classifiers are innovative and show promising results in improving generalization and perceptual alignment of classifier gradients.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive literature review, particularly in the area of semi-supervised learning and classifier-free guidance, which could have provided a richer context for the research.
- The experimental results, while showing improvements, are not significantly better than existing methods, which could limit the perceived impact of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The method's applicability is limited to classifier-guidance, which may not be as effective as classifier-free guidance methods in certain scenarios.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the semi-supervised framework, which is a significant contribution, and the results are not convincing enough to support the claims made.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the semi-supervised framework, particularly how the classifier is trained with the denoised images?
- How does the proposed method compare to other classifier-guidance methods in terms of generalization and perceptual alignment of classifier gradients?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical analysis, particularly Theorem 1, to clarify its implications and relevance to the proposed method?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and under what conditions might it not be effective?
- How does the method handle the potential for increased training costs due to the use of denoised images?
