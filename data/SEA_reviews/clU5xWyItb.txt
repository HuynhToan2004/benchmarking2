 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PaperQA, a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) agent designed for scientific question answering, which leverages LLMs and external APIs to retrieve and synthesize information from scientific literature. The system is evaluated against human experts and other LLM-based methods on a new benchmark, LitQA, and shows competitive performance. PaperQA is noted for its ability to reduce hallucinations and provide provenance, although its generalizability to other domains and its reliance on specific LLM configurations are highlighted as limitations. The paper also discusses the potential of RAG agents in scientific research, suggesting a promising direction for future work in this area.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and easy-to-follow methodology, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of the new dataset, LitQA, is a significant contribution to the field, providing a valuable resource for evaluating scientific question answering systems.
- The system's ability to reduce hallucinations and provide provenance is commendable, enhancing the reliability and trustworthiness of the generated answers.
- The paper is well-motivated, addressing a critical issue in scientific research by utilizing RAG agents to reduce hallucinations and improve the accuracy of scientific question answering.
- The system's performance is impressive, particularly in the biomedical domain, showing a strong correlation with human performance and outperforming existing methods.
- The paper provides a detailed evaluation of the system, including ablation studies and comparisons with human performance, which helps in understanding the system's strengths and limitations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the generalizability of the proposed method to other domains, particularly those with different knowledge bases or data distributions.
- The system's performance is heavily dependent on the specific LLM configurations used, which may limit its applicability in other settings.
- The paper does not discuss the potential biases in the LLMs used, which could impact the fairness and reliability of the system's outputs.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential societal impacts of the system, particularly in terms of its potential misuse or ethical concerns.
- The system's complexity, including multiple LLM instances and external APIs, may limit its practical deployment in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential limitations or negative societal impacts of the proposed system.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the generalizability of the proposed method to other domains, particularly those with different knowledge bases or data distributions?
- How does the system perform in domains outside of the biomedical domain, and what adjustments, if any, are necessary for successful deployment in these domains?
- Could you discuss the potential biases in the LLMs used and how these biases might impact the fairness and reliability of the system's outputs?
- How does the system handle the potential for hallucinations in the LLM's outputs, and what mechanisms are in place to prevent or mitigate these issues?
- Could you provide more details on the system's performance in terms of computational resources and time required for generating answers, and how these factors might impact the system's practical deployment?
- How does the system ensure the accuracy and reliability of the retrieved information, particularly in cases where the information might be outdated or incomplete?
