 **Summary:**
The paper investigates adversarial attacks on Combinatorial Multi-Armed Bandits (CMAB), focusing on reward poisoning attacks. It introduces a new notion of polynomial attackability, which characterizes the vulnerability of CMAB instances to such attacks. The paper provides a sufficient and necessary condition for polynomial attackability, highlighting the influence of whether the bandit environment is known or unknown to the adversary. The authors conduct extensive experiments to validate their theoretical findings, demonstrating the practical applicability of their theoretical results. Despite the novel approach and theoretical contributions, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in some sections, the limited scope of its experimental validation, and the potential for more comprehensive discussions on related works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic of adversarial attacks on Combinatorial Multi-Armed Bandits (CMAB), providing a novel definition of polynomial attackability which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The theoretical results are well-supported by extensive experiments, demonstrating the practical relevance and applicability of the theoretical findings.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise language, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.
- The authors provide a thorough discussion on the implications of their results, including a detailed analysis of the conditions under which CMAB instances are vulnerable to attacks.
- The paper includes a hard example that effectively demonstrates the differences in attackability between known and unknown bandit environments, providing a valuable insight into the practical implications of the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the relationship between polynomial attackability and other notions of attackability, particularly in the context of known and unknown environments.
- There are some issues with the clarity and presentation of the paper, particularly in the introduction and related works sections, which could be improved for better comprehension and engagement.
- The experimental validation is limited in scope, focusing primarily on a few specific CMAB applications, which may not adequately represent the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed approach and the potential challenges in applying the results to more complex or varied CMAB scenarios.
- The paper could be enhanced by including more examples or case studies to illustrate the practical implications of the theoretical results and to provide a deeper understanding of the attackability of CMAB instances.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between polynomial attackability and other notions of attackability, particularly in the context of known and unknown environments?
- How does the proposed approach to polynomial attackability compare or contrast with other existing methods for evaluating the vulnerability of CMAB instances to adversarial attacks?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical implications of the theoretical results and to provide a deeper understanding of the attackability of CMAB instances?
- In the context of the experimental validation, could the authors consider expanding the scope of the experiments to include a broader range of CMAB applications and to evaluate the robustness of the findings under different conditions or scenarios?
- How do the authors envision the practical application of their findings in real-world scenarios, and what steps do they recommend for practitioners seeking to mitigate the risks of adversarial attacks on CMAB systems?
