 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Physics-infused Intention NEtwork (PINE), a novel framework for crowd simulation that integrates a rule-based model with a neural network to enhance the simulation of pedestrian behavior. The model uses a feature adaptive extraction module and a force intention module to adjust the attention of pedestrians to different factors, such as goals and obstacles. It also employs a variable-step rollout training algorithm to address cumulative errors during simulation. The paper presents extensive experiments on four publicly available real-world datasets, demonstrating that PINE outperforms state-of-the-art simulation methods in terms of accuracy, physical fidelity, and generalizability. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach and the clarity of the methodological contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and experimental results.
- The combination of a rule-based model with a neural network for crowd simulation is a novel approach that enhances the simulation of pedestrian behavior by integrating physical biases and attention mechanisms.
- The use of a variable-step rollout training algorithm and an optimized loss function to address cumulative errors in simulation is innovative and effective.
- The paper presents extensive experiments on four publicly available real-world datasets, demonstrating the superior performance of the proposed method over state-of-the-art simulation methods.
- The ablation study is well-designed, providing insights into the effectiveness of the proposed modules.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation from previous works, particularly in the novelty of the proposed method.
- The methodology section is not detailed enough, and the paper does not adequately explain the integration of the neural network with the rule-based model, which could lead to confusion about the roles and contributions of each component.
- The paper does not provide sufficient comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, such as the NSP model, which limits the evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper lacks visual results and detailed explanations of the behavioral intentions of pedestrians, which are critical for validating the claimed improvements in fidelity and generalizability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of the proposed method and how it differs from previous works, particularly in terms of the integration of the neural network with the rule-based model?
- How does the paper address the issue of pedestrian behavior beyond the scope of the rule-based model?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the methodology, particularly in the integration of the neural network with the rule-based model?
- How does the paper ensure the physical consistency of the simulated pedestrian behavior, especially in scenarios where pedestrian behavior is not accurately captured by the rule-based model?
- Can the authors provide visual results and detailed explanations of the behavioral intentions of pedestrians to support the claims of improved fidelity and generalizability?
- How does the paper handle the limitations of the proposed method, and what are the implications of these limitations for the practical application of the findings?
