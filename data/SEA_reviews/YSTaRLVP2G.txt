 **Summary:**
The paper explores the role of random filters in Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) by focusing on the linear combinations of filters, particularly in the context of pointwise convolutions. The authors investigate how these random filters, when combined effectively, can achieve comparable or even superior performance to fully trainable CNNs. The study also delves into the implications of this approach on network robustness and computational efficiency. The paper presents various experiments, including those with different dataset sizes and network configurations, to validate these findings. However, concerns about the generalizability of the results to larger, more complex datasets and the practical applicability of the findings are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and intuitive explanations that enhance reader comprehension.
- The empirical results are compelling, showing that random filters can perform comparably to learned filters in certain scenarios, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a comprehensive overview of the literature, highlighting the relevance and novelty of the research.
- The experiments are well-designed and include a variety of models, datasets, and parameters, which support the robustness of the findings.
- The paper effectively demonstrates the potential of random filters, which could lead to more efficient and effective CNN designs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on CNNs and not extending to other architectures such as Vision Transformers (ViTs) or other types of neural networks.
- The theoretical underpinnings of the paper are not sufficiently strong, with many claims relying heavily on empirical evidence without a solid theoretical foundation.
- The generalizability of the findings is questionable, particularly in terms of their applicability to larger, more complex datasets and more advanced network architectures.
- The paper's conclusions are not sufficiently supported by the data presented, and the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of random filters over learned filters in all scenarios.
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the presentation of results, such as in Figure 1, which could mislead readers about the performance of different models.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies in the performance of different models as shown in Figure 1, particularly the unexpectedly low performance of MobileNet v2 on CIFAR-10?
- How does the paper's findings apply to other types of neural networks beyond CNNs, such as ViTs or other architectures that use different filtering mechanisms?
- Can the authors provide more detailed theoretical explanations for the observed phenomena, particularly the superiority of random filters in certain scenarios?
- How do the findings of this paper relate to other recent studies that also explore the use of random weights in neural networks?
- Could the authors expand on the potential practical applications of their findings, particularly in terms of designing more efficient and effective CNNs?
