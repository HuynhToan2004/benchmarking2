 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a unified signal propagation theory for transformer models, focusing on the moments of forward and backward signals through various components such as attention, FFN, and LayerNorm. The authors propose a novel initialization and scaling scheme, DeepScaleLM, aimed at maintaining the moments of outputs and gradients throughout the training process. This scheme is validated through empirical studies that demonstrate improvements in perplexity and downstream tasks compared to shallow models. The theoretical framework is also used to address issues like vanishing/exploding gradients and rank collapse. However, the paper's theoretical contributions are questioned due to assumptions that may not hold in real-world scenarios, and the practical benefits of the proposed methods are not convincingly demonstrated.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a unified signal propagation theory for transformers, which can be used to understand and mitigate issues like vanishing/exploding gradients and rank collapse.
- The authors propose a novel initialization and scaling scheme, DeepScaleLM, which can train very deep models with hundreds of layers, outperforming shallow models in perplexity and downstream tasks.
- The theoretical framework is validated through empirical studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper is well-written and clearly explains the theoretical framework, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed initialization scheme is simple yet effective, and the theoretical framework can be used to understand and mitigate various issues in deep transformers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper makes several assumptions that may not hold in real-world scenarios, such as the assumption of normal distribution for inputs, weights, and gradients, which could limit the applicability of the results.
- The practical benefits of the proposed methods are not convincingly demonstrated. The empirical results show only marginal improvements over shallow models, and the scalability of the method to larger models is not convincingly shown.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is based on a simplified model, which may not reflect the complexity of real-world scenarios, and the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis are not thoroughly justified or validated.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and scalability, and does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational overheads of the proposed methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related works and a clearer presentation of the experimental results and methodological details.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and provide more details on how these assumptions hold in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed initialization scheme compare to other existing methods in terms of performance and scalability, particularly in larger models?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons with other methods and a more detailed analysis of the computational overheads of the proposed methods?
- How does the proposed initialization scheme affect the training process, particularly in terms of the stability and convergence of the training process?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other methods in terms of the theoretical analysis and experimental results, particularly in terms of the scalability and practical benefits of the proposed methods?
