 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GAPN, a Generative Adversarial Policy Network designed to predict the structure of multi-chain protein complexes using a reinforcement learning (RL) approach. The method leverages a graph-based representation of protein complexes, where each chain is treated as a node and assembly actions are represented by edges. The RL agent, GAPN, learns to efficiently explore the combinatorial optimization space, optimizing direct docking rewards through a policy gradient. The approach is evaluated against existing methods, showing significant improvements in accuracy and efficiency. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method and the clarity of its contributions, particularly in distinguishing between the RL agent's contributions and those of the graph-based representation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their implementation.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in protein complex modeling (PCM), demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods.
- The use of reinforcement learning (RL) for protein complex modeling is innovative and provides a novel approach to the problem.
- The paper includes a detailed comparison with existing methods, providing a robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The authors have made significant efforts to ensure reproducibility by providing detailed information on the experimental setup, including the availability of the dataset used.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and challenges of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the method might not perform optimally.
- There is a lack of novelty in the approach, as the use of RL for protein complex modeling is not new, and the contributions of the RL agent are not clearly distinguished from those of the graph-based representation.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the potential societal implications of the work, which is a significant oversight.
- The paper's claims about the novelty of using RL for protein complex modeling are overstated, as similar approaches have been explored in previous works.
- The method's reliance on the graph-based representation of protein complexes raises questions about its generalizability to other types of complexes that may not conform to this structure.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational efficiency of the GAPN model, particularly in comparison to other methods like MoLPC.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach in using RL for protein complex modeling, particularly in light of previous works that have explored similar methods?
- How does the GAPN model ensure that the assembly actions it predicts are optimal, especially in complex scenarios where multiple chains may interact simultaneously?
- What are the specific challenges that the GAPN model faces in scenarios where it does not perform optimally, and how does it handle these challenges?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the GAPN model, including a comparison with other methods like MoLPC?
- How does the GAPN model handle the complexity of protein complex modeling, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for training and inference?
- In the ablation study, how does the model perform without the adversarial reward, and what does this indicate about the effectiveness of the RL agent in the proposed method?
