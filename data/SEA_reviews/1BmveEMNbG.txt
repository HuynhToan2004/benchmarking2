 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new approach for complex query answering on knowledge graphs using a neural-symbolic method called Fuzzy Inference with Truth value (FIT). This method extends beyond traditional Tree-Form queries to handle more complex existential first-order queries with a single free variable (EFO1). The authors propose a new dataset and a neural-symbolic method that integrates fuzzy logic theory to enhance the reasoning capabilities of neural link predictors. The FIT method is shown to outperform existing methods on both the newly developed dataset and existing datasets, highlighting its effectiveness. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its clarity in presentation and the need for more comprehensive evaluations and comparisons with other neural-symbolic approaches.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and comprehensive proofs of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is technically sound and innovative, addressing a significant gap in the literature by focusing on the limitations of Tree-Form queries and proposing a new approach to handle more complex queries.
- The authors have conducted thorough experiments and provide a comprehensive analysis of the proposed method, which is supported by a new dataset and extensive theoretical guarantees.
- The paper is significant as it extends the scope of complex query answering to the whole family of EFO1 queries, which is crucial for advancing research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from some clarity issues in the presentation, particularly in the explanation of the new dataset and the query graphs, which could benefit from more detailed visual aids or additional explanations.
- There is a lack of comparison with other neural-symbolic approaches, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the limitations of the proposed method, especially in terms of its applicability to more complex queries and the potential for overfitting due to the complexity of the neural link predictors.
- Some definitions and notations are not clearly explained, which could confuse readers unfamiliar with the specific terminologies used.
- The paper lacks a broader evaluation on other datasets, which could have strengthened the results and provided a more comprehensive view of the method's performance.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition of the query graph and the new dataset, possibly providing more detailed visual aids or additional explanations?
- How does the proposed method compare with other neural-symbolic approaches, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might impact its applicability to more complex queries?
- How does the FIT method handle the training of neural link predictors, especially in terms of the complexity and potential for overfitting?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the choice of t-norms and t-conorms in the neural matrix construction, possibly including a sensitivity analysis or ablation study?
- What is the impact of the query graph size on the performance of the FIT method, and how does this compare with other methods that might not be able to handle larger query graphs?
