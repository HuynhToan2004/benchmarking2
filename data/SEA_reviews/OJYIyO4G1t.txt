 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called One-class Graph Autoencoder (OLGA) for one-class learning in graphs. OLGA leverages a graph autoencoder and a hypersphere loss function to learn node representations and classify nodes within a graph. The method aims to address the limitations of existing one-class graph neural networks (OCGNNs) by improving interpretability and visualization. Extensive experiments across eight datasets demonstrate that OLGA outperforms several baselines and achieves state-of-the-art results. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the approach, as it appears to be a combination of existing methods without sufficient theoretical backing or significant performance improvements.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide extensive experimental results across eight datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of their proposed method.
- The methodology is novel, combining a graph autoencoder with a hypersphere loss function, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper addresses the limitations of existing methods in one-class learning and provides a new loss function that addresses these issues.
- The proposed method shows promising results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on most datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- There is a need for more clarity in the experimental setup, particularly in how the results are validated and the impact of different hyperparameters on the model's performance.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as it appears to be a combination of existing methods without significant innovation or theoretical justification.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the graph autoencoder architecture and its integration with the hypersphere loss function.
- The interpretability and visualization aspects of the method are not convincingly demonstrated, particularly in how the low-dimensional representations aid in model understanding.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed comparisons with existing methods, such as OCGCN and OCGAT, and by providing more extensive experimental validations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical application?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent methods like OCGCN and OCGAT, particularly in terms of performance and interpretability?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, especially how the results are validated and how different hyperparameters affect the model's performance?
- How does the graph autoencoder architecture integrate with the hypersphere loss function, and what are the implications of this integration for the model's performance and interpretability?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies demonstrating how the low-dimensional representations aid in model understanding and interpretation?
