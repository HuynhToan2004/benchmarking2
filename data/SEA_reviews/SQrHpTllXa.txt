 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CABINET, a novel framework designed to enhance the performance of large language models (LLMs) in table-based question-answering tasks. It employs an unsupervised relevance scorer and a weakly supervised parsing statement generator to identify and prioritize relevant table data, thereby reducing the impact of irrelevant information. The framework is evaluated across three benchmark datasets, demonstrating significant improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods. The approach involves a two-stage training process where the LLM is trained on a parsing statement generator to predict relevant table cells, which are then used to score the relevance of each cell. The paper also includes extensive ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of the proposed components.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to addressing the noise in table QA tasks by focusing on the relevance of table content to the input question.
- The proposed method significantly outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods on three benchmark datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the proposed method and its components.
- The approach is robust to noise and can handle large tables, which is a significant improvement over existing methods.
- The paper includes extensive ablation studies and experimental results that validate the effectiveness of the proposed components.
- The availability of code and datasets enhances the reproducibility and transparency of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the method handles the problem of noisy tables, especially in terms of the impact of row and column permutations.
- There is a lack of comparison with other baseline methods such as Binder and LEVER, which also utilize in-context learning with LLMs for table QA tasks.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The method's generalization to other types of tables, such as those with more complex structures or different formats, is not explored.
- The paper does not compare the performance of the proposed method with different types of LLMs, which could provide insights into the effectiveness of the method across different models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational resources required for training and inference, which is crucial for practical deployment.

**Questions:**
- How does the method handle noisy tables, especially in terms of row and column permutations?
- Can the proposed method be adapted to handle tables with more complex structures or different formats?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how do these impact its applicability and effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method compare with other baseline methods such as Binder and LEVER in terms of performance and computational resources?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and inference times for the proposed method and the baseline models?
- How does the method perform with different types of LLMs, and could the authors provide insights into the effectiveness of the method across different models?
