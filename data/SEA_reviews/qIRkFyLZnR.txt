 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel linear function approximation framework for offline distributionally robust reinforcement learning (DRRL), addressing the challenges of limited data and mismatches between training and test environments. The proposed method, DRVI-L, optimizes against a worst-case perturbation of the dataset to learn a distributionally robust policy. The framework is evaluated in both well-explored and less-explored dataset scenarios, demonstrating theoretical and empirical improvements over non-robust methods. The paper also introduces a value shift algorithmic technique to enhance the theoretical results and empirical performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel linear function approximation framework for offline distributionally robust reinforcement learning (DRRL), which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The authors provide theoretical results on the sample complexity of the proposed method, which is a valuable contribution to the understanding of the algorithm's performance.
- The paper is well-structured and clearly presented, making it accessible to readers and facilitating comprehension of the complex concepts discussed.
- The methodology is supported by empirical evidence, demonstrating the practical applicability and effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in offline RL, namely the robustness of policies to distribution shifts, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The authors have made a significant effort to ensure the clarity and readability of the paper, which is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance metrics.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly presented, with some figures and results being difficult to interpret or understand.
- There are several assumptions made in the paper that may limit the generalizability and applicability of the results, such as the soft state aggregation assumption and the uniform well-explored dataset assumption.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights into the method's strengths and weaknesses.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications and limitations of the assumptions made, particularly in relation to real-world scenarios.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are not sufficiently novel or distinct from existing methods, particularly in the area of robust RL, which could limit its impact.

**Questions:**

- How does the paper address the issue of over-conservatism in the RTA approach, particularly in scenarios where the action set is not binary?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to illustrate the potential conflicts between linear function approximation and robustness gain mentioned in the paper?
- How does the paper ensure that the assumptions made, such as the soft state aggregation assumption, are realistic and applicable in practical scenarios?
- Can the authors elaborate on the practical implications and limitations of the assumptions made in the paper, particularly in relation to real-world applications?
- How does the paper's methodology compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance metrics, particularly under different data coverage conditions?
