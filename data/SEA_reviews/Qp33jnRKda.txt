 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for dynamically growing neural network architectures during training by identifying and addressing expressivity bottlenecks. This approach leverages backpropagation to detect and quantify these bottlenecks, enabling the addition of new neurons to enhance expressivity. The method is evaluated on the CIFAR-10 dataset, showing competitive results compared to existing methods like GradMax. However, the paper is criticized for its limited evaluation on a single dataset, the lack of a broader experimental validation, and unclear presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and provides a clear, intuitive explanation of the proposed method, which is both innovative and practical.
- The method is theoretically grounded, with a solid mathematical foundation that supports its claims.
- The experimental results are compelling, showing that the proposed method can outperform existing methods like GradMax in certain scenarios.
- The paper is original in its approach to dynamically growing neural network architectures during training, which could have significant implications for the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader evaluation, focusing only on the CIFAR-10 dataset which may not adequately represent the method's performance across different datasets.
- The method's effectiveness in more complex or diverse datasets is not demonstrated, which is a significant limitation given the potential applications of the method.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with some sections being overly dense and difficult to follow. This could make it harder for readers to understand the method and its implications.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of redundancy in the method, which could lead to the addition of unnecessary neurons.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper does not include a thorough comparison with other methods, such as random initialization or other growth methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process for the TINY method, particularly how the architecture is grown and how this impacts the training dynamics?
- How does the method perform on other datasets, such as CIFAR-100 or ImageNet, and what insights can be gained from these evaluations?
- Can the authors clarify the method's handling of redundancy and provide examples or theoretical proofs that the method does not add redundant neurons?
- How does the method compare to other growth methods, particularly in terms of training dynamics and final performance?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples of how the method detects and addresses expressivity bottlenecks in practice?
