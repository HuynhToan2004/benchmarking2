 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FUSE-ILP, a novel differentiable inductive logic programming (ILP) method that extends the expressivity of ILP by allowing the learning of subgraph-like rules. This is achieved through a tree-like clause template and a differentiable set partitioning technique for leaf grouping. The method is demonstrated to outperform existing techniques on small datasets and is particularly effective in learning non-chain-like rules. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in presentation, insufficient experimental validation, and the absence of comparisons with more recent and relevant baselines. The method's generalization to larger and more complex datasets and its scalability are also questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-motivated and addresses the need for learning complex rules in differentiable ILP, which is crucial for handling non-trivial and non-chain-like rules.
- The proposed method is clearly explained and is supported by a detailed description of the algorithm and its implementation, making it accessible to readers.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning subgraph-like rules, which extends the capabilities of differentiable ILP techniques to handle more complex rule structures.
- The method's performance on small datasets demonstrates its effectiveness in learning complex rules, which is a significant advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in its presentation, with some sections being difficult to understand due to poor writing and insufficient explanation of key concepts.
- The experimental validation is limited, with only small datasets used, and there is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant baselines, which makes it difficult to assess the method's performance against state-of-the-art techniques.
- The method's scalability and generalization to larger and more complex datasets are not adequately addressed, raising concerns about its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method and potential negative societal impacts, which are important for understanding the method's broader implications.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the motivation behind the choice of the method and its advantages over other existing methods, particularly in terms of its expressivity and scalability?
- How does the method handle the complexity of learning subgraph-like rules, and what are the computational costs associated with this approach?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the datasets used and the parameters of the baseline methods compared?
- How does the method perform on larger and more complex datasets, and what are the implications of its scalability for its practical application?
- Could you discuss the potential limitations of the method and any negative societal impacts that might arise from its use?
