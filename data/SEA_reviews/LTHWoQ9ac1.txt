 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the algorithmic recourse problem by proposing a two-step approach that integrates preference learning into the recourse generation process. The first step involves a questionnaire framework to refine the confidence set of the Mahalanobis matrix cost for the subject. In the second step, gradient-based and graph-based methods are used to generate recourse, considering the whole confidence set of the cost matrix. The approach is evaluated against state-of-the-art baselines and demonstrates improvements in delivering cost-efficient recourse recommendations. However, concerns are raised about the scalability of the method, the assumption of a Mahalanobis cost function, and the lack of discussion on the practical implications of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic, focusing on the algorithmic recourse problem, which is relevant to various real-world applications.
- The authors propose a novel approach that integrates preference learning into the recourse generation process, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, with a clear presentation of the proposed algorithm and its components, making the paper accessible and understandable.
- The paper provides a theoretical framework for the proposed algorithm, which adds a solid foundation to the work.
- The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic and real-world datasets, and the results are convincing, showing improvements over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes a Mahalanobis cost function, which may not be realistic in all scenarios and could limit the general applicability of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the scalability of the proposed methods, particularly concerning the complexity of the semidefinite programming (SDP) problem and the computational cost of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the proposed methods, such as the feasibility of asking users to answer a large number of questions and the real-world applicability of the recourse generation process.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the methodology, particularly in the explanation of the semidefinite programming problem and the heuristic used for question selection.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed methods with existing ones, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made regarding the Mahalanobis cost function and discuss how the proposed method might be adapted for scenarios where this assumption does not hold?
- How does the proposed method handle cases where the user's answers are inconsistent or ambiguous?
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed methods, including the computational cost and complexity of the SDP problem?
- In terms of practical implementation, how feasible is it to ask users to answer a large number of questions, and what are the potential drawbacks or limitations of this approach?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical implementation with existing methods, particularly in scenarios where the number of recourse options is large?
