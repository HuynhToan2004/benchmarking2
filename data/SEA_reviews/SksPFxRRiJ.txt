 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Joint Subspace Estimation (JSE), aimed at removing spurious concepts from neural network representations by identifying orthogonal subspaces for spurious and main-task concepts. This method is evaluated across various datasets, including computer vision and natural language processing, showing improvements over existing concept-removal methods. The paper also discusses the potential of JSE in enhancing out-of-distribution generalization and interpreting neural networks. However, it faces criticism for its limited empirical evaluation, lack of theoretical justification, and unclear presentation in some sections.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Joint Subspace Estimation (JSE), which addresses the challenge of removing spurious concepts from neural network representations, a critical issue in neural network interpretability and robustness.
- The proposed method is simple, easy to understand, and empirically shows effectiveness in removing spurious concepts from neural network representations.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, with clear and well-organized content.
- The method is evaluated on both vision and NLP tasks, providing a comprehensive evaluation across different domains.
- The paper includes a variety of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in outperforming existing concept-removal methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough theoretical justification for the proposed method, particularly in explaining why the orthogonal subspace estimation is effective in removing spurious concepts.
- The empirical evaluation is limited, with insufficient comparisons to other baseline methods and a lack of comprehensive experimental results, such as missing standard deviations and detailed performance metrics.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with some sections being difficult to understand and certain notations and definitions being unclear or missing.
- The paper's reliance on strong assumptions, such as the linearity and orthogonality of subspaces, may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where these assumptions are not met.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method to larger datasets or more complex models, which could affect its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the theoretical underpinnings of the orthogonal subspace estimation and its effectiveness in removing spurious concepts?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the subspaces are not orthogonal or linear?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including standard deviations and comparisons to a broader range of baseline methods?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, particularly in terms of computational complexity and applicability to larger datasets or more complex models?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made in the method and how these assumptions affect the practical applicability of the method?
