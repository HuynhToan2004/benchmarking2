 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training paradigm called MpM, which leverages a multilingual language model to facilitate zero-shot multimodal learning across languages. This methodology is demonstrated through the development of large multimodal models, VisCPM, which achieves state-of-the-art performance in Chinese image-to-text and text-to-image generation. The approach utilizes a multilingual LLM as a pivot to transfer multimodal alignment knowledge across languages, thereby addressing the scarcity of multimodal data in non-English languages. The paper also provides open-source codes and model weights to facilitate further research.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel training paradigm called MpM, which effectively leverages multilingual LLMs to facilitate zero-shot multimodal learning across languages.
- The approach is supported by a well-designed experiment that demonstrates the effectiveness of MpM in developing large multimodal models, VisCPM, which achieves state-of-the-art performance in Chinese image-to-text and text-to-image generation.
- The methodology is well-presented and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The paper provides open-source codes and model weights, which are valuable resources for the community.
- The experiments conducted are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other multilingual multimodal models, which could help in understanding the advantages and limitations of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the model's performance might vary across different languages, particularly those with limited multimodal data.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of its applicability to other languages beyond those tested.
- The paper's novelty is questioned, as it appears to be an extension of existing methods without significant technical innovation.
- The evaluation metrics used, such as FID, are not well-suited for text-to-image tasks and may not accurately reflect the model's performance.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational resources required for training and inference, which is crucial for practical deployment.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other multilingual multimodal models, including a discussion on the advantages and limitations of their approach?
- How does the model's performance vary across different languages, particularly those with limited multimodal data?
- Can the authors elaborate on the scalability of the proposed method and discuss its applicability to other languages beyond those tested?
- How does the model's performance compare when trained on different types and quantities of data?
- What are the specific computational resources required for training and inference, and how does this impact the practical deployment of the model?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the model's performance on different types of datasets, such as those with varying levels of noise or complexity?
