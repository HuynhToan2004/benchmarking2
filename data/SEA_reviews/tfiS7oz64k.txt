 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CDiffSDF, a lightweight text-to-shape model that generates 3D shapes using latent-code-based signed distance functions (SDFs). This model integrates a diffusion denoising capability within the latent space and employs Gaussian noise during training to enhance shape reconstruction from latent codes. The model's approach allows for the generation of high-resolution shapes and is versatile, adapting to various input conditions such as text, class, and image descriptions. Despite its innovative use of latent-code SDFs and diffusion models, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its reliance on existing techniques like SDF and diffusion models. The model's performance is evaluated against several baselines, showcasing competitive results. However, concerns about the model's novelty, the complexity of the training process, and the clarity of the paper's presentation are highlighted.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a lightweight text-to-shape model that generates high-resolution shapes, addressing the need for compact models that can generate detailed geometry.
- The use of latent-code SDFs and diffusion models within the latent space is innovative, enhancing the model's ability to generate shapes influenced by a range of inputs, including text, class, and image descriptions.
- The model is robust to noise, thanks to the addition of Gaussian noise during training, which makes it resilient to perturbations that can occur during diffusion sampling.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, providing a clear and concise explanation of the model's components and their integration.
- The model's performance is competitive with existing methods, demonstrating its effectiveness in generating shapes that closely resemble the ground truth.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation of the model, including both qualitative and quantitative results, and provides a detailed analysis of the model's performance across different metrics and datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is questioned due to its reliance on existing techniques such as SDF and diffusion models, which have been previously used in other text-to-shape models.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights into the model's performance and potential areas for improvement.
- The training process for the model is complex, involving multiple stages and a large number of hyperparameters, which could make it difficult to replicate or fine-tune the model for different applications.
- The paper does not adequately compare the model with the latest state-of-the-art methods, such as those using large language models or other advanced generative models.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed visualizations and examples of the generated shapes, especially in the appendix, to better demonstrate the model's capabilities.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, especially in the introduction and related work sections.

**Questions:**
- How does the model handle complex or ambiguous text prompts? Can it generate shapes that are not explicitly described in the text?
- Could the model be adapted to generate shapes based on images or other types of input, such as 2D sketches or 3D scans?
- How does the model compare in terms of computational efficiency and inference speed with other text-to-shape models, especially those that use different representations or methods?
- Could you provide more details on the training process, including the hyperparameters used and the training data requirements?
- How does the model ensure that the generated shapes are not only accurate but also aesthetically pleasing or functional, depending on the application?
- Could you clarify the model's performance on more challenging datasets or scenarios, such as those with complex or detailed text descriptions?
