 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel transformer architecture, Symmetry-Invariant Transformer (SiT), designed to enhance generalization in reinforcement learning (RL) by leveraging symmetries in the input space. The SiT architecture incorporates Graph Symmetric Attention (GSA) to maintain graph symmetries, aiming to improve out-of-distribution generalization. The methodology is tested on various benchmarks including MiniGrid, Procgen, and Atari 100k, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper, the robustness of the experimental results, and the novelty of the approach, particularly in relation to existing works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel transformer architecture, Symmetry-Invariant Transformer (SiT), which is designed to leverage symmetries in the input space, enhancing the generalization of reinforcement learning models.
- The proposed method is evaluated on various benchmarks including MiniGrid, Procgen, and Atari 100k, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their applications.
- The concept of leveraging symmetries in the input space is innovative and could potentially be applied to other domains, such as computer vision and natural language processing.
- The experiments are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various scenarios, including out-of-distribution generalization and sample efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the key components such as the graph matrix G and the graph symmetric dropout, which are crucial for understanding the methodology.
- There is a noticeable absence of comparisons with other equivariant/invariant methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper is sometimes confusing, with unclear explanations and figures that are not well-designed or labeled, making it difficult to understand the main contributions and findings.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as similar approaches have been explored in previous works, particularly in the context of graph neural networks and vision transformers.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and efficiency of the proposed method, which could limit its practical application in real-world scenarios.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with some results showing only marginal improvements or even worse performance in certain scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and function of the graph matrix G and the graph symmetric dropout in the proposed method?
- How does the SiT architecture compare to other equivariant/invariant methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the training and testing data, hyperparameters, and the specifics of the data augmentation techniques used?
- Why was the decision made to use a transformer-based architecture instead of a CNN-based architecture, and how does this choice affect the generalization capabilities of the model?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of symmetries, such as those found in different domains like computer vision and natural language processing?
- Could you elaborate on the scalability and efficiency of the SiT architecture, particularly in terms of its practical application in real-world scenarios?
