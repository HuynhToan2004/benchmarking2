 **Summary:**
The paper investigates a novel multi-agent learning framework, termed Stateless Mean-Field Games (SMFG), which addresses large-scale multi-agent learning settings where agents play games without central coordination. The authors propose a method that converges to a Nash equilibrium with finite-sample, finite-agent guarantees, employing a regularized projected ascent algorithm. Theoretical results are supported by numerical and real-world experiments, showcasing the method's effectiveness in various scenarios. The paper's contributions include the formulation of SMFG and its Nash equilibrium, the use of variational inequalities, and the analysis of independent learning under bandit feedback models. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, unclear motivations, and lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of multi-agent learning, focusing on large-scale multi-agent settings which are common in real-world applications.
- The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the proposed method, which is supported by numerical and real-world experiments, enhancing the credibility of the results.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for understanding complex theoretical concepts.
- The introduction of the SMFG framework and its Nash equilibrium is a novel contribution to the field, providing a new perspective on multi-agent learning.
- The use of variational inequalities and the analysis of independent learning under bandit feedback models are innovative approaches that advance the understanding of multi-agent learning dynamics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions of monotonicity and Lipschitz continuity may not hold.
- The paper does not adequately compare its results with existing methods, such as those in the field of multi-player MAB, which could have provided a clearer context for the contributions of the study.
- The paper's novelty is limited as it builds on existing works, and the technical contributions are not clearly distinguished from prior research.
- The paper's motivation for focusing on large-scale multi-agent settings is not convincingly justified, and the paper does not convincingly demonstrate the necessity or effectiveness of the proposed method in such settings.
- The paper's experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in large-scale settings, as the experiments are conducted with relatively small numbers of agents.
- The paper's writing and organization could be improved to better convey the contributions and results, particularly in the introduction and related work sections.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with related works, particularly those in the field of multi-player MAB, to better contextualize the contributions of the study?

- Could the authors discuss the potential applications of the SMFG framework beyond the specific scenarios considered in the paper, and how the framework might be adapted to different types of games or environments?
