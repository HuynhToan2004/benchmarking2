 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the effectiveness of the weight balancing (WB) method in addressing the long-tailed recognition (LTR) problem. It decomposes the WB method into five components: weight decay (WD), max norm, cross entropy (CE), class-balanced loss (CB), and two-stage learning. The authors analyze the role of each component in enhancing the Fisher's discriminant ratio (FDR) and provide theoretical insights into how these components contribute to the WB's success. The paper also proposes a simplified training method that combines the first-stage effects of WD and CE with a multiplicative logit adjustment (LA) to improve classification accuracy. However, the paper's clarity and organization need improvement, and the experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the weight balancing (WB) method, which is crucial for understanding its effectiveness in the long-tailed recognition (LTR) problem.
- It introduces a novel approach by decomposing the WB method into five components, each contributing to different aspects of the LTR problem.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- Theoretical insights are provided to support the claims made about the WB method, which adds depth to the analysis.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments to validate their findings, which are supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence.
- The paper contributes to the understanding of the WB method by showing how it affects the Fisher's discriminant ratio (FDR) and other performance metrics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity and organization, making it difficult for readers to follow the arguments and the methodology used.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper does not adequately compare its findings with previous works.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods for the LTR problem, which limits the understanding of the WB method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the WB method, such as its applicability to different types of data or its effectiveness in various scenarios.
- There are several grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper, which detract from its professional quality.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the WB method, such as its potential for overfitting or its performance in scenarios with different data distributions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancy between the theoretical claims and the experimental results, particularly in the context of the WD component's impact on the FDR?
- How does the WB method perform in scenarios with different data distributions, and what are the implications for its effectiveness in such settings?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods for the LTR problem to better establish the WB method's advantages?
- In light of the findings on the WD component's impact on the FDR, how does the WB method compare to other methods that also use weight decay for feature learning?
- How do the authors address the limitations of the WB method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of data and its potential for overfitting?
