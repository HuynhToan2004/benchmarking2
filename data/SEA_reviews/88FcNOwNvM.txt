 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Decomp Diffusion, a novel method for compositional image decomposition that utilizes diffusion models to decompose images into various compositional concepts. This approach extends the COMET method by replacing the energy function with a denoising model, which is trained to predict the noise from an image given a set of latent factors. The method is demonstrated to outperform COMET in terms of image quality and disentanglement, as evidenced by both quantitative and qualitative results across multiple datasets. The paper also explores the potential of this method in generating novel images by combining factors from different datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for compositional image decomposition, which leverages the connection between energy-based models and diffusion models to achieve better image quality and disentanglement compared to COMET.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and easy to follow, making it a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments are well-designed and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method through both qualitative and quantitative results.
- The paper is well-written and easy to read, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.
- The potential applications of the method in generating novel images by combining factors from different datasets are highlighted, showing the versatility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the training process, particularly the optimization process of Equation 9, which could be improved by providing more details on the convergence of the optimization process and the impact of different learning rates.
- The method's generalizability is limited as it requires a fixed number of components, which might not be suitable for all datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with recent works on compositional image generation, such as those by Jiang et al. and Wu et al., which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper's focus on disentanglement is somewhat misleading as the results do not clearly demonstrate disentanglement, which is a critical aspect of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and potential areas for future research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the optimization process of Equation 9, including the convergence of the optimization process and the impact of different learning rates?
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent works on compositional image generation, such as those by Jiang et al. and Wu et al.?
- Could the authors clarify the concept of "compositional image decomposition" and its relationship to the "compositional image generation" discussed in other works?
- How does the method ensure the disentanglement of factors, especially in scenarios where the results do not clearly demonstrate this property?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of using different diffusion models on the performance of the method, and whether there are any constraints on the number of factors used?
