 **Summary:**
The paper introduces xMLP, a novel DNN architecture designed for private inference (PI) that utilizes square activation functions exclusively, aiming to enhance PI performance and efficiency. The architecture is based on a ViT-like structure, focusing on linear operations to minimize computational overhead. The authors claim that xMLP achieves superior accuracy and faster inference times compared to existing methods like ReLU, by leveraging the "information compounding" effect. The paper presents experiments on CIFAR-100 and ImageNet datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of xMLP in achieving better performance with fewer parameters and fewer activation layers. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty, insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and lack of clarity in certain methodological details.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel xMLP architecture that effectively replaces ReLU with square activation functions, demonstrating comparable performance and faster inference times.
- The analysis on the "information compounding" effect is insightful and provides a deeper understanding of the role of activation functions in neural networks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are convincing, showing that xMLP achieves better performance with fewer parameters and activation layers compared to ResNet.
- The proposed xMLP model demonstrates superior performance in terms of both accuracy and efficiency compared to existing methods like ReLU, setting a new standard for private inference.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the limitations of multiplication-based activation functions, offering valuable insights into their performance in neural networks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of accuracy and efficiency, which limits the ability to assess the true impact and novelty of the proposed xMLP architecture.
- The novelty of the proposed xMLP architecture is questioned as it resembles existing models like ResMLP and MLP-Mixer, and the use of square activation functions is not entirely novel.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other activation functions like GELU, which might also be suitable for private inference tasks.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodology, particularly in the definition and usage of certain terms and equations, which could hinder the understanding of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets and do not include a broader range of datasets or different types of tasks, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the xMLP architecture.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the novelty of the proposed xMLP architecture compared to existing models like ResMLP and MLP-Mixer?
- How does the xMLP architecture perform in terms of accuracy and efficiency when compared to state-of-the-art methods on larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet?
- Is there a specific reason for the choice of square activation functions over other activation functions like GELU, and how does xMLP compare in terms of accuracy and efficiency with these alternatives?
- Could you provide more details on the methodology, particularly the definitions and usage of terms and equations, to enhance the clarity and reproducibility of the research?
- How does the xMLP architecture handle different types of tasks beyond image classification, and what are the potential applications in other domains?
- Can you discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how they might affect its practical applicability?
