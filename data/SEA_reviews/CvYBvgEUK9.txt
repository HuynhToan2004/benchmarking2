 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the bilevel optimization problem with nonconvex lower-level problems, proposing a penalty method to address the challenge of finding a stationary point of the penalty function. The authors establish a connection between the penalty function and the hyper-objective, providing conditions under which the values and derivatives of the two are close. They propose a first-order algorithm to find a stationary point of the penalty function, which converges to a stationary point of the original problem under certain conditions. The paper also discusses the complexity of the proposed algorithm, showing that it is better than previous methods in certain scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in bilevel optimization, focusing on the nonconvex case which is often overlooked.
- The authors provide a novel analysis of the penalty method in the context of bilevel optimization, which is of independent interest and contributes to the understanding of the problem.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the assumptions, results, and proofs, making it accessible to readers.
- The algorithm proposed is simple and general, with potential for practical implementation in various large-scale scenarios.
- The paper presents a new landscape analysis for the penalty method in bilevel optimization, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks numerical experiments to validate the proposed algorithm, which could have demonstrated its effectiveness in practical scenarios.
- The assumptions used in the paper, such as the boundedness of the domains and the smoothness of the functions, are quite strong and may not hold in many practical applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the assumptions, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the complexity of the algorithms and the assumptions used.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of multiple solutions in the lower-level problem, which could lead to different hyper-objectives and complicate the analysis.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify whether the assumption of bounded domains (Assumption 4) is necessary for the proofs and whether it could be relaxed?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare in terms of computational complexity and convergence rate with other existing methods, especially in scenarios where the assumptions are not met?
- Could the authors provide a discussion on the practical applicability of the assumptions used in the paper, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions may not hold?
- In the context of the penalty method, how does the paper handle the issue of multiple solutions in the lower-level problem, and what implications does this have for the analysis and the results?
- Is there a possibility to extend the analysis to settings where the lower-level problem is nonconvex and the upper-level problem is convex?
- Could the authors provide a discussion on the limitations of their assumptions and the potential implications of these limitations on the practical applicability of their results?
