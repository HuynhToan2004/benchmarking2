 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) in animals, specifically addressing the challenge of multiple intentions in behavioral data. The authors propose a new algorithm, Latent (Markov) Variable Inverse Q-learning (LMV-IQL), which clusters trajectories into multiple intentions and solves the IRL problem independently for each cluster. This approach is evaluated using both simulated and real data, demonstrating its effectiveness in capturing animal intentions and providing interpretable reward functions. The method is designed to handle multiple intentions and time-varying rewards, which is crucial for understanding animal behavior in complex environments. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty and lack of comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to multiple intention inverse reinforcement learning (IRL), which is an important problem in understanding animal behavior.
- The method is evaluated on both simulated and real data, showing its effectiveness in capturing animal intentions and providing interpretable reward functions.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method.
- The use of EM for clustering trajectories and the application of IQL for each cluster is a novel approach that extends existing methods like IQL and DIRL.
- The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis and empirical results, demonstrating the method's ability to handle multiple intentions and time-varying rewards.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and detailed explanation of the differences between the proposed method and existing methods, particularly in terms of its contributions and novelty.
- There is a lack of comprehensive comparison with existing methods, which makes it difficult to assess the advantages of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a clear definition of the intentions and their transition dynamics, which could limit the understanding and applicability of the method.
- The method's scalability and generalizability are questionable, especially in complex environments where the number of intentions and their transitions are not well-defined.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as its assumptions about the number of intentions and the complexity of the behavioral data.
- The paper's reliance on known reward functions and its assumptions about the independence of intentions may not reflect real-world scenarios where intentions are interdependent.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and existing methods, particularly in terms of their contributions and novelty?
- How does the method handle the scalability and generalizability issues, especially in complex environments with many potential intentions?
- Can the authors provide more details on the transition dynamics of intentions and how these are defined and estimated in the method?
- How does the method perform when the number of intentions is not known, or when there are interdependent intentions?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the method, such as its assumptions about the independence of intentions and the known reward functions?
- How does the method compare with other methods that use parametric or Bayesian non-parametric approaches for handling multiple intentions?
