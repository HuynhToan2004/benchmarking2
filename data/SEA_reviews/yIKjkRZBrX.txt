 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for learning variable-length skills in reinforcement learning through a state-action novelty module. This module identifies decision points by leveraging offline agent experience datasets, which are used to enhance exploration and knowledge transfer across various tasks. The methodology involves a termination distribution, skill embedding space, and skill prior, all optimized using a deep latent variable model. The paper presents experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach in complex, long-horizon tasks such as robotic manipulation and maze navigation. However, the paper's methodology and presentation have been criticized for not clearly distinguishing between different components of the method, and for not providing sufficient evidence to support the claims made.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning variable-length skills through the identification of decision points, which is a significant advancement in the field of reinforcement learning.
- The methodology is well-explained and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is evaluated on complex, long-horizon tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in real-world applications.
- The use of state-action novelty to identify decision points is a novel and innovative approach that could potentially lead to significant improvements in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in distinguishing between different components of the method, such as the termination distribution, skill embedding space, and skill prior, which could lead to confusion.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with the performance of the proposed method not significantly better than existing methods like SPiRL.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims made, such as the effectiveness of the proposed method in identifying decision points and the improvement in exploration and knowledge transfer.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with figures and diagrams being difficult to understand and the methodology being overly complex and difficult to follow.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, such as the potential for overfitting to the training data and the lack of scalability in complex environments.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the distinction between the termination distribution, skill embedding space, and skill prior, and how they contribute to the overall method?
- How does the proposed method ensure that it identifies the correct decision points, and what are the implications if it fails to do so?
- Can the authors provide more detailed evidence to support the claims made about the effectiveness of the method in enhancing exploration and knowledge transfer?
- How does the method handle the complexity of identifying decision points in environments with high-dimensional state and action spaces?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the method, such as scalability and the risk of overfitting, and propose strategies to mitigate these issues?
