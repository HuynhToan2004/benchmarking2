 **Summary:**
The paper introduces H-Rockmate, a hierarchical re-materialization framework for neural networks that aims to optimize memory usage and training efficiency by recursively decomposing data-flow graphs into smaller, manageable subgraphs. This approach, based on a hierarchical partitioning and linear programming formulation, allows for the efficient computation of re-materialization schedules that minimize computational overhead within a given memory budget. The methodology is evaluated against existing approaches like Checkmate and Rockmate, demonstrating comparable performance and memory efficiency. Despite its potential, the paper is critiqued for its incremental nature, limited novelty, and lack of extensive experimental validation against a broader range of models and memory budgets.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is intuitive and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the hierarchical approach.
- The paper is well-written, with a good flow of ideas and clear explanations of the proposed method and its evaluation.
- The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in training iteration time and memory efficiency compared to baseline methods.
- The approach is original in its application of hierarchical decomposition to general neural network architectures, which is not typically addressed in existing methods.
- The method is flexible and can be combined with other re-materialization strategies, enhancing its applicability to various scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other existing methods, particularly in terms of the number of models and memory budgets tested.
- The novelty of the method is limited, as it primarily builds upon existing techniques like hierarchical decomposition and linear programming.
- The complexity analysis and the scalability of the proposed method are not adequately addressed, raising concerns about its applicability to larger and more complex neural networks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of training performance or the quality of the trained models, which is crucial for evaluating the practical impact of the proposed method.
- The method's performance is not consistently superior to existing methods across all memory budgets and model sizes, which limits its general applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the performance of the method at different memory budgets, particularly at low and high budgets?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of training performance and model quality to other existing methods like Checkmate and Rockmate?
- Could the authors clarify the complexity analysis and the scalability of the proposed method, especially in relation to the size and complexity of the neural networks tested?
- What are the specific advantages of the hierarchical approach over other decomposition methods, and how does it compare in terms of computational efficiency and memory utilization?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational overhead and memory usage when scaling to larger and more complex neural networks?
