 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new approach to quantum variational algorithms (VQAs) by focusing on Hamming Weight (HW) preserving ansatz, which is a subspace of the full Hilbert space. This subspace is characterized by a specific Hamming weight, providing a constraint that can be beneficial for quantum computing. The authors propose a new gate, the Beam-Splitter (BS) gate, which is designed to operate within this subspace, potentially addressing issues such as barren plateaus and increasing the expressivity of the VQA. The paper also explores the use of this gate in practical applications, such as ground state energy estimation and feature selection, demonstrating its potential advantages over traditional methods. However, the paper's experimental results are somewhat limited, and the practical applicability of the proposed methods in larger, more complex systems remains unclear.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to quantum variational algorithms (VQAs) by focusing on Hamming Weight (HW) preserving ansatz, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis on the capability, expressivity, and trainability of HW preserving ansatz, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear definitions and explanations of key concepts such as Hamming weight and dynamical Lie algebra.
- The proposed method is shown to be effective in practical applications, such as ground state energy estimation and feature selection, demonstrating its potential utility.
- The paper presents a new gate, the BS gate, which is designed to operate within the HW preserving subspace, potentially addressing issues such as barren plateaus and increasing the expressivity of the VQA.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental results are somewhat limited, with the largest system considered having only 16 qubits. This limits the practical applicability of the proposed methods in larger, more complex systems.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implementation of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of hardware efficiency and scalability.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed methods with existing state-of-the-art methods, which could help to establish their relative advantages or disadvantages.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed methods over existing ones are not adequately supported by experimental evidence.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, which could help to provide a more balanced view of their strengths and weaknesses.
