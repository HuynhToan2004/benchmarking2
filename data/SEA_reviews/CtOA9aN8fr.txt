 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for pruning large-scale datasets used in training multimodal models, specifically focusing on the LAION dataset. The approach involves a three-step process: deduplication, CLIP-score filtering, and density-based pruning. The authors propose a new complexity measure to determine the number of samples to retain from each cluster, which is then used to prune the dataset. This method is shown to improve performance and reduce training costs by up to 75% compared to full-dataset training. The paper also includes experiments on the DataComp Medium benchmark, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on ImageNet zero-shot accuracy.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive analysis of the proposed method.
- The experiments conducted are extensive, covering various datasets and models, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is original in its approach to dataset pruning, which is a significant problem in the field of large-scale multimodal model training.
- The proposed method is shown to improve performance over existing pruning methods, such as SSP-Pruning, and can be applied to different models and datasets.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including a comparison with other pruning methods and an ablation study on hyperparameters.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the number of clusters used in the k-means clustering.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definition and computation of the complexity measure used in the density-based pruning, which could benefit from more detailed explanations or examples.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance metrics such as training time and memory usage.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed experimental results, especially on datasets other than LAION, to demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in the context of large-scale model training.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation or examples of how the complexity measure is computed in the density-based pruning?
- How do the authors choose the number of clusters used in the k-means clustering, and what are the implications of this choice on the performance and generalizability of the method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training time and memory usage of the proposed method compared to other pruning methods?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance with other state-of-the-art methods on datasets other than LAION?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these could be mitigated?
