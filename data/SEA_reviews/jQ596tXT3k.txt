 **Summary:**
The paper explores the phenomenon where deep generative models (DGMs) assign higher likelihoods to out-of-distribution (OOD) data from simpler sources, despite not generating such samples. The authors propose a novel approach using local intrinsic dimension (LID) estimation to detect OOD data. This method is validated against various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in detecting OOD data, particularly in scenarios where DGMs exhibit high likelihoods for OOD data. The paper also introduces a method for estimating LID from a normalizing flow model, which enhances the detection performance. However, the paper is limited by its focus on normalizing flows and its evaluation on smaller datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to detecting out-of-distribution (OOD) data using likelihood peaks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it accessible and understandable, with a clear presentation of the proposed solution.
- The empirical results are robust, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and the experimental setup, which enhances reproducibility.
- The theoretical framework is well-grounded, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on normalizing flows, which may limit its applicability to other types of deep generative models.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to smaller datasets, which might not fully demonstrate its effectiveness on larger, more complex datasets.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced perspective.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods, such as those using typical set hypotheses, which could lead to confusion about the novelty of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the time and memory requirements?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use typical set hypotheses for OOD detection?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, particularly in terms of its theoretical underpinnings and practical applications?
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of the "typical set" in the context of OOD detection, and how it relates to the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the OOD data might be significantly different from the training data, such as in the case of images with different resolutions or modalities?
