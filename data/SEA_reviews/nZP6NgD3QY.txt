 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Adaptive Model Merging (AdaMerging), a novel method for merging pre-trained models in multi-task learning without the need for original training data. AdaMerging employs entropy minimization on unlabeled test samples to iteratively refine merging coefficients, either task-wise or layer-wise, which can be used to merge models effectively. This approach is validated through experiments on eight tasks, demonstrating significant performance improvements over existing methods. The methodology is supported by theoretical insights and empirical evidence, showcasing its effectiveness and potential for application in various domains.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The proposed method, AdaMerging, is innovative and addresses a significant problem in multi-task learning by automatically learning merging coefficients, which can be applied in both task-wise and layer-wise merging scenarios.
- The methodology is supported by both theoretical insights and empirical evidence, demonstrating its effectiveness through extensive experiments on eight tasks.
- The paper provides a strong positive correlation between entropy minimization and loss minimization on MTL's test data, enhancing the understanding of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, including comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and demonstrations of improved performance, generalization, and robustness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as it resembles existing methods such as task arithmetic and Fisher merging, with only minor modifications.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts.
- The experimental setup and results are limited to specific datasets and architectures, which may not generalize well to other scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with a broader range of existing methods, including some state-of-the-art approaches.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the number of tasks and layers?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger and more complex datasets, such as those used in large-scale benchmarks like ImageNet or COCO?
- Could the authors clarify the role of entropy minimization in the merging process and how it impacts the overall performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts?
