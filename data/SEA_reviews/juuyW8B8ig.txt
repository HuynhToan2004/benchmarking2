 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for learning language-informed visual concepts by distilling from pre-trained vision-language models. It proposes a framework for learning disentangled visual concepts by training a set of concept encoders to extract visual characteristics of input images along specified concept axes. The encoders are trained using a reconstruction loss and an anchor loss to ensure concept disentanglement. The method is evaluated on several datasets and shown to outperform baseline methods in terms of concept disentanglement and compositionality. The paper also discusses the limitations and potential applications of the proposed method, suggesting it as a useful tool for generating images with novel concept compositions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow descriptions of the proposed method.
- The idea of using a set of concept encoders to learn language-informed visual concepts from images is novel and interesting.
- The method is simple, effective, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and explanation of the approach.
- The paper includes a variety of experiments and ablation studies to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing better disentanglement and compositionality compared to text-based prompting baselines.
- The method can be applied to various domains, such as art, furniture, and clothing, and can be generalized to unseen concepts at test time.
- The paper includes comprehensive experiments and comparisons with baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in generating images with novel concept compositions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as the dependence on the pre-trained vision-language models and the need for a large number of training images.
- There is a lack of quantitative evaluations to support the claims of disentanglement and compositionality, and the paper does not provide metrics for the generalization to unseen concepts.
- The method's reliance on the pre-trained vision-language models might limit its applicability in scenarios where such models are not available or not applicable.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting to input images, which could affect the generalization ability of the model.
- The method's effectiveness is limited by the quality of the training data, which is primarily synthetic images generated by a pre-trained T2I model.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, including potential biases and limitations in the generalization to unseen concepts.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method, such as its dependence on pre-trained vision-language models and the need for a large number of training images?
- How does the method perform when applied to real-world images, and what are the implications for its generalization ability?
- Can the authors provide more quantitative evaluations to support the claims of disentanglement and compositionality, and metrics for the generalization to unseen concepts?
- How does the method handle the issue of overfitting to input images, and what steps are taken to prevent this?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training data generation process, including the quality and diversity of the generated images?
- How does the method compare to other methods that use different types of pre-trained models or different training strategies?
- Can the authors provide more examples of the method's application in real-world scenarios, and discuss potential applications or use cases for the proposed method?
