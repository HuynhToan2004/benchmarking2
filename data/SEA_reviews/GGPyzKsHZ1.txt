 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FLAVA, a novel method designed for continual audio-video representation learning, addressing the challenges of sparse spatiotemporal correlation and representational forgetting of audio-video relationships. FLAVA employs a localized alignment strategy to predict audio-visual tokens that are well-aligned, mitigating the risk of forgetting previously learned audio-video correlations. This methodology is tested on various datasets and shown to outperform existing continual learning methods. The paper also introduces a forget-robust multimodal patch selection mechanism to manage the dynamic shift in audio-visual data distribution over time. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its complex presentation and lack of comprehensive experimental validation across diverse datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the important problem of continual audio-video representation learning, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The proposed method, FLAVA, is novel and effectively addresses the challenges of sparse spatiotemporal correlation and representational forgetting of audio-video relationships.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed methodology.
- The experimental results demonstrate that FLAVA outperforms existing continual learning methods on various benchmark datasets, showcasing its effectiveness.
- The introduction of a forget-robust multimodal patch selection mechanism is a significant contribution to the field, enhancing the robustness of the model against forgetting.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation across diverse datasets, limiting the generalizability of the results.
- The complexity of the proposed method, including the need for rehearsal memory and the use of multiple modules, may make it less practical for real-world applications.
- The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of each component of the proposed method, such as the rehearsal memory size and the impact of different sampling strategies.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to replicate or fully understand the results.
- There are inconsistencies and unclear descriptions in the paper, such as the use of terms like "task" and "category," which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of rehearsal memory in the performance of FLAVA?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other continual learning tasks, such as text-to-image or image-to-text generation?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations and examples to illustrate the use of the AVM module and its impact on the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical deployment in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more comprehensive ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of each component of the proposed method?
- How does the paper address the limitations mentioned, such as the generalizability of the results and the practical deployment of the proposed method?
