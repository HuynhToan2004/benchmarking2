 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the scalability issues in recommendation systems by focusing on the embedding collapse phenomenon, which is characterized by low-rank embedding matrices. This issue, attributed to feature interactions, is analyzed through theoretical and empirical evidence, showing that scaling up embedding sizes does not significantly improve performance. The authors propose a multi-embedding design to address this problem, which involves incorporating embedding-set-specific interaction modules to capture diverse patterns and reduce embedding collapse. The paper presents empirical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach in improving scalability across various recommendation models. However, the paper's novelty and the depth of theoretical analysis are questioned, with suggestions for broader experimental validation and a more rigorous discussion on the implications of embedding collapse.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed multi-embedding design is simple yet effective, and the analysis of embedding collapse is insightful, offering a novel approach to mitigate the collapse phenomenon.
- The paper is supported by a strong theoretical analysis and empirical results, which validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The findings and conclusions are well-supported by experiments, and the proposed multi-embedding design is simple and easy to implement.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the embedding collapse phenomenon, which is a significant issue in recommendation systems, and proposes a novel method to address it.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of embedding collapse, particularly in relation to the scalability of recommendation systems.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned, as similar ideas have been explored in other works, such as those using multiple embedding tables.
- The theoretical analysis in Section 4.1 is considered weak and could be improved by incorporating more relevant theoretical frameworks or by providing a more rigorous analysis.
- The experimental setup could be expanded to include more datasets and a broader range of models to validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods more comprehensively.
- The paper lacks a deeper analysis of the causes of embedding collapse, which could enhance the understanding and practical application of the findings.
- There are some minor issues with the presentation and clarity of the paper, which could be addressed to improve the overall quality and impact.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on the implications of embedding collapse on the scalability of recommendation systems?
- How does the multi-embedding design compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical implementation?
- What are the specific causes of embedding collapse, and how do these contribute to the observed phenomena?
- Could you provide more detailed experimental results, including standard deviations and a broader range of datasets, to better validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods?
- How does the multi-embedding design address the issue of embedding collapse, and what are the theoretical underpinnings of this approach?
- Could you clarify the definition and usage of terms such as "information abundance" and "embedding collapse" to ensure a clearer understanding of these concepts?
