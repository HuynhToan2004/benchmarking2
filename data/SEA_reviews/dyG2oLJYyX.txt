 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DiffusionNAG, a novel framework for Neural Architecture Search (NAS) using diffusion models. It proposes a method that generates neural architectures based on task-specific requirements, leveraging a predictor to guide the diffusion process. The framework is validated through extensive experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness in scenarios like Transferable NAS and Bayesian Optimization (BO)-based NAS. Key features include the use of a score network to ensure the validity of generated architectures and the ability to adapt to different tasks without retraining the diffusion model. Despite its innovative approach, concerns are raised about the novelty and the depth of experimental validation, particularly in terms of the comparison with existing methods and the scalability of the approach to larger and more complex search spaces.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to Neural Architecture Search (NAS) by integrating diffusion models, which is a significant shift from traditional methods.
- The proposed method is well-motivated and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow.
- The use of a diffusion model for NAS is innovative, and the paper demonstrates its effectiveness through extensive experiments.
- The method's ability to generate valid architectures and adapt to different tasks without retraining the diffusion model is a notable advantage.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the proposed method, which aids in understanding the approach and its implementation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as it appears to be a direct application of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency.
- The scalability of the method to larger and more complex search spaces is not demonstrated, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim of generating architectures that follow the distribution of the search space, which is a critical aspect of the proposed method.
- The method's dependence on the accuracy of the predictor for guidance raises concerns about its robustness and the potential for overfitting to the training set.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method, including the time and resource requirements for training and inference?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing NAS methods in terms of performance and efficiency, particularly in scenarios like Transferable NAS and BO-based NAS?
- Can the authors demonstrate the scalability of the method to larger and more complex search spaces, such as those used in large-scale NAS benchmarks?
- How does the predictor guide the diffusion process, and what are the implications of using a predictor trained on a specific dataset for guiding the search?
- Could the authors provide more evidence to support the claim of generating architectures that follow the distribution of the search space?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting to the training set, and what measures are in place to ensure robustness across different datasets?
