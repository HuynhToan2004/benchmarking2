 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework, FedDPCont, designed to address the challenges of federated learning with local openset noisy labels. This framework employs a label communication mechanism to share contrastive labels, protecting privacy through label differential privacy (DP). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing that their method outperforms existing solutions in terms of performance and privacy protection. The paper also provides theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence, highlighting the robustness of the approach to label noise.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and concise explanations of the proposed methodology and the challenges addressed.
- The authors have provided theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach, showing that it outperforms existing solutions in terms of performance and privacy protection.
- The paper is original in its approach to addressing the issue of local openset noisy labels in federated learning, a problem that has not been extensively studied before.
- The proposed label communication mechanism is novel and theoretically sound, with a focus on privacy protection through label differential privacy.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions and explanations of the problem setting and the proposed method.
- The empirical results are robust and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and noise conditions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed approach, particularly in scenarios where the noise level is high or the data distribution is highly imbalanced.
- The theoretical analysis of the proposed method is insufficient, and there is a need for a more thorough discussion on the technical challenges and the novelty of the proposed method compared to existing solutions.
- The paper does not adequately address the practicality of the proposed method in scenarios where data is highly imbalanced or the noise level is high, which could affect the performance and applicability of the method.
- There are some typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected, such as inconsistencies in the use of symbols and formatting issues in the tables.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other existing methods that address similar issues in federated learning, such as FedBN and FedDyn.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed approach and how these might affect the performance in scenarios with high noise levels or imbalanced data distributions?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing solutions that address similar issues in federated learning, such as FedBN and FedDyn?
- In the context of federated learning, how does the proposed method ensure that the shared label information is sufficiently informative and does not compromise privacy too much?
- Can the authors clarify the technical challenges and the novelty of their proposed method compared to existing solutions in the field of noisy label learning?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the noise level is high, and what are the implications for the performance and applicability of the method in such scenarios?
