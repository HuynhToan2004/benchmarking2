 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel opponent modeling method named OMG (Opponent Modeling based on Subgoal inference), which focuses on predicting the subgoals of opponents in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) environments. This approach contrasts traditional methods that primarily predict opponent actions, by instead inferring the subgoals from historical trajectories. The OMG method utilizes a subgoal selector and a subgoal inference model, employing variational auto-encoders to infer subgoals. The method is evaluated in cooperative and general-sum games, showing promising results against various baselines. However, the paper suffers from several presentation issues, including unclear definitions and notations, lack of detailed experimental results, and insufficient discussion on the method's limitations and societal impacts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to opponent modeling by predicting subgoals, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that focus on predicting opponent actions.
- The method is well-motivated, with a clear rationale for focusing on subgoals rather than actions, and is supported by empirical results that demonstrate its effectiveness.
- The paper is generally well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental results.
- The use of variational inference for subgoal inference is a novel and interesting approach, providing a new perspective on opponent modeling.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various scenarios, including cooperative and general-sum games.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from several presentation issues, including unclear notations and definitions, which can make it difficult for readers to understand the methodology and results.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related works, particularly those that have also focused on subgoal modeling in multi-agent systems.
- The experimental results are not adequately presented, with figures and data lacking clarity and depth, which makes it difficult to draw robust conclusions from the results.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the societal impact of the proposed method, which is an important consideration for any technological advancement.
- The methodology, particularly the use of variational auto-encoders and the subgoal selector, is not sufficiently explained, leaving some aspects unclear or confusing.
