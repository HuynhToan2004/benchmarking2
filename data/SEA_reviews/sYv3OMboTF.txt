 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ExcelFormer, a novel neural network architecture designed for tabular data classification and regression tasks, particularly for small-scale datasets. It incorporates a semi-permeable attention module and interaction-attenuated initialization to mitigate the impact of uninformative features and disrupt rotational invariance. Additionally, it proposes two data augmentation techniques, Hid-Mix and Feat-Mix, to enhance training sample diversity. The model is evaluated against various benchmarks and shows promising results, outperforming existing methods in many cases. However, concerns about the novelty of the approach, the complexity of the model, and the generalization to larger datasets are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the problem of tabular data prediction.
- The proposed ExcelFormer model and data augmentation techniques (Hid-Mix and Feat-Mix) are novel and well-motivated, specifically designed for tabular data.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, showing superior performance over existing methods in many cases.
- The paper provides detailed ablation studies, which help in understanding the impact of different components of the model.
- The semi-permeable attention module and interaction-attenuated initialization are novel and effective, providing a new approach to handling uninformative features in tabular data.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and efficiency of the model, which could be crucial for practical deployment on small devices.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as similar methods like SPA and IAI have been previously explored, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions.
- The generalization of the model to larger datasets is not thoroughly tested, raising concerns about its scalability.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of overfitting on small datasets, which is a common challenge in tabular data prediction.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant works, such as those that use self-supervised learning or other advanced techniques for tabular data.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the training time and memory usage of the proposed model, which could be critical for practical deployment.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and efficiency of the model?
- How does the model handle the issue of overfitting on small datasets, and what measures are taken to prevent this?
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to previous works, particularly those that have explored similar methods?
- How does the model perform when compared to more recent and relevant works, such as those that use self-supervised learning or other advanced techniques for tabular data?
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison of the training time and memory usage of the proposed model with other state-of-the-art methods?
- How does the model handle the issue of rotational invariance in tabular data, and what are the implications of this design choice?
