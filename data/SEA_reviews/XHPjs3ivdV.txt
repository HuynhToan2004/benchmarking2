 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the performance of various active learning algorithms, specifically focusing on margin sampling techniques, across 69 tabular datasets using deep neural networks. It compares these methods against other state-of-the-art techniques and demonstrates that margin sampling often matches or outperforms these methods. The study also examines the impact of pre-training and different batch sizes on the effectiveness of margin sampling. Despite the extensive evaluation, the paper has been criticized for its lack of novelty, limited scope to non-tabular data, and insufficient theoretical insights.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive empirical study on active learning using margin sampling, comparing it with other state-of-the-art techniques across a diverse set of datasets.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments, covering a wide range of datasets and models, which is beneficial for understanding the performance of margin sampling in practical scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results are well-organized and presented, with detailed analysis and visualizations that aid in understanding the findings.
- The paper is significant in its contribution to the active learning community by providing a thorough analysis of margin sampling, which is often overlooked in favor of more complex methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it primarily compares margin sampling with existing methods without introducing new insights or theoretical contributions.
- The scope of the study is limited to tabular data, which may not generalize well to other types of data such as image or text data.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the computational costs of different methods, which is crucial for practitioners making decisions based on resource constraints.
- There is a lack of theoretical insights into the effectiveness of margin sampling, which could enhance the understanding of its performance.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of margin sampling, such as its potential to overfit or its effectiveness in different data regimes.
- The experimental setup and methodology, particularly the choice of datasets and hyperparameters, could be improved to better reflect real-world scenarios and to avoid potential biases.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the choice of datasets and hyperparameters to ensure that the results are not biased towards specific methods or data characteristics?
- How does the performance of margin sampling vary with different pre-training methods, and what are the implications of using different pre-training strategies?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational costs of different methods to help practitioners make informed decisions based on resource availability?
- How does the paper address the potential overfitting of margin sampling, especially in scenarios where the data is noisy or the model is under-specified?
- Could the authors provide more theoretical insights into the effectiveness of margin sampling, particularly in terms of its convergence guarantees or its ability to adapt to different data distributions?
- How does the paper ensure that the results are statistically significant and not due to random fluctuations or bias in the experimental setup?
