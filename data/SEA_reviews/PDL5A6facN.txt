 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of Nesterov's accelerated gradient (NAG) method to policy gradient (PG) in reinforcement learning (RL), proposing a new algorithm termed Accelerated Policy Gradient (APG). The authors demonstrate that APG can achieve a convergence rate of O(1/t^2) under specific conditions, which is faster than the standard PG's O(1/t) rate. Theoretical analysis supports this claim, and numerical experiments are conducted to validate the theoretical findings. The paper also discusses the practical implications of the proposed method, including the potential for improved convergence rates and the generalization of the method to other policy gradient variants.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by integrating Nesterov's accelerated gradient (NAG) with policy gradient (PG) in reinforcement learning (RL), which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, including proofs of the convergence rate of O(1/t^2), which is a significant improvement over the standard PG.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear and concise explanations that aid in understanding the complex theoretical concepts.
- The paper is significant as it fills a gap in the literature by being the first to formally analyze the convergence of NAG in the context of RL, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of optimization algorithms in this domain.
- The authors have provided a comprehensive discussion on the challenges and limitations of their approach, which adds depth and credibility to their research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more extensive numerical experiments to validate the theoretical claims and compare the performance of APG with other existing methods under different conditions.
- The paper primarily focuses on the tabular setting, which might limit the generalizability of the findings to more complex or continuous settings.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are somewhat limited by the restricted policy parameterization and the lack of a detailed discussion on the practical implications and limitations of the proposed method.
- There are some minor issues with the presentation and clarity of certain sections, which could be improved for better comprehension.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition of the sub-optimality gap and its relation to the convergence rate as stated in Theorem 1?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods, such as those mentioned in related works, in terms of convergence rate and practical performance?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications and limitations of the proposed method, particularly in real-world applications?
- In the context of the proofs and theoretical results, can the authors provide more detailed explanations or clarifications to enhance understanding?
- How does the proposed method handle the challenges and limitations discussed in the paper, such as the unbounded optimal parameter and the non-concavity of the RL objective?
