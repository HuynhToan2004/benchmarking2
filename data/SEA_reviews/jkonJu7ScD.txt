 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called masked and inverse dynamics modeling (MIND) for enhancing data efficiency in deep reinforcement learning (DRL) by learning agent-controllable representations in changing states. MIND leverages a transformer architecture to capture spatio-temporal information from consecutive frames and performs self-supervised multi-task learning through masked modeling and inverse dynamics modeling. These tasks aim to learn static and rapidly evolving state representations, respectively. The method was tested across various Atari and DMControl environments, demonstrating improved performance and data efficiency compared to existing methods. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the paper's presentation, the lack of detailed comparative analysis, and the limited scope of the experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach by combining masked modeling and inverse dynamics modeling, which is innovative and potentially beneficial for data-efficient reinforcement learning (RL).
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the proposed method and its components, which aids in understanding the approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are comprehensive, covering a wide range of environments.
- The proposed method shows promising results in improving data efficiency in deep RL, which is a significant challenge in the field.
- The paper is well-motivated and provides a clear motivation for the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its relevance and impact.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the description of the methodology and the results. This includes unclear figures and descriptions, and inconsistencies in the presentation of results.
- There is a lack of detailed comparative analysis with existing methods, which makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental details, such as the specifics of the hyperparameters used and the training conditions, which could affect the reproducibility and evaluation of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the related works, which could provide a better context for the research and highlight the novelty of the contributions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the description of the methodology, particularly the roles and interactions of the different components like the online and target networks, the predictor, and the masking and inverse dynamics modeling?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of data efficiency and performance? Can the authors provide a more detailed comparative analysis?
- What are the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments, and how were they chosen? How do these parameters affect the performance of the method?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex or diverse environments, such as those with higher dimensionality or more dynamic elements?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training conditions, such as the number of seeds used and the stability of the training process?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of environments, such as those with different visual or action spaces?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and how these could be addressed in future work?
