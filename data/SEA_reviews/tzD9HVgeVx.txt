 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AgentMixer, a novel multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) framework aimed at achieving correlated policy factorization. This framework is designed to address the challenge of asymmetric learning failure by jointly training centralized and decentralized policies, utilizing a mechanism called Individual-Global-Consistency (IGC) to ensure mode consistency between policies. AgentMixer is theoretically proven to converge to an epsilon-approximate Correlated Equilibrium and is evaluated on various MARL benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness. The paper also discusses the challenges of learning under partial observability and the importance of decentralized execution in MARL.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology, which is a significant strength in academic research.
- The concept of using the Policy Modifier (PM) and Individual-Global-Consistency (IGC) to address the challenges of asymmetric learning failure is innovative and well-explained.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis, which is crucial for understanding the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed methods.
- The use of a novel policy modifier and the Individual-Global-Consistency (IGC) mechanism to mitigate asymmetric learning failure is a significant contribution to the field of multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL).
- The experimental results are strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods in various MARL benchmarks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the proposed methods compare to existing works in the field, which could help in positioning the research within the current body of knowledge.
- There is a need for more clarity and explanation in certain sections, particularly in the definitions and notations used, which could make the paper more accessible to a broader audience.
- The paper's experimental setup and results could benefit from a more detailed analysis, including comparisons with other relevant baselines and a more comprehensive discussion on the results.
- The paper's focus on fully cooperative multi-agent games may limit its applicability to more general scenarios, which could be addressed by extending the scope of the research.
- There are some issues with the paper's presentation, such as inconsistent notation and unclear definitions, which could detract from the overall quality of the manuscript.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the term "asymmetric learning failure" and provide examples or scenarios where this issue is most prevalent?
- What are the implications of using a Gaussian distribution for the policy in continuous action spaces, and how does this affect the overall performance of the model?
- How does the paper address the issue of identifiability, and what are the assumptions made regarding the optimal joint policy and the corresponding implicit product policy?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational costs associated with the proposed methods, particularly in terms of memory and time requirements?
- How does the paper ensure that the policies learned by AgentMixer are optimal, and what are the implications of this in terms of the overall performance of the model?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the environments used and the parameters of the baselines for comparison?
