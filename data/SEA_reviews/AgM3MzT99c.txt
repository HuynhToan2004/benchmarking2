 **Summary:**
The paper introduces OMNI, a novel approach that leverages large language models (LLMs) to determine interestingness in open-ended reinforcement learning (RL) environments. OMNI utilizes LLMs to filter tasks based on their learning progress and interestingness, aiming to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of RL agents in learning new tasks. The methodology is evaluated across various environments, including Crafter, BabyAI, and AI2-THOR, showing improvements over uniform sampling and learning progress-based methods. However, the paper faces criticisms for its lack of clarity in defining "interestingness," the limited evaluation on diverse tasks, and the potential for overfitting and bias in LLM-based task selection.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative use of large language models (LLMs) to predict interestingness in open-ended reinforcement learning (RL), which is a novel approach that has not been explored extensively in prior work.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the problem, method, and evaluation, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear structure and comprehensive experimental design, which supports the robustness of the findings.
- The use of LLMs to determine interestingness is a promising direction for future research, as it leverages the models' ability to understand human preferences and behaviors.
- The paper addresses an important problem in open-ended learning, which is the selection of tasks that are both learnable and interesting, and provides a novel solution using LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining "interestingness," which is a critical aspect of the proposed method. The term is used throughout the paper without a clear definition, which could lead to confusion and misunderstanding.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a few tasks, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the approach. The tasks chosen are not diverse enough to show the method's effectiveness across different domains.
- The use of LLMs to determine interestingness could lead to overfitting and bias, as the models might be trained on biased or incomplete data. This could affect the reliability and validity of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for overfitting or bias in the LLM-based task selection, which could limit the practical applicability of the method.
- The method's reliance on LLMs for task selection could limit its applicability in domains where LLMs do not perform well, such as in zero-shot or few-shot settings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in relation to the potential biases and overfitting issues.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the LLM determines interestingness and whether this is based on a predefined set of criteria or learned from the data used?
- How does the paper address the potential for overfitting and bias in the LLM-based task selection?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the number of tasks used and the methodology for selecting interesting tasks?
- How does the paper ensure that the LLM's predictions of interestingness are not influenced by biases in the training data?
- Could you elaborate on the methodology for calculating the success rates of tasks, especially the use of EMA and the impact of this on the learning progress?
- How does the method perform when the LLM is not available or when the model's predictions are not reliable?
