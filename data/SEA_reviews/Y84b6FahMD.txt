 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the challenge of achieving counterfactual fairness in machine learning when the true causal graph is unknown, focusing on partially directed acyclic graphs (PDAGs). It proposes a min-max optimization framework that utilizes all variables, including descendants of the sensitive attribute, to estimate counterfactual treatment effects. The framework is designed to control fairness violations and optimize prediction accuracy. The approach is validated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in achieving counterfactual fairness while maintaining high prediction accuracy. The paper also discusses the limitations of the approach, including the potential for inaccuracies in causal discovery algorithms and the reliance on certain assumptions about the causal graph.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to counterfactual fairness using descendants of the sensitive attribute, which is a significant advancement over previous methods that only used non-descendants.
- The min-max optimization framework is well-explained and supported by extensive experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness in achieving counterfactual fairness while maintaining high prediction accuracy.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and illustrative examples that aid in understanding the complex concepts.
- The proposed algorithm is general and can be applied to various types of causal graphs, including CPDAGs and MPDAGs, making it a versatile tool for different scenarios.
- The paper includes a comprehensive literature review, highlighting its relevance and depth in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies heavily on the accuracy of causal discovery algorithms, which may not always be perfect, potentially leading to inaccuracies in the estimation of counterfactual treatment effects.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the paper, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key concepts such as adjustment sets and propensities.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the approach, including the potential for inaccuracies in the causal graph estimation and the reliance on certain assumptions about the causal graph.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing methods, which could help in understanding the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis on the convergence of the proposed algorithm, which is crucial for understanding its reliability and applicability in different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of adjustment sets in the context of counterfactual fairness?
- How does the proposed algorithm handle scenarios where the causal graph is not a PDAG but a DAG?
- What is the impact of the background knowledge on the performance of the algorithm, and how is this knowledge obtained in real-world scenarios?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a discussion on how their approach differs and potentially improves upon these methods?
- How does the algorithm handle the estimation of counterfactual treatment effects when the causal graph is inaccurately estimated?
- Could the authors provide a theoretical analysis on the convergence of the proposed algorithm, and discuss the assumptions required for its applicability?
