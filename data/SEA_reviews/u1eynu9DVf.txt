 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of catastrophic forgetting in language models (LMs) during fine-tuning, focusing on the prediction of forgotten examples in the pre-training dataset. The authors propose two models: a logit-based model that utilizes kernel matrices and a representation-based model that employs a trainable encoder. These models aim to predict whether examples will be forgotten based on interactions between examples, which is crucial for reducing forgetting during model refinement. The study includes extensive experiments on various LMs and datasets, demonstrating that the proposed methods can effectively forecast forgotten examples and improve model refinement. However, the paper has been criticized for its lack of novelty, clarity in presentation, and insufficient comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in model refinement, specifically focusing on the prediction of forgotten examples in pre-training datasets, which is a novel approach.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the experimental setup, methodologies, and results.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods in reducing forgetting during model refinement.
- The results are presented in a clear and organized manner, making it easy for readers to follow and understand the findings.
- The paper introduces a novel problem setup and proposes two interpretable forecasting models to forecast example forgetting, which are both innovative and relevant to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the problem setup, and the significance of forecasting forgotten examples is not convincingly argued.
- The novelty of the proposed methods is questionable as similar ideas have been explored in previous works, such as the use of kernel matrices for logit change estimation.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, especially in the figures and tables.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and the potential negative societal impacts of the research.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a discussion on the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed methods over these alternatives.
- The paper suffers from inconsistencies and potential errors in the presentation, such as missing definitions and unclear explanations in the methodology and results sections.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the motivation behind the problem setup and provide a more convincing argument for the significance of forecasting forgotten examples?
- How do the proposed methods compare in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness with other existing methods, such as those that use different forecasting models or different training methods?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the research, and how do the authors address these concerns?
- Can the authors provide more details on the methodology and results sections, particularly in terms of the definitions and explanations used?
- How does the paper handle the limitations of the proposed methods, and what are the potential avenues for future research to address these limitations?
