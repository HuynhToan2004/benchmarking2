 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of ReLU activation functions in Large Language Models (LLMs), demonstrating their potential to significantly reduce computation and weight transfer during inference while maintaining performance. The authors show that ReLU activations, despite their simplicity, can achieve similar performance to more complex activations like GELU and SiLU with minimal performance trade-offs. They also propose strategies to further enhance the inference efficiency of LLMs by leveraging sparsity patterns in ReLU-based models. These strategies include reusing activated neurons for generating new tokens and speculative decoding techniques. The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of various models, including OPT, Falcon, and Llama, and demonstrates that the use of ReLU activations can lead to significant computational savings and performance improvements.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the impact of different activation functions on the performance and computational efficiency of LLMs, demonstrating that ReLU activations can lead to significant computational savings and performance improvements.
- The authors conduct experiments on various models, including OPT, Falcon, and Llama, and across multiple datasets, providing a robust validation of their findings.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The results are impressive, showing that ReLU activations can lead to significant computational savings and performance improvements, and the paper provides a clear and compelling argument for the use of ReLU activations in LLMs.
- The paper presents novel strategies to exploit sparsity patterns in ReLU-based LLMs, such as reusing activated neurons for generating new tokens and speculative decoding techniques, which can lead to further improvements in inference efficiency.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments and ablation studies, providing a comprehensive evaluation of the impact of different activation functions on the performance and computational efficiency of LLMs.
