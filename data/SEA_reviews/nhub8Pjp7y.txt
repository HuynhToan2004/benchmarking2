 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PETA, a novel backdoor attack tailored for Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) in language models. The attack operates in a bilevel optimization framework, where the upper-level objective embeds the backdoor into a pre-trained language model, and the lower-level objective simulates PEFT to maintain the model's task-specific performance. The paper evaluates PETA across various downstream tasks and trigger designs, demonstrating its effectiveness in terms of attack success rate and unaffected clean accuracy. It also discusses a simple defense mechanism that omits PEFT in selected layers of the backdoored PLM and unfreezes a subset of these layers' parameters to neutralize the attack.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel attack named PETA, which specifically targets Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) in language models, a topic that has not been extensively explored.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed attack method is technically sound, with a detailed algorithmic explanation that aids in understanding the attack's implementation.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the attack, including its effectiveness and transferability across different PEFT methods and datasets.
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of the attack's effectiveness and proposed a simple yet effective defense mechanism.
- The paper is the first to explore the security implications of PEFT, which is a significant area of concern given the widespread use of PEFT.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is limited as it mainly focuses on the evaluation of the proposed attack without proposing a new PEFT method.
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of why PEFT makes the backdoor more effective, and the connection between PEFT and the backdoor is not sufficiently explained.
- The experimental setup is limited to RoBERTa, which might not generalize well to other large language models like Llama.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the proposed attack with existing PEFT methods or other backdoor attacks, which could have strengthened the paper's argument.
- The paper's novelty is questioned as it appears to be an extension of existing backdoor attacks to PEFT, which might not be considered a significant advancement.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed defense mechanism and its applicability to different PEFT methods.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed defense mechanism perform against other types of backdoor attacks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the sensitivity of the attack to different hyperparameters, such as the learning rate and batch size?
- How does the attack perform when the trigger is inserted during inference, and what are the implications for the model's performance?
- Could the authors clarify the connection between PEFT and the backdoor, particularly how PEFT enhances the backdoor's effectiveness?
- How does the proposed attack compare with existing PEFT methods, and what are the specific advantages of using PETA over these methods?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed attack with other existing backdoor attacks, particularly in terms of effectiveness and transferability?
