 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel adversarial attack method named Adversarial Perturbation Dropout (APD), which employs a dropout mechanism during the optimization process to enhance the transferability of adversarial examples. This method leverages class activation maps (CAM) to determine the midpoint of dropped regions, ensuring the attack rate towards the source model remains consistent even with dropped perturbations. The proposed method is evaluated on the ImageNet dataset and reportedly outperforms existing methods in terms of attack success rates. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, insufficient experimental validation, and lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the presentation is clear, with detailed explanations and visualizations that aid in understanding the proposed method.
- The proposed method, APD, is simple, intuitive, and can be applied to various adversarial attack methods, enhancing their transferability.
- The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive experiments, showing improvements over state-of-the-art methods in terms of attack success rates.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of adversarial attacks, focusing on the transferability of adversarial examples.
- The use of CAMs to determine dropped regions is a novel approach that could be beneficial for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where it may not perform optimally.
- There is a significant gap in the experimental validation, with the method being tested only on a limited number of datasets (ImageNet) and not compared against a broad range of state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper's claims regarding the effectiveness of the proposed method are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence, particularly in scenarios involving different models and datasets.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive literature review, missing out on recent significant works in the field of adversarial attacks and defenses.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable as it appears to be a combination of existing methods without significant innovation.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other dropout-based methods, such as cutout?
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the computational cost of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the attention regions of different models overlap significantly?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the attention regions are determined and how they vary across different models?
- What are the specific reasons for the observed differences in performance between the proposed method and other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of transferability and generalization?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the models used, the datasets, and the hyperparameters tuned?
- How does the proposed method perform against state-of-the-art defenses, such as those based on feature purification or adversarial training?
- Can the authors clarify the role of CAMs in the proposed method and how they are used to guide the selection of dropped regions?
