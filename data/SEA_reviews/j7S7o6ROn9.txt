 **Summary:**
The paper introduces WitnessPrune, a novel pruning algorithm for neural networks that does not require access to the training dataset or loss function. It employs a lower bound on the total variation distance using witness functions, which allows for the pruning of non-discriminative filters. The methodology is applied to various neural network architectures, including VGG and ResNet, and the results show that WitnessPrune can achieve higher accuracy with similar sparsity compared to existing methods like TVSPrune. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the method and provides empirical evidence of its effectiveness. However, the paper lacks a detailed comparison with other pruning methods and does not explore the use of more complex witness functions, which could potentially improve the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for pruning neural networks that does not require access to the training dataset or loss function, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is clearly explained and supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, demonstrating its effectiveness and practical applicability.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, showing its superiority over existing methods in terms of accuracy and sparsity.
- The use of witness functions to derive lower bounds on the total variation distance is a novel approach that has not been extensively explored in previous literature.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, which is crucial for a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other pruning methods, which could help in understanding the relative advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The empirical results are limited to a few datasets and architectures, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the method.
- The paper does not explore the use of more complex witness functions, which could potentially improve the results.
- The theoretical analysis relies heavily on the boundedness of the moments of the witness function, which may not be practical in all scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scalability and applicability to larger and more complex models.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations or societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for some readers.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other pruning methods, especially in terms of accuracy and sparsity?
- Can the method be adapted to other types of neural networks, such as transformers or recurrent neural networks?
- How does the choice of witness function affect the performance of the method?
- What are the potential limitations or societal impacts of the proposed method, and how are these addressed in the paper?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in relation to the size and complexity of the neural networks used?
- Is there any empirical evidence to support the claim that the method is effective in scenarios where the class-conditional feature distributions are not Gaussian?
