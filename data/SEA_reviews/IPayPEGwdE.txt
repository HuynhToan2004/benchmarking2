 **Summary:**
The paper investigates a variant of causal contextual bandits where the context is adaptively chosen by the environment based on the initial action selected by the learner. This setting allows for a more nuanced exploration of causal interventions, with the objective of identifying near-optimal atomic causal interventions at the initial state and post context identification to maximize reward. The authors propose a convex optimization-based algorithm to minimize simple regret in this context and provide theoretical guarantees for its performance. The paper also includes experiments to validate the theoretical results, comparing the proposed method against existing baselines. However, the paper's assumptions and the practical applicability of the model, particularly in terms of the semi-Markovian assumption and the need for a causal graph, have been critiqued.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the problem and the proposed algorithm.
- The algorithm design is novel and efficient, utilizing convex optimization for intervention selection, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the algorithm's performance, including a lower bound on the simple regret, which is a significant contribution to the literature.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing the algorithm's effectiveness in various settings.
- The paper addresses a relevant and timely problem in the field of causal contextual bandits, which has practical implications in areas like personalized medicine and targeted advertising.

**Weaknesses:**
- The assumptions made in the paper, particularly the semi-Markovian assumption and the requirement for a causal graph, are not clearly justified or explained.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical applicability of the model, especially in scenarios where the assumptions might not hold.
- The related work section could be more comprehensive, including a broader range of existing works in the field.
- The paper's results are not particularly surprising or groundbreaking, as the problem setup and the results are similar to those in existing literature.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithm and its assumptions, which could affect the robustness and generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the assumptions and the implications of the results, particularly in terms of their practical relevance and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the semi-Markovian assumption and the need for a causal graph?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the results, especially in scenarios where the assumptions might not hold?
- How does the proposed algorithm perform in more complex or realistic scenarios, such as those with hidden confounders or unobserved variables?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the semi-Markovian assumption and its implications for the model's applicability?
- How does the proposed algorithm handle scenarios where the causal graph is not known, and what are the implications of this for the algorithm's performance?
