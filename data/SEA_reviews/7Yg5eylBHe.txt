 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to Bayesian optimization (BO) in distributed multi-agent systems (MAS), focusing on scenarios where agents cooperatively optimize costly-to-evaluate black-box objectives. The proposed method, ZGS-ED-DLCB, leverages random Fourier features as a surrogate model to facilitate knowledge exchange between agents and employs an event-driven mechanism for efficient communication. Theoretical analysis and extensive experiments are provided to demonstrate the algorithm's advantages over state-of-the-art baselines. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its clarity in explaining the necessity and effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly in the context of privacy preservation and the theoretical guarantees provided.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to Bayesian optimization in fully distributed multi-agent systems, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm, which is supported by extensive experiments and comparisons with state-of-the-art baselines.
- The use of random Fourier features as a surrogate model for Gaussian processes is a creative and effective approach that allows for the exchange of local knowledge between neighboring agents, enhancing the efficiency of the optimization process.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their theoretical underpinnings, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The experiments conducted are extensive and cover a variety of scenarios, demonstrating the robustness and applicability of the proposed method across different settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the necessity and effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly in the context of privacy preservation and the theoretical guarantees provided.
- The problem setting is not clearly defined, and the paper does not adequately address the communication bandwidth and processing efficiency constraints, which are crucial for the application of the proposed method in practical scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from existing literature, particularly in the area of federated learning, which could lead to confusion about the novelty of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method, which is a significant limitation given the potential applications in large-scale distributed systems.
- The convergence analysis of the proposed algorithm is not sufficiently rigorous, and the paper does not adequately address the assumptions and limitations of the theoretical results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential avenues for future research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the necessity and effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of privacy preservation and the theoretical guarantees provided?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of communication bandwidth and processing efficiency, especially in scenarios with limited resources?
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method and how it might be adapted for use in large-scale distributed systems?
- What are the specific advantages of using the proposed method over existing methods in terms of optimization performance and efficiency?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the black-box functions are not differentiable, and what are the implications of this for the optimization process?
- Can the authors elaborate on the convergence analysis of the proposed algorithm and address any assumptions or limitations that were not adequately discussed in the paper?
