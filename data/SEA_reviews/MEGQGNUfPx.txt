 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel adversarial training method named FOMO (Forget to Mitigate Overfitting), which emulates the human brain's forgetting mechanism to enhance robustness against adversarial attacks. This method alternates between a forgetting phase where a subset of weights is randomly initialized and a relearning phase where the model learns generalizable features. The experiments conducted on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets demonstrate that FOMO achieves better robust accuracy and generalization than existing methods. However, concerns about the method's effectiveness on larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet and the absence of comparisons with some recent related works were raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear figures and a well-structured content.
- The proposed method is novel and innovative, particularly the idea of using a forgetting mechanism in adversarial training, which is supported by empirical results showing better performance compared to existing methods.
- The method is computationally efficient, requiring fewer training epochs compared to some other methods like KD-SWA.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including robustness to different types of perturbations and corruptions, and demonstrates the method's effectiveness in improving robust generalization.
- The paper is well-written and includes a detailed ablation study that supports the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with recent works such as IDBH and does not include experiments on larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet, which limits the generalizability of the results.
- The method's effectiveness in terms of computational cost and the need for additional hyperparameters (e.g., s, e_r, λ_1, λ_2) is not adequately discussed.
- The paper's theoretical justification for the forgetting mechanism is insufficient, and the method's connection to existing works on adversarial training and robust generalization is not clearly established.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of parameters and their impact on the results.
- The paper's claims about the method's ability to improve generalization are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, particularly in the context of adversarial training.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results regarding the comparison with IDBH, particularly in terms of the training time and the impact of different hyperparameters on the results?
- How does the FOMO method compare to other recent works in terms of computational cost and the need for additional hyperparameters?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the choice of parameters and their impact on the results?
- How does the FOMO method perform on larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet, and what are the potential challenges in scaling up the method?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical justification for the forgetting mechanism and its role in adversarial training and robust generalization?
- How does the FOMO method address the issue of robust overfitting, particularly in the context of adversarial training, and what are the implications for the model's generalization performance?
