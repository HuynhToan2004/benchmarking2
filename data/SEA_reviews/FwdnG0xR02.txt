 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the issue of dataset bias in vision-language models (VLMs) by introducing a novel dataset debiasing pipeline that generates synthetic, gender-balanced contrast sets using InstructPix2Pix. These contrast sets are designed to mitigate spurious correlations between gender and background context, thereby enhancing the validity of bias evaluations. The paper also proposes a method for automatic filtering of generated images based on their similarity to real images. The approach is tested using CLIP-based models on the GenSynth dataset, demonstrating improvements in bias measurement. However, concerns are raised about the generalizability of the findings beyond gender bias and the potential for introducing new biases through the synthetic data generation process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important issue of dataset bias in vision-language models, which is crucial for understanding and mitigating biases in AI systems.
- The proposed method of generating synthetic contrast sets using InstructPix2Pix is novel and could be useful for other applications requiring synthetic data.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes detailed methodologies and results that are supported by thorough evaluations.
- The introduction of a new dataset, GenSynth, and the use of a contrast set approach to address dataset bias are innovative and could be beneficial for the community.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is commendable for ethical considerations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on gender bias, which may not generalize well to other forms of bias such as race or ethnicity.
- The quality of the generated images and the accuracy of the gender labels in the synthetic dataset are not thoroughly validated, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for introducing new biases through the synthetic data generation process, which is a significant concern given the potential for amplifying existing biases.
- The methodology and results presented in the paper are not sufficiently robust to draw strong conclusions, and the paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods and datasets.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the methodology used for generating gender-neutral captions and the quality of the generated images, which are crucial for the validity of the findings.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle other forms of bias beyond gender, such as race or ethnicity?
- Can the authors provide more details on the methodology used for generating gender-neutral captions and the quality of the generated images?
- How does the paper ensure that the synthetic images are of high quality and accurately reflect the target gender change?
- How does the paper address the potential for introducing new biases through the synthetic data generation process?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method?
- How does the paper compare its findings with existing methods and datasets, especially in terms of bias measurement and dataset quality?
