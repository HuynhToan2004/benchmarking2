 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the relationship between sample efficiency and adaptivity in multi-batch reinforcement learning, focusing on the policy evaluation and best policy identification problems under linear function approximation. The authors establish lower bounds on the number of batches required for sample-efficient algorithms, showing that just having adaptivity does not guarantee sample-efficiency. The results suggest that the boundary for sample-efficiency lies between different regimes of adaptivity and depends on the problem dimension. The paper also discusses how these findings contribute to a deeper understanding of the trade-offs and possibilities in designing sample-efficient RL algorithms with low adaptivity.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a lower bound on the number of batches required for sample-efficient algorithms, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The results show that just having adaptivity does not guarantee sample-efficiency, which is a novel insight.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making the complex concepts accessible.
- The theoretical results are robust, showing a strong connection between adaptivity and sample efficiency in RL algorithms.
- The paper is a nice extension of previous works, particularly in the context of multi-batch RL, and provides a deeper understanding of the trade-offs and possibilities in designing sample-efficient RL algorithms with low adaptivity.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions, such as the assumption of deterministic transitions, limit the applicability of the results to more general settings.
- The results are not directly applicable to more general settings, such as stochastic transitions, which could limit the practical utility of the findings.
- The paper's results are limited to the policy evaluation and best policy identification problems under linear function approximation, which may not generalize to other types of RL problems or different function approximation methods.
- The paper's contribution is seen as incremental, as it builds on existing works and does not introduce entirely new concepts or methods.
- The paper's technical contribution is not clearly articulated, and the proof techniques used are not sufficiently novel or distinct from previous works.
