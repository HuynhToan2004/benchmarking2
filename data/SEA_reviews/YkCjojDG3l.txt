 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PolySketchFormer, a novel attention mechanism designed to enhance the training efficiency of large language models by replacing the softmax attention mechanism with a polynomial attention mechanism. This mechanism utilizes polynomial sketches to approximate the softmax attention, thereby reducing the computational complexity from quadratic to linear, which is crucial for handling long contexts. The paper also proposes a block-based algorithm to apply causal masks, improving the training speed without requiring the explicit realization of the attention matrix. The methodology is supported by empirical results showing comparable perplexities to softmax attention and faster training times, especially for long contexts. However, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity in explaining the polynomial attention mechanism and the rationale behind the specific polynomial degree choices.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the training efficiency of large language models by proposing a novel attention mechanism that reduces computational complexity.
- The proposed attention mechanism, PolySketchFormer, is supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results, showing a linear time complexity and faster training times for large context lengths.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a clear explanation of the proposed methodology.
- The use of polynomial sketches to approximate the softmax attention matrix is a novel and interesting approach.
- The paper demonstrates significant improvements in training efficiency, particularly for long context lengths, which is a critical area for improving the scalability of language models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the polynomial attention mechanism, particularly the rationale behind the choice of polynomial degree and its impact on performance.
- The empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant performance improvement over existing methods like FlashAttention, especially for shorter context lengths.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with other recent efficient attention mechanisms, such as Gated Attention, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of the computational resources used and the specifics of the training conditions.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, especially in the introduction and related work sections, to better situate the proposed method within the existing literature.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the choice of polynomial degree and its impact on the performance of the polynomial attention mechanism?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and computational efficiency with other recent efficient attention mechanisms, such as Gated Attention?
- Could you provide more details on the computational resources used in your experiments and the specifics of the training conditions, particularly for the FlashAttention results?
- How does the proposed method handle the non-negativity issue in the attention matrix, and what are the implications for the model's performance?
- Can you elaborate on the potential advantages of using polynomial attention over other existing methods, such as softmax attention or other polynomial attention mechanisms?
