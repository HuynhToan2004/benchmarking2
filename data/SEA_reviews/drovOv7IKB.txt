 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FreCoformer, a novel Transformer-based model for time series forecasting, designed to model both short-term and long-term temporal variations effectively. The model employs a divide-and-conquer framework, incorporating a Nystrom approximation to manage computational complexity. It processes time series data in the frequency domain, using a novel frequency patching operation and channel-wise attention mechanisms to enhance the model's ability to capture high-frequency components. Extensive experiments across multiple datasets demonstrate the model's effectiveness, although concerns are raised about its computational efficiency and the clarity of its contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to time series forecasting by processing data in the frequency domain, which is a significant shift from traditional methods that primarily focus on the time domain.
- The use of a divide-and-conquer framework and a lightweight variant of FreCoformer, which utilizes the Nystrom approximation, are innovative solutions to manage computational complexity and improve model efficiency.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their implementation, and includes extensive experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The inclusion of a lightweight variant of the model and the use of the Nystrom approximation to reduce computational complexity are commendable efforts to address the challenges posed by high computational demands in time series forecasting.
- The paper's experimental results are robust, showing that the proposed model outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods across various datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the specific contributions of each module in the proposed framework, making it difficult for readers to understand the individual impacts of each component.
- The computational efficiency of the model is a concern, with the paper not providing sufficient details on the computational costs of each module, which could hinder the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper's claims regarding the model's ability to handle both short-term and long-term temporal variations are not adequately supported by empirical evidence, particularly in scenarios where the model's performance on short-term variations is subpar.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, with some figures and tables being difficult to read, and the overall structure of the paper could benefit from better organization and clearer explanations of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed model, particularly in terms of its applicability to datasets with different characteristics or in scenarios where the model's assumptions about the data do not hold.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations on how each module in the proposed framework contributes to the overall performance of the model?
- How does the proposed model compare in terms of computational efficiency with other state-of-the-art models, especially in scenarios where computational resources are limited?
- Could the authors provide more detailed empirical evidence to support the model's claimed ability to handle both short-term and long-term temporal variations effectively?
- How does the model handle the potential for overfitting, especially in scenarios where the data is noisy or contains outliers?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the T-Net module in the overall framework, particularly in terms of its contribution to the model's performance?
- How does the model perform when applied to datasets with different characteristics, such as those with different sampling frequencies or varying levels of noise?
