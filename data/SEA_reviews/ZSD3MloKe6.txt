 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the exposure bias in diffusion probabilistic models (DPMs) by proposing a novel sampling method named Time-Shift Sampler. This method aims to alleviate exposure bias without requiring retraining, by identifying optimal time steps during sampling. Theoretical and empirical evidence supports the effectiveness of this approach, showing improvements in image quality and generation speed across various datasets and sampling methods. The methodology is designed to be compatible with existing sampling algorithms, offering a practical solution to a significant issue in DPMs. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the presentation, the generalizability of the method across different models and datasets, and the need for more rigorous comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and under-explored problem in diffusion probabilistic models (DPMs) regarding exposure bias, which is critical for improving the generation quality of images.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and does not require retraining, making it accessible and practical for various applications.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method consistently improves image quality and generation speed across multiple datasets and sampling methods, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear and detailed explanation of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The methodology is innovative, as it identifies the optimal time step during sampling to alleviate exposure bias, which is a novel approach in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the presentation of theoretical results and experimental setups, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.
- The methodology's generalizability is questionable, as it was tested only on specific models (DDPM, DDIM, S-PNDM, and F-PNDM) and datasets (CIFAR-10 and CelebA), raising concerns about its applicability to other models and datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing retraining-based methods, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper's theoretical assumptions and proofs are not adequately explained, making it difficult for readers to fully grasp the method's theoretical foundations.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational cost and efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The paper's claims about the effectiveness of the method are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence, particularly in high-resolution image datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the computational cost and efficiency of the proposed method, especially in comparison to other sampling methods?
- How does the method perform on other datasets like ImageNet, and what are the expected outcomes?
- Is the method applicable to other models beyond those tested, and if so, how does it perform?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing retraining-based methods to better establish the effectiveness of their proposed method?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of diffusion models, such as those used in video generation or other applications?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to support the theoretical assumptions and proofs presented in the paper?
- How does the method handle different types of noise or perturbations in the training data, and what are the implications for the model's performance?
