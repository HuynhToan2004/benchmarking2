 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to next-item recommendation by integrating hyperbolic geometry into the SASRec model, specifically replacing the final output layer with a linear predictor in the hyperbolic space. This modification aims to enhance the model's ability to capture complex behavioral data structures and potentially improve recommendation quality. The paper also discusses the compatibility of data with hyperbolic geometry and proposes a method for estimating hyperbolic space curvature to categorize datasets. Despite the innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its limited experimental scope, lack of comparison with more advanced models, and insufficient discussion on the computational overhead and potential negative societal impacts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by integrating hyperbolic geometry into the SASRec model, which is a significant advancement in the field of next-item recommendation.
- The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of the hyperbolic geometry used in the model, making the paper accessible to a broader audience.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear structure and logical flow, which aids in understanding the complex concepts presented.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed hyperbolic model can improve recommendation quality, particularly on datasets that are compatible with hyperbolic geometry.
- The paper is the first to explore the application of hyperbolic geometry in the context of sequential self-attention for next-item recommendations, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the computational overhead and potential negative societal impacts of using hyperbolic models, which are critical for a complete evaluation of the proposed approach.
- The experimental setup is limited, focusing only on the SASRec model and a few datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability or robustness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with more advanced models, which could provide a better understanding of its relative performance and effectiveness.
- The paper's novelty is questioned due to its reliance on existing hyperbolic models and its limited contribution to the field, particularly in terms of originality and depth of analysis.
- There are several minor issues with the presentation, such as typos and unclear explanations, which detract from the paper's professional quality.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead and potential negative societal impacts of using hyperbolic models in recommendation systems?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and computational efficiency with other advanced models, such as the latest versions of SASRec and other state-of-the-art recommender systems?
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of hyperbolic space in the context of the proposed method, especially in relation to the Poincaré ball and the hyperbolic hyperplane?
- How does the proposed method address the limitations of negative sampling in hyperbolic models, and what are the implications of this approach for the overall performance of the model?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results and comparisons, particularly on larger and more diverse datasets, to better demonstrate the generalizability and effectiveness of the proposed method?
