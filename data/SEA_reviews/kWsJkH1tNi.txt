 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the generalization error in federated learning (FL) settings, focusing on the impact of the number of communication rounds between clients and the parameter server. It proposes theoretical bounds on generalization error using PAC-Bayes and rate-distortion theory, which are applicable to a wide range of loss functions and learning algorithms. The authors demonstrate that the generalization error of federated support vector machines (FSVM) increases with the number of communication rounds, suggesting that communication frequency might not always enhance generalization power. The paper also includes experiments to validate these theoretical findings. However, the paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing works and does not sufficiently discuss the practical implications of its findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the generalization error in federated learning, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of models in practical scenarios.
- The theoretical results are well-supported by proofs and are backed by experiments on federated support vector machines (FSVM), showing the practical applicability of the theoretical findings.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors introduce a novel approach by considering the effect of communication rounds on generalization error, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper demonstrates the impact of communication frequency on the generalization error, showing that more frequent communication does not always lead to better generalization.
- The experimental results support the theoretical findings, providing evidence for the validity of the proposed bounds.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing works, particularly those that also study the generalization error in federated learning. This makes it difficult to assess the novelty and contribution of the proposed bounds.
- The practical implications of the findings are not thoroughly discussed, leaving it unclear how the theoretical results can be applied in practical settings to improve generalization.
- The paper assumes that the number of training data points and SGD iterations remains constant across different communication rounds, which might not reflect real-world scenarios where data availability varies.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the strong assumptions required for the theoretical results to hold.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed bounds and how they might be improved or refined in future work.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing works to clarify the novelty and contribution of their findings?
- How do the theoretical results translate into practical implications for improving generalization in federated learning settings?
- What are the practical challenges in applying the proposed bounds in real-world scenarios, and how can these be addressed?
- Could the authors discuss the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and how these assumptions might affect the applicability of the results in different scenarios?
- How do the proposed bounds compare with other existing bounds in terms of their effectiveness and applicability?
- What are the limitations of the proposed bounds, and how might these be addressed in future work?
