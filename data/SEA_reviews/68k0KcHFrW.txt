 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to federated learning by integrating algorithm unrolling with decentralized optimization, specifically focusing on the unrolled decentralized gradient descent (DGD) algorithm. This method, termed SURF, employs a GNN-based unrolled architecture to address the challenges of decentralized training, such as the need for whole datasets and the inherent decentralization of federated learning. The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support the convergence of the unrolled optimizer to a near-optimal region. However, the experimental setup is limited to a small number of devices and does not include comparisons with other federated learning methods, raising concerns about the generalizability and practical applicability of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by integrating algorithm unrolling with decentralized optimization, which is a significant advancement in the field of federated learning.
- The use of GNNs to parameterize the unrolled DGD algorithm is innovative and adds a new dimension to the application of GNNs in federated learning.
- The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support the convergence of the unrolled optimizer to a near-optimal region, which is a crucial aspect of the proposed method.
- The application of unrolled optimization to federated learning is a promising area of research, and the paper presents a well-articulated methodology for achieving this.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a logical structure that makes it accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental setup is limited to a small number of devices (n=100), which may not adequately represent real-world scenarios with larger numbers of devices.
- The paper lacks comparisons with other federated learning methods, which is crucial for understanding the performance and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the boundedness and Lipschitz continuity of the loss function and its gradient, may not hold in practical scenarios, which could limit the applicability of the theoretical results.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of data heterogeneity on the performance of the proposed method, which is a significant challenge in federated learning.
- The practical implementation of the proposed method, particularly the use of a central server for training, may not be feasible in all federated learning scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when the number of devices is significantly larger than 100?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the dataset used and the parameters of the GNN-based unrolled architecture?
- How does the proposed method compare with other federated learning methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors elaborate on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and discuss how these assumptions affect the applicability of the results?
- How does the proposed method handle data heterogeneity, and what are the potential impacts on its performance?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these could be mitigated?
