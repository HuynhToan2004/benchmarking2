 **Summary:**
The paper explores the role of pre-training in mitigating distribution shifts, specifically focusing on two types: in-support and out-of-support shifts. It theoretically argues that pre-training primarily helps in out-of-support shifts by providing a prior for extrapolation, but does not address in-support shifts where biases are prevalent. The paper presents empirical evidence using synthetic and natural datasets, demonstrating that pre-training improves robustness in out-of-support scenarios but not in-support ones. It also suggests that combining pre-training with interventions designed to address biases can enhance model robustness. The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the theoretical framework and empirical findings, although it lacks a detailed discussion on the implications of its results and the practical applicability of its findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical flow of ideas and clear illustrations that enhance understanding.
- The theoretical framework is robust, providing a clear understanding of the role of pre-training in mitigating distribution shifts.
- The empirical results are strong, demonstrating that pre-training can significantly enhance robustness in certain scenarios, particularly in out-of-support shifts.
- The paper introduces a novel approach by combining pre-training with interventions designed to address biases, showing that this approach can lead to more robust models.
- The study of the limitations of pre-training in addressing distribution shifts is timely and relevant, given the increasing use of pre-training in various applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the implications of its results, particularly in practical scenarios where out-of-support shifts are less common than in-support shifts.
- There is a lack of comprehensive evaluation, with the study focusing primarily on ResNet-18 and not exploring how the findings might apply to other architectures or datasets.
- The theoretical underpinnings of the paper are somewhat simplistic, particularly in the logistic regression setting, which may not fully capture the complexities of real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical challenges of applying the proposed interventions, such as the cost and feasibility of de-biasing datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough literature review and comparison with existing works, particularly those that have also studied the limitations of pre-training in addressing distribution shifts.

**Questions:**
- How does the paper's theoretical framework apply to more complex models, such as deep neural networks?
- Can the authors provide more details on the practical implications of their findings, particularly in scenarios where in-support shifts are more prevalent?
- How does the paper's approach compare to other methods for addressing distribution shifts, such as domain generalization or data augmentation?
- Could the authors elaborate on the methodology used to construct the synthetic in-support and out-of-support shifts, and discuss the potential limitations of this methodology?
- What are the specific challenges and potential solutions for applying the proposed interventions in real-world scenarios?
