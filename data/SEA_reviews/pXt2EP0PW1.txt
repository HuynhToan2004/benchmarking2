 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedFaiREE, a novel post-processing algorithm designed for fair federated learning in decentralized settings with small samples. FedFaiREE is a distribution-free algorithm that addresses fairness constraints and ensures both fairness and accuracy in federated learning environments. It utilizes a distributed quantile algorithm to construct a candidate set and select the best threshold, thereby achieving optimal fairness and misclassification error. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for both fairness and accuracy, supported by empirical results demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness in real-world scenarios. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in certain sections, potential errors in assumptions, and insufficient experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel post-processing algorithm, FedFaiREE, designed specifically for distribution-free fair learning in decentralized settings with small samples.
- FedFaiREE addresses unique challenges in decentralized environments, such as client heterogeneity, communication costs, and small sample sizes, which are frequently encountered in practical applications.
- The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for both fairness and accuracy, ensuring that the proposed algorithm achieves nearly optimal fairness when the input prediction function is suitable.
- Empirical results validate the effectiveness of the algorithm, demonstrating its practical applicability and potential impact on real-world scenarios.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the definition and explanation of key terms and concepts, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.
- There are potential errors in assumptions and theoretical guarantees, which could affect the reliability and generalizability of the results.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, with limited datasets and models used, which may not adequately demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness across different scenarios and conditions.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability and computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, which could limit its applicability in large-scale or resource-constrained environments.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, which could help in understanding the algorithm's advantages and disadvantages more clearly.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed algorithm, which could limit its acceptance in certain applications.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definitions and uses of terms such as "score-based classifier," "DEOO," and "fairness constraint"?
- How does the proposed algorithm ensure fairness in scenarios where data is not i.i.d., and how does it handle cases where the data distribution is imbalanced?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed algorithm, and how do these impacts compare to those of existing methods?
- How does the algorithm perform in terms of computational complexity and scalability, especially in large-scale or resource-constrained environments?
- Could you provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons with existing methods and results across different datasets and models?
- How does the algorithm handle label shift in the test set, and what are the implications for fairness and accuracy in such scenarios?
