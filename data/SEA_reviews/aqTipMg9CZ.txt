 **Summary:**
The paper introduces REMO, a novel self-supervised learning framework for molecular representation learning, leveraging well-defined atom-combination rules in common chemistry. REMO pre-trains graph/Transformer encoders on 1.7 million known chemical reactions, using two pre-training objectives: Masked Reaction Centre Reconstruction (MRCR) and Reaction Centre Identification (RCI). The framework aims to improve molecular property prediction and drug-drug interaction tasks by utilizing chemical reaction knowledge to model molecular representations. The paper presents extensive experiments across various datasets, showing that REMO outperforms existing methods in molecular representation learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to molecular representation learning by leveraging chemical reaction knowledge, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method, REMO, is a self-supervised learning framework that utilizes well-defined atom-combination rules in common chemistry, which is a novel approach.
- The paper provides extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and tasks, showing improvements over existing methods.
- The authors have provided a detailed description of the proposed method, including the pre-training process and two pre-training tasks: Masked Reaction Centre Reconstruction (MRCR) and Reaction Centre Identification (RCI).
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the motivation behind the study is clear, highlighting the limitations of existing masked reconstruction loss in molecular representation learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of scalability and generalizability.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definition and identification of reaction centers, which are crucial for the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the hyperparameters used for pre-training and fine-tuning, which could affect the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant omission.
- The paper's comparison with existing methods, such as Mole-BERT and GraphMVP, is not fair due to differences in the pre-training datasets used.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper's claims about the novelty of the method are not convincingly supported, as similar approaches have been explored in previous works.

**Questions:**
- How do the authors ensure that the model is not overfitting to the pre-training data, particularly given the limited size of the dataset?
- Can the authors provide a detailed explanation of how the reaction centers are identified and how this process affects the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method perform on other downstream tasks, such as molecule generation?
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of reaction centers in the proposed method, especially in terms of their impact on the model's performance?
- How do the authors address the potential negative societal impacts of their proposed method, particularly in areas such as drug discovery and development?
- Could the authors provide more details on the pre-training and fine-tuning processes, including the hyperparameters used and their impact on the results?
- How does the proposed method compare to other self-supervised learning methods for molecular representation learning, particularly in terms of scalability and generalizability?
