 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the selection bias in Large Language Models (LLMs) when answering multiple-choice questions (MCQs), focusing on how LLMs are biased towards specific option IDs. The authors propose a method named PriDe to mitigate this bias by permutating option contents on a small number of test samples, thereby separating the model's prior bias from the overall prediction distribution. This method is evaluated against strong baselines and is shown to be effective in reducing selection bias, with superior debiasing effectiveness and computational efficiency. The paper also discusses the impact of position bias and token bias on LLM performance and provides empirical evidence of these biases across various models and datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the problem, methodology, and results, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The authors provide a detailed empirical analysis of the selection bias in LLMs, which is a significant issue in the field of AI.
- The proposed method, PriDe, is simple yet effective, and the paper includes extensive experiments that support the claims made.
- The paper addresses an important issue in LLMs, namely, the selection bias in MCQs, which is a crucial problem for practical applications.
- The authors provide a novel approach to mitigate selection bias by separating the model's prior bias from the overall prediction distribution, which is both innovative and practical.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various models and datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to other types of questions and its scalability.
- The paper lacks a broader discussion on the societal implications of the findings, which could be a significant limitation for the paper's impact.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational overhead of the proposed method, which is a critical factor in practical applications.
- The paper's focus on token bias and position bias might not fully capture the complexity of the issue, as there are other potential factors that could influence LLM performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation of the proposed method, including a comparison with other state-of-the-art methods and a more detailed analysis of the results.
- The paper's claims about the impact of LLM training data on selection bias are not adequately supported, which could weaken the paper's argument.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational overhead of the proposed method and how it compares to other methods in terms of efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of questions, such as those that do not involve multiple-choice answers?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential societal implications of the findings and how they might be addressed in practical applications?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the results, including a breakdown of the performance by model size and other factors?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of the proposed method on the performance of LLMs in practical applications, especially in terms of accuracy and efficiency?
