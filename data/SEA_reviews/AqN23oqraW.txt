 **Summary:**
The paper introduces KoLA, a new benchmark designed to evaluate the world knowledge of large language models (LLMs). KoLA is structured around a four-level cognitive ability taxonomy, which includes tasks ranging from knowledge memorization to knowledge creation. The benchmark utilizes both known and evolving data sources, including Wikipedia and newly published articles, to ensure fairness and relevance. The evaluation criteria include a contrastive system with standard scores and a self-contrast metric for automatic knowledge-creating ability assessment. The paper presents intriguing findings from evaluating 28 open-source and commercial LLMs, highlighting the importance of world knowledge in LLM performance and the need for continuous benchmarking to advance the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- It introduces a novel four-level cognitive ability taxonomy, which is a significant contribution to the field of LLM evaluation.
- The benchmark, KoLA, is innovative, focusing on world knowledge evaluation and including both known and evolving data sources, which are crucial for fairness and relevance in evaluations.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of 28 open-source and commercial LLMs, offering valuable insights into their capabilities.
- The authors have conducted rigorous evaluations and included an analysis of the results, which are both insightful and valuable for the community.
- The paper is structured around three key factors: ability modeling, data, and evaluation criteria, providing a comprehensive framework for evaluating LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the relationship between the proposed benchmark and the taxonomy, which could be improved by including more examples and a deeper exploration of the taxonomy's implications.
- The data collection process and the details of data annotation are not sufficiently detailed, which could raise concerns about the benchmark's reliability and reproducibility.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of potential data leakage from evolving data sources, which could affect the fairness and validity of the evaluations.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the evolving data is selected and annotated, which could impact the benchmark's effectiveness and applicability.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential impact of different training data on the evaluation results, which could lead to biased or inconsistent findings.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the benchmark design or the potential societal impacts of the evaluations, which could be critical for understanding the benchmark's scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the data collection and annotation processes to enhance transparency and reproducibility?
- How do the authors address potential data leakage from evolving data sources, and what steps are taken to ensure fairness in evaluations?
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the proposed benchmark and the taxonomy, and provide more examples to illustrate the taxonomy's utility?
- How does the benchmark handle the potential impact of different training data on the evaluation results, and what steps are taken to mitigate these effects?
- What are the authors' plans for updating the benchmark in the future, and how will they ensure that the benchmark remains relevant and effective?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of the evaluations, particularly in terms of fairness and bias, and how these impacts are mitigated?
