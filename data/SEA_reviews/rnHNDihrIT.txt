 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Stylized Offline Reinforcement Learning (SORL), a novel method aimed at extracting diverse, high-quality policies from heterogeneous offline datasets. SORL utilizes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for clustering trajectories into distinct styles, followed by a two-stage learning process that refines these policies. The methodology is validated across various environments, including Atari games and a basketball simulation, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods. However, concerns about the novelty of the EM-based clustering approach and the clarity of some experimental results persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, Stylized Offline RL (SORL), which effectively extracts diverse and high-quality policies from heterogeneous offline datasets, addressing the limitations of both diverse RL and offline RL methods.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the superiority of SORL over baseline methods in terms of policy performance and diversity across various environments.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the EM-based style clustering and advantage-weighted style learning, making it accessible and understandable.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear structure and logical flow, enhancing its readability and comprehensibility.
- The motivation behind the research is strong, focusing on the need for both diversity and high-quality policies in offline RL, which is crucial for applications like opponent modeling and autonomous driving.

**Weaknesses:**
- The EM-based clustering approach is not entirely novel, as similar methods have been previously used in other contexts, which might limit the perceived innovation of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of SORL, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- There is a lack of clarity in the experimental results, particularly in the representation of diversity and the consistency of policies across different datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of SORL, such as its performance in scenarios with low-quality or inconsistent data, and its applicability in more complex or dynamic environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other offline RL methods that also consider diversity in their objectives, to better position the contributions of SORL.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of the EM-based clustering approach and how it differentiates from similar methods used in other contexts?
- How does SORL perform in scenarios with low-quality or inconsistent data, and what measures are in place to mitigate the impact of such data on the learning process?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of SORL, particularly in comparison to other existing methods?
- How does SORL ensure that the learned policies remain consistent across different datasets, especially when the datasets exhibit significant variations in behavioral styles?
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other offline RL methods that also consider diversity in their objectives. Can the authors discuss the advantages and disadvantages of SORL compared to these methods?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the metrics used to measure diversity and consistency, particularly in the context of the Atari games and the basketball simulation?
