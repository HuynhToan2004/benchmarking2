 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedQV, a novel aggregation method for Federated Learning (FL) that employs quadratic voting to enhance security against poisoning attacks. FedQV modifies the traditional one-person-one-vote mechanism by assigning weights based on the similarity between a client's local model and the global model, aiming to mitigate the influence of malicious clients. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for convergence and truthfulness and empirical results demonstrating FedQV's effectiveness in preventing attacks. It also discusses the integration of FedQV with other robust FL algorithms and privacy-preserving mechanisms. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the theoretical analysis, and the empirical evidence supporting the claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear motivation and explanation of the proposed FedQV method.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to support the convergence and truthfulness of FedQV, which is a novel application of quadratic voting in federated learning.
- The paper includes empirical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of FedQV in preventing attacks and its compatibility with other robust FL algorithms.
- The method is simple, easy to understand, and can be integrated with existing privacy-preserving mechanisms to enhance security.
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation, showing that FedQV outperforms other aggregation methods in terms of robustness and accuracy.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation and explanation of the quadratic voting method, which is crucial for understanding the rationale behind the proposed solution.
- The theoretical analysis and proofs are not self-contained or easy to follow, with some key assumptions and definitions missing.
- The empirical evaluation is limited to specific datasets and attack scenarios, which may not generalize to other scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the convergence rate and the impact of hyperparameters on the convergence, which are critical for understanding the practical implications of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as similar mechanisms have been previously proposed in other federated learning contexts.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other existing robust federated learning methods, which could help in understanding the relative advantages and disadvantages of FedQV.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the motivation and the rationale behind the quadratic voting method in the context of federated learning?
- How does the FedQV method compare with other existing robust federated learning methods, especially in terms of convergence rate and practical applicability?
- What are the specific advantages of using FedQV over other existing methods in terms of computational complexity, scalability, and privacy protection?
- Could the authors provide more detailed empirical results, including the impact of hyperparameters on convergence and the performance of FedQV in different attack scenarios?
- How does the FedQV method ensure privacy protection, especially in the context of secure aggregation and privacy-preserving mechanisms?
