 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel contact-based object representation and pretraining pipeline for non-prehensile manipulation, leveraging a patch-based transformer architecture to process point clouds. The approach is designed to generalize across diverse object shapes and facilitate time- and data-efficient learning. The methodology is validated through experiments in both simulation and real-world settings, demonstrating its effectiveness in achieving a high success rate in real-world scenarios. The paper also addresses the challenges of contact modeling in non-prehensile manipulation and compares the proposed method with existing approaches.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and comprehensive explanations of the methodology, making it easy to understand.
- The proposed contact-based object representation and pretraining pipeline are novel and effective, as demonstrated by experiments in both simulation and real-world settings.
- The paper is innovative in its approach to non-prehensile manipulation, which is a challenging and under-explored area in robotics.
- The use of a patch-based transformer architecture for point cloud processing is highlighted as a significant advancement, enhancing the efficiency and scalability of the training process.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear figures and a logical flow of content, which aids in understanding the complex concepts presented.
- The experiments are well-designed, providing a robust validation of the proposed method, and the results are convincing, showing that the method can achieve a high success rate in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have strengthened the argument for the superiority of the proposed method.
- The experiments are limited to a small set of real-world objects, which might not fully demonstrate the generalization capabilities of the method.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the contact prediction header and the distillation process, which are crucial aspects of the methodology.
- There is a need for more clarity on the training details, such as the specifics of the curriculum learning scheme and the number of parallel environments used.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed figures and a more comprehensive literature review, which would better situate the work within the existing body of research.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the method, such as its performance with concave objects and unstable objects, which could affect its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the contact prediction header and the distillation process, particularly how these components contribute to the effectiveness of the method?
- How does the method perform with different types of objects, such as concave objects and unstable objects, which are known to be challenging for non-prehensile manipulation?
- Could you clarify the training details, such as the curriculum learning scheme and the number of parallel environments used?
- How does the method handle objects with complex shapes or multiple contact points, which could be challenging for non-prehensile manipulation?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to include more complex tasks or scenarios, such as manipulating objects in cluttered environments or with multiple objects?
- How does the method compare with other recent approaches in non-prehensile manipulation, such as those using different representations or learning strategies?
