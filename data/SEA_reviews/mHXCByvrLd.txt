 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to offline reinforcement learning by framing the problem as an optimal transport problem between states and actions, using an action-value cost function. The authors propose an algorithm called Extremal Monge Reinforcement Learning (XMRL), which treats offline reinforcement learning as an extremal optimal transport problem. The methodology is supported by theoretical insights and empirical results on the D4RL benchmark suite, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as it heavily relies on existing frameworks and lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel perspective on offline reinforcement learning by framing it as an optimal transport problem between states and actions, using an action-value cost function.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the proposed method, supported by extensive experimental results on the D4RL benchmark suite, showing improvements over existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the authors have provided code and detailed experimental settings in the supplementary material.
- The algorithm proposed in the paper is simple and effective, and the methodology is clearly presented, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art offline reinforcement learning methods, which is crucial for establishing the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it heavily relies on existing frameworks such as Extremal Optimal Transport (ET) and Monge Reinforcement Learning (MRL).
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its convergence and generalization to different environments.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method.
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the presentation of mathematical formulations and the explanation of certain algorithms.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art offline reinforcement learning methods to establish the effectiveness of their proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex environments beyond those tested, and what are the potential limitations of the method?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its convergence and generalization to different environments?
- In the experimental setup, how does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability to existing methods?
- The paper mentions several theoretical and practical aspects that are not clearly explained, such as the use of a neural network for the action-value cost function and the role of the potential function in the algorithm. Can these aspects be clarified?
