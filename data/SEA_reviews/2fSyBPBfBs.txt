 **Summary:**
The paper investigates bilevel optimization (BLO) without the strong convexity assumption on the lower level function, which is a significant departure from traditional methods. It introduces two regularity conditions to ensure tractability and proposes algorithms to solve for the lower-level solution and upper-level stationary point. The paper presents theoretical results on the intractability of BLO without these conditions and provides a polynomial-time algorithm under specific conditions. Numerical experiments are included to support the theoretical claims. However, the paper is critiqued for its clarity and the novelty of its contributions, with concerns about the generalizability and practical applicability of the proposed conditions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and provides a comprehensive review of related work, making it accessible to readers.
- It introduces a novel perspective on bilevel optimization (BLO) without the strong convexity assumption on the lower level function, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides theoretical results on the intractability of BLO without specific conditions and proposes a polynomial-time algorithm under certain conditions, which are supported by numerical experiments.
- The authors demonstrate the intractability of BLO without strong convexity and show that it can be made tractable under mild assumptions.
- The paper is technically sound, with a clear presentation of the problem and the proposed solutions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is seen as limited, as it primarily focuses on the theoretical analysis of BLO without strong convexity, which may not be a practical or realistic assumption in many real-world applications.
- The paper lacks a discussion on how the proposed algorithms can be applied in practice, especially in scenarios where the regularity conditions are not met.
- There are concerns about the clarity and presentation of the paper, particularly in the introduction and the explanation of certain terms and assumptions.
- The paper's experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the comparison with other methods is not sufficiently detailed or fair.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the compactness of the feasible set Y and the boundedness of its diameter, are not adequately justified or explained.
- There is a lack of discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results and the generalizability of the proposed conditions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the practical applications of the proposed algorithms, especially in scenarios where the regularity conditions are not met?
- How do the authors justify the assumption of compactness and boundedness of the feasible set Y, and what are the implications if these assumptions do not hold?
- Could the authors clarify the definition and use of the term "approximately stationary" in the context of the algorithms presented?
- In the experimental results, why was the AID algorithm not included in the comparisons, and how do the results compare with other existing methods?
- Can the authors elaborate on the relationship between the proposed conditions and other conditions that might be used in practice, such as the Morse-Bott condition?
- How do the authors address the issue of intractability when the lower-level function is not strongly convex, and what are the implications for the practical applicability of their results?
