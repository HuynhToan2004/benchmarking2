 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to program synthesis from examples by leveraging a neural network-based system that iteratively refines both the listener and speaker models using a self-play mechanism. This method, termed "pragmatic," aims to enhance the performance of the synthesizer by learning from examples that are informative and pragmatic, rather than relying on human-provided examples. The paper validates the approach through a user study involving 11 participants, demonstrating that the proposed method outperforms traditional methods and matches the performance of models trained on human-annotated examples. The approach is particularly noteworthy for its ability to generate informative examples that can be used to train the listener model without requiring extensive human supervision.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical flow that enhances understanding.
- The methodology is novel and effectively demonstrated through a user study, showing that the proposed approach matches the performance of models trained on human-annotated examples.
- The approach is innovative, leveraging neural networks to amortize the search process in program synthesis, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the experimental setup and results, which support the claims made.
- The methodology is scalable, as it does not require extensive human supervision, which is a significant advantage in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed approach, particularly in terms of its applicability to more complex domains and the potential for overfitting due to the iterative nature of the training process.
- There is a need for more detailed experimental validation, including a broader range of examples and a comparison with more recent and relevant baselines to establish the robustness and generalizability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation, particularly in terms of the scalability and effectiveness of the method in more complex programming domains.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed approach, which could be significant given the potential for misuse or bias in automated program synthesis.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed comparisons with other relevant works and by providing a more comprehensive discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the rationale behind the choice of using ByT5-small models as the backbone for the listener and speaker models?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex programming domains, such as those involving more complex data types or larger program spaces?
- Is there a risk of overfitting due to the iterative nature of the training process, and if so, how does the method mitigate this risk?
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent and relevant baselines, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples of the training data used to train the models, including the types and distributions of programs and examples?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for bias or misuse in automated program synthesis, and what steps have been taken to mitigate these risks?
