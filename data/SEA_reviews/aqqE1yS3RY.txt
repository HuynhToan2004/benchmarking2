 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new benchmark dataset, BREC, designed to evaluate the expressiveness of graph neural networks (GNNs) beyond the 1-WL hierarchy. This dataset is composed of 400 pairs of non-isomorphic graphs, categorized into four groups based on graph properties and WL hierarchy. The authors propose a novel evaluation method, Reliable Paired Comparison (RPC), which measures the expressiveness of GNNs by comparing the outputs of two non-isomorphic graphs. The dataset is tested using 23 state-of-the-art GNNs, demonstrating the utility of BREC in evaluating the expressiveness of GNNs. However, concerns were raised regarding the practical applicability of the dataset, as it does not include real-world graph datasets, and the RPC method's generalizability to other types of graph datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmark dataset, BREC, which is the first to evaluate the expressiveness of GNNs beyond the 1-WL hierarchy.
- The dataset is well-structured, with a clear definition of the graph classes used, and the evaluation methodology is detailed and well-explained.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of 23 state-of-the-art GNNs, offering valuable insights into the expressiveness of different GNN models.
- The dataset is designed to address the limitations of existing datasets, such as difficulty, granularity, and scale, making it a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed dataset, particularly in terms of its practical applicability and the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not include real-world graph datasets, which could have provided a more practical context for the evaluation of GNN expressiveness.
- The evaluation methodology, Reliable Paired Comparison (RPC), is not thoroughly tested on other types of graph datasets, raising concerns about its generalizability.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of false positives in the RPC method, which could lead to misleading conclusions about the expressiveness of GNNs.
- The paper does not discuss the potential impact of the dataset on the design of future GNNs, which could be a significant contribution to the field.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed dataset compare to existing datasets in terms of practical applicability and the generalizability of the results?
- Can the RPC method be applied to other types of graph datasets, and if so, what are the implications for the evaluation of GNN expressiveness?
- How do the authors address the issue of false positives in the RPC method, and what steps are taken to ensure the reliability of the results?
- Could the authors provide more details on the generation process of the CFI graphs, particularly how they are generated and what implications this has for the expressiveness of the GNNs evaluated?
- How does the proposed dataset contribute to the design of future GNNs, and what insights can be gained from the evaluation of the 23 GNN models using BREC?
