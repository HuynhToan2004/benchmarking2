 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel information-theoretic framework for learning task embeddings in reinforcement learning, leveraging a diverse agent population to measure task similarity. The framework employs a task embedding function that maps tasks to fixed-dimensional representations, which are used to measure similarity and order tasks based on their difficulty. The paper presents two benchmarks: performance prediction and task selection, using visualizations and experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its clarity in defining key concepts, such as task similarity and task difficulty, and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel information-theoretic framework for learning task embeddings in reinforcement learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The framework uses a diverse agent population to measure task similarity, which is a novel approach that distinguishes it from existing methods.
- The paper provides a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can be applied to various environments and tasks.
- The paper is well-written and structured, with clear figures and visualizations that aid in understanding the proposed method and its applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of how it addresses the limitations of prior work in learning task embeddings.
- The definition of key concepts, such as task similarity and task difficulty, is not clearly defined, which could lead to confusion and misinterpretation of the results.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications for the choice of benchmarks and the methodology used in the experiments.
- The scalability of the proposed method to larger or more complex environments is not discussed, which is a significant limitation for practical applications.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper that detract from its professional presentation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definitions of task similarity and task difficulty, and provide examples or additional explanations to help readers better understand these concepts?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods, particularly in terms of its ability to handle more complex or varied tasks?
- What are the implications of using different types of policy distributions, such as Gaussian Mixture Models or other types of distributions, in the framework?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to tasks with continuous actions or more complex environments, such as those involving multiple agents or more complex state spaces?
- Could the authors discuss the scalability of the proposed method to larger or more complex environments, and provide insights into how it might be adapted or extended to address these challenges?
