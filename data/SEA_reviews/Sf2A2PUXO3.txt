 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for estimating predictive multiplicity using dropout techniques, specifically focusing on the exploration of the Rashomon set. The authors propose a theoretical framework that connects dropout parameters to the properties of the Rashomon set, demonstrating that dropout-based models can effectively estimate predictive multiplicity metrics. Extensive experiments are conducted to validate the proposed method, showing that it outperforms existing baselines in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy. The paper also provides empirical evidence of the effectiveness of the method across various datasets and scenarios, including ensemble methods and model selection. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the method, the clarity of the theoretical underpinnings, and the comparative analysis with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method using dropout techniques to explore the Rashomon set, which is a significant contribution to the field of predictive multiplicity estimation.
- Theoretical derivations are provided to connect dropout parameters with properties of the Rashomon set, which enhances the understanding of the method's theoretical foundations.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to validate the proposed method, showing that it outperforms existing baselines in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research and a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and future directions.
- The application of the dropout-based Rashomon set exploration to mitigate predictive multiplicity is innovative and practical, demonstrating the method's potential in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the differences between the proposed method and existing methods like dilution, which might lead to confusion about the novelty of the approach.
- The theoretical analysis, particularly in sections 3.2 and 3.3, is unclear and not well-explained, which could hinder understanding of the method's effectiveness and applicability.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, such as adversarial weight perturbation, which could have provided a more robust validation of the method's performance.
- The paper's focus on neural networks might limit its applicability to other types of models, and the assumption of equal dropout probabilities for all weights could be overly simplistic.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its practical applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between dilution and the proposed dropout method, particularly in terms of their impacts on predictive multiplicity estimation?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods, such as adversarial weight perturbation, in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- What are the practical implications of the proposed method in real-world scenarios, and how does it address the challenges of predictive multiplicity in practical applications?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to clarify the theoretical underpinnings of the method, particularly in sections 3.2 and 3.3?
- How does the proposed method handle the assumption of equal dropout probabilities for all weights, and what are the implications of this assumption in practical applications?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential future directions for research to address these limitations?
