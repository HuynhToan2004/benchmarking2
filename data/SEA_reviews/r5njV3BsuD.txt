 **Summary:**
The paper explores the convergence rates of diffusion models, focusing on linear dependence on the data dimension under mild assumptions. It introduces a novel approach that uses stochastic localization and refined treatments of discretization error to achieve tighter bounds, which are linear in the data dimension up to logarithmic factors. The paper also addresses the early stopping of diffusion models and the finite second moments of the data distribution. The theoretical results are supported by proofs that extend existing methods, such as Girsanov's theorem, to provide more precise error bounds. Despite the paper's theoretical strengths, it faces criticism for its clarity in explaining the technical details and its assumptions, which are seen as somewhat restrictive.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making the theoretical content accessible and understandable.
- The results are significant and the proofs are sound, with the main novelty being the use of stochastic localization to derive a differential inequality for the expected difference between the drift terms at different times.
- The paper provides a new perspective on diffusion models by integrating stochastic localization, which is an innovative approach to studying the convergence rates of diffusion models.
- The paper is the first to derive linear convergence bounds for diffusion models under mild assumptions, which is a significant theoretical contribution.
- The proofs are sound, and the paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples to illustrate the technical details, particularly in the proofs and the assumptions made.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the finite second moments of the data distribution and the L2-accurate score, are not clearly justified or explained.
- The paper's claims about linear dependence on the data dimension are not fully substantiated, particularly in the context of the proofs and assumptions made.
- The paper's contributions are seen as incremental, as it builds on existing works, and the novelty of the proof techniques is not clearly articulated.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the use of an OU process and the assumption of a Gaussian data distribution, may limit the general applicability of the results.
- The paper lacks empirical validation, which could strengthen the claims made about the theoretical results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the proof techniques used, particularly how they differ from previous works?
- How do the authors justify the assumptions made, such as the finite second moments of the data distribution and the L2-accurate score?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions and implications of the use of an OU process and a Gaussian data distribution?
- How do the authors address the issue of the dependence on the data dimension being linear, especially in terms of the proofs and assumptions made?
- Is there a possibility to extend the results to other types of diffusion models, such as those based on different types of stochastic differential equations?
- Could the authors provide more examples or scenarios where their results could be applied, and discuss the practical implications of their findings?
