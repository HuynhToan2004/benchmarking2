 **Summary:**
The paper investigates learning in two-player zero-sum imperfect information extensive-form games (IIEFGs) using linear function approximation. It introduces two algorithms, one based on online mirror descent (OMD) and another on follow-the-regularized-leader (FTRL), both utilizing a linear loss estimator to achieve improved regret bounds. The algorithms are analyzed for their regret performance, demonstrating a significant reduction in dependence on the cardinality of the action and information sets. The paper also includes a regret lower bound, which is the first of its kind for IIEFGs. Despite the theoretical advancements, the paper lacks experimental validation and clarity in certain sections, which could be improved for better understanding and practical application.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear and concise explanations of the algorithms and their components.
- It provides a significant contribution to the field by being the first to study IIEFGs with linear function approximation, a novel and challenging setting that has not been explored before.
- The paper includes a regret lower bound, which is a first for IIEFGs, and the authors have provided a clear and detailed explanation of the algorithms and their analysis, making it easier for readers to understand the contributions and the technical challenges involved.
- The paper is technically sound, with well-structured proofs and clear explanations of the main ideas and techniques used, which are likely to be of independent interest.
- The authors have provided a comprehensive comparison with previous works, highlighting the improvements and advancements made in the current study.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks experimental validation, which is crucial for demonstrating the practical applicability and effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
- There are some issues with the clarity and organization of the paper, particularly in the introduction and the presentation of the algorithms. Some sections, such as the discussion on the offline setting and the regret bounds, are not clearly explained or justified.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and assumptions of the proposed algorithms, as well as a comparison with other relevant works in the field.
- The dependence on the cardinality of the information set (X) and the horizon length (H) in the regret bounds is not sufficiently explained or justified, which could limit the understanding of the practical implications of the results.
- The paper could be improved by addressing some minor typographical and formatting errors, such as missing commas and inconsistencies in the use of symbols and notation.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the meaning and implications of the "offline" setting mentioned in the paper? How does it differ from the "self-play" setting discussed in other works?
- In the context of the regret bounds, can you explain why the dependence on X and H is necessary and how it impacts the practical applicability of the results?
- How does the paper address the challenge of making the results hold in high probability, particularly in the context of the linear function approximation?
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity and the scalability of the algorithms, especially in terms of the size of the game and the number of iterations required?
- How does the paper handle the issue of the "curse of dimensionality" in practical settings, where the cardinality of the information set and action space might be large?
- Can you discuss the relationship between the results presented in this paper and those in other related works, particularly those that also consider linear function approximation in different settings?
