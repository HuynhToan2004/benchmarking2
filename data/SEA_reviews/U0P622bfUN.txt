 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Federated Generative Learning (FGL), a novel approach to federated learning that leverages foundation generative models to synthesize training data from text prompts sent by clients. This method aims to address issues such as high communication costs, data heterogeneity, and privacy concerns in traditional federated learning methods. The paper demonstrates that FGL can achieve significant performance gains with minimal communication overhead and improved privacy protection. However, the method's effectiveness in handling data heterogeneity and its scalability to larger datasets are concerns. Additionally, the paper lacks comprehensive comparisons with existing methods and does not sufficiently address the potential risks associated with the use of foundation generative models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, Federated Generative Learning (FGL), which addresses the challenges of high communication costs, data heterogeneity, and privacy concerns in traditional federated learning methods.
- FGL is demonstrated to be effective in handling skewed data distributions and does not require model weights or gradients to be transmitted, thus reducing communication overhead.
- The method is designed to protect privacy by only transmitting text prompts, which are less likely to contain sensitive information compared to model weights or gradients.
- FGL shows potential in improving performance in federated learning scenarios, as demonstrated through experiments on ImageNet and DomainNet datasets.
- The paper provides a comprehensive privacy analysis, including image retrieval experiments and membership inference attacks, which shows that FGL can defend against privacy attacks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods that use foundation generative models in federated learning, which could help in understanding the advantages and disadvantages of FGL.
- The scalability of FGL to larger datasets is not adequately addressed, which is a significant concern given the potential for increased communication and computational costs.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential risks associated with the use of foundation generative models, such as data leakage and the impact of synthetic data on model performance.
- The method's effectiveness in handling data heterogeneity is not convincingly demonstrated, especially in scenarios where the data distribution is highly imbalanced.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential risks associated with the use of foundation generative models and a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods that use foundation generative models in federated learning? What are the advantages and disadvantages of FGL compared to these methods?
- How does FGL perform in scenarios where the data distribution is highly imbalanced? Could you provide more experimental results to support the effectiveness of FGL in such scenarios?
- What are the specific risks associated with the use of foundation generative models in FGL, and how are these risks mitigated?
- Could you discuss the potential impact of synthetic data on model performance, especially in scenarios where the data distribution is highly imbalanced?
- How does FGL handle the issue of data heterogeneity, especially in scenarios where clients have different data distributions?
- In terms of scalability, how does FGL perform when applied to larger datasets? Are there any potential limitations or challenges that need to be addressed?
