 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the estimation of out-of-distribution (OOD) error using the norm of classification-layer gradients, a novel approach that does not require access to test labels. The methodology involves a pseudo-labeling strategy to generate labels for OOD data, which are then used to compute gradients from a single epoch of backpropagation. Theoretical insights and empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness in estimating OOD error across various datasets and model architectures. Despite the simplicity and theoretical justification of the approach, concerns were raised about the practicality of the method, particularly in scenarios where the OOD data distribution significantly deviates from the training data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for using the norm of gradients to estimate out-of-distribution (OOD) errors.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and computationally efficient, requiring only one epoch of backpropagation, which is a significant advantage over other methods.
- The theoretical analysis provided in the paper is sound, offering a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
- The empirical results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in estimating OOD errors, as demonstrated by strong correlations with true OOD errors across various datasets and model architectures.
- The paper includes extensive experiments and ablation studies that support the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumption that the OOD data distribution is not too far from the training data might limit the practical applicability of the method in real-world scenarios where the OOD data distribution can be significantly different from the training data.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the method performs when the OOD data distribution significantly deviates from the training data, which is a critical concern for the practical deployment of the method.
- The method's reliance on pseudo-labels generated by the model could lead to incorrect gradients, which might affect the accuracy of the OOD error estimation.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that do not require access to test labels.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed experimental setups, such as the inclusion of standard deviations in results and a broader range of datasets to validate the method's effectiveness across different domains.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the method performs when the OOD data distribution significantly deviates from the training data?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the OOD data distribution is significantly different from the training data, and what are the implications of such differences on the accuracy of the OOD error estimation?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the inclusion of standard deviations in results and the use of different datasets to validate the method's effectiveness across various domains?
- How does the method compare with other state-of-the-art methods that do not require access to test labels, and what are the advantages of the proposed method over these methods?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential avenues for future research to address these limitations?
