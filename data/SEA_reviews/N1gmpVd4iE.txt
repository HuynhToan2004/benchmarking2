 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework that integrates reinforcement learning with large language models (LLMs) to develop strategic agents for the Werewolf game, a social deduction game. The framework involves using LLMs for deductive reasoning and action generation, and a population-based RL policy for decision-making. The agents are trained against a diverse set of opponents to optimize their strategic play, and the system is evaluated against other LLM-based agents and human players. The approach demonstrates robustness against adversarial human players and shows potential for zero-shot transfer to other LLMs. However, the novelty of the framework is limited, and the experiments are not sufficiently comprehensive to convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to integrating LLMs with reinforcement learning (RL) for strategic decision-making in complex environments, specifically in the context of the Werewolf game.
- The methodology is well-explained, and the paper is generally easy to follow, with clear descriptions of the training process and the evaluation setup.
- The approach shows robustness against adversarial human players and achieves higher win rates than average human players in single-human settings.
- The paper demonstrates the zero-shot transfer capability of the RL policy, indicating its potential applicability to other LLMs.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear motivation and a focus on addressing the limitations of existing LLM-based agents in multi-agent environments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the framework is limited, as similar approaches combining LLMs with RL for strategic decision-making have been previously explored.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation, particularly in terms of the diversity of opponents and the scalability of the approach to larger, more complex games.
- There is a lack of detail on how the LLM is used for deductive reasoning and action generation, which could affect the reproducibility and understanding of the method.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for exploitation by opponents, which could limit the effectiveness of the agents in real-world scenarios.
- The experiments are not sufficiently comprehensive, with only limited baseline comparisons and a lack of evaluation against more diverse and complex opponents.
- The paper's claims about the zero-shot transferability of the RL policy to other LLMs are not convincingly supported, as the experiments primarily involve training against a diverse set of opponents rather than directly evaluating the policy on unseen models.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the LLM is used for deductive reasoning and action generation, including the specific prompts and the selection process for the most likely hidden role and the confidence level?
- How does the approach handle the potential for exploitation by opponents, and what measures are in place to prevent or mitigate this?
- In terms of scalability, how does the approach perform when applied to larger, more complex games, and what modifications, if any, are required for such applications?
- Could you clarify the experimental setup, particularly the number of agents used and the specifics of the training and testing phases?
- How does the paper address the issue of information asymmetry in the game, and what implications does this have for the effectiveness of the agents?
- Can you provide more details on the zero-shot transfer experiments, including the specific LLMs used and the methodology for evaluating the policy on unseen models?
