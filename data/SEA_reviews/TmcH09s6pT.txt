 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the neural collapse phenomenon in deep neural networks (DNNs) with a large number of classes, a scenario not typically addressed in previous literature. The authors propose a new notion of neural collapse, termed generalized neural collapse (GNC), which extends the traditional neural collapse concept by considering the arrangement of classifier weights and features on the unit sphere. Theoretical analysis and empirical evidence support the GNC phenomenon, showing that it can occur under specific conditions, such as small temperature in the cross-entropy loss and spherical constraints on features and classifiers. The paper also introduces a class-mean feature classifier as an alternative to traditional training methods, which can achieve comparable performance with reduced parameters. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in defining the GNC phenomenon and its practical implications, particularly in terms of the necessity and effectiveness of the spherical constraint and the temperature parameter.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an interesting and under-explored problem in the field of neural collapse, specifically focusing on large numbers of classes, which is a novel approach.
- The theoretical analysis is well-structured and provides a clear explanation of the generalized neural collapse (GNC) phenomenon, supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a well-organized structure that facilitates understanding.
- The proposed method of using class-mean feature classifiers is a practical and potentially useful application of the GNC phenomenon.
- The paper extends the study of neural collapse to large numbers of classes, a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining and describing the GNC phenomenon, which is crucial for understanding the theoretical contributions.
- The practical implications of the GNC phenomenon are not clearly articulated, making it difficult to grasp the relevance and utility of the theoretical results.
- The empirical evidence provided does not convincingly demonstrate the GNC phenomenon, particularly in the case of spherical constraints, which are not necessary for the GNC to occur.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples to clarify the theoretical concepts and their practical applications.
- The paper's contribution to the field is somewhat limited by the lack of a clear demonstration of the necessity and effectiveness of the spherical constraint and the temperature parameter in achieving the GNC phenomenon.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, which makes it difficult to validate the claims made about the GNC phenomenon.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition and description of the GNC phenomenon, particularly how it extends or differs from the traditional neural collapse?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of spherical constraints in the theoretical analysis, and how do these constraints affect the practical applicability of the GNC phenomenon?
- How does the temperature parameter affect the GNC phenomenon, and what are the implications of setting it to zero as suggested in the paper?
- Can you provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical implications of the GNC phenomenon in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed class-mean feature classifier compare in terms of performance and efficiency to traditional training methods, especially in scenarios with large numbers of classes?
- In light of the empirical evidence presented, how can the GNC phenomenon be more convincingly demonstrated, especially in the context of spherical constraints and temperature parameters?
