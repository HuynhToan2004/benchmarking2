 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ADELT, a source-to-source transpiler designed to convert code between deep learning frameworks like PyTorch, Keras, and MXNet. ADELT employs a novel approach by separating the transpilation process into two stages: skeleton transpilation using a large language model and API keyword mapping using domain-adversarial training. The methodology involves training a generator and discriminator to learn API keyword mappings, which are then used to translate code between frameworks. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its limited evaluation scope, focusing only on three frameworks and not adequately comparing against other state-of-the-art methods. The paper also lacks comprehensive ablation studies and does not convincingly demonstrate the necessity of its specific components.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The proposed method of decoupling code skeleton transpilation and API keyword mapping is innovative and addresses a significant problem in the field of code transpilation.
- The use of domain-adversarial training for API keyword mapping is a novel approach that adds depth to the methodology.
- The paper introduces a new benchmark for code transpilation, which is valuable for the community.
- The authors have made significant efforts to ensure the reproducibility of their work by providing code, corpus, and evaluation benchmarks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation is limited to only three deep learning frameworks, which does not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks comprehensive ablation studies, particularly for the decoupling of code skeleton transpilation and API keyword mapping.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method against other state-of-the-art methods, which is crucial for establishing the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method.
- The methodology does not convincingly demonstrate the necessity of domain-adversarial training for API keyword mapping, and the results of the ablation study do not support its effectiveness.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could be a significant drawback for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- There are concerns about the generalizability of the method to other programming languages and the scalability of the model to larger codebases.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the specific architectures used for the generator and discriminator in the domain-adversarial training?
- How does the method handle the translation of different programming languages, especially those that have different syntax or structure?
- Could the authors elaborate on the decoupling of code skeleton transpilation and API keyword mapping, and how this improves the performance of the transpiler?
- Why was the decision made to not compare the proposed method against other state-of-the-art methods, and what would be the implications of such a comparison?
- The paper mentions that the method is decoupled into code skeleton transpilation and API keyword mapping. Could the authors provide more insights into how this decoupling affects the performance and applicability of the method?
- How does the method handle the translation of large codebases, and what are the scalability limits of the model?
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method. Could the authors provide a discussion on the potential limitations and future directions for research in this area?
