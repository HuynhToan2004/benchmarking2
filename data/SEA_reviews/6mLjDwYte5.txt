 **Summary:**
The paper explores the integration of instruction tuning with sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models to enhance the performance of large language models (LLMs) without increasing computational complexity. It demonstrates that MoE models, when combined with instruction tuning, can outperform dense models on various benchmark tasks. The authors conducted experiments across different settings, including direct fine-tuning, instruction tuning followed by few-shot or zero-shot generalization, and instruction tuning followed by task-specific fine-tuning. The results show that MoE models benefit significantly from instruction tuning, particularly in scenarios where computational resources are constrained. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty, as the combination of MoE and instruction tuning is a well-known approach. The experimental results, while showing improvements, are seen as not sufficiently distinct from existing models to warrant publication.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, with clear presentation of the research problem, methodology, and results.
- The empirical study is comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of instruction tuning for MoE models.
- The paper provides a detailed ablation study on various hyper-parameters, which is beneficial for future research.
- The authors conduct experiments across different settings, including direct fine-tuning, instruction tuning followed by few-shot or zero-shot generalization, and instruction tuning followed by task-specific fine-tuning.
- The paper demonstrates that instruction tuning can benefit MoE models, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as the combination of MoE and instruction tuning is a well-known approach.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently distinct from existing models like Flan-PaLM, with only marginal improvements observed.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting in MoE models, which could limit the practical applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational efficiency of MoE models compared to dense models, which is crucial for understanding the practical benefits of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed approach, such as the potential for overfitting and the need for a large number of experts which could increase memory requirements.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the computational efficiency of MoE models compared to dense models, especially in terms of FLOPs and memory requirements?
- How does the proposed approach compare to other existing methods that combine MoE and instruction tuning?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and inference costs of the proposed models, especially in comparison to dense models?
- How does the paper address the issue of overfitting in MoE models, particularly in the context of instruction tuning?
- Can the authors discuss the potential of using different routing strategies, such as expert-choice or token-choice, to improve the performance of MoE models?
