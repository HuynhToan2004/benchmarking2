 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GradSimCore, a method for selecting a coreset of representative training instances based on the similarity of loss gradients. This approach aims to identify samples that closely approximate the gradients of the entire training dataset, thereby potentially reducing computational requirements and improving training efficiency. The method is evaluated across various datasets, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet, and is shown to outperform existing coreset selection algorithms in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive theoretical analysis, and insufficient comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the proposed method is simple and easy to implement.
- The proposed method, GradSimCore, shows good performance in terms of accuracy and efficiency compared to other coreset selection algorithms.
- The method is intuitive and has potential for further improvement, making it a promising baseline for future research.
- The paper provides a detailed explanation of the algorithm and its implementation, which is crucial for reproducibility.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms other methods, especially in smaller coreset sizes, showing its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of both accuracy and efficiency.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it is a simple adaptation of existing methods like CRAIG and GRAD-MATCH.
- The paper does not provide a detailed theoretical analysis of the method, which could help in understanding its effectiveness and limitations.
- The experimental setup is not robust enough, with only a few different architectures and coreset sizes considered.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the experimental results and the methodology, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, including both accuracy and efficiency metrics?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency with other methods, especially in larger datasets like ImageNet-1K?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods like CRAIG and GRAD-MATCH, especially in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of the method, including the assumptions and limitations?
- How does the method perform in different architectures and coreset sizes, and what are the implications of these variations on the performance?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of architectures and coreset sizes, and the statistical significance of the results?
- How does the method handle the selection of hyperparameters, such as the threshold for neighborhood identification, and what are the implications of these choices on the performance?
