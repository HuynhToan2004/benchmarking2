 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel adversarial patch attack method using optimal transport to push the feature distribution of attacked images towards a predefined target distribution, aiming to enhance the transferability of the attack across different models. The approach is validated through extensive experiments on ImageNet-1K, showing superior transferability compared to existing methods. The methodology involves optimizing the Wasserstein loss to minimize the difference between the feature distributions of clean and perturbed images, which is claimed to be more effective in physical world scenarios. Despite the promising results, concerns are raised about the clarity of the experimental setup, the lack of detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, and the potential for overfitting due to the limited dataset size.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel adversarial patch attack method that utilizes optimal transport to minimize the difference between the feature distributions of clean and perturbed images, enhancing the transferability of the attack across various models.
- The method is well-motivated and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it outperforms existing methods in terms of transferability.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method across a wide range of models, including both CNNs and transformers, which is a significant strength in demonstrating the method's versatility.
- The paper includes both digital and physical experiments, which adds to the credibility of the results and shows the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in several experimental details, such as the choice of parameters like p and K, and the specifics of the experimental setup, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- There is a significant gap in the transferability results between the proposed method and existing methods, especially when compared to the TnT method, which raises concerns about the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods, such as M3D, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's advantages or limitations.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which detract from the professional quality of the work.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the choice of parameters p and K, and how these were determined?
- How does the proposed method compare to state-of-the-art methods like M3D, particularly in terms of transferability and robustness?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the data split and the choice of models for training and testing?
- How does the proposed method perform against different types of defenses, such as LGS and other robustification methods?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the transferability of the attack across different models, including the specific models used for training and testing?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of overfitting, and what measures were taken to prevent this during the experiments?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the target distribution and how it affects the transferability of the attack?
