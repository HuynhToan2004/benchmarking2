 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ViCor, a framework that integrates large language models (LLMs) with vision-and-language models (VLMs) to address visual commonsense reasoning (VCR) tasks. It categorizes VCR into two sub-problems: visual commonsense understanding (VCU) and visual commonsense inference (VCI). The proposed method leverages LLMs to classify the problem type and direct VLMs accordingly, enhancing the reasoning capabilities by allowing VLMs to focus on specific visual elements guided by LLMs. The paper presents a collaborative approach where LLMs actively direct VLMs to concentrate on relevant visual elements, which supports potential commonsense inferences. The framework is evaluated on two VCR benchmark datasets, showing promising results that outperform existing methods without in-domain supervised fine-tuning. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty and in-depth analysis, and the clarity of its methodological contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and challenging problem in the field of visual commonsense reasoning (VCR), which has not been extensively explored before.
- The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of the methodology and results, which aids in understanding the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to integrating large language models (LLMs) and vision-and-language models (VLMs) to address VCR tasks.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods without in-domain supervised fine-tuning.
- The paper includes an ablation study that helps in understanding the impact of different components of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its novelty and the depth of its contributions to the field.
- The methodology section is somewhat confusing, and the paper could benefit from clearer explanations and potentially restructuring the methodology into simpler steps.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its approach with state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the perceived effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper's reliance on LLMs for problem classification and VLM for visual commonsense understanding might be seen as a limitation, as it does not fully utilize the potential of LLMs in VCR tasks.
- The paper's experimental setup and the selection of datasets (specifically, the use of only two datasets) could be expanded to include more diverse and challenging datasets to validate the proposed method's generalizability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of their method to the field of VCR, particularly in terms of novelty and depth of analysis?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods, especially those that also leverage LLMs and VLMs for VCR tasks?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations and possibly restructure the methodology section to enhance the clarity and understanding of the proposed method?
- How does the paper address the potential limitations of relying on LLMs for problem classification and VLMs for visual commonsense understanding?
- Could the authors consider expanding the experimental setup to include more diverse and challenging datasets to validate the proposed method's generalizability?
