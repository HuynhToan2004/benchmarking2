 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the stability and convergence issues in Generative Adversarial Imitation Learning (GAIL) by treating it as a dynamic system and applying control theory. The authors propose a controlled GAIL algorithm that stabilizes the training process by adjusting the loss function with a controller, aiming to achieve asymptotic stability. Theoretical analysis is provided to support the claims, and experiments on MuJoCo tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the controlled GAIL algorithm in achieving better performance compared to vanilla GAIL. However, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental scope, lack of comprehensive theoretical analysis, and unclear presentation in some sections.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important issue in GAIL, namely the instability and convergence to a suboptimal solution, which is crucial for the practical application of GAIL.
- The approach of using control theory to analyze and stabilize the training process is innovative and provides a new perspective on addressing the challenges in GAIL.
- The theoretical analysis provided is rigorous, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed controlled GAIL algorithm, showing better performance compared to vanilla GAIL.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the controlled GAIL algorithm is simple to implement, potentially leading to better performance in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental evaluation is limited, focusing only on MuJoCo tasks, which may not generalize well to other environments or tasks.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other existing methods, such as those using dataset aggregation or behavioral cloning, which could provide a more comprehensive view of the performance of the proposed algorithm.
- The theoretical analysis is not fully comprehensive, particularly in terms of the assumptions made and the conditions required for the stability of the controlled GAIL algorithm.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, especially in sections where the notation and the flow of the content are unclear or confusing.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implications of the proposed method, such as the scalability and efficiency in more complex or larger-scale environments.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the controlled GAIL algorithm performs in more complex or larger-scale environments, and discuss any potential scalability issues?
- How does the performance of controlled GAIL compare to other methods like dataset aggregation or behavioral cloning, especially in terms of convergence speed and stability?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly in relation to the stability of the controlled GAIL algorithm?
- In the experimental results, why does the controlled GAIL algorithm not show a significant improvement in performance compared to vanilla GAIL in some environments, such as Hopper and Swimmer?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of mode collapse, which is a known problem in GAIL?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of the proposed method, such as its applicability in real-world scenarios and its impact on the efficiency and effectiveness of GAIL?
