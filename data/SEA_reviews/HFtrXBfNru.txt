 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the problem of temporal generalization in evolving graphs, focusing on the representation distortion caused by the evolution of graph structures and features. It introduces a theoretical framework to analyze the lower bound of representation distortion and proposes a method called SMART, which utilizes self-supervised graph reconstruction to minimize information loss. The method is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world evolving graphs, showing promising results. The paper also discusses the theoretical and practical implications of representation distortion and proposes a baseline for estimating temporal generalization performance without human annotation after deployment.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and practical problem of temporal generalization in evolving graphs, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- It provides a theoretical analysis of the representation distortion caused by graph evolution and proposes a lower bound of distortion, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method, SMART, is straightforward and effective, and is supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results, demonstrating its effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world evolving graphs.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is essential for a broad audience.
- The experimental results are promising, showing that the proposed method can achieve good estimation performance and reduce information loss effectively.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on node regression tasks, which may limit its applicability to other types of tasks such as node classification.
- The theoretical analysis and assumptions, particularly Assumption 1, are not adequately justified, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts, which are crucial for a comprehensive understanding of the technology.
- The experimental setup and results could be improved, particularly in terms of the clarity and comprehensiveness of the results, and the comparisons with other baselines.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous discussion on the limitations and potential improvements of the proposed method, as well as a more detailed comparison with existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions and their implications on the results, particularly Assumption 1?
- How does the proposed method perform on other types of tasks, such as node classification?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the datasets used and the training parameters?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and generalization performance?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential directions for future research?
