 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Projected Off-Policy Q-Learning (POP-QL), a novel actor-critic algorithm aimed at addressing the mismatch between off-policy data and the distribution visited by the learned policy. This method is designed to prevent divergence and reduce approximation error by reweighting off-policy samples and constraining the policy. Theoretical contributions are made by extending Kolter's 2011 work to the MDP setting and proving the convergence of TD updates under specific conditions. Experiments on standard benchmarks and in environments with suboptimal data-collection policies demonstrate the method's effectiveness. However, the paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods and does not convincingly demonstrate its superiority over state-of-the-art algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a clear structure, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed algorithm is simple and effective, with a theoretical analysis that is sound and well-explained.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to address the distribution shift problem in off-policy reinforcement learning, which is a significant challenge in the field.
- The experimental results are promising, showing that the proposed method outperforms other methods in some cases.
- The theoretical results are well-presented, and the paper effectively uses examples to illustrate the theoretical concepts.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other existing methods, particularly those that address similar issues in off-policy RL.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with the proposed method sometimes underperforming compared to other methods, especially in more challenging environments.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of variance in the policy updates, which is a significant concern in off-policy RL.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its approach, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational cost and scalability of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other existing methods, particularly those that address similar issues in off-policy RL?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational cost and scalability with other methods, especially in large-scale environments?
- Can the authors elaborate on the limitations of their approach and discuss when it might not be the best choice for solving off-policy RL problems?
- How does the proposed method handle the variance in policy updates, and what steps have been taken to mitigate this issue?
- In the experimental results, why does the proposed method sometimes underperform compared to other methods, and what could be the reasons for this?
