 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FAVOR, a novel multimodal learning framework designed to enhance the understanding of audio-visual inputs by multimodal large language models (LLMs). It employs a causal Q-Former structure and a diversity loss to capture the causal relations between audio-visual frames and to ensure diverse joint representations. FAVOR demonstrates strong performance across various audio-visual tasks, including zero-shot understanding of audio-visual tasks and co-reasoning abilities. The framework also proposes a new benchmark, AVEB, which evaluates the performance of audio-visual LLMs on single-modal and cross-modal tasks. Despite its innovative approach and promising results, the paper faces criticisms regarding the novelty of its contributions, the clarity of its methodology, and the fairness of its comparisons.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed FAVOR framework demonstrates strong performance across various audio-visual tasks, including zero-shot understanding of audio-visual tasks and co-reasoning abilities.
- The introduction of a new benchmark, AVEB, for evaluating the performance of audio-visual LLMs on single-modal and cross-modal tasks is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed model, including ablation studies and a detailed analysis of the proposed framework.
- The FAVOR model's ability to handle causal relations between audio-visual frames using the causal Q-Former structure is highlighted as a significant strength.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other existing models like Video-LLaMA and Macaw-LLM, which might have similar capabilities in handling audio-visual inputs.
- The methodology section is not clearly explained, particularly the synchronization module and the handling of different frame rates for audio and video.
- There is a lack of detailed explanation regarding the training data used, which is crucial for understanding the model's performance.
- The novelty of the contributions is questioned, as the use of causal Q-Former and diversity loss seems to be a straightforward extension of existing models like Q-Former.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of model size, which might limit the fairness of comparisons with other models.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims made about the model's performance, such as the 20% improvement in causal reasoning video QA tasks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other existing models like Video-LLaMA and Macaw-LLM, particularly in terms of handling audio-visual inputs?
- Could the authors clarify the methodology section, especially the synchronization module and the handling of different frame rates for audio and video?
- How does the FAVOR model handle the causal relations between audio-visual frames, and what specific advantages does it offer over other models in this regard?
- What are the specific contributions of the proposed model that differentiate it from existing models like Q-Former?
- Could the authors provide more detailed evidence to support the claims made about the model's performance, particularly the 20% improvement in causal reasoning video QA tasks?
- How does the FAVOR model handle the diversity loss during training, and what impact does it have on the model's performance?
