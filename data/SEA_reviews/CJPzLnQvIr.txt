 **Summary:**
The paper introduces QuickDrop, a novel federated unlearning method that leverages dataset distillation to reduce computational overhead during the unlearning and recovery phases. QuickDrop allows clients to generate a compact, distilled dataset, which significantly reduces the need for extensive computational resources compared to existing federated unlearning approaches. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets, showing a substantial reduction in computational costs. However, concerns are raised about the method's effectiveness in terms of its impact on model accuracy, particularly in scenarios involving multiple unlearning requests and large-scale datasets. The paper also faces criticism for its limited evaluation on small-scale datasets and the lack of a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel federated unlearning method, QuickDrop, which effectively utilizes dataset distillation to reduce computational overhead during the unlearning and recovery phases.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, with a well-structured presentation that is easy to follow.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, demonstrating its effectiveness in terms of computational efficiency and model accuracy.
- The method is evaluated on three standard datasets, showing a significant reduction in computational costs compared to existing federated unlearning approaches.
- The integration of dataset distillation into federated learning is a novel approach that reduces the computational overhead of unlearning and recovery phases.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art federated unlearning methods, which could help in understanding the relative performance of the proposed method.
- The method's effectiveness is not adequately demonstrated, particularly in scenarios involving multiple unlearning requests and large-scale datasets.
- The evaluation is limited to small-scale datasets, which may not adequately represent the performance of the method on larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not provide a theoretical analysis of the proposed method, which could enhance the understanding of its effectiveness and limitations.
- The method's performance is evaluated only on a few datasets, which may not be sufficient to generalize the findings across different datasets and scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the handling of multiple unlearning requests and the potential impact on model accuracy.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a comparison with state-of-the-art federated unlearning methods to better understand the relative performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios involving multiple unlearning requests, and what are the potential impacts on model accuracy?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the distilled dataset is used during the recovery phase, particularly in terms of its impact on model accuracy?
- Could the authors consider evaluating the method on larger, more complex datasets to validate its effectiveness in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential issue of data leakage during the distillation process, and what measures are in place to prevent this?
