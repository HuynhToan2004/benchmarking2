 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a Bayesian framework for joint reconstruction of multiple CT scans using Implicit Neural Representations (INRs). This framework leverages latent variables to capture inter-object relationships, enhancing the reconstruction quality by integrating prior information throughout the training process. The methodology is evaluated against various baselines, demonstrating improvements in reconstruction quality, susceptibility to overfitting, and generalizability. Despite its promising results, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, unclear presentation, and lack of comprehensive experimental validation, particularly in terms of computational complexity and real-world applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel Bayesian framework for joint reconstruction of multiple CT scans using Implicit Neural Representations (INRs), which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the proposed framework and its implementation.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The experiments conducted are extensive, providing a robust evaluation of the proposed method against various baselines.
- The paper effectively integrates prior information throughout the training process, which is crucial for enhancing the reconstruction quality.
- The use of latent variables to capture inter-object relationships is a novel approach that could lead to more accurate and efficient reconstructions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, particularly in the introduction and related work sections, which could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, especially in terms of computational complexity and real-world applicability.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it builds upon existing techniques such as INR-based CT reconstruction and Bayesian frameworks.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which is a significant concern in the application of INRs to CT reconstruction.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could help in understanding the relative performance of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in comparison to other methods?
- How does the proposed method perform in real-world scenarios, and what are the practical implications of its use in clinical settings?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the latent variables in the proposed method, particularly in terms of their contribution to the overall performance?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of overfitting, which is a known challenge in the application of INRs to CT reconstruction?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of their proposed method, and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of reconstruction quality, computational efficiency, and generalizability?
