 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to learning Nash equilibria in zero-sum games, focusing on the Regularized Follow-the-Regularized-Leader (RegFTRL) algorithm. RegFTRL extends the FTRL framework by incorporating an additional regularization term, which enhances convergence to Nash equilibria in both normal-form and extensive-form games. The paper also proposes a practical implementation, FollowMu, which integrates RegFTRL with neural networks for model-free learning. Theoretical analyses demonstrate that RegFTRL achieves linear convergence to approximate Nash equilibria without optimistic updates or assumptions of uniqueness. Empirical results validate the algorithm's effectiveness in various game scenarios, including both full-information and neural-based settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, RegFTRL, which is a significant contribution to the field of learning Nash equilibria in zero-sum games.
- RegFTRL offers a unique perspective by integrating regularization techniques with FTRL, providing a fresh angle on the problem of convergence to Nash equilibria.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- Theoretical analysis is robust, supported by both theoretical proofs and empirical results, which demonstrate the effectiveness of RegFTRL in various game scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations that aid in understanding the complex theoretical concepts.
- The proposed algorithm, RegFTRL, is shown to converge to an exact Nash equilibrium in extensive-form games, which is a notable achievement.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples in the preliminaries section to enhance comprehension for readers not familiar with the specific game types discussed.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly those that use regularization techniques, which could help position the work more effectively within the current research landscape.
- The assumptions used in the theoretical analysis are strong, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications and limitations of these assumptions.
- The paper's empirical results are limited to specific games, and it would be beneficial to include a broader range of games to validate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and computational efficiency of the proposed algorithms, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- The paper does not provide sufficient intuition or explanation for the choice of the regularization function used in the experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and applicability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the reference strategy in the theoretical results, particularly in relation to the assumptions made about the interior stationary point of the regularized equilibrium?
- How does the choice of the regularization function affect the theoretical and empirical results, and could the authors provide more details on this aspect?
- Is there a possibility to extend the theoretical results to include a general class of regularization functions, rather than being limited to specific forms like entropy regularization?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed algorithms, particularly in large-scale game scenarios?
- How do the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis translate to practical scenarios, and what are the implications if these assumptions are not met in real-world applications?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the regularization function in the experiments and discuss how this choice affects the convergence rate and the quality of the Nash equilibrium found?
