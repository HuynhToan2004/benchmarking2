 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel gradient approximation algorithm and a smooth computation graph aimed at addressing the challenges of long-horizon trajectory planning in nonlinear environments. The proposed method leverages gradient sampling and neurosymbolic encoding to improve the quality of stochastic gradients, thereby enhancing the scalability of backpropagation over long time-horizon trajectories. The approach is tested across various motion planning applications, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling complex spatio-temporal and sequential tasks. Despite the innovative approach, concerns are raised about the clarity of the presentation, the novelty of the contributions, and the thoroughness of the experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant challenge of training recurrent neural networks (RNNs) for long-horizon trajectory planning, proposing a novel gradient approximation algorithm and a smooth computation graph.
- The method is well-motivated and provides a clear and concise explanation of the problem and the proposed solution.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear presentation of the problem, methodology, and results, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method shows promising results in handling complex spatio-temporal and sequential tasks, demonstrating its potential to advance the field of learning-based control.
- The paper is original in its approach to handling long-horizon temporal tasks, which is a significant challenge in the field of autonomous agents.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from poor presentation, with several sections being hard to follow and not well-explained, particularly the use of notation and the explanation of the proposed method.
- The contributions of the paper are not clearly articulated, with some sections appearing to repeat existing ideas without sufficient novelty or depth.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not adequately compare its approach with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- The experimental results are not convincingly presented, with insufficient details on the experimental setup, and the paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation against relevant baselines.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of local optima in training RNNs, which is a critical challenge in long-horizon trajectory planning.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the use of notation, particularly in Section 3.1, and provide a clearer explanation of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing approaches in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially in handling long-horizon trajectories?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup and results, including the specifics of the neural network architecture and the training parameters used?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of local optima in training RNNs, and what strategies are employed to mitigate this issue?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their approach and how these might impact the practical applicability of their method in real-world scenarios?
