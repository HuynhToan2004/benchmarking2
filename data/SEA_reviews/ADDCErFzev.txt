 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of varying dropout rates on the robustness and dimensionality of representations in deep neural networks (DNNs), specifically focusing on AlexNet architectures. It explores how dropout affects the representational geometry of DNNs, comparing these effects to those observed in biological systems, such as the human visual cortex. The authors find that a dropout rate of 70% optimizes both model robustness and alignment with human brain representations. This study contributes to the understanding of how DNNs can mimic the trade-offs between sparse and distributed coding in biological systems, potentially advancing the field by providing insights into the neural mechanisms of robustness and representation in artificial and biological networks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely question regarding the trade-off between robustness and efficiency in neural network representations, using a simple yet effective approach of varying dropout rates.
- The experimental results are well-presented, with clear figures and a clear methodology that makes the paper easy to follow.
- The study extends previous work by considering the effects of dropout on model performance and robustness, and demonstrates that dropout can be used to balance efficiency and robustness in neural networks.
- The paper provides insights into the neural mechanisms of robustness and representation in artificial and biological networks, contributing to a better understanding of how neural networks mimic the trade-offs between sparse and distributed coding in biological systems.
- The results are robust, showing that the optimal dropout rate for balancing efficiency and robustness is around 70%, which is consistent across different models and datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the potential mechanisms behind the observed effects of dropout on model robustness and alignment with human brain representations.
- The novelty of the findings is limited as the paper primarily demonstrates that dropout can be used to balance efficiency and robustness in neural networks, which is a well-known property of dropout.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the work, including the generalizability of the findings to other model architectures and the implications of the results for the broader field of neural network research.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the effects of dropout to other regularization techniques, such as L1 and L2 penalties on weights, which could also influence the geometry of representations and their emergent robustness and brain correspondence.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the potential mechanisms behind the observed effects of dropout, as well as a broader comparison of dropout to other regularization techniques.

**Questions:**
- How does the dropout rate affect the trade-off between robustness and efficiency in neural networks? Are there any specific mechanisms that underlie this relationship?
- Can the findings of this study be generalized to other model architectures, such as transformers or convolutional neural networks?
- How do the results of this study compare to other regularization techniques, such as L1 and L2 penalties on weights?
- What are the implications of the findings for the broader field of neural network research, particularly in terms of developing more robust and efficient neural networks?
- How does the paper's approach to measuring robustness compare to other methods, such as those used in previous studies that have found different optimal dropout rates?
