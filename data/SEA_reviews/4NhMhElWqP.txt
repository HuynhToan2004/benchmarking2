 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the DAM (Deep Data-Dependent Approximate Analytical Model), a novel approach to time series forecasting that utilizes a long-tail sampling method to ingest irregularly sampled data and outputs basis coefficients of a continuous function of time. The model's architecture includes a transformer backbone and a history sampling regime (HSR) that ensures efficient access to the distant past while maintaining focus on recent history. The DAM is evaluated across multiple datasets, demonstrating superior performance in zero-shot transfer and very-long-term forecasting compared to existing state-of-the-art methods. The paper also explores the model's interpretability and flexibility, making it a significant contribution to the field of time series forecasting.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to time series forecasting with a focus on handling irregularly sampled data, which is a significant challenge in the field.
- The use of a long-tail sampling method and the transformer backbone is innovative and well-explained, providing a robust framework for handling diverse data distributions and patterns.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the model's architecture and training procedures, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The empirical results demonstrate the model's effectiveness in zero-shot transfer and very-long-term forecasting, showcasing its practical applicability and potential for real-world applications.
- The inclusion of an ablation study and visualizations of attention weights and basis coefficients enhances the understanding of the model's inner workings and its ability to capture long-term trends.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the model, particularly concerning its performance on datasets with sharp changes and its inability to handle multivariate forecasting.
- The choice of using basis functions for time series forecasting is not convincingly justified, especially when compared to other methods like PatchTST.
- The model's reliance on a transformer backbone and the use of a history sampling regime (HSR) might limit its applicability to other types of data distributions or patterns.
- There is a lack of comparison with other advanced time series forecasting models, such as those using frequency-domain modeling or other multi-scale modeling techniques.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of missing data or the potential for overfitting due to the model's complexity and the need for extensive training.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the model handles datasets with sharp changes or irregular data distributions?
- How does the model perform when applied to multivariate forecasting tasks?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of using basis functions for time series forecasting and discuss how this approach compares to other methods?
- How does the model ensure interpretability, and what specific advantages does it offer over other methods in this regard?
- Could the authors elaborate on the training process and the choice of hyperparameters, particularly how these were optimized for different datasets?
- How does the model handle the issue of missing data, and what strategies are employed to mitigate the potential for overfitting?
