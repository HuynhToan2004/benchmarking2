 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SEABO, a method for offline imitation learning (IL) that utilizes a KD-tree to assign rewards based on the proximity of unlabeled transitions to expert demonstrations. The method constructs a KD-tree from expert demonstrations, allowing it to efficiently search for the nearest neighbor in the dataset, which is used to assign a reward signal. This method is evaluated on various D4RL datasets, showing that it can enhance the performance of existing offline RL algorithms. Despite its simplicity and the potential for computational efficiency, concerns were raised about the method's scalability, the need for more complex datasets, and the lack of comparison with certain baseline methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the proposed method is simple and effective, enhancing the performance of offline RL algorithms.
- The method does not require additional processing on the offline dataset, which is a significant advantage over some other methods.
- The idea of using a KD-tree to assign rewards based on proximity to expert demonstrations is innovative and can be applied to various offline RL algorithms.
- The paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation, comparing the proposed method with a variety of baselines, and demonstrates its effectiveness in improving the performance of existing methods.
- The method does not require training reward models or discriminators, which can save computational resources.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's scalability is a concern, as it requires the construction of a KD-tree from the entire expert dataset, which could be computationally expensive and memory-intensive for large datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for distribution shift between the expert dataset and the unlabeled dataset, which could affect the performance of the method.
- The evaluation is limited to simpler datasets, and the method's performance on more complex or diverse datasets is not explored.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the potential limitations of the method, such as its applicability to environments with high-dimensional state spaces or complex tasks.
- There is a lack of comparison with certain baseline methods, such as DemoDICE and SMODICE, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the method's effectiveness.
- The method's reliance on the KD-tree for nearest neighbor search might not be the most efficient or effective approach for all datasets, as other methods like HNSW or Ball-tree might perform better in certain scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the method handle the potential for distribution shift between the expert dataset and the unlabeled dataset?
- Could the method be adapted to work with more complex or diverse datasets, such as those found in the AntMaze or Adroit domains?
- How does the method perform when the expert demonstrations are not from the same distribution as the unlabeled dataset?
- Is there a possibility to use a different distance metric or a more advanced search algorithm to improve the method's performance?
- How does the method compare to other offline imitation learning methods that do not require expert demonstrations, such as those based on energy models?
- Can the method be extended to handle environments with high-dimensional state spaces or complex tasks, such as those found in robotics or autonomous driving?
