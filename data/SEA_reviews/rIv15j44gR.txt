 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the estimation of heterogeneous treatment effects (HTE) in scenarios where treatment outcomes are not immediately observable, instead requiring a delay before the outcome is known. This delay is modeled as a latent variable, which complicates the estimation process. The authors propose a method called CFR-DF, which utilizes a modified EM algorithm to estimate both the eventual potential outcomes and the response times. This method is tested on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in estimating HTE with delayed responses. The paper also discusses the challenges of delayed feedback in HTE estimation and provides theoretical proofs and empirical evidence to support its approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodologies and theoretical proofs.
- The problem of delayed feedback in HTE estimation is addressed, which is a significant issue in real-world applications.
- The proposed method, CFR-DF, is novel and provides a principled learning approach that utilizes the EM algorithm to estimate potential outcomes and response times.
- The paper includes extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The theoretical framework is solid, and the proofs are provided in the appendix, which adds to the credibility of the paper.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the assumptions made, particularly the unconfoundedness and time independence assumptions, which are crucial for the validity of the results.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the methodology, especially in the EM algorithm section, which could benefit from additional examples or visual aids.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that handle delayed feedback in HTE estimation.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the assumptions required for identifiability and the potential biases introduced by the EM algorithm.
- The paper could be improved by including more realistic datasets and scenarios that better reflect real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the unconfoundedness and time independence assumptions?
- How does the proposed method handle the confounding bias introduced by covariates?
- What are the implications of the time sufficiency assumption, and how does it affect the identifiability of the causal parameters?
- Can you provide more details on the choice of datasets used in the experiments, particularly why the datasets were chosen and how they reflect real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform when the observation time is short, and what are the implications for the accuracy of the HTE estimates?
- Could you discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential avenues for future research to address these limitations?
