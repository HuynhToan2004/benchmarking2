 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called the Stochastic Resonance Transformer (SRT) aimed at improving the performance of pre-trained Vision Transformers (ViTs) by addressing quantization artifacts. SRT operates by perturbing input images with random translations and then averaging the outputs to enhance spatial details. This method is demonstrated to be effective in various dense prediction tasks, including video object segmentation and monocular depth prediction, without requiring additional training or modifications to the ViT architecture. The authors also discuss potential limitations and future work, including the impact of SRT on inference speed and the need for further validation on a broader range of tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, the Stochastic Resonance Transformer (SRT), which effectively super-resolves ViT embeddings without additional training or modifications to the ViT forward pass.
- SRT is a versatile visualization tool that can be applied to any layer of any pre-trained ViT model, offering valuable insights into ViT model characteristics.
- The enhanced embeddings from SRT can be seamlessly integrated into any task that utilizes ViT as a feature extractor, thereby serving as a test-time augmentation and ensemble method.
- SRT demonstrates significant enhancements on a range of diverse vision tasks, including dense prediction tasks, and shows improvements in video object segmentation, monocular depth prediction, and unsupervised salient region segmentation.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational cost of SRT, particularly how it affects inference speed.
- The method's effectiveness is not validated on a broader range of tasks beyond dense prediction, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not compare SRT with other existing methods that use ensembling or averaging of outputs, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The performance improvement of SRT is not substantial, and the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the observed improvements are not due to increased awareness of semantic boundaries in images.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of SRT and does not provide a thorough analysis of the computational cost and inference time of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of the concept of "super-resolving" and does not discuss the impact of SRT on the inference speed of ViT models.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost of SRT, particularly how it affects inference speed?
- How does the proposed method perform on a broader range of tasks beyond dense prediction?
- Can the authors compare SRT with other existing methods that use ensembling or averaging of outputs to provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method?
- Is there a plan to validate the effectiveness of SRT on a wider range of tasks to confirm its generalizability?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of SRT and provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost and inference time of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform on tasks that do not require high-resolution features, such as image classification?
- Can the authors provide more details on the concept of "super-resolving" and explain how SRT achieves this effect?
