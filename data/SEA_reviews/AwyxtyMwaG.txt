 **Summary:**
The paper explores the phenomenon of function vectors (FVs) in large language models (LLMs), focusing on their role in in-context learning (ICL) tasks. FVs are characterized as vectors within hidden states that trigger specific procedures in LLMs, showing robustness across different contexts. The authors employ causal mediation analysis to identify these vectors and demonstrate their effectiveness in various tasks, including semantic vector composition. Despite the novelty of the concept, the paper's experimental scope is limited, focusing on only a few tasks and models, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the findings. Additionally, the paper's presentation and clarity could be improved to enhance understanding and reproducibility.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel concept of function vectors (FVs) within hidden states of LLMs, which are causally related to task performance and can be extracted and manipulated.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it easy to follow, and the paper is well-written, with clear and concise descriptions of the experimental setup and results.
- The paper presents a significant contribution to the understanding of how LLMs process and store information, potentially advancing the field of mechanistic interpretability.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of FVs in various tasks and their generalizability across different models.
- The paper is original in its approach, focusing on the causal effects of attention heads in LLMs, which is a less explored area in previous studies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive analysis of the compositionality of FVs, which is crucial for understanding their utility in more complex tasks.
- The experimental scope is limited, focusing on only a few tasks and models, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's presentation and clarity could be improved, with some sections being difficult to read and understand, and a lack of intuitive explanations for certain concepts and figures.
- The methodology relies heavily on the causal mediation analysis, which may not fully capture the complexity of the task-related information within the LLMs.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training of the models used, which is crucial for reproducibility and understanding the experimental setup.
- The paper's claims about the generalizability of FVs across different tasks and models are not convincingly supported by the experimental evidence.

**Questions:**
- How do FVs perform in more complex tasks, such as those involving natural language generation?
- Can FVs be used to enhance the performance of LLMs in zero-shot settings?
- How do FVs compare to other methods of encoding tasks within LLMs, such as task vectors or soft prompts?
- How do FVs affect the training dynamics of LLMs, particularly in terms of the distribution of attention weights and the overall model performance?
- Can FVs be used to improve the interpretability of LLMs, by providing insights into the mechanisms underlying task performance?
- How do FVs relate to other forms of task-related information within LLMs, such as soft prompts or task vectors?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the training of the models used, including the training data and hyperparameters, to enhance reproducibility and transparency?
