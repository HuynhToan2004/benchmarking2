 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PEAR, a novel hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework that leverages adaptive relabeling of expert demonstrations to generate subgoal supervision. PEAR integrates reinforcement learning and imitation learning to optimize HRL agents, providing theoretical bounds on sub-optimality and a generalized plug-and-play framework. The method is evaluated on both simulated and real-world robotic tasks, demonstrating improved performance and sample efficiency over existing methods. Despite its innovative approach, concerns are raised about the clarity of presentation, the depth of experimental validation, and the novelty of the method compared to existing works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) using adaptive relabeling of expert demonstrations, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the algorithm and its components, making it accessible to readers.
- The paper provides theoretical analysis, including bounds on sub-optimality and a generalized plug-and-play framework, which adds a solid theoretical foundation to the proposed method.
- The empirical results show that PEAR outperforms several baselines in both simulation and real-world experiments, demonstrating its practical effectiveness.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear figures and a logical structure that facilitates understanding of the proposed method and its implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the theoretical contributions, particularly in the context of HRL and the role of imitation learning (IL) in the proposed method.
- There is a noticeable absence of related work in the field of HRL, which could have provided a more robust context for the research.
- The experimental setup and results are not adequately detailed, with insufficient ablation studies and a lack of discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on performance.
- The paper suffers from inconsistencies in terminology and notation, which can confuse readers and detract from the clarity of the presentation.
- There are several minor typographical and formatting errors that need correction for a more professional presentation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role of imitation learning (IL) in the proposed method and how it contributes to the overall performance of PEAR?
- Why was the decision made to use a single expert trajectory for relabeling, and how does this affect the generalization of the method?
- How does the choice of the Q-value threshold affect the performance of PEAR, and what are the implications of using a low threshold?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the theoretical contributions, particularly in the context of HRL and the role of IL?
- How does the paper address the issue of non-stationarity in HRL, and what are the specific contributions of PEAR in this regard?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of PEAR, and consider including more extensive ablation studies to better understand the method's robustness and generalizability?
