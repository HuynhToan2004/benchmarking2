 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ZegOT, a method for zero-shot semantic segmentation that leverages the optimal transport of text prompts to pixel embeddings from a frozen CLIP encoder. This approach, which includes a Multi-Prompt Optimal Transport Solver (MPOT), aims to diversify the learned knowledge and robustly handle unseen categories. Extensive experiments across three benchmark datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of ZegOT, showing superior performance compared to previous methods. However, concerns about the novelty and the necessity of the MPOT module persist, as the method heavily relies on pre-trained CLIP models and the use of MPOT seems overly complex for its supposed benefits.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and includes extensive experiments, which are well-documented in the supplementary materials.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with a clear motivation and a good explanation of the method.
- The paper demonstrates significant performance improvements over previous methods, showing superior results on various benchmark datasets.
- The introduction of optimal transport for prompt learning is innovative and adds a new perspective to the field.
- The method is well-motivated and effectively avoids overfitting on seen classes by using a frozen CLIP encoder and a small number of learnable parameters.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper heavily relies on the pre-trained CLIP model, which may limit its applicability to other models or datasets.
- The use of the MPOT module, while innovative, is not clearly justified in terms of its necessity or effectiveness compared to simpler matching methods.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its practical applicability.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned, as it builds upon existing methods and the MPOT module appears to be a minor modification rather than a significant innovation.
- The method's complexity, particularly in the use of multiple layers of pixel embeddings and the MPOT module, could make it difficult to implement and optimize.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the claimed superiority of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the MPOT module compare to simpler matching methods, such as self-attention or bipartite matching?
- Can the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the MPOT module in the context of the proposed method?
- Is there a risk of overfitting the model to the seen classes, especially given the use of a frozen CLIP encoder and a small number of learnable parameters?
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets or with different pre-trained models, and what are the implications of these variations on the performance?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and inference speed of the proposed method compared to existing methods?
- What are the specific limitations of the proposed method, and how do these affect its practical applicability?
