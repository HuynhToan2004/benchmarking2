 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SFAVEL, an unsupervised pretraining framework for fact verification that leverages pretrained language models to distill self-supervised features into high-quality claim-fact alignments. This method uses a contrastive loss function to encourage feature alignment between claims and evidence, while preserving semantic relationships across corpora. The framework is evaluated on the FEVER fact verification benchmark, achieving state-of-the-art results with a linear evaluation. The approach aims to address the challenge of unsupervised fact verification by producing semantically meaningful and compact features for claims, which can be aligned with source information.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear description of the proposed method.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on the FEVER fact verification benchmark with a linear evaluation, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The use of pre-trained language models to distill self-supervised features into high-quality claim-fact alignments is a novel approach that could potentially reduce the need for annotated data.
- The paper includes a comprehensive ablation study that shows the impact of different components of the loss function, providing insights into the method's effectiveness.
- The method's potential to reduce the need for annotated data is significant, as it could make fact verification more accessible and cost-effective.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential challenges.
- The comparison with other methods, particularly those that use different backbones or different types of knowledge graphs, is not comprehensive, which could affect the evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not provide detailed insights into the design of the knowledge model, which could be crucial for understanding the method's performance.
- The evaluation is limited to the FEVER dataset, which might not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the method to other datasets or domains.
- The paper does not discuss the potential impact of the method on the hallucination problem in fact verification, which is a significant concern in the field.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed explanation of the design of the knowledge model and how it impacts the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform on other fact verification datasets, such as the FB15k-237 dataset?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of the method on the hallucination problem in fact verification?
- Why was the decision made to use a random knowledge model as the backbone, and what are the implications of this choice for the method's performance?
- How does the method handle the issue of fact hallucination, and what steps have been taken to mitigate this issue?
