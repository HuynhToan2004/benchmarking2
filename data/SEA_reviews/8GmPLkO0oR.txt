 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DNAct, a novel method for learning multi-task policies in robotic manipulation, integrating NeRF pre-training and diffusion training to enhance semantic understanding and multi-modality in action sequences. DNAct leverages pre-trained 2D features from stable diffusion models to learn 3D semantic representations, which are then used to guide diffusion training for policy learning. The method is evaluated on both simulation and real-robot environments, showing improved performance over existing baselines. Despite its innovative approach, concerns were raised about the clarity of the paper, the need for more detailed comparisons, and the potential limitations in generalization to more complex or diverse scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to learning multi-task policies in robotic manipulation, combining NeRF pre-training and diffusion training, which is a new idea in this field.
- The methodology is clearly described, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed approach.
- The results are impressive, showing significant improvements over existing baselines in both simulation and real-robot environments.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to read, which is crucial for academic publications.
- The authors provide a clear motivation for their work and describe the method in a detailed and coherent manner, which enhances the understanding of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency.
- The method's generalization to more complex or diverse scenarios is not thoroughly explored, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in the clarity of figures and the explanation of certain methodological aspects.
- There is a need for more detailed experimental setups and results, including the specifics of the demonstration collection and the evaluation metrics used.
- The paper's reliance on pre-trained 2D features from stable diffusion models may limit its adaptability to different scenarios or environments.
- The method's performance is not compared across different tasks, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to more complex or diverse scenarios?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the diffusion model in the training process?
- How are the demonstrations collected, and what are the specifics of the evaluation metrics used in the experiments?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training data and the generalization capabilities of the model to different environments or tasks?
- How does the model handle the multi-modality of the demonstrations, and what are the implications of this approach in terms of generalization and adaptability?
