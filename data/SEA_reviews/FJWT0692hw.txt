 **Summary:**
The paper explores a novel training objective for autoregressive language models, focusing on minimizing divergences between occupancy measures to address the compounding error problem. It introduces a backspace action to allow models to correct errors and formulates the sequence generation problem as an imitation learning problem. The approach is evaluated using a SequenceMatch loss, which is shown to outperform traditional maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in text generation and arithmetic tasks. The paper also discusses the use of occupancy measures and divergences to improve model performance, particularly in handling out-of-distribution (OOD) sequences. Despite its theoretical contributions and empirical results, the paper faces criticism for its limited evaluation scope and the need for a broader range of experiments to substantiate its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the compounding error problem in autoregressive sequence generation by formulating the problem as an imitation learning problem and developing a novel masking scheme.
- It introduces a new training objective, SequenceMatch, which is shown to improve performance over traditional maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in text generation and arithmetic tasks.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations, making it accessible to readers.
- The use of occupancy measures and divergences to improve model performance is an interesting approach, and the paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the advantages of different divergences.
- The paper's methodology is based on the IQ-learn algorithm, which is a well-known method in imitation learning, and it effectively utilizes this framework to address the compounding error problem.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks comprehensive experimental results, particularly in terms of the diversity of experiments. It only evaluates the proposed method on two different tasks and does not include a broader range of experiments to substantiate its claims.
- The paper's claims about the compounding error problem are not well-supported, and the evidence provided is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the problem's existence.
- The experimental setup and results are not clearly presented, which makes it difficult for readers to understand the significance and implications of the findings.
- The paper's reliance on a specific divergence (chi2-divergence) might limit its applicability to other types of models or tasks, and the reasons for choosing this divergence over others are not adequately explained.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its approach, such as the computational overhead of the SequenceMatch loss and the potential for the backspace action to be overused or misused by the model.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and significance of the backspace action in the model's performance? How does it affect the model's behavior, especially in terms of its ability to correct errors and generate fluent sequences?
- How does the SequenceMatch loss compare to other training objectives, such as those based on different divergences or those that do not include the backspace action?
- In the context of the arithmetic task, how does the model handle different types of errors, such as incorrect base or incorrect operation? Can it adapt its behavior to different types of errors, or is it limited to correcting specific types of errors?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the arithmetic task and the model's performance metrics? How do these metrics compare to those of other models, and what do they reveal about the model's effectiveness?
- The paper discusses the use of occupancy measures and divergences to improve model performance. Can the authors elaborate on how these measures are used in practice and what specific benefits they offer over other methods?
