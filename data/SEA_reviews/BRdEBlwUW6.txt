 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the robust fairness problem in adversarial training, focusing on the impact of inter-class similarity on class performance. It presents a theoretical analysis and empirical evidence showing that class-wise distance significantly affects the difficulty of classes, especially in adversarial settings. Based on these findings, the authors propose a novel method called Distance-Aware Fair Adversarial Training (DAFA), which adjusts loss weights and adversarial margins based on inter-class distances to enhance the robustness of adversarial training. The method aims to improve the performance of hard classes without sacrificing the performance of easy classes, as demonstrated through experiments on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and STL-10 datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to addressing the robust fairness problem in adversarial training by focusing on inter-class similarity, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors conduct both theoretical and empirical analyses, which provide strong evidence for the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, DAFA, is simple yet effective, and the experimental results demonstrate its superiority over existing methods.
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the robust fairness problem, which is a timely and relevant topic in machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the experimental setup and the choice of datasets.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to binary classification tasks, which may not fully capture the complexity of multi-class scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There are some issues with the clarity and presentation of the paper, such as unclear figures and inconsistencies in the experimental setup.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method to large-scale datasets, which is a significant limitation for its practical application.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies in the experimental setup, such as the warm-up epoch and the choice of datasets, compared to existing methods?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different model architectures, such as ViT and LSTM?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical analysis, particularly how it applies to multi-class scenarios?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues when applied to large-scale datasets?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational complexity and training time?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between average and worst-case robust accuracy, especially in the context of the proposed method?
