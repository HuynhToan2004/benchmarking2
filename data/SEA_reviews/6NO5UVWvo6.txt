 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for medical image segmentation, specifically targeting medical organs in CT and MRI scans. The method leverages a point-supervised contrastive variance (PSCV) loss function to exploit the spatial distribution properties of organs and their variance distribution map representations, aiming to enhance the model's discriminative and smooth segmentation capacity. The proposed method is evaluated on two medical image datasets and shows superior performance compared to existing weakly supervised methods. The paper is commended for its innovative approach in using contrastive variance loss to improve segmentation accuracy with limited annotations. However, concerns are raised about the lack of comprehensive ablation studies and the absence of comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and presents a novel approach to medical image segmentation using point-supervised contrastive variance loss, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method is technically sound and shows promising results, demonstrating its effectiveness through experiments and ablation studies.
- The paper addresses a practical problem of limited annotations in medical imaging, which is a common issue in the field.
- The use of contrastive learning to enhance segmentation accuracy with limited annotations is an innovative approach that could be beneficial for other medical imaging tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study, particularly regarding the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the proposed method.
- There is a noticeable absence of comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer benchmark for the proposed method's performance.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse datasets and evaluating the method's performance under different conditions, such as varying batch sizes and different backbone networks.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant omission given the medical application of the work.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different medical imaging modalities, such as X-ray or ultrasound images?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential directions for future research?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and the methodology used for data splitting?
- How does the proposed method handle class imbalance, which is a common issue in medical imaging datasets?
- What are the potential applications of the proposed method beyond medical imaging, and how might it be adapted for other domains?
