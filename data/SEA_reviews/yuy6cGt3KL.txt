 **Summary:**
The paper investigates model selection in conditional average treatment effect (CATE) estimation, focusing on binary treatments and nuisance models. It evaluates 34 metrics across 78 datasets, employing AutoML for hyperparameter tuning and causal ensembling. The study highlights the effectiveness of doubly robust and TMLE-based metrics, and introduces new metrics like adaptive propensity clipping and targeted learning. Despite the comprehensive experimental setup and the variety of metrics tested, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of theoretical insights, limited novelty, and potential issues in the clarity and presentation of results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental setup is comprehensive, including a large number of datasets and CATE estimators.
- The introduction of novel metrics, such as adaptive propensity clipping and targeted learning, adds depth to the evaluation of CATE model selection.
- The use of AutoML for hyperparameter tuning and causal ensembling for model selection enhances the practical applicability of the findings.
- The paper provides a thorough comparison of various metrics and CATE estimators, contributing to a better understanding of their relative performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks theoretical insights, which could help in understanding the performance of different metrics and estimators.
- The novelty of the paper is limited as many of the proposed metrics are adaptations of existing methods.
- The results are presented in a confusing manner, with inconsistencies between tables and the text, and potential errors in the data analysis.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the study, including the assumptions made and the potential impact of these assumptions on the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the findings beyond the specific datasets and methods used.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies in the results, particularly the unexpected outcomes in Table 1 and the inconsistencies between tables?
- How does the paper address the generalizability of the findings to other datasets and settings?
- Could the authors provide more theoretical insights into the performance of different metrics and estimators?
- What are the implications of the assumptions made in the study, and how do they affect the validity and reliability of the results?
- How do the authors justify the choice of metrics and estimators, especially in light of the limited novelty and the potential for overfitting in the model selection process?
