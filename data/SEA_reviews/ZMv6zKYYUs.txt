 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to data assimilation for semi-linear PDEs using a neural operator framework. The proposed method, termed NODA, integrates prediction and correction operations to estimate solutions to PDEs from sparse, noisy measurements. This is achieved by leveraging the structure of semilinear PDEs and the observer design from function spaces. The methodology is validated through experiments on the Kuramoto-Sivashinsky, Navier-Stokes, and Korteweg-de Vries equations, demonstrating robustness to noise and the ability to correct predictions over long time horizons. However, the paper's experimental scope is limited, and it lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, which might limit its applicability to more complex or realistic scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The problem of data assimilation for semi-linear PDEs is relevant and the proposed solution is novel, using neural operators for prediction and correction.
- The methodology is theoretically grounded and is supported by experiments that demonstrate its effectiveness, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and handling noise in measurements.
- The paper provides a systematic approach for data assimilation, which is crucial for improving the estimation quality and correcting evolving trajectories based on sparse, noisy measurements.
- The approach is flexible and can be adapted to various PDEs, including those with different measurement operators and noise levels.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly those that use neural operators for data assimilation, such as those mentioned in [1].
- The experimental scope is limited to simple PDEs, which might not fully demonstrate the method's capabilities in more complex or realistic scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its methodology, which could be a significant drawback for readers looking to apply the method in different contexts.
- The paper's approach might be seen as incremental as it heavily relies on existing works, particularly those by Afshar et al. (2023), without sufficient novelty or significant advancement over these prior works.
- The paper's experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with limited validation on different initial conditions and a lack of discussion on the impact of different hyperparameters on the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific advantages of their method over existing methods, particularly those mentioned in [1]?
- How does the method perform when applied to more complex or realistic PDEs, such as those involving high-dimensional systems or more complex nonlinearities?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their methodology and how these might affect its applicability in different scenarios?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the measurement operator is unknown, and how does this impact the estimation accuracy?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the choice of initial conditions and the impact of different hyperparameters on the results?
- How does the method perform in terms of computational complexity and scalability, especially when dealing with large-scale systems?
