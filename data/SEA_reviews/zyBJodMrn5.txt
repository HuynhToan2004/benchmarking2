 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new benchmark named gCOG, designed to evaluate the generalization capabilities of neural networks in multimodal reasoning tasks. This benchmark extends the COG task by incorporating compositional generalization splits and multimodal inputs, focusing on three types of generalization: distractor, systematic, and productive. The study examines various neural network architectures including RNNs, GRUs, and transformers, to understand the impact of architecture on generalization performance. The results suggest that transformer-based models, particularly those with cross-attention mechanisms, perform better in certain types of generalization, such as distractor and systematic generalization. However, the paper does not adequately address the novelty of the task design or the generalizability of the findings beyond the specific tasks evaluated.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical flow of information.
- The introduction of a new benchmark, gCOG, is a significant contribution to the field, providing a useful tool for future research in multimodal reasoning.
- The paper is original in its approach, introducing a new benchmark that extends prior work by including multimodal inputs and a new task design.
- The results are well-presented, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the findings.
- The paper addresses an important and timely question regarding the generalization capabilities of neural networks, particularly in multimodal reasoning tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the task design is questionable, as it closely resembles previous works such as COG and gSCAN, with only minor modifications.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the generalizability of the findings beyond the specific tasks evaluated, which could limit the practical impact of the research.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the study, such as the choice of model architectures and the scope of the generalization benchmark.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of model architectures and the potential impact of different architectural components on generalization performance.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims made about the effectiveness of cross-attention mechanisms in enhancing generalization performance.
- The paper could be improved by including more diverse model architectures and conducting experiments on larger datasets to strengthen the claims made.

**Questions:**
- How does the performance of the models on the gCOG task compare to their performance on other multimodal reasoning tasks?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of model architectures and the rationale behind the specific configurations used?
- How do the findings from this study translate to more complex or realistic multimodal reasoning tasks?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the training data and hyperparameters used?
- What are the potential implications of the findings for the development of more generalizable neural network architectures?
- How does the gCOG task compare to other existing benchmarks in terms of its ability to evaluate generalization capabilities?
