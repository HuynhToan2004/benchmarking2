 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel exploration strategy for deep Q-learning (DQN) called beta-DQN, which leverages a behavior function learned from replay memory to construct diverse policies. This method differentiates actions based on their frequency at each state, improving exploration and reducing computational overhead. The beta-DQN framework includes a Q-function for in-sample learning, a Q-mask for action value reduction, and a behavior function for action selection. The paper also includes experiments on the MiniGrid and MinAtar environments, demonstrating improvements over standard DQN in some cases. However, the paper has been critiqued for its incremental novelty, lack of comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, and limited evaluation in diverse environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel exploration strategy for deep Q-learning (DQN) that effectively enhances exploration and reduces computational overhead.
- The proposed method, beta-DQN, is straightforward to implement and does not require significant hyperparameter tuning, making it accessible for a broad range of tasks.
- The experiments conducted on the MiniGrid and MinAtar environments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over standard DQN in some cases.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive discussion on the methodology and its implementation.
- The proposed method addresses the challenge of efficient exploration in reinforcement learning, which is crucial for advancing the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which is essential for establishing the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method.
- The evaluation of the proposed method is limited to only two environments, which does not sufficiently demonstrate its generalizability across diverse environments.
- The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the computational overhead introduced by the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its scope and applicability.
- The method's performance in diverse environments and its sensitivity to hyperparameters are not thoroughly analyzed, which are crucial for understanding its robustness and effectiveness.

**Questions:**

- What is the computational overhead introduced by the proposed method, and how does it compare to other methods in terms of efficiency?
- How sensitive is the proposed method to hyperparameters, and what are the implications of this sensitivity for its practical application?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the method's performance in diverse environments and its sensitivity to hyperparameters to better understand its robustness and effectiveness?
