 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of two long-tailed learning methods, Contrastive Loss and PLM Loss, to the trajectory prediction task using the Trajectron++ model on the NuScenes dataset. The primary goal is to evaluate the effectiveness of these methods in addressing the long-tail problem in trajectory prediction. The study employs the KDE metric to assess the performance of the models in predicting trajectories for both pedestrians and vehicles. Despite the straightforward approach and the use of a well-known dataset, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and lack of significant contributions. The reviewers suggest that the paper could benefit from a broader range of datasets and a more rigorous evaluation, including the use of more recent and varied baseline models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The study addresses an important problem in the field of trajectory prediction, focusing on long-tailed learning methods which are crucial for predicting rare and complex behaviors.
- The use of the KDE metric to evaluate the performance of trajectory prediction models is a significant contribution, as it allows for a more comprehensive assessment of the model's ability to handle the full distribution of trajectories.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed methods, including both quantitative and qualitative analyses, which helps in understanding the effectiveness of the long-tailed learning methods in trajectory prediction.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily applies existing long-tailed learning methods to a new dataset without introducing significant new insights or contributions.
- The evaluation is limited to a single dataset, which might not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other recent and relevant works in the field, which could provide a more robust validation of the proposed methods.
- The paper's focus on the Trajectron++ model limits the scope of its findings, as it does not explore how the methods might perform with different model architectures or datasets.
- There are concerns about the fairness of the comparison due to differences in model training details, such as the use of different datasets and training parameters.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the study, which could include the generalizability of the findings to other datasets or the impact of different model architectures.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other recent and relevant works in the field, especially those that have used different datasets or model architectures?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the Trajectron++ model as the baseline, and what would be the implications of using different model architectures?
- Could the authors clarify the differences in model training details, such as the use of different datasets and training parameters, and how these affect the fairness of the comparison?
- Given the limitations of the study, what are the authors' plans for future research, and how might they address the concerns regarding the generalizability of the findings?
