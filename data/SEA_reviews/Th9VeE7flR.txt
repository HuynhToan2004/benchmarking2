 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for source-free domain adaptation, focusing on label propagation to enhance pseudo-label reliability. It employs an affinity matrix derived from both current and historical models to refine pseudo-labels, mitigating the confirmation bias in self-training. The approach leverages batch normalization adaptation and data augmentation to improve model performance. Extensive experiments are conducted on various datasets, demonstrating the method's effectiveness. However, the paper lacks comparisons with some state-of-the-art methods and does not thoroughly address the impact of different hyperparameters on performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to source-free domain adaptation, focusing on label propagation and affinity matrix fusion to refine pseudo-labels, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is clearly explained, making it accessible to readers, and the paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed approach.
- The paper includes extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, with strong results across multiple datasets and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.
- The use of historical affinity fusion is highlighted as a key component of the proposed method, which helps mitigate confirmation bias and improve the accuracy of pseudo-labels.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and good presentation of the proposed method and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the selection of hyperparameters, particularly the choice of m and its impact on performance.
- There is a lack of comparative analysis with other recent state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the sensitivity to hyperparameter choices and the generalizability of the results across different datasets and domains.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its practical applicability and scalability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scalability and efficiency.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of data augmentation techniques used and their impact on the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed insights into the selection of hyperparameters, particularly the choice of m and its impact on performance?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability and scalability?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of datasets or domains, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the dataset or the complexity of the model?
