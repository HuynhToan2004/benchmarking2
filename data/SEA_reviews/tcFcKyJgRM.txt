 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HeaP, a novel framework for executing web tasks using large language models (LLMs). HeaP decomposes complex web tasks into a hierarchy of sub-tasks, each solved by a low-level, closed-loop policy. This approach leverages LLMs to generate hierarchical prompts for planning and executing tasks, which are evaluated against a range of baselines on various web tasks. The framework shows promise in reducing the amount of data required for training and generalizing across different tasks and web interfaces. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty, unclear presentation, and insufficient empirical evidence to support some of its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to task decomposition, which effectively reduces the complexity of web tasks into manageable sub-tasks, enhancing the efficiency and effectiveness of LLMs in executing web tasks.
- The framework is supported by a comprehensive evaluation against a range of baselines, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method in terms of task success rates and the number of required training examples.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear writing that facilitates understanding of the proposed methodology and its implementation.
- The authors have provided a detailed description of the framework, including the generation of task and policy prompts, which is supported by extensive ablation studies and qualitative examples.
- The paper is significant in its contribution to the field of web task automation, offering a potentially more efficient and effective method compared to existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, particularly in the introduction and methodology sections, which could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples to aid understanding.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it appears to be a simple application of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The empirical evaluation is limited, with insufficient data on the number of seeds used and the statistical significance of the results, which could undermine the credibility of the claims made.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of its approach, such as the potential for misuse due to its execution on open-domain environments and the need for further research on error recovery and task verification.
- There are concerns about the generalization of the approach to more complex web tasks, particularly those involving visual-only components or long tables, which might require additional data or different approaches.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the task planner generates a plan, specifically how it decides which policy to call and in what order?
- How does the framework handle tasks with visual-only components or long tables, which might require different approaches?
- Could the authors provide more details on the error recovery and task verification mechanisms, especially how these are integrated into the hierarchical policy framework?
- Is there a possibility to extend the framework to include multi-modal models for tasks involving both text and visual inputs?
- Can the authors explain the discrepancies in the evaluation metrics, such as the success rates reported in different tables and figures, and clarify whether these metrics are statistically significant?
- How does the framework perform when faced with more complex tasks or different web interfaces, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in such scenarios?
