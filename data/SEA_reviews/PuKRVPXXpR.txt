 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Resistance Transformation (ResTran) to address the homophilous bias in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by transforming feature vectors into a graph representation using a k-means objective. This transformation aims to balance homophily and heterophily in graph data, which is a significant challenge in GNNs. The method is theoretically justified through connections to spectral clustering and effective resistance, and empirically validated on several datasets. Despite its simplicity and potential, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, and insufficient experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant issue of homophily bias in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), which is a critical problem in graph learning.
- The proposed method, Resistance Transformation (ResTran), is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- Theoretical justification is provided for ResTran from an effective resistance, k-means, and spectral clustering perspective, which helps in understanding its theoretical underpinnings.
- The method is empirically validated on several datasets, showing its effectiveness in improving vertex classification performance.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and structured, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodologies and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to semi-supervised learning (SSL) tasks and does not include a comprehensive evaluation of its performance on other tasks such as unsupervised learning or transductive settings.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with ResTran showing inferior performance compared to GNNs in most cases, and the method's effectiveness is not clearly demonstrated.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of heterophily bias, which is a significant challenge in graph learning.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and computational efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in semi-supervised learning tasks?
- Can you provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its potential drawbacks?
- How does the proposed method handle heterophily bias, which is a significant challenge in graph learning?
- Can you provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons with other methods and a discussion on the method's effectiveness in different settings?
- How does the proposed method balance the trade-off between homophily and heterophily in graph data?
