 **Summary:**
The paper explores the convergence of the Stein variational gradient descent (SVGD) algorithm in terms of KL divergence, focusing on the sub-linear convergence rate under specific conditions. The authors introduce a novel concept of $(\epsilon,\delta)$-approximate gradient flow to analyze the convergence behavior of SVGD. This approach is applied to SVGD to show that it is a $(c_0, 0)$-AGF, which implies sub-linear convergence in KL divergence. The paper also discusses the practical implications of these findings, particularly in the context of finite particle settings, although it does not fully address the convergence guarantees in these scenarios. The theoretical results are supported by numerical experiments, which are, however, limited in scope and scale.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new concept of $(\epsilon,\delta)$-approximate gradient flow (AGF) and demonstrates that SVGD is a $(c_0, 0)$-AGF, which is a significant theoretical contribution to the field.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the convergence of SVGD in KL divergence, which is a novel approach that has not been extensively studied before.
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions, assumptions, and proofs, which are supported by both theoretical analysis and numerical experiments.
- The authors discuss the limitations of their work and provide a detailed explanation of the assumptions and their implications, which enhances the transparency and credibility of the research.
- The paper presents a novel approach to the convergence analysis of SVGD, which is a significant step towards understanding the behavior of this algorithm in practical settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing works, such as Korba et al. (2021), which could have helped in highlighting the novelty and contributions of the proposed method.
- The assumptions made in the paper, particularly Assumption 6, are considered strong and may not hold in practical settings, which could limit the applicability of the results.
- The numerical experiments are limited in scope and scale, which makes it difficult to draw robust conclusions about the performance of the algorithm in more complex or varied scenarios.
- The paper does not address the convergence guarantees for SVGD with a finite number of particles, which is a significant limitation given the practical relevance of this setting.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the assumptions made and how they affect the applicability and robustness of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison with existing works, such as Korba et al. (2021), to highlight the novelty and contributions of their method?
- How do the authors justify the strong assumptions made in their analysis, particularly Assumption 6, and what are the implications if these assumptions do not hold in practical settings?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of their results, especially in scenarios where the assumptions are not met?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex or varied settings, such as those involving high-dimensional data or different types of kernel functions?
- Could the authors discuss the possibility of extending their analysis to finite particle settings and how this could be achieved?
- In light of the limitations discussed, how do the authors plan to address these issues in future work and ensure the robustness and applicability of their results?
