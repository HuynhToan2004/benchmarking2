 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Search-Adaptor, a method designed to enhance the performance of large language models (LLMs) in information retrieval tasks by modifying the original text embeddings. This method, which does not require access to the LLM's parameters, uses a ranking loss and two regularizers to adapt embeddings to specific datasets. The authors claim that Search-Adaptor can be applied to both API-based and open-source LLMs, demonstrating improvements over zero-shot retrieval and fine-tuning methods. The approach is evaluated across 14 BEIR datasets and 17 multilingual datasets, showing consistent and significant performance gains. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, unclear presentation, and insufficient comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method, Search-Adaptor, is simple, intuitive, and effective, demonstrating consistent and significant improvements over zero-shot retrieval and fine-tuning methods.
- The method is model-agnostic and data-agnostic, which enhances its applicability across various datasets and models.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and understandable, and includes detailed experimental results that support the claims made.
- The method's simplicity and the use of a shared embedding space for both queries and corpus are highlighted as key factors contributing to its effectiveness.
- The method's ability to adapt to small data regimes and its applicability to both API-based and open-source LLMs is a significant advantage, especially in scenarios where full access to model parameters is not possible.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited, as it primarily combines existing techniques such as ranking loss, recovery loss, and prediction loss, which have been previously used in similar contexts.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other similar methods, such as Promptagator, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's advantages and disadvantages.
- The presentation of the paper is confusing and lacks clarity, particularly in the explanation of the method's components and their interactions.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, which is a critical concern in adapting models to new tasks without overwriting existing knowledge.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis and discussion on the impact of different hyperparameters and model configurations on the performance of the method.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the method performs in scenarios with varying data sizes and quality, which could affect its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other similar methods, such as Promptagator, to clarify the advantages and disadvantages of Search-Adaptor?
- How does the method perform when applied to different model sizes, and what are the implications for its effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the method's components, particularly the ranking loss and the regularization terms, to enhance understanding and reproducibility?
- What are the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments, and how do they impact the performance of the method?
- How does the method handle different data qualities and sizes, and what are the implications for its practical applicability in real-world scenarios?
- Can the authors clarify the method's impact on the retrieval performance when applied to different types of datasets, such as those with varying data densities or semantic similarities?
