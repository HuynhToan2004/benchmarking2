 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training method for spiking neural networks (SNNs) called Forward Gradient Training (FGT). This method avoids the traditional backpropagation across layers, instead using a forward propagation approach that calculates gradients through unidirectional forward connections and feedback from the top layer. The authors claim that this method provides similar performance to backpropagation while being more biologically plausible and hardware-friendly. The paper presents extensive experiments across various datasets and network architectures, including fully connected and convolutional networks, demonstrating the effectiveness and robustness of the proposed method. Despite its novelty, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty compared to existing methods and its experimental validation on smaller, less complex datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to training spiking neural networks (SNNs) that is biologically plausible and hardware-friendly.
- The proposed method, Forward Gradient Training (FGT), is an interesting alternative to traditional backpropagation (BP) and provides a fresh perspective on training SNNs.
- The paper provides extensive experimental validation, demonstrating the effectiveness and robustness of the proposed method across various datasets and network architectures.
- The paper includes a comprehensive literature review, positioning the proposed method within the current research landscape.
- The method is supported by theoretical results and empirical evidence, showing its potential as a competitive alternative to BP for training SNNs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other existing methods for training SNNs, such as those that use momentum feedback connections or other variance reduction techniques.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it seems to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The experimental validation is limited to smaller, less complex datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on more challenging tasks.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the biological plausibility of the method and the practical implementation details, such as the computational complexity and hardware requirements.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison with other methods for training SNNs, including those that use momentum feedback connections or other variance reduction techniques?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and hardware requirements with other methods, especially in larger and more complex networks?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the biological plausibility of the method and discuss how it could be implemented in real biological systems?
- How does the method perform on more challenging datasets, such as those with higher resolution images or more complex tasks?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential negative societal impacts?
- How does the method handle stochastic neuron models, and what are the implications for the robustness and reliability of the trained networks?
