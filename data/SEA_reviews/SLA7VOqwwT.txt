 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection and uncertainty estimation in deep ensembles, focusing on the efficient use of OOD data during training. The proposed method, Split-Ensemble, divides a classification task into complementary subtasks, each with its own training objective and OOD data. This allows for the sharing of early layers across submodels, reducing computational costs and improving accuracy and OOD detection performance. The methodology involves iterative splitting and pruning from a shared backbone model, creating a tree-like architecture where each branch corresponds to a submodel. Empirical results demonstrate that Split-Ensemble outperforms other ensemble methods in terms of accuracy and OOD detection across various datasets, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and Tiny-ImageNet.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical flow that makes the content accessible and understandable.
- The proposed method, Split-Ensemble, is novel and offers a practical approach to out-of-distribution (OOD) detection and uncertainty estimation in deep ensembles, which is a significant problem in machine learning.
- The methodology is empirically validated with strong results, demonstrating its effectiveness across various datasets and tasks.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results, which supports the claims made about the efficiency and effectiveness of the Split-Ensemble approach.
- The paper addresses a relevant and timely topic, which is the efficient handling of OOD data in deep learning models, a critical issue in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without a clear differentiation from prior works.
- The method's reliance on the semantic closeness of classes and the assumption that OOD classes are more numerous than ID classes may limit its applicability in scenarios where these assumptions do not hold.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with other methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and OOD detection performance.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method to larger, more complex datasets or models, which could limit its practical utility.
- The method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios, such as in medical imaging or natural language processing, is not explored, which could limit its broader applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method performs when the assumptions about class semantic closeness and OOD class dominance are not met?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and OOD detection performance, especially on larger and more complex datasets?
- Could the authors elaborate on the method's scalability and applicability to different domains, such as medical imaging or natural language processing?
- What are the specific advantages of the Split-Ensemble method over other existing methods in terms of OOD detection and uncertainty estimation?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific datasets used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of ensemble diversity, which is crucial for effective OOD detection and uncertainty estimation?
