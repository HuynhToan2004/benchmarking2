 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the limitations of linear MDPs by showing that the feature dimension required to represent transition probabilities is proportional to the state space size, which can lead to large feature dimensions in environments where the number of reachable states is small. To address this, the authors propose a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) algorithm with linear function approximation that utilizes a novel framework called "dynamics aggregation" to group similar sub-MDPs based on their dynamics. This approach is designed to reduce the feature dimension and improve regret bounds. The paper also provides theoretical analysis and numerical experiments to support its claims, although it faces criticism for its clarity, the complexity of its proofs, and the practical applicability of its assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) by proposing a new framework called "dynamics aggregation," which groups sub-MDPs based on similar dynamics, potentially improving the efficiency of learning and generalization across different sub-problems.
- The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis and proofs for the proposed framework and algorithm, which are backed by numerical experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of reinforcement learning by questioning the assumptions of linear MDPs and proposing a new approach to handle hierarchical structures in MDPs.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the concepts and theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is hard to follow due to its complex proofs and notations, which are not adequately explained, especially in the main text.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the availability of dynamics aggregation mappings and the knowledge of the aggregated state space, are considered too strong and may not be practical in many real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as the scalability of the algorithm and the complexity of the proofs.
- The paper does not adequately compare its results with existing literature, which could help in understanding the novelty and contribution of the work.
- The paper does not provide sufficient empirical results to support the theoretical claims, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions are not met.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the availability of dynamics aggregation mappings and the knowledge of the aggregated state space? How do these assumptions affect the practical applicability of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
