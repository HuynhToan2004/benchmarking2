 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to generating counterfactual explanations (CEs) using semantic graphs to represent input data, aiming to provide more interpretable and actionable explanations compared to traditional pixel-level edits. The method leverages a GNN-powered model to compute graph edit distances (GED) for counterfactual retrieval, which is model-agnostic and can be applied across various modalities. The paper presents extensive experiments on datasets like CUB, Visual Genome, and COVID-19, demonstrating the method's effectiveness and efficiency. The proposed approach aims to shift the focus from low-level features to high-level concepts, enhancing the explanatory power of CEs. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, particularly in terms of the method's scalability and effectiveness on more complex datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to generating counterfactual explanations (CEs) using semantic graphs, which shifts the focus from low-level features to high-level concepts, potentially providing more interpretable and actionable explanations.
- The method is model-agnostic, which enhances its applicability across various modalities and classifiers, including non-neural models.
- The proposed method is efficient, leveraging graph matching GNNs for approximation and significant acceleration compared to exact Graph Edit Distance (GED) algorithms.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- Extensive experiments are conducted on four diverse datasets with three neural classifiers, demonstrating the method's effectiveness and efficiency.
- The paper is the first to emphasize semantic graphs as a vehicle for CEs, allowing the transition from low-level features to concepts.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of scalability and effectiveness on more complex datasets.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it builds upon previous works such as Dervakos et al. (2023), with only minor modifications.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the reliance on annotations which may not always be available or accurate.
- The method's generalizability and robustness to noisy or incomplete data are not thoroughly explored.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of using semantic graphs for CEs and potential solutions to these limitations.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of using GED as a similarity metric and the potential impact of different semantic similarity metrics on the results.
- The paper could provide more detailed explanations and examples of how the method handles complex relationships between concepts and how it handles noisy or incomplete data.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle complex relationships between concepts, especially when these relationships are not directly represented in the scene graph?
- How does the method handle noisy or incomplete data, and what are the potential implications for the quality of the counterfactual explanations?
- Could the authors provide more details on the method's scalability and its ability to handle large-scale datasets?
- How does the method compare in terms of efficiency and effectiveness with other existing methods, particularly in scenarios where the ground truth labels are not available?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of different semantic similarity metrics on the results, and how the method handles the trade-off between efficiency and accuracy?
- How does the method ensure the generalizability of the generated counterfactual explanations, especially in scenarios where the data distribution differs from the training set?
- Could the authors provide more examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications of the proposed method in real-world scenarios?
