 **Summary:**
The paper explores privacy-preserving fine-tuning of pre-trained language models using parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods, specifically LoRA, in a two-party split learning setting. It addresses the privacy concerns of clients by proposing a method that obfuscates gradients and activations to prevent label leakage. The method involves adding noise to gradients and averaging activations from multiple LoRA adapters. The paper also introduces a regularization term to prevent the model from learning to leak labels. The proposed method is evaluated on various NLP tasks, showing promising results in terms of privacy and performance. However, the paper's evaluation is limited to a few datasets, and the method's scalability and generalizability to other PEFT methods and larger models are not thoroughly explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a timely and important problem of privacy-preserving fine-tuning of pre-trained models, which is crucial in the era of large language models.
- The proposed method is intuitive, well-motivated, and easy to implement, with a clear presentation that makes it accessible to readers.
- The paper includes visualizations of gradients and activations, which effectively demonstrate the privacy risks and the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The experiments are well-designed and show promising results, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in preventing label leakage while maintaining performance.
- The paper provides a novel approach to privacy-preserving fine-tuning, particularly in the context of PEFT, which has not been extensively explored.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods for privacy-preserving fine-tuning, which could provide a better understanding of the proposed method's advantages and limitations.
- The evaluation is limited to a few datasets, which might not be sufficient to demonstrate the method's effectiveness across a broader range of applications.
- The paper does not discuss the scalability of the proposed method to larger models or other PEFT methods, which could limit its applicability in more complex or diverse settings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the convergence of the proposed method, which is crucial for ensuring the accuracy and effectiveness of the fine-tuning process.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on related works, particularly those that address similar issues in privacy-preserving machine learning.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the regularization term used in the method and its impact on the model's performance and privacy?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other PEFT methods, such as adapter-based methods or prompt-tuning methods?
- Could the authors discuss the scalability of the method to larger models and more complex fine-tuning scenarios?
- What are the implications of the proposed method for the convergence of the fine-tuning process, and how does it compare to other privacy-preserving methods in terms of convergence speed and accuracy?
- How does the proposed method compare to other privacy-preserving methods in terms of computational overhead and practical deployment in real-world applications?
