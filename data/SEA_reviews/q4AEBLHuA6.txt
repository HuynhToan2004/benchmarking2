 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to solving high-frequency and multi-scale PDEs using Gaussian processes (GPs). The method involves modeling the power spectrum of the solution with a mixture of student t or Gaussian distributions, which are then transformed into a covariance function using the Wiener-Khinchin theorem. This allows for the estimation of the mixture weights in the log domain, inducing sparsity and effectively capturing the solution frequencies. The paper also employs a Kronecker product structure in the covariance matrix to improve computational efficiency. The method is tested against several benchmark PDEs, showing superior performance compared to traditional numerical methods and other machine learning approaches.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methodology and its implementation.
- The introduction of a spectral mixture kernel for solving high-frequency and multi-scale PDEs is innovative and effectively utilizes the Wiener-Khinchin theorem to model the power spectrum of the solution.
- The use of a mixture of student t or Gaussian distributions to model the power spectrum of the solution is a novel approach that allows for the estimation of mixture weights in the log domain, inducing sparsity and effectively capturing the solution frequencies.
- The paper is well-motivated and provides a comprehensive review of related work, positioning the research within the current literature.
- The computational efficiency of the proposed method is demonstrated through the use of a Kronecker product structure in the covariance matrix, which significantly reduces computational costs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of PDEs and the sensitivity of the results to the number and scales of Gaussian variances.
- The experimental validation is limited to a few synthetic benchmark PDEs, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the method to real-world applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the method, particularly in high-dimensional or large-scale problems.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with other machine learning methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and the handling of high-frequency and multi-scale components.
