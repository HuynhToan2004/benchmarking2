 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a unified framework for learning with imprecise labels, integrating various forms of imprecise label configurations such as partial labels, semi-supervised learning, and noisy label learning. The framework employs an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to model the imprecise label information, treating precise labels as latent variables. This approach allows for the handling of multiple imprecise label configurations simultaneously, which is a significant advancement in the field. The paper demonstrates that the proposed method outperforms existing techniques in various settings, including partial label learning, semi-supervised learning, and noisy label learning. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the EM algorithm, the clarity of the experimental setup, and the potential for overfitting due to the learning of noise models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a unified framework for learning with various imprecise label configurations, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The framework is validated through extensive experiments on various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed derivations and explanations that aid in understanding the proposed methods.
- The Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm used in the framework is well-explained, and the paper provides a detailed derivation of the variational lower bound.
- The paper addresses a practical problem by unifying the handling of multiple imprecise label configurations, which is a common issue in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the EM algorithm used in the framework is limited, as similar approaches have been previously used in other contexts.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework, particularly in terms of scalability and applicability to more complex datasets or settings.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent or state-of-the-art methods, which could have strengthened the paper's claims.
- The experimental setup and results are not clearly presented, making it difficult to assess the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for overfitting due to the learning of noise models, which could lead to biased or inaccurate predictions.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the EM algorithm and the computational efficiency of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of the EM algorithm used in the framework and how it differs from similar approaches in previous works?
- How does the proposed framework handle the scalability issues, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger or more complex datasets?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the framework handle the potential for overfitting due to the learning of noise models? What measures have been taken to mitigate this risk?
- Could the authors elaborate on the experimental setup and results, particularly in terms of the baseline methods used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- What are the implications of the EM algorithm in practical applications, and how does it affect the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed methods?
