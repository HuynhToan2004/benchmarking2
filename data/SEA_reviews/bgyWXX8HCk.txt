 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel protocol named ZkAudit, which allows model providers to conduct audits without revealing their models or training data. This protocol employs zero-knowledge proofs (ZKPs) to ensure the authenticity and integrity of model training, thereby enhancing transparency and accountability in machine learning. The authors claim that their method can handle audits such as copyright, censorship, and counterfactual audits, with minimal loss in accuracy. The protocol is designed to be efficient and scalable, with experiments conducted on real-world datasets like CIFAR-10 and ImageNet. However, concerns were raised about the practicality of the protocol, particularly its scalability and the assumptions it makes about the model provider's trustworthiness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to auditing machine learning models without revealing the training data or model weights, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors have provided a detailed description of the protocol, including its components and the steps involved, which is commendable for its clarity and comprehensiveness.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the concepts and a logical flow that makes it easy to follow.
- The experiments conducted on real-world datasets such as CIFAR-10 and ImageNet demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue of transparency in machine learning, which is crucial given the increasing use of proprietary models in various industries.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper makes strong assumptions about the model provider's trustworthiness, which may not be realistic in many scenarios, especially when dealing with large corporations or organizations with significant financial interests.
- The scalability of the protocol is questionable, as the computational requirements and costs associated with ZK-SNARKs for gradient descent could limit its practical use in larger models or more complex datasets.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability, which could undermine the perceived advantages of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for malicious behavior by the model provider, which could undermine the effectiveness of the auditing process.
- The paper's claims about the efficiency and scalability of the proposed method are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which could lead to skepticism about the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the model provider is not trustworthy? What measures are in place to prevent them from cheating the system?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods in terms of computational efficiency, practical applicability, and scalability?
- How does the proposed method ensure that the model provider does not manipulate the audit process to their advantage?
- Given the computational and cost implications of the ZK-SNARKs for gradient descent, how do the authors justify the practicality and scalability of the proposed method, especially in larger models or more complex datasets?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the traversal ordering in the auditing process, particularly how it affects the efficiency and effectiveness of the audits?
