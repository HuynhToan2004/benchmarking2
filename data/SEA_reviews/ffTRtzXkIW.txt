 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PAGAR, a novel method for addressing reward misalignment in imitation learning (IL) through inverse reinforcement learning (IRL). PAGAR utilizes a protagonist-antagonist framework to search for reward functions that optimize performance under both successful and misaligned reward functions. This approach aims to ensure that the learned policy can perform well across different reward scenarios, thereby mitigating the risk of suboptimal or misaligned policies. The paper provides theoretical insights and empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of PAGAR, particularly in complex and zero-shot IL tasks. It also discusses the limitations and potential extensions of the method, suggesting future directions for research.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and intuitive method for reward misalignment in IRL-based IL, which is well-motivated and supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results.
- The protagonist-antagonist guided adversarial reward design is innovative and provides a novel approach to addressing reward misalignment.
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions and proofs that are easy to follow, enhancing the reader's understanding of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in complex and zero-shot IL tasks.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in IRL-based IL, which is the reward misalignment, and provides a practical on-and-off policy approach to implement the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and its potential negative societal impacts, which could be addressed in the conclusion section.
- The method requires a set of reward functions, which might not be readily available in all scenarios, and the selection of these functions could significantly impact the performance of the learned policy.
- The computational complexity of the method, particularly in high-dimensional environments, is not discussed, which could limit its applicability in practical scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, which could affect the generalizability and applicability of the results.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse environments and benchmarks to better validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the relationship of PAGAR to other existing methods, such as IQ-Learn, which also address reward misalignment in IRL-based IL.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the reward function set R is selected and its impact on the performance of the learned policy?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the reward function set R is not provided, and how does it compare to other methods that do not require such a set?
- Could the authors elaborate on the computational complexity of the method, particularly in high-dimensional environments, and discuss potential strategies for reducing this complexity?
- What are the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, and how do they affect the generalizability and applicability of the results?
- How does PAGAR compare to other methods that address reward misalignment in IRL-based IL, such as IQ-Learn?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of environments and the benchmarks used, to better validate the effectiveness of the proposed method?
