 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LayerNAS, a novel Neural Architecture Search (NAS) algorithm that addresses the complexity of multi-objective NAS by formulating it as a combinatorial optimization problem. By decoupling constraints and model quality, LayerNAS reduces the search complexity from exponential to polynomial. The algorithm is designed to handle layer-wise architecture search, allowing for a more efficient and effective search process. Extensive experiments on benchmarks like ImageNet and NATS-Bench demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness in terms of efficiency, stability, and performance. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, and the limited scope of the search space.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces LayerNAS, a novel algorithm for Neural Architecture Search (NAS) that effectively transforms the multi-objective NAS problem into a combinatorial optimization problem.
- LayerNAS significantly reduces the search complexity from exponential to polynomial, which is a significant improvement over traditional NAS methods.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly presents the problem, methodology, and results, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- Extensive experiments are conducted on benchmarks such as ImageNet and NATS-Bench, demonstrating the effectiveness of LayerNAS in terms of efficiency, stability, and performance.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the LayerNAS algorithm, including its assumptions, methodology, and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and assumptions of LayerNAS, particularly the assumptions regarding the independence of search options across layers.
- There is a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art NAS methods, which makes it difficult to assess the relative performance of LayerNAS against the current best practices.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of search costs, such as training time, which is crucial for evaluating the efficiency of NAS algorithms.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of LayerNAS, which could be important for optimizing the algorithm's performance.
- The search space is limited to specific types of architectures, which may not be generalizable to other types of architectures.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of accuracy variation across different training trials, which could affect the reliability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations and assumptions of LayerNAS, particularly the assumptions regarding the independence of search options across layers?
- How does LayerNAS compare to other state-of-the-art NAS methods in terms of performance, efficiency, and stability?
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the search costs, such as training time, for LayerNAS and other NAS methods?
- How do different hyperparameters of LayerNAS impact its performance, and what are the optimal settings for different types of architectures?
- Can LayerNAS be adapted to search for architectures with different types of layers, such as convolutional layers or attention layers?
- How does LayerNAS handle the issue of accuracy variation across different training trials, and what measures are taken to ensure the reliability of the results?
