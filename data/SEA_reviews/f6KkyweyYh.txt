 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for converting biological sequences into images using Bezier curves, which are then utilized for classification using deep learning models. This approach is claimed to be superior to existing methods such as Chaos Game Representation (CGR) and One-Hot Encoding (OHE) in terms of performance and interpretability. The methodology involves mapping each character in a sequence to multiple Bezier curves, which are then used to create images for classification. The paper presents results on three datasets, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in protein subcellular localization, host classification, and anticancer activity prediction. However, the paper suffers from several issues including unclear methodology, insufficient baseline comparisons, and lack of detailed experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to convert biological sequences into images using Bezier curves, which is a new method in the field.
- The proposed method achieves better performance compared to existing methods like Chaos Game Representation (CGR) and One-Hot Encoding (OHE) in some classification tasks.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear descriptions of the proposed method and the experimental results.
- The method's potential to enhance interpretability by converting sequences into images is highlighted, which could be beneficial for understanding sequence information.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the methodology, particularly the Bezier curve mapping and the creation of images from sequences.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with some results showing only marginal improvements or similar performance.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with the latest state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the claimed improvements.
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in presentation, with some sections being difficult to understand and some figures and tables not being adequately explained.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to large datasets or different types of sequences.
- There are several typographical and grammatical errors throughout the paper, which could detract from its professionalism and readability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the Bezier curve mapping and the creation of images from sequences?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance and interpretability?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of baseline methods and the experimental conditions?
- How does the method perform on larger or more complex datasets, and what are the implications for its scalability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results and analysis, particularly for the Human DNA and SMILES String datasets, which are currently under-explored in the paper?
- How does the method handle different types of sequences, such as nucleotide sequences, and what are the implications for its applicability in these contexts?
