 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel transformer-based method for model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) in Atari environments. The method involves a transformer architecture that models the world dynamics using a decoder and learns a policy using an encoder, both integrated with a VQ-VAE for discrete representation learning. The approach is claimed to outperform existing methods such as DreamerV3 and IRIS on the Atari 100k benchmark, achieving state-of-the-art results. However, the paper is criticized for its incremental nature, lack of comparison with more recent and relevant baselines, and insufficient justification for design choices.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the method and results.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art results on the Atari 100k benchmark, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The use of transformers for both world and policy modeling is innovative and potentially beneficial for MBRL.
- The paper provides a thorough ablation study, which helps in understanding the impact of different components of the method.
- The visualization of attention maps is insightful, showing the aggregation of information from past states.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with relevant baselines, particularly newer methods like DreamerV3 and IRIS, which could have provided a clearer picture of its relative performance.
- The justification for using transformers for modeling world dynamics and policy learning is insufficiently detailed, and the paper does not adequately compare its approach with other transformer-based methods.
- The experimental results, while showing improvements, are not convincingly superior to existing methods, especially in terms of median scores.
- The paper's claims about the novelty and effectiveness of using discrete representations are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its method, such as its applicability to continuous action spaces.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with DreamerV3 and IRIS, especially in terms of training and evaluation details?
- How does the proposed method compare with other transformer-based MBRL methods, such as TransDreamer and TWM?
- Can the authors clarify the choice of using discrete representations and its impact on performance?
- Why was the decision made to use VQ-VAE for representation learning, and how does it compare with other methods like autoencoders or self-attention mechanisms?
- How does the method handle partial observability, and what are the implications for its performance in more complex or realistic environments?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the training and evaluation of the method, including the number of seeds used and the statistical significance of the results?
