 **Summary:**
The paper introduces EpiMISR, a novel multi-image super-resolution (MISR) method that utilizes epipolar geometry to enhance resolution in images captured from different camera positions and orientations. EpiMISR employs a transformer-based processing of radiance feature fields and a CAP module for epipolar feature extraction, followed by a MIFF module for feature fusion. The method is evaluated against existing MISR methods on the DTU dataset, showing improvements in performance. However, the novelty and the technical contribution of the paper are questioned due to its similarity to existing methods and the lack of significant performance improvements.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the method is clearly explained with figures.
- The proposed method, EpiMISR, introduces a novel setting for MISR that can handle images captured from arbitrary camera positions and orientations, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in handling large geometric disparities.
- The use of epipolar geometry and the explicit handling of image registration are highlighted as innovative aspects that distinguish EpiMISR from existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The technical contribution of the paper is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods, particularly in the use of transformer-based processing and epipolar geometry, which have been previously explored in other works.
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as it mainly combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that have been published after the submission of the paper.
- The performance improvement of EpiMISR over existing methods is not significant, and in some cases, the performance of simpler methods like bicubic upsampling followed by convolution is comparable.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the sensitivity to camera parameter estimation and the potential impact of using different SISR backbones.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation on different datasets and a more detailed analysis of the performance of the MIFF module.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the technical contributions of the paper and how it differs from existing methods in terms of methodology and performance?
- How does the proposed method handle the sensitivity to camera parameter estimation, and what are the implications of using different SISR backbones?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that have been published after the submission of the paper?
- What are the specific advantages of using a transformer-based processing over other methods, and how does this contribute to the overall performance of the proposed method?
- How does the paper address the issue of potential data leakage from the DTU dataset, which is known to be used in NeRF training?
- Could the authors provide more results on other datasets to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method in different scenarios?
