 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Quantization-Aware Adaptive Coreset Selection (ACS), aimed at improving the efficiency of quantization-aware training (QAT) by selectively choosing a subset of training data that is most informative for the training process. This approach is based on two metrics: Error Vector Score (EVS) and Disagreement Score (DS), which quantify the importance of each sample during QAT. The methodology is validated through experiments on ResNet-18 and MobileNetV2 using the CIFAR-100 and ImageNet-1K datasets. The results show that ACS can significantly enhance the training efficiency with minimal performance degradation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear motivation and a clear structure, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, demonstrating significant improvements in efficiency and performance over traditional QAT methods.
- The introduction of the Error Vector Score (EVS) and Disagreement Score (DS) as metrics to quantify the importance of each sample during QAT is novel and insightful.
- The paper presents a comprehensive analysis of the proposed method, including theoretical analysis and practical demonstrations, which are supported by extensive experiments.
- The method is adaptable and can be applied to various network architectures, datasets, and quantization settings, enhancing its applicability and relevance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not convincingly presented, with inconsistencies in the reported results and a lack of detailed analysis or discussion.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other existing quantization methods, such as post-training quantization (PTQ), which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper's focus on specific datasets and network architectures may limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational overhead of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be applied to other quantization methods, such as PTQ, and how would this affect the results?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational overhead with other methods, especially in scenarios where the training data is limited?
- Is there a possibility to extend the proposed method to include other metrics, such as forgetting events, to enhance its effectiveness?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the standard deviations of the reported results and a more comprehensive analysis of the experimental outcomes?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger or more complex datasets, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in such scenarios?
