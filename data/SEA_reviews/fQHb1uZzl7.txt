 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a Transformer-based architecture, UFC, for dense matching tasks that integrates both feature and cost aggregation. The method leverages self-attention to jointly aggregate features and cost volumes, followed by cross-attention for further aggregation. It also employs a coarse-to-fine design to refine correspondences across multiple scales. The method is evaluated on standard benchmarks for semantic and geometric matching, showing significant improvements over existing methods. The paper also includes extensive ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of each component. However, concerns are raised regarding the novelty of the method, as it heavily relies on existing techniques such as Transformer and self/cross-attention, and the paper's presentation and clarity need improvement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations of the proposed method and its components.
- The method is straightforward, intuitive, and effectively integrates feature and cost aggregation, which is a novel approach in dense matching.
- The paper includes extensive ablation studies that validate the effectiveness of each component, demonstrating the benefits of the proposed method over existing approaches.
- The experimental results are impressive, showing significant improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods on several benchmarks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including detailed visualizations and comparisons with recent works, which helps in understanding the method's contributions and limitations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the advantages and limitations of the proposed method compared to existing approaches, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- There are concerns about the novelty of the method, as it heavily relies on existing techniques such as Transformer and self/cross-attention, which have been extensively used in similar tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the computational complexity and the generalizability of the method across different datasets and tasks.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with related works, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency.
- The presentation of the paper needs improvement, particularly in the clarity and organization of the methodology section and the overall structure of the paper.
- There are some minor errors and inconsistencies in the paper, such as missing references and typos, which need to be corrected.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in terms of FLOPs and memory usage?
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets or tasks beyond those evaluated in the paper?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the coarse-to-fine design in the method's performance?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in large-scale applications?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the design choices, particularly the decision to use both feature and cost aggregation and the rationale behind the coarse-to-fine design?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the feature and cost aggregation might not be optimal, and what are the potential failure cases of the proposed method?
