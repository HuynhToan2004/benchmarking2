 **Summary:**
The paper explores the identifiability of latent representations in multi-view data settings, where each view is a non-linear mixture of a subset of underlying latent variables. It extends previous work by allowing for partial observability and multiple views, demonstrating that shared information across views can be learned up to a smooth bijection using contrastive learning. Theoretical contributions include identifiability results for shared content blocks and the application of these results to special cases of prior works. The paper also includes experimental validations on synthetic and real-world datasets, showcasing the practical applicability of the theoretical findings. Despite its strengths, the paper is critiqued for its limited experimental scope, potential over-reliance on auxiliary variables, and the need for a more detailed discussion on related works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper extends the setting of identifiability of latent representations in multi-view data, allowing for partial observability and multiple views, which is a significant advancement over previous works.
- The theoretical contributions are solid, with clear and well-written proofs that are easy to follow, making the paper accessible to a broader audience.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions, intuitions, and discussions that aid in understanding complex theoretical concepts.
- The experimental results are promising, showing the practical applicability of the theoretical findings, and the paper effectively demonstrates how it unifies and extends previous works.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear presentation of ideas and results, and includes a comprehensive review of related works, which is beneficial for readers seeking a deeper understanding of the topic.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental validation is limited, with only a few datasets used, and the results are not sufficiently robust to demonstrate the general applicability of the proposed methods.
- The paper relies heavily on auxiliary variables, which could limit its practical applicability in real-world scenarios where such variables may not be available or reliable.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on related works, particularly those that are closely related but not directly cited, to provide a more comprehensive overview of the existing literature.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the smoothness and continuity of the latent variable distribution, are not discussed in detail, which could limit the understanding of the theoretical underpinnings.
- The paper's experimental setup and methodology could be improved to better demonstrate the practical applicability of the theoretical findings, particularly in more complex and varied data scenarios.
