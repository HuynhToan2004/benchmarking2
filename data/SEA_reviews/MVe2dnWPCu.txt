 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PICLE, a novel modular continual learning framework that employs a probabilistic search strategy to evaluate module compositions without extensive training. PICLE utilizes two probabilistic models to assess perceptual and latent transfer capabilities, demonstrating scalability and superior performance over existing methods. The framework is tested on benchmark suites, showcasing its effectiveness in handling long problem sequences and its ability to outperform previous state-of-the-art methods. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the methodology, particularly in the explanation of the probabilistic models and the assumptions used in the search strategy.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel probabilistic search strategy for modular continual learning, which is well-motivated and clearly articulated.
- The proposed method, PICLE, is shown to outperform previous state-of-the-art methods on long problem sequences, demonstrating scalability and effectiveness.
- The use of a probabilistic framework to evaluate module compositions without extensive training is innovative and could potentially reduce computational costs.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the methodology and the experiments, making it easy to follow.
- The introduction of a new benchmark, BELL, is a significant contribution to the field, providing a new tool for evaluating modular continual learning methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the probabilistic models used in the method, particularly how they are applied in different scenarios and the assumptions made.
- There is a lack of clarity in the descriptions of the algorithms and the notations used, which could confuse readers and hinder reproducibility.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of the computational cost and the practicality of the approach in real-world scenarios.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper does not sufficiently compare PICLE with other methods in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the selection of hyperparameters and the impact of these choices on the performance of the method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definitions and roles of different notations used in the paper, especially those related to the probabilistic models and the search strategies?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, particularly in terms of computational cost and the practicality of the approach in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the probabilistic models used in the method, including the assumptions made and the implications of these assumptions?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage?
- What are the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments, and how were these chosen? What is the impact of these choices on the performance of the method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the selection of the neural network architecture and the training parameters?
