 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the use of distributional ensembles in reinforcement learning, focusing on the combination of different projections and representations to enhance exploration and uncertainty estimation. Theoretical properties of these projection ensembles are analyzed, and a new algorithm is proposed that uses ensemble disagreement to measure exploration bonuses. The paper is evaluated on the behavior suite benchmark and VizDoom, demonstrating performance improvements over existing methods in directed exploration problems. However, the paper's theoretical contributions are questioned due to a lack of novelty and clarity, and the empirical results are not convincingly superior to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces the concept of projection ensembles, which is novel and contributes to the field of distributional reinforcement learning.
- Theoretical properties of these projection ensembles are analyzed, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise sections that effectively convey the ideas and methodologies used.
- The combination of different projection operators and representations in an ensemble of distributional value learners is a novel approach that could lead to significant improvements in exploration and uncertainty estimation.
- The paper is well-motivated, with a clear and well-structured introduction that effectively communicates the significance of the problem and the proposed solution.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical contributions are questioned due to a lack of novelty and clarity. Specifically, the proof of Proposition 1 and the definition of the projection mixture operator are not sufficiently explained or justified.
- The empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods. The paper lacks a clear demonstration of how the ensemble disagreement is measured and how it contributes to the performance improvements.
- The paper's related work section is insufficiently detailed, missing out on significant related works that use ensembles of distributional models to derive aleatoric and epistemic risk measures.
- The paper's methodology and experimental setup are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to replicate the results or understand the full implications of the findings.
- The paper's assumptions and theorems, such as the assumption that the operator ΩMTPi is a contraction mapping, are not well-justified or explained, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the proof of Proposition 1, particularly the definition and role of the operator ΩMTPi in the proof?
- How does the paper define and measure ensemble disagreement? Is it based on the Wasserstein distance or another metric?
- In the empirical results section, how does the paper ensure that the observed performance improvements are not due to the ensemble size rather than the diversity of projections and representations?
- How does the paper justify the assumption that the operator ΩMTPi is a contraction mapping?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the hyperparameters used and the selection criteria for these parameters?
- How does the paper address the issue of potential biases in the ensemble disagreement measurement, especially when the ensemble members are trained independently?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of baselines and the rationale behind their selection?
