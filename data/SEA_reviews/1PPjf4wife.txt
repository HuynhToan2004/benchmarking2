 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Functionally-Aligned Multi-Agents (FAMA) framework, which leverages large language models (LLMs) to enhance coordination in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) environments. The framework is designed to address the challenges of sample efficiency and generalizability in MARL by utilizing LLMs as agents, enabling them to communicate naturally in natural language. The FAMA framework integrates a centralized critic to improve coordination capabilities and employs a communication module for intuitive, natural language inter-agent message passing. The paper presents experiments in two multi-agent textual environments, demonstrating that FAMA outperforms independent learning LLMs and traditional symbolic RL methods. However, the paper's novelty and the depth of its methodological contributions are questioned, as the use of LLMs in MARL and natural language communication are not entirely novel.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), focusing on enhancing coordination through the use of large language models (LLMs).
- The proposed FAMA framework is well-motivated and effectively addresses the challenges of sample efficiency and generalizability in MARL, showing potential for broad applications.
- The integration of LLMs with MARL is a promising approach, leveraging the capabilities of LLMs to enhance coordination and performance in multi-agent systems.
- The paper provides a detailed explanation of the FAMA framework, including its components and how they function together, which aids in understanding the proposed solution.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the FAMA framework, showing that it outperforms independent learning LLMs and traditional symbolic RL methods in multi-agent textual environments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is questioned as the use of LLMs in MARL and natural language communication are not entirely novel, with similar approaches having been previously explored.
- The experimental design is limited, with only two environments used, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the FAMA framework.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing baselines, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the effectiveness of different communication methods.
- The integration of LLMs into MARL is not thoroughly analyzed, and the paper does not provide sufficient insights into the specific challenges and solutions related to this integration.
- There are concerns about the scalability and generalizability of the FAMA framework, as it has only been tested in limited environments and does not address more complex or diverse MARL scenarios.
- The paper's writing and presentation could be improved for better clarity and coherence, particularly in the explanation of the FAMA framework and its components.

**Questions:**
- Can you elaborate on how the FAMA framework addresses the challenges of sample efficiency and generalizability in MARL?
- How does the FAMA framework compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and the effectiveness of different communication methods?
- What are the specific challenges and solutions related to integrating LLMs into MARL, and how does the FAMA framework address these challenges?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the selection of environments and the rationale behind choosing them?
- How does the FAMA framework handle different communication scenarios, and what are the implications of using different communication methods on the performance of the system?
- Can you provide more insights into the scalability and generalizability of the FAMA framework, particularly in more complex or diverse MARL scenarios?
