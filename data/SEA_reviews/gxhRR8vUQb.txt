 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to mesh deformation by leveraging the sliced Wasserstein distance (SWD) to optimize the deformation of cortical surfaces from volumetric MRI data. The method represents meshes as probability measures, allowing for more efficient and effective comparisons between meshes. The SWD is used to learn the deformation trajectories of points on the surface, which are optimized using a neural ordinary differential equation (ODE). The approach is validated through extensive experiments on three datasets, showing improvements over existing methods in terms of geometric accuracy, self-intersection ratio, and consistency. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its clarity, particularly in the explanation of the loss function and the methodology, and the lack of detailed comparisons with other metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to mesh deformation using the sliced Wasserstein distance (SWD) to optimize the deformation trajectories of points on the surface, which is a significant improvement over traditional methods like Chamfer distance.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear definitions and detailed explanations of the mathematical concepts used, such as the use of oriented varifolds to represent meshes and the application of SWD for efficient computation.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear structure and logical flow, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The paper includes extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in terms of geometric accuracy, self-intersection ratio, and consistency.
- The paper addresses a critical issue in mesh deformation by providing a new metric that overcomes the limitations of traditional set-based approaches, such as the Chamfer pseudo-distance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the loss function used in the experiments, particularly the SWD loss, which is crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with other metrics, such as the Chamfer distance, to fully understand the advantages and disadvantages of using SWD.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, with some results (e.g., in Table 3) not being statistically significant or not including error bars, which undermines the credibility of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach, such as the potential for stochastic memory during training and the lack of generalizability to other deformation tasks beyond CSR.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the theoretical foundations of the SWD and its application to mesh deformation, particularly in terms of its theoretical guarantees and practical implications.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the specific implementation of the SWD loss in your experiments, including how it is computed and how it contributes to the optimization of the deformation trajectories?
- How does the SWD loss compare to other metrics, such as the Chamfer distance, in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- Can you clarify the role of the varifold representation in your method and how it contributes to the overall effectiveness of the approach?
- How does the proposed method handle the stochastic memory during training, and what steps are being taken to mitigate this issue?
- In terms of the theoretical foundations of the SWD, could you provide a more detailed discussion on its properties and how it relates to other metrics like the Chamfer distance?
- How do the results in Table 3 compare statistically, and could you provide error bars to support the claimed improvements over existing methods?
