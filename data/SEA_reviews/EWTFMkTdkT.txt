 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for learning latent dynamical systems, named LaDID, which leverages a transformer-based architecture to separate realization-specific and realization-invariant dynamics. The model is designed to handle both regular and irregular time series data, including sparse data, and is tested against several state-of-the-art methods across various benchmarks. The approach is evaluated on both ODE and PDE-based processes, demonstrating superior performance in predicting the dynamics of complex systems. The paper also explores the application of this method in few-shot learning scenarios, showing its ability to generalize well to novel system interventions. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty, insufficient comparisons with recent works, and lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the latent space and the dynamics of the system.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem in learning dynamical systems, focusing on the separation of realization-specific and realization-invariant dynamics, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is clearly described, and the paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in predicting the dynamics of complex systems.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, comparing it to several state-of-the-art methods across various benchmarks, and the results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms these methods.
- The paper is well-written and the authors provide a detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed method, which is appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the latent space and the dynamics of the system, which could be improved by including visualizations or more detailed descriptions.
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it builds on existing methods like MSVI, with only minor modifications such as the use of a transformer and a different loss function.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with recent works, such as those by Iakovlev et al. (2023), which address similar problems.
- There are several typographical and grammatical errors throughout the paper, which could detract from its professionalism.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities and challenges.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of sparsity in data, which is crucial for the application of the method in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the dynamics of the system, particularly how the latent space evolves over time?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent works that address similar problems, such as those by Iakovlev et al. (2023)?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the transformer in the model and how it contributes to the separation of realization-specific and realization-invariant dynamics?
- How does the method handle sparse data, and what are the implications of this for the accuracy of the predictions?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the loss functions used and their impact on the model's performance?
- The paper mentions a few-shot learning approach; could the authors elaborate on this and how it relates to the overall methodology?
