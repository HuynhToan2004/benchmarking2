 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel set of fidelity measures for evaluating the explainability of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by addressing the limitations of existing metrics, particularly their susceptibility to distribution shifts. The proposed metrics are grounded in information theory and are robust to such shifts, enhancing their applicability in real-world scenarios. The paper provides both theoretical and empirical evidence, demonstrating the superior alignment of these metrics with gold standard benchmarks. The authors aim to improve the evaluation of GNNs by addressing the challenges posed by distribution shifts and providing a theoretical framework that supports the proposed metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear definitions and well-organized content, making it accessible to readers.
- The authors address a significant and relevant problem in the field of explainable AI, focusing on the limitations of existing fidelity metrics for evaluating the explainability of GNNs.
- The paper introduces a novel set of fidelity measures that are grounded in information theory and are robust to distribution shifts, enhancing their applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The theoretical framework and empirical validation provided by the paper are solid, demonstrating the superior alignment of the proposed metrics with gold standard benchmarks.
- The paper provides a thorough discussion on the limitations of existing fidelity metrics and proposes a new set of metrics that are theoretically grounded and empirically validated, showing that they are more robust to distribution shifts.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed metrics, particularly in scenarios where the distribution shift is not significant or where the graph structure is complex.
- There is a lack of comparative analysis with other fidelity metrics or alternative explanation methods, which could help in better understanding the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed metrics.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on how the proposed metrics perform in real-world applications, which is crucial for evaluating their practical utility.
- The paper's theoretical analysis and proofs are not self-contained, which could make it difficult for readers to fully understand the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed metrics.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implementation of the proposed metrics, particularly in scenarios where the ground truth explanation subgraph is not available.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the practical implementation of the proposed metrics, especially in scenarios where the ground truth explanation subgraph is not available?
- How do the proposed metrics perform in real-world applications, and what are the implications of these performance metrics for the practical utility of the metrics?
- Could the authors provide a comparative analysis of the proposed metrics with other fidelity metrics or alternative explanation methods?
- How do the proposed metrics handle the issue of distribution shifts in practical scenarios, and what are the implications of these shifts on the accuracy and reliability of the metrics?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and roles of certain terms and concepts used in the paper, such as "explainability of a classification task" and "explainability of a classifier for a given task"?
