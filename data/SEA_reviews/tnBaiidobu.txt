 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the generalization capabilities of CLIP models, specifically focusing on the role of data similarity between training and test sets in its performance. It explores how the high train-test similarity in the LAION dataset contributes to CLIP's zero-shot performance on benchmarks like ImageNet-Sketch and ImageNet-R. The authors prune LAION to create a 100M subset that matches the performance of the full dataset, suggesting that while high similarity is a factor, it is not the sole determinant of CLIP's effectiveness. The paper also introduces a 100M subset of LAION that can be used to train CLIP models with similar performance, providing a valuable resource for future research.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by examining the similarity between the training and test sets, which is crucial for understanding the generalization capabilities of CLIP models.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a comprehensive analysis of the results.
- The experimental design is robust, and the paper provides a detailed analysis of the results, which are supported by both qualitative and quantitative evidence.
- The introduction of a 100M subset of LAION is a significant contribution that could be useful for future research in this area.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and logical flow, making it easy for readers to understand the authors' arguments and findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on CLIP's performance on ImageNet-based benchmarks, which might limit the generalizability of the findings to other datasets or models.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the study, which could provide a more balanced perspective on the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the similarity metric used, particularly how it impacts the results and the choice of the similarity threshold.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential influence of other factors such as model architecture or training strategies on CLIP's performance.
- The paper could be improved by including more comprehensive comparisons with other models or datasets to validate the findings and enhance the robustness of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the choice of the similarity threshold and how it affects the results?
- How does the paper's findings apply to other models or datasets, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the results?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies in performance between different pruning strategies, particularly the near-pruning and far-pruning methods?
- Is there a possibility to extend the analysis to include other models or datasets to validate the findings and enhance the robustness of the results?
- How does the paper's findings compare with other models that have been trained on similar datasets, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the results?
