 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to aligning AI systems with human values by focusing on the concept of "concept alignment" within the framework of value-guided construal. The authors argue that misalignments in concepts can lead to significant misalignments in values, which can hinder the successful integration of AI systems with human decision-making processes. The paper presents a theoretical framework and experimental results, including human studies, to support this claim. The approach is validated through a case study using a simple gridworld environment and human participants, demonstrating that agents that jointly model construals and rewards perform better than those that only model rewards. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental scope, lack of comprehensive theoretical backing, and insufficient empirical evidence to substantiate its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and significant concept, "concept alignment," which is crucial for ensuring that AI systems align with human values and decision-making processes.
- The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical framework and experimental results, including human studies, which demonstrate the practical implications of the concept alignment problem.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience, including both experts and non-experts in the field.
- The experimental results are promising, showing that agents that jointly model construals and rewards perform better than those that only model rewards.
- The paper's focus on the importance of concept alignment in AI systems is timely and relevant, given the increasing importance of AI in various domains.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive theoretical backing for its claims, particularly in the area of inverse construal, which is a critical component of the proposed framework.
- The experimental setup is limited to a simple gridworld environment, which may not adequately represent real-world scenarios where concept alignment is most critical.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed approach to more complex and realistic scenarios.
- The empirical evidence provided is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not adequately address potential negative societal impacts, particularly in terms of the potential for AI systems to reinforce or amplify existing biases in human decision-making.
- The paper's reliance on human studies for validation may not be sufficient to establish the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical explanation of the inverse construal problem and how it relates to the overall concept of concept alignment?
- How does the proposed approach handle scenarios where there are multiple possible construals, and how does it ensure that the correct construal is inferred?
- How does the approach perform in more complex and realistic scenarios, such as those involving multiple agents or more complex reward structures?
- Can the authors provide additional empirical evidence to support the claims made in the paper, particularly in terms of the effectiveness of the proposed approach in more complex and realistic settings?
- How does the approach address the potential for AI systems to reinforce or amplify existing biases in human decision-making?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup and the methodology used in the human studies to ensure the validity and reliability of the results?
