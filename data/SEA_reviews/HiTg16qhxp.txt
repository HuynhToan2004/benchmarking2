 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Dynamic Neural Response Tuning (DNRT), a novel mechanism inspired by biological neuron response patterns that aims to enhance the performance of artificial neural networks (ANNs). DNRT comprises two components: Response-Adaptive Activation (RAA) and Aggregated Response Regularization (ARR). RAA dynamically adjusts the response condition based on input signals, while ARR imposes constraints on the Gaussian distribution of neural responses to enhance category specificity. The methodology is tested across various ANN architectures and datasets, showing significant improvements in classification accuracy and generalization to diverse tasks and domains. However, concerns about the novelty of the approach and the clarity of its biological inspiration persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to modeling neural response dynamics in artificial neural networks (ANNs) inspired by biological neurons, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method, Dynamic Neural Response Tuning (DNRT), shows promising results in enhancing the performance of ANNs across various architectures and tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and node classification.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides extensive experimental validation, demonstrating the effectiveness of DNRT in improving neural network performance.
- The authors have conducted a thorough ablation study to understand the contributions of different components of DNRT, which adds depth to the research.
- The paper includes a detailed discussion on the biological inspiration behind DNRT, which enhances the understanding of the method's foundational principles.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the biological inspiration for DNRT, particularly in how it models dynamic response conditions and information aggregation in biological neurons.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with existing dynamic thresholding methods in neural networks and other state-of-the-art activation functions.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental details, such as the number of layers with ARR and the specific values of hyperparameters like momentum and balanced parameter.
- The paper's methodology and experimental setup are not sufficiently novel, as the dynamic thresholding approach has been previously explored in other works.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of DNRT compared to other activation functions, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- The paper's claims about the novelty of DNRT and its superiority over existing activation functions are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of DNRT with existing dynamic thresholding methods in neural networks and other state-of-the-art activation functions?
- How does DNRT perform in terms of computational cost compared to other activation functions, and what are the practical implications of this cost in large-scale applications?
- Could the authors clarify the biological inspiration behind DNRT and how it models dynamic response conditions and information aggregation in biological neurons?
- In the context of the experiments, how does DNRT perform in terms of generalization to new, unseen data, and what are the implications for its practical application in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that aim to improve neural network performance, such as those based on attention mechanisms or other dynamic response models?
