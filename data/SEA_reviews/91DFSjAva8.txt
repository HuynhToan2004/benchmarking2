 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel offline-to-online reinforcement learning (RL) method named Sample Efficient Reward Augmentation (SERA), which utilizes a pre-trained offline RL policy to compute Q-conditioned entropy as an intrinsic reward. SERA aims to enhance exploration and mitigate the performance drop during the early stages of online fine-tuning. The methodology is evaluated on several tasks from the D4RL benchmark, showing significant improvements over existing baselines. Despite its theoretical contributions and empirical success, concerns about the clarity of the motivation, the depth of experimental validation, and the generalizability of the method across different RL algorithms and domains persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to offline-to-online RL by utilizing pre-trained offline RL policies to compute Q-conditioned entropy as intrinsic reward, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, with a comprehensive discussion on the properties of SERA, including the proof of monotonic policy improvement.
- Extensive experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various model-free RL algorithms, showing significant improvements over baseline methods.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, with clear explanations of the methodology and its applications.
- The proposed method is generalizable and can be applied to different offline RL algorithms, enhancing its applicability and utility in diverse RL settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for a comprehensive understanding of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The experimental validation could be strengthened by including more complex environments and comparing the results with a broader range of baselines to validate the effectiveness of the method across different scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity in the motivation behind the use of SERA, particularly in how it addresses the challenges of offline-to-online RL, such as the distribution shift and misalignment between offline and online training stages.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of overestimation of out-of-distribution (OOD) state actions, which is a significant challenge in offline RL.
- The paper's theoretical contributions, while interesting, are somewhat limited in scope and depth, focusing primarily on the convergence of SERA and not providing a broader theoretical framework for its application.
- The experimental setup and results, while promising, are not sufficiently detailed or comprehensive, particularly in terms of the experimental setup, hyperparameter tuning, and the generalizability of the results across different environments and RL algorithms.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the motivation behind the use of SERA and how it addresses the challenges of offline-to-online RL, particularly in terms of distribution shift and misalignment between offline and online training stages?
- How does SERA perform in more complex environments, such as those involving high-dimensional observations or more complex dynamics?
- Could the authors elaborate on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the hyperparameter tuning and the selection of baselines for comparison?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent offline-to-online RL methods, such as those mentioned in the literature review?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios?
