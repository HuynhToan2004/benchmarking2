 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach, Best Response Shaping (BRS), for training agents in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) environments where agents must cooperate to maximize social welfare. BRS trains an agent by differentiating through an opponent approximating the best response, conditioning on the agent's policy through a question answering (QA) mechanism. This mechanism allows the agent to learn a cooperative policy by retaliating against an opponent's defection. The method is tested in the Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma and Coin Game, demonstrating improved performance compared to existing methods like POLA. However, the paper has been criticized for its limited scope, focusing only on two-player games, and for not sufficiently differentiating its contributions from existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) by using a question answering mechanism to condition the detective opponent on the agent's policy, which is a creative and innovative idea.
- The method is well-motivated and clearly presented, with a good discussion of related work, making it accessible and understandable.
- The paper addresses an important problem in MARL by focusing on training agents to cooperate in general-sum games, which is a significant challenge in the field.
- The method shows promise in improving the performance of agents in cooperative scenarios, as demonstrated in the experiments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the question answering mechanism and its role in the overall method, which could be confusing for readers.
- The experimental results are not convincing, particularly in the Coin Game where the results are not significantly better than existing methods like POLA.
- The paper's scope is limited to two-player games, which may not generalize well to more complex scenarios involving multiple agents.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing methods, such as those that use question answering in policy evaluation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and operation of the question answering mechanism in the BRS method?
- How does the BRS method compare to other methods that use question answering in policy evaluation, particularly in terms of performance and methodological contributions?
- What are the specific advantages of the BRS method over existing methods, such as POLA, in terms of scalability, performance, and theoretical contributions?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the hyperparameters used in the simulations, to enhance the reproducibility and transparency of the results?
- How does the BRS method handle scenarios where the agent's policy is not differentiable, and what are the implications of this for the overall effectiveness of the method?
