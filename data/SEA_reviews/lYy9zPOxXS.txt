 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Topology-Informed Graph Transformer (TIGT), which integrates topological positional embeddings, dual-path message-passing layers, and global attention mechanisms to enhance the discriminative power of Graph Transformers. TIGT leverages non-isomorphic universal covers based on cyclic subgraphs to ensure unique graph representations, thereby improving the model's ability to distinguish graph isomorphisms. The theoretical analysis and experimental results demonstrate that TIGT outperforms existing graph transformers in classifying synthetic datasets aimed at distinguishing isomorphism classes of graphs. However, the paper lacks comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and does not include node-level tasks in its experiments, which are crucial for validating the model's effectiveness across various graph-level tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The integration of topological positional embeddings and the dual-path message-passing layer is innovative and well-motivated, enhancing the discriminative power of Graph Transformers.
- The theoretical analysis and mathematical justifications provided in the paper are solid, contributing to the understanding of the model's capabilities.
- The empirical results show that TIGT outperforms existing graph transformers in classifying synthetic datasets aimed at distinguishing isomorphism classes of graphs.
- The paper provides a clear and comprehensive description of the model's architecture, which aids in understanding its design and functionality.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and parameter efficiency.
- The paper does not include node-level tasks in its experiments, which are crucial for validating the model's effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some results showing inferior performance compared to existing methods, and the results on some datasets are missing.
- The paper's motivation and the significance of the proposed method are not clearly articulated, which could affect the understanding and acceptance of the model's contributions.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the number of runs for averaging and the statistical significance of the results.
- The paper's contribution is limited to graph-level tasks, which might not fully demonstrate the model's capabilities across various graph-level tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational complexity and parameter efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform on node-level tasks, and what are the results compared to existing methods?
- Can the authors clarify the motivation behind the proposed method and its significance in enhancing the discriminative power of Graph Transformers?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of over-smoothing, which is a common challenge in Graph Transformers?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the number of runs for averaging and the statistical significance of the results?
- In the context of the theoretical analysis, can the authors elaborate on how the proposed method addresses the issues of over-smoothing and the restricted expressive power of Graph Transformers?
