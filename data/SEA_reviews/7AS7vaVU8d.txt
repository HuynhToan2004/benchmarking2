 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the PerSE model, a personalized story evaluation system designed to predict individualized reviews and comparisons based on a few reviews from the same reviewer. It leverages a novel dataset, Per-MPST, and an existing dataset, Per-DOC, to train the model, which shows significant improvements over GPT-4 in predicting personalized reviews and pairwise preferences. The model is evaluated against various baselines and demonstrates its effectiveness in capturing reviewer preferences, though concerns are raised about the generalizability of the results and the scalability of the method. The paper also discusses the limitations of the model, such as its reliance on constant reviewer preferences and the potential for the model's predictions to deviate from reviewer preferences.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to personalized story evaluation, leveraging a new dataset and a personalized story evaluation model (PerSE) that predicts personalized reviews and pairwise preferences, demonstrating significant improvements over GPT-4.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear visuals and detailed analysis, including a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the model.
- The paper presents a significant improvement in personalized story evaluation, which is a challenging and under-explored area in natural language processing.
- The dataset construction and model training are detailed, providing a solid foundation for further research and application in personalized story generation.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the datasets and the model's operation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation of the model is limited to a single dataset, which may not generalize well to other datasets or real-world scenarios.
- The model's performance heavily relies on the number of reviews from the same reviewer, which could limit its scalability and applicability to real-world settings where reviewers might not provide multiple reviews.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other personalized evaluation models, which could help in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method.
- The model's reliance on constant reviewer preferences may not reflect real-world scenarios where reviewer preferences can change over time.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method, such as its reliance on the quality and diversity of the dataset, the potential for the model to deviate from reviewer preferences, and the scalability of the model to larger datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the model be adapted to other datasets or real-world scenarios, such as personalized product reviews or social media comments?
- How does the model perform when the number of reviews from the same reviewer is limited, and what are the implications for its scalability and applicability?
- Could the model be extended to include more aspects of personalization, such as demographic information or user behavior?
- How does the model handle scenarios where the reviewer's preferences are not consistent across different reviews, and what are the implications for the model's predictions?
- Could the authors provide more details on the dataset construction, particularly how the reviewer preferences are determined and whether there is any overlap between the training and test sets?
- How does the model compare to other personalized evaluation models, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the proposed method's effectiveness and applicability?
