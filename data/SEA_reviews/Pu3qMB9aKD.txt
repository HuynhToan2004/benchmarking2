 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to generating graphs from textual descriptions using a pretrained large language model (LLM). This methodology, which incorporates message passing layers into the LLM's architecture, allows for the incorporation of graph structure into the generation process. The approach is evaluated on two datasets, PCQM4M and WebNLG, demonstrating improvements in parsability and diversity. However, the paper's methodology is criticized for its lack of novelty and the absence of comprehensive comparisons with other baseline models. The use of only two datasets and the lack of detailed experimental results further limit the paper's impact.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly presented.
- The approach of generating graphs from textual descriptions using a pretrained LLM is novel and innovative.
- The paper includes a thorough analysis of the proposed method, comparing it with other baselines and demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The introduction of message passing layers into the LLM's architecture is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper includes a detailed appendix with additional results and discussions, which are helpful for understanding the methodology and its implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger graphs and the computational resources required.
- There is a notable absence of comparisons with other baseline models, such as Grapher and REGEN, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper only uses two datasets, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The methodology section could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples, particularly regarding the node disambiguation and message passing layers.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the novelty of the approach, as similar methods have been previously explored in other works.
- The evaluation metrics used, such as parsability and diversity, are not sufficiently explained or justified, which could affect the credibility of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other baseline models, particularly Grapher and REGEN, to better understand the advantages and limitations of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle the generation of larger graphs, and what are the computational resources required?
- Could the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the gating term in the model, as mentioned in Section 3.3?
- How does the model ensure the generation of valid graphs that do not violate physical laws, particularly in the context of molecule generation?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples and explanations of the message passing layers and node disambiguation to aid in understanding the methodology?
- Why were only two datasets used for evaluation, and how do the authors justify the choice of these datasets?
