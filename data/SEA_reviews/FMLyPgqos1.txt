 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Panacea, a novel federated learning framework that leverages graph hypernetworks to enhance personalization and robustness in federated learning. It integrates a graph neural network (GNN) module and a graph generator to model client relationships, allowing for more effective knowledge sharing and personalization. Theoretical analysis and extensive experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the framework's effectiveness in terms of accuracy, speed, and robustness. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the experimental setup, the novelty of the graph hypernetwork approach, and the lack of comparative analysis with other recent federated learning methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using graph hypernetworks in federated learning, which is an innovative direction for personalized federated learning.
- Theoretical analysis and experimental results are provided to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, which shows promising results on various datasets.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and detailed algorithm descriptions that enhance understanding.
- The proposed method is original and extends the application of hypernetworks in federated learning, which is an interesting approach to tackle the heterogeneity issue.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method achieves better performance compared to other baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the experimental setup, particularly the dataset and model parameters, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The novelty of the graph hypernetwork approach is questionable, as similar methods have been previously explored in other federated learning frameworks.
- The paper does not provide sufficient comparative analysis with other recent federated learning methods, which could have provided a better context for evaluating the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The theoretical analysis is limited, and the paper does not adequately discuss the assumptions and limitations of the theoretical results, which could affect the reliability of the conclusions drawn.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not address the scalability of the proposed method, which is a significant concern for federated learning applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the dataset and model parameters, to ensure the reproducibility and comparability of the results?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent federated learning methods, especially those that also use graph neural networks or hypernetworks?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations on how the graph generator is trained and its role in the overall system?
- In the theoretical analysis, can the authors discuss the assumptions and limitations of the results to provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle scalability issues in federated learning applications, and what measures are in place to ensure its effectiveness in large-scale deployments?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these could impact its practical application?
