 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for efficiently computing Fourier transforms on non-equispaced grids using a Vandermonde matrix, which is integrated into neural operator models to handle arbitrary point distributions. This methodology aims to enhance the computational efficiency and accuracy of neural operators in solving PDEs, particularly on irregular grids. The approach is evaluated against various benchmarks and is shown to outperform existing methods like FNO, Geo-FNO, and NUFFT. The method's ability to handle non-uniform grids and its integration with neural operators are highlighted as significant contributions. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of comprehensive theoretical analysis and detailed comparative experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel method for computing Fourier transforms on non-equispaced grids using a Vandermonde matrix, which is integrated into neural operator models, enhancing the accuracy and computational efficiency.
- The methodology is demonstrated to be effective across various benchmarks, showing improvements over existing methods like FNO, Geo-FNO, and NUFFT.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its implementation details, making it accessible to readers.
- The authors have successfully demonstrated the method's ability to handle arbitrary point distributions, which is a significant advancement over traditional methods that require uniform grids.
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed method, particularly concerning the stability and convergence of the Vandermonde matrix and the computational complexity of the inverse transformation.
- There is a lack of detailed comparison with other methods like Geo-FNO and NUFFT, which could provide a clearer picture of the method's advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper could benefit from more diverse benchmarking, including a broader range of PDEs and different types of neural operators to validate the method's applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are sometimes unclear or not well-explained, which could hinder the reader's understanding of the method's performance and impact.
- The paper's claims regarding the computational efficiency and the need for a Vandermonde matrix for each training sample are not convincingly justified, especially in terms of practical implementation and scalability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed method, particularly concerning the stability and convergence of the Vandermonde matrix and the computational complexity of the inverse transformation?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods like Geo-FNO and NUFFT in terms of computational complexity, accuracy, and practical implementation?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly in the Burgers' Equation benchmark, where the results are not clearly explained?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed method scale with the number of modes and the size of the input data?
- Is there a practical limitation on the number of modes that can be used with the proposed method, and how does this affect the accuracy and computational efficiency?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of neural operators, such as those based on wavelets or other spectral transformations?
