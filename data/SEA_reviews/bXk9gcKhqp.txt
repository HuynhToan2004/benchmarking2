 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph neural network (GNN) architecture called SimpleNet, which utilizes a new polynomial filter based on graph information activation (GIA) theory. The authors demonstrate that the expressive power of GNNs does not depend on the polynomial degree and that the convergence rate is linear, challenging the traditional polynomial filter design in GNNs. The SimpleNet architecture, incorporating a linear combination of positive and non-positive activations, shows competitive performance on both homogeneous and heterogeneous datasets. Theoretical insights are provided on the expressive power and convergence rate of GNNs, which are supported by empirical results on benchmark node classification datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new polynomial filter based on graph information activation (GIA) theory, which provides a novel perspective for interpreting polynomial filters in GNNs.
- The theoretical analysis on the expressive power and convergence rate of GNNs is comprehensive and insightful, challenging the traditional polynomial filter design in GNNs.
- The proposed SimpleNet architecture, which combines positive and non-positive activations, shows competitive performance on both homogeneous and heterogeneous datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations and proofs that support the theoretical claims.
- The experiments conducted on benchmark node classification datasets show the superiority of the proposed SimpleNet compared to existing GNNs in both accuracy and running time.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the GIA theory and its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The experimental results, particularly on some datasets like Penn94 and Chameleon, are not as strong as expected, raising concerns about the generalizability of the SimpleNet architecture.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed methods, especially in relation to other state-of-the-art methods.
- There is a need for more comprehensive comparisons with other GNNs, including a broader range of datasets and metrics to assess the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion on the graph information activation theory and its implications for the design of GNNs.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the GIA theory and how these might affect the practical application of the SimpleNet architecture?
- Why does SimpleNet perform poorly on the Penn94 dataset compared to other methods, and what insights can be gained from this observation?
- Is there a possibility to extend the GIA theory to non-linear cases, and how might this impact the design of GNNs?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed methods, especially in comparison to other state-of-the-art GNNs?
- How do the authors justify the choice of metrics, such as micro-F1 score, and could other metrics be considered to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the SimpleNet architecture?
