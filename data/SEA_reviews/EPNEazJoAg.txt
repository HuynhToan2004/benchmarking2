 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for quantifying feature interactions in machine learning models, focusing on the Rashomon set of models that achieve similar accuracy on a task. The method involves a greedy search algorithm to identify the range of feature interaction scores (FIS) within this set, and proposes visualization tools like halo plots and swarm plots to aid in understanding these interactions. The approach is tested across various datasets, including synthetic and real-world tasks, and is claimed to provide insights into the nature of feature interactions in models. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in defining and motivating the concept of FIS, the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, and the limited comparative analysis with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue of understanding feature interactions in machine learning models, which is crucial for model interpretability and robustness.
- The authors introduce a novel method for quantifying feature interaction strength, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive review of related literature.
- The proposed visualization tools, such as halo plots and swarm plots, are innovative and help in understanding the nature of feature interactions.
- The methodology is supported by a variety of experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and concise definition of the feature interaction score (FIS) and the feature interaction score cloud (FISC), which makes it difficult for readers to grasp the core contributions of the study.
- There is a significant computational cost associated with the proposed algorithm, which could limit its practical application.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods, particularly in terms of the novelty and practical implications of the proposed methods.
- The experimental validation of the proposed method is limited, and the paper does not convincingly demonstrate the superiority or practical utility of the FISC over existing methods.
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity and precision in some sections, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key concepts and methodologies.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, nor does it provide a detailed discussion on the societal impact of the work.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and the role of the feature interaction score (FIS) in their methodology?
- How does the proposed method differ from existing approaches, and what are the specific advantages of using the FISC over other methods?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including a comparison with other methods and a discussion on the computational complexity of their approach?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential issues of overfitting or underfitting in the models used for feature interaction analysis?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical applications of their method and provide real-world examples where the FISC has been successfully applied?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of model selection bias, particularly in scenarios where the choice of model might influence the results?
