 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel pruning method for Large Language Models (LLMs) called Wanda, which employs a pruning metric that incorporates both weights and input activations to determine the importance of each weight. This method avoids the need for retraining or weight updates, making it computationally efficient. The approach is evaluated on LLaMA and LLaMA-2 across various language benchmarks, showing that it significantly outperforms existing pruning methods like magnitude pruning and competes with recent methods involving intensive weight updates. The method is highlighted for its simplicity and effectiveness in preserving performance while reducing the number of parameters in LLMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method, Wanda, is simple yet effective, with a straightforward approach to pruning weights and activations.
- The method does not require retraining or weight update, which is a significant advantage in terms of computational efficiency.
- The paper presents a novel pruning metric that considers both weights and input activations, which is a unique contribution to the field.
- The empirical results show that Wanda significantly outperforms the established baseline of magnitude pruning and performs competitively against recent methods involving intensive weight update.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of Wanda on LLaMA and LLaMA-2 across various language benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other pruning methods, such as those using different norms for activation magnitudes or other pruning techniques like LoRA.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods, such as Optimal Brain Damage (OBD).
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational complexity of the pruning process, particularly the overhead introduced by the need to compute norms for each input activation.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient implementation details, such as the code for the proposed method, which could hinder reproducibility.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of layers in LLMs.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other pruning methods, particularly those using different norms for activation magnitudes or other pruning techniques like LoRA?
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of the pruning process, especially the overhead introduced by the need to compute norms for each input activation?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to include other types of layers in LLMs, such as attention layers?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of tasks beyond language modeling?
- Could you provide more details on the implementation of the method, including the code for the proposed method, to enhance reproducibility?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential negative societal impacts, particularly in terms of fairness and bias in LLMs?
