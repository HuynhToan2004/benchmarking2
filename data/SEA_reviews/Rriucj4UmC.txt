 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for reconstructing cortical surfaces from infant brain MRI data using a flow-based approach that integrates spherical mapping with the cortical surface reconstruction (CSR) process. The methodology involves deforming a spherical template mesh to match the white and pial surfaces, which facilitates the mapping of geometric features to the sphere for visualization and downstream processing. The proposed method is evaluated against several state-of-the-art methods and is shown to enhance mesh regularity and reduce geometric errors, while maintaining high computational efficiency. The methodology is validated on a dataset of infants from 0 to 12 months of age, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling the unique challenges posed by infant brain development.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in the field of cortical surface reconstruction (CSR) from MRI data, which is crucial for understanding brain structure and function.
- The methodology is novel, integrating spherical mapping with CSR in a unified framework, which is a significant advancement over traditional methods that require additional time-consuming spherical mapping steps.
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the methodology and the results, which are supported by a comprehensive evaluation against state-of-the-art methods.
- The use of a flow-based approach for CSR is innovative and potentially more efficient compared to traditional methods that involve multiple steps and longer processing times.
- The paper demonstrates significant improvements in mesh regularity and reduced geometric errors compared to state-of-the-art deep learning approaches, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The evaluation is limited to a single dataset, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the method across different populations or imaging modalities.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is a critical factor in practical applications.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the technical contributions of the proposed method, particularly in differentiating it from existing methods like SurfFlow and CorticalFlow++.
- The paper does not adequately address the challenges posed by the use of dual-modal inputs, which could affect the method's performance and applicability.
- The method's effectiveness in handling large deformations and its ability to maintain mesh uniformity across different brain volumes are not thoroughly discussed.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in practical scenarios where speed is a critical factor?
- Can the method be adapted to handle other imaging modalities or different populations, and what would be the expected outcomes?
- How does the method ensure that the reconstructed surfaces maintain their topology, particularly in cases where the template mesh may not perfectly match the subject's anatomy?
- What are the specific technical contributions of the proposed method that differentiate it from existing methods like SurfFlow and CorticalFlow++?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency and the memory requirements of the proposed method, particularly in comparison to other state-of-the-art methods?
- How does the method handle the challenges posed by the use of dual-modal inputs, and what are the implications for the method's performance and applicability?
