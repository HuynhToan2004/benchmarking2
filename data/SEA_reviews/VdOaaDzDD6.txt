 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the multi-armed bandit problem with ranking feedback, where the learner only receives the ranking of arms based on previous pulls, not their actual rewards. The authors explore both stochastic and adversarial settings, providing theoretical results on the regret bounds and the impossibility of achieving logarithmic regret in instance-dependent scenarios. They propose two algorithms, DREE and R-LPE, to address the challenges of this new setting. The paper also includes experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms over existing baselines. However, the paper suffers from several issues such as poor writing quality, unclear proofs, and a lack of comparison with existing literature, particularly in the field of dueling bandits.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a novel setting of multi-arm bandits with ranking feedback, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided is solid, with proofs that are mostly sound.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions and explanations that aid in understanding the complex concepts.
- The algorithms proposed, such as DREE and R-LPE, are well-explained and provide a solid foundation for future research in this area.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, showing that they outperform existing baselines in certain scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from poor writing quality, with several grammatical errors and unclear statements that make it difficult to follow.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definitions and explanations, particularly in the mathematical definitions and their applications.
- The proofs provided are often hand-wavy and lack sufficient detail, making it difficult to verify their correctness.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing literature, particularly in the field of dueling bandits, which is similar in some aspects.
- The experimental results are limited to a small number of runs, which might not be sufficient to draw robust conclusions.
- The paper does not adequately address the implications of ranking feedback in practical scenarios, which could limit its applicability.
