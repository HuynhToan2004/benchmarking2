 **Summary:**
The paper explores the integration of predictive auxiliary objectives in deep reinforcement learning (RL) systems, comparing their effects to representational changes observed in the brain. The authors employ a deep Q-learning network with a predictive auxiliary objective, using a gridworld foraging task to demonstrate how these objectives can prevent representational collapse and enhance learning in resource-limited architectures. The paper also investigates the impact of predictive horizons on representational transfer and the correspondence between predictive model behavior in RL and neural activity in the brain, particularly in the hippocampus and visual cortex. The study is novel in its approach to modeling multi-region interactions in the brain, offering a unique perspective on how predictive learning in the hippocampus influences representation learning in other brain regions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel perspective by modeling multi-region interactions in the brain, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The use of a simple gridworld foraging task effectively demonstrates the effects of predictive auxiliary objectives in deep RL systems.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors have conducted a thorough investigation into the effects of predictive auxiliary objectives, which is supported by a variety of experiments and simulations.
- The findings are original and offer a new perspective on the role of the hippocampus in learning, which is particularly interesting and relevant to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison to prior work, making it difficult to assess its novelty and contribution to the existing literature.
- The empirical validation of the model's claims is limited, and the paper does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the findings beyond the specific tasks and environments used.
- The paper's focus on a single task and a simple network architecture limits the scope of its conclusions and the robustness of its findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the model and the broader implications of the findings, which could be crucial for understanding the practical applications and future directions of the research.
- The paper's claims about the brain are not well-supported by empirical evidence, and the analogy between RL systems and brain regions is not convincingly argued.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the predictive auxiliary objective influences the learning process in the RL system, particularly in terms of its impact on representation learning?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of their model, such as its simplicity and generalizability?
- Could the authors elaborate on the role of the hippocampus in learning and its relationship to other brain regions, particularly in the context of predictive modeling?
- In light of the paper's claims about the brain, how do the authors justify the analogy between RL systems and brain regions, and what evidence supports these claims?
- How do the authors envision the practical applications of their findings, and what are the potential implications for future research in this area?
