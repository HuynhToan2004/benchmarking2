 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the integration of categorical features in graph neural networks (GNNs) for recommender systems, specifically focusing on the representation of these features as hyperedges in a bipartite graph. The authors explore the performance of GNNs with different categorical feature representations, including one-hot encoding, hyperedges, and node features, using three datasets. The study concludes that hyperedges provide the best performance, suggesting that they can effectively capture high-order interactions between items. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive theoretical analysis, and insufficient comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research.
- The use of hyperedges to model high-order interactions between items is innovative and shows potential for enhancing the performance of recommender systems.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of existing methods for incorporating categorical features into graph neural networks (GNNs), which is beneficial for understanding the current state of research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as using hyperedges to represent categorical features is not new and has been previously explored in other studies.
- The paper lacks a theoretical analysis to support the effectiveness of hyperedges, which could strengthen the argument for their use.
- The comparison with existing methods is insufficient and lacks depth, particularly in terms of explaining why hyperedges outperform other methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the methodology used for creating hyperedges and how they are integrated into the GNN model.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the improvements are sometimes marginal or not statistically significant.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of using hyperedges, such as the potential for increased computational complexity and the need for careful handling of feature categorization.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how hyperedges are created and integrated into the GNN model?
- How does the performance of the proposed method compare when using different numbers of hyperedges?
- How does the computational complexity of using hyperedges compare to other methods, and what are the implications for scalability?
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a discussion of the specific advantages of using hyperedges over other approaches?
- How do the authors address the potential for overfitting or biased feature categorization when using hyperedges?
- Can you provide more detailed experimental results, including standard deviations and statistical significance tests, to support the claims of superior performance?
