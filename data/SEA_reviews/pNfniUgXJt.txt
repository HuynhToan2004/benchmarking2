 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for learning ODEs from a limited number of snapshots using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and a Wasserstein-based loss function. The method, named Symbolic Distribution Flow Learner (SDFL), leverages MCTS for symbolic search, ensuring permutation invariance, and optimizes a discrete version of the Wasserstein loss to recover ODEs from a set of sampled trajectories. Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness in recovering ODEs, particularly in the context of stochastic network dynamics. The paper also discusses the computational complexity and the need for further optimization in terms of computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning ODEs using a limited number of snapshots, which is a significant advancement in the field of symbolic regression.
- The use of a discrete version of the Wasserstein loss for optimizing ODEs is innovative and provides a robust framework for learning dynamics from limited data.
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the problem, methodology, and results, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The theoretical results, such as Theorem 1 and Proposition 1, are well-explained and provide a solid foundation for the proposed method.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing competitive performance compared to existing methods like TrajectoryNet and JKONet.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other existing methods, particularly those that also use MCTS for symbolic regression. This omission limits the evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness against similar approaches.
- The computational complexity of the proposed method is high, which might limit its practical applicability, especially in large-scale problems.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently robust, with only two experiments conducted on small datasets. More comprehensive experiments on larger, more diverse datasets are needed to validate the method's effectiveness.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the method handles noisy or incomplete data, which is crucial for evaluating its robustness and generalizability.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the choice of the UCT function in the MCTS algorithm and its impact on the performance of the method?
- How does the proposed method handle permutation invariance in the data, and what are the implications of this approach on the results?
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of the method and how it scales with the size of the problem?
- How does the method perform in the presence of noisy or incomplete data, and what are the implications of these conditions on the results?
- Could you elaborate on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and the experimental conditions, to better understand the results and their implications?
- Is there a possibility of extending the method to handle higher-order differential equations, and if so, what would be the challenges and potential solutions?
