 **Summary:**
The paper introduces "hot PATE," a novel extension of the PATE framework tailored for generative AI models, specifically designed for tasks that require diverse responses. Hot PATE modifies the traditional PATE approach by allowing each teacher model to generate a response distribution, which is then aggregated to preserve privacy and diversity. The method is demonstrated to achieve privacy-utility trade-offs comparable to or better than baseline "cold" PATE, through both analytical and empirical means. The paper also discusses how hot PATE can be applied to diverse tasks and provides theoretical and empirical evidence of its effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The proposed method, hot PATE, is novel and extends the PATE framework to generative AI models, providing a privacy-preserving approach for diverse tasks.
- The paper provides both theoretical and empirical evidence to support the proposed method, demonstrating its effectiveness in preserving privacy and diversity.
- The authors discuss the limitations of the method, such as the need for a sufficient number of teachers possessing the knowledge to be transferred, and provide a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential applications of hot PATE.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear introduction and background on PATE and its extensions, followed by a detailed explanation of the proposed method and its components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability and scalability, especially when the number of teachers or the complexity of the tasks increases.
- The empirical evaluation of hot PATE is limited, focusing on a single task with a small number of queries, which may not adequately demonstrate the method's effectiveness in more complex or varied settings.
- The paper could provide more concrete examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications of hot PATE, especially in scenarios where the method could be particularly beneficial.
- The paper's reliance on the PATE framework and its extension to generative AI models might limit its appeal to a broader audience, particularly those not familiar with PATE.
- There are some unclear definitions and notations in the paper, which could be improved for better clarity and understanding.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications of hot PATE in different scenarios?
- How does the number of teachers affect the diversity and privacy of the aggregated responses in hot PATE?
- Is there a specific reason why the empirical evaluation was limited to a single task with a small number of queries? How would the method perform in more complex or varied settings?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and notations used in the paper, particularly in the algorithm sections, to improve understanding and clarity?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for private aggregation of distributions, especially in terms of privacy and diversity preservation?
