 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ABC-RL, a novel method for logic synthesis that leverages a pre-trained policy network and Monte-Carlo tree search (MCTS) to optimize synthesis recipes. ABC-RL utilizes a similarity score between new and existing designs to determine the contribution of the pre-trained agent in the search process. The method aims to improve the quality of results (QoR) and reduce runtime by adjusting the contribution of the pre-trained agent based on the novelty of the design. The approach is tested on a set of benchmarks and shows promising results, outperforming existing methods in terms of both QoR and runtime efficiency. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, and insufficient experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is novel and effective, with a clear motivation and well-structured presentation of the methodology.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are promising, showing improvements in QoR and runtime efficiency.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, including an ablation study and comparisons with other methods.
- The methodology is innovative, combining reinforcement learning (RL) with Monte-Carlo tree search (MCTS) and a retrieval-guided approach, which is novel in the context of logic synthesis.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- There is a significant gap in the experimental results compared to existing methods, particularly in terms of the QoR metric, which is not adequately explained.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as it seems to be a combination of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have strengthened the paper's claims.
- The methodology's reliance on similarity scores and the selection of benchmarks for training and testing raises concerns about the generalizability and robustness of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the method to larger or more complex circuits, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of their proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of runtime efficiency and QoR with other state-of-the-art methods, especially those that use different search algorithms or learning techniques?
- Could the authors explain the discrepancies in the QoR results between their method and existing methods, and provide more insights into the selection of benchmarks for training and testing?
- How does the method perform on larger or more complex circuits, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in scaling up the method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and validation of the retrieval-guided approach, and how it impacts the overall performance of the method?
