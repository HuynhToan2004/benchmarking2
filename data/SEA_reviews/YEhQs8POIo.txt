 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework called Private Evolution (PE) for generating Differentially Private (DP) synthetic data using foundation model APIs. This approach does not require training models, instead relying on the APIs to generate synthetic data, which is then used to train classifiers. The PE framework iteratively refines the synthetic data by selecting parents with high fitness scores and generating offspring that closely resemble the private data. The paper provides empirical evidence of the effectiveness of this approach, showing that it can achieve state-of-the-art performance with lower privacy costs compared to existing methods. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope to image datasets, lack of theoretical analysis, and potential issues with the scalability and generalizability of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to generating Differentially Private (DP) synthetic data using foundation model APIs, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that rely on training models from scratch.
- The proposed PE framework is simple yet effective, and the paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the algorithm, making it accessible and understandable.
- The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the PE framework in generating high-quality synthetic data with lower privacy costs compared to existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the complex concepts presented.
- The potential of the PE framework to democratize the deployment of DP synthetic data in industry is highlighted, which could have significant implications for data privacy and security.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on image datasets limits the generalizability of the findings to other data types, such as text or audio, which could be crucial for broader applications of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis on the convergence of the PE framework, which could undermine the credibility of the empirical results.
- The paper's approach is criticized for being too simple and not adequately addressing the complexities of generating DP synthetic data, particularly in scenarios where the distribution shift between private and public data is significant.
- The paper's claims regarding the novelty and effectiveness of the PE framework are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which do not consistently outperform existing methods.
- There are concerns about the scalability of the PE framework, particularly in terms of the computational resources required and the potential for scalability issues as the number of iterations increases.
- The paper's discussion on the limitations and potential improvements of the PE framework is relegated to the appendix, which could make it less accessible to readers.

**Questions:**
- Can the PE framework be adapted to other data types, such as text or audio, to generate DP synthetic data in those domains?
- How does the PE framework ensure that the synthetic data remains faithful to the original private data, especially when there is a significant distribution shift between the private and public datasets?
- What are the specific computational resources required to run the PE framework, and how does the number of iterations affect the computational demands?
- How does the PE framework handle the potential for overfitting to the private data, especially when the number of iterations is large?
- Could you provide more details on the privacy analysis of the PE framework, particularly how it ensures that the synthetic data remains DP compliant?
- How does the PE framework compare to other existing methods in terms of privacy costs and utility, especially when the number of iterations is varied?
- In the experimental results, why does the PE framework sometimes underperform compared to existing methods, and how does this affect the practical applicability of the framework?
