 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Early-Stopping Self-Consistency (ESC), a novel method aimed at reducing the computational cost of Self-Consistency (SC) in large language models (LLMs) without compromising performance. ESC dynamically adjusts the sampling process by truncating it when entropy within a window is low, indicating high confidence in the sampled answers. This method is supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results showing significant reductions in sampling times across various benchmarks, maintaining comparable performance to SC. The paper also proposes a control scheme for ESC, which adjusts parameters based on performance and cost expectations, providing a practical solution for real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method, Early-Stopping Self-Consistency (ESC), is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, making it accessible for practical applications.
- ESC demonstrates significant improvements in reducing the number of samples required compared to Self-Consistency (SC), achieving substantial reductions in computational cost.
- The method is supported by theoretical analysis, including the derivation of an upper bound on the probability of inconsistency and the derivation of an expectation of sampling cost and voting performance.
- ESC shows robustness across various sampling strategies, including different window sizes, sampling sizes, and demonstrations, indicating its general applicability.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of ESC across multiple benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing sampling times while maintaining comparable performance to SC.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, such as the method proposed by Aggarwal et al. (2023), which also addresses early stopping in self-consistency.
- There is a need for more detailed analysis and discussion on the practical implications of the control scheme, particularly how it balances performance and cost expectations.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive experimental validation, including on larger datasets and different model sizes, to better demonstrate the generalizability and effectiveness of ESC.
- The theoretical analysis provided is not sufficiently detailed or rigorous, particularly in the derivation of expectations and the assumptions made about the probability distribution P.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed descriptions of the experimental setup and results, as well as providing a clearer explanation of the theoretical assumptions and their implications.

**Questions:**
- How does ESC perform when applied to different model sizes, such as smaller or larger models?
- Can you provide more details on the practical implementation of the control scheme, particularly how it balances performance and cost expectations?
- How does the method perform when applied to open-ended generation tasks, and what are the implications for the control scheme in such scenarios?
- Could you clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the assumption that the sampled outcomes are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.)?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of reasoning tasks, such as those involving complex logical relationships or natural language processing?
