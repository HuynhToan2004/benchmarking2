 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to animal behavioral analysis using a hierarchical vector quantization (VQ) technique applied to mouse behavior data. The method involves a two-level VQ-VAE, where the top-level codes represent coarse behavioral categories and the bottom-level codes capture finer-grained kinematic details. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method by comparing it with state-of-the-art approaches, showing that it can accurately categorize behaviors and generate realistic motion sequences. The paper also explores the application of this method to other species, such as humans, and discusses its potential for generating realistic motion sequences and mapping behaviors across different species. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation on a single dataset and the need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the methods used, including the use of a hierarchical vector quantization (VQ) approach to categorize behaviors and generate realistic motion sequences.
- The proposed method shows promise in accurately categorizing behaviors and generating realistic motion sequences, as demonstrated by comparisons with state-of-the-art approaches.
- The paper is original in its application of VQ-VAE to behavioral data, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, with detailed descriptions of the model architecture and training procedures, which aids in reproducibility.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including comparisons with existing methods and demonstrations of its effectiveness on different datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on one dataset (mouse), which limits the generalizability of the results. More evaluations on other datasets, such as rats, would be beneficial to assess the method's applicability across different species.
- The comparison with existing methods is limited, and the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion and comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in the field.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, including its potential biases and assumptions, which could affect its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's reliance on a single dataset and its lack of comparison with other methods in terms of computational efficiency and other performance metrics raises concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the results, particularly in terms of their relevance and applicability to real-world behavioral analysis tasks.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when applied to other species, such as rats?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, including the specifics of the training data and the hyperparameters used?
- How does the method compare in terms of computational efficiency with other methods, and what are the implications of this in terms of practical applications?
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its assumptions and potential biases?
- How does the method handle variations in behavioral patterns across different individuals or experimental conditions?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other methods in terms of performance metrics, such as accuracy, computational efficiency, and generalizability?
