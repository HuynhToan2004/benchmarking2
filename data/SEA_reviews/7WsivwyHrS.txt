 **Summary:**
The paper introduces YOQO, a novel label-only membership inference attack (MIA) that significantly reduces the number of queries required to 1, thereby improving efficiency and reducing detection risks. YOQO operates by identifying a special area around the target sample, termed the improvement area, and crafting a query sample with a hard label that accurately reflects the target sample's membership. The attack is evaluated across various datasets and model architectures, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness against various defenses. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the method, the clarity of the presentation, and the generalizability of the results, particularly in scenarios with different training set sizes.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, YOQO, is novel and addresses the significant challenge of reducing the number of queries needed for membership inference attacks, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering various datasets and model architectures, which demonstrates the robustness and adaptability of the YOQO attack.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology, including the optimization process and the use of shadow models, which are essential for understanding the attack's effectiveness.
- The results show that YOQO is effective against various state-of-the-art defenses, indicating its practical applicability and potential impact on the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is questioned as it closely resembles existing works, particularly the gap attack, with minor modifications.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The generalizability of the results is unclear, particularly in scenarios where the training set size varies significantly.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which could detract from its professional presentation.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the ethical implications of the attack, which is a critical consideration in the context of privacy and security.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters on the attack's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of the proposed method in relation to existing works, particularly the gap attack?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the training set size is significantly different from what was used in the experiments?
- Can the authors provide a detailed explanation of how the method performs when the victim model is trained on a dataset that is not identical to the shadow dataset?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the victim model is trained on a dataset that is a subset of the shadow dataset?
- Can the authors elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the attack's effectiveness?
- Could the authors address the typographical and formatting errors noted in the paper to improve its professional presentation?
