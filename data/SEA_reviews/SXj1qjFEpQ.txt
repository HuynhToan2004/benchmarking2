 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called FNSDA, which leverages the Fourier domain for generalizing dynamical systems across different environments. FNSDA separates Fourier modes into common and environment-specific components, allowing for efficient adaptation to new environments by updating only the environment-specific parameters. This approach is contrasted with traditional methods that require significant parameter updates across the entire network. The method is tested on various datasets, demonstrating its ability to generalize effectively across different dynamical systems. However, the paper has been criticized for its limited scope in terms of the types of dynamical systems considered and the lack of comparison with more recent methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is novel and demonstrates significant improvements in generalization performance compared to existing methods.
- The use of Fourier domain adaptation for dynamical systems is innovative and well-explained, with clear motivation and methodology.
- The methodology is sound, with a clear explanation of the method's operation and its advantages over existing methods.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's performance, including ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with more recent methods, which could provide a clearer picture of the method's advantages and limitations.
- The experimental setup is limited to simple dynamical systems, which may not fully demonstrate the method's capabilities in more complex or realistic scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of distribution shifts, which could impact the method's performance in practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is critical for its practical deployment.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of Fourier modes and their impact on the method's performance.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a comparison with more recent methods to better position the proposed method within the current research landscape?
- How does the method perform when the distribution shifts significantly from the training data?
- Is there a possibility to include a discussion on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, especially in comparison to other methods?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of Fourier modes on the method's performance, possibly through an ablation study?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the environmental parameter 'ce' in the method's operation and its impact on the method's performance?
