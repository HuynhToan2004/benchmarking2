 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, ITPNet, for instantaneous trajectory prediction, specifically designed for scenarios where only two observed locations are available. This method leverages a backward forecasting mechanism to reconstruct unobserved historical trajectory information and a Noise Redundancy Reduction Former (NRRFormer) to filter out noise and redundancy in the predicted latent feature representations. The approach is validated on the Argoverse and nuScenes datasets, demonstrating significant performance improvements over existing methods. The methodology is flexible, allowing integration with various trajectory prediction models, and is shown to outperform baseline models in both accuracy and diversity of predicted trajectories.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the proposed method, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed method, ITPNet, is a novel approach that effectively handles the challenge of instantaneous trajectory prediction by utilizing a backward forecasting mechanism and a Noise Redundancy Reduction Former (NRRFormer) to filter out noise and redundancy in the predicted latent feature representations.
- The paper includes a thorough analysis of the limitations of existing models and the design of the NRRFormer to address these issues, providing a robust framework for handling limited trajectory data.
- The experiments conducted on the Argoverse and nuScenes datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of ITPNet, showing significant improvements in performance compared to baseline models.
- The method's ability to generalize with different backbone models and its potential to be integrated into existing trajectory prediction models makes it a versatile and practical solution for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the assumptions made about the availability of unobserved trajectory data and the potential for inaccuracies in the reconstruction of these data.
- There is a need for more comprehensive comparisons with other relevant methods, especially those that also focus on handling limited trajectory data, to establish the superiority of ITPNet more convincingly.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters and model configurations on the performance of ITPNet, as well as a discussion on the scalability of the method with varying lengths of unobserved trajectories.
- The paper's experimental results, while showing improvements, are not sufficiently convincing in demonstrating the superiority of ITPNet over existing methods, particularly in scenarios with more complex or varied trajectory data.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant in autonomous driving applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the selection of the hyperparameters used in the experiments and discuss the sensitivity of the results to these parameters?
- How does the proposed method perform when the unobserved trajectory data is inaccurate or noisy? What measures can be taken to mitigate the effects of such data on the model's predictions?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the LSTM architecture for the backward forecasting mechanism and discuss the potential benefits or drawbacks of using other architectures like RNNs or transformers?
- In terms of scalability, how does the method perform when the length of the unobserved trajectory increases? Is there a point at which the method's performance saturates or starts to decline?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other relevant methods, particularly those that also focus on handling limited trajectory data, to establish the superiority of ITPNet more convincingly?
