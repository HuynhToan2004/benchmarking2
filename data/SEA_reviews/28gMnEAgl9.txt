 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new benchmark for evaluating abstract reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) by adapting datasets from visual reasoning tasks to text-based formats. It evaluates various LLMs on this benchmark and shows that they perform poorly, with GPT-4 being the best performer. The authors also explore different techniques like chain-of-thought prompting and code generation to enhance LLM performance, but these methods do not significantly improve results. The paper highlights the limitations of current LLMs in abstract reasoning tasks and suggests that these models lack the fundamental properties for strong abstract reasoning. However, the paper's contribution is seen as limited as it primarily evaluates LLMs without significant advancements in methodology or insights into why LLMs struggle with abstract reasoning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of large language models (LLMs) on abstract reasoning tasks, which is a novel approach to understanding the capabilities of LLMs in this domain.
- The authors have created a new benchmark for evaluating LLMs on abstract reasoning tasks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and includes detailed experimental results and analysis, which are valuable for understanding the current state of LLMs in abstract reasoning.
- The paper is the first to systematically evaluate LLMs on abstract reasoning tasks, which is a critical area for understanding the limitations and potential of LLMs in complex reasoning tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not provide new insights or methodologies for improving LLMs in abstract reasoning tasks, which limits its contribution to the field.
- The evaluation of LLMs on abstract reasoning tasks is limited to a few models (GPT-2, GPT-3.5-Turbo, GPT-4, LLaMA-7B, Alpaca, Alpaca-LoRA), which may not be representative of the broader LLM landscape.
- The paper's conclusions are somewhat expected, as LLMs are known to struggle with abstract reasoning tasks, and the results do not offer significant new insights into why this is the case.
- The paper's methodology and results lack depth and novelty, as the evaluation techniques and datasets used are not significantly different from those used in previous studies.
- The paper's contribution to the field is seen as limited as it primarily evaluates LLMs without advancing the methodology or providing new insights into why LLMs struggle with abstract reasoning.

**Questions:**
- How do the authors plan to extend their evaluation to include a broader range of LLMs, including open-source models, to ensure a more comprehensive understanding of LLM capabilities in abstract reasoning?
- What are the specific reasons why LLMs struggle with abstract reasoning tasks, and how might these issues be addressed in future research or model development?
- Could the authors elaborate on the methodology used in their evaluation, particularly the selection and adaptation of datasets from visual reasoning tasks to text-based formats?
- How do the authors address the potential for overfitting in their evaluations, especially given the limited number of models tested?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the types of errors made by LLMs in abstract reasoning tasks, and how might these errors be corrected or mitigated?
