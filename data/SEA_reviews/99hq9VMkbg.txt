 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of quantization on the performance of DETR-based object detectors, focusing on critical-category performance. It proposes a Fisher-aware mixed-precision quantization scheme and Fisher-trace regularization to enhance the critical-category performance. The authors identify that certain critical categories are more sensitive to quantization and have inferior generalization after quantization-aware training. The paper provides theoretical and empirical evidence linking performance gaps and corresponding loss landscapes with the Fisher information framework. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited scope, particularly in the types of quantization and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with other methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel approach to studying the impact of quantization on critical-category performance in DETR-based object detectors, which is a significant issue in practical applications.
- The authors conduct extensive theoretical and empirical analysis, including the derivation of the Fisher trace of critical-category objectives and the correlation between the Fisher trace and critical-category performance.
- The paper proposes two novel techniques, a Fisher-aware mixed-precision quantization scheme and Fisher-trace regularization, which are shown to improve the performance of critical categories.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly in improving the critical-category performance of DETR detectors.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is limited as it only considers uniform quantization and does not explore other types of quantization, such as log-compression, which might provide a more robust evaluation.
- The experimental setup is not comprehensive enough, particularly in terms of the number of categories used in the critical-category analysis and the lack of comparison with other methods like pruning.
- The paper's focus on DETR models limits its applicability to other object detection models, such as Faster RCNN and YOLO, which could also benefit from the proposed methods.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed methods, which is crucial for practical deployment.
- The paper's theoretical analysis and experimental results are not sufficiently convincing, particularly in demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods across different categories and quantization settings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of why the proposed methods are effective and how they compare to other methods like pruning?
- How do the authors define "critical categories" in their experiments, and what criteria are used for this definition?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed methods with other state-of-the-art methods, including a discussion on the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed methods?
- How do the authors ensure that the proposed methods are effective across different categories and quantization settings, especially in scenarios where the performance gaps between critical and non-critical categories are significant?
