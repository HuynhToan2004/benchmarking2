 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the self-similarity and long-range dependence (LRD) in language, employing a large language model (LLM) to analyze the fractal structure of texts. It quantifies these properties using metrics like the Hurst parameter and the fractal dimension, demonstrating their correlation with the performance of LLMs. The study shows that the Hurst parameter for language is approximately 0.75, indicating a balance between determinism and randomness, and suggests that longer contexts in LLMs might enhance their performance in domains with high Hurst parameters. The paper also explores the connection between fractal patterns and scaling laws, showing that these patterns influence the performance of LLMs in different contexts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to quantifying the self-similarity and long-range dependence (LRD) in language, providing a precise formalism for understanding these properties.
- The empirical findings are supported by a robust methodology, including the use of large language models (LLMs) and a variety of datasets, which are well-chosen and appropriate for the study.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and accessible, making complex concepts such as fractal patterns and scaling laws understandable for a broad audience.
- The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the topic and provide a comprehensive overview of related works, which is beneficial for readers seeking a deeper understanding of the field.
- The paper provides valuable insights into the relationship between the fractal structure of language and the performance of LLMs, which could be particularly useful for future research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples of the methodologies used, particularly in the section on the Joseph effect, to aid in understanding the complex concepts presented.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the study, which could help readers understand the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper's claims about the "intelligent behavior" of LLMs are not well-supported and could be seen as overstated, as the paper does not convincingly demonstrate how the fractal structure of language directly contributes to the intelligence of LLMs.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the impact of different language models on the results, as the current study primarily uses one model.
- The paper does not sufficiently address how the results might vary across different types of texts or languages, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the methodology for estimating the Joseph effect was applied in your study?
- How does the performance of LLMs vary when trained with different context lengths? Are there specific scenarios where longer contexts significantly improve performance?
- Could you elaborate on the relationship between the fractal structure of language and the performance of LLMs, particularly in terms of how these structures influence the ability of LLMs to understand and generate text?
- How do the findings of this study relate to other scaling laws in language, such as those described by Heap's law and Zipf's law?
- Could you discuss the potential implications of your findings for the development of more effective LLMs, particularly in terms of how the fractal structure of language might be leveraged to improve model performance?
