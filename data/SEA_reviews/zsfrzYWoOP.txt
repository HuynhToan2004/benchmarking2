 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FABRIC, a training-free method for incorporating iterative feedback into text-to-image models, leveraging self-attention layers in diffusion models to condition on feedback images. This approach allows for the generation of images that align with user preferences, demonstrated through a series of experiments that showcase the method's ability to enhance image quality and adhere to feedback. The paper also explores the trade-off between quality and diversity in image generation and provides a method for evaluating generative models based on human feedback. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty and insufficient experimental validation, particularly in terms of its effectiveness in diverse scenarios and its comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible.
- The proposed method, FABRIC, is simple, intuitive, and effective, as demonstrated by its ability to improve image quality and adhere to feedback.
- The method is versatile and can be applied to any diffusion model, enhancing its applicability and utility.
- The paper provides a novel approach to incorporating iterative feedback into diffusion models, which is a significant advancement in the field of generative models.
- The methodology includes a robust evaluation methodology that allows for the quantification of the performance of generative visual models based on human feedback, providing a rigorous assessment of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and logical flow, making it easy for readers to understand the proposed methods and their implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and generalizability across different types of images and feedback scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing methods, such as those using null-text inversion or other iterative feedback techniques, which could have provided a more comprehensive understanding of its effectiveness and novelty.
- The paper's reliance on binary feedback from users might limit the depth of feedback and understanding of user preferences, which could be addressed by incorporating more nuanced feedback mechanisms.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently robust, with some results showing only marginal improvements or inconsistencies across different metrics and scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity introduced by the method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the ethical implications of the method, particularly in terms of its potential misuse or abuse, which could be addressed by including a section on ethical considerations.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other iterative feedback techniques, such as those using null-text inversion or other image editing methods?
- Can the method be extended to incorporate more nuanced feedback from users, such as continuous ratings or detailed textual feedback?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of images, such as those with complex or ambiguous content?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the feedback is conflicting or ambiguous, and what are the implications of such scenarios for the quality and diversity of the generated images?
- Could the method be adapted to incorporate feedback from multiple users, and how might this affect the quality and diversity of the generated images?
- What are the computational costs associated with the proposed method, and how do these costs compare with those of other methods in terms of efficiency and practicality?
- How does the method perform in terms of image fidelity and diversity, and what are the trade-offs between these two metrics?
