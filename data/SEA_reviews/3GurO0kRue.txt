 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called SHE for addressing subpopulation imbalances in machine learning models, focusing on the discovery and balancing of hidden subpopulations in training data. The approach involves maximizing the predictive information from inputs to labels through optimal data partitioning, which is achieved by applying the LogSumExp operation to outputs. Theoretical analysis and empirical evidence are provided to demonstrate the effectiveness of SHE, showcasing its ability to improve the robustness of subpopulation imbalance. The method is evaluated across various benchmark datasets, demonstrating its superiority over existing methods in handling implicit subpopulation imbalances.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a novel and under-explored problem of subpopulation imbalance in machine learning, which is a significant challenge in real-world applications.
- The proposed SHE method is theoretically sound and empirically validated, showing its effectiveness in identifying hidden subpopulations and encouraging subpopulation-balanced predictions.
- The paper is well-structured, clearly presenting the problem and the proposed method, with a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the superiority of SHE over existing methods.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis of SHE, showing its promise under implicit subpopulation imbalance, and conduct extensive experiments to verify its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the term "implicit subpopulation imbalance," which could lead to confusion about the nature of the problem being addressed.
- The relationship between the proposed method and existing methods such as clustering and subgroup discovery is not adequately clarified, which could lead to questions about the novelty and distinctiveness of the proposed approach.
- The experimental setup and results, particularly the use of a balanced test set, could be improved to better reflect the real-world scenarios where subpopulation imbalance is prevalent.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on how the proposed method could be applied in practical scenarios, such as healthcare or criminal justice, which could limit the understanding of its real-world applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of fairness and bias.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a clearer definition and explanation of the term "implicit subpopulation imbalance" and how it differs from other types of imbalance?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational complexity and scalability, especially when applied to large-scale datasets?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of fairness and bias, and propose strategies to mitigate these risks?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to datasets with different levels of imbalance, and what are the implications for the performance and generalization of the model?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the performance of SHE on datasets with different levels of imbalance and the impact of varying hyperparameters on the model's performance?
