 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SciBench, a benchmark designed to evaluate the reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) in scientific problem-solving. SciBench consists of two datasets: an open set of college-level problems from mathematics, chemistry, and physics textbooks, and a closed set of problems from undergraduate-level exams in computer science and mathematics. The benchmark is unique in its focus on open-ended, free-response questions, which are more challenging than typical multiple-choice questions. The authors conducted experiments using five representative LLMs with various prompting strategies, revealing that current models fall short of satisfactory performance. They also conducted a user study to categorize errors made by LLMs into ten problem-solving abilities, providing insights into the limitations of current models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new benchmark, SciBench, which focuses on open-ended, free-response questions, offering a more comprehensive evaluation of LLMs' scientific problem-solving capabilities.
- The dataset is carefully curated from college-level textbooks and exams, ensuring its relevance and complexity.
- The paper provides detailed solutions to each question, enhancing the understanding of the evaluation process.
- The authors conduct experiments using five representative LLMs, analyzing their performance and providing insights into the limitations of current models.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and detailed examples, making it accessible and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed benchmark, particularly the potential for data leakage due to the use of widely-used textbooks and exams.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the benchmark's evaluation process, with concerns about the fairness of zero-shot evaluations and the potential for data leakage from few-shot examples.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for data leakage from the datasets used, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper's claim about the benchmark's ability to mitigate data leakage is not convincingly supported, as the datasets are not sufficiently described in terms of their uniqueness and accessibility.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the current evaluation methods and a clearer explanation of the benchmark's design choices.

**Questions:**
- How does the benchmark ensure that the questions are not easily accessible online, and what steps have been taken to minimize the risk of data leakage?
- What specific steps have been taken to ensure the fairness of the zero-shot evaluations, especially considering the potential for data leakage from few-shot examples?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the few-shot examples were selected to avoid potential data leakage?
- How does the benchmark's evaluation process compare to other existing benchmarks in terms of data leakage risk and evaluation methodology?
- Could the authors clarify the benchmark's design choices, particularly the decision to focus on open-ended, free-response questions, and how this aligns with the broader goals of the evaluation?
