 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel federated bandit optimization algorithm, Fed-GO-UCB, designed to handle generic non-linear objective functions. It is the first to achieve sublinear communication cost and cumulative regret, which is a significant advancement in federated learning. The algorithm is composed of two phases: a uniform exploration phase and an optimistic exploration phase, each with distinct objectives and communication strategies. The theoretical analysis and empirical results are provided to support the claims of improved performance and communication efficiency. However, concerns about the novelty of the algorithm and the clarity of its presentation have been raised, suggesting that it might not be a significant departure from existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is the first to study federated bandit optimization with generic non-linear objective functions, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- It presents a novel distributed regression oracle and individual confidence set construction, which are original contributions to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the theoretical results are supported by rigorous proofs.
- The algorithm is shown to have sublinear communication cost and cumulative regret, which is a notable improvement over previous methods.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm in real-world scenarios, showing significant improvements over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the communication cost and cumulative regret, which are crucial for evaluating the algorithm's performance.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the algorithm, particularly in the explanation of the communication protocols and the role of the distributed regression oracle.
- The novelty of the algorithm is questionable, as it appears to be a straightforward extension of existing methods, such as the GO-UCB algorithm, without significant innovation.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the algorithm, such as the potential for increased communication overhead due to the need for model updates at each client.
- The empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic datasets, which may not fully represent real-world scenarios and could benefit from testing on more diverse, real-world datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the communication cost and cumulative regret in comparison to other methods, particularly under different conditions such as varying numbers of clients and function dimensions?
- How does the distributed regression oracle affect the communication cost and the overall performance of the algorithm?
- What are the specific advantages of the Fed-GO-UCB algorithm over existing methods, particularly in terms of communication efficiency and computational complexity?
- How does the algorithm perform in scenarios with a large number of clients or in settings where the function is more complex?
- Could you provide more details on the implementation of the algorithm, particularly in terms of the communication protocols and the role of the distributed regression oracle?
- How does the algorithm handle the potential for increased communication overhead due to the need for model updates at each client?
