 **Summary:**
The paper explores the phenomenon of robust overfitting in adversarial training (AT) by focusing on the role of cross-class features in model training. It proposes a novel metric, CAS, to quantify the usage of cross-class features and presents theoretical insights on how these features are utilized during AT. The authors demonstrate that models tend to rely more on class-specific features as training progresses, which can lead to robust overfitting. To mitigate this issue, they propose a weight-average guided knowledge distillation method, WAKE, which preserves cross-class features and enhances robustness. Empirical evidence supports these findings, showing that WAKE outperforms vanilla AT and other methods in terms of adversarial robustness and clean accuracy.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel perspective on robust overfitting in adversarial training, focusing on the role of cross-class features which are less explored in previous literature.
- It provides a comprehensive empirical analysis, including visualizations of saliency maps and the correlation matrix, which effectively illustrates the differences between the best and last checkpoints.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the proposed method, WAKE, is straightforward and simple to implement.
- Theoretical insights are provided to support the findings, which are crucial for understanding the robust overfitting phenomenon.
- The paper introduces a new metric, CAS, to measure the usage of cross-class features, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The proposed method, WAKE, shows superior performance compared to vanilla AT and other methods, indicating its effectiveness in mitigating robust overfitting.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on adversarial training with a large perturbation bound, which might not generalize well to other settings such as small perturbation bounds or different types of adversarial attacks.
- The methodology of using a weight-averaged model as the teacher model for knowledge distillation is not entirely novel, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with only marginal improvements reported.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not address how the findings could be applied to other types of adversarial training or datasets.
- The theoretical analysis is somewhat simplistic and does not fully capture the complexity of the robust overfitting phenomenon, which might limit the practical applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and a more comprehensive evaluation against a broader range of baselines.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the term "cross-class features" and provide a more detailed explanation of how these features are defined and used in the context of adversarial training?
- How does the proposed method, WAKE, perform against other state-of-the-art methods, especially in terms of adversarial robustness and clean accuracy?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of adversarial attacks, such as untargeted attacks or attacks with different perturbation bounds?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical insights provided and discuss how these insights might be applied to other types of adversarial training or datasets?
- How does the proposed method compare with other knowledge distillation methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
