 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to accelerate sampling in diffusion models by utilizing Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) as the reverse transition operator in the DDIM framework. The proposed method aims to match the first and second order central moments of the DDPM forward marginals by constraining the parameters of the GMM. The methodology is tested across various datasets such as CelebAHQ, FFHQ, and ImageNet, demonstrating improvements in FID and IS metrics. The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical results, showcasing the method's potential in reducing the number of sampling steps required to generate high-quality images. However, concerns about the novelty of the method, the clarity of the presentation, and the fairness of the experimental comparisons were raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) as the reverse transition operator in the DDIM framework, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the theoretical foundations and practical implications of the proposed GMM-based approach.
- The paper is well-structured and provides a comprehensive overview of the method, including detailed experimental results and a thorough literature review.
- The methodology is supported by empirical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in generating high-quality images with fewer sampling steps.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's limitations and suggests potential avenues for future research, which shows a commitment to continuous improvement and innovation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and training costs associated with the proposed GMM-based approach, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it appears to be a straightforward extension of existing methods, such as those by Watson et al., 2021, without sufficient differentiation or justification.
- The paper suffers from issues with clarity and presentation, particularly in the introduction and methodology sections, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.
- There is a lack of comparative analysis with other state-of-the-art methods, such as those mentioned in the literature, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The experimental results, while promising, are not sufficiently compelling to justify the claims made about the method's superiority over existing methods, especially in terms of the number of sampling steps required.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the potential negative societal impacts of its use.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the computational complexity and training costs associated with the proposed GMM-based approach, and discuss how these compare to other methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent works in terms of performance and novelty, particularly those mentioned in the literature?
- What are the specific advantages of the GMM-based approach over other methods, and how do these translate into practical benefits for users?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts, and suggest ways to mitigate these issues?
- In light of the concerns about the novelty and clarity of the paper, could the authors revise the manuscript to address these issues and provide a clearer, more compelling argument for the effectiveness of their method?
