 **Summary:**
The paper explores the impact of retrieval augmentation on long-form question answering (LFQA) by analyzing how different retrieval document sets affect the generation of answers using various language models (LMs). The study includes both manual and automatic evaluations, focusing on the attribution of generated answers to in-context evidence documents. The authors collect human annotations to evaluate methods for automatic attribution and provide insights into how retrieval augmentation influences the generation of long-form answers. The research also highlights the challenges in automatically identifying unsupported sentences and proposes potential directions for future work.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and under-explored topic in the field of retrieval-augmented language models (LMs) for long-form question answering (LFQA), providing a novel analysis on the impact of retrieval augmentation on LMs.
- The study includes a variety of retrieval document sets, which adds depth to the analysis, and the paper is well-organized, making the content accessible and understandable.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments and collected human annotations, which are valuable for understanding the impact of retrieval augmentation on LMs.
- The paper provides insights into how retrieval augmentation influences the generation of long-form answers, which is crucial for improving the performance of LMs in LFQA.
- The authors have conducted an in-depth analysis of attribution with newly annotated data, which can serve as a benchmark for evaluating attribution.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and distinction between retrieval-augmented LMs and other types of LMs, which could lead to confusion about the specific focus of the research.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing works, particularly those that have already addressed similar issues in retrieval-augmented LMs.
- The study primarily focuses on a single dataset, which limits the generalizability of the findings. Including additional datasets could provide a more robust understanding of the phenomenon.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the impact of retrieval augmentation on the performance of LMs, which could be a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the findings, which is a critical consideration for any research in this area.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and distinction between retrieval-augmented LMs and other types of LMs to avoid confusion?
- How does the paper differentiate its contributions from existing works, particularly those that have addressed similar issues in retrieval-augmented LMs?
- Can the authors provide more details on how they ensure the quality and reliability of the human annotations used in the study?
- How do the findings of this study apply to other types of QA tasks, such as factoid QA?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the findings and propose strategies to mitigate these risks?
