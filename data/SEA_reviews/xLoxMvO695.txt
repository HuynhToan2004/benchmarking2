 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for formal theorem proving using large language models (LLMs), specifically focusing on the subgoal-based demonstration learning method. This methodology involves breaking down complex tasks into smaller, more manageable subgoals, which are then organized and optimized using diffusion models to enhance the performance of LLMs in formal theorem proving. The framework is evaluated on the miniF2F dataset, demonstrating significant improvements in pass rates and sampling efficiency compared to existing methods. Despite these advancements, concerns were raised about the generalizability of the results to more complex or varied theorem proving scenarios, and the potential limitations of the approach in handling more complex or varied theorem proving problems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to formal theorem proving using LLMs, specifically focusing on the subgoal-based demonstration learning method, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the subgoal refinement process and demonstration reorganization using diffusion models, supported by detailed ablation studies and experimental results.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear structure that effectively communicates the complex ideas involved.
- The results demonstrate significant improvements in pass rates and sampling efficiency compared to existing methods, highlighting the practical applicability of the proposed approach.
- The integration of subgoal-based learning methodologies with diffusion models is a novel approach that effectively addresses the challenges of demonstration formatting and organization in formal theorem proving.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (miniF2F), which may not adequately represent the generalizability of the results to other datasets or more complex theorem proving scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the scalability of the approach, especially concerning the complexity and size of theorems it can handle, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The methodology's reliance on human-written subgoal-based proofs as a starting point may introduce bias or inaccuracies that could affect the overall performance of the system.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of using diffusion models for demonstration reorganization, such as the need for extensive training data and the potential for overfitting.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed approach, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of the technology.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed approach perform on other datasets or more complex theorem proving scenarios? Are there plans to evaluate the method on a broader set of problems?
- Can you provide more details on the scalability of the approach, particularly concerning the complexity and size of theorems it can handle?
- How does the system handle situations where the human-written subgoal-based proofs are inaccurate or biased?
- Could you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed approach and how these might be mitigated?
- How does the system handle situations where the subgoal-based proofs are not reachable from the initial state or cannot reach the passing state of the proof?
- What is the rationale behind using diffusion models for demonstration reorganization, and how does this approach compare to other methods?
- How does the system ensure that the subgoals are reachable from the initial state and can reach the passing state of the proof?
- What are the specific advantages of using diffusion models for demonstration reorganization, and how do these advantages translate into practical improvements in performance?
