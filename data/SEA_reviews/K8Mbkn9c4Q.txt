 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TablEye, a method that transforms tabular data into image-like formats, enabling the application of few-shot learning techniques from the image domain to tabular data. This approach involves a two-stage process: first, tabular data is transformed into images using a novel distance-based method, and then, these images are used as inputs for few-shot learning algorithms. The methodology is evaluated on several datasets, showing promising results compared to existing methods like TabLLM and STUNT. However, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty, unclear presentation, and insufficient experimental validation, particularly in terms of scalability and the generalization of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an interesting and potentially impactful problem of few-shot learning in tabular data, which is an under-explored area.
- The method of transforming tabular data into images for few-shot learning is novel and could be a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method, TablEye, demonstrates a good performance in few-shot tabular learning, outperforming some existing methods like STUNT and TabLLM.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The use of a distance-based method to transform tabular data into images is an innovative approach that could be beneficial for future research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, particularly in the methodology section, which makes it difficult to understand the details of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as it appears to be a straightforward application of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The experimental setup is limited, with a lack of diverse datasets and a small number of test samples, which raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of the number of features and the size of the datasets used.
- The comparison with other methods like TabLLM and STUNT is not fair due to differences in model sizes and the use of different datasets, which could skew the results.
- The paper suffers from several grammatical errors and typos, which need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the details of the proposed method, particularly in the methodology section, to make it more understandable?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issue, especially in datasets with a large number of features?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the diversity of datasets used and the number of test samples?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors address the concerns about the fairness of the comparison with other methods, particularly in terms of model sizes and dataset diversity?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the tabular data is not well-defined or has missing values?
