 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to adversarial robustness by combining transduction and rejection, which allows the model to selectively classify or reject test inputs based on their perturbations. Theoretical analysis and empirical evaluations demonstrate that this method can achieve robustness guarantees under certain conditions, although it requires a significant computational overhead. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on datasets like MNIST and CIFAR-10, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper's theoretical contributions are seen as incremental, and the practical implications of the high computational cost are not thoroughly discussed.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to adversarial robustness by combining transduction and rejection, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed method, which is a strong point of the paper.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The paper introduces a new adaptive attack specifically designed for the transduction+rejection setting, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implications of the high computational cost associated with the proposed method, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The theoretical analysis seems to be an incremental extension of existing works, and the novelty of the theoretical contributions is not clearly articulated.
- The paper's reliance on strong assumptions, such as the bounded perturbation assumption, might limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, particularly in terms of the robustness accuracy and the practical applicability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the high computational cost associated with the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness with existing methods, especially when the attacker is also using the same method?
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of the theoretical contributions and how they differ from existing works?
- In the experimental setup, why were specific hyperparameters chosen, and how do these choices affect the robustness of the model?
- How does the proposed method perform against different types of adversarial attacks, particularly those that are not specifically designed for the transduction+rejection setting?
