 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new multilingual code retrieval benchmark, M2CRB, which includes pairs of text and code in various natural languages such as Spanish, Portuguese, German, and French, along with code snippets in languages like Python, Java, and JavaScript. The dataset is created through an automated filtering process and verified by human annotators. The paper explores the use of this dataset to train models capable of mapping from natural languages to code, demonstrating the effectiveness of models trained on this dataset in handling unseen language pairs. The research also investigates the impact of different design choices on the observed generalization capabilities, showcasing the utility of the dataset in advancing code retrieval capabilities.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new dataset, M2CRB, which is a significant contribution to the field, providing a valuable resource for future research on multilingual code retrieval.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and the results are clearly presented with tables and figures.
- The research addresses a relevant and interesting problem by exploring the ability of models to generalize to unseen language pairs, which is crucial for multilingual code retrieval.
- The paper conducts extensive experiments to evaluate the performance of various models on the new dataset, providing insights into the effectiveness of different design choices.
- The dataset and the methodology used to create it are novel and contribute to the field by addressing the scarcity of paired data in languages other than English.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address the novelty of the approach, as similar methods have been previously explored in the field.
- The dataset size is limited, which could impact the robustness and generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the limitations of the dataset and the potential biases that could affect the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its results with existing datasets like CodeSearchNet, which could have provided a more robust validation of the findings.
- The methodology used to create the dataset, particularly the use of automated filtering and human verification, is not thoroughly explained, which could lead to questions about the reliability and validity of the dataset.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed approach and does not adequately address how the findings could be applied in practical scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of their approach in comparison to existing methods in the field?
- How does the size of the dataset impact the results, and what steps are being taken to ensure the dataset is representative and unbiased?
- Could the authors provide more details on the methodology used to create the dataset, particularly the automated filtering and human verification processes?
- How does the proposed approach perform when compared to existing datasets like CodeSearchNet, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the generalizability of the findings?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their approach and how these might affect the practical applicability of the findings?
- How does the paper address the issue of potential biases in the dataset, and what steps are being taken to mitigate these biases?
