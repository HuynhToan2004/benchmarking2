 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LLM-GNN, a novel pipeline for label-free node classification on graphs, leveraging Large Language Models (LLMs) to annotate nodes and subsequently training a Graph Neural Network (GNN) on these annotations. The authors address challenges in active node selection and annotation quality, employing a heuristic for active node selection and a confidence-aware post-filtering mechanism. The methodology is evaluated across multiple datasets, demonstrating the potential of LLM-GNN to achieve high accuracy with minimal cost. The paper also explores various prompts and filtering strategies to optimize the annotation process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel label-free pipeline, LLM-GNN, which effectively integrates Large Language Models (LLMs) with Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to address the challenges of annotation quality and diversity in graph node classification.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear motivation and detailed experimental results that support the claims made.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experimental results that validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The integration of LLMs and GNNs is a promising approach for label-free node classification on graphs, which is an emerging and relevant topic in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for a comprehensive understanding of its applicability and ethical implications.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as similar approaches using LLMs for graph node classification have been previously explored, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and computational complexity of the proposed method, which could limit its applicability to larger, more complex graphs.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance metrics and computational efficiency, which could undermine the claims of its superiority.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the proposed method to other types of graphs beyond text-attributed graphs, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and calculation of the C-Density heuristic and its role in active node selection?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of performance and computational efficiency with existing methods, particularly in large-scale graph node classification tasks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the LLM-GNN method, especially when applied to larger, more complex graphs?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting when using LLM-generated labels, and what measures are in place to mitigate this risk?
- Could the authors expand on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and discuss how these could be mitigated or addressed?
