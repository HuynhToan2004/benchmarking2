 **Summary:**
The paper introduces VoiceGen, a novel text-to-speech (TTS) system that leverages a diffusion-based variation network to generate diverse voice styles from text prompts. The system includes a speech language understanding (SLU) module and a large language model (LLM) to create text prompts based on speech attributes, such as gender and speed. The paper also introduces a dataset of 20,000 text prompts and a pipeline for automatic prompt generation, aiming to address the one-to-many problem in TTS. The methodology involves a reference speech encoder that predicts voice style from text prompts, and a variation network that models voice variability not captured by text prompts. The paper presents results showing that VoiceGen outperforms existing methods in terms of voice style control and text prompt quality. However, it also faces criticisms regarding the clarity of the methodology, the lack of comprehensive comparisons, and the potential over-reliance on specific text prompts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, VoiceGen, is innovative and addresses a significant gap in the text-to-speech (TTS) field by utilizing a diffusion-based variation network to model voice variability not captured by text prompts.
- The automatic prompt generation pipeline is a valuable contribution, reducing the need for human intervention and improving the scalability of the TTS system.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, demonstrating its effectiveness through rigorous testing and comparison to existing methods.
- The paper is well-motivated, addressing the challenges of one-to-many problems and data-scale challenges in TTS systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the methodology and the descriptions of the reference speech encoder and variation network.
- The experiments are limited to a small dataset (44K hours) and do not include a comprehensive comparison with other methods or baseline models, which could undermine the credibility of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the automatic prompt generation pipeline, especially in terms of handling a large number of text prompts.
- The paper does not discuss the potential for over-reliance on specific text prompts, which could limit the diversity and naturalness of the generated voices.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the performance of the reference speech encoder and variation network across different text prompts, which could affect the generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the methodology, particularly the reference speech encoder and variation network, to enhance understanding and reproducibility?
- How does the variation network ensure that the generated voices are not overly similar to the reference speech, especially when the reference speech is not available during inference?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the automatic prompt generation pipeline, especially in terms of handling a large number of text prompts?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for over-reliance on specific text prompts, and what measures are in place to ensure diversity and naturalness in the generated voices?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their method, particularly in terms of privacy and security concerns?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of handling different text prompts, and what are the implications of this for the generalizability of the results?
