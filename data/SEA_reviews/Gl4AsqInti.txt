 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the Hessian structure in the context of deep learning, focusing on the Nonlinear Modeling Error (NME) and its role in the performance of second-order methods like SAM. It highlights that the NME, often overlooked due to its indefiniteness and small eigenvalues, plays a crucial role in feature exploration and the success of second-order methods. The authors demonstrate that the NME is sensitive to the choice of activation functions and that neglecting it can lead to suboptimal performance. They also show that weight noise, which is often considered equivalent to gradient penalties, is not as effective as directly penalizing the NME. The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, including experiments on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, to support its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear and concise introduction that effectively sets the stage for the rest of the paper.
- The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support their claims, demonstrating the importance of the Nonlinear Modeling Error (NME) in the Hessian and its implications for the performance of second-order methods.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of deep learning, focusing on the role of the NME in the Hessian and its impact on the success of second-order methods.
- The authors conduct extensive experiments, including on large-scale datasets like ImageNet, which are crucial for validating their claims.
- The paper provides a novel perspective on the role of the Hessian in deep learning, particularly the NME, which is often overlooked in the literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with marginal improvements over SGD and SAM, raising questions about the practical significance of the findings.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other second-order methods and baselines, which could have provided a clearer picture of the advantages and limitations of the proposed methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the findings for the design of activation functions and the potential for new optimization algorithms.
- There are some minor issues with the presentation and clarity of certain sections, such as the discussion on the GN and NME parts of the Hessian and the definition of the NME matrix.
- The paper could be improved by addressing some typographical errors and ensuring that all references are correctly formatted.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical significance of the marginal improvements observed in the experimental results?
- How does the choice of activation function affect the performance of second-order methods, and could the authors provide more insights into this?
- What are the implications of the findings for the design of new optimization algorithms that take into account the NME and the role of activation functions?
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of the NME matrix in the context of the Hessian, and discuss its implications for the design of activation functions?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of the NME in different network architectures and training scenarios, and what are the implications of these limitations?
