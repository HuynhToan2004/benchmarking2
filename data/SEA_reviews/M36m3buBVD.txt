 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Long-term Spatial Prompt Tuning (LSPT) framework for visual representation learning, which integrates long-term gated prompts to mitigate forgetting issues in visual prompt tuning. This approach, inspired by human brain mechanisms, leverages both temporal and spatial coding to enhance the retention of parameters from earlier blocks and perpetually accumulate class-specific features. The methodology is evaluated on various benchmarks, demonstrating significant improvements over existing baselines. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of presentation, and the fairness of comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed method.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, showing significant improvements in various benchmarks, particularly on FGVC and VTAB-1K datasets.
- The integration of long-term gated prompts and the use of LSTM to mitigate forgetting issues are innovative and contribute to the field of visual representation learning.
- The paper provides a clear motivation for the method and includes comprehensive ablation studies to verify the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method's performance is impressive, demonstrating significant improvements over existing baselines.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it builds upon existing works like VPT and GaPT, with only minor modifications.
- The paper lacks detailed theoretical analysis and explanation of why the proposed method works effectively, particularly in terms of its brain-inspired design.
- The method's reliance on the availability of large-scale datasets like ImageNet-1K for pretraining might limit its applicability in scenarios where such data is not readily available.
- The comparison with state-of-the-art methods like PET and other recent prompt tuning methods is missing, which could undermine the claimed superiority of the proposed method.
- The paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in the introduction and the explanation of certain methodological details.
- The method's scalability and its impact on performance with different backbone models are not thoroughly explored.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other backbone models like Swin-Transformer or other self-supervised models?
- Can the authors provide more detailed theoretical analysis or experimental evidence to support the claims made about the method's effectiveness and its brain-inspired design?
- How does the method handle the issue of forgetting in the model, particularly in the context of long-term learning?
- Could the authors clarify the method's performance when using different numbers of learnable prompt tokens?
- How does the method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
