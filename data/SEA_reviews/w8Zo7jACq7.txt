 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel model-free algorithm, Pruning-Refinement-Identification (PRI), for identifying the best policy in Constrained Markov Decision Processes (CMDPs) with sublinear regret and constraint violation. The algorithm is designed to output a near-optimal policy with high probability, achieving a significant improvement in regret and constraint violation bounds. It leverages a property called "limited stochasticity" to ensure that the optimal policy involves only a few stochastic decisions. The PRI algorithm is evaluated in both synthetic and grid-world environments, demonstrating its effectiveness. The paper also discusses the theoretical aspects of the algorithm and its implications for the field of RL, including its potential to converge to an optimal policy.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel model-free algorithm for online CMDPs that achieves optimal regret and minimal constraint violation, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The algorithm is supported by theoretical proofs and empirical evidence, demonstrating its effectiveness and practical applicability.
- The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work, highlighting the novelty and relevance of their approach.
- The paper introduces a new algorithm that converges to an optimal policy, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the algorithm, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions may not hold.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing algorithms, which could help in better understanding the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the unique solution to the linear programming problem, may limit the general applicability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the algorithm and the scalability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of constraint violation, which is a critical aspect of CMDPs.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by including more detailed explanations of the algorithms and proofs, as well as more intuitive explanations of the theoretical results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper and discuss the implications of these assumptions on the applicability of the results?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare with other existing algorithms in terms of computational complexity and scalability?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the proofs and algorithms, particularly the "limited stochasticity" property and the proof of Lemma 1?
- How does the algorithm handle scenarios where the assumptions do not hold, and what are the potential consequences of these violations?
- Can the authors discuss the implications of the "limited stochasticity" property for the design of other RL algorithms?
- How does the proposed algorithm handle scenarios with multiple optimal solutions, and what are the implications of this for the practical application of the algorithm?
