 **Summary:**
The paper presents a novel family of algorithms, LinIMED, designed to address the contextual bandit problem in the stochastic linear bandit setting. This family includes three variants, each with a different approach to estimating the square gap, which is used to determine the index of the arm to be pulled. The algorithms are theoretically analyzed, demonstrating a regret bound of O(dT) in the varying arm setting, which is shown to outperform existing algorithms like LinUCB and LinTS in both synthetic and real-world datasets. The paper is well-structured, clearly presenting the theoretical analysis and empirical results, although it lacks a detailed discussion on the computational efficiency and the necessity of the square gap estimation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new family of algorithms, LinIMED, which is a natural extension of the IMED algorithm, specifically designed for the contextual bandit problem in the stochastic linear bandit setting.
- The theoretical analysis of the LinIMED algorithm is comprehensive, including a detailed proof sketch of Theorem 1 and a discussion on the regret bound.
- The empirical results demonstrate the superior performance of LinIMED compared to existing algorithms such as LinUCB and LinTS, both in synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the algorithms and their theoretical underpinnings, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- The use of a simple yet effective indexing method in LinIMED allows for efficient computation of the index, which is crucial for real-time decision-making in bandit problems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational efficiency of LinIMED, particularly in terms of the computational cost and scalability with the number of arms and context dimensions.
- The necessity and impact of the square gap estimation in LinIMED are not clearly justified, raising questions about the algorithm's design and the potential for alternative approaches.
- The proofs and theoretical analyses, while correct, are seen as somewhat standard and not sufficiently novel to meet the high standards of the conference.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the discussion on the assumptions and the proofs, which could benefit from more detailed explanations or examples.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the LinIMED algorithm, which could include scenarios where it might not perform optimally or where it may have practical limitations.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the computational efficiency of LinIMED, particularly in terms of the computational cost and scalability with the number of arms and context dimensions?
- How does the LinIMED algorithm compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability to other state-of-the-art algorithms, such as those based on Thompson Sampling or Bayesian optimization?
- In the proof of Theorem 1, could you clarify the assumptions and the proof steps, particularly regarding the use of the "peeling device" and the dependence on the context dimension d?
- How does the LinIMED algorithm handle scenarios where the contextual bandit problem is more complex, such as when the contexts are correlated or when there are multiple contexts per arm?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the square gap estimation in LinIMED and discuss the potential implications of using other divergence measures, such as KL-divergence?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly in the synthetic dataset used in Section 6.1, and discuss the implications of these choices on the results?
