 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HiGen, a novel hierarchical graph generative network designed to capture the hierarchical structure of graphs. It generates graph sub-structures in a coarse-to-fine fashion, focusing on community structures and cross-edges between them. The model is evaluated on various benchmark datasets and demonstrates state-of-the-art performance in terms of graph quality. It employs a recursive factorization of the distribution of edges in the hierarchical graph using a multinomial distribution, enabling the autoregressive generation of each community. The paper also addresses scalability issues by generating communities in parallel and predicting cross-edges using separate neural networks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel graph generative network that captures the hierarchical nature of graphs, enabling scalable graph generation for large and complex graphs.
- The model effectively generates community graphs with integer-valued edge weights in an autoregressive manner, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive theoretical analysis and experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of hierarchical graph generative networks to model the hierarchical structure of graphs is a novel approach that has not been extensively explored in previous research.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the model architecture and its implementation, which is crucial for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance, scalability, and efficiency.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with some results showing only marginal improvements over existing methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of scalability, particularly in terms of the computational complexity of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as it primarily builds upon existing models like GRAN, and the methodology does not significantly deviate from previous approaches.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the potential for overfitting due to the hierarchical structure, the need for a large amount of training data, and the potential for the method to only work with hierarchical graphs.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance, scalability, and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup, including the dataset characteristics, model architecture, and training details?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of scalability, particularly in handling very large graphs?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential solutions or future directions for research?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of scalability, particularly in terms of computational complexity and memory requirements?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the choice of the Louvain algorithm for graph partitioning and its impact on the performance of the model?
