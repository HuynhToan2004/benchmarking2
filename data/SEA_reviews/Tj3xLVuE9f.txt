 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of shortcut learning in neural networks, focusing on how model architecture influences the reliance on predictive versus available features. It introduces a generative framework to synthesize datasets with varying feature predictivity and availability, and demonstrates that nonlinear models tend to rely on less predictive but more available features, a phenomenon termed "shortcut bias." The authors show that this bias is more pronounced in models with deeper architectures and nonlinear activations. They also provide theoretical insights using Neural Tangent Kernels to explain the bias in ReLU networks. The paper's empirical findings are supported by experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, although the novelty and clarity of the presentation have been criticized.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the methodology and results.
- The authors introduce a novel approach to systematically manipulate feature predictivity and availability, which is a significant contribution to the understanding of shortcut learning.
- The paper provides both theoretical and empirical evidence to support the findings, which are well-organized and presented.
- The experiments are carefully designed to show that availability can trump predictivity, which is a critical insight for the community.
- The paper is original in its approach, using a generative procedure to create synthetic datasets that can be used to study shortcut learning in a controlled environment.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is somewhat limited as the concept of availability is not new, and the experiments do not convincingly demonstrate a significant difference between models with different architectures.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with some sections being difficult to follow and certain figures and equations being unclear or misleading.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are not fully integrated with the empirical findings, and there is a lack of detailed discussion on how the theoretical insights relate to the experimental results.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the findings to real-world datasets or the practical implications of the shortcut bias in different architectures.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the study, which could affect the credibility and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of "availability" in the context of their study, especially how it relates to the predictivity of features?
- How does the availability of features vary with the depth and width of the network, and what are the specific implications for shortcut learning?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the theoretical insights and how they relate to the empirical findings?
- In the context of the experiments, how do the authors ensure that the results are not confounded by other factors, such as the correlation between features and labels?
- How do the findings of this study compare to existing literature on shortcut learning, and what are the implications for practical applications of deep learning models?
