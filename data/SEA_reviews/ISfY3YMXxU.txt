 **Summary:**
The paper introduces INRet, a novel framework designed for retrieving and comparing implicit neural representations (INRs) of 3D shapes, which are typically represented by implicit functions like signed distance functions, unsigned distance functions, and occupancy fields. INRet employs an MLP encoder and a convolutional encoder to extract embeddings from INRs, which are then compared using cosine similarity. The framework is capable of handling various INR architectures and implicit functions, and it demonstrates superior retrieval accuracy and efficiency compared to existing methods. The paper also proposes a new metric, Category-Chamfer, which evaluates the similarity between INRs based on their category and chamfer distance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a novel and important problem of retrieval and comparison of implicit neural representations (INRs), which is a critical aspect of managing and organizing 3D data.
- The proposed method, INRet, is innovative and well-motivated, with a focus on generating embeddings from INRs that can be compared using cosine similarity, thereby enabling the retrieval of similar shapes.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results demonstrate the superiority of INRet over existing methods, showcasing its effectiveness in retrieving INRs with feature grids, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper introduces a new metric, Category-Chamfer, which is a more challenging and comprehensive metric compared to existing methods.
- The methodology is flexible and can handle different INR architectures and implicit functions, enhancing its applicability and relevance to current research trends.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of INRet, which is crucial for understanding its scope and applicability.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned as the embedding extraction and comparison methods are considered straightforward and not sufficiently innovative.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the generalization ability of INRet to other datasets or more complex 3D shapes, which could limit its practical utility.
- The paper does not compare INRet with the most recent and relevant methods, which could provide a clearer picture of its competitive advantage.
- There are inconsistencies in the experimental results, particularly in the retrieval speed and accuracy of INRet, which are not convincingly explained.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the training and inference times of INRet, as well as the computational efficiency of the embedding extraction process.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the training and inference times of INRet, particularly how these times compare to other methods like inr2vec?
- How does INRet handle the retrieval of shapes that are not well-represented in the training dataset?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the Category-Chamfer metric and its advantages over existing metrics?
- How does INRet handle the retrieval of shapes that have different implicit functions or architectures?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending INRet to handle other types of INRs, such as those based on meshes or other representations?
- Can the authors elaborate on the design choices of the MLP encoder and the Conv3D encoder, particularly how these choices impact the performance and efficiency of INRet?
