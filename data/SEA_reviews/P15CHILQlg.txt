 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to enhance the performance of generative flow networks (GFlowNets) by learning energy decomposition, termed LED-GFN. This method decomposes the energy of terminal states into learnable potentials associated with state transitions, enabling the training of flow functions with the evaluation of intermediate states or transitions. This approach aims to address the challenges of evaluating intermediate energies, which can be costly or misleading, by providing more informative local credits. The paper presents extensive experiments across various domains, including set generation, bag generation, molecular graphs, RNA sequences, and maximum independent sets, demonstrating the effectiveness of LED-GFN compared to existing methods like FL-GFN and GFN.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the proposed method.
- The proposed method, LED-GFN, is novel and effective, with good experimental results across various domains.
- The paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation, including extensive ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The idea of decomposing the energy of a terminal state into learnable potentials associated with state transitions is innovative and addresses the limitations of existing methods like FL-GFN.
- The paper presents a detailed comparison with the FL-GFN method and other baselines, demonstrating the superiority of LED-GFN in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the energy function is not decomposable.
- The theoretical justification for the proposed method is insufficient, especially concerning the optimal policy and the convergence of the energy decomposition.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of the number of potentials needed for effective decomposition.
- The experimental results, while extensive, are not convincingly superior to existing methods in all cases, and the paper does not sufficiently compare the performance of LED-GFN with other state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these limitations affect the performance of LED-GFN?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Can you provide a theoretical justification for the proposed method, particularly concerning the optimal policy and the convergence of the energy decomposition?
- How do you address the issue of scalability in terms of the number of potentials needed for effective decomposition?
- Can you elaborate on the computational efficiency of the proposed method and how it compares to other methods in terms of training time and inference speed?
