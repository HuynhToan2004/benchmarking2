 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AdaMergeX, a novel method for cross-lingual transfer using parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) techniques like LoRA and IA3. AdaMergeX decomposes the ability of a model in a task and language into "task ability" and "language ability." The method merges adapters to transfer task ability from the source language to the target language, utilizing a reference task to obtain language ability. The paper claims that AdaMergeX outperforms other state-of-the-art methods in cross-lingual transfer and general adapter merging methods. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the method's presentation, the assumptions made about the similarity of distributions across tasks, and the fairness of comparisons with other methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and well-organized content.
- The method is simple, intuitive, and effective, showing strong empirical performance across various tasks and languages.
- The paper provides extensive evaluations and detailed analyses, including ablation studies on source languages, reference tasks, backbone models, and merging methods.
- The proposed method outperforms other state-of-the-art methods in cross-lingual transfer and general adapter merging methods, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper introduces a novel method of merging adapters, which is adaptive to the structure of adapters, and provides a new approach to cross-lingual transfer.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that the divergences between adapters fine-tuned in different languages on a particular task follow the same distribution across diverse tasks, which might not hold true in all scenarios.
- The comparison with other methods like XLT is not fair due to differences in the training data used.
- The paper lacks clarity in the method's presentation, with some sections being confusing and hard to follow.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different adapter types and the scalability of the method.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the computational cost and the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other adapter merging methods, which could have strengthened the argument for the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when using different backbone models like XLM-R, and what are the results compared to other methods like MAD-X and LF-SFT?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost and the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance?
- Is there a way to verify the assumption that the divergences between adapters fine-tuned in different languages on a particular task follow the same distribution across diverse tasks?
- Could the authors clarify the method's presentation, particularly in sections that are currently confusing and hard to follow?
- How does the proposed method compare to other adapter merging methods, and what are the advantages of the proposed method over these?
