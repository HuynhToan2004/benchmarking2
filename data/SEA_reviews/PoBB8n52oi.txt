 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel linear-time alternative to self-attention, named SummaryMixing, aimed at enhancing the efficiency of speech recognition models. This method involves summarizing an utterance by the mean of vectors for all time steps and then combining it with time-specific information. The proposed method is evaluated across various datasets and models, including Branchformer and Conformer, and shows significant improvements in training and inference times, as well as memory reduction. The experiments demonstrate that SummaryMixing can match or surpass the performance of Multi-Head Self-Attention (MHSA) while reducing computational complexity. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with other efficient attention mechanisms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-designed method.
- The proposed method, SummaryMixing, is simple and effective, showing improvements in training and inference time while maintaining or improving performance.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering various datasets and models, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The methodology is well-presented, with clear figures and detailed explanations, which aids in understanding the proposed approach.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the computational complexity and memory usage of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other efficient attention mechanisms, such as FastFormer and HyperMixer, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the method is limited, as it primarily replaces self-attention with a mean operation, which has been previously explored in other contexts.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the impact of the proposed method on the performance of different models, such as Branchformer and Conformer, which could lead to confusion about its general applicability.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis on the choice of parameters and the effect of different settings on the performance of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experiments, including different model sizes and datasets, to better demonstrate the generalizability and effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other models beyond Branchformer and Conformer?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other efficient attention mechanisms, such as FastFormer and HyperMixer, to clarify the advantages of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform on different datasets, particularly those with varying acoustic conditions and language complexity?
- What are the specific settings and parameters used in the proposed method, and how do these affect the performance?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of the proposed method on the training and inference times of other tasks beyond speech recognition?
