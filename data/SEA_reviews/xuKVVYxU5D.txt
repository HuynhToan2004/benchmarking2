 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel model-free algorithm for Distributionally Robust Reinforcement Learning (DRRL) under the Cressie-Read family of f-divergence. This algorithm, named DRQ, is designed to handle a single trajectory of data, which is a departure from traditional model-based DRRL approaches that require extensive data from an MDP simulator. The algorithm is shown to converge asymptotically and demonstrates robustness and sample complexity improvements over existing methods. The paper also provides theoretical guarantees and empirical validation through experiments, showcasing the algorithm's effectiveness in both simple and more complex environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a logical flow that makes it easy to follow.
- The introduction of a model-free DRRL algorithm that can handle a single trajectory of data is a significant advancement in the field, particularly relevant for scenarios where data collection is challenging.
- The algorithm's design is innovative, employing a three-timescale framework that effectively utilizes each incrementally arriving sample, and is supported by asymptotic convergence guarantees.
- The paper provides empirical results that demonstrate the robustness and sample complexity of the proposed algorithm, showing improvements over existing methods.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, with proofs that are well-articulated and supported by empirical evidence.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing model-based DRRL algorithms, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical implementation.
- The experimental setup is limited to simple environments, which may not fully demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness in more complex or realistic scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of its approach, such as the assumption of a finite action space and the potential for over-conservatism in the learned policies.
- There is a lack of discussion on the practical implications of the algorithm's assumptions, such as the finite action space and the choice of divergence measures.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and practical implementation of the algorithm, especially in larger or more complex environments.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed algorithm compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical implementation with existing model-based DRRL algorithms, particularly in scenarios with large state-action spaces?
- Can the authors provide more details on the choice of divergence measures and how this affects the robustness and sample complexity of the algorithm?
- How does the algorithm perform in more complex or realistic environments, such as those involving continuous state and action spaces?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for over-conservatism in the learned policies and how this might affect the practical applicability of the algorithm?
- Is there a possibility to extend the algorithm to handle stochastic transition kernels or other types of model misspecification?
