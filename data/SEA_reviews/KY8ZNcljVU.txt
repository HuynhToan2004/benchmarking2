 **Summary:**
The paper introduces NetInfoF, a framework designed to measure and utilize network usable information (NUI) in graph neural networks (GNNs). NetInfoF comprises two main components: NetInfoF_Probe, which measures NUI without training, and NetInfoF_Act, which uses NUI to solve link prediction and node classification tasks. The framework is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing promising results in link prediction. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and the limited scope of evaluation to link prediction.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow content, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method, NetInfoF, is innovative and provides a new perspective on measuring the usability of network information in GNNs.
- The paper includes both theoretical and empirical results, which support the claims made about the effectiveness and scalability of NetInfoF.
- The method is computationally efficient, with a time complexity that scales linearly with the number of edges, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation, demonstrating the superiority of NetInfoF over existing methods in link prediction tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as it appears to be a combination of existing methods such as SlimG and SVD on the adjacency matrix.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential shortcomings.
- There is a significant overlap in the content between the main paper and the appendix, which could be streamlined to improve readability and focus.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the claims of its superiority.
- The paper primarily focuses on link prediction, neglecting other tasks such as node classification, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's evaluation on node classification tasks is insufficient, with only a few datasets used, which may not be representative of the method's performance across different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of NetInfoF over existing methods like SlimG and SVD on the adjacency matrix?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the graph structure and node features carry contradictory or misleading information?
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss how these could be addressed in future work?
- In the experimental setup, why were only a few node classification datasets used, and what were the reasons for not including more diverse datasets?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with a large number of negative edges, and what are the implications for the accuracy of the results?
