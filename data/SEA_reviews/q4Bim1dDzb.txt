 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for inverse rendering, named UniVoxel, which represents a scene using a voxel-based approach that integrates geometry, materials, and illumination. The method leverages spherical Gaussians to model local incident light radiance, enhancing the representation of illumination. UniVoxel optimizes the training time significantly, reducing it from hours to minutes, while maintaining or improving reconstruction quality. The method is evaluated against existing state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating improvements in efficiency and quality. Despite its innovative approach and practical benefits, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and lack of comparisons with certain related works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the motivation is clear.
- The method is technically sound, with a clear and logical presentation.
- The paper demonstrates a significant reduction in training time compared to existing methods.
- The results show improvements in reconstruction quality and novel view synthesis.
- The proposed method is simple, efficient, and effective, with clear explanations of the methodology and the experimental setup.
- The paper includes ablation studies that demonstrate the importance of different components of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the method is limited, as similar approaches using voxelization for inverse rendering have been explored in previous works.
- There is a lack of comparisons with related works, such as TensoIR and other voxel-based methods, which could have strengthened the paper's claims.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of material-lighting ambiguity, which is a significant challenge in inverse rendering.
- The paper does not discuss the handling of complex illumination scenarios, which could affect the method's performance.
- The method's applicability to more complex or varied scenes, such as those with multiple materials or dynamic lighting conditions, is not explored.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method?
- How does the method handle complex illumination scenarios, and what are the implications for the method's performance in such scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the handling of material-lighting ambiguity in the method?
- How does the method perform with varying illumination conditions, and what are the implications for the method's performance in such scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the method, particularly in terms of memory and computational resource requirements?
- How does the method perform with more complex or varied scenes, such as those with multiple materials or dynamic lighting conditions?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending the method to handle more complex scenes or varying illumination conditions?
