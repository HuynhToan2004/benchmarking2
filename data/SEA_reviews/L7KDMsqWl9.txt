 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the HHD-Ethiopic dataset, a significant contribution to the field of historical handwritten Ethiopic OCR. This dataset, consisting of 79,684 text-line images, is divided into three sets: a training set from the 19th to 20th centuries, and two test sets, one for IID and one for OOD scenarios. The dataset is used to evaluate the performance of various OCR models, including CTC, attention, and transformer-based methods, against human annotations. The results show that some models, such as HPopt-Attn-CTC, can outperform human annotators on specific test sets. The paper also discusses the challenges of OCR in historical Ethiopic scripts, including complex diacritics and the scarcity of labeled data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new dataset, HHD-Ethiopic, which is a valuable resource for researchers in the field of historical handwritten Ethiopic OCR.
- The dataset is well-documented, including a detailed description of the data collection and annotation process, which is crucial for reproducibility and transparency.
- The paper includes a thorough evaluation of various OCR models, including both CTC and transformer-based methods, which provides a comprehensive comparison of their performance.
- The dataset and source code are made available, which facilitates further research and development in the field.
- The paper discusses the challenges of OCR in historical Ethiopic scripts, such as the complexity of diacritics and the scarcity of labeled data, which are important considerations for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the specific challenges posed by the Ethiopic script, such as the complexity of diacritics, which could have been addressed in the introduction or related work sections.
- The methodology for training the models, particularly the choice of hyperparameters and the impact of these choices on performance, is not adequately explained.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed visual examples of the Ethiopic script and its diacritics to aid in understanding the challenges of OCR in this script.
- The performance of the models on the test sets, particularly the OOD set, is not convincingly explained, raising questions about the generalizability of the models.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the dataset, such as its size and the potential for bias in annotation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples of the Ethiopic script and its diacritics, possibly in a visual format, to aid in understanding the challenges of OCR in this script?
- How were the hyperparameters chosen for the models, and what impact did these choices have on performance?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for bias in the annotation process, and how this might affect the performance of the OCR models?
- What steps were taken to ensure the quality and accuracy of the human annotations, and how were these annotations verified?
- How do the authors plan to address the limitations of the dataset, such as its size and potential for bias, in future research?
