 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CrossLoco, a novel framework for translating human motions into robot actions using a guided unsupervised reinforcement learning approach. This method employs cycle-consistency-based rewards to maximize the mutual information between human and robot motions, ensuring that the robot's actions correspond closely to the human's intended movements. The framework has been tested on the Aliengo quadrupedal robot, demonstrating its effectiveness in various scenarios, including walking, running, hopping, and dancing. Despite its innovative approach and promising results, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation scope, lack of comparison with more recent methods, and insufficient discussion on the generalizability and scalability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach using cycle-consistency-based rewards to maximize mutual information between human and robot motions, which is a novel application of this concept in the context of human motion to robot control.
- The methodology is clearly presented, making it easy to understand and reproduce, with a detailed description of the experimental setup and results.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through compelling qualitative results, showing that the robot can accurately track human motions and adapt to different styles of walking.
- The use of a cycle-consistency-based reward term to ensure correspondence between human and robot motions is a significant contribution to the field, potentially enhancing the applicability and generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a good ablation study, highlighting the importance of the proposed method in comparison to simpler baselines.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning its generalizability and scalability.
- The evaluation is limited to a single quadrupedal robot model and does not include a comparison with more recent methods, which could provide a clearer picture of the method's effectiveness and relevance.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a crucial consideration for any technological advancement.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the challenges in translating human motions to robot actions, which could benefit from a more comprehensive analysis.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with more recent and relevant methods, such as those mentioned in the references, which could provide a more robust validation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the generalizability and scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of robots and the complexity of the tasks it can handle?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent methods, especially those that use similar approaches but might not have been directly compared in the paper?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
- Could the authors provide more detailed insights into the challenges in translating human motions to robot actions, and how these challenges were addressed in the development of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle the complexity and variability of human motions, particularly in scenarios where the robot needs to adapt to different styles or variations in human motion?
