 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CO-MOT, a novel method aimed at enhancing the performance of end-to-end multi-object tracking (e2e-MOT) by addressing the imbalance in positive samples during training. This issue is addressed through a cooperative label assignment strategy that allows tracked objects to be reassigned to detection queries, thereby improving feature augmentation and generalization ability. Additionally, a shadow concept is introduced to augment queries with multiple shadow queries, optimizing the most challenging query in the set. The methodology is evaluated across multiple datasets and shows promising results, although it requires further validation on larger, more diverse datasets to fully substantiate its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant issue in e2e-MOT by focusing on the label assignment strategy during training, which is crucial for improving performance in tracking-by-detection methods.
- The proposed CO-MOT method introduces a cooperative label assignment strategy and a shadow concept to address the imbalance in positive samples and enhance generalization ability, which are novel approaches in the field.
- The methodology is supported by extensive ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed techniques.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive set of experiments that validate the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The introduction of a shadow set for augmenting queries is a novel approach that enhances the method's robustness and adaptability, particularly in challenging tracking scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the computational costs associated with the introduction of the shadow set, which could impact the practical deployment of the method.
- The method's effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated across a wide range of datasets, particularly those with large variations in object classes and tracking difficulties.
- There are concerns about the method's scalability, especially in scenarios with a large number of objects, which could lead to increased computational demands and potential inefficiencies.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and inference processes, particularly how the shadow queries are initialized and managed, which could affect the method's reproducibility and adaptability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these could be addressed in future work.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational costs associated with the introduction of the shadow set, including the additional FLOPs and memory requirements?
- How does the method perform on datasets with a large number of objects, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in such scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the training and inference processes, particularly how the shadow queries are initialized and managed?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the shadow queries may not effectively track objects due to initialization errors or other factors?
- What are the specific challenges encountered when adapting the method to different datasets, and how are these addressed?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the training and testing data splits, and the specifics of the Hungarian matching algorithm used?
