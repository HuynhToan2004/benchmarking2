 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the expressive power of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) in counting and listing substructures, focusing on the use of random node features to enhance their capabilities beyond traditional graph isomorphism testing. It introduces a novel architecture, Moment-GNN, which leverages higher-order moments to represent substructures, showing superior performance in various tasks such as cycle detection, graph classification, and molecular property prediction. Theoretical analysis and empirical evidence support the claims of Moment-GNN's ability to count and detect substructures effectively, even in out-of-distribution scenarios. Despite the theoretical contributions, the paper's empirical validation is somewhat limited, and the practical implications of using random node features are not thoroughly explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using random node features to enhance the expressive power of GNNs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis is robust and provides a deeper understanding of the expressive power of GNNs, particularly in the context of counting and listing substructures.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear and convincing proofs that support the theoretical claims.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed Moment-GNN architecture, showing improvements over existing methods in various tasks such as cycle detection and graph classification.
- The paper addresses a gap in the literature by focusing on the expressive power of GNNs in terms of counting substructures, which is a less explored area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implications and limitations of using random node features, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the scalability of the proposed method.
- The empirical validation is somewhat limited, with the experiments being conducted on relatively small datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of its advantages and disadvantages.
- There are some minor issues with the clarity and organization of the paper, particularly in the presentation of the results and the theoretical proofs, which could benefit from better structuring and more detailed explanations.
- The paper does not address the issue of inductive bias, which is crucial for understanding the generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the computational efficiency and scalability of the Moment-GNN architecture, especially in comparison to other GNN architectures?
- How does the use of random node features affect the computational complexity and memory requirements of the model?
- Is there a possibility to extend the Moment-GNN architecture to handle larger or more complex graphs, and what would be the challenges involved?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of using random node features and how this compares to other methods that use node features for enhancing the expressive power of GNNs?
- How does the Moment-GNN architecture handle the issue of inductive bias, and what steps were taken to ensure that the results are generalizable across different datasets?
- In light of the theoretical claims, can you provide more detailed proofs or clarifications on how the Moment-GNN architecture counts and lists substructures, especially in the context of the experimental results?
