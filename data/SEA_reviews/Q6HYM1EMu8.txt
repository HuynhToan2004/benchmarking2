 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LARG2, a method that leverages large language models (LLMs) to generate reward and goal functions for robotic manipulation tasks. The approach involves using LLMs to transform textual task descriptions into executable code, which is then used to train reinforcement learning (RL) policies. This methodology aims to automate the process of defining rewards and goals, which are typically labor-intensive in RL applications. The paper presents a series of experiments demonstrating the effectiveness of LARG2 in generating valid and relevant goals and rewards for manipulation tasks. However, the paper has been critiqued for its reliance on hand-designed prompts, the complexity of the reward function generation process, and the lack of clarity in its presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in robotics by automating the generation of reward and goal functions for reinforcement learning (RL) tasks.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the method's components and the design of the prompts used to guide the large language model (LLM).
- The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in generating valid and relevant goals and rewards for manipulation tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The method has the potential to significantly reduce the labor-intensive process of defining rewards and goals in RL, which is a significant challenge in many real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the generated rewards and goals, particularly in terms of their effectiveness in training RL policies.
- There is a significant reliance on hand-designed prompts, which may limit the generalizability of the method to other tasks or domains.
- The complexity of the reward function generation process, which involves multiple components and parameters, could make it difficult to implement and optimize.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of hallucination in the LLM's output, which could lead to incorrect or irrelevant rewards and goals.
- The presentation of the paper is confusing in places, particularly in the figures and the descriptions of the experiments, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that use similar approaches but with different models or methodologies.

**Questions:**
- How do you ensure that the generated rewards and goals are effective in training RL policies? Could you provide more details on the evaluation metrics used and the results obtained?
- How do you handle the issue of hallucination in the LLM's output? What strategies do you use to minimize the risk of generating incorrect or irrelevant rewards and goals?
- Could you provide more details on the design of the prompts used to guide the LLM? How were these prompts designed, and what considerations were taken into account?
- How does the method perform when trained on a different set of tasks or domains? Could you provide some examples or results from such experiments?
- The paper mentions the use of a "code validation and auto-correction" process. Could you explain this process in more detail? How does it work, and what are the specific steps involved?
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods. Could you provide a more comprehensive comparison, particularly in terms of the methodology and the results obtained?
