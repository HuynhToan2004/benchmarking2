 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel episodic reinforcement learning (ERL) algorithm called Temporally-Correlated Episodic RL (TCE), which integrates step-based information into policy updates while maintaining the benefits of ERL, such as smooth trajectories and broad exploration. TCE segments trajectories into smaller parts, enabling the calculation of trajectory likelihood and advantage for each segment, which is then used to update the policy. The method is evaluated across various simulated robot manipulation tasks, showing competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art step-based RL and ERL methods. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in describing the method's contributions and the experimental setup, which includes issues such as unclear descriptions of the proposed method, insufficient ablation studies, and potential overestimation of performance due to the use of a low-level tracking controller.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the problem of episodic RL, which is an important and under-explored area in reinforcement learning.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it easy to follow, and the paper includes a comprehensive review of related works.
- The proposed method, TCE, effectively integrates step-based information into policy updates, which is a novel approach.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures that aid in understanding the proposed method.
- The experimental results show that TCE matches the sample efficiency of step-based RL and achieves comparable performance to state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of exploration strategies and conducts comprehensive evaluations on multiple simulated robotic manipulation tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in describing the contributions of the proposed method, particularly in the abstract and introduction sections.
- There is a lack of detailed information on the experimental setup, including the environment, task specifics, and the policy architecture.
- The paper does not include an ablation study, which could help in understanding the impact of different components of the proposed method.
- The performance of TCE is overestimated due to the use of a low-level tracking controller, which might not be feasible for certain tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its potential to struggle with tasks characterized by particularly sparse reward settings.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with a variety of state-of-the-art step-based RL methods, which could provide a more robust evaluation.
- The paper's claims about the efficiency of TCE are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the contributions of the proposed method and how it differs from existing methods, especially in terms of policy updates and trajectory segmentation?
- How does the performance of TCE compare to other step-based RL methods when using the same low-level tracking controller?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the environment, task specifics, and the policy architecture?
- How does TCE handle tasks with sparse rewards, and what are the potential limitations in such scenarios?
- Could you include an ablation study to understand the impact of different components of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of sample efficiency and performance?
