 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to graph contrastive learning (GCL) by proposing a smoothed graph contrastive learning (SGCL) model. SGCL utilizes a graph batch-generating strategy to partition graphs into multiple subgraphs, enhancing scalability for large-scale graphs. The model adjusts the significance of positive/negative pairs in contrastive loss by incorporating three distinct smoothing techniques, aiming to yield smoothed positive/negative pairs. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of SGCL through extensive experiments, showing improvements over existing methods in both node and graph classification tasks. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper's presentation, the novelty of the approach, and the necessity of the proposed techniques, particularly in terms of their contribution to the field beyond existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and the motivation behind the proposed method is clear.
- The proposed method, SGCL, is effective for both small-scale and large-scale graphs, as demonstrated by experimental results.
- The paper provides a clear description of the method and the experiments, with good visualization in figures like Figure 2 and Figure 3.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, and the paper is well-written, with comprehensive experiments that validate the effectiveness of the method.
- The use of three smoothing techniques in the contrastive learning objective is a novel approach, and the experiments show that these techniques can improve performance in both node and graph classification tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the smoothing techniques and their impact on the loss function.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable as similar techniques have been used in previous works, such as those by Wu et al. (2023) and Xie et al. (2023).
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing methods in terms of computational cost and efficiency, especially in large-scale graph settings.
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the impact of different smoothing techniques on the performance and efficiency of the model.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential future directions for research.
- There are several typographical and grammatical errors throughout the paper that need correction.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the smoothing techniques affect the loss function and the overall performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational cost and efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods in large-scale graph settings?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other smoothed contrastive learning methods, such as those mentioned in the references?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of false negatives in the graph, and what are the implications of this on the overall performance of the model?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of different smoothing techniques on the performance and efficiency of the model?
- In terms of the experimental results, can the authors provide more detailed explanations for the observed improvements in performance, especially in the large-scale graph settings?
