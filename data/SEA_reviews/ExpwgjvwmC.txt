 **Summary:**
The paper introduces OmniInput, a novel model-centric evaluation framework that utilizes the output distribution of a trained model to construct a test set and evaluate its precision and recall at different output values. This approach allows for a fine-grained comparison between models, particularly when their performance is similar on pre-defined datasets. The framework is demonstrated to be effective in revealing new insights into model performance and robustness, especially in scenarios where models are trained with different methods. However, concerns are raised about the scalability of the approach and the need for more comprehensive evaluations across various models and datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces OmniInput, a novel model-centric evaluation framework that allows for a fine-grained comparison between models, particularly when their performance is similar on pre-defined datasets.
- The approach of using the output distribution of a trained model to construct a test set and evaluate its precision and recall at different output values is innovative and could provide new insights into model performance and robustness.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise language that effectively communicates the main ideas and findings.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in revealing new insights into model performance and robustness.
- The paper provides a novel perspective on model evaluation, focusing on the output distribution of a model, which is a unique approach compared to traditional data-centric evaluation methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The scalability of the approach is a concern, as it may not be practical for large-scale models or datasets. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability limitations of the proposed method.
- The paper primarily evaluates binary classification tasks, which limits the generalizability of the findings to more complex tasks such as multi-class classification or regression.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive evaluation across different types of models, such as transformer models or large language models, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential biases introduced by the model-centric evaluation approach, particularly in scenarios where the model's output distribution may not accurately reflect the true distribution of data.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the need for large amounts of labeled data and the potential for overfitting to the training data.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of computational complexity and practical applicability for large-scale models or datasets?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the model's output distribution may not accurately reflect the true distribution of data, and what steps can be taken to mitigate potential biases introduced by the model-centric evaluation approach?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for overfitting to the training data and how this could be addressed in future evaluations?
- How does the proposed method compare to other model-centric evaluation methods, particularly in terms of effectiveness and practical applicability?
- Could the authors provide more examples or case studies demonstrating the practical application of the proposed method in real-world scenarios, particularly in domains where model reliability and safety are critical?
