 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for knowledge transfer in reinforcement learning (RL) using curriculum learning and value functions. The approach modifies the complexity of tasks by breaking them down into simpler sub-tasks, which facilitates the transfer of knowledge from easier to harder tasks. This methodology is demonstrated to be effective in both classical RL problems and real-world control tasks, showing improvements in sample efficiency and learning performance. The paper also discusses the challenges in transferring knowledge from simpler to more complex tasks and proposes a method to address these challenges. The method's novelty lies in its ability to transfer knowledge without reusing neural network weights or requiring state/action mappings for different tasks, allowing for the expansion of neural network architecture to accommodate new tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and experimental results.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and can be applied to a wide range of tasks, including both classical and real-world control tasks.
- The method is novel in its approach to knowledge transfer without reusing neural network weights or requiring state/action mappings for different tasks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through extensive experiments, showing improvements in sample efficiency and learning performance.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the method and its application, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other knowledge transfer methods in RL, which could help in understanding the relative advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide valuable insights into its applicability and potential challenges.
- The experimental results, while promising, are somewhat limited in scope, with only four environments used for evaluation. Expanding the evaluation to include a more diverse set of environments could strengthen the paper's claims.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the area of curriculum learning and transfer learning in RL.
- There are some issues with clarity and organization in the paper, particularly in the description of the methodology and the experimental setup.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be adapted to transfer knowledge from a single source task to a target task, rather than requiring multiple source tasks?
- How does the method handle the transfer of knowledge when the source task is not optimal but still provides useful information for the target task?
- Could the method be extended to transfer knowledge across different domains, such as transferring knowledge from a game domain to a control domain?
- How does the method compare in terms of sample efficiency and learning performance to other transfer learning methods in RL, especially those that use similar approaches?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed method and potential areas for future research?
