 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Prototypical Influence Function (PIF) regularizer aimed at enhancing the performance of test-time adaptation (TTA) methods by optimizing the influence of test data on the loss of model-trusting data. The method leverages influence function to quantify the impact of test data on model-trusting data and proposes approximations to make the computations more feasible for TTA scenarios. The PIF regularizer is shown to improve the performance of existing TTA methods across various benchmark datasets, including CIFAR-10-C and ImageNet-C. The paper also discusses the challenges in approximating the influence function and the choice of model-trusting data, which is crucial for the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using influence functions to enhance the performance of test-time adaptation (TTA) methods, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed Prototypical Influence Function (PIF) regularizer is well-motivated, well-explained, and provides a theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear problem definition and well-structured sections that facilitate understanding.
- The experiments conducted are thorough and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed PIF regularizer across various benchmark datasets.
- The paper addresses the challenge of test-time adaptation, which is crucial in real-world scenarios, and provides a practical solution to this problem.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the choice of model-trusting data and its impact on the results, which is a critical aspect of the proposed method.
- There is an inconsistency in the experimental results, particularly in the performance of the proposed method compared to existing methods like SAR, which raises concerns about the robustness of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity introduced by the proposed method, which could limit its practical applicability in resource-constrained environments.
- The paper's claim that the influence function is impractical for TTA is not convincingly supported, and the choice of approximations (e.g., cosine similarity) lacks a robust theoretical justification.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a more rigorous comparison with existing methods to establish the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the selection of model-trusting data and its impact on the results? How sensitive is the method to the choice of model-trusting data?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the test data distribution is significantly different from the training data?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of approximations used in the PIF regularizer, particularly the use of cosine similarity and the approximation of the Hessian matrix?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing TTA methods that also employ influence functions or similar concepts?
- Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity introduced by the proposed method and discuss potential strategies for reducing this complexity?
