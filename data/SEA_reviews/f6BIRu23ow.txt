 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TriSAM, a zero-shot cortical blood vessel segmentation method that leverages the Segment Anything Model (SAM) to segment blood vessels in Volume Electron Microscopy (VEM) images. The methodology involves a multi-seed tracking framework that uses Tri-Plane selection, SAM-based tracking, and recursive redirection to segment blood vessels in 3D volumes without model training or fine-tuning. The paper also presents the BvEM benchmark, a large-scale public dataset specifically designed for cortical blood vessel segmentation in VEM images, which includes data from three mammal species. The proposed method, TriSAM, demonstrates superior performance on the BvEM benchmark across different species, highlighting its utility and potential impact on the field of neuroimaging.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel zero-shot cortical blood vessel segmentation method, TriSAM, which leverages the Segment Anything Model (SAM) to segment blood vessels in Volume Electron Microscopy (VEM) images without model training or fine-tuning.
- TriSAM's multi-seed tracking framework includes Tri-Plane selection, SAM-based tracking, and recursive redirection, which effectively exploits the 3D blood vessel structure and achieves superior performance compared with existing zero-shot and supervised methods.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the TriSAM method and the BvEM benchmark, including the dataset design, image volume standardization, blood vessel annotation, and evaluation metrics.
- The BvEM benchmark is the largest-to-date public benchmark specifically designed for cortical blood vessel segmentation in VEM images, which is crucial for advancing research in the field of neuroimaging.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes detailed explanations of the methodology, making it accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance metrics such as Dice similarity coefficient and Jaccard index.
- The paper does not provide a detailed explanation of the Tri-Plane selection process and the rationale behind the selection of the best plane for tracking.
- The methodology section is not clearly written, making it difficult to understand the specific contributions of each component of the TriSAM method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the TriSAM method or the BvEM benchmark, which could provide valuable insights into the potential challenges and future research directions.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive evaluation of the TriSAM method on other datasets or in different imaging modalities, which could strengthen the claims of its generalizability and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the computational efficiency of the TriSAM method, including the inference time and the number of parameters used?
- How does the TriSAM method compare with other methods in terms of Dice similarity coefficient and Jaccard index?
- Could you clarify the selection process for the best plane for tracking in the Tri-Plane selection process?
- How does the TriSAM method perform on other datasets or in different imaging modalities?
- Could you discuss the limitations of the TriSAM method and the BvEM benchmark, and suggest potential avenues for future research?
