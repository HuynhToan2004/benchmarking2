 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to enhance the generalization of graph neural networks (GNNs) by proposing a plug-and-play module called InterpGNN-GW. This module leverages a global workspace to store and broadcast important node embeddings, facilitating information exchange between distant nodes. Theoretical insights are provided through a PAC-Bayesian bound, which quantifies the interplay between training and test nodes, thereby improving generalization performance. Extensive experiments on both small-scale and large-scale graphs demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing superior performance over existing GNNs. The paper also addresses the issue of information flow between distant nodes in large-scale graphs, which is critical for GNNs' generalization capabilities.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel perspective on enhancing the generalization ability of GNNs through the interplay between training and testing sets, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed InterpGNN-GW module is a plug-and-play method that can be integrated into existing GNNs to improve their performance, making it a practical solution for improving GNNs' generalization.
- The paper is well-structured and provides clear explanations, making it accessible to readers.
- The theoretical results are well-presented and provide a solid foundation for the proposed method.
- The experiments are extensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and settings, showing improvements in generalization performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples to illustrate the concept of "interplay" between training and test nodes.
- The novelty of the proposed method is not clearly distinguished from existing methods, particularly in the area of graph transformers.
- The paper could provide more insights into the design of the global workspace module, especially its role in enhancing the generalization ability of GNNs.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and memory efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for its scalability to larger graphs.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to other GNN architectures and its performance on very large graphs.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including a discussion on the fairness of the experimental setup.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or illustrations to clarify the concept of "interplay" between training and test nodes?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and memory efficiency?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and suggest potential directions for future research?
- How does the proposed method perform on very large graphs, and what are the potential challenges in scaling it up?
- Can the authors provide more details on the design of the global workspace module and its impact on the generalization performance of GNNs?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of structural unfairness in GNNs, and what are the implications for fairness in graph learning applications?
