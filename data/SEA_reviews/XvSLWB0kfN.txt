 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of cultural and linguistic diversity on the semantic coverage of image captions across different languages. It employs a comprehensive evaluation using various datasets, models, and metrics to demonstrate that multilingual captions exhibit broader semantic coverage than monolingual ones. The study also explores how models trained on multilingual data perform well across different languages, challenging the assumption of objective perception in computer vision. Despite its rigorous evaluation and interesting findings, the paper has been critiqued for its limited scope, particularly its focus on a small set of languages and the potential for overgeneralization of its findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a comprehensive evaluation.
- The study demonstrates that multilingual captions have broader semantic coverage than monolingual captions, which is an interesting and important finding.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments and analyses, including evaluating the performance of models trained on multilingual data versus monolingual data and comparing different language families.
- The paper challenges the assumption that perception is objective, showing that models trained on multilingual data perform well across different languages, which could lead to more inclusive and diverse models in computer vision.
- The paper includes a human evaluation and a study on how different languages influence the descriptions of images, which adds depth to the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claims of cultural influence on the semantics of captions are not sufficiently supported by the experimental design, which primarily focuses on a small set of languages.
- The paper could benefit from a more diverse set of languages to better understand the generalizability of the findings.
- The semantic coverage of captions is measured using a limited set of metrics, which might not fully capture the richness and diversity of language use.
- The paper's assumptions and conclusions are not always clearly articulated, making it difficult to fully understand the implications of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the study, which could include the potential for overgeneralization based on the limited set of languages analyzed.

**Questions:**
- How were the 7 languages chosen for the study, and what criteria were used to select them?
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of the paper, especially how the findings challenge or support existing assumptions in computer vision?
- How do the authors address the potential for overgeneralization based on the limited set of languages analyzed?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the metrics used to measure semantic coverage, particularly how they account for the richness and diversity of language use?
- How do the authors envision the practical applications of their findings in the development of more inclusive and diverse models in computer vision?
