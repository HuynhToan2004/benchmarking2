 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the problem of learning an agent-centric state representation in a finite-memory POMDP setting, where the state can be decoded from a sequence of past observations. It introduces the Masked Inverse Kinematics with Actions (MIK+A) objective, which is shown to be Bayes optimal and can recover the agent-centric state from partially observable settings. The paper provides theoretical insights into the Bayes optimal classifier and empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of MIK+A over other objectives. It also discusses the challenges in extending multi-step inverse kinematics to FM-POMDPs and provides a counterexample to show the failure of the Forward-Jump objective. The paper is structured around a detailed analysis of different objectives and their theoretical and empirical implications, with a focus on the FM-POMDP setting.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel setting of learning agent-centric state representations in finite-memory POMDPs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- It provides a thorough analysis of different objectives for learning agent-centric state representations and presents a counterexample to show the failure of the Forward-Jump objective, highlighting the challenges in extending multi-step inverse kinematics to FM-POMDPs.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and rigorous theoretical analysis, including the Bayes optimal classifier for the proposed MIK+A objective, which is novel and has not been studied before.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of MIK+A over other objectives, showing that it can recover the agent-centric state from partially observable settings.
- The paper is structured around a detailed analysis of different objectives and their theoretical and empirical implications, providing a comprehensive understanding of the problem and its solutions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is somewhat limited by the assumption that the agent-centric state is decodable from a short history of observations, which may not hold in more complex or realistic settings.
- The experimental results are somewhat limited, with a lack of comparisons to existing works and a focus on a single task, which might not generalize well to other settings.
- The paper could benefit from more discussion on the practical implications and limitations of the proposed methods, especially in terms of scalability and applicability to real-world scenarios.
- There is a need for more clarity in the presentation of the paper, particularly in the introduction and related work sections, to better motivate the research and position it within the existing literature.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed experimental results and comparisons, especially in the visual offline RL task, to demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the assumption that the agent-centric state is decodable from a short history of observations?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex or realistic settings, such as those involving high-dimensional, non-Markovian observations?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the MIK+A objective and its advantages over other objectives, particularly in terms of its theoretical and empirical benefits?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of scalability, computational efficiency, and practical applicability?
- Can you provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons to existing works and results on a broader range of tasks, to better demonstrate the effectiveness and generalizability of the proposed methods?
