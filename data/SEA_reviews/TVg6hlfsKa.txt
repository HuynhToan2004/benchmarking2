 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method named SelaVPR, which leverages pre-trained models to adapt to the visual place recognition (VPR) task, focusing on both global and local feature adaptations. It utilizes a hybrid global-local adaptation method to enhance the model's capability in identifying and matching visual features from different places. The method includes a global adaptation module that fine-tunes the pre-trained model to focus on static, discriminative landmarks, and a local adaptation module that outputs dense local features for re-ranking. Experimental results demonstrate that SelaVPR outperforms state-of-the-art methods on several VPR benchmarks, achieving high recall rates with less training data and training time. The method's efficiency is highlighted by its minimal runtime, making it suitable for real-time applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and informative figures that aid in understanding the proposed method.
- The method effectively utilizes pre-trained foundation models for visual place recognition (VPR), demonstrating a novel approach to adapting these models for VPR tasks.
- The proposed method shows significant improvements over state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods, as evidenced by the experimental results and ablation studies.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear and detailed explanations of the methodology and experimental setups.
- The use of mutual nearest neighbor local feature loss for training the local adaptation module is innovative and contributes to the efficiency of the VPR process.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other recent methods such as MixVPR, which could provide a clearer picture of the method's performance relative to the state of the art.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational cost and efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The paper does not provide sufficient qualitative results to support the claims made about the method's effectiveness in challenging scenes.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the training data's influence on the method's performance and a comparison with other pre-trained models to assess the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the training data used in the experiments and how it influences the method's performance?
- How does the proposed method compare with other pre-trained models, such as DINO and DINOv2, in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide qualitative results or examples to support the claims made about the method's effectiveness in challenging scenes?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of perceptual aliasing, which is a significant challenge in VPR?
- Could the authors clarify the computational cost and efficiency of the proposed method, especially in comparison to other state-of-the-art methods?
