 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PatchMixer, a novel CNN-based architecture for long-term time series forecasting, leveraging depthwise separable convolutions to capture both local and global temporal patterns. This model, which utilizes dual forecasting heads for linear and nonlinear components, is designed to outperform state-of-the-art methods in terms of prediction accuracy and computational efficiency. The model's performance is validated across seven benchmark datasets, demonstrating improvements over existing CNN-based models and some transformer-based models. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art models, and potential over-reliance on the patch-based representation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methodologies and results.
- The proposed method, PatchMixer, is novel and effective, particularly in leveraging depthwise separable convolutions to capture both local and global temporal patterns, which is crucial for long-term time series forecasting.
- The model's dual forecasting heads are innovative, allowing for the modeling of both linear and nonlinear components, which enhances the model's ability to capture intricate temporal patterns in time series data.
- PatchMixer achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on seven time-series forecasting benchmarks, outperforming the state-of-the-art method and the best-performing CNN by 3.9% and 21.2%, respectively.
- The model is computationally efficient, being 3x faster for inference and 2x faster during training compared to current SOTA models, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is somewhat limited, as the use of patch-based representation and dual heads is not entirely new.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance metrics across different datasets and forecasting lengths.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its potential over-reliance on the patch-based representation and the need for further research into its integration with external temporal features.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how it compares to other state-of-the-art models, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its strengths and weaknesses.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental details, such as the number of parameters, training time, and inference time, which are crucial for reproducibility and validation of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the model, including the number of parameters, training time, and inference time, to better understand the practical implications of using PatchMixer?
- How does the proposed model perform on datasets with different characteristics, such as those with high noise levels or those with different seasonalities?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art models, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance metrics across different datasets and forecasting lengths?
- How does the proposed model handle different data types, such as image or text data, and what are the potential applications in these areas?
- In light of the concerns about the novelty and the limited comparison with state-of-the-art models, can the authors clarify the unique contributions of their model and how it compares to existing models in terms of performance and efficiency?
