 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of aggregated labels in machine learning, focusing on two loss functions: instance-level loss and bag-level loss. The authors propose a novel interpolating loss function that combines elements of both, providing a theoretical analysis of its performance in a high-dimensional data setting. They also introduce a differentially private learning mechanism, demonstrating its effectiveness through numerical experiments. The paper delves into the theoretical aspects of these losses, comparing their properties and providing a framework for understanding their impact on model performance and privacy. Despite its theoretical strengths, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental scope and the need for a broader evaluation of its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow.
- The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed interpolating loss function, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper addresses a relevant and interesting problem of learning from aggregated responses, which is crucial in privacy-preserving machine learning.
- The numerical experiments provided support the theoretical findings, enhancing the credibility of the results.
- The paper introduces a differentially private learning mechanism, which is a valuable addition to the field of privacy-preserving machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on linear regression models, which might limit its applicability to other types of models.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, with only a few datasets used, which might not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review, particularly in the area of label proportions and learning from aggregated labels.
- The theoretical results are derived under specific assumptions, such as the linear model and Gaussian features, which may not hold in more general settings.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which is essential for a balanced assessment of the research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between their proposed method and existing methods, such as those mentioned in the related works section?
- How does the proposed method perform in settings where the linear model does not hold, or where the features are not Gaussian?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of their methods, especially in relation to the size of the datasets used?
- How does the proposed method handle non-linear models, and what are the implications of this for the theoretical results?
- Could the authors elaborate on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and discuss how these assumptions affect the generalizability of the findings?
- In the context of differential privacy, how does the proposed method ensure that the noise added to the aggregated responses does not significantly impact the model's performance?
