 **Summary:**
The paper introduces IdentityChain, a novel framework designed to evaluate the self-consistency of Code Large Language Models (Code LLMs). It proposes a new metric, Test Output Match (TOM) score, which is used to assess the semantic equivalence between natural language and code, ensuring the model's consistency across different tasks. The framework is evaluated on various models, showing that even the most advanced models like GPT-4 and GPT-3.5 struggle with self-consistency. The paper highlights that existing metrics like Pass@K are insufficient for evaluating self-consistency and proposes a new approach that checks the semantic identity of generated code and natural language. The framework is also shown to be useful for debugging and improving model performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new and interesting problem of self-consistency in Code LLMs, which is not addressed by existing metrics like Pass@K.
- The proposed metric, Test Output Match (TOM), is novel and provides a new dimension for evaluating code LLMs, focusing on the semantic equivalence between natural language and code.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the proposed methodology is simple yet effective.
- The framework is evaluated on multiple models, showing that even the most advanced models like GPT-4 and GPT-3.5 fail to maintain self-consistency, highlighting the need for a more robust evaluation metric.
- The framework can be used as a model debugging tool to expose weaknesses of Code LLMs, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed TOM score with other existing metrics, particularly in terms of its effectiveness and practical applicability.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a small number of models and tasks, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's claims about the self-consistency of code LLMs are not convincingly supported by the data, and the results presented do not clearly demonstrate a correlation between self-consistency and overall performance.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the proposed metric, TOM, handles different types of errors in code generation, such as syntax errors or runtime errors.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not clearly explained, particularly in the appendix, which could be improved for better clarity and understanding.

**Questions:**
- How does the TOM score perform when compared to other existing metrics, particularly in terms of its effectiveness and practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on how the TOM score correlates with human-judged results, especially in scenarios where the code generated by the model is incorrect?
- How does the proposed metric handle different types of errors in code generation, such as syntax errors or runtime errors?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results presented in the appendix, particularly in the context of the HumanEval benchmark?
- How does the proposed framework compare to existing debugging techniques, and what are the specific advantages of using IdentityChain for debugging purposes?
