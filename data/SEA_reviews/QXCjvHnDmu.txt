 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method using a genetic algorithm (GA) to generate adversarial prompts for Large Language Models (LLMs) under black-box conditions, aiming to induce harmful or undesirable responses. The authors propose a fitness function that optimizes the adversarial suffix by minimizing the negative log-likelihood of generating a specific target output. The method is evaluated against two LLMs, showing high attack success rates (ASRs) across different settings. The paper also discusses the limitations and ethical implications of the proposed attack, suggesting potential countermeasures. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in explaining the attack's effectiveness and its reliance on a specific fitness function which may not be universally applicable.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method.
- The introduction of a novel black-box jailbreak attack on LLMs using a genetic algorithm is innovative and addresses a significant research gap.
- The methodology is well-explained, with a focus on automating the process of jailbreaking LLMs, which is a significant challenge in the field.
- The paper includes comprehensive evaluations and ablation studies, which help to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The transferability of the attack to different models and the use of a simple fitness function are highlighted as strengths, demonstrating the generalizability of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the effectiveness of the attack and the specifics of the fitness function used, which is crucial for understanding the method's efficacy.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental details, such as the specifics of the adversarial suffix and the generation process, which are essential for reproducibility and validation of the results.
- The method's applicability to different LLM architectures and its transferability to other AI systems beyond LLMs are not adequately addressed, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, including its potential impact on model performance and the ethical implications of using such attacks.
- The experimental setup and the choice of parameters, such as the population size and the number of generations, are not well-justified, which raises concerns about the robustness and reliability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the effectiveness of the attack and the specifics of the fitness function used? How does the fitness function ensure the attack's success, and why is it effective?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different LLM architectures, and what are the implications for its transferability to other AI systems?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the adversarial suffix generation process and the parameters used?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods, and what are the specific advantages or disadvantages of using a genetic algorithm over other approaches?
- Given the potential ethical concerns, how do the authors address the societal impact of their work, and what measures can be taken to mitigate any negative consequences?
