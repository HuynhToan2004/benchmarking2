 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GASSIP, a method that integrates graph sparsification and architecture search to optimize lightweight graph neural networks (GNNs). The approach leverages curriculum learning to iteratively prune operations and graph edges, aiming to reduce model complexity while maintaining performance. The method is evaluated on five datasets, demonstrating its ability to search for efficient GNNs with fewer parameters and sparser graphs. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in explaining the methodology, particularly the role of graph sparsification in the architecture search process. Additionally, concerns were raised about the method's scalability and the potential for overfitting due to the iterative nature of the pruning process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by integrating graph sparsification and architecture search, which is a significant advancement in the field of Graph Neural Architecture Search (GNAS).
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear notations and formulations that aid in understanding the proposed approach.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing baselines in terms of performance and efficiency.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, which is crucial for readers to engage with the content.
- The use of curriculum learning for graph sparsification is innovative and adds a new dimension to the GNAS problem.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the role of graph sparsification in the architecture search process. It is unclear whether graph sparsification is used to guide the search or if it is applied after the search.
- The methodology section is not detailed enough, particularly in explaining how graph sparsification is applied and how it impacts the architecture search.
- There is a potential for overfitting due to the iterative nature of the pruning process, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of handling large graphs, which is a critical limitation for practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, including more recent and relevant works in the field of GNAS.
- The paper does not provide a detailed theoretical analysis of the convergence of the proposed iterative optimization algorithm, which is a significant gap in the methodological validation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role of graph sparsification in the architecture search process? Is it used to guide the search or applied after the search?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting due to the iterative nature of the pruning process?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the methodology, particularly in the application of graph sparsification and its impact on the architecture search?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods in terms of scalability and performance on larger, more complex graphs?
- Could the authors provide a more comprehensive comparison with recent and relevant works in the field of GNAS to better position their contributions?
- What are the implications of the iterative nature of the pruning process on the generalizability of the results?
