 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DiffMaSIF, a novel deep learning method for rigid-body protein-protein docking that utilizes a surface-based encoder-decoder architecture. This approach leverages surface-based encoders to predict binding site complementarity and rigid-body docking, potentially outperforming existing methods in scenarios with low co-evolution and minimal sequence conservation. The methodology involves a diffusion model that integrates geometric pre-training tasks to enhance the learning of physical complementarity. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is critiqued for its lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, insufficient ablation studies, and unclear presentation of results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel surface-based encoder-decoder architecture for protein-protein docking, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that heavily rely on sequence-based features.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the geometric pre-training tasks used to enhance the learning of physical complementarity.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through experimental results, showing improvements over existing methods in certain scenarios.
- The use of a diffusion model for protein-protein docking is innovative and potentially advantageous in scenarios where co-evolutionary signals are weak or absent.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures that aid in understanding the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of the number of poses generated and the evaluation metrics used.
- There is a lack of ablation studies, which makes it difficult to determine the individual contributions of the various components of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of protein-protein interactions and its generalizability to different datasets.
- The presentation of results is unclear, with figures and tables not providing sufficient detail or clarity.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the novelty of the method compared to existing approaches, particularly in terms of the use of surface-based representations and diffusion models.
- The paper suffers from several typographical and grammatical errors, which detract from its professionalism and readability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and roles of different proteins in the docking process, particularly in terms of the receptor and ligand?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements to other state-of-the-art methods?
- Can the authors provide more details on the pre-training tasks used and their impact on the final results?
- How does the proposed method perform on different datasets, particularly those that are not used in the current experiments?
- Could the authors provide more detailed ablation studies to better understand the contributions of different components of the proposed method?
- Can the authors clarify the presentation of results, particularly in terms of the metrics used and the clarity of the figures and tables?
