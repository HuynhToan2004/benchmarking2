 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Self-Paced Augmentations (SPAug), a novel approach to dynamically adjusting augmentation intensity for each sample based on its training statistics. This method aims to improve model robustness by tailoring augmentation intensity to the difficulty of individual samples, using a binary mapping function to differentiate between "easy" and "hard" samples. The approach is evaluated on datasets such as CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, showing improvements over baseline methods like AugMix and RandomAugment. However, the method's effectiveness is questioned when applied to more complex datasets like ImageNet, and the paper lacks comprehensive comparisons with other adaptive augmentation methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to adaptive augmentation by adjusting the augmentation intensity based on the sample's training statistics, which is a significant departure from traditional uniform augmentation policies.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the experimental setup and results, and the paper is generally easy to follow.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear motivation and a straightforward methodology that is easy to implement, making it accessible for practical applications.
- The experimental results show improvements over baseline methods on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other adaptive augmentation methods, such as AdaAug, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The method's effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated on larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet, raising concerns about its scalability and applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The experimental setup and results are not comprehensive enough, with a limited number of baseline methods compared and a lack of detail on the hyperparameter settings used.
- The paper's novelty is questioned as it appears to be a simple modification of existing methods, and the theoretical analysis provided is insufficient to fully understand the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The method's impact on computational overhead and training time is not adequately addressed, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other adaptive augmentation methods, such as AdaAug?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the performance of the method on larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet?
- How does the method handle the computational overhead and training time, especially when applied to larger datasets?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the method's performance?
- Is there a theoretical analysis available that could help understand the proposed method's effectiveness in more depth?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of augmentations, such as color jittering or rotation?
