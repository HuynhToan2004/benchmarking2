 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel parameter-free automatic model adaptation protocol for ConvNets, specifically the Rep-Adapter, which allows for soft balancing between pre-trained and fine-tuned filters. This adapter module, which can be converted into a single weight layer during inference, avoids the need for additional parameters. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that Rep-Adapter simulates a ConvNet layer with each filter fine-tuned at different learning rates. The method is evaluated across various datasets and tasks, showing its effectiveness and efficiency. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty and insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its applications.
- The Rep-Adapter is a novel approach that can be merged into a single layer during inference, eliminating the need for additional parameters during inference.
- The methodology is supported by theoretical analysis and extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the Rep-Adapter.
- The paper addresses the challenge of transfer learning by proposing an automatic model adaptation protocol that balances between fine-tuning and linear probing.
- The Rep-Adapter is a parameter-free method that can simulate a ConvNet layer with each filter fine-tuned at different learning rates, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency.
- The novelty of the Rep-Adapter is limited as it builds upon existing methods like LoRA and Progressive Networks.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, such as LoRA and Visual Prompt Tuning.
- The paper does not provide sufficient empirical evidence to support claims about the efficiency of the Rep-Adapter.
- There are concerns about the method's ability to achieve soft balancing between pre-trained and fine-tuned filters, as it may not effectively combine the advantages of both.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, which is a significant concern in transfer learning.

**Questions:**
- Can the Rep-Adapter be applied to other types of models, such as Vision Transformers?
- How does the Rep-Adapter compare with other parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, such as LoRA and Visual Prompt Tuning?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the Rep-Adapter, particularly in terms of inference speed and memory usage?
- Is there a theoretical explanation for why the Rep-Adapter is effective in balancing between fine-tuning and linear probing?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the Rep-Adapter with other methods, including a discussion on how the Rep-Adapter's performance compares in different scenarios?
