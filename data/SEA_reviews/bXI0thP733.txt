 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel backdoor defense mechanism using generative modeling to mitigate the effects of poisoned data in neural networks. The methodology involves training a self-supervised feature extractor to map input data into a latent space, followed by per-class generative modeling to detect and relabel poisoned data. This approach aims to improve the robustness of neural networks against backdoor attacks by identifying and correcting poisoned data, thereby enhancing the overall model performance. The paper's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on various datasets, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet, showcasing the method's ability to reduce attack success rates while maintaining high clean accuracy.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to backdoor defense using generative modeling, which is an innovative direction in the field.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the proposed techniques and their applications.
- The paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experimental results that validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem in the field of backdoor attacks, which is crucial for enhancing the security and reliability of machine learning systems.
- The use of self-supervised feature extractors and per-class generative modeling is an interesting and potentially impactful approach to addressing backdoor attacks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for a comprehensive understanding of its applicability and ethical implications.
- The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, with insufficient comparison to existing defense methods and a lack of discussion on the generalizability of the results across different datasets and attack types.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive ablation studies to better understand the contributions of different components of the proposed method.
- There is a noticeable absence of discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, which is essential for practical deployment in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for the proposed method to be circumvented by advanced adversarial attacks, which could limit its effectiveness in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when the attacker is aware of the defense mechanism? Is there a potential for the attacker to adapt their strategy to bypass the defense?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of training time and resource requirements?
- How generalizable is the proposed method across different datasets and attack types? Could the authors provide additional experimental evidence to support the method's effectiveness across a broader range of scenarios?
- Is there a potential for the proposed method to be circumvented by advanced adversarial attacks, and if so, how might the method be adapted to address these threats?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the hyperparameters used in the proposed method and their impact on the method's effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing defense methods in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements?
