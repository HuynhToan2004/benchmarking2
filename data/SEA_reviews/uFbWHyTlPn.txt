 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel differentially private stochastic gradient descent algorithm, DiceSGD, which addresses the challenges of gradient clipping bias and constant bias in traditional DP-SGD methods. DiceSGD introduces a clipped error feedback mechanism to eliminate the clipping bias, allowing for an arbitrary choice of clipping threshold independent of the problem. The algorithm is analyzed under both strong convexity and PL conditions, providing convergence guarantees and differential privacy under Renyi DP. Empirical results show that DiceSGD outperforms existing methods on standard datasets, offering both utility and privacy guarantees. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited empirical evaluations, lack of comparisons with recent methods, and insufficient theoretical justification for the choice of hyperparameters.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel algorithm, DiceSGD, which addresses the issue of gradient clipping bias in traditional DP-SGD methods, providing a fresh perspective on the problem.
- The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm, including convergence proofs and differential privacy under Renyi DP.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm in achieving higher accuracies than DPSGD-GC while maintaining the same level of DP guarantee.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making the complex theoretical concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The introduction of an error feedback mechanism to eliminate clipping bias is a significant contribution to the field of differentially private optimization.
- The paper is original in its approach, introducing a novel error-feedback mechanism to eliminate clipping bias, which is not commonly seen in existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other recent methods, such as those using adaptive clipping thresholds or alternative gradient clipping strategies.
- The empirical evaluations are limited to a few datasets, and the paper does not include comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive understanding of the algorithm's performance.
- The theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions, such as bounded variance and bounded gradients, which may not hold in practical scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of hyperparameter selection, particularly the choice of clipping thresholds, which is crucial for the performance of the algorithm.
- The experimental setup and results lack clarity and depth, with insufficient details provided on the experimental conditions and results, making it difficult to evaluate the robustness and reliability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the potential negative societal impacts of its application.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and the hyperparameter selection process?
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent methods that address similar issues in DP-SGD, such as those using adaptive clipping thresholds or alternative gradient clipping strategies?
- Could the authors provide more detailed theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support the claim that the method can achieve nearly the same utility bound as DPSGD without gradient clipping?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the assumptions of bounded variance and bounded gradients do not hold?
- What are the implications of the choice of clipping thresholds on the performance of the algorithm, and how are these thresholds selected in practical applications?
- Can the authors provide more detailed discussions on the limitations of the proposed method and the potential negative societal impacts of its application?
