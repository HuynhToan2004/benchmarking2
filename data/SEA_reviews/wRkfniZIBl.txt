 **Summary:**
The paper introduces IsoCLR, a novel self-supervised learning approach for RNA isoform representation. It employs a contrastive learning objective to learn robust representations across species, focusing on RNA half-life and mean ribosome load prediction tasks. The method leverages functional similarities between sequences generated through alternative splicing and gene duplication to create a new dataset and contrastive objective, aiming to improve performance on these tasks. The authors claim that IsoCLR outperforms existing SSL methods and matches supervised performance when fine-tuned. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned, as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods like BYOL to RNA sequences.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of the main ideas and methodology.
- The authors demonstrate that IsoCLR outperforms existing SSL methods on RNA half-life and mean ribosome load prediction tasks, showing competitive performance with supervised methods when fine-tuned.
- The use of functional similarities between sequences generated through alternative splicing and gene duplication is an innovative approach that leverages the unique properties of genomic data to create a new dataset and contrastive objective.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the dataset and contrastive objective, which are critical for understanding the methodology and its applications.
- The experimental results show that IsoCLR is effective in the low data regime, which is crucial for practical applications where data availability is limited.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation across various downstream tasks, which could have provided a more robust validation of the method's effectiveness.
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it appears to be a simple extension of existing methods like BYOL to RNA sequences, without significant architectural or algorithmic innovation.
- The choice of benchmarks for evaluating the method's effectiveness is questionable, as the paper does not compare against the most relevant or recent methods in the field.
- The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to support the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly concerning the choice of augmentations and their impact on performance.
- The method's generalizability to other RNA-related tasks and its scalability to larger datasets are not adequately addressed.
- There are concerns about the method's ability to handle complex RNA structures like RNA secondary structure, which could affect the accuracy of downstream predictions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the contrastive learning objective is adapted for RNA sequences, particularly in terms of the specific augmentations used?
- How does the method perform on other RNA-related tasks beyond half-life and ribosome load prediction?
- Could the authors provide more details on the dataset construction process, including the selection criteria for positive and negative pairs?
- How does the method handle the potential issue of overfitting, especially in the low data regime?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of benchmarks and provide a more detailed comparison with the most relevant and recent methods in the field?
- How does the method handle the potential issue of the model being too specialized to the specific tasks used for evaluation, and what steps have been taken to ensure the method's generalizability to other tasks?
