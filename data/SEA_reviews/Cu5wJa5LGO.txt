 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework named Learning Latent Causal Processes (LLCP) for Video Question Answering (VideoQA), focusing on causal reasoning in video content. The framework utilizes a Temporal Multivariate Generative Model (TMGM) to identify causal relationships in video events, facilitating both accident attribution and counterfactual prediction. The model is trained using self-supervised local auto-regression, eliminating the need for annotated question-answer pairs. The framework's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing comparable performance to supervised methods. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, unclear explanations of certain methodological choices, and potential limitations in scalability and generalizability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, LLCP, which employs a causal perspective to enhance video reasoning, reducing annotation reliance and improving reasoning ability.
- The proposed TMGM is a novel approach to learn local latent causal processes, which is applied to both cause-based and effect-based reasoning tasks.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the methodology and experimental results.
- The use of a self-supervised training strategy and the application of causal mechanisms to model interactions among agents are innovative and contribute to the field.
- The experimental results demonstrate that LLCP can achieve comparable performance with supervised methods without using paired question-answer annotations for training.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy for readers to understand the proposed methods and the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, especially in terms of performance metrics and methodological approaches.
- The paper does not clearly explain how the historical states are obtained and how the temporal multivariate generative model is trained.
- The scalability and generalizability of the proposed method are limited due to the use of pre-trained multiple object tracking models, which may not be applicable in all scenarios.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the size and distribution of datasets used, and the specifics of the training and testing procedures.
- The paper does not include an ablation study to determine the impact of the number of frames and the number of objects on the performance of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation on larger-scale datasets and a broader range of video content to validate the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the historical states are obtained and how the temporal multivariate generative model is trained?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger-scale datasets with more complex video content?
- Can you clarify the role and impact of the historical states in the model's performance?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the number of frames or the number of objects in the video varies?
- Could you include an ablation study to determine the impact of the number of frames and the number of objects on the performance of the proposed method?
- In terms of the experimental setup, could you provide more details on the size and distribution of the datasets used, and the specifics of the training and testing procedures?
