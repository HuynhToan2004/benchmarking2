 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Fair Collapse (FaCe), for estimating the transferability of pre-trained models. FaCe is based on the concept of neural collapse, focusing on the variance collapse term and a class fairness term. The variance collapse term assesses class separation and within-class compactness, while the class fairness term quantifies fairness towards each class. The method is evaluated across various pre-trained models and datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in estimating transferability. The paper also provides a comprehensive experimental analysis, comparing FaCe with existing methods and showcasing its competitive performance. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in defining key terms, such as "transferability," and for not sufficiently distinguishing its approach from existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for estimating transferability using the neural collapse phenomenon, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it easy to follow, and the paper provides a clear and comprehensive experimental analysis.
- The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and text classification, which demonstrate the effectiveness and generalization of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is intuitive and simple, making it potentially applicable in practical scenarios.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including an ablation study, which supports the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining key terms, such as "transferability," which could lead to confusion among readers.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods, particularly in terms of the novelty of the class fairness term and the overall methodology.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical backing for the proposed method, which could limit the understanding of its effectiveness and applicability.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including a broader range of pre-trained models and datasets to validate the robustness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition of "transferability" and its relevance to the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability with existing methods?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing methods, particularly in terms of its effectiveness and applicability in different scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the pre-trained models are not well-trained or where the target datasets are significantly different from the pre-training datasets?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training hyperparameters used in the experiments to ensure the reproducibility of the results?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of bias towards specific classes in pre-trained models, and what are the implications of this bias in practical applications?
