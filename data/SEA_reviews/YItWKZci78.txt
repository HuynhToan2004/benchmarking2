 **Summary:**
The paper introduces novel algorithms for solving distributional minimax problems using mean-field Langevin dynamics, specifically focusing on entropy-regularized saddle point problems. It proposes two algorithms: a single-loop algorithm (MFL-AG) and a double-loop algorithm (MFL-ABR), both designed to converge to the mixed Nash equilibrium. The authors demonstrate the convergence of these algorithms under various settings, including time and particle discretization, and provide theoretical guarantees for their performance. The paper also includes numerical experiments that support the theoretical claims, showing that the proposed methods can outperform existing methods like MFL-DA. However, concerns about the clarity of presentation and the practical implications of the results were noted, particularly in terms of the computational complexity and the real-world applicability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces novel algorithms for solving distributional minimax problems using mean-field Langevin dynamics, which are original and well-explained.
- The algorithms are designed to converge to the mixed Nash equilibrium, a significant contribution to the field of distributional minimax optimization.
- The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the performance of the proposed algorithms, which are supported by numerical experiments that demonstrate their effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the theoretical results and practical applications, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors address the challenges of handling multiple learning agents and provide a novel analysis of propagation of chaos, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, which is crucial for understanding their practical applicability.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed algorithms with existing methods, such as MFL-DA, which could have provided a clearer picture of their relative advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithms and the conditions under which they may not perform optimally.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the results, particularly in terms of the real-world applicability of the theoretical findings.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need correction to enhance the readability and professionalism of the paper.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms and discuss how it compares to existing methods?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness with other state-of-the-art methods for solving distributional minimax problems?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, possibly including a comparison with other methods on real-world datasets?
- Is there a possibility to extend the theoretical results to nonconvex-nonconcave parametrizations, which could broaden the applicability of the findings?
- Can the authors address the concerns regarding the clarity and completeness of the proofs, particularly in the appendices?
