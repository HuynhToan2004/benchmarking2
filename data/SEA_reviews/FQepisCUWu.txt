 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ChatEval, a multi-agent framework designed to enhance the evaluation of text quality by utilizing multiple language models (LLMs) in a debate-like setting. The system employs various communication strategies and diverse role prompts to simulate human-like evaluation processes, aiming to improve the accuracy and correlation with human assessments. The framework is tested against existing methods and demonstrates superior performance, although concerns are raised about its novelty and the depth of its contributions compared to existing literature.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods.
- The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods through comparisons with existing baselines.
- The approach of using multiple agents to simulate human-like evaluation processes is innovative and provides a novel perspective on evaluating text quality.
- The use of diverse role prompts and different communication strategies is a significant contribution to the field, enhancing the realism and effectiveness of the evaluation process.
- The qualitative analysis provided is insightful, offering a deeper understanding of how the agents evaluate texts and providing a human-mimicking evaluation process.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as similar frameworks have been previously explored, such as using multiple LLMs for evaluation and incorporating diverse role prompts and communication strategies.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, particularly concerning the scalability and applicability of the approach across different domains and datasets.
- There is a need for more detailed analysis and comparisons with existing methods, especially in terms of the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed communication strategies and the use of diverse role prompts.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experiments, including a broader range of datasets and a more rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods to solidify the claims of superior performance.
- The paper's contribution is somewhat limited as it primarily focuses on using LLMs for evaluation, which is a well-explored area, and does not offer significant new insights or methodologies.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed approach perform on other datasets or tasks beyond the ones tested in the paper?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the choice of communication strategies and their impact on the evaluation process?
- How does the proposed approach compare with other methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Can the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed methods and discuss potential directions for future research?
- Is there a possibility to include human evaluations in the comparison to better understand the effectiveness of the proposed methods in mimicking human judgments?
