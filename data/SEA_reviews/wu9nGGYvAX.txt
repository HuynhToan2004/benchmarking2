 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the ability of neural networks, specifically CLIP-pretrained vision transformers (ViTs) and ResNets, to learn and generalize the same-different relation in images. The study includes experiments on various datasets, comparing models trained from scratch and those pretrained on ImageNet or CLIP. Key findings include that CLIP-pretrained models perform well in generalizing the same-different relation, while models trained from scratch or on ImageNet do not. The paper also explores the impact of different pretraining methods and datasets on model performance. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the study, as it primarily reiterates known issues with CNNs and does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from prior works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise figures and a logical structure that makes it easy to follow.
- The study is comprehensive, including various architectures, pretraining methods, and fine-tuning datasets, which enhances the reliability of the results.
- The findings are significant, particularly the observation that CLIP-pretrained models can generalize the same-different relation to out-of-distribution stimuli, suggesting that these models have learned a genuinely abstract concept of equality.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the generalization performance of different models, which is crucial for understanding the capabilities of neural networks in learning abstract visual relations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the study is limited, as it primarily reiterates known issues with CNNs and does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from prior works.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the study, particularly concerning the generalizability of the findings to other architectures and datasets.
- There is a need for more robust experimental validation, such as using a larger number of seeds to ensure the statistical significance of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the inductive biases of different architectures, particularly the preference of ViTs for comparing color or texture, which could limit their ability to generalize to abstract shapes.
- The paper could benefit from a broader discussion on the generalizability of the findings to other types of visual relations and the impact of different pretraining methods on model performance.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the inductive biases of different architectures, particularly how these biases affect the generalization of models to abstract shapes?
- How does the paper address the limitations of the study, particularly concerning the generalizability of the findings to other architectures and datasets?
- Could the authors elaborate on the impact of different pretraining methods on model performance, especially in relation to the generalization of models to out-of-distribution stimuli?
- What are the authors' thoughts on the potential impact of different dataset sizes on model performance, particularly in relation to the generalization of models to abstract shapes?
- How do the authors explain the observed differences in model performance between different datasets, such as the preference of ViTs for comparing color or texture versus shape?
