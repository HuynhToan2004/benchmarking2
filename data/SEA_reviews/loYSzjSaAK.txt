 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Submodular Reinforcement Learning (SubRL), a novel approach to reinforcement learning (RL) that handles non-additive rewards modeled using submodular set functions. This framework aims to capture diminishing returns and is particularly useful in scenarios where rewards depend on the history of states visited. The paper presents a policy gradient-based algorithm, SubPO, which optimizes submodular rewards by greedily maximizing marginal gains. Theoretical analysis is provided for the general case, showing that SubRL is hard to approximate, and for specific cases, it achieves constant-factor approximation guarantees. Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of SubPO in various applications such as biodiversity monitoring, Bayesian experiment design, and informative path planning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework for reinforcement learning (RL) with submodular rewards, which is a significant departure from traditional RL methods that typically handle additive rewards.
- The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including a hardness result for the general case and constant-factor approximation guarantees under specific conditions.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their applications.
- The proposed algorithm, SubPO, is simple and effective, as demonstrated by strong empirical performance across a variety of applications.
- The paper provides a lower bound on the hardness of approximating the problem, which is crucial for understanding the practical limitations of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples of submodular rewards, particularly in the context of real-world applications.
- The experimental results are limited to relatively simple environments, which may not fully demonstrate the capabilities of the proposed methods in more complex settings.
- The paper could provide more insights into the computational efficiency and complexity of the proposed methods, especially in relation to the complexity of the submodular reward function.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the proposed methods compare to other approaches, such as those that use non-Markovian representations or different types of policy gradient methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed methods, such as their applicability in scenarios with more complex reward structures or in environments with non-stationary dynamics.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or scenarios where submodular rewards are naturally encountered in real-world applications?
- How does the proposed algorithm perform in more complex environments, such as those with higher-dimensional state and action spaces?
- Can the authors clarify the computational efficiency and complexity of the proposed methods, particularly in relation to the complexity of the submodular reward function?
- How does the proposed method compare to other approaches, such as those that use non-Markovian representations or different types of policy gradient methods?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed methods and discuss potential future directions for research in this area?
