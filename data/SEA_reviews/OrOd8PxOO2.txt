 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to learning a universal motion representation for humanoid control, leveraging a variational autoencoder (VAE) to encode the latent space. The methodology involves training a VAE on a large dataset of human motion to learn a prior, which is then used to generate human-like motion for various tasks. This approach is supported by extensive experiments, showing that the learned representations can be used to control a humanoid in various scenarios, including VR controller tracking and free-form motion tracking. Despite the innovative approach, concerns are raised about the novelty of the VAE architecture and the clarity of the methodology, particularly in the presentation of the training and testing datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant problem of learning a universal motion representation, which is a crucial step towards achieving more realistic and human-like motion in various applications.
- The methodology is clearly explained, with a detailed description of the training and testing datasets, and the results are supported by comprehensive ablation studies.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the supplementary materials provide additional insights into the methodology.
- The proposed method is straightforward and shows good performance in the VR controller tracking task, demonstrating its effectiveness in practical applications.
- The use of a variational autoencoder (VAE) to learn a latent space for human motion is innovative and potentially useful for generating more natural and varied motions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining the training and testing datasets, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the results.
- The novelty of the VAE architecture is questionable, as similar approaches have been used in previous works.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of latent space collapse, which is a significant problem in VAE-based methods.
- The performance of the proposed method is not consistently superior to existing methods, such as those based on physics-based motion representations.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, which limits the evaluation of its effectiveness and novelty.
- The paper suffers from some inconsistencies in the results and methodology, such as the performance of the PHC+ method and the use of different datasets for training and testing.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and differences between the training and testing datasets, especially in relation to the AMASS dataset?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and novelty?
- What is the specific role and impact of the variational bottleneck in the VAE architecture, and how does it contribute to the learning of motor skills?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process of the PHC+ method and explain why it achieves a 100% success rate?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of latent space collapse, and what steps are taken to prevent this in the VAE training?
- What are the implications of using a smaller latent space (32d) compared to other methods, and how does this affect the generation and diversity of the motions?
