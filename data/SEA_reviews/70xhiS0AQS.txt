 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TaskBench, a benchmark designed to evaluate large language models (LLMs) in task automation. TaskBench operates by first decomposing user instructions into sub-tasks, then invoking appropriate tools, and predicting parameters for those tools. This process is evaluated through a dataset generated using a "back-instruct" method, which involves generating user instructions based on tool graphs. The benchmark uses a variety of metrics to assess the performance of LLMs in task decomposition, tool invocation, and parameter prediction. The paper also includes a human evaluation component to validate the effectiveness of the benchmark.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces TaskBench, a novel benchmark for evaluating large language models (LLMs) in task automation, which addresses a critical and timely issue in the field.
- It proposes TaskEval, a set of metrics specifically designed to assess the performance of LLMs in task decomposition, tool invocation, and parameter prediction, providing a comprehensive evaluation framework.
- The paper includes a human evaluation component, which adds a valuable layer of validation to the results.
- The dataset construction process is detailed, providing a clear understanding of how the benchmark was created.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, making it accessible and understandable to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed benchmark, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- There is insufficient discussion on the similarities and differences between TaskBench and other existing benchmarks, which could help in positioning TaskBench within the existing landscape of benchmarks.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for LLMs to generate incorrect tool invocation graphs, which could significantly impact the results.
- The dataset and evaluation metrics are not sufficiently robust or diverse, raising concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive analysis of the generated data, which could provide insights into the quality and utility of the benchmark.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential biases in the benchmark, which could limit the validity of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the back-instruct method compare to other methods for generating user instructions based on tool graphs?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the tool graphs were constructed and how they were used to generate the user instructions?
- How does TaskBench compare to other existing benchmarks for evaluating LLMs in task automation?
- What are the potential biases in the benchmark, and how might these affect the results?
- How does the benchmark ensure that the LLMs are not just memorizing the tool graph to generate the correct user instructions?
- Could the authors provide more details on the human evaluation process, including the criteria used to select the samples and the number of human evaluators involved?
- How does the benchmark handle the potential for LLMs to generate incorrect tool invocation graphs?
