 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TreeDQN, a novel reinforcement learning method designed to learn branching heuristics for the Branch-and-Bound (B&B) algorithm in combinatorial optimization problems. The method views the variable selection task as a tree Markov Decision Process (MDP), utilizing a geometric mean loss function to optimize the size of the B&B tree. The authors claim that TreeDQN outperforms other RL methods and traditional heuristics in terms of tree size and execution time. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to similarities with existing methods like FMCTS, and the practical applicability of the method in real-world scenarios is not adequately addressed.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the proposed method and its application to the branching problem in combinatorial optimization.
- The use of geometric mean loss is a novel approach that effectively reduces the variance of tree sizes, enhancing the stability of learning.
- The paper includes a thorough evaluation of the method, demonstrating its effectiveness through empirical results and comparisons with other methods.
- The proposed method, TreeDQN, is simple and easy to implement, making it accessible for practical applications.
- The paper is structured well, with a clear introduction, background, and methodology, which facilitates understanding and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its practical applicability and scalability to larger, more complex problems.
- There is a lack of novelty in the approach, as it closely resembles existing methods like FMCTS, with similar theoretical underpinnings and practical implementations.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and scalability of the method, especially in comparison to other RL methods and traditional heuristics.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other RL methods for combinatorial optimization, which could have highlighted the advantages of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the learning process and the role of hyperparameters in the effectiveness of the method.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the tree size is not the primary metric of interest?
- Can the authors clarify the computational complexity and scalability of the method, particularly in comparison to other RL methods and traditional heuristics?
- What are the specific advantages of TreeDQN over other RL methods for combinatorial optimization, and how does it compare in terms of performance and efficiency?
- How does the method handle the variability in tree sizes, especially in cases where the tree size distribution is highly variable?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential avenues for future research to address these limitations?
