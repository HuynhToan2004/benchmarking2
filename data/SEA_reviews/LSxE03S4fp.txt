 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for imitator learning (ItorL), focusing on deriving an imitator module that can reconstruct task-specific policies from limited expert demonstrations, without requiring further fine-tuning. The proposed method, Demo-Attention Actor-Critic (DAAC), integrates imitation learning into a reinforcement learning paradigm, utilizing a demonstration-based attention mechanism to trace expert states effectively. The framework is evaluated in a new navigation benchmark and a robot environment, demonstrating its effectiveness in both seen and unseen tasks. Despite its innovative approach and empirical success, the paper is criticized for its clarity in presentation, particularly in the explanation of the reward function and the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and challenging topic, Imitator Learning (ItorL), which is significant for advancing the field of reinforcement learning (RL).
- The proposed Demo-Attention Actor-Critic (DAAC) method is innovative and well-motivated, with a strong theoretical foundation.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of DAAC across both seen and unseen tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The inclusion of a new navigation benchmark and a robot environment enhances the practical applicability and reproducibility of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in the explanation of the reward function, particularly how it is derived and how it influences the learning process.
- The experimental setup and the choice of baselines are not adequately justified, which could affect the perceived fairness and validity of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability in different environments and its scalability.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need correction to enhance the professionalism and readability of the paper.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalization ability of the DAAC method to unseen tasks, which is a critical aspect of the proposed framework.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the derivation and role of the reward function in the learning process?
- How does the DAAC method generalize to unseen tasks, and what are the specific conditions under which this generalization occurs?
- How does the performance of the DAAC method vary with the number of demonstrations, and what are the implications of this for practical applications?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of baselines and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- In the context of the navigation benchmark, how does the method perform when the agent's coordinate is not provided, and what are the implications of this for the general applicability of the method?
