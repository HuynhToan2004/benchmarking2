 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the HAZARD challenge, a new benchmark for evaluating decision-making abilities of embodied agents in dynamic environments. It features three disaster scenarios: fire, flood, and wind, which require agents to rescue valuable items while adapting to changing conditions. The benchmark utilizes a large language model (LLM) to aid in common sense reasoning and decision-making. The authors propose a novel LLM-based agent and conduct an in-depth analysis of its performance. The paper is praised for its innovative approach and the comprehensive evaluation of LLM-based agents, although concerns are raised about the limited scope of the disaster scenarios and the need for more diverse and realistic simulations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmark, HAZARD, which is specifically designed to evaluate the decision-making capabilities of embodied agents in dynamic environments.
- The authors have developed an LLM-based agent and conducted an in-depth analysis of its performance, highlighting the potential of large language models in embodied decision-making tasks.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The HAZARD benchmark provides a comprehensive evaluation of LLM-based agents, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The introduction of a new benchmark, HAZARD, offers a new perspective on how embodied agents can be evaluated, particularly in dynamic and challenging environments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The benchmark is limited in scope, focusing on only three disaster scenarios (fire, flood, and wind), which may not adequately represent the complexity and diversity of real-world disasters.
- The evaluation of the LLM-based agent is limited to these three scenarios, which may not fully demonstrate the agent's capabilities in more varied or realistic scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other LLM-based agents or decision-making methods, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the agent's performance.
- The physical simulation in the benchmark is limited, and the interaction between the agent and the environment is not fully explored.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the work, which is a significant oversight.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the three disaster scenarios and discuss the potential for including other types of disasters in future versions of the benchmark?
- How does the LLM-based agent perform in scenarios where the disaster conditions are not known in advance, and how does it adapt to new or unexpected conditions?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the LLM-based agent interacts with the environment and how it adapts to changing conditions?
- How does the benchmark ensure that the LLM-based agent is not just memorizing the training data and applying it to new scenarios without true understanding or generalization?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their work and how they plan to mitigate these risks?
