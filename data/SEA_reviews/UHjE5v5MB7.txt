 **Summary:**
The paper explores the phenomenon of "grokking" in deep learning models, focusing on how neural networks trained on algorithmic datasets, specifically modular arithmetic tasks, can achieve robust generalization despite memorizing incorrect labels. The study employs the inverse participation ratio (IPR) to quantify the degree of memorization and generalization, showing that regularization techniques like weight decay and dropout can influence the network's behavior. The authors also introduce a novel phase diagram to illustrate the network's dynamics during training and demonstrate how different regularization methods affect the memorization and generalization capabilities of the network. The paper further investigates the impact of regularization on the network's behavior, showing how it can shift the network from memorizing to generalizing.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and interesting phenomenon, "grokking," which is not well-studied in the literature.
- The authors provide a detailed and comprehensive analysis of the phenomenon, including a phase diagram and a novel metric, the inverse participation ratio (IPR), to quantify the degree of memorization and generalization.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a good balance of theory and empirical results.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are convincing, supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence.
- The study provides insights into the behavior of neural networks under different regularization methods, particularly weight decay and dropout, and their impact on memorization and generalization.
- The paper is the first to study the effect of label corruption on grokking, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's findings might not generalize well to other types of datasets or architectures, as it focuses primarily on modular arithmetic tasks and two-layer MLPs.
- The paper lacks a broader discussion on the implications of the findings for more complex or realistic datasets, which might not exhibit the same phenomena.
- The paper's focus on memorization and generalization might be too narrow, as it does not address other aspects of robustness, such as robustness to adversarial attacks or data distribution shifts.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the study, particularly the generalizability of the findings to other types of datasets and architectures.
- The paper could be improved by including more experiments to validate the claims made about the effects of regularization on the network's behavior.
- The paper's presentation could be clearer in some parts, particularly in the introduction and the discussion of the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and usage of the terms "coexistence," "partial inversion," and "full inversion" in the context of their study?
- How does the choice of the quadratic activation function affect the results, and could other activation functions yield different outcomes?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the analytical solution presented in the paper relates to the findings on the modular addition task?
- How do the authors address the potential issues of overfitting or underfitting in their experiments, especially in the context of the modular arithmetic task?
- Can the authors elaborate on the implications of their findings for other types of datasets or architectures, particularly those that do not exhibit the same phenomena as the modular arithmetic task?
- How does the paper's analysis of regularization techniques, such as weight decay and dropout, contribute to a better understanding of these methods in more complex or realistic settings?
