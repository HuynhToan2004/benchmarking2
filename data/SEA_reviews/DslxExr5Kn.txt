 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Actor-Predicts-Critic (APC) algorithm, which aims to address the non-stationarity issue in Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) by allowing agents to predict global representations from local observations during training. This is achieved by utilizing a centralized critic that encodes global observations into global representations, which are then predicted by the actor using local observations. The APC algorithm is tested across various benchmarks, including StarCraft II, Google Research Football, and Multi-Agent Mujoco, demonstrating improved performance over existing baselines. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods, and concerns are raised about its generalizability and the clarity of its contributions to the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) by focusing on the non-stationarity issue, which is crucial for the development of effective MARL algorithms.
- The proposed APC algorithm is well-motivated and well-explained, with a clear description of its components and their roles.
- The experiments conducted on various benchmarks, including StarCraft II, Google Research Football, and Multi-Agent Mujoco, demonstrate the effectiveness of the APC algorithm in improving performance over existing baselines.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, which is essential for academic research.
- The use of historical information to enhance the prediction of global representations is a novel approach that could potentially improve the performance of MARL algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the APC algorithm is limited as it primarily combines existing methods (MAT and CTDE) without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the specific contributions of the APC algorithm to the field of MARL, making it difficult to distinguish its unique advantages over existing methods.
- The experimental results, while showing some improvements, are not consistently superior across all benchmarks, and the improvements are sometimes marginal or not statistically significant.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational costs associated with the APC algorithm, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the APC algorithm with other relevant baselines, such as ACE and HAPPO, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of its performance.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the specific contributions of the APC algorithm to the field of MARL, particularly how it differs from and improves upon existing methods like MAT and CTDE?
- How does the APC algorithm handle the non-stationarity issue in MARL, and what are the implications of this approach in terms of algorithm stability and convergence?
- Why was the choice made to use GRU over other architectures like LSTM or RNN, and how does this impact the performance and stability of the APC algorithm?
- Can you provide more details on the computational costs associated with the APC algorithm, including training and inference times, and how these compare to other state-of-the-art MARL algorithms?
- How does the APC algorithm perform in scenarios where the global information is only available at training time, and what are the implications of this limitation in practical applications?
- Could you elaborate on the potential negative societal impacts of the APC algorithm, particularly in terms of fairness and strategic manipulation in multi-agent systems?
