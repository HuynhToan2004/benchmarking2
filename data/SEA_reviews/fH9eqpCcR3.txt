 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Multiple Physics Pretraining (MPP), a novel approach to pretraining physical surrogate models that can predict the dynamics of multiple heterogeneous physical systems simultaneously. MPP employs a transformer-based architecture to embed multiple physical systems into a shared embedding space, enabling autoregressive prediction of dynamics. The model is evaluated using datasets from PDEBench, demonstrating its ability to outperform task-specific baselines and showcase its transfer capabilities in low-data settings. Despite its innovative approach, concerns were raised about the novelty of the method, the lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, and the limited scope of the experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach for task-agnostic pretraining of physical surrogate models, which is significant for the field of scientific machine learning.
- The authors provide a comprehensive literature review, effectively positioning their work within the existing body of research.
- The proposed method demonstrates the ability to learn multiple types of physical systems and transfer this knowledge to new systems with limited training examples, showing potential for application in various fields.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed description of the architecture, training settings, and experimental details.
- The experimental results show that the pretrained models can outperform task-specific baselines and showcase their transfer capabilities in low-data settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the proposed method compares to existing works, particularly those that also employ transformer-based architectures for pretraining.
- The experiments are limited in scope, focusing primarily on fluid benchmarks and not adequately demonstrating the generalizability of the method across different types of physical systems.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the number of physical systems that can be effectively embedded into the shared embedding space.
- There is a lack of comprehensive ablation studies to support the design choices of the method, such as the use of axial attention and the choice of the number of systems for pretraining.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive comparisons with other methods, including those that use different architectures or training strategies, to better establish the advantages of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing works that use transformer-based architectures for pretraining?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of physical systems beyond those tested in the experiments?
- What are the implications of using different architectures or training strategies, and how do these compare to the proposed method in terms of performance and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the method, particularly how the number of physical systems affects the performance and the embedding capacity of the model?
- How does the method handle the transferability of learned representations to new physical systems, and what are the limitations of this transferability?
