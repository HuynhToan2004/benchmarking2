 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel watermarking method for large language models (LLMs) during the fine-tuning phase. The proposed method, named "Double-I watermark," embeds watermark information into the model without affecting its performance. The watermark is designed to be robust against various attacks and is imperceptible to users. The method is evaluated across different fine-tuning techniques and shows robustness against various attacks, including model quantization and pruning. The paper also discusses the challenges of watermarking LLMs and provides a theoretical analysis of the watermark's effectiveness. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the watermarking approach, especially in scenarios where the fine-tuning data is not under the direct control of the model owner.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a timely and significant problem of protecting the copyright of customized models in the fine-tuning phase of LLMs, which is crucial for business owners and developers.
- The proposed watermarking method is novel, robust, and imperceptible, making it effective in preventing unauthorized usage of models.
- The method is evaluated on various fine-tuning methods and shows robustness against attacks such as model quantization and pruning.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including a theoretical analysis of the watermark's effectiveness.
- The method is scalable and efficient, making it suitable for large models and real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practicality of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the fine-tuning data is not under the direct control of the model owner.
- The method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios is questionable, as it relies on the assumption that the model owner has full control over the fine-tuning data, which is not always the case.
- The method's robustness against attacks such as model inversion and textual inversion is not adequately discussed, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other existing watermarking methods for LLMs, which could provide a better understanding of its advantages and limitations.
- The method's reliance on specific data patterns (e.g., judge-type questions) could limit its applicability to other types of data or models, which may not follow the same pattern.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method ensure the watermark's effectiveness in real-world scenarios where the fine-tuning data is not under the direct control of the model owner?
- Can the method be adapted to other types of data or models that do not follow the same pattern as the "judge-type question" used in the current implementation?
- How does the method perform against other types of attacks, such as model inversion and textual inversion?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the watermark is verified, especially in scenarios where the model's output is not controlled by the model owner?
- How does the method ensure that the watermark remains robust and imperceptible over time, especially in scenarios where the model is continuously updated or fine-tuned?
