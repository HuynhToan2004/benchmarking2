 **Summary:**
The paper explores the problem of estimating Global Average Treatment Effects (GATE) in an identity fragmentation setting, where users interact with multiple devices, and the treatment effect on one device influences the outcome on another device. The authors propose a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) model to estimate the GATE by utilizing a generative model that factors the joint distribution of latent variables, including unobserved exposure and noisy exposure. This model is trained on a simulated dataset, demonstrating its ability to estimate GATE effectively, although its performance on real-world datasets is not evaluated. The paper also discusses the challenges of estimating GATE in the presence of device fragmentation, which is a significant issue in online A/B testing.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem in online A/B testing, focusing on the estimation of global treatment effects in a fragmented identity setting.
- The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the problem and the proposed solution, supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for readers in the field.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it outperforms existing estimators in terms of bias and robustness.
- The use of a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) model to estimate treatment effects in the presence of identity fragmentation is innovative and addresses a critical issue in online experimentation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions, such as the assumption that the exposure is additive and the neighborhood superset, are not adequately justified or explained, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks empirical results on real-world datasets, which could have validated the effectiveness of the proposed method in more practical settings.
- The paper's experimental design is not robust enough to support the claims made, particularly the claim that the proposed method outperforms existing estimators.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made, especially regarding the additive exposure and neighborhood superset, which are crucial for the validity of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed justification for the assumptions made in the paper, particularly regarding the additive exposure and neighborhood superset?
- How does the proposed method perform when the assumptions are violated?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and under what conditions might it not be applicable?
- Could the authors provide empirical results on real-world datasets to validate the effectiveness of their method in practical scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
