 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TRAM, a comprehensive benchmark designed to evaluate the temporal reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) across various tasks. TRAM consists of ten distinct datasets, each with multiple subtasks, totaling 526,668 questions. The benchmark includes foundational temporal understanding tasks, temporal interpretation and computation tasks, and advanced temporal and conceptual understanding tasks. The authors conducted experiments using leading LLMs such as GPT-4, GPT-3.5, and BERT-style models, showing that while LLMs have made progress, they still lag behind human performance. The paper provides a detailed analysis of the models' performance and identifies areas for improvement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces TRAM, a comprehensive benchmark for temporal reasoning tasks, which includes 10 datasets and 38 subtasks, providing a unified framework for evaluating the temporal reasoning capabilities of LLMs.
- TRAM covers a broad spectrum of temporal reasoning tasks, ranging from foundational temporal concepts to intricate temporal interpretations, and includes a diverse set of datasets from various sources.
- The paper presents a detailed evaluation of leading LLMs such as GPT-4, GPT-3.5, Llama-2, and BERT-style models, providing insights into their strengths and limitations in temporal reasoning tasks.
- The authors conduct a thorough analysis of the models' performance and identify consistent challenges in temporal reasoning faced by current LLMs, highlighting the need for further research to improve the capabilities of LLMs in understanding and reasoning about time.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise presentation of the benchmark, tasks, and experimental results, making it accessible and understandable for readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the benchmark, such as the potential for LLMs to resort to random guessing due to the multiple-choice question format, which could mislead performance evaluations.
- The evaluation of the models' performance is limited to a subset of the test set, which may not fully reflect the models' capabilities across the entire dataset.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the potential biases in the datasets used and does not explore the impact of different model sizes or architectures on performance.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the benchmark, such as the potential for LLMs to resort to random guessing due to the multiple-choice question format, which could mislead performance evaluations.
- The paper does not provide insights into how the performance of LLMs could be improved, nor does it discuss potential biases in the datasets used.

**Questions:**
- How were the 5 questions per category in the few-shot setting selected, and how representative are these questions of the overall dataset?
- Could you provide more details on the training data used for the BERT-style models, and how it was selected from the original datasets?
- How do you address the potential for LLMs to resort to random guessing due to the multiple-choice question format, and how does this affect the performance evaluations?
- What are the potential biases in the datasets used, and how do these biases affect the evaluation of the models' performance?
- How do different model sizes or architectures impact the performance of LLMs on temporal reasoning tasks, and could you provide insights into how the performance could be improved?
