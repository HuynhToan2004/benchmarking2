 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for generating directed graphs using a denoising autoencoder-based approach. The method leverages the global structure of directed graphs through their Laplacian dynamics, employing a heat equation-based noise injection to corrupt the adjacency matrix. The decoder reconstructs the corrupted representation by utilizing the topological information of the graph. The approach is validated through experiments on synthetic datasets, demonstrating its ability to generate directed graphs that follow the distribution of the training dataset, even in multimodal cases. The paper also explores the application of heat kernels to non-symmetric cases, providing a theoretical foundation for the method's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to generating directed graphs, which is a significant advancement in the field of graph generation.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear and detailed explanations of the proposed approach and its theoretical foundations.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can generate directed graphs that follow the distribution of the training dataset, even in multimodal cases.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the proposed approach, including a theoretical foundation based on heat kernels and a detailed discussion of the experimental setup and results.
- The use of heat kernels to generate directed graphs is a novel application that extends the traditional use of heat kernels in undirected graph generation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the motivation behind the choice of the heat equation for generating noise and the rationale behind using a heat kernel in directed graphs.
- The experimental results are limited to small-scale datasets, which may not convincingly demonstrate the scalability or generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the model, such as the specifics of the denoising autoencoder used and the training process.
- The theoretical contributions, while interesting, are not fully integrated into the main text and are relegated to the appendix, which may limit their accessibility and impact.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with existing baselines, particularly in terms of performance metrics and scalability.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the motivation behind the choice of the heat equation for generating noise and how it enhances the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle the generation of directed graphs with a large number of nodes, and what are the computational challenges associated with scaling the method to larger datasets?
- Can you clarify the role of the heat equation in the proposed method and how it contributes to the generation of directed graphs?
- How does the method perform when compared to existing baselines, particularly in terms of performance metrics and scalability?
- Could you provide more details on the implementation of the denoising autoencoder and the training process, including any specific hyperparameters used and their impact on the model's performance?
