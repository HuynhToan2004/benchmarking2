 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TrASPr, a novel method for predicting RNA splicing outcomes using a combination of transformer models and Bayesian optimization (BO). TrASPr leverages a pre-trained BERT model for splice site detection and integrates additional features to predict splicing outcomes. The BO algorithm, BOS, utilizes TrASPr to optimize splicing outcomes based on user-defined constraints. The methodology is evaluated on various datasets, including GTEx and MGP, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods. The paper also explores the application of TrASPr in designing RNA sequences with specific splicing characteristics. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in explaining the Bayesian optimization algorithm and its reliance on pre-trained models, which might limit its generalizability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel method for predicting RNA splicing outcomes using a combination of transformer models and Bayesian optimization (BO), which is a novel approach in the field.
- The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method through rigorous evaluations, showing improvements over existing methods in various datasets.
- The paper is well-written and provides a clear motivation for the research, with detailed explanations of the methodology and experimental setups.
- The use of Bayesian optimization for splicing outcome design is a significant contribution, potentially enhancing the practical applicability of the research.
- The authors have provided code and data, which is a valuable resource for the community and facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the Bayesian optimization algorithm used, particularly in how it is adapted for the splicing design task and how it compares to other methods.
- There is a potential issue with the generalizability of the model due to its reliance on pre-trained models like BERT, which might not capture the full complexity of the splicing process.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons with existing methods, including more recent and relevant works such as those by Zeng and Li (2022) and Jha et al. (2017).
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different datasets and the potential for overfitting due to the use of pre-trained models.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed discussions on the computational costs and scalability of the proposed methods, especially in large-scale applications.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the Bayesian optimization algorithm is adapted for the splicing design task and how it compares to other methods?
- How does the performance of the proposed method vary when applied to different datasets, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
- Could you clarify the role and impact of the pre-trained models, particularly BERT, on the performance and generalizability of the proposed method?
- How do the authors address the potential for overfitting in the model, especially given the reliance on pre-trained models?
- Could you provide more details on the computational costs and scalability of the proposed methods, especially in large-scale applications?
- How does the proposed method handle the complexity of splicing regulation, particularly in regions that are not well-defined or have low information content?
