 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for detecting text generated by large language models (LLMs) using a Bayesian surrogate model. The proposed method selects typical samples based on Bayesian uncertainty and interpolates scores from these samples to other samples, improving query efficiency and reducing the number of queries needed compared to existing methods like DetectGPT. The approach uses a Gaussian process model to map samples to LLM probability and quantify uncertainty, which is crucial for selecting typical samples. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive experiments on various datasets, showing improved performance and efficiency under a low query budget.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem of detecting LLM-generated text, which is crucial for preventing misuse and ensuring trust in AI-generated content.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, simple, and effective, using a Bayesian surrogate model to select typical samples based on Bayesian uncertainty, which is a novel approach in this field.
- The method is shown to be more efficient than existing methods like DetectGPT, requiring fewer queries to the source LLM and achieving similar performance with up to 2 times fewer queries and 3.7% higher AUROC at a query number of 5.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making the complex methodology accessible to a broader audience.
- The experiments are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and LLM architectures.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the choice of typical samples and the selection criteria, which could affect the reliability and robustness of the method.
- The method's performance and efficiency depend on the quality and diversity of the typical samples, which might not be consistently available or representative.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis on the impact of different perturbation models and the selection of hyperparameters, which could affect the generalizability and applicability of the method.
- The method's reliance on the Bayesian surrogate model might limit its adaptability to different types of LLMs and text generation scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and scalability of the method, especially in scenarios with large datasets or complex LLMs.
- The method's evaluation is limited to specific datasets and LLMs, which might not fully demonstrate its effectiveness in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more insights into how typical samples are selected and the criteria used for their selection?
- How does the choice of perturbation model affect the method's performance, and is there a way to optimize this choice based on the specific LLM being used?
- How sensitive is the method to the choice of hyperparameters, and what strategies can be used to optimize their selection?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations and challenges of using the Bayesian surrogate model in different scenarios, and how these might be addressed?
- How does the method perform under different computational constraints, and what are the potential scalability issues that might arise with larger datasets or more complex LLMs?
- Are there any plans to evaluate the method on a broader range of LLMs and text generation scenarios to validate its generalizability?
