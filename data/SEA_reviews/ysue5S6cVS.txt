 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel sampling strategy for backdoor attacks, named Confidence-Driven Boundary Sampling (CBS), which selects samples with lower confidence scores to improve the stealthiness of backdoor attacks. The method leverages confidence scores to identify samples near the decision boundary, potentially reducing the detectability of backdoor attacks. The authors argue that this approach can enhance the resilience of backdoor attacks against various defense mechanisms, as demonstrated through empirical evaluations on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets using different backdoor attack methods. The paper is well-structured and provides a thorough analysis of the proposed method, although it has been critiqued for its limited novelty and the lack of theoretical justification for its effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The idea of using confidence scores to identify boundary samples is novel and interesting, offering a new perspective on backdoor attacks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, comparing it with existing methods and demonstrating its effectiveness through empirical results.
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of the proposed method, including visualizations and theoretical analysis, which supports the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is structured clearly, with a logical flow that makes it easy to follow and understand the authors' arguments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as similar approaches have been previously explored in adversarial robustness, which could undermine the perceived innovation of the work.
- The paper lacks a detailed theoretical analysis and justification for why the proposed method is effective, which could strengthen the argument for its effectiveness.
- The paper's focus on image classification tasks limits the generalizability of the findings to other types of data, such as text or speech.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly robust, with some results showing inconsistencies or insignificant improvements over existing methods.
- The paper's assumption that the attacker can selectively poison samples is unrealistic and not adequately justified, which could limit the practical applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of trigger detection, which is a critical aspect of backdoor attacks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify why the proposed method is effective and provide a detailed theoretical analysis to support its claims?
- How does the proposed method perform when the attacker does not have access to the victim model's training dataset?
- Can the authors explain the inconsistencies in the experimental results, particularly the lower success rates observed with some defense mechanisms?
- How does the proposed method perform when the attacker cannot selectively poison samples, as is typically the case in real-world scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more detailed justification for the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the assumption that the attacker can selectively poison samples?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use different sampling strategies, such as those that use high-confidence samples?
