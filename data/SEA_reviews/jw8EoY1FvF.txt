 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Delayed Local-SGD (DLSGD), a framework designed to enhance training efficiency in distributed and federated learning settings. DLSGD allows clients to train using outdated models, reducing the need for synchronization and improving scalability. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that DLSGD achieves linear speedup with respect to the number of participating clients, ensuring convergence rates comparable to traditional Local-SGD methods. The paper also includes numerical experiments to validate the efficiency and scalability of DLSGD in real-world scenarios. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as similar ideas have been explored in previous works, and for the lack of a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear and concise presentation that effectively communicates the main ideas and results.
- The proposed Delayed Local-SGD (DLSGD) framework is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, making it accessible for practical applications.
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the DLSGD framework, demonstrating convergence rates that are comparable to those of classic Local-SGD methods for solving nonconvex problems.
- The paper includes numerical experiments to validate the efficiency and scalability of the DLSGD framework in real-world scenarios, which helps to substantiate the theoretical claims.
- The authors have successfully addressed the straggler effect in Local-SGD, a significant issue in distributed and federated learning, by allowing clients to train using outdated models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as similar ideas have been explored in previous works, particularly in the context of asynchronous algorithms and stale gradients.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art algorithms, which could help to establish the relative performance and efficiency of the DLSGD framework.
- The theoretical analysis assumes that clients can be arbitrarily delayed, which might not be realistic in practical scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational and communication costs associated with the use of receive/send buffers, which could impact the practical implementation of the DLSGD framework.
- The convergence results are not significantly different from existing methods, and the paper does not provide a detailed discussion on how the proposed method addresses the straggler effect compared to existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art algorithms to establish the relative performance and efficiency of the DLSGD framework?
- How does the DLSGD framework perform in scenarios where clients have varying levels of heterogeneity in their local objective functions?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of different client participation schemes on the performance of the DLSGD framework?
- How does the DLSGD framework handle scenarios where clients have varying levels of computational and communication capabilities?
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the computational and communication costs associated with the use of receive/send buffers in the DLSGD framework?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of the proposed method on the straggler effect compared to existing methods?
