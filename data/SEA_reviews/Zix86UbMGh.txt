 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ProofNet, a benchmark designed for autoformalization and formal proving of undergraduate-level mathematics. It comprises 371 examples, each containing a formal theorem statement in Lean 3, a natural language theorem statement, and a natural language proof. The dataset is derived from undergraduate mathematics textbooks, focusing on topics such as real and complex analysis, linear algebra, abstract algebra, and topology. The paper also introduces proofGPT, a language model trained on the ProofNet dataset, and demonstrates its capabilities through experiments. The paper highlights the challenges in formalizing mathematical statements and proposes techniques to enhance the performance of language models in this domain.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmark, ProofNet, which is a valuable resource for the community, providing a well-curated dataset of 371 examples.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The paper is the first to propose autoformalization of undergraduate mathematics, which is a significant step towards advancing the field.
- The introduction of proofGPT, a language model trained on the ProofNet dataset, demonstrates the potential of using large language models for theorem proving.
- The paper is well-motivated, focusing on the autoformalization of undergraduate-level mathematics, which is a critical step towards developing a large language model that can reason about mathematics.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the results, which is helpful for understanding the capabilities and limitations of the models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the performance of proofGPT, with limited testing on different models and datasets.
- The paper does not discuss the potential societal impacts of the work, which could be a significant consideration for future applications of the technology.
- The paper's focus on undergraduate-level mathematics might limit the generalizability of the findings to more complex, research-level mathematics.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the results might apply to more complex mathematical problems, which could be a significant limitation for the practical applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on how the results might be used to guide the development of future systems, which could be a critical consideration for advancing the field.

**Questions:**
- How do the results of proofGPT on undergraduate-level mathematics compare to its performance on more complex, research-level mathematics?
- Can you discuss the potential societal impacts of the work, especially in terms of how it might be used to advance mathematics education or research?
- How might the results be used to guide the development of future systems for autoformalization and formal proving of undergraduate-level mathematics?
- Are there any plans to expand the dataset to include more complex, research-level mathematics, and how might this affect the performance of proofGPT?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the training and testing data used for proofGPT?
- How do the authors ensure the reliability and accuracy of the formalizations generated by the models, especially in cases where the models might make errors?
