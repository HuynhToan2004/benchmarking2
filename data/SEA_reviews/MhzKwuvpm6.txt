 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework called Reinforced Imitation Learning (RILe) that integrates adversarial imitation learning and inverse reinforcement learning to train a student agent using expert trajectories. This approach involves a teacher agent that learns a reward function from expert trajectories, which is then used to train the student agent, while a discriminator determines whether the student's actions align with expert trajectories. The method is tested on both Atari and MuJoCo environments, demonstrating improvements over traditional methods like GAIL and AIRL. However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in presentation, missing details in the experimental setup, and a lack of comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by combining adversarial imitation learning and inverse reinforcement learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method is well-explained and easy to understand, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods like GAIL and AIRL.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the differences between the proposed method and existing methods, particularly in terms of the novelty and advantages over existing approaches.
- The experimental setup is not clearly described, making it difficult to replicate the results and understand the conditions under which the experiments were conducted.
- There are missing details in the experimental results, such as the number of expert trajectories used and the performance of the method with different numbers of trajectories.
- The paper suffers from poor writing quality, with numerous typos, grammatical errors, and unclear figures that hinder understanding.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods, which limits the evaluation of its effectiveness and novelty.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between their method and existing methods, particularly in terms of novelty and advantages?
- How does the proposed method handle the stability issues mentioned, especially in complex environments?
- Why was the decision made to use only one expert trajectory, and how does this affect the performance of the method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the number of expert trajectories used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the method perform with different numbers of expert trajectories, and what are the implications of using different numbers of trajectories?
- Can the authors provide more clarity on the figures and equations used in the paper to improve understanding?
