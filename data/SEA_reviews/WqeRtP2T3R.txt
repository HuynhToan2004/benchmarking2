 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for zero-shot classification that leverages inferred attributes to encode and account for diversity within a class, improving upon the traditional one-vector-per-class paradigm. This approach is validated on multiple datasets and shown to outperform standard zero-shot classification methods. The method involves using a large language model to generate attributes that describe subpopulations within classes, which are then used to compute a similarity score for each test image. The paper also explores the trade-offs between overall accuracy and accuracy on the worst classes, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in handling diverse and atypical instances.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem in the field of zero-shot classification, focusing on the limitations of the traditional one-vector-per-class paradigm.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and well-motivated, with a focus on improving performance on the hardest classes and subpopulations.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed and clear figures that aid in understanding the proposed method.
- The methodology is validated on multiple datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving performance over standard zero-shot classification.
- The paper provides a novel approach to handling diversity within a class, which is a significant issue in zero-shot classification.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its method from existing works, such as CHiLS, which also uses a similar approach to attribute inference and nonlinear prediction consolidation.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definition and measurement of diversity within classes, which could affect the reliability and reproducibility of the results.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to datasets with ground-truth attributes, which may not generalize well to other datasets without such attributes.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of fairness and bias.
- The method's scalability and computational efficiency are not thoroughly evaluated, which could limit its practical application in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of their method with CHiLS, particularly in terms of the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed attributes and nonlinear prediction consolidation?
- How does the paper define and measure diversity within classes, and how does this definition affect the results and conclusions drawn from the study?
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability and computational efficiency of the proposed method, especially in terms of its practical applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the method handle the potential for bias and fairness issues, particularly in terms of the attributes used for prediction and their impact on different subpopulations?
- Can the authors provide more examples or case studies to demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in handling diverse and atypical instances, and how this translates into real-world applications?
