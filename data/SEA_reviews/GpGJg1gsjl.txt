 **Summary:**
The paper investigates active learning (AL) on graph-structured data, specifically focusing on uncertainty sampling (US) for node classification. It introduces a novel benchmark for US approaches, distinguishing between epistemic and aleatoric uncertainties, and provides theoretical analysis on the alignment of US with AL. The paper also proposes a new ground-truth Bayesian uncertainty measure based on the data-generating process, which is validated through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets. Despite the theoretical contributions, the paper faces criticism for its lack of novelty in uncertainty estimation and its reliance on outdated baselines. Additionally, the practical applicability of the proposed uncertainty estimation method is questioned due to its high computational complexity.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and detailed theoretical analysis.
- The authors introduce a new benchmark for uncertainty sampling (US) approaches in active learning (AL) on graphs, which is the first of its kind.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of uncertainty estimation and its alignment with active learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed ground-truth Bayesian uncertainty estimates are novel and theoretically sound, contributing to the understanding of uncertainty in AL on graphs.
- The paper addresses the important issue of uncertainty estimation in AL, which is often overlooked in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty in its uncertainty estimation method, which is based on existing models like the Contextual Stochastic Block Model (CSBM).
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art (SOTA) uncertainty estimation methods for graphs, which could have strengthened the argument for the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and practical applicability.
- The paper relies on outdated baselines, such as the GEEM method, which has been surpassed by more recent methods in terms of performance.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the proposed method could be applied in practical scenarios, which limits the practical relevance of the theoretical contributions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art uncertainty estimation methods for graphs, particularly in terms of computational complexity and practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data-generating process is not fully known or accurately modeled?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of the theoretical contributions, particularly in terms of how the proposed uncertainty estimation method could be applied in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and practical applicability with other methods that aim to disentangle epistemic and aleatoric uncertainties in AL on graphs?
- Is there a possibility to extend the proposed method to other types of data beyond graphs, such as tabular or textual data?
