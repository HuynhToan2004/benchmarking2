 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to dataset distillation in reinforcement learning (RL) contexts, termed "behavior distillation," which aims to synthesize a small dataset of state-action pairs that can train an RL agent to perform competitive tasks without requiring access to expert data. The method, HaDES, employs a meta-evolutionary outer loop to optimize the synthetic dataset and a supervised learning inner loop for behavioral cloning. The approach is demonstrated to generalize across different architectures and hyperparameters, and it also shows potential in multi-task agent training and supervised dataset distillation. However, the paper's scope is somewhat limited by its focus on a narrow set of environments, and the method's computational cost and scalability are noted as significant challenges.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to dataset distillation in reinforcement learning (RL) contexts, which is an interesting and potentially impactful area of research.
- The proposed method, HaDES, is well-motivated and the paper provides a thorough analysis of its components and hyperparameters.
- The experiments are well-designed and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method across a variety of tasks, including multi-task agent training and supervised dataset distillation.
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the methodology and results, and includes a detailed discussion on the limitations of the approach.
- The method shows potential in reducing the computational cost of training RL agents, particularly in environments where expert data is not available.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is somewhat limited as it primarily focuses on a narrow set of environments, which may not generalize well to other, more complex environments.
- The computational cost of the method is significant, particularly in environments with high-dimensional observations, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could help to better understand the relative advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.
- The paper's claims about the method's ability to generalize to different architectures and hyperparameters are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- The method's dependence on the number of parameters in the evolved policy limits its scalability, particularly in pixel-based environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the approach and how these might impact its practical applicability.

**Questions:**

- How does the method ensure that the synthetic datasets are not biased towards specific architectures or hyperparameters?
- Could you elaborate on the method's ability to generalize to different architectures and hyperparameters, particularly in light of the experimental results presented?
