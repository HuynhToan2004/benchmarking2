 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for backdoor trigger inversion (BTI) that decouples benign features from backdoor features, aiming to enhance the efficiency and accuracy of backdoor removal. This methodology is designed to address the limitations of existing BTI methods, which often struggle with generating triggers that closely resemble those used by adversaries. The proposed methodology is evaluated across multiple datasets and attack scenarios, showing promising results in terms of backdoor removal and pre-processing-based defenses. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in certain sections, particularly the methodology behind the decoupling of benign features and the generalizability of the results across different datasets and model architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the methodologies and results presented.
- The proposed method of decoupling benign features is novel and addresses a significant limitation of existing backdoor trigger inversion (BTI) methods.
- The paper includes comprehensive evaluations on multiple datasets and attack scenarios, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in backdoor removal and pre-processing-based defenses.
- The experiments are extensive, including both backdoor-removal and pre-processing-based defenses, which are crucial for practical applications.
- The paper provides a clear motivation and detailed explanations of the methodologies used, which helps in understanding the proposed solutions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the decoupling process, particularly how the benign features are separated from the backdoor features, which could be a critical point of interest for readers.
- The generalizability of the results is questionable due to the limited testing on a subset of ImageNet and the use of only ResNet-18 models.
- The paper does not discuss the potential for the backdoor generator to be used for malicious purposes, which could be a significant concern.
- There are inconsistencies in the presentation of results, particularly in the reporting of benign accuracy (BA) and attack success rate (ASR) across different tables, which could confuse the reader.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential negative societal impacts and the generalizability of the results across different datasets and model architectures.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the decoupling process, particularly how the benign features are separated from the backdoor features?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other datasets and model architectures, such as those used in other studies?
- Could the authors clarify the inconsistencies in the reporting of BA and ASR across different tables?
- Is there a possibility that the backdoor generator could be used for malicious purposes, and how does the paper address this potential risk?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of efficiency and effectiveness with other state-of-the-art methods, especially those that use different model architectures or datasets?
