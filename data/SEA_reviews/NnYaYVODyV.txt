 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel self-supervised learning approach using a perceptual group tokenizer (PGT) that groups input patches to refine feature representations, aiming to achieve state-of-the-art performance on ImageNet-1K with a linear probe. The PGT model iteratively hypothesizes contexts for pixels or superpixels, refining feature representations through a series of grouping operations. This method, while showing competitive performance, is noted for its adaptive computation and interpretability, although it is computationally expensive and may not outperform the latest state-of-the-art methods. The paper also explores the use of different sampling methods and the impact of group token dimensions on model performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel architecture, the Perceptual Group Tokenizer (PGT), which effectively groups tokens for feature refinement, achieving state-of-the-art performance on ImageNet-1K with a linear probe.
- The PGT model's design, including multi-grouping with multi-seeding and adaptive computation without re-training, is innovative and well-explained.
- The paper is well-written, with clear descriptions of the PGT model and its components, making it accessible and understandable.
- The experiments are comprehensive, including ablation studies that provide insights into the model's design choices and performance under different conditions.
- The paper demonstrates the interpretability of the model through visualizations of attention maps, showing the model's ability to learn meaningful features.
- The PGT model's adaptive computation capability allows for customization of the number of group tokens during inference, enhancing its applicability in various scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the PGT model with the latest state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of its performance relative to current advancements.
- The paper's claim of adaptive computation might be overstated, as the model's performance does not significantly vary with different group token numbers during inference.
- The computational cost of the PGT model is high, which could limit its practical application in resource-constrained environments.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the PGT model, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper's novelty is questioned, as similar approaches using grouping operations for visual representation learning have been explored previously.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which is a significant concern in deep learning models.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between the PGT model and other models that use grouping operations for visual representation learning, particularly in terms of novelty and performance?
- How does the PGT model compare in terms of computational efficiency and performance with other state-of-the-art models, especially in resource-constrained environments?
- What are the specific advantages of the PGT model over other models in terms of interpretability and adaptive computation?
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the PGT model and how these might affect its practical application?
- How does the PGT model handle the issue of overfitting, and what measures have been taken to mitigate this risk?
- The paper mentions the use of different sampling methods; could the authors provide more details on how these methods affect the model's performance and interpretability?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the model's training process and how it might be optimized for better performance and efficiency?
