 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Variational Disentangled Cross-domain Knowledge Alignment Method (VDKA) for multimodal recommendation, focusing on cross-domain knowledge transfer and domain-specific representation learning. VDKA utilizes a variational multimodal graph attention encoder, variational optimization objectives, and disentangled representation objectives to enhance the accuracy of domain representation. The methodology involves adversarial learning to align knowledge across domains, aiming to improve the effectiveness of multimodal recommendation systems. The paper presents experimental results on multiple datasets, demonstrating the superiority of VDKA over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, the clarity of the writing, and the adequacy of the experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant problem of cross-domain multimodal recommendation, which is crucial for enhancing the performance of recommendation systems.
- The proposed method, Variational Disentangled Cross-domain Knowledge Alignment (VDKA), is well-articulated and innovative, integrating variational autoencoder, graph attention encoder, and adversarial learning to address the challenges of multimodal recommendation.
- The paper is well-organized, providing a clear and comprehensive explanation of the methodology, including the use of variational multimodal graph attention encoders, variational optimization objectives, and disentangled representation objectives.
- The experimental results are robust and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing significant improvements over existing methods in terms of performance metrics such as Hit Rate (HR), Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG), and Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR).
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily combines existing techniques without introducing significant new methodologies or insights.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential negative societal impacts.
- The writing quality needs improvement, with issues such as inconsistent notation, unclear definitions, and grammatical errors.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, with a lack of comparison to state-of-the-art models and a limited scope of experiments, focusing on only two datasets.
- There is a lack of detailed explanation and justification for the design choices, such as the use of specific components like the variational multimodal graph attention encoder and the adversarial learning approach.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive literature review, missing out on recent and relevant works in the field of multimodal recommendation.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and coherence, with some sections being hard to follow due to complex notation and unclear explanations.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the specific design choices of the variational multimodal graph attention encoder and the adversarial learning approach?
- How does the proposed method handle the distribution discrepancy between different domains, and what measures are taken to ensure effective knowledge transfer?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the hyperparameters used in the model, particularly their sensitivity and optimal values?
- How does the proposed method compare to state-of-the-art models in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Could the authors expand on the experimental validation to include more datasets and a broader range of baseline models?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how are these addressed or mitigated?
