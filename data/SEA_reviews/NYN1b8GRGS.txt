 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GIM, a self-training framework that leverages internet videos to learn generalizable image matching models, addressing the challenge of limited scalability in existing data construction pipelines. GIM trains models on standard domain-specific datasets and enhances them with complementary matching methods, creating dense labels on nearby frames of novel videos. The framework's effectiveness is demonstrated through the creation of the ZEB benchmark, which evaluates the cross-domain generalization performance of different methods. GIM's application to various architectures shows improvements in zero-shot performance and cross-domain generalization, particularly in scenarios like 3D reconstruction and visual localization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem by focusing on the generalization of image matching models, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The proposed method, GIM, is well-motivated and effectively addresses the problem of limited scalability in existing data construction pipelines.
- The creation of the ZEB benchmark is a significant contribution, providing a valuable resource for evaluating the cross-domain generalization performance of different methods.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and architectures.
- The paper provides a novel approach by using internet videos as a data source, which is a more diverse and practical alternative to traditional datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the specifics of the dataset used, such as the number of videos and the diversity of scenes, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper could provide more insights into the efficiency and practicality of using internet videos as a data source, including the computational costs and the availability of high-quality video data.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the failures or limitations of the proposed method, which would help in understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of domain-shift in the data, which is a significant challenge in real-world applications.
- The paper could provide more detailed ablation studies to clarify the impact of different components of the proposed method, such as label propagation and data augmentation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the dataset used, including the number of videos, the diversity of scenes, and the quality of the data?
- How does the proposed method handle domain-shift in the data, and what strategies are used to mitigate the effects of this shift?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational costs associated with using internet videos as a data source?
- How does the proposed method compare to other self-training approaches, and what are the specific advantages of using GIM over these alternatives?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the failure cases of the proposed method, and discuss potential strategies for improving the robustness of the model in these scenarios?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of data diversity, particularly in scenarios where the model is trained on a limited set of video data?
