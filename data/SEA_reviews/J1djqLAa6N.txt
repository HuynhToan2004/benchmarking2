 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for computing higher-order derivatives in deep equilibrium layers (DEQ) to enhance the efficiency of score matching models. By integrating DEQ into score matching models, the authors aim to reduce memory requirements and improve computational efficiency, which is crucial for high-dimensional data and complex models. The methodology leverages implicit differentiation and phantom gradients to compute these derivatives, demonstrating significant improvements in memory usage and computational costs over existing methods. Empirical evaluations across various models and datasets showcase the effectiveness of the proposed approach, particularly in density estimation and generative modeling tasks. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the approach, the depth of theoretical analysis, and the clarity of presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, providing a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method.
- The methodology is well-motivated and supported by strong theoretical foundations, particularly the use of implicit differentiation and phantom gradients for computing higher-order derivatives.
- The empirical evaluations are comprehensive and robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various models and datasets.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in score matching models, which is the high computational and memory costs associated with Hessian computations.
- The proposed method is novel and shows significant improvements in memory usage and computational efficiency over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of data and the potential impact on model performance.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as the use of implicit differentiation and phantom gradients in DEQ is already well-known.
- The theoretical analysis provided is not sufficiently deep, particularly in the area of convergence guarantees and the complexity of the implicit differentiation process.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed experimental results and a broader range of datasets to strengthen the empirical validation of the proposed method.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved to better highlight the contributions and the novelty of the work.
- The paper could provide more clarity on the implementation details and the practical implications of the proposed method, especially in terms of its integration with existing models and its scalability to larger datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of their work beyond the known techniques in DEQ literature?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational and memory efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in large-scale applications?
- What are the potential limitations or challenges in applying the proposed method to different types of data or models?
- Could the authors provide more detailed theoretical analysis, particularly on the convergence guarantees and the complexity of the implicit differentiation process?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data distribution is not well-behaved or when the model complexity is high?
- Can the authors provide additional experimental results or comparisons with other models to further validate the effectiveness of their approach?
