 **Summary:**
The paper explores the randomized parameter-sharing (RPS) technique for model compression, focusing on its advantages over pruning-based methods. It introduces two new techniques, STABLE-RPS, to address stability issues in RPS and to ensure Pareto-continuity. The authors compare RPS with various pruning methods and show that RPS can achieve higher accuracy at lower compression rates. Theoretical insights and empirical results support the superiority of RPS over pruning, particularly in scenarios with high compression rates. The paper also discusses the limitations of RPS and provides suggestions for future research.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to model compression using randomized parameter-sharing (RPS), which is a promising alternative to traditional pruning-based methods.
- The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of RPS, demonstrating its superiority over pruning in certain scenarios.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and includes a comprehensive literature review, making it accessible and informative for readers.
- The experimental results are robust, showing that RPS can achieve competitive accuracy even at high compression rates, which is a significant advantage over pruning-based methods.
- The theoretical insights provided help in understanding the underlying mechanisms of RPS, enhancing the reader's comprehension of the methodology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with the latest pruning methods, which could have strengthened the argument for the superiority of RPS.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, especially in terms of accuracy, and the improvements are sometimes marginal.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational overhead of RPS, which could be a significant drawback compared to pruning methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of RPS, such as the potential for reduced accuracy and the computational cost of parameter sharing.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of RPS, including its scalability and applicability to different types of neural networks.
- The theoretical contributions are somewhat limited, and the paper could benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs between different parameters in the RPS method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with the latest pruning methods to clarify the superiority of RPS?
- How does the computational overhead of RPS compare to pruning methods, especially in terms of training time and hardware requirements?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of RPS, such as its scalability and applicability to different types of neural networks?
- Is there a potential for RPS to be adapted for use in training models, rather than just for model compression?
- How does the parameter sharing technique compare to other methods like low-rank factorization and quantization, especially in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between different parameters in the RPS method, particularly in relation to the compression rate and accuracy?
