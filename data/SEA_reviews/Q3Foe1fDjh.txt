 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel probabilistic framework for hierarchical clustering, focusing on the optimization of expected scores such as the Dasgupta cost and Tree-Sampling Divergence. This approach, termed Expected Probabilistic Hierarchies (EPH), leverages differentiable hierarchy sampling and unbiased subgraph sampling to optimize these metrics. Theoretical contributions include the demonstration that the optimal values of these expected scores match those of their discrete counterparts, which is a significant advancement over previous methods. The paper also provides empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of EPH, showing that it outperforms existing methods in most cases. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the method due to its computational complexity and the lack of a clear demonstration of its advantages over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the motivation, methodology, and results, with a clear introduction and a comprehensive literature review.
- The introduction of a new probabilistic framework for hierarchical clustering is innovative, addressing a gap in the current literature.
- Theoretical contributions are robust, particularly in demonstrating the equivalence between expected and discrete scores, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is well-motivated and the paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, supported by extensive experimental results.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broader audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear demonstration of the advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, particularly in terms of practical applications and computational efficiency.
- The complexity of the method, particularly in terms of computational cost and memory requirements, is not adequately addressed, which could limit its scalability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and a more comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons with other methods on different datasets.
- The presentation of results could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and the inclusion of more detailed visualizations and explanations.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of convergence to the global optimum when optimizing using gradient descent methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations for the theoretical properties of the expected scores, particularly the claim that Exp-Das is neither convex nor concave?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational complexity and memory requirements compared to other methods, especially in large-scale applications?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with other methods, including a discussion on the practical advantages and disadvantages?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of convergence to the global optimum when optimizing using gradient descent methods?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and suggest areas for future research?
- In the experimental section, can the authors provide more detailed visualizations and explanations to help clarify the results and their implications?
