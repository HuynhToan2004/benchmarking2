 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AutoDAN, an automatic and interpretable adversarial attack method designed to bypass perplexity filters and jailbreak language models. It leverages a two-step optimization process to generate adversarial prompts that are both readable and effective, demonstrating superior transferability to black-box models. The method is evaluated against various baselines and shown to outperform existing methods in terms of attack success rates and transferability. The paper also explores the potential of AutoDAN in performing additional tasks like prompt leaking, highlighting its versatility and potential impact on LLM security.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive evaluation that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method across various scenarios.
- AutoDAN introduces a novel approach by combining jailbreaking and readability objectives, which is a significant departure from existing methods that often compromise one for the other.
- The method's ability to generate interpretable adversarial prompts is a notable strength, as it allows for a deeper understanding of the attack strategies and their impact on LLMs.
- The transferability of the attacks to black-box models is a significant advantage, as it demonstrates the practical applicability of the method in real-world scenarios.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including a thorough comparison with existing methods and a detailed analysis of the generated prompts, which enhances the understanding of the attack strategies and their impact on LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the hyperparameters used in the experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The method's reliance on a pre-defined prompt template could limit its applicability in more complex or varied attack scenarios.
- There is a potential issue with the transferability of the attacks to models other than those used in the experiments, which could limit the practical utility of the method.
- The paper does not discuss the potential limitations or negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern given the potential misuse of the generated prompts.
- The evaluation primarily focuses on one model (Vicuna), which may not adequately represent the performance across different models or in more varied attack scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the method handle scenarios where the prompt template does not adequately capture the attacker's intent or the target LLM's behavior?
- Can the method be adapted to generate adversarial prompts for different LLM architectures, such as encoder-decoder models?
- How does the method perform when the adversarial prefix is inserted before the user request, and what are the implications for the attack success rate?
- Can you provide more details on the hyperparameters used in the experiments, including the sensitivity of the results to these parameters?
- How does the method ensure that the generated prompts do not inadvertently reveal information about the attacker or the target LLM, potentially compromising the security of the attack?
- Could you elaborate on the method's effectiveness in more varied attack scenarios, such as those involving different LLM architectures or more complex user requests?
