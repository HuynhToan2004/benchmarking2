 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel decomposition-based synthesis strategy named ExeDec, designed to enhance compositional generalization in neural program synthesis. This strategy involves training models to predict execution subgoals, which are then used to synthesize programs step-by-step. The method is evaluated using a meta-benchmark on two popular datasets, RobustFill and DeepCoder, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods. The paper also proposes a meta-benchmark for measuring compositional generalization in program synthesizers and discusses its application to large language models (LLMs). Despite its innovative approach, the paper is critiqued for its limited experimental scope, lack of comprehensive baseline comparisons, and potential overfitting issues.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise descriptions of the proposed method and experiments.
- The idea of decomposing a program into smaller sub-programs and synthesizing them one by one is innovative and has shown significant performance improvements over baseline methods.
- The proposed method, ExeDec, is original and demonstrates a novel approach to compositional generalization in neural program synthesis.
- The paper introduces a new meta-benchmark for measuring the compositional generalization abilities of program synthesizers, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is supported by strong empirical results and the paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks comprehensive baseline comparisons, particularly with recent state-of-the-art methods in the field of program synthesis.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to two datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the size and complexity of the datasets used, which could affect the reproducibility and applicability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the scalability and applicability of the approach to more complex, real-world programming tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address potential overfitting issues, which could affect the robustness and generalizability of the results.
- The paper's claims about compositional generalization are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which are primarily focused on program synthesis rather than generalization.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with other recent state-of-the-art methods in program synthesis, particularly in terms of performance and generalization capabilities?
- Can you provide more details on the datasets used, such as their size, complexity, and diversity?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to more complex, real-world programming tasks, such as those involving multiple libraries or more complex program structures?
- How does the proposed method handle overfitting, particularly in the context of the meta-benchmark and the compositional generalization tasks?
- Could you elaborate on the potential applications and limitations of the proposed method in practical programming scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare with other approaches that use large language models (LLMs) for compositional generalization in program synthesis?
