 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for aligning language models with human preferences using reinforcement learning, specifically focusing on improving generalization across different data groups. The approach involves a two-stage training process: first, a critic model is trained to predict group labels based on trajectory data, and then a policy model is trained with a regularization term that encourages the policy to closely resemble the SFT model. This method aims to address the challenge of reward hacking by introducing a KL penalty that varies based on the group label predicted by the critic model. The paper presents experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of this method in improving generalization and handling out-of-distribution data, as validated by both human and GPT-4 evaluations. However, concerns are raised regarding the clarity of the method's application to RLHF and the robustness of the experimental results, particularly in terms of the statistical significance and the clarity of the presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow sections, and includes a comprehensive appendix.
- The proposed method is novel, combining ideas from invariant learning and RLHF, which is a novel approach to addressing the challenges in RLHF.
- The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method through various evaluations, including human and GPT-4 evaluations.
- The method's ability to improve generalization and handling of out-of-distribution data is highlighted, which is crucial for applications in real-world scenarios.
- The paper includes an ablation study that effectively demonstrates the contributions of each component of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the method handles out-of-distribution (OOD) data, which is crucial for understanding the robustness of the approach.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its method from existing approaches, particularly those using KL penalties for RLHF.
- The experimental results are not convincingly robust, with concerns about the statistical significance of the results and the potential for overfitting due to the use of a single seed.
- The paper could benefit from clearer presentation and better organization, particularly in the introduction and methodology sections.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the proposed method, such as the computational costs and the scalability of the approach.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the method handles OOD data, especially in terms of generalization to unseen data?
- How does the method ensure that the KL penalty is effective in preventing reward hacking, especially in scenarios where the reward model may be flawed?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the number of seeds used and the statistical significance of the results?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Could the authors provide more insights into the method's performance under different conditions, such as with different models or in more complex scenarios?
