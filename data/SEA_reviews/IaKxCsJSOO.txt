 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the use of Tractable Probabilistic Models (TPMs) to enhance the performance of Reinforcement Learning (RL) algorithms in offline RL tasks, particularly focusing on the tractability of inference in these models. The authors demonstrate that the tractability of inference is crucial for optimizing RL algorithms, especially in environments where labeled returns are suboptimal. They propose a method called TRIFLE, which leverages TPMs to compute arbitrary marginal/condition probabilities, thereby improving the evaluation-time performance of offline RL tasks. The paper provides empirical evidence of TRIFLE's superior performance over other RL methods, particularly in stochastic environments and safe RL tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the critical issue of inference-time optimality in offline RL tasks, which is often overlooked in current RL research.
- The use of Tractable Probabilistic Models (TPMs) to enhance the tractability of inference in RL tasks is a novel and promising approach.
- The paper provides a clear and well-structured presentation of the problem, the proposed method, and the experimental results, making it accessible to both experts and non-experts in the field.
- The authors demonstrate the importance of tractability in RL tasks and provide a solid empirical evaluation that shows the superior performance of their proposed method, TRIFLE, over other RL methods.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and detailed empirical insights that support the claims made.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential drawbacks of the proposed method, especially in terms of computational complexity and scalability.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the TPMs used, which could enhance the understanding of their effectiveness in RL tasks.
- The experimental setup and the results reported are somewhat limited, with only a few environments tested, which might not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other RL methods, especially those that address similar issues of inference-time optimality.
- There is a need for more detailed explanations and justifications for the choice of TPMs and the specific implementation details, such as the use of beam search and the handling of constraints.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially in larger or more complex environments?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of RL tasks, such as those involving continuous actions or more complex state spaces?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of TPMs and the specific implementation details, particularly the use of beam search and the handling of constraints?
- What are the specific advantages of using TPMs over other models, such as diffusion models, and how does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness?
- How does the paper address the potential issues of overfitting or underfitting in the TPMs used, and what measures are in place to ensure the robustness and generalizability of the findings?
