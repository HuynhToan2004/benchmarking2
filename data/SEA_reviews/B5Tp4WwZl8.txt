 **Summary:**
The paper explores the communication complexity of Error Feedback (EF) algorithms in distributed optimization, focusing on the TopK sparsification and EF21 method. It presents a novel theoretical analysis of EF21 with TopK sparsification, showing that under specific conditions, EF21 can achieve better communication complexity than vanilla Gradient Descent (GD). The paper also introduces a new step-size adjustment method for EF21, which is validated through numerical experiments. The theoretical contributions are supported by experiments, although some reviewers noted issues with the clarity and completeness of the proofs and the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of EF21 with TopK sparsification, which is a significant advancement in the field of distributed optimization.
- The authors have identified a potential issue in the theoretical analysis of EF21 and proposed a new step-size adjustment method, which could lead to improvements in the practical application of EF21.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the theoretical contributions are supported by numerical experiments.
- The authors have highlighted the discrepancy between the theoretical and practical performance of EF21, which is an interesting and relevant observation in the context of distributed optimization.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the proofs and the definitions of key variables. This makes it difficult for readers to fully understand the theoretical contributions.
- The experimental setup is not clearly described, and the results are not convincingly demonstrated to validate the theoretical claims.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its work, such as the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and the practical implications of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the implications of the theoretical results for practical applications, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions might not hold.
- The paper does not compare its results with other existing methods, which could help in understanding the relative performance of EF21 with TopK sparsification.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the proofs and definitions in the paper to make them more accessible to readers?
- How does the paper address the practical implications of the theoretical results, especially in scenarios where the assumptions might not hold?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of EF21 with other existing methods to better understand the relative performance of EF21 with TopK sparsification?
- How do the authors justify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly in light of the potential limitations in practical applications?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the theoretical analysis to other distributed optimization methods, such as those based on gradient tracking or other compressors?
