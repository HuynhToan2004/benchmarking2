 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the theoretical foundations of score-based generative models (SGMs) by focusing on the approximation and generalization of SGMs for a class of sub-Gaussian probability distributions. It establishes that if the tilting exponent is a Barron function, the distribution generated by empirical score matching approximates the target distribution in the total variation distance with a dimension-independent rate. The authors demonstrate that the score function associated with the forward process exhibits low-complexity structures, which can be efficiently approximated by deep neural networks. This analysis provides a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of SGMs, especially in high-dimensional scenarios, and offers insights into the generalization and approximation capabilities of SGMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and understandable, even for readers not deeply familiar with the subject.
- The paper provides a novel and interesting analysis of the generalization and approximation capabilities of score-based generative models (SGMs) for a class of sub-Gaussian probability distributions.
- The results are significant, particularly the independence of the sample complexity bound from the dimension, which is a crucial contribution to the field.
- The paper is original, presenting the first theoretical justification for the success of SGMs in high dimensions without relying on the manifold hypothesis.
- The proofs are well-structured and clear, with detailed explanations of the technical challenges and solutions used.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is quite technical and requires a strong background in the field to fully understand, making it potentially difficult for a broader audience.
- The results are limited to a specific class of distributions, which might not generalize well to more complex or varied scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a lower bound for the sample complexity, which is crucial for understanding the optimality of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical findings, particularly in terms of how the results might influence the design or implementation of SGMs in real-world applications.
- There are some minor issues with the presentation and notation, which could be clarified to improve readability and understanding.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the assumptions used in the paper could be relaxed to potentially generalize the results to a broader class of distributions?
- Could the authors provide more details on the practical implications of the theoretical findings, particularly how they might influence the design or implementation of SGMs in real-world applications?
- How do the results compare to existing works, such as those by Oko et al. and Chen et al., which also address the theoretical foundations of SGMs?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the path norm in the approximation results and discuss how it affects the generalizability of the findings?
- In terms of the practical implementation of SGMs, how do the theoretical results translate into concrete improvements or new design strategies?
