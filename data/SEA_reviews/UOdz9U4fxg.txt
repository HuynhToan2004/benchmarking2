 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for reverse engineering of deceptions (RED) by integrating a GAN inversion problem with a block-sparse recovery problem, aiming to provide linear convergence guarantees for the RED problem. This approach replaces the signal model with a GAN generator and models adversarial perturbations as block-sparse vectors. Theoretical analysis and empirical results on various datasets, including MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, and CIFAR-10, are presented to support the proposed method's effectiveness. The paper also discusses the challenges of the RED problem and provides a theoretical analysis of the convergence of the iterates to the global optimum of the GAN inversion problem.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to the Reverse Engineering of Deceptions (RED) problem by combining GAN inversion with block-sparse recovery, which is both theoretically interesting and practically applicable.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust and well-supported, with the authors demonstrating linear convergence to the global optimum of the GAN inversion problem.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations that make the complex theoretical concepts accessible to readers.
- The experimental results are comprehensive, including both synthetic and real data, which support the theoretical claims and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses the RED problem, which is crucial for understanding adversarial attacks and developing robust defenses, and provides a significant contribution to the field by relaxing assumptions about data distribution and providing theoretical guarantees.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could be crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The assumption of linearity in the model might not hold for complex datasets, and the paper does not sufficiently address this potential limitation.
- The paper's experimental setup could be improved by including more varied datasets and comparing the proposed method against a broader range of baselines to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The paper's theoretical results are not particularly surprising or groundbreaking, as they build on existing work and do not offer significant new insights or improvements.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made and their implications, particularly in relation to the linearity of the model and the practical applicability of the theoretical results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical application?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent works in the field of RED, particularly those that do not rely on the linearity assumption?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and discuss how these assumptions might impact the practical applicability of the results?
- Is there a potential for the proposed method to be adapted for use with other types of generative models, such as VAEs or normalizing flows?
- How does the paper's theoretical analysis extend to more complex datasets that do not conform to the linearity assumption used in the analysis?
