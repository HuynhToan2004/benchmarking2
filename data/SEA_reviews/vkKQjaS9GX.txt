 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new diversity measure for training a set of policies in reinforcement learning, focusing on the steady-state probability distribution of other policies to identify states frequently visited. The authors argue that this measure is independent of the current policy, thereby avoiding issues such as diversity escalation and the need for indicator random variables. The proposed method is compared against existing diversity measures, showing improvements in robustness and diversity. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental evaluation, particularly in continuous control environments, and the lack of detailed justification for the new diversity measure's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the study of diversity measures in reinforcement learning.
- The proposed diversity measure is novel and well-explained, particularly in relation to the limitations of existing measures such as mutual information and successor features.
- The methodology is straightforward and practical, with a focus on encouraging diversity in policy learning.
- The paper provides a theoretical justification for the proposed diversity measure, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments are well-designed, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method in both discrete and continuous control environments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed justification for the proposed diversity measure, particularly in terms of its effectiveness and the rationale behind its design.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, with insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art methods and a lack of comprehensive testing across different environments and hyperparameters.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in high-dimensional state spaces.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need correction.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might impact its practical application.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed justification for the proposed diversity measure, particularly in terms of its theoretical underpinnings and practical effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors elaborate on the scalability of the proposed method, especially in high-dimensional state spaces?
- How does the proposed method handle the diversity escalation issue, and what measures have been taken to ensure that the diversity is not overly emphasized?
- Could the authors clarify the notation and definitions used in the paper, particularly in the equations and the definitions of key terms like "ideal diversity measure"?
- How does the proposed method perform in different types of environments, such as stochastic or deterministic environments, and what are the implications of these differences for the effectiveness of the method?
