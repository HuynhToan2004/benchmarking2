 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to multi-view representation learning, termed TOMRL, which integrates task-specific modulation into view-specific encoding and fusion modules. This method uses a meta-learner to map task embeddings to view-specific parameters, enhancing the performance of existing multi-view representation learning approaches. The framework, TOMRL, is evaluated on four multi-view datasets, demonstrating consistent improvements over baseline methods. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited experimental scope, focusing on small-scale datasets, and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods. The presentation of the paper is also noted as poor, with unclear figures and insufficient details on experimental setups.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to multi-view representation learning, specifically focusing on task-oriented modulation which is a significant departure from existing methods.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the proposed method and its components, making it accessible and understandable.
- The paper provides extensive experimental results, including ablation studies and comparisons with baseline methods, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of a meta-learner to map task embeddings to view-specific parameters is a novel and effective approach that enhances the adaptability of the model to different tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the figures and tables are well-designed, contributing to the clarity of the presentation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental scope is limited, focusing on small-scale datasets which may not adequately demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in more complex, real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing multi-view representation learning methods, which could have provided a clearer understanding of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The presentation of the paper is poor, with figures that are hard to read and understand, and the experimental setups are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult for readers to replicate or build upon the work.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and the scalability of the proposed method, which could limit its applicability to larger datasets or more complex scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and potential challenges.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the modulation parameters in the performance of the model? Specifically, how do these parameters contribute to the overall effectiveness of the model?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of tasks, such as classification and clustering, and what are the specific advantages or disadvantages in these scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setups, including the specific hyperparameters used and the computational resources required for training and testing the model?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational complexity and scalability? What are the potential limitations or challenges in applying the method to larger or more complex datasets?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its applicability or effectiveness in different scenarios?
