 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to online world model learning using a linear regressor coupled with a high-dimensional non-linear random feature generator called Locality Sensitive Sparse Encoding (LOSSE). This method aims to mitigate catastrophic forgetting by updating the model incrementally without re-training on all previous data. The LOSSE features are designed to be sparse and high-dimensional, which allows for efficient updates and better performance in various reinforcement learning tasks. The paper validates the approach through experiments in both supervised learning and reinforcement learning settings, showing improvements over existing methods in terms of sample and computation efficiency. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited empirical evaluation and lack of comparison with more recent or complex methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to online world model learning by using a linear regressor with high-dimensional non-linear random features, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The methodology is supported by a solid theoretical foundation, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in both supervised learning and reinforcement learning settings.
- The paper is well-organized and provides a clear explanation of the methodology, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The use of locality sensitive sparse encoding (LOSSE) is innovative and helps in mitigating catastrophic forgetting, which is a significant issue in online learning scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The empirical evaluation of the proposed method is limited, with only a few environments tested, which raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with more recent or complex methods, which could have provided a better understanding of the method's relative performance.
- The methodology, particularly the use of LOSSE features and the incremental update process, is not clearly explained, which could hinder understanding and replication of the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on how the feature dimension is chosen and how it affects the performance of the method.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive experimental validation, including additional tasks and environments, to further demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the feature dimension is chosen and how it affects the performance of the method?
- How does the proposed method compare with other continual learning methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and forgetting behavior?
- Can the authors clarify the process of updating the model weights using LOSSE features and the incremental update process?
- How does the method perform when the state space is high-dimensional, such as in image-based tasks?
- Would it be possible to include more recent or complex methods in future comparisons to better understand the relative performance of the proposed method?
