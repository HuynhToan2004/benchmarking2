 **Summary:**
The paper investigates how transformer models process the carrying over algorithm in arithmetic tasks, specifically focusing on three-digit addition. The authors demonstrate that transformers can learn to carry over digits by implementing the algorithm in a modular fashion, with different parts of the network handling specific tasks such as determining whether to carry over and performing the actual carry operation. The study also explores how these mechanisms are applied in larger language models, albeit with varying effectiveness. The research is based on experiments with transformer models trained on 3-digit addition, which are then extended to 6-digit addition through priming or fine-tuning. However, the paper's scope and methodology are criticized for their simplicity and potential over-generalization, as the findings may not translate well to more complex or varied mathematical operations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of how transformers implement the carrying over algorithm for integer addition, which is a significant contribution to understanding the inner workings of these models.
- The findings are supported by a variety of experiments, including ablation studies, which help to validate the conclusions drawn.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The study is original in its focus on the implementation of the carrying over algorithm in transformers, offering a novel perspective on how these models process mathematical operations.
- The paper provides a systematic study of the attention patterns and residual streams of transformers, which is beneficial for understanding the mechanisms behind these models' behavior.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to the carrying over algorithm in 3-digit addition, which might not generalize well to other mathematical operations or larger inputs.
- The methodology used, such as the choice of hyperparameters and the training dataset, is not adequately justified or explained, which could limit the reproducibility and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its approach, which could include the simplicity of the model architecture and the lack of complexity in the mathematical operations studied.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the findings can be applied to improve transformers' performance in mathematical reasoning tasks, which is a significant limitation given the paper's focus on transformer models.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims about the generalization of the findings to larger language models or more complex mathematical operations.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify why the paper focuses on 3-digit addition and not larger inputs? What are the implications of this choice for the generalizability of the findings?
- How do the results of this study apply to larger language models, such as those with more layers or heads? What are the specific implications for the implementation of the carrying over algorithm in these models?
- In the context of the findings, what are the implications for improving transformers' performance in mathematical reasoning tasks? How can these insights be used to develop more effective models?
- Could you provide more details on the methodology used in this study, particularly the choice of hyperparameters and the training dataset? How were these choices made, and what were the considerations behind them?
- How do the findings of this study compare to existing research on transformers' ability to perform mathematical operations? What are the unique contributions of this study, and how do they advance the current understanding of transformers in this context?
