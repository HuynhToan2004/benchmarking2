 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Clover, a novel paradigm for verifiable code generation that integrates formal verification tools with large language models (LLMs). Clover aims to ensure the correctness and consistency of code, annotations, and docstrings by employing a closed-loop verification process. This process involves LLMs for generating annotations from code and docstrings, and vice versa, as well as checking the equivalence of these components. The evaluation of Clover on a benchmark dataset of 60 Dafny programs shows promising results, with the system achieving high acceptance rates for correct instances and zero tolerance for incorrect ones. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation scope, lack of detailed analysis on the reliability of LLMs, and potential biases in the benchmark dataset.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue in the field of automated code generation, focusing on ensuring the correctness of generated code, annotations, and docstrings.
- The proposed paradigm is novel and innovative, integrating formal verification tools with LLMs to enhance the reliability and accuracy of generated code.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical flow that makes the content accessible and understandable.
- The evaluation of the proposed method shows promising results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the Clover system in detecting incorrect code instances.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis and a mathematical rationale for the proposed method, which adds depth to the understanding of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation is limited to a small dataset of 60 Dafny programs, which may not adequately represent the performance of the proposed method on larger or more complex datasets.
- The paper lacks detailed analysis on the reliability of LLMs, which are a critical component of the Clover system. More insight into the performance of LLMs in generating correct annotations and docstrings is needed.
- The benchmark dataset used in the evaluation is biased towards simpler programs, which might not reflect the performance of the Clover system on more complex or realistic scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing verification methods or other state-of-the-art approaches, which could help in understanding the advantages and limitations of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its applicability and effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the reliability of LLMs in generating correct annotations and docstrings? What are the specific failure modes and how do they impact the performance of the Clover system?
- How does the Clover system perform on larger or more complex datasets? Are there any plans to expand the evaluation to include a broader range of programs?
- Could you discuss the potential biases in the benchmark dataset and how they might affect the results? How might these biases be addressed in future evaluations?
- Are there any plans to open-source the dataset and code used in the evaluation to facilitate further research and reproducibility of the results?
- How does the Clover system compare to other verification methods or state-of-the-art approaches in terms of performance, reliability, and scalability?
- Could you elaborate on the potential limitations of the proposed method and how these might impact its practical application in real-world scenarios?
