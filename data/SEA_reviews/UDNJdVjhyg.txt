 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph representation learning method based on maximizing graph entropy, leveraging orthonormal representations for node and graph representations. The authors propose a method that approximates graph entropy using Shannon entropy and chromatic entropy to address the computational challenges associated with the original graph entropy definition. The methodology involves two GNNs for learning node and graph representations, and the paper includes extensive experiments to validate the approach's effectiveness across various graph-level and node-level tasks. Despite its theoretical contributions and empirical validations, the paper faces criticisms for its limited novelty and the lack of a detailed comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The authors effectively integrate graph entropy into graph representation learning, providing a novel approach to capturing graph information.
- The method is supported by extensive experiments that demonstrate its effectiveness across various graph-level and node-level tasks, showing improvements over existing methods.
- The paper introduces a novel method of learning graph representations using graph entropy, which is a significant contribution to the field of graph representation learning.
- The methodology is robust, with a well-designed iterative optimization algorithm that effectively addresses the computational challenges associated with graph entropy.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency, which could help establish the superiority of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned as it primarily uses existing graph entropy definitions without significant innovation.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its contributions and challenges.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodology, particularly in the explanation of the GNN models used and the training procedures, which could confuse readers.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, which could limit the reproducibility and understanding of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and computational complexity of the proposed method, which could limit its applicability to larger or more complex graphs.

**Questions:**
