 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for generating natural counterfactuals, which allows for backtracking in causal models to achieve more realistic and plausible outcomes. The authors propose a method that optimizes the distance between the actual and counterfactual worlds while ensuring the naturalness of the generated counterfactuals. This approach is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing improvements over traditional non-backtracking counterfactuals. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of this method and its potential applications in various domains, including decision-making and explanation. However, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity in explaining the method's novelty and its practical implications, as well as the lack of comprehensive empirical evaluation and comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of counterfactual reasoning, focusing on the generation of natural counterfactuals which align better with real-world scenarios.
- The introduction of a novel optimization framework for generating natural counterfactuals, which integrates naturalness and minimal change, is a notable contribution.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions and a detailed methodology that makes it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, providing empirical evidence of its effectiveness.
- The paper discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the method, including the concept of naturalness and minimal change, which are crucial for understanding the method's operation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the novelty of the proposed method, particularly in relation to existing works such as von Kugelgen et al. (2022).
- The empirical evaluation of the method is limited, with only a few datasets used and no comparison with other methods such as von Kugelgen et al. (2022).
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples of how the method handles causal backtracking, especially in scenarios where it is necessary or not.
- The paper's reliance on the assumption of a recursive causal model might limit its applicability to more complex or non-linear causal relationships.
- The method's practical implications and its applicability to real-world scenarios are not thoroughly discussed, which could limit the understanding of its potential impact.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where causal backtracking is necessary or not? Can you provide examples or scenarios where this might be the case?
- How does the method compare to other existing methods, such as von Kugelgen et al. (2022)? Could you provide a more detailed comparison in terms of methodology, results, and practical implications?
- Could you clarify the role and definition of "naturalness" in the context of the method? How does this concept relate to the practical implementation and evaluation of the method?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the causal graph is not known or is incomplete? What are the implications of this for the method's applicability and effectiveness?
- Can you provide more detailed examples or scenarios where the method's approach to backtracking is particularly beneficial or necessary? How does this compare to the approach taken by von Kugelgen et al. (2022)?
