 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called CLIPSelector, which utilizes the CLIP model to select clean samples from a noisy dataset. The method involves training a classifier on the CLIP's vision encoder with the noisy dataset and then using this classifier to identify clean samples. The selected samples are then used in a semi-supervised learning approach called MixFix, which re-labels and introduces clean samples to the training set. The paper claims that CLIPSelector can effectively distinguish between clean and noisy samples, enhancing the performance of downstream tasks. The methodology is tested on various datasets, showing competitive results against existing methods. However, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty, unclear presentation, and lack of comprehensive experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear structure that effectively communicates the proposed method.
- The idea of using CLIP to select clean samples from a noisy dataset is innovative and could potentially reduce the reliance on self-confirmation biases.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear definitions and explanations of the various components, such as CLIPSelector and MixFix.
- The paper provides a good balance between theoretical understanding and practical application, offering insights into how CLIP can be used for noisy label learning.
- The experimental results are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method on several benchmark datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as the use of CLIP for noisy label learning is not entirely new, with similar ideas previously explored in other works.
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of how the CLIP model is used for sample selection and the role of the CLIP vision encoder in the proposed method.
- There is a significant gap in the performance of the CLIP zero-shot classifier compared to state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the credibility of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of domain gaps between the CLIP model and the target dataset, which could affect the performance of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not consistently strong across all datasets, and the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the factors contributing to these variations.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the CLIP model is used for sample selection and whether the CLIP vision encoder plays a significant role in this process?
- How does the proposed method handle different types and ratios of noise in the dataset, and what are the implications of these variations on the performance of the method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and evaluation of the CLIP zero-shot classifier, including the specifics of the training data and the evaluation metrics used?
- How does the proposed method compare with other sample selection methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Can the authors elaborate on the choice of using the CLIP model for sample selection and how this choice impacts the overall performance of the method?
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential future research directions. Can the authors provide such insights?
