 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Neural Fine-Tuning Search (NFTS) method for few-shot learning, focusing on the optimal arrangement of adapters and fine-tuning layers in a pre-trained neural network. NFTS is designed to adapt to various neural network architectures, including ResNets and ViTs, and has been tested on the Meta-Dataset and Meta-Album benchmarks, showing state-of-the-art performance. The method involves a two-stage search process: one during meta-training to pre-select a set of models, and another during meta-testing to select the most suitable architecture for the given support set. The paper also discusses the challenges of managing the adaptation/generalization trade-off in few-shot learning and provides a comprehensive analysis of the discovered architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel Neural Fine-Tuning Search (NFTS) method that systematically discovers the optimal arrangement of adapters and fine-tuning layers, offering a more systematic approach to few-shot learning compared to previous methods.
- The NFTS method is capable of adapting to various neural network architectures, including ResNets and ViTs, and has been tested on the Meta-Dataset and Meta-Album benchmarks, achieving state-of-the-art performance.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the discovered architectures, which helps in understanding the behavior of the NAS method in different scenarios.
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of the search space, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of the NAS method in different scenarios.
- The proposed method is capable of adapting to various neural network architectures, including ResNets and ViTs, which is a significant advantage in the field of few-shot learning.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are well-designed, with comprehensive ablation studies and analysis of the discovered architectures.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the two-stage search process during meta-training and meta-testing, and the necessity and effectiveness of this approach are not convincingly argued.
- The evaluation is limited to only two datasets, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods on Meta-Album, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The methodology of the paper, particularly the two-stage search process, is not well-explained, and the rationale behind the choice of N=3 for the number of candidates is not convincingly justified.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other NAS-based methods, which could have provided a better understanding of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The computational cost of the proposed method is not discussed, which is a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the two-stage search process during meta-training and meta-testing, and why this approach was chosen over a single-stage search?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods on Meta-Album, especially in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over other NAS-based methods, and how does it address the challenges of managing the adaptation/generalization trade-off in few-shot learning?
- Could you provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method and discuss how this cost compares with other methods in terms of practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of neural network architectures beyond ResNets and ViTs?
- Can you provide more insights into the decision-making process behind the choice of N=3 for the number of candidates, and how this choice impacts the performance and computational efficiency of the method?
