 **Summary:**
The paper explores the open-set domain generalization problem in the context of referring video segmentation, introducing a novel CLIP-based Reasoning Prompt (CRPrompt) and Retrieval-augmented Instance Normalization (RaIN) to enhance the model's ability to recognize and segment unseen objects. The CRPrompt integrates text and visual prompts to improve the text-object matching capability, while RaIN retrieves visual objects with similar semantic concepts to enhance robustness against domain shifts. The methodology is evaluated on two datasets, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper's novelty and the clarity of its presentation have been questioned, with concerns about the generalizability of the proposed methods beyond the specific tasks addressed.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to open-set domain generalization in referring video segmentation, which is a significant problem in the field.
- The proposed CLIP-based Reasoning Prompt (CRPrompt) effectively integrates text and visual prompts to enhance text-object matching ability, which is a novel application of CRPrompt in the context of referring video segmentation.
- The Retrieval-augmented Instance Normalization (RaIN) method is innovative, allowing for the retrieval of similar visual objects to enhance the model's robustness, which is not commonly seen in existing methods.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their applications.
- The methodology is supported by comprehensive experimental results and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation from existing methods, particularly in the application of CRPrompt and RaIN in referring video segmentation. The novelty of these components is questionable as similar approaches have been previously used in other tasks.
- The method's generalizability is limited to the specific task of referring video segmentation, and it is unclear how well the proposed components could be adapted to other tasks or domains.
- The paper's writing and presentation need improvement, with numerous typos, grammatical errors, and unclear figures and tables that hinder understanding.
- The methodology's dependence on the CLIP model raises concerns about overfitting and the model's ability to generalize to unseen classes.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to existing methods, particularly in the application of CRPrompt and RaIN in referring video segmentation?
- How do the authors ensure that the model does not overfit to the seen classes, especially given the dependence on the CLIP model?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the CRPrompt and RaIN components, possibly with visual aids to enhance understanding?
- In the context of the experimental results, how do the authors address the potential for data leakage, especially in the zero-shot domain generalization setting?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other tasks or domains, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in these applications?
