 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to estimating graph-based statistical quantities using a method called repelling random walks. This method, which is a variant of Quasi-Monte Carlo sampling, is designed to improve the concentration properties of estimators by inducing correlations between the trajectories of random walkers on a graph. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this method through various applications, including the estimation of graph kernels, PageRank vectors, and graphlet concentrations. The paper provides both theoretical and empirical evidence to support the claims, showing that the repelling random walk method can outperform traditional methods in certain scenarios. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of rigorous mathematical proofs, especially in the proofs of theorems, and for not sufficiently distinguishing its contributions from existing literature.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow proofs and a clear introduction to the problem and its applications.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, making it accessible to a wide range of researchers.
- The paper presents a novel approach to random walks on graphs, which could be of interest to a broader audience, including those in machine learning and graph theory.
- The method is shown to be effective in improving the concentration properties of estimators, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a good discussion on related work, which helps to contextualize the research within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks rigorous mathematical proofs for some of its claims, particularly in the proofs of theorems, which could undermine the credibility of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing literature, particularly in the area of random walks on graphs and Quasi-Monte Carlo methods.
- The paper's focus on graph-based applications may limit its appeal to a broader audience, as the methodology could be applied to other domains.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper's reliance on theoretical results that are only applicable in specific scenarios (e.g., when the number of walkers is smaller than the minimum node degree) may limit the general applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the proofs of the theorems and provide more rigorous mathematical proofs to support the claims made?
- How does the proposed method compare to other Quasi-Monte Carlo methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the implementation of the method, including any assumptions or restrictions that need to be met for the method to be effective?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the number of walkers is not smaller than the minimum node degree?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these could be mitigated?
