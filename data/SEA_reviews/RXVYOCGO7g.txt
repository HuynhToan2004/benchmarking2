 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel backdoor defense mechanism for federated learning (FL) systems, named NIRA, which utilizes noise-guided robust aggregation to filter out malicious models. This method leverages a surrogate dataset containing pure noise to train models, ensuring that the model's performance on this dataset is a reliable metric for detecting backdoor attacks. The approach aims to address privacy concerns by using noise data instead of real data, thereby mitigating the risk of data leakage. Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of NIRA against various backdoor attacks, showing superior performance over existing defense methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed method.
- The motivation behind the research is strong, focusing on the privacy risks associated with using real data for backdoor defense in federated learning.
- The proposed method, NIRA, is simple, intuitive, and effective, with a novel approach to backdoor defense using noise-guided robust aggregation.
- The experimental results are strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of NIRA against various backdoor attacks and showing superior performance compared to existing defense methods.
- The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical findings that support the effectiveness of NIRA, including a lower bound on the transferred statistical robustness.
- The method is scalable and can be integrated with most federated learning algorithms, making it adaptable to different systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The motivation for using noise data specifically, rather than other forms of data, is not well justified.
- The method's reliance on a surrogate dataset containing pure noise may not be practical in real-world scenarios where data is not easily obtainable or is highly heterogeneous.
- The method's effectiveness in scenarios with high levels of data heterogeneity or in the presence of strong adversaries is not thoroughly explored.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential privacy risks associated with the use of noise data, which could still contain statistical information that could be used to infer sensitive data.
- The experimental setup is limited to specific datasets and attack scenarios, which may not fully represent the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art backdoor defense methods, which could provide a more robust evaluation of its effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with high levels of data heterogeneity or in the presence of strong adversaries?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of using noise data specifically for backdoor defense and discuss the potential privacy risks associated with this approach?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art backdoor defense methods in terms of effectiveness and practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the success rates of different types of attacks and the impact of varying data heterogeneity levels?
- How does the proposed method handle adaptive attacks where the attacker is aware of the defense mechanism and adjusts their strategy accordingly?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending the method to other types of attacks, such as label-flipping attacks, and how it might be adapted to different federated learning scenarios?
