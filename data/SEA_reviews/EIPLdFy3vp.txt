 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AutoTCL, a novel data augmentation framework designed for time series data in self-supervised contrastive learning. The framework is based on a factorization approach that separates informative and task-irrelevant components of time series data, allowing for adaptive augmentations that maintain semantic information while enhancing diversity. The proposed method, AutoTCL, is evaluated on various benchmark datasets and demonstrates competitive performance improvements over existing methods, showing a reduction in MSE and MAE in forecasting tasks and an increase in accuracy in classification tasks. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the method, including its relation to information theory and its ability to preserve semantics and introduce variance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.
- The proposed method is novel and well-motivated, addressing the challenge of applying general augmentation techniques to time series data without relying on domain-specific knowledge.
- The methodology is theoretically grounded, particularly in the context of information theory, and includes a novel factorization-based framework for data augmentation.
- The empirical results show significant performance improvements over existing methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies, which help to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The method's generalizability to other tasks and datasets beyond forecasting is not thoroughly explored.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that use similar data augmentation techniques.
- The theoretical analysis, while interesting, does not sufficiently explain the practical benefits of the proposed method in terms of improving performance.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the reliance on certain assumptions about the data and the potential impact of hyperparameter choices.
- The experimental setup and results are not always clearly presented, making it difficult to fully understand the contributions of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the generalizability of the proposed method to other tasks and datasets beyond forecasting?
- How does the method perform when compared to other data augmentation techniques, such as those mentioned in the related work section?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the mask in the proposed method and how it affects the performance of the model?
- Is there a possibility to include more detailed experimental results and comparisons in the main text to better demonstrate the contributions of the proposed method?
