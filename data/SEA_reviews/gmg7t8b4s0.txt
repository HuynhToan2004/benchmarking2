 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ConFAIDE, a benchmark designed to evaluate the privacy reasoning capabilities of instruction-tuned large language models (LLMs) in contextual settings. The benchmark consists of four tiers, each increasing in complexity, with the final tier assessing contextual privacy reasoning and theory of mind capabilities. The authors demonstrate that even commercial models like GPT-4 and ChatGPT reveal private information in inappropriate contexts, highlighting the need for more sophisticated privacy-preserving approaches. The paper is well-received for its innovative approach, clear presentation, and thorough evaluation, although some concerns are raised about the benchmark's complexity and the clarity of its motivation and contribution.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmark, ConFAIDE, which evaluates the privacy reasoning capabilities of LLMs, a significant and overlooked area in current research.
- The benchmark design is well-articulated, with clear motivation and a logical progression from simple to complex scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes detailed annotation and evaluation methods, ensuring transparency and reproducibility.
- The experiments are thorough, providing detailed results and comparisons between LLMs and human judgments, highlighting the limitations of current models.
- The paper addresses a timely and important issue of privacy leakage in LLMs, which is a critical concern for the deployment of these models in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on evaluating privacy reasoning capabilities of LLMs is commendable, but the benchmark's complexity and the rapid evolution of LLMs might limit its utility over time.
- The motivation and contribution of the work are not clearly articulated, particularly in relation to existing literature and the specific contributions of the proposed benchmark.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the benchmark, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness.
- There is a need for more detailed analysis and discussion on the results, particularly the discrepancies between human and model judgments, and the implications of these findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the methodology and design choices, especially in the context of the benchmark's design and the evaluation methods used.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the benchmark's contribution and how it advances the understanding or evaluation of LLMs in privacy contexts?
- How does the benchmark ensure that the privacy reasoning capabilities of LLMs are evaluated fairly across different models and contexts?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or scenarios from the benchmark to illustrate the types of questions or prompts used, and how these reflect real-world privacy scenarios?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of the benchmark, such as its applicability to different types of LLMs or its ability to evaluate privacy reasoning in more complex or nuanced scenarios?
- Could the authors expand on the implications of the results, particularly the discrepancies between human and model judgments, and how these might inform future research or applications of LLMs in privacy-sensitive contexts?
