 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new benchmark, GVTBench, designed to evaluate the visual tokenizer's ability to capture semantic and fine-grained visual understanding capabilities in multimodal large language models (MLLMs). The authors compare different visual tokenizers, including fully/weakly supervised and self-supervised models, and investigate the impact of pre-training supervision on visual tokenizer performance. They find that self-supervised models perform better in fine-grained perception but lack semantic understanding, while fully/weakly supervised models capture more semantics but struggle with fine-grained perception. To address these limitations, the authors propose a new visual tokenizer, GVT, which integrates the strengths of both approaches through feature distillation. The GVT achieves state-of-the-art performance on various vision-language tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive comparison of different visual tokenizers, including fully/weakly supervised and self-supervised models, which helps in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
- The authors introduce a new benchmark, GVTBench, which is specifically designed to evaluate the visual tokenizer's ability to capture semantic and fine-grained visual understanding capabilities in multimodal large language models (MLLMs).
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a detailed analysis of different visual tokenizers, which is crucial for advancing research in this area.
- The proposed new visual tokenizer, GVT, achieves state-of-the-art performance on various vision-language tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of existing methods and proposes a new visual tokenizer that integrates the strengths of both semantic and fine-grained visual understanding, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on how the proposed visual tokenizer, GVT, differs from existing visual tokenizers like CLIP, especially in terms of its novelty and effectiveness.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental validation for the proposed visual tokenizer, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different models and datasets.
- There is a lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art models, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational costs of using different visual tokenizers, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the visual tokenizer is trained, which could be crucial for reproducibility and understanding the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed visual tokenizer, GVT, differ from existing visual tokenizers like CLIP in terms of its architecture and training methodology?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the visual tokenizer is trained, including the specifics of the training dataset and the training process?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different models and datasets, and what are the implications for its generalizability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational costs associated with using different visual tokenizers, and how these costs compare in terms of performance and efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other state-of-the-art models, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the validity of the findings?
- Can the authors provide more experimental results to validate the effectiveness of the proposed visual tokenizer, especially on more challenging datasets or tasks?
