 **Summary:**
The paper introduces OpenIns3D, a novel method for 3D open-vocabulary instance segmentation without requiring 2D image inputs. This method employs a "Mask-Snap-Lookup" scheme, where the Mask module generates class-agnostic mask proposals from 3D point clouds, the Snap module creates synthetic images from these masks, and the Lookup module uses these images to assign category names to the masks. The paper claims that OpenIns3D achieves state-of-the-art results on various indoor and outdoor datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and flexibility. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the methodology, and the generalization of the results to more diverse datasets and real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in 3D open-world understanding, which is crucial for various applications.
- The proposed method, OpenIns3D, is well-motivated and novel, particularly in its ability to operate without 2D image inputs, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method achieves state-of-the-art results on multiple datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and utility.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the results are convincing, showing the method's ability to outperform existing methods in various benchmarks.
- The approach is flexible and can be adapted to different datasets and scenarios, including outdoor and indoor environments, which enhances its applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that use 2D image inputs, which could help establish the method's relative performance.
- The method's novelty is questioned as it bears similarities to existing methods like PointCLIP, and the technical contributions are not clearly distinguished.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process, such as the size of the training dataset and the computational resources required.
- There are concerns about the generalization of the method to more diverse datasets and real-world scenarios, particularly outdoor environments where lighting conditions and object shapes vary significantly.
- The paper's evaluation metrics and methodology are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to assess the method's performance and robustness.
- The paper does not address the potential limitations of the method, such as its reliance on synthetic images and its scalability to larger, more complex datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the novelty of the proposed method compared to existing methods like PointCLIP and other relevant works?
- How does the method perform when using different types of 2D open-world models, and what are the implications of these choices on the results?
- Could you provide more details on the training process, such as the size and diversity of the training dataset, and the computational resources required?
- How does the method handle varying lighting conditions and object shapes in outdoor environments, and what are the implications of these variations on the method's performance?
- Can you explain the discrepancies in the results reported in different tables and figures, and provide a detailed explanation of the metrics used in the evaluation?
- How does the method ensure the accuracy and robustness of the mask proposals, and what are the implications of using low-quality masks on the overall performance?
