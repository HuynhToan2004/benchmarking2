 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Dynamic Scene Transformer (DyST), a novel approach for learning latent neural scene representations from monocular video, enabling controlled view generation and dynamic scene understanding. DyST leverages a synthetic dataset, DySO, to co-train with real-world data, achieving a balance between synthetic and real-world data. The methodology involves a latent control swap scheme for disentangling scene dynamics and camera pose, which is critical for generating novel views. The paper also presents a detailed analysis of the model's latent representations and their application in dynamic scene representation. However, concerns about the novelty, clarity of methodological explanations, and the generalizability of the results to more complex scenes were raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem of learning latent neural scene representations from monocular video, which is crucial for generating novel views and understanding dynamic scenes.
- The proposed method is well-motivated and innovative, particularly the use of a latent control swap scheme for disentangling scene dynamics and camera pose, which is a novel approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the figures and supplementary videos effectively illustrate the method's capabilities.
- The DyST model is capable of learning meaningful latent representations for dynamic scenes, which can be applied to real-world videos, demonstrating its practical utility.
- The introduction of the DySO dataset, a novel synthetic dataset, provides a valuable resource for future research in dynamic scene representations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of view synthesis and latent space analysis, which could undermine the perceived effectiveness and novelty of the proposed method.
- The paper's reliance on a synthetic dataset for co-training raises concerns about the generalizability of the model to more complex, real-world scenes.
- The methodology and certain technical aspects of the paper are not clearly explained, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the implementation and its implications.
- The paper's focus on simpler scenes and limited camera motion might not adequately demonstrate the model's capabilities in more complex or varied scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations or societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be crucial for understanding its broader implications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the latent control swap scheme specifically aids in the disentanglement of scene dynamics and camera pose?
- How does the DyST model handle complex scenes with multiple moving objects or more varied camera motions?
- What are the specific benefits of using the DySO dataset for co-training, and how does it compare to other potential datasets for this purpose?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples of how the latent camera and scene dynamics control latents are extracted and used in the model?
- How does the DyST model compare to other methods in terms of view synthesis and latent space analysis, particularly in more complex or varied scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential limitations or societal impacts of the proposed method, and how these might be addressed or mitigated?
