 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for learning a navigable map in 3D environments by training an auxiliary agent to navigate using a latent spatial representation, which is optimized for navigability rather than reconstruction. The method involves training an auxiliary agent to navigate to subgoals, which are then used to train the main agent on the navigable map. The paper also discusses the use of this method in real-world scenarios, demonstrating its effectiveness in simulated and real environments. The method is evaluated against various baselines and shows superior performance in simulated and real environments, particularly in scenarios with domain shifts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is innovative, using a blind agent to learn a navigable map, which is a novel approach to representation learning for navigation.
- The method is evaluated in both simulated and real environments, demonstrating its effectiveness in both scenarios.
- The paper provides a detailed discussion on the methodology and its implications, including a thorough analysis of the results and comparisons with other methods.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that the method outperforms existing approaches in various metrics.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of navigation, specifically focusing on the problem of sim2real transfer, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of certain concepts, such as the "blindness" property and the "navigation loss" function, which are not well-defined in the abstract or early sections of the paper.
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and scope.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing approaches that also use auxiliary tasks for representation learning, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- There are some issues with the clarity and presentation of the paper, such as unclear figures and a lack of detailed descriptions in certain sections.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed experimental setups and results, including more detailed descriptions of the environments used and the evaluation metrics.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the generalizability of the method to other types of environments or navigation tasks, which could limit its applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify what is meant by the "blindness" property and how it affects the learning of the navigable map?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use auxiliary tasks for representation learning, such as those mentioned in the related work section?
- What are the specific advantages of using a blind agent for learning the navigable map, and how does this approach differ from other methods that use auxiliary tasks?
- How does the method perform in different types of environments, such as those with more complex or dynamic layouts?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific environments used and the evaluation metrics?
- How does the method handle different types of noise in the environment, and what are the implications of this for the learning of the navigable map?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their method and how these might affect its practical application?
