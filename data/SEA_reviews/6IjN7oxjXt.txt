 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CURE, a novel adversarial training method that employs a gradient prominence criterion to selectively update weights, aiming to enhance the robustness of neural networks against adversarial attacks. CURE dynamically updates weights based on their significance, which is determined by the gradient magnitude across both natural and adversarial samples. The method is evaluated on various datasets and architectures, demonstrating improved robustness and generalization over existing methods. The authors also explore the layer-wise learning capabilities during the transition from standard to adversarial settings, providing insights into the mechanisms of selective adversarial training.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the figures are clear, enhancing the reader's understanding of the content.
- The proposed method, CURE, is simple, intuitive, and effective, as demonstrated through comprehensive experiments on multiple datasets and architectures.
- The method's novelty lies in its gradient prominence criterion, which is used to selectively update weights, leading to improved robustness and generalization.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the layer-wise learning capabilities during the transition from standard to adversarial settings, offering valuable insights into the mechanisms of selective adversarial training.
- The empirical findings demonstrate that selectively updating specific layers while preserving others can substantially enhance the network's learning capacity.
- The paper is the first to introduce the layer-wise analysis of neural networks during the transition from a standard to an adversarial setting, which is crucial for understanding the underlying factors driving the trade-off between standard and robust generalization.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the proposed method differs from other adversarial training methods, particularly in terms of its novelty and effectiveness.
- There is a need for a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential negative societal impacts and ethical considerations.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity introduced by the additional gradient calculation and weight updating steps, which could impact training efficiency.
- The method's effectiveness is primarily demonstrated on small-scale datasets like CIFAR-10, which might not fully represent its performance on larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not include a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that have been proposed since 2022, which could provide a clearer picture of CURE's performance relative to the latest advancements.
- The paper's claims regarding the reduction of robust overfitting are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which could benefit from a more rigorous validation.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method differ from existing adversarial training methods, particularly in terms of its novelty and effectiveness?
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, especially those that have been proposed since 2022?
- How does the computational complexity introduced by the additional gradient calculation and weight updating steps affect the training efficiency?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet?
- How does the method perform under different attack strengths, and what are the implications for its robustness?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the layer-wise learning capabilities during the transition from a standard to an adversarial setting?
- How does the method address the issue of robust overfitting, and what are the specific mechanisms that contribute to this improvement?
