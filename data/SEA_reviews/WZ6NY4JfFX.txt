 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the zero-shot performance of generative Vision-Language Models (VLMs) in image-text retrieval tasks, specifically focusing on the Visual Generative Pre-Training Score (VisualGPTScore). It explores how these models can be repurposed for discriminative tasks, such as image-text retrieval, by computing the match score of generating a particular text string given an image. The paper also addresses the challenges of unnatural language distributions in benchmarks, proposing a probabilistic post-processing scheme to control linguistic bias. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its lack of novelty and significant performance improvements, as well as its dependence on simplified assumptions and limited evaluation to image-to-text retrieval tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to repurpose generative VLMs for discriminative tasks, particularly in image-text retrieval, by computing the VisualGPTScore, which shows potential for improving zero-shot performance.
- The methodology is supported by theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a comprehensive literature review, making it accessible to readers.
- The authors address the challenges of unnatural language distributions in benchmarks and propose a probabilistic post-processing scheme to control linguistic bias in generative VLMs.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the discrepancy in performance across benchmarks and offers simple strategies for improving performance with debiasing.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks significant novelty and improvement in performance, as the VisualGPTScore and the debiasing method are not sufficiently distinct from existing methods.
- The paper's focus on image-to-text retrieval tasks may limit its applicability to other tasks such as text-to-image retrieval.
- The assumptions made, such as the identical image likelihood across train and test data, are too strong and may not hold in practical scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for biases in the generative VLM, which could impact the performance and generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the computational cost and efficiency of the proposed methods compared to existing methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies or comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, which could strengthen the claims of the proposed method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- How does the paper ensure the effectiveness of the debiasing method, especially in scenarios where the language distribution on the testset is different from the trainset?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the null images are sampled and how they impact the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method perform on other tasks, such as text-to-image retrieval, and what are the potential implications for other VLM-based applications?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly concerning the image likelihood and the language prior, and discuss how these assumptions might affect the performance and generalizability of the results?
- How does the paper address the potential for biases in the generative VLM, and what steps are taken to mitigate these biases in the experimental setup?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including a discussion on the computational cost and efficiency of the proposed methods?
