 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HyperFields, a method that leverages a dynamic hypernetwork to generate NeRFs conditioned on textual prompts. This system learns a mapping from text to NeRFs, enabling zero-shot generation of novel scenes and accelerated convergence for out-of-distribution scenes. The approach involves training a hypernetwork to predict weights of NeRFs, conditioned on text embeddings and previous NeRF activations. This is achieved through a two-stage process: first, training a set of single-prompt NeRFs using Score Distillation Sampling, followed by distilling these NeRFs into the hypernetwork. The method is evaluated on a set of 72 scenes and shows promising results in both in-distribution and out-of-distribution scenarios, demonstrating accelerated convergence and zero-shot generalization capabilities.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to conditioning NeRF generation on textual prompts, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology of using a dynamic hypernetwork to predict the weights of NeRFs, conditioned on text embeddings and previous NeRF activations, is both innovative and effective.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are extensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper's approach allows for zero-shot generation of novel scenes and accelerated convergence for out-of-distribution scenes, which is a significant improvement over existing methods.
- The use of a hypernetwork to learn a mapping from text token embeddings to the space of NeRFs is a novel and effective approach that enables predicting novel in-distribution and out-of-distribution scenes.
- The paper includes a comprehensive ablation study that demonstrates the critical role of the dynamic architecture and NeRF distillation in the expressivity of HyperFields.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other methods, such as DreamFusion, which could help in understanding the relative performance of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient quantitative evaluations, relying heavily on qualitative results which may not fully demonstrate the robustness or generalizability of the method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the scalability of the approach to larger, more complex scenes or the generalization to a wider range of text prompts.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the training time and computational resources required for the proposed method, which could be a significant barrier for practical deployment.
- The paper does not discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be important for understanding the broader implications of the technology.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison with other methods, such as DreamFusion, to better understand the relative performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger, more complex scenes, and what are the scalability limitations?
- Can the authors provide more quantitative evaluations to support the qualitative results presented in the paper?
- How does the proposed method handle a wider range of text prompts, and what are the generalization limitations?
- Could the authors discuss the training time and computational resources required for the proposed method, and how these compare to other methods?
- How does the proposed method handle different text encoders, and what are the implications of using different encoders on the performance of the method?
