 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two new metrics, Total Impact Measurement (TIM) and State Impact Measurement (SIM), to quantify the influence one agent can have on another in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) settings, specifically in the average reward setting. These metrics are designed to measure the impact of one agent's actions on another's expected returns, providing a theoretical framework for understanding and quantifying influence structures in MARL systems. The paper also proposes approximation algorithms for these metrics and discusses their convergence properties. Despite the theoretical contributions, the paper lacks empirical validation and a clear comparison with existing methods, which limits its practical applicability and the strength of its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces novel metrics, TIM and SIM, which provide a new perspective on quantifying the influence one agent can have on another in MARL systems.
- The theoretical framework is robust, supported by rigorous proofs and a clear explanation of the assumptions and limitations.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a logical structure that aids in understanding the complex concepts.
- The paper addresses an important and understudied problem in MARL, which could have significant implications for understanding and improving agent interactions in complex environments.
- The proposed methods are general and can be applied to different settings, including the average reward setting, which is often overlooked in the literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical validation, making it difficult to assess the practical applicability and effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The assumptions made, such as the ergodicity of the Markov chain and the boundedness of the policy, are not clearly justified or discussed, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its methods with existing ones, making it difficult to assess the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not provide a clear application of the metrics in practical scenarios, which could limit the understanding of their real-world utility.
- The paper's focus on the average reward setting might limit its applicability to more common settings like the discounted reward setting.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer discussion of the limitations and assumptions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the ergodicity of the Markov chain and the boundedness of the policy?
- How do the proposed metrics compare to existing methods in terms of effectiveness and applicability?
- Could the authors provide a more concrete application of the metrics in a practical scenario, such as a multi-agent game or a real-world MARL setting?
- How do the authors envision the metrics being used in practice, and what insights could they provide that other metrics might not?
- Could the authors discuss the potential extensions or adaptations of their methods to other settings, such as the discounted reward setting?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations and assumptions of their methods, and what are the implications of these assumptions on the generalizability of the results?
