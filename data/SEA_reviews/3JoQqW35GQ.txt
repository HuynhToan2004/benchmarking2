 **Summary:**
The paper presents a novel approach to solving linear inverse problems using pretrained flow models, aiming to reduce manual tuning requirements. The methodology involves integrating IIGDM gradient correction with flow sampling, allowing for the adaptation of diffusion models to arbitrary paths. This approach is validated across various noisy linear inverse problems on high-dimensional datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to closely related diffusion-based methods. The authors also explore the conversion between flow models and diffusion models, enhancing the versatility of their approach. Despite its innovative approach and empirical validation, the paper is critiqued for its limited scope in terms of dataset variety and the need for further theoretical insights.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for solving linear inverse problems using flow models, which is a significant advancement in the field of image restoration.
- The proposed method is training-free, requiring no fine-tuning of the pretrained flow model, which simplifies the application process.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology, including the mathematical derivations and experimental setups.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing diffusion-based methods in terms of perceptual quality.
- The conversion between flow models and diffusion models is a notable contribution, enhancing the versatility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on solving linear inverse problems, which may limit its applicability to more complex, non-linear inverse problems.
- The dataset used in the experiments is limited to specific datasets, which may not fully represent the generalizability of the method.
- The theoretical justification for the weighting schemes used in the method is lacking, which could affect the reliability and robustness of the results.
- The paper's methodology is somewhat similar to existing methods like IIGDM, with the main difference being the use of flow matching models, which might not be a significant departure from existing techniques.
- The paper's claims of superior performance over diffusion-based methods are not substantiated with a comprehensive comparison across different datasets and noise levels.
- The paper does not adequately address the societal impact of its findings, which could be a critical consideration for the application of the proposed method in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed method be extended to solve non-linear inverse problems, and if so, what modifications would be necessary?
- How does the method perform when applied to other types of inverse problems, such as those involving non-linear degradation models?
- Could the authors provide more details on the theoretical justification for the weighting schemes used in the method?
- What are the specific advantages of using flow models over diffusion models in the context of solving linear inverse problems?
- How does the method handle different types of noise, particularly in scenarios where the noise variance is unknown?
- Could the authors elaborate on the societal impact of their findings, particularly in terms of potential applications and ethical considerations?
