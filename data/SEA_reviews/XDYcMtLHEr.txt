 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel variant of the Lewis Game, termed Multi-Round Indecisive Lewis Game (MRILG), which incorporates a noisy communication channel and multiple interaction rounds. This extension aims to enhance the robustness of communication protocols by allowing agents to handle varying levels of noise and uncertainty. The authors propose a new listener architecture that utilizes a meta-action to decide whether to ask for another round or make a prediction. The paper evaluates this architecture against various game variants and baseline models, demonstrating its effectiveness in noisy environments. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its close resemblance to existing works, particularly the Lewis Game, and the lack of significant empirical evidence supporting the claimed advancements.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of emergent communication, focusing on the robustness of communication protocols in noisy environments.
- The authors introduce a novel game variant, MRILG, which extends the original Lewis Game by incorporating a noisy communication channel and multiple interaction rounds, enhancing the complexity and realism of the communication scenario.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of a new listener architecture that incorporates a meta-action to decide whether to ask for another round or make a prediction is a novel approach that could potentially improve communication efficiency and robustness.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, comparing it to various game variants and baseline models, which helps to validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is limited as it closely resembles existing works, particularly the Lewis Game, and does not sufficiently differentiate itself from these prior studies.
- The evaluation of the proposed method is not robust enough, with insufficient empirical evidence to support the claims of its effectiveness and robustness.
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the listener's action space and the reward function, which could lead to confusion about the game's rules and incentives.
- The paper's methodology and experimental setup could benefit from more detailed descriptions and justifications, especially regarding the choice of parameters and the impact of different settings on the results.
- The paper's contribution to the field is not clearly articulated, particularly in terms of its novelty and the specific advancements it offers over existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to existing works, particularly in terms of the game's design and the listener's architecture?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of performance and robustness, especially in scenarios with varying levels of noise and uncertainty?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the listener's action space and the reward function, particularly in how these elements influence the game's dynamics and the agents' decision-making processes?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex environments or scenarios, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in scaling up the approach to larger or more complex settings?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of parameters and settings in their experiments, and how these choices impact the results and the generalizability of the findings?
