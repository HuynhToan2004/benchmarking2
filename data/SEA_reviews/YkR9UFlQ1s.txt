 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Non-Backtracking Graph Neural Network (NBA-GNN), which addresses the issue of redundant message passing in traditional GNNs by avoiding backtracking. This approach is supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results, showing improved performance in various graph learning tasks such as node classification and graph classification. The authors demonstrate that NBA-GNN can enhance the expressivity of GNNs and reduce the over-squashing problem, as evidenced by improved performance on benchmark datasets. However, the paper also highlights the increased computational cost and potential limitations in handling large-scale graphs with high average degree.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in GNNs by proposing a novel approach to reduce redundancy in message passing, which is a significant issue in traditional GNNs.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust and supports the proposed method, including a sensitivity analysis and a discussion on the over-squashing problem.
- The empirical results are strong, showing significant improvements over baselines in various tasks, and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow.
- The concept of non-backtracking in GNNs is novel and potentially impactful, as it could lead to more efficient and effective graph learning models.
- The paper is well-positioned within the current literature, highlighting its relevance and the importance of the addressed problem.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other recent works that have also addressed the problem of backtracking in GNNs, which could provide a more comprehensive view of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The computational cost of NBA-GNN is higher than traditional GNNs, which might limit its applicability in large-scale or resource-constrained environments.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the handling of large-scale graphs with high average degree, which is a common scenario in many applications.
- The theoretical results, such as Theorem 1, are not convincingly supported, and the proofs provided in the appendix are not detailed enough to fully understand the claims made.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive experimental validation, including comparisons with other non-backtracking GNNs and a broader range of datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the comparison with other recent works that have addressed similar issues in GNNs?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational cost and efficiency with other non-backtracking GNNs?
- Can the authors clarify the proofs and theoretical results, particularly Theorem 1, to make the claims more convincing?
- In the context of large-scale graphs with high average degree, how does NBA-GNN perform? Are there specific scenarios where it might struggle or fail to provide significant improvements?
- How does NBA-GNN handle graphs with different types of edges, such as directed or weighted graphs?
- The paper mentions that NBA-GNN might be more effective in sparse graphs. Can the authors provide more insights or experimental evidence to support this claim?
