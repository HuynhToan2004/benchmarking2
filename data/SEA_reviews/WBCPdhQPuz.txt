 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DAS2C, a distributed adaptive algorithm for solving nonconvex-strongly-concave minimax problems. It addresses the challenges of inconsistency in locally computed adaptive step sizes by employing an adaptive stepsize control protocol that ensures consistency among nodes. The algorithm is designed to achieve near-optimal convergence rates and is compared against existing methods in both theoretical and experimental settings. The paper presents a counterexample to demonstrate the potential non-convergence of naive distributed adaptive methods and provides a convergence analysis of the proposed algorithm. Despite the theoretical and experimental validations, concerns are raised about the novelty and the practical applicability of the algorithm, particularly in settings where the assumptions may not hold.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem of nonconvex minimax problems in a distributed setting, which is crucial for many real-world applications.
- The proposed algorithm, DAS2C, is simple, easy to implement, and does not require knowledge of problem-dependent parameters, making it practical for use in various scenarios.
- The paper provides a comprehensive convergence analysis, including a counterexample that demonstrates the potential non-convergence of naive distributed adaptive methods, which helps in understanding the challenges and the necessity of the proposed algorithm.
- The algorithm is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world tasks, showing its effectiveness and robustness across different settings.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, which is crucial for its accessibility and applicability to a broader audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as the algorithm and the analysis closely resemble those in previous works, particularly the TiAda algorithm. The main contribution seems to be the application of TiAda to a distributed setting, which may not be sufficient for a high-tier conference like ICLR.
- The paper relies on strong assumptions, such as the strong concavity in the dual variable and the boundedness of the gradient, which may not hold in practical scenarios.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some figures showing similar performance across different methods, which does not clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithm.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the convergence analysis, particularly in explaining how the inconsistency terms in the algorithm are handled and the implications of these terms on the convergence rate.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which could detract from its professionalism and clarity.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison between DAS2C and TiAda, highlighting the specific contributions and improvements of DAS2C?
- How does the proposed algorithm handle the strong concavity assumption in practical scenarios, especially when this assumption may not hold?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the convergence analysis, particularly how the inconsistency terms are handled and their impact on the convergence rate?
- How does the proposed algorithm perform in scenarios where the assumptions do not hold, and what are the implications of these assumptions on the practical applicability of the algorithm?
- The paper mentions a counterexample where the iterates are stationary. Can the authors clarify whether this is a desirable outcome or if it indicates a failure of the algorithm?
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper. Could these be corrected to improve the readability and professionalism of the paper?
