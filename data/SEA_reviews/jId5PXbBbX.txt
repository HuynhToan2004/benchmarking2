 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel UCB-type algorithm for learning predictive state representations (PSRs) in both online and offline settings. This algorithm is designed to address the computational and statistical challenges of learning PSRs, which are crucial for sequential decision-making problems in POMDPs and other general settings. The authors propose a new bonus term that upper bounds the total variation distance between the estimated and true models, ensuring computational tractability and sample complexity. The theoretical contributions are supported by rigorous proofs and comparisons with existing methods, demonstrating the algorithm's efficiency and effectiveness. The paper also discusses the potential of extending the algorithm to other models and settings, suggesting a broader applicability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and structured, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The introduction of a UCB-type algorithm for learning PSRs is a novel approach, which is both computationally tractable and statistically efficient.
- The algorithm guarantees a last-iterate guarantee, ensuring the output of a near-optimal policy.
- The paper provides theoretical analysis and proofs, which are sound and support the claims made.
- The proposed algorithm is the first of its kind to handle PSRs in both online and offline settings, offering a new perspective on the application of UCB-type algorithms in this context.
- The paper is original in its approach, using a UCB-type algorithm to address the challenges of learning PSRs, which are significant in the field of reinforcement learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithm, particularly in scenarios where it may not perform optimally or where it may not be applicable.
- There is a lack of experimental validation to support the claims made about the algorithm's efficiency and effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the assumptions made, especially in the context of PSRs and their applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and the organization of content, especially in the appendices.
- The paper could provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods, including computational and statistical complexities, to better highlight the advantages of the proposed algorithm.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the societal impact of the proposed algorithms, particularly in terms of fairness and bias in decision-making processes.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and usage of the term "physical meaning" as it is not well-defined in the paper?
- How does the algorithm perform in scenarios where the assumptions made, such as the well-conditioned PSR assumption, do not hold?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, especially in comparison to existing methods?
- Is there a potential for the algorithm to be extended to other models or settings beyond PSRs? If so, what are the main challenges and potential solutions?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the UCB coefficient in the algorithm and its impact on the performance of the algorithm?
- How does the algorithm handle the exploration-exploitation trade-off in practical scenarios, and what are the implications of this for the overall performance of the algorithm?
- Can the authors provide more details on the assumptions made in the algorithm, particularly in terms of their realism and applicability in different settings?
