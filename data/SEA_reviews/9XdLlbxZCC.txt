 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MC-JEPA, a method that integrates self-supervised learning with optical flow estimation, aiming to enhance the learning of both motion and content features in videos. The methodology involves a joint-embedding predictive architecture that enables the sharing of an encoder between the self-supervised learning and optical flow estimation tasks. The approach is evaluated on various datasets, showing promising results in optical flow estimation and content feature learning, particularly in tasks like instance segmentation and video segmentation. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and comprehensive explanations of the methodology and the results.
- The integration of self-supervised learning with optical flow estimation is a novel approach, which allows for the joint learning of motion and content features in videos.
- The methodology is evaluated on a wide range of datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in various downstream tasks such as instance segmentation and video segmentation.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the training process, including the balancing of loss terms and the impact of different components on performance.
- The authors have conducted extensive ablation studies to understand the effects of different components and hyperparameters on the performance of the model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the specific problem it aims to solve, particularly in terms of its contributions to the field of self-supervised learning.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as the integration of self-supervised learning with optical flow estimation has been previously explored.
- The performance of the method is not consistently superior to existing methods, as shown in some comparisons, particularly in optical flow estimation and content feature learning.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a discussion on the specific advantages of the proposed method over these approaches.
- The paper suffers from some typographical errors and unclear figures, which could be improved for better clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific advantages of their approach over existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the method perform when applied to other datasets or tasks beyond those tested in the paper?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational costs and training times associated with their method, especially in comparison to other methods?
- In the ablation studies, what were the specific reasons for the observed performance improvements or declines when different components were introduced or removed?
- The paper mentions a lack of clear motivation for the problem being solved. Can the authors elaborate on the practical applications or theoretical implications of their method?
