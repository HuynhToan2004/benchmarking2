 **Summary:**
The paper explores the label-only membership inference attacks (MIAs) in the context of data poisoning, proposing a novel method named Chameleon. This method leverages an adaptive poisoning strategy to enhance the effectiveness of MIAs, focusing on the low false positive rate (FPR) regime. The authors demonstrate that existing MIAs struggle in this regime and propose a new method to address this issue. The paper presents a theoretical analysis and empirical results, showing that the Chameleon attack can significantly improve the true positive rate (TPR) compared to existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the proposed method due to its high computational complexity and the need for a large number of queries and model training.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for label-only membership inference attacks (MIAs) that effectively addresses the challenges in the low false positive rate (FPR) regime.
- The theoretical analysis provided is insightful, and the experimental results are well-designed and support the claims made.
- The proposed method is practical, efficient, and simple, requiring only 64 queries to the target model to succeed in the distinguishing test, significantly less than previous methods.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the problem and the proposed solution.
- The authors provide a good discussion on the limitations of their work, which is appreciated for its transparency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the practicality of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the large number of queries required and the computational complexity introduced by the need to train multiple models.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to scenarios where the attacker has access to the training data distribution, which may not be practical in real-world applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the robustness of the method against different types of defenses, such as differential privacy.
- The paper's title is misleading as it claims to be the first analysis on the impact of poisoning on MIAs, but similar works have been previously published.
- The paper's experimental setup is not detailed enough, and the results presented in the abstract are not adequately supported by the main text.
- The paper's claims about the effectiveness of the proposed method are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which are limited to a few datasets and models.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the attacker does not have access to the training data distribution?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the poisoning strategy affects the performance of the model in terms of accuracy?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing MIAs that use confidence scores, and what are the implications of using different types of scores like logits?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the OUT models in the adaptive poisoning stage and whether they are trained on the poisoned dataset?
- How does the method perform against different types of defenses, such as differential privacy, and what are the implications of using stronger defenses like DP-SGD?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including a broader range of datasets and models, to support the claims made about the effectiveness of the proposed method?

**Paper Decision:**
