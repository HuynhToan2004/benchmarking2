 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ChannelViT, a novel variant of the Vision Transformer (ViT) architecture designed for multi-channel imaging tasks. ChannelViT creates patch tokens from each input channel and incorporates learnable channel embeddings to preserve channel-specific information. A new regularization technique, Hierarchical Channel Sampling (HCS), is proposed to enhance the robustness of the model when only partial channels are available during testing. The paper evaluates ChannelViT on datasets such as ImageNet, JUMP-CP, and So2Sat, showing improvements over ViT in classification tasks and generalization capabilities. However, concerns are raised about the novelty and the marginal performance improvements over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel ChannelViT architecture that effectively addresses the challenges of multi-channel imaging, particularly in domains like microscopy and satellite imaging.
- The proposed Hierarchical Channel Sampling (HCS) technique is a significant contribution, enhancing the robustness of the model by ensuring uniform weighting across different channel combinations.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over the baseline ViT on various datasets such as ImageNet, JUMP-CP, and So2Sat.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including comparisons with other models and ablation studies that help in understanding the contributions of different components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art models such as FANs, which could provide a clearer understanding of the model's performance relative to current research.
- The performance improvements over the baseline ViT are marginal, and the paper does not adequately address why these improvements occur.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, particularly in terms of training and inference times, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the generalization capabilities of the model, especially in scenarios where the model is trained on one channel and tested on another.
- The paper's claims regarding the robustness of the model to channel sparsity are not adequately supported, and there is a need for more experimental validation to substantiate these claims.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, including training and inference times?
- How does the model perform when trained on one channel and tested on another, particularly in scenarios where the test channel is not present during training?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of extending the model to handle multi-channel classification tasks, where each channel corresponds to a distinct class?
- Can the authors elaborate on the decision-making process behind the choice of parameters such as the number of channels and the sampling method in Hierarchical Channel Sampling?
- How does the model handle scenarios where the input channels are not uniformly distributed, and what are the implications of this for the model's performance?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including standard deviations, to better support the claims made in the paper?
