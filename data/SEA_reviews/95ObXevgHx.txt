 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the temporal dynamics of language processing in the human brain using a deep language model (GPT2-XL) and electroencephalography (ECoG) data. The study examines how the neural activity in the brain correlates with the model's internal processing layers, suggesting a temporal hierarchy in language comprehension. The authors find that the sequence of neural activity in the brain matches the sequence of processing in GPT2-XL, with earlier layers responding earlier in time and later layers responding later. This study provides a novel approach to understanding the neural mechanisms of language processing and suggests that deep language models can serve as a framework for understanding brain dynamics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed methods and results presented.
- The use of ECoG data to study the temporal dynamics of language processing is innovative and provides a higher temporal resolution compared to traditional methods like fMRI.
- The study demonstrates that the sequence of neural activity in the brain matches the sequence of processing in GPT2-XL, which is a significant finding in understanding the neural mechanisms of language processing.
- The paper contributes to the ongoing research on how the brain processes language, providing a novel perspective on how deep language models can serve as a framework for understanding brain dynamics.
- The authors provide a detailed explanation of the methodology, including the use of linear encoding models to predict neural activity, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on the correlation between the temporal dynamics of neural activity and the processing layers of GPT2-XL, but does not explore the relationship between specific layers of GPT2-XL and the neural activity in different brain areas.
- There is a lack of discussion on the implications of the findings for the development of more effective language models or for improving our understanding of language processing in the brain.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from previous studies that have also used ECoG data to investigate the neural correlates of language processing.
- The paper's claims about the unique contributions of intermediate representations induced by the layers and the dynamic of language comprehension in the brain are not well-supported by the provided evidence.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the study, such as the generalizability of the findings to other models or the impact of different training datasets on the results.

**Questions:**
