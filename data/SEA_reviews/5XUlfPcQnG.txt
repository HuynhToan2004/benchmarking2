 **Summary:**
The paper presents a novel simulation-based approach for training Reinforcement Learning (RL) agents to optimize energy efficiency in commercial buildings. The authors introduce a lightweight simulator calibrated with real-world data to train agents, focusing on minimizing energy consumption and carbon emissions. The simulator uses a finite difference method to approximate heat transfer and a simplified HVAC model to manage temperature and energy. The paper also discusses the challenges of simulating complex building systems and proposes a method for offline training of RL agents in a simulated environment. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of technical depth, limited experimental validation, and inadequate discussion on the practical implications of its findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of HVAC control, focusing on reducing energy consumption and carbon emissions in commercial buildings.
- The proposed method for offline training of RL agents in a simulated environment is innovative and could potentially be scaled up to thousands of buildings.
- The simulator is designed to be lightweight and calibrated with real-world data, which enhances its accuracy and applicability.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors have demonstrated the effectiveness of their approach by training an RL agent on the simulator and showing that it can learn an improved policy.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the technical challenges and methodological aspects of simulating complex building systems, which could limit its applicability to other domains.
- The experimental validation is limited, with only one simulated environment used, which may not adequately demonstrate the scalability or generalizability of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of buildings and its scalability to larger, more complex systems.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of its findings, such as the potential impact on real-world energy consumption and carbon emissions.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods and a deeper analysis of the performance of the RL agent trained on the simulator.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the simulator was calibrated using real-world data and how this calibration affects the accuracy and applicability of the simulator?
- How does the simulator handle the variability in building layouts and HVAC configurations, and what are the implications for its scalability and applicability to different types of buildings?
- Can you discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method in terms of its applicability to different types of buildings and its scalability to larger, more complex systems?
- How does the RL agent trained on the simulator compare to other existing methods in terms of energy efficiency and carbon emission reduction?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup and the results of the RL agent's performance in real-world scenarios?
