 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the application of coreset theory in clustering with noisy data, proposing a new coreset definition that considers the ratio of costs for different center sets. This new definition is aimed at addressing the limitations of traditional coreset definitions in noisy environments, where the optimal center set may not be significantly different from other center sets. The authors provide theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support their claims, comparing the proposed coreset with traditional measures under various noise conditions. The paper also discusses the practical implications of the new coreset definition, particularly in terms of its robustness to noise and its applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an interesting and relevant problem in the context of coreset theory for clustering with noisy data, which is a significant practical concern in many applications.
- The authors propose a novel coreset definition that considers the ratio of costs for different center sets, which is a novel approach that could potentially lead to more robust and effective coresets.
- The theoretical analysis provided is solid, with clear proofs and a well-structured presentation that aids in understanding the complex concepts.
- The empirical results are convincing, showing that the proposed coreset definition outperforms traditional measures in terms of robustness to noise.
- The paper is well-written and accessible, with clear explanations of the theoretical results and their practical implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes a specific noise model (noise model I) which might not be the most general or practical in all scenarios, limiting the applicability of the results.
- The proofs and assumptions made in the paper, particularly in theorems and lemmas, are not always clearly explained or justified, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the implications and assumptions behind the results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implications and limitations of the proposed coreset definition, particularly in terms of its computational complexity and scalability.
- There are several minor typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected to improve the readability and professionalism of the paper.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed coreset definition and potential directions for future research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions and implications of the Bernstein condition used in the paper, particularly in relation to the noise model I?
- How does the proposed coreset definition perform under different noise models, such as noise model II, which might be more practical in certain scenarios?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the proofs and assumptions used in the theorems and lemmas, particularly in the appendices where these proofs are discussed?
- How does the proposed coreset definition compare in terms of computational complexity and scalability with other coreset definitions, and what are the practical implications of these differences?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending the proposed coreset definition to other learning tasks beyond clustering, and what are the challenges and opportunities in doing so?
