 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedRC, a novel algorithm for Federated Learning (FL) that addresses the challenges of concept shifts by employing robust clustering techniques. FedRC groups clients with similar data distributions into clusters, allowing for the training of separate models for each cluster. This approach is contrasted with existing methods like FedAvg and other clustered FL methods, showing improvements in performance across various datasets and neural network architectures. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the clustering method and provides a convergence analysis. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of presentation, and the lack of comprehensive experimental validation, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the scalability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of federated learning, focusing on the concept shift, which is crucial for improving the performance of federated learning models.
- The proposed method, FedRC, is well-motivated and clearly explained, with a detailed algorithm description that aids in understanding the implementation.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in various datasets and neural network architectures.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the convergence rate of the clustering algorithm, which adds to the theoretical robustness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the inclusion of figures and examples enhances the clarity of the presentation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation from existing methods, particularly in the clustering approach, which appears similar to existing methods like FedEM.
- The theoretical analysis, particularly the convergence rate, is not adequately supported by experimental results, which could undermine the credibility of the claims.
- The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in certain sections, particularly in the explanation of the objective function and the convergence analysis.
- There is a notable absence of experimental validation on larger datasets and more complex neural network architectures, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The computational efficiency of the proposed method is not discussed, which is a significant concern for federated learning applications where communication costs are critical.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method to larger datasets or a larger number of clients, which could limit its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between their proposed method and existing methods like FedEM, particularly in terms of the clustering approach and its impact on performance?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, especially in terms of the number of clients and the dataset size?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the convergence rate of the proposed method and its comparison with other existing methods?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational efficiency, and what are the implications for federated learning applications where communication costs are critical?
- Can the authors elaborate on the convergence analysis and the assumptions made, particularly in relation to the smoothness and bounded gradient assumptions?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of concept shift, and can this be validated through experiments or additional theoretical analysis?
