 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for decision-time planning (DTP) in imperfect-information games, focusing on the update equivalence between global and local algorithms. This framework, which includes Mirror Descent Update Equivalent Search (MD-UES) and Magnetic Mirror Descent Update Equivalent Search (MMD-UES), aims to address the challenges of handling large amounts of non-public information in games. The framework is evaluated against state-of-the-art methods in Hanabi and other games, showing competitive or superior performance. The authors propose that this approach can be applied to settings with large amounts of private information, such as games with complex information structures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework for decision-time planning (DTP) in imperfect-information games, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The framework is well-explained and easy to follow, with clear definitions and detailed explanations of the algorithms and their implications.
- The experiments conducted are extensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms in various settings, including against state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper provides a clear and concise presentation of the framework and its applications, making it accessible to readers with a background in the field.
- The theoretical results are well-supported by empirical evidence, showing the practical applicability of the framework.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the specific advantages of the proposed algorithms over existing ones.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework, which could affect its applicability in more complex or varied game scenarios.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently detailed, with some results being relegated to the appendix, which could make it difficult for readers to fully understand the implications of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the generalizability of the framework to other types of games or scenarios, particularly those with different information structures or rules.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed theoretical discussions and proofs for the claims made, especially regarding the convergence and optimality of the proposed algorithms.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific advantages of their proposed algorithms over existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- How does the framework perform in games with different information structures or rules, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in applying it to such games?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, possibly including a comparison with a broader range of existing methods and different game scenarios?
- How does the framework handle scenarios where the information available to players is incomplete or uncertain, and what are the implications of this for the performance of the proposed algorithms?
- Are there any specific scenarios or types of games where the framework is not applicable, and if so, what are the reasons for these limitations?
