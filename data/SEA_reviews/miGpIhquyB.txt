 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) in generating synthetic datasets for classification tasks. It introduces a novel evaluation framework that assesses the quality of generated datasets based on five distinct characteristics: faithfulness, diversity, conformity, complexity, and performance. The study explores how these characteristics interact, revealing trade-offs between them, and evaluates various models across different training paradigms. The paper also compares instruction-tuned and vanilla models, demonstrating how instruction tuning affects the generation of synthetic datasets. The evaluation framework is tested across different domains and model families, providing insights into the generative capabilities of LLMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel evaluation framework for synthetic data generation by LLMs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation across different models, training paradigms, and domains, which is commendable for its thoroughness.
- The study reveals interesting trade-offs between various characteristics of generated datasets, offering insights into the behavior of LLMs in dataset generation.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the figures are clear, enhancing the readability and comprehension of the content.
- The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the topic and provide valuable insights into the characteristics of generated datasets, which is crucial for understanding the capabilities and limitations of LLMs in dataset generation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the choice of metrics used, particularly the rationale behind using the Distinctness-k metric and the choice of classifiers.
- The scope of the study is limited to classification tasks, which might not fully capture the generative capabilities of LLMs across other tasks.
- There is a potential bias in the evaluation due to the use of DistilBERT as the classifier, which might not accurately reflect the performance of other models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing datasets and models to establish a baseline for the generated datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the evaluation framework, particularly the potential biases introduced by the choice of reference datasets and the classifier.
- The evaluation is limited to a few domains, which might not fully capture the generative capabilities of LLMs across a broader range of applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of why the Distinctness-k metric was chosen and how it was adapted for the study?
- How do the authors address the potential biases introduced by using DistilBERT as the classifier, and what steps were taken to ensure the fairness of the evaluation?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of reference datasets and how they were selected to ensure their relevance and representativeness?
- How do the authors plan to address the potential limitations of the evaluation framework, particularly in terms of the scope of domains and the choice of models evaluated?
- Can the authors provide more details on the prompting strategy used for generating synthetic datasets, especially how it influences the results observed?
- How do the authors justify the choice of metrics and the evaluation framework, particularly in light of the potential biases and limitations discussed?
