 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a unified framework named UGSL for benchmarking graph structure learning (GSL) methods. It integrates over ten existing methods and thousands of architectures, offering a comprehensive analysis of their strengths and weaknesses. The framework aims to provide a unified perspective on GSL methods, enabling a deeper understanding of their components and their impact on performance. The authors conducted extensive experiments across six datasets, analyzing the effects of different components and architectures. Despite its strengths in unifying diverse GSL methods and providing a systematic benchmarking approach, the paper faces criticisms for its lack of novelty, limited dataset diversity, and potential for deeper analysis of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a unified framework for benchmarking graph structure learning (GSL) methods, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- It integrates over ten existing methods and thousands of architectures, offering a comprehensive analysis of their strengths and weaknesses.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the methodology is clear and well-explained.
- Extensive experiments were conducted across six datasets, analyzing the effects of different components and architectures, which provides valuable insights for future research in this area.
- The paper is a valuable resource for the GSL community, offering a benchmark for future research and insights that will help researchers design more effective GSL models.
- The authors are committed to open-sourcing both their code and data upon the acceptance of their paper, allowing other researchers to reproduce their results, build on their work, and use their code to develop their own GSL models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it primarily focuses on integrating existing methods without introducing new components or architectures.
- The dataset diversity is limited, with the experiments conducted only on six datasets, which might not adequately represent the full spectrum of applications in GSL.
- The analysis of the results is limited, focusing on best-performing components rather than providing a deeper analysis of why certain components perform better or worse.
- The paper does not provide a clear rationale for why the proposed unified framework is necessary or how it contributes significantly to the field beyond existing methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently address scalability issues, which could limit its applicability to large-scale datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art GSL methods, which could help in understanding the relative performance of the proposed framework.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed framework, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential shortcomings.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed rationale for why the proposed unified framework is necessary and how it contributes significantly to the field beyond existing methods?
- How do you plan to address the scalability issues to make the framework applicable to large-scale datasets?
- Can you provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art GSL methods to help understand the relative performance of the proposed framework?
- What are the limitations of the proposed framework, and how do these impact its applicability and effectiveness?
- How do you plan to address the potential for overfitting in the models due to the complexity of the framework?
- Could you provide more insights into the graph structures learned by GSL methods and their correlation with downstream quality?
- How does the proposed framework handle the scalability issues, particularly in large-scale datasets?
- Could you discuss the potential for extending the framework to other tasks beyond node classification, such as graph classification or link prediction?
