 **Summary:**
The paper explores the evaluation of generative models in distributed learning tasks with heterogeneous data distributions, focusing on the inconsistencies in model rankings when using different aggregation methods for metrics like FID and KID. It demonstrates that FID-avg and FID-all can lead to different optimal models, while KID-all and KID-avg yield consistent rankings. The theoretical analysis is supported by experiments on synthetic and real image datasets, showcasing the practical implications of these findings. The paper also discusses the limitations of FID in federated learning and proposes KID as a potentially superior alternative.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue of evaluating generative models in federated learning scenarios, where data is distributed across multiple clients, each with potentially different data distributions.
- The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the inconsistencies in model rankings based on different aggregation methods.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and easy-to-follow proofs, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The findings are significant, particularly the observation that KID-all and KID-avg yield the same rankings, which is not the case for FID.
- The paper is well-motivated, with a clear focus on the challenges of evaluating generative models in federated learning settings, and the experiments are well-designed to support the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on FID as the primary evaluation metric could limit its applicability to other types of data, such as text and audio, where FID might not be as effective.
- The experimental results are somewhat limited, focusing mainly on image data and not exploring other types of data or different federated learning algorithms.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation of other metrics beyond FID and KID, such as precision/recall and density/coverage scores.
- The paper's conclusions are somewhat limited by the scope of the experiments, which are primarily based on synthetic data and small-scale real-world data.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential limitations of the findings, such as the assumption of convexity in the analysis and the applicability of the results to larger-scale federated learning scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their findings, particularly the assumptions made in the analysis and the applicability of the results to larger-scale federated learning scenarios?
- How does the paper's focus on FID as the primary evaluation metric affect the applicability of the findings to other types of data, such as text and audio?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including a broader range of data types and federated learning algorithms?
- What are the potential implications of the findings for the design of federated learning algorithms specifically tailored for generative model training?
- How do the authors envision the use of KID as a potential replacement for FID in federated learning scenarios, and what are the potential challenges or limitations associated with this approach?
