 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SiBBInGS, a novel framework for discovering functional building blocks (BBs) in multi-state time-series data. SiBBInGS leverages a graph-based dictionary learning approach to handle variability in trial duration, missing samples, and varied sampling rates. The method is designed to be resilient to noise and random initializations, and it enables the identification of state-specific and state-invariant BBs. The paper provides extensive evaluations on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the framework's ability to recover ground truth BBs and identify meaningful patterns in various domains such as web search and brain recordings. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the presentation of results and the discussion of related work.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework, SiBBInGS, which addresses the challenge of discovering fundamental representational units in multi-dimensional data, specifically focusing on functional building blocks (BBs) that can adjust their temporal activity and component structure across trials.
- The framework is robust, capable of handling variability in trial duration, missing samples, and varied sampling rates, and is resilient to noise and random initializations.
- SiBBInGS enables the identification of state-specific vs. state-invariant BBs and allows for controlled variations in BB structure and per-trial temporal variability.
- The paper provides extensive evaluations on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the framework's ability to recover ground truth BBs and identify meaningful patterns in various domains such as web search and brain recordings.
- The methodology is clearly explained, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the methodology, particularly in the sections describing the data model and the algorithm.
- The presentation of results, especially in figures, could be improved for better clarity and understanding.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with related works, particularly in the areas of tensor factorization and graph-based methods.
- The paper's reliance on certain assumptions, such as the boundedness of the temporal activity matrix and the correlation between different BBs, may limit its applicability in certain scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the data model and the algorithm used in SiBBInGS, particularly how the data is modeled and how the algorithm operates?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- What are the specific contributions of the SiBBInGS framework compared to existing methods, and how does it address the limitations of these methods?
- How does the method handle missing data, and what are the implications of this in practical applications?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications of the method in various domains?
- How does the method handle different types of data, such as continuous data, and what are the implications of this in terms of data preprocessing and analysis?
