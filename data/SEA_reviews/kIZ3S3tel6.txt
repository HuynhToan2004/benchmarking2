 **Summary:**
The paper explores a novel phenomenon in neural network optimization, focusing on the influence of paired groups of outliers with strong opposing signals. These outliers, characterized by consistent, large magnitude features, significantly affect the training dynamics by causing early optimization to enter a narrow valley that balances opposing groups, leading to subsequent sharpening and rapid loss spikes. The paper provides both theoretical and experimental evidence, including a two-layer linear network analysis and experiments on various datasets, to substantiate these observations. It also discusses the implications of these findings for modern training practices and suggests modifications to existing optimization algorithms to enhance performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper identifies a new phenomenon in neural network optimization, providing a novel perspective on the training dynamics and their causes.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the paper is clearly written, making the findings accessible and understandable.
- The paper introduces a new theoretical framework that explains the behavior of neural networks under certain conditions, contributing to a better understanding of the training dynamics.
- The findings have practical implications, suggesting modifications to existing optimization algorithms that could potentially improve performance.
- The paper connects the identified phenomenon to other related phenomena, providing a more comprehensive view of neural network optimization.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a formal definition for "opposing signals," which could limit the rigor and clarity of the analysis.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed or robust, with some results (e.g., the effects of opposing signals on different datasets) not being thoroughly explored.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive explanation for why opposing signals exist or how they affect different datasets, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from more rigorous theoretical analysis and quantitative measurements to support the claims made about the effects of opposing signals.
- The paper's claims about the effects of opposing signals on different datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10 vs. CIFAR-100) are not sufficiently substantiated, and the results are not consistently applied across different datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide a formal definition for "opposing signals" and clarify how they are identified and measured in the experiments?
- How do opposing signals affect the training dynamics in different datasets, particularly in those where the effects are not as pronounced?
- Could you elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of the observed effects of opposing signals on neural network training, and how these relate to the overall optimization process?
- How do the results of this study compare to other related works that have also explored the effects of opposing signals in neural network training?
- Can you provide more detailed experimental results, including quantitative measurements and comparisons across different datasets, to support the claims made about the effects of opposing signals?
