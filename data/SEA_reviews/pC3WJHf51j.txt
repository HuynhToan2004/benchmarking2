 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel self-supervised learning framework to pre-train foundation models on wearable biosignals, specifically photoplethysmography (PPG) and electrocardiogram (ECG) data from the Apple Heart and Movement Study (AHMS). This framework aims to generalize well across different health conditions and demographics, leveraging a large-scale dataset of over 140,000 participants. The pre-trained models are shown to encode health information, and the paper includes extensive ablation studies to evaluate the effectiveness of various components such as positive pair selection and augmentation techniques. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its limited evaluation scope, focusing only on demographic information, and the lack of direct comparisons with existing models or clinical evaluations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is the first to pre-train foundation models using large-scale PPG and ECG data collected via wearable consumer devices, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed self-supervised learning framework is well-designed, including a participant level positive pair selection strategy, stochastic augmentation module, and regularized contrastive loss optimized with momentum training, which is innovative and effective.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods, including ablation studies that showcase the importance of different components of the self-supervised learning framework.
- The application of self-supervised learning to wearable biosignals is promising, especially in the context of large-scale datasets, and the results demonstrate that the pre-trained foundation models encode health information effectively.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a direct comparison with existing models or clinical evaluations, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness and applicability of the proposed models in real-world scenarios.
- The evaluation is limited to demographic information, which may not fully capture the potential of the pre-trained models. Expanding the evaluation to include more diverse health conditions or clinical outcomes could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the models' capabilities.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the models perform on downstream tasks beyond demographic information, such as disease prediction or health outcome prediction, which are critical for assessing the practical utility of the models.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the generalizability of the models across different populations or subgroups, which is a significant concern given the diversity of the AHMS dataset.
- The paper does not discuss the potential ethical implications of using self-supervised learning on sensitive health data, which is a critical issue that needs to be addressed.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the performance of the models on downstream tasks beyond demographic information? What are the specific health conditions or clinical outcomes that the models can predict effectively?
- How do the models perform on different subgroups of the population, such as those with different ethnicities or health conditions? Are there any notable differences in model performance across these groups?
- How do the models handle the potential for bias in the data, especially given the large and diverse dataset used?
- Could the authors clarify the rationale behind the choice of participant level positive pair selection over segment level positive pair selection? What are the specific advantages of using participant level pairs, and how do they impact the model's performance?
- How do the authors address the issue of potential overfitting to the demographic information in the data? What steps have been taken to ensure that the models are not simply memorizing this information without learning more complex health patterns?
- Could the authors discuss the potential ethical implications of using self-supervised learning on sensitive health data, and how they plan to address these concerns in future work?
