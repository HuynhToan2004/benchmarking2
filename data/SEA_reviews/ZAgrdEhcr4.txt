 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to enhance the efficiency of evolutionary algorithms (EAs) in solving large-scale multi-objective optimization problems (LMOPs) by integrating a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) trained to learn the improvement representations of solutions. This approach, termed deep improvement representation (DIR), is designed to accelerate the convergence of evolutionary populations towards global optimality. The paper presents a methodology that iteratively stacks MLPs to create a larger model, which is then used to learn the DIR of solutions, thereby improving the optimization process. Experiments were conducted using benchmark problems to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing significant improvements in convergence speed and solution quality. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the approach, the clarity of presentation, and the limited scope of experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning improvement representations, which is a significant contribution to the field of evolutionary algorithms.
- The proposed method is well-motivated and clearly explained, making it accessible to readers.
- The use of deep learning to enhance the search process in evolutionary algorithms is an interesting and promising direction.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow descriptions of the proposed method and the experiments.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in solving multi-objective optimization problems, showing improvements in convergence speed and solution quality.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of existing methods for large-scale multi-objective optimization, which is beneficial for readers unfamiliar with the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.
- The presentation of the paper needs improvement, particularly in the clarity of the figures and the organization of the content.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The experimental validation is limited to synthetic problems, which may not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in real-world applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting in the training of the MLP, which could affect the generalization ability of the model.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for assessing its practical applicability.
- The paper's contribution is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods such as those by Mittal et al. (2021), and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the MLP in the proposed method, particularly in terms of its contribution to the overall performance?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of overfitting in the training of the MLP, and what steps have been taken to mitigate this risk?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, including the training and inference times for the MLP?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and solution quality?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential negative societal impacts and practical constraints?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of diversity in the solutions, which is crucial for effective optimization?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the training data on the performance of the MLP, and discuss the implications of using different training data sets?
