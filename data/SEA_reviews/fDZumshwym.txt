 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel data condensation method using a Hierarchical Memory Network (HMN) architecture, which consists of three tiers: dataset-level, class-level, and instance-level memories. The dataset-level memory stores general features shared by all images, while class-level and instance-level memories encode class-specific and instance-specific features, respectively. The method employs gradient matching loss to ensure the condensed data maintains the same distribution as the original data. The paper also includes a pruning method to reduce redundancy in the condensed data, improving data efficiency. The method is evaluated against various baselines and shows superior performance in terms of accuracy and efficiency. However, the paper lacks comprehensive experimental results and theoretical analysis, and the method's scalability and the generalizability of the results are questionable.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel data parameterization method, Hierarchical Memory Network (HMN), which effectively reduces data redundancy by pruning images based on their importance.
- The proposed HMN architecture is well-motivated, with a clear three-tiered structure that intuitively makes sense and is supported by empirical evidence.
- The method is evaluated against a strong set of baselines, showing promising results, especially in terms of accuracy and efficiency.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear figures and a logical structure that aids in comprehension.
- The use of a hierarchical memory network to store condensed data is a novel approach, which allows for more efficient data storage and retrieval.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks comprehensive experimental results, particularly in terms of scalability and generalizability. There is a need for more extensive experiments across a broader range of datasets and scenarios to validate the method's effectiveness.
- The method's reliance on a specific training loss (gradient matching loss) and its compatibility with other loss functions is not thoroughly explored, which could limit its applicability in various scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed theoretical analysis of the method, which could help in understanding the underlying principles and limitations of the HMN architecture.
- There are concerns about the method's computational efficiency and scalability, particularly in large-scale applications where the method's performance under different conditions (e.g., different storage budgets, different datasets) is not adequately addressed.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, and the method's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods like HaBa and LinBa.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the specific role and function of the dataset-level memory in the HMN architecture?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of over-smoothing in the data condensation process, and what are the potential negative impacts of this issue on the method's performance?
- What are the potential applications of the proposed method beyond data condensation, and how does it compare to other methods in terms of efficiency and effectiveness in these applications?
- Could you provide more details on the computational efficiency and scalability of the method, particularly in large-scale applications?
- How does the proposed method perform under different storage budgets, and what are the implications of these budgets on the method's performance?
- Can you elaborate on the method's compatibility with other training losses, and how does it perform when used in conjunction with these losses?
