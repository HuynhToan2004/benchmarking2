 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Greedy Sequential Execution (GSE), a novel method designed to address cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) challenges in both homogeneous and heterogeneous tasks. GSE employs an individual utility function, which is a modification of the Q-value function, to capture the interactions between agents. This utility function is used to calculate a greedy marginal contribution, serving as a credit value for the sequential execution policy. The method is tested across various domains, including MAgent and Overcooked, demonstrating superior performance in both homogeneous and heterogeneous scenarios. The paper also addresses the challenges of credit assignment and the relative over-generalization problem, which are crucial in MARL.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the introduction and related work sections are clearly articulated.
- The proposed method, Greedy Sequential Execution (GSE), is novel and addresses significant challenges in cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), particularly the issues of credit assignment and the relative over-generalization problem.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing significant improvements in performance across various domains.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the limitations of current methods and presents a novel approach to address these challenges.
- The paper is structured to clearly articulate the research question, methodology, and results, enhancing its readability and comprehensibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the term "homogeneous" in the context used, which could lead to confusion regarding the type of tasks being addressed.
- The motivation for using the Shapley Value as a credit assignment method is not well-explained, and the paper does not adequately address the computational complexity introduced by the need to evaluate Shapley values for all possible permutations of actions.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability and performance in scenarios where the number of agents exceeds the current settings.
- There is a lack of detailed comparison with existing methods, such as MAVEN and CDS, which could have provided a clearer picture of the advantages and disadvantages of GSE.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the number of seeds used and the variability in results, which could affect the reliability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and usage of the term "homogeneous" in the context of the tasks being addressed?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability with existing methods like MAVEN and CDS?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the number of seeds used and the variability in results?
- How does the proposed method perform when the number of agents exceeds the current settings, and what are the implications of this for the method's scalability and applicability?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of their research and how they address these in their methodology?
