 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel learning framework named CAST, which integrates image segmentation and recognition into a single model, leveraging hierarchical segmentation for recognition. CAST employs adaptive segment tokens to group pixels into coarser segments, facilitating concurrent segmentation and recognition. This approach is evaluated on various benchmarks, showing improved performance in semantic segmentation and hierarchical segmentation compared to state-of-the-art methods. The paper also explores the use of superpixels for segmentation and proposes a graph pooling module for hierarchical segmentation. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its limited evaluation on downstream tasks, lack of comparison with more recent methods, and insufficient discussion on the efficiency and effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel learning framework, CAST, which integrates image segmentation and recognition into a single model, enabling concurrent segmentation and recognition with a single model.
- The proposed method of using superpixels as the basic unit for segmentation and the graph pooling module for hierarchical segmentation are innovative and well-explained.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, and includes comprehensive evaluations on several benchmarks.
- The use of adaptive segment tokens and hierarchical segmentation for recognition is a significant contribution to the field, showing potential for more efficient and effective segmentation and recognition.
- The paper provides code in the supplementary material, which is a valuable resource for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough evaluation on downstream tasks, particularly in comparison to existing methods such as SAM, which could undermine the claims of superior performance.
- There is a lack of discussion on the efficiency of the proposed method, particularly in terms of computational cost and training time, which could be critical for practical deployment.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other recent methods, such as HSG, to establish the superiority of the proposed method more convincingly.
- The paper's claims regarding the superiority of hierarchical segmentation and its impact on recognition are not adequately supported by experimental evidence, especially in terms of how the hierarchical segmentation improves recognition accuracy.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed visualizations and explanations of the segmentation results, particularly in complex scenes where segmentation errors are more likely to occur.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the hierarchical segmentation results in better recognition accuracy, and provide more detailed experimental evidence to support this claim?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational cost and training time compared to existing methods, and what are the implications for practical deployment?
- Could the authors elaborate on the design choices of the graph pooling module, particularly the decision to use centroids from the previous level as the initial centroids for the next level?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of different segmentation granularities for different images, and what are the implications for the performance and efficiency of the method?
- Can the authors provide more detailed visualizations and explanations of the segmentation results, especially in complex scenes where segmentation errors are more likely to occur?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent methods, such as HSG, in terms of performance and efficiency, and what are the key advantages of the proposed method over these existing approaches?
