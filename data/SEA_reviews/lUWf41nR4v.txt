 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Program Machine Policies (POMPs), a novel approach that integrates programmatic RL and state machine policies to tackle long-horizon tasks. POMPs represent policies as a set of programs, each with a specific behavior, which are combined with a transition function to enable the agent to switch between these programs. This approach allows for the generalization of policies across different tasks and the handling of long-horizon problems, demonstrated through experiments in the Karel domain. The paper also includes ablation studies to justify the effectiveness of its components, such as the diversity multiplier and the compatibility of programs. However, the evaluation is limited to the Karel domain, and the scalability and generalization of the method to more complex tasks are not thoroughly explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to programmatic RL, combining the strengths of programmatic RL and state machine policies, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is clearly presented, with a well-structured paper that effectively communicates the research objectives and results.
- The experiments are thorough, including ablation studies and comparisons with baseline methods, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for disseminating research findings.
- The approach is novel and innovative, providing a new perspective on how to tackle long-horizon tasks in RL.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is limited to the Karel domain, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method to other, more complex environments.
- The scalability of the method is not thoroughly explored, particularly in terms of how it handles larger or more complex tasks.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential failure modes and scenarios where the method might not perform optimally.
- The evaluation metrics used, such as program sample efficiency and inductive generalization, are not well-defined or clearly explained, which could limit the understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational cost and complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other programmatic RL approaches, particularly in terms of generalizability and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of how it handles larger or more complex tasks?
- How does the diversity multiplier affect the search process, and what are the implications of using different values for this parameter?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and calculations of the evaluation metrics used in the paper, particularly program sample efficiency and inductive generalization?
- What are the failure modes of the proposed method, and under what conditions does it not perform optimally?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state machine RL methods, particularly in terms of performance and interpretability?
