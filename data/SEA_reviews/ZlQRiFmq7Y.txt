 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Vocabulary Disentangled Retrieval (VDR), a novel retrieval-based framework designed to learn disentangled representations by utilizing natural language as a proxy for data variation. VDR employs a bi-encoder model to represent both data and natural language in a vocabulary space, thereby enabling the model to distinguish dimensions that capture intrinsic characteristics within data through its natural language counterpart. The methodology is evaluated on 15 retrieval benchmark datasets, demonstrating superior performance over previous bi-encoder retrievers. The paper also explores the interpretability of the method through human evaluations, showing that it is on par with state-of-the-art captioning models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to learning disentangled representations by leveraging natural language as a source of supervision, which is both innovative and practical.
- The methodology is evaluated on 15 retrieval benchmark datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness across various scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results showcase the superiority of VDR over previous bi-encoder retrievers with comparable model size and training costs, indicating its efficiency and effectiveness.
- The use of natural language as a proxy for data variation is a novel and interesting approach that could lead to further advancements in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- There is a lack of comprehensive analysis of the interpretability of the learned representations, which is crucial for understanding the method's effectiveness and applicability.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with the latest state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the claimed superiority of VDR.
- The use of natural language as a proxy for data variation might not be effective in all scenarios, particularly when the data does not have a clear linguistic counterpart.
- The method's reliance on a pre-trained BERT model could limit its applicability to non-English languages or other languages without similar pre-trained models.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity introduced by the additional components such as the DST head and the disentanglement module.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the interpretability of the learned representations, possibly through additional experiments or visualizations?
- How does the proposed method compare with the latest state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss scenarios where it might not be effective?
- How does the method handle cases where the data does not have a clear linguistic counterpart?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of the method's reliance on a pre-trained BERT model on its applicability to non-English languages or other languages without similar pre-trained models?
