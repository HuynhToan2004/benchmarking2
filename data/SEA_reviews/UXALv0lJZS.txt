 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for speech separation using a deterministic model, a pre-trained diffusion model, and a learned alignment model. The authors propose a theoretical upper bound for the performance of speech separation, which is enhanced by integrating the outputs of these models in the frequency domain. The methodology involves combining the outputs of the deterministic model with those of the diffusion model, which are then aligned and mixed to improve the separation quality. The paper provides empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness by comparing it with existing methods and demonstrating its ability to surpass certain benchmarks. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the theoretical contributions and the practical implications of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability and the soundness of the theoretical claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel method for speech separation, integrating a deterministic model with a pre-trained diffusion model and a learned alignment model, which is supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence.
- The methodology is clearly explained, and the results are convincingly presented, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper provides a new theoretical upper bound for speech separation, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method shows improvements in speech separation, particularly in scenarios with multiple sources, and provides a new perspective on the use of generative models in speech separation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the generative model is integrated into the separation process, which is crucial for understanding the methodology.
- The theoretical contributions, particularly the derivation of the upper bound and the analysis of mutual information, are not clearly explained and appear to be based on assumptions that are not well justified.
- There are several issues with the clarity and presentation of the paper, including unclear definitions and notations, and a lack of detailed explanation for some figures and equations.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to noisy datasets and its potential impact on the performance of existing models.
- The experimental setup and the results are not convincingly presented, with some results (like the performance on LibriSpeech) being significantly lower than expected.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the generative model is integrated into the separation process and how it contributes to the improvement in separation quality?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of noises, especially in scenarios where the noise level is high?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process for the learned alignment model and the selection of hyperparameters?
- What are the implications of the theoretical upper bound for practical applications, and how does it compare to the actual performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform on datasets with different characteristics, such as noisy or low-quality recordings?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a discussion on the advantages and disadvantages of their approach?
