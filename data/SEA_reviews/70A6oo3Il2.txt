 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AdaFlood, a novel regularization technique that dynamically adjusts the flood level for each training sample based on its difficulty, as estimated by an auxiliary network. This approach aims to enhance generalization performance by adapting to the unique challenges of each training instance. The methodology is evaluated across various datasets and tasks, demonstrating improved performance over existing flood-based methods. Despite its innovative approach, concerns are raised about the scalability of the method, particularly in large-scale applications, and the lack of detailed experimental results and theoretical justification.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel regularization technique, AdaFlood, which dynamically adapts the flood level for each training sample based on its difficulty, enhancing the generalization performance of neural networks.
- The methodology is clearly explained, with detailed experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach across various datasets and tasks.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the theoretical intuition behind the proposed method and its implementation, including the use of an auxiliary network for estimating flood levels.
- AdaFlood is shown to be effective in improving generalization performance on various datasets, and the authors provide theoretical justification for its effectiveness.
- The method is compatible with any optimizer and can be used in conjunction with other regularization techniques, making it versatile and applicable in different scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed experimental results, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for the auxiliary network training and the scalability of the method in large-scale applications.
- There is a lack of comparison with other regularization methods, which could help in understanding the relative advantages of AdaFlood.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the method, especially in large-scale applications where the computational cost of training an auxiliary network for each sample could be prohibitive.
- The theoretical analysis provided is limited, and there is a need for a more comprehensive discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of training times and resource requirements for different methods, which could be crucial for practical deployment.
- The method's reliance on the accuracy of the auxiliary network's predictions raises concerns about its robustness and the potential for overfitting if the auxiliary network is not well-trained.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the computational resources required for training the auxiliary network and how scalable the method is in large-scale applications?
- How does the proposed method compare with other regularization techniques, such as dropout or label smoothing, in terms of generalization performance and computational efficiency?
- Can you elaborate on the choice of the correction function and its impact on the performance of the method?
- In the context of the theoretical analysis, how does the method ensure that the auxiliary network's predictions are accurate enough to avoid significant errors in flood level estimation?
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison of training times and resource requirements for different methods to help in practical deployment decisions?
- How does the method handle the potential for overfitting if the auxiliary network is not well-trained?
