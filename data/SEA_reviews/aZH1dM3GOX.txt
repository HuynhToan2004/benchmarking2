 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to Multi-Task Reinforcement Learning (MTRL) by proposing a method called Mixture Of ORthogonal Experts (MOORE). MOORE leverages a Gram-Schmidt process to orthogonalize the representations of a mixture of experts, ensuring diversity and promoting the learning of distinct skills. The method is evaluated on benchmarks such as MiniGrid and MetaWorld, demonstrating improved performance over existing baselines. The approach aims to address the challenge of learning shared representations that capture both common and unique characteristics of tasks, which is crucial for generalization in MTRL.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed method and its components.
- The idea of learning a mixture of orthogonal experts is novel and interesting, with potential for improving performance in multi-task reinforcement learning (MTRL) by ensuring diversity among the learned representations.
- The experiments are well-designed, including ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper includes a comprehensive literature review, situating the work within the current research landscape.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear mathematical formulations and algorithmic descriptions that aid in understanding the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and applicability to more complex or varied tasks.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity of the Gram-Schmidt process, which could affect the practical implementation of the method.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the research.
- The experimental results, while showing some improvements, are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the improvements are sometimes marginal or within the error bars.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its method from existing works in the field, particularly in terms of the novelty and the depth of the contributions.
- There is a lack of clarity in the motivation behind the method, especially concerning the necessity and benefits of orthogonalizing the experts.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the definition of the Stiefel manifold and its role in the proposed method?
- How does the method ensure that the learned representations are orthogonal across different tasks, especially when the number of experts (k) increases?
- Could you provide more details on the computational complexity of the Gram-Schmidt process and its impact on the practical implementation of the method?
- How does the method compare to other orthogonalization methods, such as those based on the singular value decomposition (SVD)?
- Can you explain the rationale behind using the Gram-Schmidt process specifically and how it contributes to the overall effectiveness of the method?
- How do you address the potential limitations of the method, particularly in terms of scalability and applicability to more complex or varied tasks?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the tasks used and the experimental conditions?
