 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FLAT-Chat, a novel gradient flattening attack on the last linear layer of a language model, aimed at inferring words used in training. The methodology is designed to be efficient and effective, particularly in large-scale settings with high output vocabulary and realistic training batch sizes. The paper also explores the use of differential privacy as a defense mechanism against such attacks. The approach is validated through experiments on machine translation and language modeling tasks, showing that it can recover a significant portion of words used in training. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the attack and the depth of theoretical justification, with some reviewers suggesting that the paper may not meet the acceptance threshold.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel gradient flattening attack on the last linear layer of a language model, which is designed to be efficient and effective in large-scale settings.
- The approach is validated through experiments on machine translation and language modeling tasks, demonstrating its ability to recover a significant portion of words used in training.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, and includes both theoretical analysis and practical demonstrations.
- The proposed attack is shown to be effective in recovering words used in training, and the use of differential privacy as a defense mechanism against such attacks is explored.
- The paper addresses the important problem of privacy in federated learning, specifically focusing on the risk of data leakage through gradients.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing attacks, particularly in terms of efficiency and effectiveness, which could undermine the claimed novelty and superiority of the proposed method.
- The theoretical justification for the attack's effectiveness is insufficient, and the paper does not adequately explain why the attack works, especially in scenarios where the gradient flattening operation might not be effective.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed attack and its potential failures, which could affect the reliability and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational costs and practical implications of the proposed attack, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's focus on a specific type of attack (gradient flattening) might limit its broader impact and applicability to other types of attacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specifics of the attack, such as how many words are recovered and the computational resources required?
- How does the proposed attack compare to existing attacks in terms of efficiency and effectiveness?
- What are the potential limitations or failure cases of the proposed attack, and how might these impact the reliability and applicability of the findings?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational costs and practical implications of implementing the proposed attack?
- How does the proposed attack perform when applied to different types of language models or in different training scenarios?
