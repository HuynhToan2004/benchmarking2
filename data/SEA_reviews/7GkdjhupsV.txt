 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to self-supervised contrastive learning by utilizing mutual information to determine positive sample pairs in a video setting. The method involves extracting patches from the first frame of a video, estimating their mutual information, and using these estimates to select twin patches for contrastive learning. This approach is evaluated on several datasets, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and STL-10, showing improvements over baseline methods. The methodology is based on the use of a tracking model to estimate mutual information between patches, which is then used to select positive pairs. However, the paper has been criticized for its limited evaluation scope, lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and insufficient justification for certain design choices.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to positive sample selection in contrastive learning, using mutual information to determine positive pairs, which is a novel application in the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it easy to understand, and the paper is well-written, enhancing its readability.
- The experiments conducted on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and STL-10 datasets show improvements over baseline methods, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of video data for estimating mutual information is a creative approach that could be beneficial for improving the performance of self-supervised learning methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation, as it only compares the proposed method with a limited number of baseline methods and does not include state-of-the-art methods like DINO.
- The paper's claims about the method's effectiveness are not sufficiently supported by experimental evidence, particularly in terms of its generalization to larger datasets like ImageNet.
- There are concerns about the novelty of the method, as similar approaches have been previously explored, such as using mutual information for positive sample selection in contrastive learning.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of its approach, particularly the potential negative impacts of insufficient entropy and camera jittering on the estimation of mutual information.
- The paper's focus on image-level tasks limits its applicability to video-level tasks, and the methodology might not be suitable for other types of datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method compares with state-of-the-art methods like DINO, especially in terms of performance and efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets like ImageNet, and what are the potential challenges in scaling up the method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the twin patches are selected and how they impact the performance of the model?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of using a tracking model to estimate mutual information, and how does this approach compare with other methods like using ground truth bounding boxes or annotated videos?
- How does the method handle cases where there is insufficient entropy or camera jittering, and what are the potential impacts of these factors on the estimation of mutual information?
