 **Summary:**
The paper investigates whether large language models (LLMs) exhibit human-like response biases, focusing on survey design as a case study. It employs a dataset of question pairs with biased and non-biased perturbations to evaluate LLM responses, comparing them to human behavior. The study finds that most LLMs do not consistently reflect human-like behavior, with instruction fine-tuning potentially exacerbating these differences. Despite the novel approach and the comprehensive evaluation of various LLMs, the paper is criticized for its limited scope, potential bias in question selection, and lack of direct comparison with human responses.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely question about the human-likeness of LLMs, which is crucial for understanding their potential as proxies for human behavior.
- The research is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The evaluation is comprehensive, covering a wide range of models and biases, and includes a large number of question pairs, which enhances the reliability of the findings.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the results, including insights into the impact of model training and the correlation between model behavior and human opinions.
- The authors have made significant efforts to ensure the reproducibility of their work by providing detailed experimental setups and data sources.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on survey design as a case study might limit its applicability to other domains where LLMs are used as human proxies, such as crowdsourcing annotation tasks.
- The selection of biases might introduce bias into the study, as the authors did not select biases randomly but based on prior studies, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not directly compare LLM responses with human responses, which is crucial for understanding the extent to which LLMs mimic human behavior.
- The evaluation of LLM responses is limited to a single prompt, which might not fully capture the variability in LLM behavior across different prompts.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the statistical significance of the observed differences between LLM and human responses, which is necessary to establish the robustness of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the study, such as the potential impact of different prompting strategies on LLM behavior and the generalizability of the findings beyond the specific biases studied.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the methodology for selecting the biases and the rationale behind choosing these specific biases?
- How were the human responses obtained for the questions in the dataset, and what measures were taken to ensure the representativeness and reliability of these responses?
- Could you provide more details on the statistical significance of the observed differences between LLM and human responses, especially in terms of the p-values and effect sizes?
- How do the findings of this study relate to the broader question of whether LLMs can accurately mimic human behavior in various domains?
- What are the implications of the findings for the use of LLMs as human proxies in different applications, and how might these findings influence the development and deployment of LLMs in such contexts?
