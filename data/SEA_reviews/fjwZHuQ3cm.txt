 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LIMANS, a novel method for generating adversarial perturbations by decomposing them into universal and specific components. This approach allows for the creation of adversarial examples that can transfer effectively across different models and datasets, addressing the challenges of both universality and specificity in adversarial attacks. The method involves a dictionary of universal perturbations and a set of coefficients specific to each input, enabling the optimization of adversarial examples to be more tractable. The paper presents empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of LIMANS in transferability and robustness against adversarial example detectors. However, the method's scalability and the generalization of the universal perturbations to other datasets are concerns, as is the lack of a detailed analysis on the transferability and robustness of the learned perturbations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology.
- The proposed method, LIMANS, is novel and innovative, offering a new perspective on adversarial perturbations by decomposing them into universal and specific components.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in terms of transferability and robustness against adversarial example detectors.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of adversarial attacks, focusing on the transferability and robustness of adversarial examples.
- The methodology is scalable, allowing for the optimization of adversarial examples to be more tractable, which is a crucial aspect for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis on the transferability and robustness of the learned perturbations, which are crucial aspects of the proposed method.
- The scalability of the method is a concern, particularly in terms of how it handles large-scale datasets like ImageNet.
- The method's effectiveness is limited by the number of universal perturbations used, which could affect its generalization to other datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalization of the universal perturbations to other datasets, which is a significant limitation.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper lacks a comparison with some recent methods, such as those by Zhang et al. and Qin et al., which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis on the transferability and robustness of the learned perturbations?
- How does the method handle scalability, particularly in terms of its applicability to large-scale datasets like ImageNet?
- Can the authors provide more details on the generalization of the universal perturbations to other datasets?
- How does the method perform when compared to other recent methods, such as those by Zhang et al. and Qin et al.?
- Could the authors provide more insights into the computational complexity of the proposed method and discuss potential strategies for reducing it?
