 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to non-transferable learning (NTL) by separating content and style factors through a causal model. The authors propose a dual-path knowledge distillation framework to enhance the disentanglement of these factors, aiming to improve the performance of NTL models. This methodology is supported by a variational inference framework and a causal model that distinguishes between content and style factors, which are then used to guide learning in NTL tasks. Extensive experiments across various datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing NTL methods. However, concerns about the clarity of the paper, the necessity of the proposed components, and the generalization of the results to more complex or varied datasets were raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to the problem of non-transferable learning (NTL).
- The introduction of a causal model and a variational inference framework to disentangle content and style factors is innovative and adds a theoretical depth to the study.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in various NTL tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper includes a detailed explanation of the methodology, including the training process and the use of knowledge distillation, which helps in understanding the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear introduction and a detailed explanation of the methodology, making it accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the method might not perform optimally.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the methodology, with some sections being unclear or not well-explained, which could hinder understanding.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalization of the results to more complex or varied datasets, which could limit the practical applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical justification for the use of the variational inference framework and the causal model, which could strengthen the argument for their effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the effectiveness of the proposed components such as the dual-path knowledge distillation and the use of style augmentation.
- There is a need for more comprehensive experimental validation, including additional datasets and ablation studies to better understand the impact of the proposed components on the performance of the model.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process, particularly how the VAE is trained and how the student model is initialized?
- How does the proposed method perform when the distribution shifts are more significant, and what are the potential limitations in such scenarios?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the style augmentation in the source-only NTL task and its impact on the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of generalization to more complex or varied datasets?
- Can the authors provide more theoretical justification for the use of the variational inference framework and the causal model, particularly in relation to their effectiveness in disentangling content and style factors?
- What are the potential applications of the proposed method in real-world scenarios, and how could it be adapted to address specific challenges in these applications?
