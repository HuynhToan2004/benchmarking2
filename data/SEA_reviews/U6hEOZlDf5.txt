 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for relative object pose estimation using a hypothesis-and-verification framework. The method involves generating multiple pose hypotheses, transforming them into 3D volumes, and evaluating their alignment with a reference volume to determine the most plausible pose. This approach is tested on a new benchmark, GROP, which includes datasets like Objaverse and LINEMOD, and is shown to outperform existing methods in generalizable relative object pose estimation. The method's robustness is demonstrated across various datasets, and it is highlighted for its ability to handle large-scale pose variations and unseen objects.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel hypothesis-and-verification framework for relative object pose estimation, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-motivated, clearly written, and easy to follow, with a strong focus on the application of the hypothesis-and-verification paradigm.
- The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation on a new benchmark, GROP, which includes datasets like Objaverse and LINEMOD, demonstrating the method's robustness and generalizability.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on the benchmark, showcasing its effectiveness in relative pose estimation.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed information about the training process, including the training time, GPU memory usage, and the number of training images.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities and potential areas for improvement.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that use global feature embedding for 3D information extraction.
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the 3D-aware verification mechanism and the 3D object representation learning module.
- The paper does not adequately address the impact of noisy object bounding boxes on the method's performance.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when the object is partially occluded?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process, such as training time, GPU memory usage, and the number of training images?
- How does the method handle the presence of multiple objects in the image, and what are the implications for the accuracy of the pose estimation?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the 3D object representation learning module and the 3D-aware verification mechanism in the overall performance of the method?
- How does the method handle the noisy object bounding boxes, and what are the implications for the accuracy of the pose estimation in such scenarios?
