 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to enhancing the performance of deep ensembles, particularly in scenarios with limited labeled data. This method, referred to as $\nu$-ensembles, involves fitting each unlabeled data point with a different random label for each ensemble member, which is expected to improve the diversity and calibration of the ensemble. The theoretical analysis is based on PAC-Bayesian bounds, demonstrating that the approach can lead to lower negative log-likelihood and higher diversity on testing samples. Empirical evaluations on datasets such as CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 show that $\nu$-ensembles can outperform standard ensembles in terms of calibration and diversity. However, the paper is criticized for its limited empirical evaluation and the lack of comparison with more recent and relevant baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to improving the calibration of deep ensembles in the small data regime, which is a significant challenge in machine learning.
- The proposed method is simple, easy to implement, and does not require additional computational resources compared to standard deep ensembles.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis based on PAC-Bayesian bounds, which guarantees that the final ensemble is diverse, accurate, and well-calibrated on test data.
- The empirical evaluations demonstrate that $\nu$-ensembles are more diverse and provide better calibration than standard ensembles, sometimes significantly.
- The paper is the first to propose a PAC-Bayesian analysis of the test performance of the trained ensemble in terms of negative log-likelihood and diversity.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- The empirical evaluation is limited to small datasets (CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100) and does not include larger datasets such as ImageNet, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with more recent and relevant baselines, such as Agree to Disagree and other diversity-promoting methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the computational and memory requirements of the proposed method, which are crucial for practical deployment in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the hyperparameter tuning and its impact on the performance of the proposed method.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its applicability in different scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare with more recent and relevant baselines, such as Agree to Disagree and other diversity-promoting methods?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the computational and memory requirements of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the dataset and the number of ensemble members?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential issue of overfitting to random labels, especially in scenarios where the labeled dataset is small?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the hyperparameter tuning and its impact on the performance of the proposed method?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method?
