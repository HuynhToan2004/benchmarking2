 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for sampling from a generative model using a joint-particle time-evolving potential, referred to as "particle guidance." This method aims to improve sample diversity by adding a potential term to the reverse diffusion process, which guides the sampling towards more diverse modes. Theoretical analysis supports the method's ability to sample from a joint distribution, and empirical results show improvements in sample diversity and quality across various applications, including image and molecule generation. The paper also discusses the practical implementation of this method, including a comparison with other methods like SVGD and classifier guidance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel method for sampling from a generative model, which is a significant contribution to the field of generative models.
- The theoretical analysis provided in the paper is robust, offering insights into the method's theoretical underpinnings.
- The empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness in improving sample diversity and quality, which is crucial for applications in areas like image and molecule generation.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors have conducted thorough experiments, including both synthetic and real-world datasets, which support the method's effectiveness.
- The methodology is simple yet effective, and the paper provides a clear and comprehensive description of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with existing methods, such as classifier guidance and other potential-based sampling methods, which could help in understanding the method's advantages and disadvantages.
- The method's practical implementation, particularly the choice of hyperparameters like the number of particles and the number of steps, is not well-explained or justified.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational efficiency of the method, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup, particularly in sections where the methodology is introduced.
- The method's reliance on a pre-defined potential function might limit its applicability in more complex scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and classifier guidance, particularly in terms of the potential functions used?
- How does the proposed method compare with other potential-based sampling methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup, particularly in sections where the methodology is introduced?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the potential function might not be well-defined or where the number of particles is not sufficient to capture the full complexity of the distribution?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
- How does the method scale with the number of particles and the number of steps, and what are the computational implications of these parameters?
