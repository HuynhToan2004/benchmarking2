 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of Sobolev training in neural networks, focusing on the convergence acceleration of rectified linear unit (ReLU) networks within the student-teacher framework. It introduces a novel approach using Chebyshev spectral differentiation to approximate target derivatives when they are not available, thereby enhancing the practical applicability of Sobolev training. The paper provides theoretical evidence and empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of this method in various settings, including regression problems and denoising autoencoders. Despite its novelty, the paper is criticized for its limited scope to ReLU networks and the lack of comprehensive experimental validation across different architectures and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel perspective on Sobolev training by combining it with the student-teacher framework, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors introduce a new numerical differentiation technique that is more efficient than traditional methods, which could be beneficial in practical applications.
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of theorems and proofs, which enhances the reader's understanding of the theoretical contributions.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in the context of denoising autoencoders, showing improved generalization and accelerated convergence.
- The paper contributes to the understanding of the dynamics of ReLU networks in the student-teacher setting, which is an important area of research in deep learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to ReLU networks, which may not generalize well to other types of neural networks, such as those using other activation functions like sigmoid or tanh.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, particularly in terms of the number of experiments and the complexity of the datasets used. The paper could benefit from more comprehensive testing across various architectures and real-world scenarios.
- The proofs and theoretical results are not sufficiently robust or general, as they are limited to specific network architectures and training scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant oversight in ethical considerations.
