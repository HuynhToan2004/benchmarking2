 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GLoRA, a novel parameter-efficient fine-tuning method that integrates various existing methods into a unified framework. This framework allows for the tuning of weights, features, and input tokens, and includes a layer-wise search for optimal configurations. The authors claim that GLoRA outperforms existing methods across multiple benchmarks, including vision and language models, with minimal computational overhead. The approach is designed to be adaptable to different datasets and tasks, and is evaluated on a variety of datasets, showing consistent performance improvements over existing methods. However, the paper's novelty and the clarity of its contributions are questioned, as it heavily relies on existing methods and does not sufficiently differentiate itself.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined problem.
- The proposed GLoRA framework is a unified formulation of various PEFT methods, demonstrating strong performance on multiple benchmarks.
- The approach is flexible, allowing for the tuning of weights, features, and input tokens, and includes a layer-wise search for optimal configurations.
- The methodology is supported by comprehensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of GLoRA across a variety of datasets and tasks.
- The paper provides detailed experimental results, including comparisons with existing methods and ablation studies that help clarify the role of different components.
- The proposed method is efficient, with minimal computational overhead during inference.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is questioned as it heavily relies on existing methods and does not sufficiently differentiate itself.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the novelty of the proposed method, with concerns that it may not represent a significant advancement over existing methods.
- The experimental setup and results are not always clear or convincing, with some results (e.g., in Table 2) not being statistically significant or not clearly explained.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and inference times of the GLoRA method, which is crucial for evaluating its practical applicability.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the evolutionary search method used in the GLoRA method, which could affect the reliability and generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of the proposed method and how it differentiates from existing methods?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of training and inference times with other PEFT methods, especially in terms of efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results and comparisons, especially in the few-shot learning and domain generalization sections, to support the claims made about the effectiveness of the GLoRA method?
- How does the GLoRA method handle the issue of hyperparameter tuning, especially in terms of the search space and the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the design choices of the GLoRA method, particularly in terms of the layer-wise search and the role of different components in the method?
