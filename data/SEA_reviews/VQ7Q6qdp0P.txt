 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of "concept forgetting" in foundation models, particularly focusing on the CLIP model, during end-to-end fine-tuning. The authors propose a new regularizer, LDIFS, which minimizes the distance between the features of the original model and those obtained from the fine-tuned model, aiming to reduce concept forgetting without compromising performance on downstream tasks. The paper presents empirical evidence demonstrating that the proposed method outperforms existing fine-tuning methods in terms of preserving knowledge from the pre-training phase. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as similar methods have been previously explored, and for its scope being limited to the CLIP model.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to addressing concept forgetting in foundation models, specifically through the use of a new regularizer that minimizes the distance between features of the original model and those obtained from the fine-tuned model.
- The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms existing fine-tuning methods in terms of preserving knowledge from the pre-training phase, as evidenced by reduced concept forgetting without significant performance degradation on downstream tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are clearly described, which aids in understanding the methodology and results.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the concept forgetting phenomenon and its impact on the performance of foundation models, which is significant given the current interest in large language models and multimodal models.
- The proposed method, LDIFS, is simple yet effective, and the paper presents a clear and convincing case for its effectiveness in reducing concept forgetting.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to the CLIP model, which may not generalize well to other foundation models such as BERT or GPT.
- The novelty of the paper is limited as similar methods have been previously explored, such as the use of L2 regularization to prevent forgetting.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods for continual learning, which could help in evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed method more robustly.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational overhead introduced by the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- The paper's claims about the universality of the proposed method across different datasets and models are not substantiated, which could limit its applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the proposed method could be applied to other foundation models beyond CLIP?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods for continual learning, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors elaborate on the computational overhead introduced by the proposed method and discuss potential strategies for mitigating this in practical applications?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over other existing methods for preventing concept forgetting, particularly in terms of its ability to generalize across different datasets and models?
