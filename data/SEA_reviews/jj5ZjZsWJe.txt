 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two novel compressed federated learning algorithms, SCALLION and SCAFCOM, designed to address the challenges of data heterogeneity and partial client participation in federated learning. SCALLION employs unbiased compression, while SCAFCOM uses biased compression, both aiming to reduce uplink communication costs by half compared to existing methods. The paper provides theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence of the algorithms' efficacy, demonstrating their ability to achieve fast convergence rates under arbitrary data heterogeneity and without assumptions on compression errors. The experimental results show that the proposed methods outperform existing compressed federated learning methods under the same communication budget.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise content that is easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed algorithms, SCALLION and SCAFCOM, are innovative and provide significant contributions to the field of federated learning, addressing the challenges of data heterogeneity and partial client participation.
- The paper is technically sound, with a thorough analysis of the proposed algorithms, including convergence analysis and empirical results that demonstrate the algorithms' effectiveness.
- The paper is significant in its contribution to the field, potentially influencing future research and applications in federated learning.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed methods outperform existing compressed federated learning methods under the same communication budget.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithms, which could help in understanding their scope and applicability.
- The theoretical results are not presented in a clear and accessible manner, making it difficult for readers without a strong background in the field to fully understand the implications and contributions of the research.
- There is a lack of experimental validation for some of the claims made in the paper, such as the effectiveness of the proposed methods in handling arbitrary data heterogeneity and partial participation.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational overhead and resource requirements of the proposed methods, especially in comparison to existing methods.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the bounded variance of stochastic gradients, are not clearly justified or discussed, which could limit the generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly regarding the bounded variance of stochastic gradients?
- How does the proposed method handle arbitrary data heterogeneity and partial participation in practical scenarios? Can the authors provide experimental evidence to support these claims?
- What are the specific computational and resource requirements of the proposed methods, and how do these compare to existing methods?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithms and their potential impact on the practical applicability and scalability of the methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of communication and computation costs, especially under different levels of data heterogeneity and client participation?
