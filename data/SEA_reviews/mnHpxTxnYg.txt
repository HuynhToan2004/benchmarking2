 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the privacy implications of using Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) in a black-box setting, where adversaries only have access to samples from the generator, not the discriminator. It introduces a suite of membership inference attacks against GANs in this context and evaluates these attacks on image and tabular datasets, including CIFAR10 and genomic data. The authors propose a detector-based attack that trains a second network to score samples based on their likelihood of being generated by the GAN, which is shown to be effective in some scenarios. However, the paper's theoretical contributions are limited, and the experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a good balance of theoretical discussion and practical implementation details.
- The proposed attack is simple and effective, particularly in the black-box setting, and is supported by a solid theoretical foundation.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the attack's effectiveness across various datasets, including genomic and image datasets, which helps in understanding the attack's applicability in different contexts.
- The paper includes a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed attack, which is crucial for understanding its practical implications.
- The paper is original in its approach, focusing on a black-box setting where only the generator is accessible, which is a novel and relevant setting for privacy concerns.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks significant theoretical contributions, particularly in the novelty of the proposed attack and its theoretical justification.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper does not sufficiently compare its results with other recent works in the field.
- The paper's claims about the privacy leakage of GANs being lower than other generative models are not well-supported by the experimental evidence provided.
- The paper's scope is limited to GANs, which might not generalize well to other types of generative models.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the attacks, such as their impact on real-world privacy concerns.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancy between the theoretical analysis and the experimental results, particularly in terms of the generalization of the theoretical results to other types of generative models?
- How does the proposed attack perform when compared to other recent works in the field, especially in terms of privacy leakage and attack effectiveness?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, such as the specific AUC values and ROC curves for each dataset and attack method?
- How does the proposed attack perform when the attacker has access to both the generator and the discriminator, and how does this compare to the black-box setting?
- Can the authors elaborate on the practical implications of the attacks, particularly in terms of their impact on real-world privacy concerns?
