 **Summary:**
The paper explores the performance of centralized and decentralized federated learning (FL) methods, specifically focusing on FedAvg and DFedAvg. It provides a theoretical analysis on the generalization error and excess risk, comparing the stability and generalization ability of these methods. The authors claim that centralized FL generally outperforms decentralized FL in terms of generalization, and that the choice between the two depends on the topology of the communication graph. The paper also includes experimental validations, although the experiments are criticized for their limited scope and depth. The theoretical analysis is supported by empirical evidence, but the practical applicability and the assumptions made are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear and comprehensive analysis of the generalization error and excess risk in both centralized and decentralized federated learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis is supported by empirical evidence, enhancing the credibility of the findings.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed comparison between centralized and decentralized federated learning.
- The authors provide a novel analysis of excess risk, which is crucial for understanding the performance of federated learning algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to specific algorithms (FedAvg and DFedAvg), which may not generalize well to other federated learning algorithms.
- The experimental setup is limited to one dataset (CIFAR10), and the results are not sufficiently comprehensive or convincing to support the theoretical claims.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the bounded gradient assumption, are not well-justified or discussed in depth, which could limit the applicability of the results.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of centralized federated learning over decentralized federated learning are not sufficiently substantiated with empirical evidence.
- The paper's presentation and organization could be improved, particularly in terms of readability and the clarity of the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in their theoretical analysis, particularly the bounded gradient assumption, and discuss how these assumptions affect the applicability of the results?
- How does the paper's analysis compare to other federated learning algorithms that do not rely on the bounded gradient assumption?
- Can the authors provide more detailed empirical evidence to support their claims about the superiority of centralized federated learning over decentralized federated learning?
- Why is the paper focused only on FedAvg and DFedAvg, and could the authors consider extending their analysis to other federated learning algorithms?
- How do the authors address the issue of client-drift in their analysis, and what are the implications of this on the performance of federated learning algorithms?
