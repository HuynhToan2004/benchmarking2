 **Summary:**
The paper explores the controllability of large language models (LLMs) by treating them as discrete-time dynamical systems in the embedding space of sub-word tokens. The authors argue that LLMs can be controlled to any state by suitable choice of prompts, viewing meanings as equivalence classes of complete trajectories of tokens. They introduce necessary and sufficient conditions for controllability, focusing on the functional characteristics of trained embedding. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the responsible deployment of LLMs and AI bots, suggesting potential applications in designing bots that avoid undesirable states.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to studying the controllability of LLMs, focusing on the functional characteristics of trained embeddings, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- It presents a clear and concise exposition, making complex concepts accessible and understandable.
- The paper is well-organized and structured, with a clear motivation and well-defined problem statement.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis of controllability in LLMs, which is a novel and interesting direction for research in this area.
- The paper offers insights into the controllability of LLMs, which is crucial for understanding the behavior and potential applications of these models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical evidence or experimental validation to support the theoretical claims, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could be a significant drawback for readers looking to apply the method in practice.
- The paper is not self-contained and requires prior knowledge of dynamical systems and control theory to fully understand the contributions.
- The paper's claims about the controllability of LLMs are not substantiated with sufficient experimental evidence, which could limit the generalizability and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of how the findings could be applied to improve the design or deployment of LLMs.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition of "meaning" used in the paper and how it relates to the functional characteristics of trained embeddings?
- How do the authors ensure the reliability and validity of the theoretical results, particularly in light of the lack of empirical evidence?

- How do the authors address the limitations of their method, particularly in terms of its applicability and generalizability to different types of LLMs and applications?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup and results, including the specific LLMs used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How do the authors envision the practical implementation of the controllability of LLMs, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in achieving this goal?
