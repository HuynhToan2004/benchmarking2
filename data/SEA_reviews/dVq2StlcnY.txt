 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for interpretable and generalizable graph neural networks (GNNs) using a multilinear extension of subgraph distributions. It critiques existing methods for approximating subgraph multilinear extensions (SubMT) and proposes a new method, Graph Multilinear Net (GMT), to address these issues. The GMT method is evaluated against various GNN architectures and datasets, demonstrating improved interpretability and generalization capabilities. The theoretical analysis and experimental results support the proposed framework, although concerns are raised about the clarity of certain definitions and the generalizability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the theoretical framework and experimental results.
- The proposed method, GMT, is simple yet effective, demonstrating significant improvements in interpretability and generalization over existing methods.
- The paper provides a novel perspective on subgraph multilinear extension, which is a significant advancement in the field of interpretable GNNs.
- The theoretical analysis is solid, and the empirical results are convincing, showing that GMT outperforms existing methods in terms of both interpretability and generalization.
- The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important problem in the field of explainable GNNs, which is of significant interest to the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the causal subgraphs and spurious subgraphs, which are crucial for understanding the proposed method.
- The computational complexity of the proposed method is not discussed, which is a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper's empirical results are limited to specific datasets and models, and it would be beneficial to see how the method performs on a broader range of datasets and models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical application.
- The paper's focus on interpretable GNNs for graph classification tasks might limit its applicability to other types of tasks, such as node classification or link prediction.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scope and applicability.
