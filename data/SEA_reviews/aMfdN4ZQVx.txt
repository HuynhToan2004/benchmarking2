 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Deep Concept Injection (DCI) that enables pretrained language models (PLMs) to perform zero-shot cross-modal tasks without requiring additional training. DCI operates by injecting visual concepts as both additional input text tokens and augmenting intermediate features within each feed-forward network, allowing PLMs to perceive and reason over multimodal inputs. The method is evaluated on various benchmarks, including video and visual question answering, and shows promising results. However, concerns were raised about the method's generalization to other tasks and the need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Deep Concept Injection (DCI), which enables pretrained language models (PLMs) to perform zero-shot cross-modal tasks without requiring additional training.
- DCI is evaluated on various benchmarks, including video and visual question answering, and shows promising results, outperforming or matching the performance of models that require training.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly explained, with detailed explanations of the concepts used and their impact on model performance.
- The paper provides a comprehensive comparison of DCI with other methods, including ablation studies that show the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The approach is innovative, as it utilizes pretrained vision-text contrastive models to extract visual concepts and injects them into the PLM, which is a different approach from traditional methods that rely on training or fine-tuning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods that perform zero-shot cross-modal tasks, which could undermine the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the generalization of the proposed method to other tasks beyond those evaluated. The paper only evaluates DCI on video and visual question answering tasks, which limits the understanding of its applicability to other cross-modal tasks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could be crucial for understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, especially considering the potential for increased computational demands due to the use of additional text tokens and augmentation in intermediate features.
- There are some inconsistencies and potential errors in the paper, such as missing references and incorrect citations, which could affect the credibility and reliability of the work.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed method generalize to other tasks beyond those evaluated? Could you provide examples or evidence of its applicability to other types of cross-modal tasks?
- Could you discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and how these might impact its practical application?
- How does the computational efficiency of the proposed method compare to other methods, especially in terms of the number of parameters and computational resources required?
- Can you clarify the process of extracting visual concepts from the visual encoder and how these concepts are used to augment the intermediate features within each feed-forward network?
