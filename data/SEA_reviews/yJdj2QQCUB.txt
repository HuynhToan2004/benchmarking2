 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Graph Positional and Structural Encoder (GPSE), a novel approach to learning positional and structural encodings for graphs, which is trained to predict a diverse set of predefined positional and structural encodings. This model, which can be used to augment any graph neural network (GNN), is shown to perform well across various graph prediction tasks and datasets, including molecular graphs. The GPSE model is designed to address the challenges of over-smoothing and over-squashing in GNNs by using a deep architecture, residual connections, gating mechanisms, and virtual nodes. The model's performance is evaluated on multiple datasets and shown to outperform existing methods in some cases. However, concerns are raised about the novelty and the empirical performance, particularly in terms of its scalability and computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to learning positional and structural encodings for graphs, which is a significant advancement in the field of graph neural networks (GNNs).
- The model is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of its design choices and their rationale, which enhances its credibility and reproducibility.
- The empirical results are promising, demonstrating the model's effectiveness in improving the performance of various GNN models across multiple datasets.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive set of experiments that validate the model's capabilities.
- The inclusion of a diverse set of predefined positional and structural encodings (PSEs) in the training process is a novel approach that enhances the model's ability to capture a wide range of graph information.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the model's performance against other GNN architectures and pre-trained models.
- There is a notable absence of theoretical analysis on the model's expressivity, which could provide a deeper understanding of its capabilities and limitations.
- The model's scalability is a concern, especially when applied to large-scale datasets, as it may require extensive computational resources and training time.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the model, particularly in terms of its generalizability and applicability to different types of graphs and tasks.
- The novelty of the model is questionable, as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of over-smoothing in the model, which is a critical concern in GNNs.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the model's performance against other GNN architectures and pre-trained models?
- How does the model perform on other types of graphs, such as social networks or citation graphs?
- Could the authors elaborate on the model's performance on larger-scale datasets, and discuss any potential strategies for scaling the model to handle such data?
- What are the specific advantages of using GPSE over other existing methods, such as GraphLoG, in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the model handle the issue of over-smoothing, and what measures have been taken to address this?
- Could the authors clarify the model's expressivity and how it relates to the model's ability to learn complex graph structures?
