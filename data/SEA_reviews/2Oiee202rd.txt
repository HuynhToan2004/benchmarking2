 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel zero-shot image classification method named PerceptionCLIP, which leverages CLIP to infer contextual attributes from images and then uses these attributes to classify objects. This method, inspired by human visual perception, aims to improve zero-shot classification accuracy by conditioning on contextual attributes such as background and orientation. The approach is evaluated across various datasets and shows significant improvements over baseline methods, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling spurious correlations and enhancing interpretability. The paper also explores the use of different attributes and their impact on classification performance, highlighting the method's potential to reduce reliance on spurious features.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, with a clear motivation and intuitive approach inspired by human visual perception.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method across multiple datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.
- The use of CLIP to infer contextual attributes from images is innovative and shows potential for improving zero-shot classification performance.
- The method's ability to reduce reliance on spurious features and enhance interpretability is highlighted through various experiments and visualizations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the annotation function used to map discrete values to natural language text, as it is crucial for the method's success.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as it resembles prompt ensembling methods and does not sufficiently differentiate itself.
- The method's dependence on the CLIP model's ability to accurately infer contextual attributes raises concerns about its reliability and generalizability.
- The paper could provide more insights into how the method handles spurious correlations and whether it can be applied to other vision-language models.
- The experimental setup and results could be improved by including more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and providing a more comprehensive ablation study.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the annotation function is applied to different datasets and how it ensures the accuracy of the textual descriptions generated?
- How does the proposed method compare to prompt ensembling methods, particularly in terms of novelty and effectiveness?
- Can you provide more details on how the method handles spurious correlations and whether it can be applied to other vision-language models?
- How does the method perform when using different values of the temperature parameter, and what are the implications of this on the model's performance?
- Could you include more detailed experimental results and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods to better establish the effectiveness of your proposed method?
