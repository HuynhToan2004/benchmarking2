 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of task similarity and overparameterization on catastrophic forgetting in continual learning, focusing on a two-task linear regression model with random orthogonal transformations. It derives an analytical expression for the worst-case expected forgetting under this model and demonstrates that intermediate task similarity leads to the most forgetting in highly overparameterized models. The paper also presents numerical experiments to validate these findings, showing that the theoretical results align with empirical observations. Despite its theoretical rigor and novel insights, the paper's scope is limited to linear models and simple continual learning settings, which may limit its applicability to more complex scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel analysis of catastrophic forgetting in continual learning, focusing on the joint effects of task similarity and overparameterization, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical results are supported by numerical experiments, demonstrating the practical applicability of the findings.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the theoretical results and their implications.
- The analysis of the joint effects of task similarity and overparameterization is original and provides new insights into the dynamics of forgetting in continual learning.
- The paper includes a comprehensive discussion on related work, which helps in situating the research within the broader context of the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on linear models and simple continual learning settings might limit its applicability to more complex scenarios involving non-linear models and more realistic continual learning tasks.
- The analysis assumes that the second task is a random orthogonal transformation of the first task, which may not reflect real-world scenarios where tasks are more complex or involve different data distributions.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its analysis, such as the applicability of its findings to more complex models or scenarios beyond the two-task setting.
- The paper's experimental setup and the choice of parameters (e.g., the width of the MLP) are not adequately justified, which could affect the validity of the results.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the realizability of tasks and the use of a two-task model, might be overly restrictive and not generalizable to more complex or varied learning scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancy between the assumptions in their analysis and the real-world applicability of their findings?
- How does the paper's analysis apply to more complex models or scenarios, such as those involving non-linear models or more realistic continual learning tasks?
- Could the authors provide more detailed justification for the choice of parameters and experimental setups used in their study?
- How does the paper's analysis compare or contrast with other related works, such as those by Evron et al. (2022) and Lin et al. (2023)?
- Could the authors elaborate on the implications of their findings for practical applications in continual learning, particularly in terms of designing more effective algorithms or architectures?
