 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Continuous Invariance Learning (CIL), to address the challenges of learning invariant features in continuous domain problems. Traditional methods, such as IRM, are shown to be ineffective in such settings due to the large number of domains and limited samples per domain. CIL proposes an adversarial procedure that measures and controls the conditional independence between labels and continuous domain indices given the extracted features, thereby ensuring that the class variable is discrete. The method's theoretical advantages and empirical results are validated across both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its superiority over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise presentation of the proposed method, CIL, and its theoretical underpinnings.
- The experimental results are robust and convincing, showing that CIL outperforms existing methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The theoretical analysis is thorough and provides a strong foundation for the proposed method, with clear connections to the practical implementation of CIL.
- The paper effectively demonstrates the limitations of existing methods, particularly in continuous domain tasks, and provides a novel solution that addresses these limitations.
- The empirical results are comprehensive and include both synthetic and real-world datasets, providing a robust validation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples of the synthetic datasets used in the experiments, particularly in section 4.1.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might impact its practical applicability.
- The theoretical analysis and empirical results are not sufficiently connected, which could confuse the reader about the practical implications of the theoretical findings.
- There is a need for more discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in relation to other existing methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and how these assumptions affect the applicability of the method in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or explanations of the synthetic datasets used in the experiments, particularly in section 4.1?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of continuous domain indices, and what are the implications of this in practical applications?
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the theoretical analysis and the empirical results, especially in how these relate to the practical implementation of CIL?
- How does the computational complexity of CIL compare to other existing methods, and what are the implications of this in terms of scalability and practical application?
- Could the authors discuss the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and how these assumptions might impact the applicability of the method in real-world scenarios?
