 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedLoGe, a novel framework for Federated Long-tailed Learning (Fed-LT), which integrates representation learning and classifier alignment within a neural collapse framework to address data imbalance issues in both global and local models. FedLoGe employs a Static Sparse Equiangular Tight Frame Classifier (SSE-C) to prune noisy features and enhance data representations, alongside a Global and Local Adaptive Feature Realignment (GLA-FR) module to align global features with client preferences. The framework is evaluated on various datasets, showing improvements over existing methods. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the proposed method and the clarity of the paper's presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in Federated Learning (FL) by focusing on the performance of both global and local models, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The methodology is well-motivated, with clear explanations and visualizations that enhance understanding of the proposed techniques.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes extensive experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The use of a shared backbone for capturing global trends and individualized classifiers for local refinements is a novel approach that could inspire further research in the field.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the feature degeneration problem and proposes a method to address it, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and concise explanation of the main technical contributions, making it difficult for readers to grasp the core innovations.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with marginal improvements over existing methods, and the paper does not adequately compare its methods with state-of-the-art models.
- The paper suffers from a lack of novelty as it primarily combines existing techniques without introducing significant new insights or methodologies.
- The paper's structure and presentation need improvement, particularly in the clarity of notations and the organization of content, which could make the paper more accessible to readers.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and potential negative societal impacts, which are essential for a comprehensive understanding of the technology's implications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the main technical contributions and how they address the specific challenges in Fed-LT?
- How does the proposed method compare with state-of-the-art models in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more intuitive explanations for the terms and equations used in the paper, especially those that are not standard in the field?
- How does the paper address the issue of fairness in federated learning, particularly in scenarios where data is imbalanced across clients?
- Could the authors clarify the notations and figures used in the paper to improve readability and understanding?
- In the context of federated learning, how does the paper ensure the security and privacy of the data being shared among clients?
