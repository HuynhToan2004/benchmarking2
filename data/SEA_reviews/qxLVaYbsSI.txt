 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TwinSight, a novel approach to federated semi-supervised learning (FSSL) that employs a dual-model framework to address the challenges of label scarcity and gradient conflicts in federated learning. TwinSight integrates both supervised and unsupervised models, each trained on different objectives, to leverage the synergy between them. The supervised model uses labeled data, while the unsupervised model utilizes unlabeled data, and both models are trained with a neighborhood-preserving constraint to maintain consistency in data features. The method is tested on various datasets, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, the fairness of comparisons, and the practical applicability of the approach, particularly in terms of communication overhead and scalability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the problem of label scarcity is clearly defined.
- The proposed method, TwinSight, is novel and effective, with a clear motivation and a well-designed approach.
- The paper provides extensive experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of TwinSight in improving performance in federated learning scenarios.
- The use of a dual-model framework with supervised and unsupervised models is innovative, allowing for mutual guidance and insight from different perspectives of labeled and unlabeled data.
- The paper is well-structured, making it easy to understand the methodology and the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other semi-supervised learning methods and does not discuss the relationship with other dual-model frameworks, which could have enriched the discussion.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as similar approaches have been previously explored in federated learning, such as the use of dual-model frameworks and neighborhood-preserving constraints.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of communication overhead and the number of communication rounds required.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the number of clients, the number of data samples per client, and the hyperparameter settings, which could affect the reproducibility and fairness of the comparisons.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer explanation of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method compared to existing approaches.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between their proposed method and other dual-model frameworks, especially in terms of how TwinSight differs from these existing methods?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, particularly in terms of communication overhead and the number of communication rounds required?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the number of clients, the number of data samples per client, and the hyperparameter settings, to ensure a fair comparison with other methods?
- How does the proposed method compare with other semi-supervised learning methods, especially in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors elaborate on the motivation behind the twin-model paradigm and how it addresses the specific challenges in federated learning, particularly in terms of label scarcity and gradient conflicts?
