 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel defense mechanism, BaDExpert, against backdoor attacks on deep neural networks (DNNs). The approach involves training a backdoor expert model using a small set of intentionally mislabeled clean samples, which are then used to detect backdoor inputs during inference. This methodology allows for the extraction of backdoor functionality from the original model, preserving the backdoor behavior while mitigating the risk of backdoor attacks. The defense is evaluated across multiple datasets, including CIFAR10, GTSRB, and ImageNet, and against various attack types, demonstrating its effectiveness in detecting backdoor inputs and maintaining clean accuracy. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the approach and its scalability to large datasets like ImageNet.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to defending against backdoor attacks by extracting backdoor functionality from a backdoored model, which is distinct from existing methods that focus on trigger reconstruction.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, easy to follow, and supported by extensive experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness against various backdoor attacks across multiple datasets.
- The use of a backdoor expert model to filter backdoor inputs is an innovative approach that can potentially improve the detection of backdoor inputs in real-world scenarios.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, demonstrating its effectiveness against a variety of backdoor attacks and its ability to maintain clean accuracy.
- The methodology is clearly explained, making it accessible to readers, and the paper is well-written, enhancing its readability and comprehension.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the method to larger datasets, such as ImageNet, which could limit its practical applicability.
- There is a concern about the novelty of the approach, as similar ideas have been explored in previous works, such as those that focus on unlearning backdoor models.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for adaptive attacks, which could bypass the defense mechanism by altering the backdoor triggers during inference.
- The method's reliance on a small set of mislabeled clean samples could limit its effectiveness if the samples are not sufficiently diverse or representative of the dataset.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant if the method is used to detect backdoor attacks in critical applications such as healthcare or finance.
- There is a need for a more rigorous comparison with existing methods, particularly those that use similar approaches to detect backdoor inputs, to clearly establish the advantages of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the backdoor expert model is constructed, specifically how the mislabeled clean samples are obtained and how they are used in the training process?
- How does the proposed method perform against adaptive attacks, particularly those that modify the backdoor triggers during inference?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated?
- How does the method perform when the attacker uses different mislabeling strategies, such as using clean labels for benign samples and poisoned labels for backdoored samples?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the dataset?
- How does the method perform when the adversary uses different trigger types, such as global transformations or input-specific triggers?
- Can the authors discuss the potential of the method in detecting backdoor attacks in real-world scenarios, especially in applications where the clean accuracy is critical?
