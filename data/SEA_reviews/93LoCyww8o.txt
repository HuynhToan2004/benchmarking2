 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a Hybrid Internal Model (HIM) for legged locomotion control in quadruped robots using minimal sensors like joint encoders and an Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU). The HIM method leverages contrastive learning to implicitly model environmental dynamics as a latent embedding, enhancing robustness and adaptability in unpredictable environments. The method is tested in both simulation and real-world scenarios, demonstrating its effectiveness and generalizability. The paper also discusses the challenges of using minimal sensors for locomotion and the need for further research on the generalizability of the method across different types of terrain and robots.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear and concise presentation of the main ideas and contributions.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and well-explained, with a clear comparison to existing methods and a thorough experimental evaluation.
- The method demonstrates good performance in both simulation and real-world scenarios, showing potential for broader applications in locomotion control.
- The use of minimal sensors for locomotion is a significant contribution, reducing the reliance on complex sensor setups and improving the practicality of the approach.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of ablation studies that effectively demonstrate the contributions of the different components of the proposed method.
- The method's ability to converge quickly with minimal training samples and its robustness to environmental disturbances are notable strengths.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning its generalizability to different types of terrain and robots.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the computational efficiency of the method, which could be a concern for real-time applications.
- The method's reliance on a specific sensor configuration (joint encoders and IMU) might limit its applicability to robots without these sensors, such as those with depth sensors.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the implications of the method's simplicity, particularly how it might affect the complexity of the learned dynamics model.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, especially in terms of the effectiveness of the proposed method in handling different types of terrain and robots.
- There are some minor presentation issues, such as unclear figures and typos, which could detract from the clarity and professionalism of the paper.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and real-time applicability to other methods?
- Could the authors provide more details on the generalizability of the method to different types of terrain and robots?
- How does the method perform when the sensor configuration deviates from the standard setup used in the experiments?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the convergence of the method with different training parameters, such as the number of samples used?
- How does the method handle different types of terrain, especially those not included in the training environment?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the IMU in the method and how it affects the learning process?
- Is there a possibility to include video results in the supplementary material to better demonstrate the method's performance?
