 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel task of Visual Data-Type Identification, evaluating 39 Vision-Language Models (VLMs) on a dataset of 1,350 images with 27 different data-types, including geometric, pixel, style, and semantic transformations. The study reveals that scaling model size does not significantly improve performance, and that large-scale models like IDEFICS perform worse than smaller models like CLIP. The authors suggest that this could be due to the models' inability to learn data-type discriminative features from their pre-training datasets, which lack sufficient data-type specific information. The paper also proposes a fine-tuning strategy using a specialized dataset, TeDaTy, to improve VLM performance. Despite the innovative approach and the creation of new datasets, the paper is criticized for its limited scope in terms of data-types and the lack of a clear distinction between data-types and domain shifts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow content, and includes informative figures and tables.
- The introduction of a new task, Visual Data-Type Identification, is timely and relevant, addressing the limitations of current models in handling different visual data-types.
- The creation of two new datasets, TypeIdent and TeDaTy, provides a valuable resource for the community, contributing to the advancement of visual data-type understanding in VLMs.
- The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of 39 VLMs, which helps in understanding the performance of various models across different data-types.
- The findings on the performance of VLMs on different data-types are insightful, highlighting the need for a more nuanced understanding of visual data-types.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear distinction between data-types and domain shifts, which could lead to confusion about the nature of the problem being addressed.
- The experimental setup is limited, focusing only on a subset of data-types and not adequately addressing the generalizability of the findings across different types of images.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the impact of data-type information on model performance, particularly in terms of whether the models are learning to identify data-types or simply distinguishing between different types of images.
- The novelty of the proposed task is questionable, as similar tasks have been explored in previous works, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods.
- The paper's conclusions are not robustly supported by the data, particularly in the claims about the impact of data-type information on model performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the study and the implications of the findings for future research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the distinction between data-types and domain shifts, and provide examples or scenarios where these concepts are distinct?
- How do the authors ensure that the models are learning to identify data-types and not just distinguishing between different types of images?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the data-type identification task and how it differs from other tasks in terms of its impact on model performance?
- How do the authors address the potential bias in the dataset towards certain data-types, and what steps were taken to ensure that the results are not skewed by this bias?
- Could the authors elaborate on the implications of their findings for the development of more effective VLMs, and suggest potential directions for future research in this area?
