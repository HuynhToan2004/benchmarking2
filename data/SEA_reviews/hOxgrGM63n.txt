 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the sampling from strongly log-concave distributions using the kinetic Langevin Monte Carlo (KLMC) algorithm with the randomized midpoint discretization method. The authors provide non-asymptotic error bounds for this method, which are claimed to be more efficient than previous bounds. The paper also discusses the theoretical properties of this method, including its convergence guarantees and the dependence on the condition number. It extends the analysis to the Euler discretization method, showing improved bounds for the W2-error. The paper is well-written and provides clear explanations, although the novelty of the proof techniques and the practical implications of the theoretical results are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the presentation is clear and straightforward.
- The authors have provided a clear and detailed explanation of the randomized midpoint discretization method, which is crucial for understanding the theoretical results.
- The paper provides an upper bound on the W2-error for the kinetic Langevin Monte Carlo algorithm with the randomized midpoint discretization method, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proof techniques used are novel and could be of independent interest, particularly in the context of the summation by parts technique.
- The paper provides a unified treatment of various LMC variants, which is beneficial for understanding the convergence guarantees and the dependence on initialization.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The novelty of the proof techniques is questionable as similar methods have been used in previous works, such as those by Shen and Lee (2019).
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art algorithms, which could help in evaluating its effectiveness and efficiency.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the strong convexity of the potential function, may not hold in many practical scenarios, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its approach, which could include the computational complexity of the proposed method and the impact of different initial conditions on the sampling error.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, especially in terms of their applicability to real-world problems?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art algorithms in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the strong convexity assumption, and discuss how these assumptions affect the generalizability of the results?
- In the proof of Theorem 1, why is the error deflation achieved by the randomized midpoint method not guaranteed for the Euler discretization?
