 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CO2, a novel asynchronous distributed training method designed to minimize communication overhead by overlapping communication with computation. CO2 achieves this by employing a local-updating strategy and asynchronous communication, which allows for the full overlap of communication with computation. This approach is supported by two techniques: staleness gap penalty and outer momentum clipping, aimed at enhancing convergence and training stability. The paper provides both theoretical and empirical evidence, demonstrating the effectiveness of CO2 through extensive experiments across various domains including computer vision and natural language processing. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and the need for a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its implementation.
- The methodology is supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed CO2 method.
- The CO2 method is innovative, achieving full overlap of communication with computation, which is a significant advancement in distributed training.
- The paper provides a comprehensive comparison of the CO2 method with other existing methods, showing its superiority in certain scenarios.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear figures and a well-structured presentation that makes it easy to understand the contributions and findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other asynchronous SGD methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the advantages and disadvantages of the CO2 method.
- The novelty of the CO2 method is limited as it primarily combines existing techniques such as asynchronous communication and local updating, without significant innovation.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the CO2 method, which could help in understanding its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios.
- The empirical results are not always convincing, with some experiments showing marginal improvements or no significant differences compared to baseline methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the convergence analysis and the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other asynchronous SGD methods to clarify the advantages and disadvantages of the CO2 method?
- How does the CO2 method perform in scenarios where the communication bandwidth is significantly reduced?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the hardware and software configurations used?
- Why is the CO2 method only tested up to 128 GPUs, and what are the plans for testing on larger clusters?
- Could the authors provide more details on the convergence analysis, particularly the assumptions made and the implications of these assumptions on the practical applicability of the CO2 method?
