 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedCDA, a novel federated learning (FL) method that selectively aggregates local models from multiple rounds to improve model accuracy. The authors propose a new aggregation method that minimizes the divergence between local models, thus maintaining their data knowledge. Theoretical analysis and extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of FedCDA over existing methods. The paper also discusses the convergence of the proposed method and provides a detailed algorithm for its implementation. However, the paper lacks clarity in its writing, and the experimental results, while promising, are not convincingly superior to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow content, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method, FedCDA, is novel and theoretically grounded, offering a new perspective on federated learning (FL) through selective aggregation of local models from multiple rounds.
- The method is supported by theoretical analysis and extensive experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency.
- The paper is original in its approach, focusing on the aggregation of cross-round local models, which is a less explored area in FL.
- The algorithm is clearly explained, with a detailed algorithm provided in the appendix, which aids in understanding the method's implementation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from inconsistencies in notation and lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the introduction and related work sections.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art techniques.
- The theoretical analysis, while comprehensive, does not fully address the convergence of the proposed method under non-convex conditions, which is a significant limitation.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and memory requirements of the proposed method, which are critical for practical implementation.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the impact of different batch sizes and client selections on the performance of the method.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the notation inconsistencies and typographical errors noted in the paper?
- How does the proposed method compare with state-of-the-art techniques in terms of computational efficiency and memory requirements?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the hyperparameters used and their impact on the results?
- How does the proposed method perform under different data distributions and model architectures, and what are the implications for its practical applicability?
- Could you elaborate on the theoretical assumptions and their practical implications, especially concerning the convergence of the proposed method under non-convex conditions?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of statistical heterogeneity in FL, and what are the implications for the generalization of the learned model?
