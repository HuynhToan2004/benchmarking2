 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for generating counterfactuals in latent causal models, focusing on the invertible latent domain causal model (ILD). The authors propose a canonical form for ILDs, which allows for the derivation of a necessary and sufficient condition for counterfactual equivalence. This approach is supported by theoretical results and empirical evidence, showing that the proposed method can outperform naive baselines in generating counterfactuals. The method is evaluated using both simulated and image-based datasets, demonstrating its practical utility. Despite some concerns regarding the clarity and depth of the experimental setup, the paper is generally well-received for its innovative approach and theoretical contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for generating counterfactuals using invertible latent domain causal models (ILD), which is a new approach that is not commonly used in this context.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise definitions, proofs, and theoretical results that are easy to follow.
- The proposed method is validated through both simulated and real-world experiments, showing its practical utility and potential for real-world applications.
- The theoretical results are interesting and provide a strong foundation for the proposed method, including the existence of equivalent canonical ILDs and the relationship between intervention sparsity and the effectiveness of the method.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in causal representation learning, which is of high interest to the ICLR community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The experimental setup is not well-described, making it difficult to evaluate the robustness and reliability of the results.
- The paper assumes that the causal variables are latent, which might not be a realistic assumption in all scenarios, and the methodology does not adequately address this limitation.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the assumptions made in the theoretical results, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer presentation of the assumptions and limitations.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when the causal variables are not latent? Can the authors provide examples or scenarios where this assumption holds?
- How does the method perform when the causal graph is not a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG)?
- Can the authors clarify the definition and usage of the "canonical ILD" concept, especially how it relates to the practical application of the method?
- How does the method handle the assumption of invertibility in the latent causal model, and what are the implications if this assumption does not hold?
- Could the authors elaborate on the computational complexity of the proposed method and discuss any potential limitations or challenges in its practical implementation?
- How does the method ensure that the generated counterfactuals are valid and meaningful, especially in scenarios where the assumptions of invertibility and sparsity might not hold?
