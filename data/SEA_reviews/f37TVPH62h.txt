 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the variance reduction in compound returns, specifically focusing on n-step and lambda-returns, in the context of reinforcement learning (RL). It introduces a new approach called Piecewise lambda-Return (PiLaR), which approximates the variance-reduction property of lambda-returns by averaging two n-step returns. The authors demonstrate that PiLaR can reduce variance and improve sample efficiency compared to n-step returns, especially in deep RL applications where lambda-returns are computationally expensive. Theoretical analyses support these claims, showing that PiLaR has a lower variance than n-step returns under certain conditions. However, the paper's theoretical contributions are limited to the contraction rate of the compound return, and the practical implications of the variance reduction are not convincingly demonstrated across a broader range of tasks or environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel concept of compound returns, which generalizes the use of n-step and lambda-returns, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the variance reduction in RL.
- The introduction of the Piecewise lambda-Return (PiLaR) algorithm is a significant contribution, offering an efficient approximation to the lambda-return while maintaining its variance-reduction property.
- The theoretical analysis of the variance reduction in compound returns is robust and well-articulated, supported by clear and concise mathematical formulations.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and logical structure, making it accessible to readers with varying levels of expertise in RL.
- The empirical results are convincing, demonstrating the practical applicability of the theoretical findings, particularly in the context of PiLaR's performance in deep RL environments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical contributions are limited to the contraction rate of the compound return, which may not be sufficient to fully capture the variance reduction properties of the compound returns.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the uniformity of TD errors and their correlation, are not convincingly justified or supported by empirical evidence.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of PiLaR over n-step returns across a broad range of tasks or environments, especially in more complex or varied scenarios.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods.
- There is a noticeable gap between the theoretical analysis and the empirical results, particularly in the demonstration of PiLaR's effectiveness across different environments and learning scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly Assumption 2, and provide empirical evidence to support these assumptions?
- How does the variance reduction property of PiLaR compare to other variance reduction techniques, such as those based on importance sampling or other variance reduction methods?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the performance of PiLaR in more complex or varied environments, and a comparison of its computational efficiency with other variance reduction methods?
- How does the choice of weights in the compound returns affect the variance reduction, and can the authors provide a more detailed analysis or experimental results on this aspect?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their approach and potential negative societal impacts, particularly in the context of deep RL applications where variance reduction could be critical?
