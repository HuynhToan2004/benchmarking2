 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ReVaR, a novel method for learning instance-dependent weights to quantify predictive uncertainty in neural networks. This approach utilizes a bi-level optimization framework with a meta-objective that minimizes dropout variance to approximate Bayesian predictive uncertainty. The method is evaluated across various datasets and tasks, including selective classification, calibration, and domain adaptation, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods. The paper also presents a theoretical analysis and empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of ReVaR in handling different types of uncertainty, such as aleatoric and epistemic.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a good balance of theoretical analysis and empirical evidence.
- The methodology is novel, combining variance minimization with bilevel optimization, which is well-motivated and supported by theoretical analysis.
- The paper demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods in various tasks, including selective classification, calibration, and domain adaptation.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering a wide range of tasks and datasets, and are well-designed to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the method and its contributions, with a well-structured presentation that makes it accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where it might not perform well or where it could be improved.
- There is a need for more clarity in the notation and definitions used, especially in the mathematical formulations, to avoid confusion.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing methods, including a more detailed discussion on the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method compared to these approaches.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the computational complexity and time required for training, which could be crucial for practical implementation.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous analysis of the assumptions made in the theoretical sections, particularly regarding the optimal weighting of training instances.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the computational complexity and time required for training the proposed method, especially in comparison to existing methods?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the training data is noisy or where the test data distribution significantly differs from the training data?
- Can you clarify the notation and definitions used in the mathematical formulations, particularly in the context of bilevel optimization and the meta-loss function?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability, especially in scenarios with large datasets or complex models?
- Can you elaborate on the assumptions made in the theoretical sections, particularly regarding the optimal weighting of training instances, and how these assumptions affect the practical implementation of the method?
