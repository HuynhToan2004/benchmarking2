 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel task planner for room rearrangement under partial observability, utilizing a hierarchical approach that includes a search network and a deep RL-based planner. The search network predicts the most probable receptacle for unseen objects, while the RL-based planner optimizes action sequences for simultaneous object search and rearrangement. The authors also propose a new benchmark dataset, RoPOR, and a set of metrics to evaluate the effectiveness of rearrangement planning. The methodology is evaluated against state-of-the-art methods, showing superior performance in terms of efficiency and effectiveness. However, concerns are raised about the complexity of the approach, the clarity of the methodology, and the practical applicability of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and challenging task of rearrangement in a partially observable environment, which is a significant advancement in the field of embodied AI.
- The introduction of a novel benchmark dataset, RoPOR, and a set of metrics specifically designed for evaluating the effectiveness of rearrangement planning is a valuable contribution to the research community.
- The methodology proposed, including the use of a search network and a deep RL-based planner, is innovative and shows promising results in terms of efficiency and effectiveness.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear and detailed explanations of the methodology, experiments, and results, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The experiments are comprehensive and convincing, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches in terms of efficiency and effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is overly complex, with multiple components (search network, graph-based state representation, deep RL-based planner, proxy reward network, etc.) which may not be necessary for achieving the desired outcomes.
- The methodology and implementation details are not clearly explained, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the proposed methods and their implications.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the potential negative societal impacts of the research.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its results with existing methods, such as those by Ghosh et al., which might lead to misleading conclusions about the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical applicability of the results, particularly in real-world scenarios where the assumptions of perfect motion planning and manipulation capabilities might not hold.
- There are issues with the clarity and organization of the paper, with some sections being hard to follow and figures not being well-explained.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the necessity and effectiveness of each component in the proposed method, particularly the search network and the deep RL-based planner?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where objects are occluded due to clutter on receptacles, and what are the implications of this limitation on the practical applicability of the method?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the methodology and implementation, especially in sections where the details are currently lacking?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods in terms of computational complexity and resource requirements?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their research and propose strategies for mitigating these impacts?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where objects are initially outside the agent's field of view but are visible from a different perspective?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and testing of the proposed method, including the size and complexity of the training dataset and the specifics of the training and testing protocols?
