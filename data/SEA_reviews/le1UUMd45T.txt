 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel learning-based improvement method for multi-objective combinatorial optimization problems (MOCOPs), leveraging a "Learn to Improve" (L2I) paradigm through a Weight-related Policy Network (WRPN). This method is integrated into multi-objective evolutionary algorithm (MOEA) frameworks, enhancing the search direction guidance and improving the Pareto set in model inference. The proposed WRPN outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods on classic MOCOPs such as the multi-objective traveling salesman problem and multi-objective vehicle routing problem. The paper also discusses a shared baseline and a quality enhancement mechanism to mitigate issues like local optima and diversity. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental scope, lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, and unclear presentation of the methodology and results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to solving multi-objective combinatorial optimization problems (MOCOPs) by integrating a "Learn to Improve" (L2I) paradigm with a Weight-related Policy Network (WRPN), which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed WRPN is generic and can be integrated into various MOEA frameworks such as NSGA-II, MOEA/D, and MOGLS, making it versatile and adaptable to different optimization scenarios.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the WRPN method, demonstrating its effectiveness through experiments on two classic MOCOPs, the multi-objective traveling salesman problem and multi-objective vehicle routing problem.
- The authors have designed a shared baseline and a quality enhancement mechanism to address issues like local optima and diversity, which are crucial for improving the performance of MOCOPs.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and includes detailed explanations of the methodology, making it accessible and understandable to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the number of non-dominated solutions generated.
- The experimental results are limited to two specific MOCOPs, and it would be beneficial to see how the WRPN performs on a broader range of problems to validate its general applicability.
- There is a need for more detailed descriptions of the network architecture and training details, especially the specifics of the weight-related policy network and the training parameters.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed method and a deeper exploration of the impact of different training parameters on the performance.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, especially in the main text and the appendices.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for ensuring the ethical and responsible application of the technology.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the number of non-dominated solutions generated?
- How does the WRPN perform on a broader range of MOCOPs, and what are the specific challenges or advantages in applying the method to different types of problems?
- Could the authors elaborate on the specifics of the weight-related policy network, such as the number of layers, neurons, and activation functions used?
- How were the training parameters, such as the learning rate and number of epochs, selected, and what impact do these parameters have on the performance of the WRPN?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations and negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
