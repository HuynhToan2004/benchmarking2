 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph generation method named Destination-Predicting Diffusion Mixture (DruM), which models the graph topology by predicting the destination of the diffusion process. DruM employs a mixture of diffusion processes conditioned on the endpoint in the data distribution, which guides the diffusion process toward the predicted destination, enhancing convergence speed. The method is evaluated on diverse datasets including general graphs and molecules, demonstrating superior performance over existing diffusion models. It also introduces a new training objective for predicting the destination, which is crucial for accurate graph generation. Despite its technical strengths, the paper is criticized for lacking a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods and for potential issues in the clarity and organization of the presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel graph generation method, DruM, which predicts the destination of the diffusion process to model graph topology, a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is technically sound, with a well-designed model and a novel training objective that enhances the accuracy and efficiency of graph generation.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a logical structure that aids in understanding the complex mathematical concepts.
- The proposed method shows superior performance over existing diffusion models on various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and potential impact.
- The use of the destination mixture matching objective for training is innovative and provides a clear explanation of the method's operation.
- The paper is well-motivated, addressing the limitations of existing diffusion models in modeling graph topology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the ability to generate graphs with complex structures.
- The method's effectiveness in generating graphs with complex topologies or specific structures (e.g., planar graphs, graphs with specific edge distributions) is not adequately demonstrated.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with some figures and equations being hard to follow or understand.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the method handles graphs with continuous features, such as 3D molecules, which could limit its applicability in certain domains.
- The paper's experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the improvements in performance are sometimes marginal.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its practical applicability or scalability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method handles graphs with continuous features, such as 3D molecules?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency with existing methods, especially in generating complex graphs?
- Can the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in generating graphs with specific structures or topologies, such as planar graphs or graphs with specific edge distributions?
- How does the method ensure the generation of valid graphs, especially in scenarios where the prior distribution might not fully capture the data distribution?
- Could the authors clarify the notations and equations used in the paper, particularly those that are hard to follow or understand?
- How does the proposed method handle the generation of graphs with different node features, and what are the implications for the model's performance and scalability?
