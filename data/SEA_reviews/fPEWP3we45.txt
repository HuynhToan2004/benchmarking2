 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Pattern Observer (PrObe), a method designed for adaptable error detection (AED) in few-shot imitation (FSI) policies. PrObe is trained on both successful and failed rollouts in base environments, utilizing a pattern extractor and a pattern flow generator to identify discernible patterns in policy feature representations. It employs a temporal-aware triplet loss to optimize error prediction, demonstrating superior performance over existing baselines in various few-shot imitation tasks. The method's effectiveness is validated through comprehensive evaluations, showcasing its ability to detect errors in novel environments and its robustness against behavioral changes.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem of error detection in few-shot imitation (FSI) policies, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- The proposed method, PrObe, is well-motivated and effectively addresses the challenges of detecting errors in novel environments, no impulse signals, and online detection.
- The method is technically sound, with a well-designed architecture that includes a pattern extractor, pattern flow generator, and embedding fusion, optimized by supervised and unsupervised objectives.
- The paper is well-written, making the methodology and experimental results clear and understandable, with a good balance between theoretical analysis and practical demonstration.
- The evaluation is comprehensive, involving seven complex multi-stage FSI tasks, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the performance of the proposed method, comparing it against strong baselines and showing consistent improvements.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the role and impact of the task-embeddings in the proposed method, particularly how they are utilized within the PrObe architecture.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method to more complex or realistic environments, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the proposed method could be adapted or extended to handle different types of errors or scenarios beyond those tested.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and visualizations of the learned pattern representations to enhance understanding and reproducibility.
- The method's reliance on specific types of errors (e.g., those with significant visual differences or task misunderstandings) might limit its effectiveness in scenarios where errors are less apparent or where the policy's behavior is not directly observable.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the task-embeddings within the PrObe architecture, particularly how they are utilized to map the task-embeddings into a space similar to the pattern flow?
- How does the proposed method handle errors that are not visually distinct or where the policy's behavior is not directly observable?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the method could be adapted or extended to handle different types of errors or scenarios beyond those tested?
- In the context of the limitations mentioned, what are the specific scenarios or types of errors that the proposed method might struggle to detect?
- How does the method handle the issue of scalability, particularly in more complex or realistic environments?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations and visualizations of the learned pattern representations to enhance understanding and reproducibility?
