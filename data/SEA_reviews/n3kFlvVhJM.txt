 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Adapted Dense Retrieval (ADR), a method designed to enhance the performance of retrieval systems by adapting pre-trained embeddings to task-specific notions of similarity. ADR utilizes a parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) approach, specifically adapters, to modify the embeddings of queries and corpus elements. The method is evaluated across various datasets and retrieval tasks, showing improvements over general-purpose embeddings. The paper also discusses the challenges of heterogeneous and strict retrieval, and how ADR addresses these issues by learning residual adaptations from a dictionary.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components.
- The idea of adapting pre-trained embeddings to task-specific notions of similarity is innovative and addresses a significant gap in current retrieval systems.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over general-purpose embeddings in various retrieval tasks.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the proposed method, including the use of adapters and the training process, which is crucial for understanding the method's implementation.
- The method's simplicity and adaptability to different retrieval tasks, such as heterogeneous and strict retrieval, make it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art retrieval models, which could provide a better understanding of the method's performance relative to existing methods.
- The experimental results are limited to specific datasets and retrieval tasks, raising concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational overhead of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for real-world applications.
- The paper's claims about the necessity of the proposed method are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which could lead to questions about the method's effectiveness.
