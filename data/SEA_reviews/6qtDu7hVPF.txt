 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of transformer-based models for value function approximation in reinforcement learning (RL), specifically focusing on the generative decomposition of the action-value function. The authors propose using transformers to predict the state given an action and return, leveraging pre-trained models to enhance performance. The methodology is tested on tasks such as chess and gridworlds, showing some improvements over behavioral cloning and other methods. However, the paper is criticized for its limited evaluation scope, lack of comparison with more contemporary RL algorithms, and unclear presentation in certain sections.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical foundations.
- The use of transformers for value function approximation is an innovative approach that shows potential for enhancing RL capabilities.
- The methodology is supported by extensive empirical evaluations, demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed techniques.
- The paper is structured to be accessible to readers with varying backgrounds in RL and transformer models, making it a valuable resource for understanding the intersection of these fields.
- The use of pre-trained models for value function approximation is a promising direction, showing potential for more efficient and effective RL systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with more contemporary RL algorithms, which limits the evaluation of its effectiveness against state-of-the-art methods.
- The evaluation scope is limited to simple tasks like chess and gridworlds, which may not adequately demonstrate the method's capabilities in more complex or realistic RL environments.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of certain sections, particularly Section 4, which could benefit from more detailed explanations or restructuring for better flow.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach, such as the potential for overfitting or the generalizability of the results to more complex environments.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with some results (e.g., in chess) showing only marginal improvements over simpler methods like behavioral cloning.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on the advantages of using transformers for value function approximation over traditional methods, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- How does the method perform when applied to more complex or realistic RL environments, such as Atari or MuJoCo tasks?
- In the chess evaluation, why was the discriminative decomposition not tested against an oracle policy, and how does the method compare to simpler baselines like behavioral cloning?
- What are the specific advantages of using a transformer-based model over a traditional RL algorithm like DQN, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and learning capabilities?
- Could the authors provide more details on the pre-training of the state predictor and the choice of hyperparameters, such as the number of return buckets and the concentration parameter of the Dirichlet-Multinomial model?
- How does the method handle the complexity of RL environments, especially in terms of state and action spaces, and what are the implications for the scalability and generalizability of the results?
