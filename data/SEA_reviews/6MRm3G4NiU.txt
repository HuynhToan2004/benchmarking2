 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel structure-aware vocabulary that integrates residue and structure information of proteins, termed "SA-token sequence," to enhance protein language models (PLMs) by incorporating 3D structural information. The proposed methodology, named SaProt, is trained on a large dataset of 40 million protein sequences and structures, surpassing the capabilities of existing PLMs like ESM. SaProt utilizes a combination of residue and structure tokens, which are derived using Foldseek, to capture geometric conformation information of each residue in relation to its spatial neighbors. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive evaluations across ten downstream tasks, showcasing its superior performance over existing baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel structure-aware vocabulary that effectively integrates both residue and 3D geometric features, enhancing the representation of proteins in protein language models (PLMs).
- The proposed SaProt model, trained on a large dataset of 40 million protein sequences and structures, achieves state-of-the-art performance on ten protein function prediction tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive evaluations and ablation studies, providing valuable insights into the model's performance and capabilities.
- The availability of code and pre-trained models, along with the dataset used, will significantly benefit the research community by facilitating further research and development in the field.
- The paper is significant in its contribution to the field by integrating 3D structural information into PLMs, which has the potential to significantly advance the understanding and prediction of protein structures and functions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the training time and computational cost of SaProt compared to other models like ESM-2, which could be crucial for understanding the practicality and scalability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not thoroughly discuss the impact of different masking strategies for structure tokens on the model's performance, which could be an important factor in optimizing the model's training and inference.
- There is a potential overfitting issue when integrating predicted structures with BERT-style training, which is not adequately addressed in the paper.
- The paper's reliance on Foldseek for encoding protein structures might limit the model's ability to capture detailed structural information, and the accuracy of the Foldseek encoding is not thoroughly validated.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the model's performance is influenced by the accuracy of the Foldseek encoding and the potential impact of using different protein structure prediction methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed information on the training time and computational cost of SaProt compared to other models like ESM-2?
- How does the model's performance vary with different masking strategies for structure tokens, and what are the optimal strategies for training and inference?
- Can the authors elaborate on the potential overfitting issue when integrating predicted structures with BERT-style training, and how this was addressed in the model's development?
- How does the model's performance compare when using different protein structure prediction methods, such as AlphaFold2 versus Foldseek?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of using different protein structure prediction methods on the model's performance and the accuracy of the Foldseek encoding?
