 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel evaluation paradigm, T4D, which assesses the capability of large language models (LLMs) in determining proper actions based on mental states of others, challenging their ability to bridge social reasoning to actions. The authors propose a zero-shot prompting framework, FaR, to enhance LLMs' performance on T4D tasks. The study compares LLMs with human performance and demonstrates that while LLMs struggle with T4D, human performance is significantly higher. The paper also presents an ablation study to understand the effectiveness of the FaR framework and its components, and discusses the generalization of the proposed method across different scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel evaluation paradigm, T4D, which challenges LLMs to bridge social reasoning to actions, and proposes a new zero-shot prompting framework, FaR, to enhance LLMs' performance on T4D tasks.
- The authors conduct an ablation study to understand the effectiveness of the FaR framework and its components, and discuss the generalization of the proposed method across different scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of the methodology and results, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The research addresses a critical and timely question regarding the capability of LLMs in determining proper actions based on mental states of others, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the challenges LLMs face in T4D tasks and offers a detailed breakdown of reasoning challenges, which could be beneficial for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on GPT-4 as the primary LLM model raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings to other models.
- The T4D task and its conversion from existing benchmarks (e.g., ToMi) might not accurately reflect the complexity and nuance of real-world social interactions, potentially limiting the validity of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential biases in the data used to train LLMs, which could affect the fairness and reliability of the comparisons made between LLMs and humans.
- The paper's reliance on a single model (GPT-4) for the majority of its experiments could limit the generalizability of the findings to other models.
- The analysis of the reasoning challenges in T4D tasks is not comprehensive, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed exploration of these challenges to provide a deeper understanding of the task's complexity.
- The paper's focus on zero-shot prompting might not fully capture the potential of LLMs in T4D tasks, as it does not explore the use of few-shot prompting or other methods that might enhance performance.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the data used to train the LLMs, including the sources and types of data, to ensure a fair comparison with human performance?
- How do you ensure that the reasoning challenges identified in the T4D tasks are truly unique to the T4D paradigm, and not simply a reflection of existing biases or limitations in the data used to train the LLMs?
- How does the FaR prompting framework compare to other prompting strategies, especially in terms of its generalizability and effectiveness across different models and tasks?
- Can you discuss the potential implications of the FaR framework on the development of LLMs that can effectively handle T4D tasks, and how this could impact the field of AI research?
- How do you address the potential biases in the data used to train LLMs, and what steps are being taken to ensure that the results are not skewed by these biases?
