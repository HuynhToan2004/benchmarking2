 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for explaining RBF-kernel Support Vector Machines (SVMs) in neuroimaging data analysis. This approach, termed Estimated Activation Pattern (EAP), aims to generate global explanations in kernel space, which are less affected by noise variables compared to other feature importance methods. The method is evaluated on both simulated and real EEG/MEG datasets, demonstrating its ability to identify relevant channels and improve interpretability. The paper also discusses the limitations of the method, including its inability to provide local explanations and its potential limitations in different types of kernel methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method.
- The method is novel and innovative, offering a new approach to explaining RBF-kernel SVMs, particularly in neuroimaging data analysis.
- The methodology is supported by strong experimental results, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in generating global explanations.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear and concise presentation of the method and its application.
- The method is computationally efficient, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the method, which is critical for a complete understanding of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The method's applicability is limited to RBF-kernel SVMs and cannot be extended to other types of kernel methods, which may limit its utility in broader applications.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the method handles noise variables, which is a significant concern in neuroimaging data analysis.
- The method's focus on global explanations may not provide sufficient insight into the specific contributions of individual features or channels, which is crucial for understanding complex neuroimaging data.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the assumptions made by the method, which could affect its generalizability and applicability.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to a few datasets, which may not be sufficient to demonstrate the method's effectiveness across a broader range of scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the method handle noise variables in neuroimaging data, and what measures are taken to ensure the robustness of the explanations against noise?
- Can the method be adapted to provide local explanations, and if so, what are the potential challenges and limitations of such an adaptation?
- How does the method perform when applied to other types of kernel methods, and what are the expected outcomes?
- Could you provide more details on the assumptions made by the method and how they affect the interpretability of the results?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and interpretability, especially in complex neuroimaging data scenarios?
- Can you elaborate on the method's ability to identify relevant channels and its impact on the overall performance of the model?
