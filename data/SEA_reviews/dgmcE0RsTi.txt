 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SimCAS, a method designed to process long sequences using pre-trained transformers. It addresses the computational challenges of self-attention operations by dividing long sequences into chunks, aligning inter-chunk information, and selecting representative hidden states for decoding. The method leverages reinforcement learning to optimize the selection policy, treating transformers as environments with attention scores and downstream performance feedback as rewards. The paper claims that SimCAS outperforms other long-sequence processing baselines and is highly scalable, demonstrating effectiveness in tasks such as long-text abstractive summarization and reading comprehension.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a simple yet effective method for long-sequence processing using pre-trained transformers, which is compatible with existing models and does not require customized self-attention implementations.
- The method is intuitive, easy to understand, and has been tested on a variety of datasets, showing promising results in terms of performance and scalability.
- The use of reinforcement learning (RL) to optimize the token selector is innovative and adds a layer of complexity to the method, potentially improving the model's ability to focus on task-relevant tokens.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The method's compatibility with existing pre-trained language models (PLMs) and its ability to handle both long and short sequence tasks are significant advantages over other methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other long-sequence processing methods, particularly those that use efficient attention operations or other non-chunking strategies.
- The method's reliance on the BERTScore metric for evaluation raises concerns about the fairness of the comparison, as other methods may not use this metric.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the method, which is crucial for understanding its efficiency.
- The method's scalability is questionable, as it may not be suitable for very long sequences or sequences with a large number of tokens, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method in detail, which could help readers better understand its scope and applicability.
- The paper does not compare its method with other state-of-the-art methods that use efficient attention operations, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform on very long sequences, and what are the computational costs associated with processing such sequences?
- Can the method be adapted to handle sequences with a large number of tokens, such as those found in molecular structure analysis?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods that use efficient attention operations, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- What are the specific computational complexities of the SimCAS method, and how do these compare to other long-sequence processing methods?
- How does the method handle the variability in the number of tokens across different sequences, and what are the implications for the selection of tokens for decoding?
- Could the method be adapted to other types of long sequences, such as time series data, and how would this affect its performance and applicability?
