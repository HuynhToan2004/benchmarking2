 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for causal discovery in time series data using a doubly robust approach based on Granger causality. It extends the DoubleML framework to the time series setting, leveraging Granger causality to identify direct causes without requiring assumptions like causal faithfulness or causal sufficiency. The methodology is theoretically grounded, showing consistency and asymptotic results, and is supported by empirical evaluations on semi-synthetic datasets. Despite its theoretical contributions, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity in explaining the methodology, particularly in the presentation of Granger causality and the application of the DoubleML framework. The empirical evaluations, while comprehensive, are limited to semi-synthetic data, which raises concerns about the generalizability of the results to real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and challenging problem in causal discovery from time series data, focusing on the identification of direct causes without strong assumptions like causal faithfulness or causal sufficiency.
- The methodology is theoretically sound, with proofs and empirical results that support the claims.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and its application, making it accessible to readers not specialized in the area.
- The use of the DoubleML framework for causal discovery is innovative, providing a robust theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
- The paper presents a novel approach that allows for the identification of direct causes without requiring strong assumptions, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a clearer presentation and explanation of the methodology, particularly in sections where Granger causality and the DoubleML framework are discussed.
- The empirical evaluations are limited to semi-synthetic data, which may not fully reflect the performance of the method in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, which is necessary to establish the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive empirical evaluations, including real-world datasets and comparisons with other methods, to validate the claims more robustly.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the method and discuss how these assumptions impact the applicability and effectiveness of the proposed approach?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where there are multiple causes for a given outcome, and how does it handle confounding effects?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the method, especially in terms of the number of variables and time steps?
- How does the method compare to other existing methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- In light of the concerns about the clarity of the methodology and the empirical evaluations, can the authors provide additional examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications and effectiveness of the proposed method?
