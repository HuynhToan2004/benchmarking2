 **Summary:**
The paper explores the characterization of equivariant layers with point-wise activations, focusing on the admissible representations and activation functions. It extends the results from Wood and Shawe-Taylor (1996) by considering continuous activation functions and non-finite groups, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the equivariance properties of neural networks. The authors derive a theorem that characterizes the maximal set of equivariant classes and their dual pairings, which are crucial for designing equivariant networks. This theorem is applied to various practical scenarios, including rotation-equivariant networks, invariant graph networks, and geometric graph processing, highlighting both the limitations and possibilities of these models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive characterization of equivariant layers with point-wise activations, which is a significant extension of the results from Wood and Shawe-Taylor (1996).
- The authors have derived a theorem that characterizes the maximal set of equivariant classes and their dual pairings, which is a novel contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation and detailed proofs, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The practical implications of the theoretical results are discussed, providing insights into the limitations and possibilities of using equivariant networks in various applications.
- The paper is well-positioned within the existing literature, providing a clear discussion of related work and its contributions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is quite dense and assumes a high level of background knowledge, which may make it challenging for readers without a strong foundation in representation theory and group theory.
- The paper does not provide code, which could limit its reproducibility and accessibility to practitioners.
- The practical relevance of the results is not clearly demonstrated, and the paper lacks experimental validation, which could help in demonstrating the applicability of the theoretical findings.
- The paper's focus on finite-dimensional representations and point-wise activations may limit its applicability to more complex and realistic scenarios, such as those involving infinite-dimensional representations or more complex activation functions.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the results for practical applications, particularly in terms of how the theoretical findings can be used to design more effective equivariant networks.
