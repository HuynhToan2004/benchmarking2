 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework called Semantic Inference (SINF) to enhance the efficiency of deep neural networks (DNNs) by leveraging semantic similarities among different classes. SINF uses a small auxiliary classifier to identify the semantic cluster of an input object, then activates the corresponding subgraph from the base DNN to obtain the final prediction. The proposed Discriminative Capability Score (DCS) is used to extract cluster-specific subgraphs, which are critical for efficient inference. The framework is tested on the CIFAR100 dataset, showing significant reductions in inference time with minimal accuracy loss. The paper also provides a detailed analysis of the redundancy in latent representations and the semantic similarity among classes, which are crucial for the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to semantic inference that leverages the redundancy in latent representations to reduce computational load, which is a significant advancement in the field of neural network efficiency.
- The proposed Discriminative Capability Score (DCS) is a novel and effective method for extracting cluster-specific subgraphs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the proposed methods and experimental results, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The experimental results show that the proposed methods can achieve significant reductions in inference time with minimal accuracy loss, demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed framework.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the redundancy in latent representations and the semantic similarity among classes, which are crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods, which is crucial for ensuring the ethical and responsible use of the technology.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary classifier, which could significantly impact the overall efficiency of the system.
- The experimental results are primarily based on the CIFAR100 dataset, which may not generalize well to other, more complex datasets such as ImageNet.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practicality of the proposed methods in real-world scenarios, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for training and inference.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a discussion on the computational efficiency and accuracy trade-offs.
- The paper could be improved by including more detailed visualizations and explanations of the filter activations and their impact on the overall performance of the network.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed framework perform when applied to more complex datasets such as ImageNet?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary classifier?
- How does the proposed framework compare in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy with other state-of-the-art methods?
- Could the authors provide more detailed visualizations and explanations of the filter activations and their impact on the overall performance of the network?
- How does the proposed framework handle semantic similarity between classes that are not directly related, such as "dog" and "car"?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and inference times required for the proposed methods, and how these times compare to those of other methods?
