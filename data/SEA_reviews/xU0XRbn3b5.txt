 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the privacy risks associated with overparameterized models, focusing on the stability and feature alignment in the context of information retrieval attacks. It introduces two new measures: stability and feature alignment, which are crucial for understanding the attack resistance of models. The authors provide theoretical insights into these measures, particularly in the context of random features and neural tangent kernel regression, and validate these findings through empirical experiments. The paper discusses the implications of these measures on the privacy and security of models, suggesting that a model's stability and feature alignment can influence its vulnerability to attacks. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental scope, focusing only on binary classification, and its assumptions which might not generalize well to other types of data or models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces two new measures, stability and feature alignment, which are novel and well-defined, providing a theoretical framework for understanding the privacy implications of overparameterized models.
- The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of these measures, demonstrating their connection to the generalization capability of models, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear and logical structure that facilitates understanding of the complex concepts presented.
- The experimental results are convincingly presented, showing a good correlation between the theoretical predictions and empirical observations, which strengthens the credibility of the findings.
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue in the field of machine learning, focusing on the privacy and security of models, which is a critical concern in the era of data-driven applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus is limited to binary classification, which might not generalize well to other types of data or models, such as those used in multi-class or multi-label classification tasks.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the over-parameterization and high-dimensional data assumptions, might not hold true for all types of data, limiting the applicability of the results.
- The experimental setup is somewhat simplistic, focusing only on binary classification and not exploring the impact of the attacker's query on the model's performance or the stability of the model.
- The paper lacks a broader discussion on the implications of the findings, particularly in terms of their practical applications and the potential societal impacts of the research.
- There are some typographical errors and inconsistencies in the paper, such as unclear definitions and missing references, which could detract from the overall quality and clarity of the work.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of the feature map in the context of the paper, particularly in relation to the stability and feature alignment measures?
- How does the paper address the potential societal impacts of the research, particularly in terms of privacy and security implications for users?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup, including the choice of binary classification and the rationale behind the specific experimental conditions used?
- How do the authors justify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the over-parameterization and high-dimensional data assumptions, and what are the implications if these assumptions do not hold true for all types of data?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical applications of the findings, particularly in terms of how the stability and feature alignment measures can be used to enhance the security and privacy of machine learning models?
