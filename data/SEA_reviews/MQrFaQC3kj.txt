 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the fairness-accuracy trade-off in machine learning models by proposing a method to estimate the optimal trade-off curve for a given dataset and accuracy threshold. It employs the You-Only-Train-Once (YOTO) framework to train a single model that can represent a range of fairness-accuracy trade-offs, thus reducing computational burden. The method constructs confidence intervals around this curve, providing a statistically grounded perspective on acceptable fairness violations for a given accuracy threshold. The paper is evaluated across various datasets and demonstrates that the proposed method can guide practitioners in making accuracy-constrained fairness decisions across different data modalities. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach and the theoretical underpinnings of the confidence intervals.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely issue in the field of fairness in machine learning, focusing on the estimation of fairness-accuracy trade-off curves, which is a critical aspect of fairness-aware machine learning.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly explained, with a focus on practical applications.
- The use of the You-Only-Train-Once (YOTO) framework to estimate fairness-accuracy trade-off curves is innovative and computationally efficient, reducing the need for retraining multiple models.
- The paper includes a comprehensive empirical evaluation across various datasets, including tabular, computer vision, and natural language processing, demonstrating the versatility of the proposed method.
- The introduction of confidence intervals around the fairness-accuracy trade-off curve provides a statistically grounded perspective on acceptable fairness violations, which is crucial for practitioners in making informed decisions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the novelty of the approach compared to existing literature, particularly in terms of the methodology and its theoretical contributions.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the confidence intervals, which are a significant contribution of the work.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the proposed methodology can be applied in scenarios where sensitive attributes are scarce, which is a common issue in real-world datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability in scenarios with limited data resources.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the theoretical assumptions and their implications, especially concerning the construction of confidence intervals.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the novelty of their approach compared to existing literature, particularly in terms of methodology and theoretical contributions?
- How does the proposed methodology perform in scenarios where sensitive attributes are scarce? Could the authors provide more insights or examples on how their method can be applied in such settings?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of the confidence intervals, particularly how they are constructed and the assumptions made in their construction?
- How does the proposed methodology compare to other methods that construct confidence intervals for the fairness-accuracy trade-off curve? What are the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method compared to these other methods?
- Could the authors provide more details on the assumptions made in the construction of the confidence intervals and discuss the implications of these assumptions on the practical applicability of the method?
