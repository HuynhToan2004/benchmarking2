 **Summary:**
The paper explores a novel debate framework for verifying AI models, focusing on the efficiency of the verifier's queries. The authors propose a method where the verifier, instead of querying the AI models directly, relies on a set of polynomial-time provers to debate the correctness of a computation. This approach allows for a significant reduction in the number of queries needed, which is crucial for scalability. The paper also discusses how this method can be adapted to various settings, including those with stochastic oracles and with a witness. However, the paper lacks empirical evaluations and clear practical implications, which limits its applicability and impact.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations that make complex concepts accessible.
- The introduction of the concept of "doubly-efficient debate" is novel and provides a new perspective on the verification of computational problems.
- The paper presents several settings and theorems that demonstrate the versatility and applicability of the proposed method in various scenarios.
- The theoretical results are robust and contribute significantly to the understanding of the verification process in AI systems.
- The paper effectively connects the theoretical concepts with practical implications, such as the use of LLMs as oracles, which enhances the relevance and applicability of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical evaluations, which makes it difficult to assess the practical effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not clearly demonstrate how the theoretical results can be translated into practical applications, which limits its immediate impact.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the proposed methods can be implemented in real-world scenarios, particularly in terms of the scalability and efficiency of the verifier's queries.
- The paper's focus on the efficiency of the verifier's queries might not fully address the complexity of the verification process, particularly in scenarios where the computations are complex or the oracles are less reliable.
- The paper's assumptions about the computational power of the provers and the reliability of the oracles are not sufficiently justified or explained, which could limit the generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies that demonstrate how the theoretical results can be applied in practical scenarios?
- How can the proposed methods be adapted to handle scenarios where the computations are more complex or the oracles are less reliable?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made about the computational power of the provers and the reliability of the oracles, and discuss how these assumptions affect the practical applicability of the results?
- How does the paper address the issue of scalability, particularly in terms of the number of queries needed for verification as the complexity of the computations increases?
- Could the authors discuss the potential implications of their findings for the broader field of AI safety, particularly in terms of the practical applications and the broader societal impact of their work?
