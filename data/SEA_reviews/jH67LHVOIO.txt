 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LITCAB, a lightweight calibration mechanism for large language models (LLMs) designed to address the calibration issues in LLMs, particularly in open-ended question-answering tasks. LITCAB involves a single linear layer that predicts a bias term based on sentence representations, which is then added to the LLM's output logits. The method is evaluated on a newly constructed benchmark, CAT, consisting of six open-ended QA tasks, ranging from short phrases to paragraphs. The experiments demonstrate that LITCAB outperforms baseline methods in terms of calibration, and the paper provides insights into the relationship between model size, calibration, and task performance. However, the paper has been critiqued for its reliance on GPT-4 for claim verification, which might limit its applicability in closed-source models, and for not adequately addressing the scalability and computational cost of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and practical calibration mechanism, LITCAB, which does not require additional training or fine-tuning of the LLM and adds only a small number of extra parameters.
- The paper constructs a benchmark, CAT, which evaluates the calibration of LLMs in open-ended QA tasks with varying-length answers, and provides insights into the relationship between model size, calibration, and task performance.
- The methodology of LITCAB is lightweight, making it suitable for a wide range of LLMs, and the paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the calibration of seven state-of-the-art non-source LLMs.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research and a detailed evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the results, which is helpful in understanding the implications of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies on GPT-4 for claim verification, which might limit its applicability in closed-source models that are only accessible via closed APIs.
- The scalability of the proposed method is not adequately addressed, particularly in terms of how it performs with larger models and how it compares to other methods in terms of computational cost.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of LLMs and its generalizability across different domains.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other calibration methods, particularly in terms of computational cost and the scalability of the proposed method.
