 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Federated Continual Learning (FCL) framework named Concept Matching (CM), designed to address the challenges of catastrophic forgetting and concept interference in federated learning. The CM framework employs clustering and concept matching algorithms to group client models based on similar concepts and update global models accordingly. This approach allows for the efficient handling of concept drift and ensures the scalability of the model across multiple clients. The framework is evaluated on various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness compared to existing methods. However, concerns were raised regarding the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the methodology, and the scalability of the framework in more complex and diverse settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method.
- The proposed Concept Matching (CM) framework is novel and addresses a significant problem in Federated Learning (FL) by effectively managing catastrophic forgetting and interference among clients.
- The framework is flexible, allowing for the use of different clustering, aggregation, and concept matching algorithms, enhancing its applicability in various scenarios.
- The evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the CM framework in improving performance over state-of-the-art systems, with results showing scalability with the number of clients and model size.
- The paper is the first to address the challenges of Federated Continual Learning (FCL) by combining Federated Learning (FL) and Continual Learning (CL), which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as the idea of using clustering to solve the interference problem in FL is not new.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the framework, particularly in terms of the number of clients and the complexity of the model.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodology, particularly in the concept matching algorithms and the role of the server in updating the global concept models.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of concept drift, which is a critical aspect of continual learning.
- The paper's experimental setup is limited, using only six datasets, which may not be sufficient to demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of the proposed framework.
- There is a lack of discussion on the privacy implications of the framework and how it compares to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and communication overhead.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the framework, particularly in terms of the number of clients and the complexity of the model?
- How does the proposed framework handle the issue of concept drift, which is a significant challenge in continual learning?
- Could the authors clarify the concept matching algorithms and the role of the server in updating the global concept models?
- How does the framework compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and communication overhead?
- Can the authors provide more details on the privacy implications of the framework and how it addresses the challenges of federated learning in a privacy-preserving manner?
- How does the framework perform in more complex and diverse settings, such as those involving different types of data or larger models?
