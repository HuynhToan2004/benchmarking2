 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method to enhance Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) in molecular graph learning by introducing "Neural Atoms," which aggregate information from atomic groups within a molecule. This approach allows GNNs to capture long-range interactions effectively, reducing the interaction scope of arbitrary node pairs into a single hop. The method is evaluated on three long-range graph benchmarks and demonstrates significant performance improvements over existing methods. The authors also establish a connection between their method and the Ewald summation method, enhancing the theoretical understanding of the proposed approach. Despite its strengths, the paper has some concerns regarding the novelty and the clarity of the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel concept of "Neural Atoms," which effectively captures long-range interactions in molecular graphs, enhancing the performance of GNNs.
- The methodology is supported by a physical perspective, connecting it with the Ewald summation method, which is a notable theoretical contribution.
- The proposed method is architecture-agnostic and computation-efficient, which makes it easily applicable to various GNNs and enhances its practical utility.
- The paper provides extensive experiments on long-range molecular datasets for property prediction and structure reconstruction, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across different tasks.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, and includes detailed experimental setups and hyperparameters, ensuring reproducibility.
- The connection between the proposed method and Ewald summation provides a strong theoretical foundation, enhancing the credibility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its applicability in certain scenarios.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it seems to be a combination of existing techniques such as GNNs and self-attention mechanisms.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including a broader range of datasets, including those with 3D coordinate information, and comparing the proposed method with more recent and relevant baselines.
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for large-scale molecular graphs.
- The physical interpretation of the method, particularly its connection to Ewald summation, is not adequately explained, which could limit the understanding of its effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational efficiency and the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of its practical applicability to large-scale molecular graphs.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of graphs, such as social networks or road networks?
- Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability for large-scale molecular graphs?
- How does the method perform when compared to more recent and relevant baselines, such as the latest versions of GCN and GINE?
- Could you elaborate on the physical interpretation of the method, particularly how it connects to Ewald summation and how this connection enhances the effectiveness of the method?
- How does the method handle the issue of over-squashing in GNNs, which is a known challenge in capturing long-range interactions?
- Could you provide more details on the grouping strategy used in the method, particularly how it is determined and how it affects the performance of the model?
- How does the method handle the issue of computational efficiency, especially when compared to other methods like SAN+LapPE?
