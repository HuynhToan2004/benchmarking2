 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel tabular oversampling framework, LITO, which leverages the capabilities of generative language models to synthesize minority samples. This framework involves progressively masking important features of majority class samples and imputing them towards the minority distribution. To ensure the quality of the synthesized samples, the authors employ a self-authentication mechanism where the language model predicts the labels of its own generated samples, filtering out ill-converted samples. The framework is tested across multiple datasets and imbalance ratios, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving classification performance. The methodology is innovative, utilizing language models to enhance tabular data, although concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach and the clarity of its presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is innovative, utilizing language models to enhance tabular data, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and imbalance ratios.
- The ablation study provides a detailed analysis of the method's components, enhancing the understanding of its functionality.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in machine learning, namely the handling of class imbalance in tabular data, which is a common issue in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the generative language model is trained on tabular data, which is crucial for understanding the methodology.
- There is a significant gap in the literature review, particularly in the discussion of related works, which could provide a richer context for the research.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing baselines, which limits the assessment of its novelty and effectiveness.
- The method's complexity and the need for fine-tuning the language model on specific datasets could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method with large datasets, which is a significant limitation for its broader application.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and logical flow, which could enhance its readability and understanding.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the training process of the generative language model on tabular data and how this impacts the performance of the oversampling process?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for handling class imbalance in tabular data, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the computational requirements of their method, including the time and resource costs associated with training and fine-tuning the language model?
- How does the method perform with different types of data, such as time-series data, and what adjustments, if any, are necessary for such applications?
- Could the authors elaborate on the scalability of their method with larger datasets and how they plan to address the challenges posed by the increasing complexity and size of the data?
