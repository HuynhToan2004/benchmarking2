 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of Goodhart's law in the context of reinforcement learning (RL), focusing on the impact of reward misspecification on the performance of RL agents. It proposes a method to quantify the magnitude of the Goodhart effect and provides theoretical insights into its occurrence in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs). The paper introduces an early stopping method to mitigate the Goodhart effect and presents empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of this approach. It also discusses the limitations and potential future directions of the research.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and accessible, making it easy to follow and understand the concepts presented.
- The authors provide a novel approach to quantifying the Goodhart effect, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, supported by empirical evidence, and offers a fresh perspective on the Goodhart effect in RL.
- The paper addresses a timely and important problem, focusing on the impact of reward misspecification in RL, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed early stopping method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the early stopping method, particularly in terms of its practical applicability and the assumptions it makes.
- The experimental setup and the choice of environments and reward functions are not thoroughly justified, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous theoretical analysis, particularly in terms of the assumptions made and the generalizability of the results to different types of MDPs.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and practicality of the early stopping method, which could be a significant barrier to implementation in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could be improved by including more diverse experimental settings and a broader range of reward functions to better reflect real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the early stopping method perform in more complex environments, such as those with larger state and action spaces?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and discuss how these assumptions might affect the applicability of the results?
- How does the paper address the computational complexity of the early stopping method, particularly in terms of the computational resources required?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the early stopping method and how these might impact its practical implementation?
- Is there a possibility to extend the analysis to more general classes of MDPs, such as those with continuous state and action spaces?
- How does the paper address the issue of reward misspecification, particularly in scenarios where the reward function is not perfectly aligned with the true objective?
