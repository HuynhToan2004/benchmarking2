 **Summary:**
The paper explores incentivized black-box model sharing, proposing a novel framework that includes a Weighted Ensemble Game (WEG) and a Fair Replication Game (FRG) to distribute rewards and payments based on the contribution of each party. The WEG utilizes Shapley values to measure contributions, while the FRG ensures fairness in payouts. Theoretical analysis and empirical evidence support the proposed methods, demonstrating their effectiveness in improving model performance and ensuring individual rationality. However, the paper's experimental setup and assumptions have been critiqued for their simplicity and potential lack of realism, suggesting that more complex and varied scenarios should be considered.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework for incentivized black-box model sharing, which is a significant contribution to the field of collaborative machine learning.
- Theoretical analysis is robust, providing a solid foundation for the proposed methods, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- Empirical evidence supports the effectiveness of the proposed methods, showing that they can improve model performance and ensure individual rationality.
- The paper is well-motivated, addressing the challenges of data sharing and providing a clear overview of related work.
- The methodology is innovative, combining ideas from cooperative game theory with machine learning, and the paper provides a closed-form solution for reward and payoff allocation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental setup is overly simplistic, assuming that all parties have the same training data and that the host has access to the true labeling function, which may not reflect real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and potential negative societal impacts.
- The paper does not adequately compare its methods with existing works, such as Rozemberczki & Sarkar (2021), which could provide a better context for the contributions of the current study.
- The paper does not address how the proposed methods would perform in scenarios with heterogeneous data or when different parties have different target domains, which are crucial for practical applications.
- The theoretical results, particularly the proofs and assumptions, are not thoroughly explained, which could hinder the understanding of the paper's contributions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the proposed methods could be adapted to scenarios with heterogeneous data or different target domains?
- How do the authors justify the assumptions made in their theoretical analysis, particularly regarding the generalization error and the availability of the true labeling function?
- Could the authors elaborate on the societal implications of their work, especially in terms of data privacy and security in black-box model sharing?
- How does the proposed framework compare with existing works in terms of incentivizing collaboration and ensuring fairness in model sharing?
- In light of the simplistic experimental setup, how do the authors plan to address more complex and varied scenarios in future work?
