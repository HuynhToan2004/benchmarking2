 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Branch-GAN, a novel approach to adversarial training for text generation using Transformer models. This method generates multiple branching sequences from each training sample, integrating both next-step prediction loss and adversarial loss, which enhances the model's ability to generate diverse and high-quality texts. The authors demonstrate that Branch-GAN outperforms traditional models in terms of text quality, as evidenced by both human evaluations and automatic metrics. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as similar methods have been explored in the past, and its practical applicability due to its computational complexity.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for training Transformers in a GAN setup, which effectively enhances text generation capabilities with fewer model parameters.
- The dataset used in the study is extensive and contains manual rankings of text quality, which is a valuable resource for future research.
- The experimental results show that Branch-GAN outperforms traditional LLMs in terms of text quality, as evidenced by both human evaluations and automatic metrics.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making the complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The method is applicable to sequential data in general, which could have broad applications.
- The authors explore automatic metrics that correlate well with human perceived text quality, providing a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as similar methods using GANs for text generation have been previously explored.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss or compare its method with existing approaches, such as the adversarial training of LLMs.
- The computational complexity of the method, which involves generating multiple branching sequences from each training sample, could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of exposure bias in language models, which is a significant limitation.
- The method's effectiveness might be limited by the model size, as the experiments primarily use small models, and it's unclear how the method would perform with larger models.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the method and does not compare its results with other methods in terms of automatic metrics like perplexity or TTR.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the main differences between their method and existing approaches, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and model performance?
- How does the method perform when applied to larger models, such as 13B or 70B models?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the method and discuss potential strategies for reducing it?
- In the context of the experiments, why was the repetition penalty used only for the fictional stories and not for the Wikipedia dataset?
- How does the method address the issue of exposure bias in language models, and what are the implications of this approach for the overall performance of the model?
