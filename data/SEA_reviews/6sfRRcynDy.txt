 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection using hyperspherical energy, a concept derived from the von Mises-Fisher distribution. This approach aims to address the limitations of unconstrained energy scores from models trained with cross-entropy loss, which may not reflect the true log-likelihood. The method is theoretically grounded and shows competitive performance on OOD detection benchmarks, demonstrating both theoretical and empirical advantages over existing methods. The paper is well-structured, clearly presented, and supported by rigorous mathematical derivations and empirical evaluations. It also discusses the limitations and potential future directions of the proposed method, including the need for further exploration of the temperature parameter and the potential impact of different training objectives on OOD detection performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical foundations.
- The novel use of von Mises-Fisher (vMF) distribution to model the hyperspherical representations is innovative and provides a new perspective on OOD detection.
- The method's computational efficiency is highlighted, making it faster than KNN-based methods and potentially more suitable for real-time applications.
- The paper provides a rigorous mathematical derivation of the hyperspherical energy, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in terms of OOD detection performance.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear presentation of the methodology, experimental setup, and results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the choice of temperature parameters and the potential impact of different training objectives on OOD detection performance.
- There is an absence of visualization of the learned representations, which could help in understanding the method's effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the differences between the proposed method and existing methods, such as CIDER, to clarify the novel contributions.
- The method's reliance on the assumption of a mixture of vMF distributions for OOD detection might not be robust enough, especially when the data distribution is more complex or varied.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant in safety-critical applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to CIDER in terms of computational efficiency and performance, especially when using similar training objectives?
- What is the effect of different temperature parameters on the OOD detection performance, and how does this impact the method's effectiveness?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations or visualizations of the learned representations to better understand the method's functionality?
- How does the proposed method handle OOD detection in more complex or varied data distributions, and what are the potential limitations in such scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed method in more detail, particularly in safety-critical applications?
- In the experimental setup, how were the temperature parameters chosen for different datasets, and what was the rationale behind these choices?
