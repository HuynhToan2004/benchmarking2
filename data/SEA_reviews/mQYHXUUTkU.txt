 **Summary:**
The paper introduces BrainSCUBA, a method for generating natural language captions that describe images predicted to maximally activate individual voxels in the brain. This method leverages a pre-trained large language model and a contrastive vision-language model to derive voxel-wise captions, which are then used to synthesize images that align with these captions. The method is validated through a fine-grained voxel-level captioning across higher-order visual regions, showing that the generated captions are semantically coherent and yield high predicted activations. The paper also demonstrates the utility of BrainSCUBA in uncovering fine-grained semantic selectivity in body-selective areas, suggesting potential applications in neuroscience research.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to generating natural language captions for images predicted to maximally activate individual voxels in the brain, leveraging a pre-trained large language model and a contrastive vision-language model.
- The method is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the experimental setup and results.
- The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of the method's effectiveness, including quantitative measures of image similarity and qualitative assessments by human evaluators.
- The use of a data-driven approach for generating voxel-wise captions and synthesizing images from these captions is innovative and could have significant implications for neuroscience research.
- The method is validated through a fine-grained voxel-level captioning across higher-order visual regions, demonstrating its effectiveness in capturing the semantics of voxel-wise brain activation.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the generated captions and their alignment with pre-identified functional regions, which is crucial for understanding the neural mechanisms underlying visual processing.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, including potential biases in the language model and the generalizability of the results across different populations or stimulus sets.
- The method's reliance on a pre-trained language model and a contrastive vision-language model may limit its applicability to other brain regions or stimulus sets that are not well-represented in these models.
- There is a lack of comparison with other methods, such as BrainDiVE, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential impact of the method on neuroscience research, particularly in terms of its practical utility and the ethical implications of using deep learning models to interpret brain activity.
- The method's application is limited to specific brain regions or stimulus sets, which may limit its generalizability to other areas of neuroscience research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role of the contrastive vision-language model in the method and its impact on the results?
- How does the method handle the potential biases in the language model, and what steps have been taken to mitigate these biases?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process for the fMRI encoder and the linear probe, including the specifics of the loss function and training parameters?
- How does the method perform when applied to different brain regions or stimulus sets that are not well-represented in the pre-trained models?
- Can the authors discuss the potential implications of the method for neuroscience research, particularly in terms of its practical utility and the ethical considerations involved in using deep learning models to interpret brain activity?
