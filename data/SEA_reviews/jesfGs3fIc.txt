 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for encoding neural network weights into a compact representation, termed Set-based Neural Network Encoder (SNE), which can predict the generalization performance of neural networks based on their weights alone. This method leverages set-to-set and set-to-vector functions to encode neural network parameters, enabling the prediction of performance across different datasets and architectures. The authors demonstrate that SNE outperforms existing baselines on cross-dataset tasks and provide the first results on cross-architecture tasks. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty and the lack of comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach for encoding neural network weights into a compact representation, which is capable of encoding neural networks of arbitrary architecture.
- The authors propose two new tasks: cross-dataset and cross-architecture neural network performance prediction, which are significant contributions to the field.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the set-to-set and set-to-vector functions used, and the experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the proposed method is straightforward and intuitive, with a focus on encoding neural network weights without the need for specific architectural knowledge.
- The introduction of a new task, cross-architecture neural network performance prediction, is a valuable addition to the field, and the paper provides the first set of results on this task.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in the cross-dataset task where it only compares with two baselines.
- There is a lack of clarity in the motivation for the proposed method, particularly in explaining why the method is more effective than existing approaches.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to other types of neural networks like recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and transformers.
- The paper does not provide a detailed explanation of the set-to-set and set-to-vector functions used, which are crucial components of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with the paper not reporting standard deviations or statistical significance tests, which could undermine the reliability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of memory and computational requirements.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the motivation behind the proposed method and how it addresses the limitations of existing approaches?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the set-to-set and set-to-vector functions used in the method, including their specific implementations and theoretical underpinnings?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of neural networks, such as RNNs and transformers?
- Can the authors elaborate on the experimental setup, particularly in terms of the choice of model zoos and the hyperparameter tuning process?
- How does the proposed method handle the variability in neural network weights, especially in terms of its ability to generalize across different datasets and architectures?
