 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the mechanisms behind factual recall in large language models (LLMs) by employing mechanistic interpretability techniques such as Direct Logit Attribution (DLA) to attribute logits to individual tokens. The authors identify distinct mechanisms, including subject heads, relation heads, mixed heads, and MLPs, which work together additively to produce correct answers. These mechanisms are analyzed through a dataset specifically designed for factual recall, showing how they constructively interfere to produce accurate responses. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for understanding how LLMs store and retrieve knowledge.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear structure and well-organized presentation.
- The authors have made significant efforts to disentangle the contributions of different model components, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The analysis is comprehensive and covers a wide range of model components, including attention heads, MLPs, and residual connections, providing a detailed understanding of how these components interact to produce factual recall.
- The paper provides a novel approach to studying factual recall by focusing on the interaction between different model components, which is a departure from previous studies that focused on individual components.
- The dataset created for this study is specifically designed for factual recall, which is a valuable resource for future research.
- The findings are robust and provide a deeper understanding of how LLMs store and retrieve knowledge, which is crucial for advancing the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's methodology and dataset are limited in scope, focusing only on a specific model (Pythia-2.8b) and a narrow set of relationships, which may limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of the study, which could affect the credibility of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the role of the prefix in factual recall, which is crucial for understanding the overall process.
- There is a lack of quantitative analysis in the results section, which could strengthen the paper's claims.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work and a clearer delineation of the novel contributions.
- The paper's claims about the additive nature of model components and their constructive interference are not convincingly supported by the data presented.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definitions and roles of different model components, such as "subject heads," "relation heads," and "mixed heads"?
- How do the authors justify the claim that the model components work additively, and can they provide more quantitative evidence to support this claim?
- Could the authors elaborate on the role of the prefix in factual recall and how it interacts with the subject and relation in the prompt?
- How do the findings of this study relate to other forms of knowledge, such as commonsense knowledge or procedural knowledge?
- Can the authors discuss the implications of their findings for the design and training of LLMs, particularly in terms of improving factual recall and knowledge retrieval?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of their methodology and dataset, and what steps could be taken to address these limitations in future research?
