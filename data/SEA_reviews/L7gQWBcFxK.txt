 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel importance sampling strategy for stochastic gradient descent (SGD) in machine learning, leveraging the loss gradient of the output layer to estimate importance scores. This approach aims to improve the convergence rate of SGD by focusing on data points that contribute significantly to the loss. The method is validated across various datasets, including image and point cloud datasets, and is shown to outperform uniform sampling and other importance sampling methods. The paper also discusses the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is critical for practical applications. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, as similar methods have been previously proposed, and the empirical evidence provided does not convincingly demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations and a logical structure that facilitates understanding.
- The proposed method is simple and intuitive, making it accessible and potentially easy to implement in practical scenarios.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and tasks, showing improvements over existing methods.
- The use of loss gradients to compute importance scores is a novel approach that has been validated through experiments, showing the method's effectiveness in improving convergence rates and reducing computational overhead.
- The paper provides a clear comparison of the proposed method with existing ones, highlighting its advantages in terms of computational efficiency and practical implementation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of loss functions and its impact on convergence rates.
- There is a lack of novelty in the methodology, as similar approaches have been previously proposed, which questions the originality of the work.
- The empirical evidence provided does not convincingly demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods, with some results showing only marginal improvements or even inferior performance in certain scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational overhead of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical applications where computational resources are limited.
- There are several typographical and grammatical errors throughout the paper, which detract from its professional presentation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of loss functions and its impact on convergence rates?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational overhead to existing methods, especially in practical applications where computational resources are limited?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including standard deviations and additional datasets, to better demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- In light of the concerns regarding the novelty of the approach, how does the paper distinguish its contributions from those of previous works, particularly in terms of methodological innovations and empirical validations?
- The paper mentions using autograd for gradient computation in some experiments. Could the authors explain how this affects the computational efficiency and the overall effectiveness of the method?
