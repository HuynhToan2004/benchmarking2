 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GR-1, a GPT-style model for visual robot manipulation, trained on the Ego4D dataset and fine-tuned on robot datasets. GR-1 predicts robot actions and future images in an end-to-end manner, demonstrating strong performance on the CALVIN benchmark and in real-world scenarios. The model leverages a large-scale video dataset for pre-training, which enhances its ability to generalize across various tasks and environments. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty and lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods. Concerns were also raised about the clarity of the experimental setup and the potential for overfitting due to the use of large-scale datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with comprehensive experiments and thorough ablation studies.
- The proposed method, GR-1, demonstrates strong performance in both simulation and real-world scenarios, outperforming existing methods in zero-shot generalization and data efficiency.
- The use of a large-scale video dataset for pre-training enhances the model's ability to generalize across various tasks and environments, and the incorporation of video prediction as a regularizer improves the model's performance.
- The model's architecture is simple and effective, with the ability to generate both actions and future images in an end-to-end manner, which is a significant advantage for robot manipulation tasks.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the experimental setup and results, which is crucial for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of data efficiency and zero-shot generalization capabilities.
- The novelty of the approach is limited as similar models have been proposed in the past, and the technical contributions are not clearly differentiated from existing works.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could be crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experimental setup and results are not clearly presented, with some results and methodologies relegated to the appendix, which could hinder the comprehension of the paper.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential for overfitting due to the use of large-scale datasets, which could limit the generalizability of the model.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the model's generalizability and its ability to handle different types of robot manipulation tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly the differences between the results presented in the appendix and those in the main text?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of data efficiency and zero-shot generalization capabilities?
- Can you provide more details on the model's architecture, particularly how the video prediction signal guides action predictions and how the model handles different types of robot manipulation tasks?
- How do you address the potential for overfitting due to the use of large-scale datasets, and what measures have been taken to prevent this?
- Could you discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its applicability in real-world scenarios?
