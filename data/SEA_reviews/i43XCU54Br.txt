 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel dynamic collaboration framework, Dynamic LLM-Agent Network (DyLAN), designed to enhance the performance and efficiency of Large Language Model (LLM) agents in complex tasks like reasoning and code generation. DyLAN employs an automatic agent team optimization algorithm based on an unsupervised metric called Agent Importance Score, enabling the selection of optimal agents based on their contributions. The framework allows for multiple rounds of agent interactions and inference-time agent selection, with an early-stopping mechanism to improve performance and efficiency. DyLAN demonstrates significant improvements in accuracy and efficiency compared to baseline methods across various benchmarks. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation scope, lack of comprehensive ablation studies, and unclear implementation details.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel dynamic collaboration framework, Dynamic LLM-Agent Network (DyLAN), which enables agents to interact for multiple rounds in a dynamic architecture, enhancing performance and efficiency.
- DyLAN utilizes an automatic agent team optimization algorithm based on an unsupervised metric termed Agent Importance Score, which effectively selects the best agents based on their contributions.
- The framework is task-agnostic, allowing for fast adaptation to new situations, and incorporates an inference-time agent selection and early-stopping mechanism, enhancing efficiency.
- DyLAN demonstrates high accuracy, efficiency, and stability in general reasoning, arithmetic reasoning, and code generation tasks, with significant improvements over baseline methods.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experiments, which validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation of DyLAN is limited to a few benchmarks, and the paper lacks comprehensive ablation studies, which could provide a deeper understanding of the framework's components and their impact on performance.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There are unclear implementation details, particularly concerning the role of the "LLM Ranker" and the definition of the Agent Importance Score, which could affect the reproducibility and understanding of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the computational cost and a comparison of the performance of DyLAN with different LLM backbones to better understand its scalability and effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for overfitting or the impact of hyperparameter choices on performance.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the implementation of the "LLM Ranker" and how it contributes to the overall performance of DyLAN?
- How is the Agent Importance Score defined and calculated, and what are the implications of this calculation on the performance and interpretability of the results?
- What are the specific roles and contributions of the agents in DyLAN, and how are they selected and optimized?
- How does DyLAN handle scenarios where agents may provide contradictory or misleading information, and what measures are in place to ensure the accuracy and reliability of the outputs?
- Could you elaborate on the computational cost of DyLAN and how it compares to other methods in terms of efficiency and scalability?
- How does the performance of DyLAN vary with different LLM backbones, and what are the implications of this for the generalizability and applicability of the framework?
