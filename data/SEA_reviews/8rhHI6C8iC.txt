 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework named Hot-Pluggable Federated Learning (HPFL) to address the Selective Federated Learning (SFL) problem, where clients encounter unseen test data distributions. HPFL decomposes the model into a global shared feature extractor and multiple personalized plug-in modules, enabling clients to select and download suitable plug-ins for inference. The framework is evaluated against various baselines, showing promising results. However, concerns about the novelty and clarity of the contributions, the practicality of the proposed methods, and the robustness of the experimental results are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and understandable content, and includes comprehensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework.
- The proposed framework is simple and intuitive, and the authors provide a clear motivation for the proposed Selective Federated Learning (SFL) problem.
- The paper includes a detailed literature review and extensive experiments on various federated learning datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The theoretical analysis provided in the paper is solid, and the paper is easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed framework has the potential to address practical issues in federated learning, such as the generalization of personalized models to unseen test data distributions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and detailed explanation of the contributions, making it difficult to understand the novelty and significance of the proposed method.
- The selection process of plug-ins is not well-explained, and the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of the number of clients and the size of the model.
- There is a lack of discussion on the privacy implications of sharing plug-ins and auxiliary information, which is crucial for the practical implementation of the proposed framework.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing methods, particularly in the area of personalized federated learning.
- The experimental setup and results are not robust enough, with inconsistencies in the performance of the proposed method across different datasets and configurations.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the contributions of their work and provide a detailed explanation of how the proposed method addresses the Selective Federated Learning (SFL) problem?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, particularly in terms of the number of clients and the size of the model?
- What are the specific privacy risks associated with sharing plug-ins and auxiliary information, and how does the proposed method mitigate these risks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the selection process of plug-ins and how it impacts the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- What are the implications of the experimental results, particularly the inconsistencies in performance across different datasets and configurations?
