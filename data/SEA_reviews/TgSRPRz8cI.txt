 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Patch-DM, for generating high-resolution images using a denoising diffusion model. The method involves training on small image patches and synthesizing larger images by combining features from neighboring patches, thereby avoiding boundary artifacts. This approach is validated through experiments on various datasets, including LSUN-Bedroom and LSUN-Church, demonstrating superior performance over existing methods. The paper also explores the use of global conditions, positional embeddings, and collage in pixel space to enhance the model's performance. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its lack of novelty in the feature collage strategy and the limited comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant issue of boundary artifacts in patch-based image synthesis, proposing a novel feature collage strategy that effectively alleviates these artifacts.
- The method is straightforward, easy to follow, and the results demonstrate its effectiveness, showing high-quality generated images.
- The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in reducing artifacts and improving image quality.
- The method's simplicity and the ability to generate high-resolution images using a smaller model size make it a promising approach for practical applications.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to understand, and includes visual results that are compelling, showcasing the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of model size, training time, and inference speed.
- The novelty of the feature collage strategy is questionable, as similar strategies have been previously explored in other works, such as those mentioned in the review.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include issues like the inability to handle complex global structures or the potential for inconsistencies in generated images.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to specific datasets and does not include comparisons with a broader range of methods, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's performance.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of images, such as those with complex global structures or varying image styles?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and inference time for the proposed method, especially compared to other state-of-the-art methods?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle different image resolutions or sizes without significant modifications?
- How does the method handle the inconsistencies that might arise when generating images from different patches, especially when these patches are not directly adjacent?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other methods, particularly those that use similar strategies or have comparable performance metrics?
