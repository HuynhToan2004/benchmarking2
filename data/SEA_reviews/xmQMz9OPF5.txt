 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of random initialization as a teacher model in masked knowledge distillation (MKD) for self-supervised visual representation learning. The authors propose a multi-stage masked distillation pipeline that utilizes a randomly initialized model as the teacher, which is updated at breakpoints during training. This approach is evaluated across various downstream tasks including classification, transfer learning, object detection, and semantic segmentation, showing competitive performance with existing methods. The paper also investigates the effects of different teacher models on student performance and the impact of random initialization on model training.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to masked knowledge distillation using a randomly initialized teacher model, which is a significant deviation from traditional methods that use pre-trained models as teachers.
- The proposed method is shown to be effective in improving the performance of self-supervised visual representation learning, particularly in downstream tasks such as classification, transfer learning, object detection, and semantic segmentation.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a thorough analysis of the effects of different teacher models on student performance and the impact of random initialization on model training.
- The findings challenge the common belief that the choice of the teacher model is crucial for learning good visual representations, suggesting that the design of the target representation may not be essential for effective visual representation learning.
- The experiments are extensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can achieve comparable or even superior performance to existing methods without the need for a pre-trained teacher model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of training time and resource requirements compared to other methods.
- There is a noticeable absence of comparisons with other self-supervised methods, particularly those that use similar random initialization strategies, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations or challenges associated with using a randomly initialized teacher model, such as the potential for lower performance in early stages of training or the impact on model generalization.
- The results reported in the paper are not always consistent with those reported in other studies, which could affect the credibility of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer delineation of its contributions to the field of self-supervised learning.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other self-supervised methods, particularly those that use similar random initialization strategies?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and scalability to other methods, especially in large-scale applications?
- Can the authors elaborate on the potential limitations or challenges associated with using a randomly initialized teacher model, and how these were addressed in the research?
- Given the findings that a random teacher model can be effective, how does this compare to the effectiveness of other self-supervised methods that use different strategies for teacher model selection?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of model generalization, especially when applied to new or different datasets?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies between the results reported in their paper and those reported in other studies, particularly in terms of the methodology and experimental setups used?
