 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel metric, Gradient Subspace Distance (GSD), aimed at measuring the utility of public datasets for private machine learning tasks. This metric quantifies the distance between the gradient subspaces of private and public datasets, which is empirically shown to be correlated with the model's utility when one dataset acts as private and the other as public. The GSD metric is used to select the most suitable public dataset for a given private task, demonstrating its effectiveness in both transfer learning and pre-conditioning settings. Theoretical analysis and empirical results show that the GSD metric can predict the utility of public datasets, providing a practical tool for dataset selection in differentially private machine learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel metric, Gradient Subspace Distance (GSD), which is a simple and effective tool for selecting public datasets in both transfer learning and pre-conditioning settings.
- The GSD metric is well-correlated with model utility, as demonstrated through empirical results that show a monotonic increase in utility as the GSD decreases.
- The theoretical analysis provided demonstrates that the excess risk of Gradient Embedding Perturbation (GEP) scales with the GSD, further validating the utility of this metric.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed metric is simple to compute and does not require extensive computational resources, making it practical for use in real-world scenarios.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis that supports the utility of the GSD metric, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art methods for dataset selection, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the GSD metric.
- The experimental setup is limited to smaller datasets and models, which may not fully demonstrate the scalability or effectiveness of the GSD metric in more complex or larger-scale scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational efficiency and privacy implications of the GSD metric, which could be crucial for practical implementation in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of public datasets and their impact on the selection process, as well as a more thorough comparison with other methods for dataset selection.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and usage of the "probe network" mentioned in the paper?
- How does the GSD metric compare to other state-of-the-art methods for dataset selection, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and privacy implications?
- Is there a possibility to extend the GSD metric to other types of private machine learning tasks, such as federated learning or differential privacy in deep learning?
- How does the GSD metric perform in scenarios where the private and public datasets have significant distribution shifts?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency and privacy implications of the GSD metric, particularly in terms of its scalability and practical implementation in real-world scenarios?
