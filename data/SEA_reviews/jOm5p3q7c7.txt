 **Summary:**
The paper explores the sample complexity of learning optimal policies for Average Reward Markov Decision Processes (AMDPs) under the uniformly ergodic assumption. It employs a reduction to a Discounted MDP (DMDP) and a variance-reduced algorithm to achieve optimal sample complexity, which is matched with the lower bound. The authors demonstrate that their algorithm achieves the optimal dependence on the mixing time, improving upon previous works that relied on worst-case bounds. The theoretical analysis is supported by numerical experiments, although the practical applicability of the uniform ergodicity assumption is questioned due to its rarity in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem in reinforcement learning (RL) by focusing on the sample complexity of learning optimal policies for Average Reward Markov Decision Processes (AMDPs).
- The authors provide a novel approach by combining existing techniques in a new and insightful way, achieving optimal sample complexity for uniformly ergodic AMDPs.
- The theoretical analysis is supported by numerical experiments, which demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear introduction of the problem and its relevance to the field.
- The authors provide a thorough literature review, positioning their work within the existing body of knowledge and highlighting its contributions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's reliance on the uniform ergodicity assumption, which is not commonly encountered in real-world scenarios, limits the practical applicability of the results.
- The paper's contribution appears incremental, primarily relying on the application of existing techniques rather than introducing new methods or insights.
- There are concerns about the novelty of the work, as it seems to be a straightforward combination of existing ideas without significant new contributions.
- The paper's theoretical analysis and numerical experiments are limited to the tabular setting, which may not generalize well to more complex settings like function approximation or general state-space MDPs.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical challenges of estimating the mixing time in real-world applications, which could be a significant barrier to the implementation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the practical challenges and potential solutions for estimating the mixing time in real-world applications?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- In the context of the numerical experiments, can the authors clarify the choice of parameters and the experimental setup to ensure that the results are robust and generalizable?
- Could the authors elaborate on the novelty of their work beyond the straightforward application of existing techniques?
- Is there a plan to extend the theoretical analysis and numerical experiments to more complex settings, such as function approximation or general state-space MDPs?
