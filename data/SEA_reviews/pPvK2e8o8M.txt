 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to enhance large language models (LLMs) by training them to assess their own competence in generating factual answers. This is achieved by fine-tuning models on diverse datasets, using a meta-cognitive error average as the loss function for gradient updates. The methodology involves training a separate LoRA model to evaluate the model's responses, which is then used to train the main model. The paper claims that this approach helps reduce hallucination in text generation by improving the models' ability to self-assess. However, the paper's experimental setup and the clarity of its methodology have been critiqued, with concerns about the generalizability of the findings and the lack of comprehensive baseline comparisons.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach to mitigate hallucination in text generation by training models to assess their own competence, which is a novel application of meta-cognition in language models.
- The methodology is clearly presented, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed algorithm.
- The paper includes a comprehensive dataset of 16,000 questions across 160 domains, which can be useful for training and evaluating large language models.
- The authors have demonstrated that their approach can effectively reduce hallucination in text generation, as shown in the experiments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of efficiency and effectiveness. It would benefit from a more detailed discussion on how its approach compares to other methods like BARTScore, GPTScore, and SelfCheckGPT.
- The experimental setup is not sufficiently robust, with limited comparisons and a lack of discussion on the statistical significance of the results.
- There are issues with clarity and organization in the paper, particularly in the explanation of the methodology and the experimental setup.
- The paper's focus on factual questions and answers might limit the generalizability of the findings to other types of questions or responses.
- The method's reliance on a separate LoRA model for evaluation raises questions about its scalability and the potential for overfitting to specific models.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the meta-cognitive ability of the model is evaluated, especially in terms of its adaptability to different fine-tuned versions of the main model?
- How does the paper address the potential for overfitting, especially since the method involves training a separate LoRA model for evaluation?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and evaluation process, including the number of iterations and the specifics of the experimental setup?
- In light of the paper's claims about reducing hallucination, how does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- The paper mentions a potential for the method to be adapted to different models. Can the authors elaborate on how this would be achieved and what implications it might have for the scalability and generalizability of the findings?
