 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to fairness in graph neural networks (GNNs) by addressing both group and individual fairness. It proposes a new metric for individual fairness within groups and utilizes adversarial learning to ensure group fairness. The methodology includes a loss function that balances individual fairness within groups and group fairness, which is evaluated on various datasets. The paper claims that the proposed method outperforms existing models in terms of fairness metrics while maintaining prediction accuracy. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in definitions and theorems, insufficient experimental validation, and potential issues with the novelty and practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant issue in graph learning by focusing on both group and individual fairness, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The introduction of a new metric for individual fairness within groups is a valuable contribution to the research, providing a new perspective on fairness in graph learning.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed methodology and the experimental setup.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, covering a variety of datasets and metrics, and the results showcase the effectiveness of the proposed method in achieving fairness while maintaining prediction accuracy.
- The paper introduces a new loss function that balances individual fairness within groups and group fairness, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in definitions and theorems, particularly in the definitions of group fairness and theorems related to the new loss function.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implications of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where sensitive attributes are not available.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, as shown in Table 1, where the proposed method's performance is either comparable or worse in terms of accuracy.
- The paper's approach to fairness is somewhat limited by the assumptions made, such as the need for sensitive attributes to be available and the use of binary sensitive attributes, which may not reflect real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods and a more thorough analysis of the results, particularly in terms of the trade-offs between fairness and accuracy.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definitions of group fairness and the theorems related to the new loss function, particularly in the context of the paper's methodology?
- How do you address the limitations of the proposed method, especially in scenarios where sensitive attributes are not available or where the assumptions about the sensitive attributes do not hold?
- Can you provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the experimental setup and the metrics used?
- How do you ensure that the proposed method is effective in scenarios where the sensitive attributes are not binary, and how does this affect the fairness metrics used?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of datasets and the rationale behind the experimental design?
- What are the practical implications of the proposed method, and how does it perform in real-world scenarios where fairness is a critical concern?
