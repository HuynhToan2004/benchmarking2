 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for calibrating generative Large Language Models (LLMs) using a Pareto optimal self-supervision approach. This method leverages available programmatic supervision to systematically calibrate LLM responses, producing a risk score for each response without additional manual effort. The framework is evaluated on standard relation extraction and classification tasks in biomedical and general domains, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods. The paper also proposes a dynamic prompting strategy that enhances GPT-4's performance, surpassing state-of-the-art supervised models without human-labeled training data. The approach is based on multi-objective optimization, aiming to address the weighting dilemma in traditional weak supervision methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using Pareto optimal self-supervision to calibrate LLM responses, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method is evaluated on standard relation extraction and classification tasks in biomedical and general domains, demonstrating its effectiveness and applicability.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methodologies and theoretical underpinnings.
- The empirical results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in terms of error calibration, as demonstrated by a significant improvement in GPT-4's performance.
- The paper is innovative in its use of Pareto optimal learning to address the weighting dilemma in weak supervision, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the calibration performance and computational efficiency of the proposed method.
- The evaluation of the method's effectiveness is limited to specific tasks and domains, raising questions about its applicability and effectiveness in more complex or diverse scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant given the widespread use of LLMs in various applications.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of some sections, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key terms and concepts.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its methodology, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing methods, including a discussion on the calibration performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex or diverse scenarios, such as those involving multiple LLMs or different domains?
- Can the authors discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed method and how these impacts could be mitigated?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and explanations of key terms and concepts, such as the Pareto optimal harmonizer and the POLAR score?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting or bias in the LLM responses, especially in scenarios where the supervision functions may not be entirely reliable?
