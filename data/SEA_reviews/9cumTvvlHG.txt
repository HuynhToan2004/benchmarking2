 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an innovative approach to implicit chain-of-thought reasoning in language models, focusing on the vertical reasoning of hidden states in different layers, rather than the horizontal generation of intermediate tokens. This method involves training a student model to predict the final answer based on the hidden states of a teacher model, which has been trained on explicit chain-of-thought reasoning. The paper proposes a three-step process: mind-reading the teacher, thought emulation, and coupling and optimization, to enhance the student model's ability to reason implicitly. Experiments on multi-digit multiplication tasks and a grade school math problem dataset demonstrate that this approach can outperform baselines that directly produce the answer. However, the paper's focus on simple mathematical tasks and its lack of broader experimentation on more complex, natural language tasks limit its generalizability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to implicit chain-of-thought reasoning, which is a significant departure from traditional methods of reasoning in language models.
- The proposed method shows promise in improving the performance of language models, particularly in tasks that require multi-step reasoning, such as arithmetic operations.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method can outperform baselines that directly produce the answer, indicating its effectiveness.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of language models, specifically focusing on the reasoning ability of language models, which is crucial for advancing the capabilities of these models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the generalizability of the proposed method beyond simple mathematical tasks. It would be beneficial to explore how the method performs on more complex, natural language tasks.
- The paper does not include a detailed comparison with other implicit reasoning methods, which could provide a better understanding of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on simple arithmetic tasks, which might not adequately demonstrate the method's effectiveness in more complex scenarios.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the explanation of certain technical details and the motivation behind certain design choices.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential societal impact of the proposed method, which could be significant if the method is adopted by large language models.

**Questions:**
