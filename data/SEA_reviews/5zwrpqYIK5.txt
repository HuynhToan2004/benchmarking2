 **Summary:**
The paper explores the outlier-robust orthogonal regression problem on manifolds, focusing on the estimation of essential matrices in computer vision. It proposes two algorithms, Riemannian subgradient method (RSGM) and iteratively reweighted least squares (IRLS), to address this problem. The RSGM is designed for manifolds with a retraction map, while IRLS is suitable for manifolds where the weighted least squares problem can be easily solved. The paper provides theoretical analysis on the convergence and global minimizers of these algorithms, supported by numerical experiments that demonstrate their robustness against outliers. The manuscript also discusses the practical implications of these algorithms in real-world scenarios, albeit with some limitations in terms of computational complexity and the scope of manifold applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in computer vision, specifically the estimation of essential matrices, which is crucial for applications such as 3D reconstruction and augmented reality.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis on the convergence and global minimizers of their proposed algorithms, enhancing the paper's credibility and depth.
- The manuscript is well-written, with clear explanations of the algorithms and theoretical results, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed algorithms are novel and potentially useful for other outlier-robust estimation problems, offering a theoretical framework for understanding the behavior of these algorithms.
- The paper includes numerical experiments that demonstrate the robustness of the proposed algorithms against outliers, providing empirical evidence of their effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, particularly in relation to the size of the dataset and the computational resources required.
- The scope of manifold applications is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on the essential manifold, which might not be representative of other types of manifolds commonly used in computer vision.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its methods with existing works, such as those that address similar problems using different methodologies.
- The paper could be more explicit in its writing, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key terms and assumptions, to enhance clarity and accessibility.
- The paper's claims about the practicality and computational efficiency of the proposed algorithms are not convincingly supported by empirical evidence, which could undermine the credibility of these claims.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of their algorithms, especially in relation to the size of the dataset and the computational resources required?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the essential manifold as the primary focus of their research, and what implications does this have for the generalizability of their findings to other types of manifolds?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the retraction map in Algorithm 1 and its impact on the computational efficiency and accuracy of the algorithm?
- In light of the limitations discussed in the paper, what are the authors' plans for future research to address these issues and expand the scope of their findings?
- How do the authors envision the application of their algorithms in real-world scenarios, and what are the practical implications of their work for the field of computer vision?
