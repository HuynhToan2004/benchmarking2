 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Residual Factorized Fourier Neural Operator (Res-F-FNO) to enhance the prediction of 3D turbulent flows using the Factorized Fourier Neural Operator (F-FNO). The key innovation is the incorporation of residual connections to the F-FNO architecture, which allows for the simulation of small-scale dynamic flows and the reduction of prediction errors. The paper also proposes a skip-corrector to address the accumulation of errors over multiple time steps, and a new training methodology that samples random time steps as initial conditions to improve training efficiency. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on a dataset of turbulent flow around a cube, showing improvements over the baseline F-FNO.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces the Residual Factorized Fourier Neural Operator (Res-F-FNO), which effectively reintroduces small-scale dynamic flows in 3D flow simulations, improving the accuracy of wind field modeling.
- The proposed skip-corrector is innovative and helps in reducing the accumulation of errors over multiple time steps, enhancing the prediction accuracy.
- The training strategy of randomly selecting time steps as initial conditions is a novel approach that reduces the number of training iterations required for convergence.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the proposed methods, including the use of figures and mathematical formulations to illustrate the proposed methods.
- The proposed methods are evaluated on a dataset of turbulent flow around a cube, showing improvements over the baseline F-FNO in both training efficiency and prediction accuracy.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's motivation for the research is not clearly articulated, and the problem addressed seems somewhat artificial and limited to the specific dataset used.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and does not compare the proposed methods with other existing approaches, such as using different loss functions or regularization techniques.
- The experiments are limited to a single dataset, which may not generalize well to other datasets or real-world applications.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training hyperparameters, such as the learning rate and batch size, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The paper's writing quality could be improved, with several typos and unclear sentences that need correction.
- The paper does not discuss the potential limitations of the proposed methods, such as their applicability to different types of PDEs or the scalability of the proposed techniques to larger or more complex datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the motivation behind the research and the specific problem addressed by the proposed methods?
- How do the proposed methods compare to other existing approaches, such as using different loss functions or regularization techniques?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training hyperparameters and their impact on the results?
- How do the proposed methods perform on other datasets, and can they be scaled to larger or more complex datasets?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of their proposed methods and how they might be addressed in future work?
- How does the skip-corrector affect the training process, and what are the implications of using different skip intervals?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and testing loss curves, and how do they compare across different models and training conditions?
