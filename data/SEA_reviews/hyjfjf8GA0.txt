 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the behavioral changes in a heterogeneous population of agents in competitive games, focusing on the overgeneralization problem in current models like AlphaStar and NeuPL. The authors propose a novel method called Joint Entropy Minimization (JEM) to address this issue by allowing each agent to learn individualized policies, reducing the similarity of their action distributions to the average behavior of the population. This approach aims to enhance the diversity of strategies and improve the performance of specialists, thereby reducing the performance disparity among agents. The paper provides empirical evidence through experiments in the Naruto Mobile game, demonstrating the effectiveness of JEM in promoting specialization and reducing behavioral similarity.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely question about how to model heterogeneous populations in multi-agent learning, focusing on the overgeneralization problem in current models.
- The proposed method, Joint Entropy Minimization (JEM), is well-motivated and theoretically grounded, showing potential for improving performance and reducing performance disparity.
- The empirical results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of JEM in promoting specialization and reducing behavioral similarity.
- The paper is well-written, making the ideas and experiments clear and easy to understand, and provides a thorough analysis of the behavioral changes in the population.
- The authors have conducted a comprehensive evaluation of their method, including detailed analysis of the results, which supports the claims made in the paper.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other methods that aim to reduce overgeneralization, such as those mentioned in related work, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the effectiveness of JEM.
- There is a significant increase in training time due to the need for individualized neural nets for each agent, which could limit the scalability of the method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup, including the specifics of the Naruto Mobile game and the training parameters used.
- The writing style of the paper needs improvement, particularly in terms of clarity and consistency in notation and definitions.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of JEM, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its applicability in different scenarios, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other methods that aim to reduce overgeneralization, such as those mentioned in related work?
- How does the computational cost of JEM compare to other methods, and what are the implications for scalability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the Naruto Mobile game and the training parameters used?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how do these affect the generalizability of the findings?
- How does the proposed method perform in different scenarios, such as games with different numbers of players or different types of actions?
- Can the authors clarify the notations and definitions used in the paper to improve readability and understanding?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency in different scenarios?
