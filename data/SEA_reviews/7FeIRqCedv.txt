 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SLiMe, a novel method for few-shot semantic segmentation that leverages text-conditioned Stable Diffusion models. The approach optimizes text embeddings to enhance segmentation accuracy, using a weighted accumulated self-attention map and cross-attention map to improve segmentation results. SLiMe demonstrates its effectiveness through extensive experiments on various datasets, showcasing its ability to outperform existing methods. Despite its strengths, the paper has some limitations, such as the requirement for high-quality segmentation masks and the potential for noisy segmentations in specific scenarios. The methodology, while innovative, is not without its challenges, particularly in handling tiny objects and ensuring consistent segmentation across different images.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and intuitive figures that aid in understanding the proposed method.
- The approach is novel and innovative, using text-conditioned Stable Diffusion models to optimize text embeddings for improved segmentation accuracy.
- Extensive experiments are conducted, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets, including PASCAL-Part, CelebAMask-HQ, and the ADE20K dataset.
- The paper is well-organized and provides a comprehensive analysis of the method's performance, including a detailed discussion of the limitations and potential future directions.
- The use of weighted accumulated self-attention maps and cross-attention maps is a significant contribution to the field of few-shot semantic segmentation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in handling tiny objects and ensuring consistent segmentation across different images.
- The requirement for high-quality segmentation masks may limit the applicability of the method in real-world scenarios where such masks are not readily available.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost and efficiency of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The method's performance in handling occluded objects or scenes with complex backgrounds is not thoroughly evaluated, which could limit its applicability in certain scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation on a broader range of datasets and scenarios, including real-world applications and more diverse object categories.
- The paper does not provide sufficient comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, which could help in better understanding the relative performance of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost and efficiency of the proposed method, including the training and inference times?
- How does the proposed method handle occluded objects or scenes with complex backgrounds? Can the authors provide examples or additional evaluations on such scenarios?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in handling tiny objects and ensuring consistent segmentation across different images?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational cost, efficiency, and segmentation accuracy?
- Can the authors provide more details on the optimization of text embeddings and the selection of parameters such as alpha and beta? How do these parameters affect the segmentation results?
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets, such as the ADE20K dataset, which was not evaluated in the current study?
