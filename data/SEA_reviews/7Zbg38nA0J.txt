 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of "grokking" in neural networks, focusing on the competition between memorizing and generalizing circuits during training. It proposes a theory that explains how the efficiency of generalizing circuits, which are typically slower to learn but more efficient in terms of parameter norm, can outperform memorizing circuits in large datasets. The theory introduces the concepts of "ungrokking" and "semi-grokking" to describe the behavior of networks when trained on smaller or critical dataset sizes. The paper validates these concepts through experiments on a modular arithmetic task, showing that the generalizing circuits become more dominant as the dataset size increases, and that ungrokking occurs when the dataset size is significantly reduced. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for understanding neural network training dynamics and the role of weight decay in circuit efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and under-explored phenomenon in neural networks, providing a novel explanation for the observed behaviors through the lens of circuit efficiency.
- The proposed theory is well-supported by experimental evidence, including the demonstration of semi-grokking and ungrokking in a modular arithmetic task.
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the theory and its implications, and is supported by a comprehensive set of experiments.
- The introduction of the concept of "efficiency" in neural networks is a significant contribution, providing a new perspective on how circuits can be optimized for different tasks.
- The paper provides a theoretical framework that is precise enough to predict novel phenomena, such as ungrokking and semi-grokking, which are not commonly observed in neural network training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper focuses on a specific type of neural network, which may limit the generalizability of the findings to other architectures or tasks.
- The experiments are primarily conducted on a single task, which might not fully demonstrate the universality of the observed phenomena.
- The theoretical framework relies on strong assumptions, such as the separation of circuits and the efficiency metric, which may not hold in all practical scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the role of other regularization techniques, such as dropout, in circuit efficiency, which could affect the conclusions drawn.
- There are concerns about the clarity and completeness of the experimental setup, particularly in the description of the memorization-only and generalization-only networks, which could affect the reproducibility and validation of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the theory apply to different architectures, such as convolutional or recurrent networks, which might behave differently due to their unique characteristics?
- Is there a way to quantify the efficiency of circuits in a more principled way, perhaps using metrics like Bayesian information gain or cross-entropy?
- How does the efficiency metric relate to the capacity of the network, and could the observed behaviors be attributed to other factors, such as the capacity or the optimization process?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the definitions and training procedures for the memorization-only and generalization-only networks?
- How does the paper address the potential impact of different regularization techniques, such as dropout, on the observed phenomena?
