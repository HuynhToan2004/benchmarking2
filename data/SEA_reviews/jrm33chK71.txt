 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of fine-tuning a feature extractor on a subset of base classes to enhance few-shot learning performance. It proposes various methods for selecting these base classes, including task-informed, domain-informed, and uninformed settings. The paper evaluates these methods across multiple datasets and settings, demonstrating that fine-tuning on a subset of base classes can lead to improvements in accuracy. The authors also explore different strategies for selecting base classes and provide insights into the conditions under which these methods are most effective. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty and insufficient empirical evidence to support its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of the research and the experimental setup.
- The motivation behind the study is clear, and the proposed methods are easy to implement and understand.
- The study investigates the effect of fine-tuning on a subset of base classes, which is a novel approach to improving few-shot learning performance.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and settings, including both task-informed and domain-informed selection methods.
- The findings are supported by a robust experimental design, including comparisons to state-of-the-art methods and a thorough analysis of the results.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow and understand the different components of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- There is a lack of comparison with other baseline methods, particularly those that use the same pre-trained feature extractor, which could help establish the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not provide sufficient empirical evidence to support the claims made, particularly regarding the superiority of the proposed methods over existing ones.
- The paper's contribution to the field is questioned due to its reliance on a well-known idea of fine-tuning on a subset of base classes, which has been previously explored.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational costs associated with the proposed methods, which could be a significant barrier for practical implementation.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the results, particularly in how the different methods compare in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency.

**Questions:**

- How does the proposed method compare to other baseline methods that use the same pre-trained feature extractor?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the computational costs associated with the proposed methods, including training and inference times?
- How does the paper address the potential for domain shift between the support and query examples, and what are the implications of this for the effectiveness of the proposed methods?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly in terms of how the different methods compare in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency?
- How do the authors plan to address the concerns regarding the novelty and contribution of the paper to the field of few-shot learning?
