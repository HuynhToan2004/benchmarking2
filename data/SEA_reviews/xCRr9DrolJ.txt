 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Score Regularized Policy Optimization (SRPO), a novel method that utilizes pre-trained diffusion models to regularize the policy gradient without requiring diffusion sampling during evaluation. SRPO leverages the score function of the diffusion model to approximate the KL divergence between the learned policy and the behavior distribution, enhancing computational efficiency and potentially improving performance in offline reinforcement learning tasks. The method is evaluated on D4RL tasks and shows promising results, although it has some limitations and uncertainties regarding its generalizability and the theoretical justification of its effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with a clear presentation of the proposed method and its components.
- The proposed SRPO method is innovative and offers a novel approach to regularizing policy optimization using diffusion models, which is computationally efficient and potentially applicable in various domains.
- The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of SRPO, showing improvements in computational efficiency and performance on D4RL tasks compared to existing methods.
- The paper provides a clear mathematical explanation of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings, which is supported by detailed derivations and empirical evidence.
- The method's simplicity and effectiveness in reducing computational complexity while maintaining performance are notable, particularly in the context of offline RL where computational efficiency is critical.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other methods, particularly in terms of the evaluation metrics used (e.g., average reward, success rate, and time efficiency).
- The method's reliance on the accuracy of the diffusion model and the behavior cloning model raises concerns about its generalizability and robustness, especially in scenarios where these models may not accurately represent the behavior distribution.
- There is a lack of theoretical justification for the effectiveness of the proposed method, which could be strengthened by a more rigorous theoretical analysis.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of tasks and its scalability to more complex environments.
- The presentation of some equations and the overall clarity of the paper could be improved to better align with the content and to enhance reader comprehension.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other methods, including different evaluation metrics and a more comprehensive discussion on the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method compared to existing approaches?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the diffusion model or the behavior cloning model does not accurately represent the behavior distribution? What are the implications of these inaccuracies on the performance of the method?
- Can the authors provide more theoretical insights into the proposed method, particularly in terms of its effectiveness and the conditions under which it is most applicable?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the behavior distribution is not well-represented by the diffusion model or the behavior cloning model?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and uses of certain variables and parameters in the equations presented in the paper to improve reader comprehension?
- How does the method perform in more complex or varied environments, such as those found in the D4RL tasks? What are the implications of these variations on the performance of the method?
