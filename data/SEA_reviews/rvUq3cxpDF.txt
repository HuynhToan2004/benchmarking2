 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Latent Action Policies from Observation (LAPO), a method that learns latent action representations from observation-only datasets, which are then used to train policies for reinforcement learning (RL) tasks. LAPO employs an inverse dynamics model (IDM) and a forward dynamics model (FDM) to infer latent actions, which are then mapped to true actions using a decoder. The method is evaluated on the Procgen benchmark, showing that it can achieve expert-level performance in RL tasks with minimal fine-tuning. The paper also discusses the limitations of the method, including the dependency on the quality of the observation-only dataset and the potential for misalignment between latent actions and true actions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for learning latent action representations from observation-only datasets, which is a significant advancement in the field of reinforcement learning (RL).
- The approach is well-motivated, with a clear and well-written exposition that makes the methodology and results accessible.
- The methodology is supported by a thorough experimental evaluation, including a detailed analysis of the learned latent action space, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure that facilitates understanding of the method and its implications.
- The results show that the learned policies can be effectively fine-tuned to achieve expert-level performance in RL tasks, which is a significant achievement.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The dependency of the method on the quality of the observation-only dataset is not adequately addressed, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The method's reliance on a decoder to map latent actions to true actions introduces additional complexity and potential errors, which are not fully discussed.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing approaches, such as ILPO and FICC, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to more complex environments and larger datasets.
- The method's performance in environments with high degrees of partial observability is not convincingly demonstrated, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including its applicability to different types of environments and the quality of the observation-only dataset?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing approaches such as ILPO and FICC, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and inference times for the proposed method, and how these compare with other methods in terms of computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential misalignment between latent actions and true actions, and what steps are taken to mitigate this issue?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the size and quality of the observation-only dataset used in the experiments?
- How does the proposed method perform in environments with high degrees of partial observability, and what steps are taken to ensure that the learned policies are effective in such scenarios?
