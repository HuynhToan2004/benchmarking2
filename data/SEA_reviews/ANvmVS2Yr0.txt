 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the generalization capabilities of denoising neural networks, specifically focusing on the inductive biases that enable these networks to learn from small datasets and achieve strong generalization. It explores how these networks perform shrinkage operations in a basis adapted to the underlying image, suggesting a geometry-adaptive harmonic basis (GAHB) for optimal denoising. The authors demonstrate that these networks, when trained on different subsets of the CelebA dataset, converge to the same score function, indicating a shared inductive bias. The paper also discusses how this inductive bias influences the denoising performance on various image classes, such as Cα images and low-dimensional manifolds. However, the paper lacks a formal definition of the GAHB class and does not provide sufficient experimental evidence to support the claims about the generalization capabilities of the networks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel perspective on the generalization capabilities of denoising neural networks, which is a significant and timely topic given the recent concerns about memorization in such networks.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the inductive biases of denoising neural networks, demonstrating that these networks perform shrinkage operations in a basis adapted to the underlying image, which is a critical aspect of their generalization performance.
- The paper is well-written and clearly presents its ideas, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The authors conduct a thorough investigation into the properties of the trained denoiser, particularly focusing on the properties of the trained denoiser, which is directly related to the score function and the density from which the data are drawn.
- The paper includes several experiments that demonstrate the strong generalization of denoising neural networks, showing that they converge to a unique continuous density model that is independent of the specific training samples.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a formal definition of the GAHB class, which is crucial for understanding the properties and behavior of the inductive bias of denoising neural networks.
- The experiments conducted are limited to the CelebA dataset, which might not adequately represent the generalization capabilities of denoising neural networks across different datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on how the inductive biases of the network are aligned with the data distribution, which is a critical aspect of understanding the generalization performance of these networks.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is necessary for a complete understanding of the applicability and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not include experiments on larger datasets like LSUN-Bedroom, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the generalization capabilities of denoising neural networks.
- The paper does not address how the inductive biases of the network are aligned with the data distribution, which is a critical aspect of understanding the generalization performance of these networks.

**Questions:**
- How does the inductive bias of the network align with the data distribution, and what are the implications of this alignment for the generalization performance of denoising neural networks?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the inductive biases of the network are influenced by the training dataset?
- How do the authors define the GAHB class formally, and what are the specific properties that make it effective for denoising tasks?
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of the "true" distribution of images mentioned in the paper?
- How does the paper's analysis relate to the work by Yoon et al. (2023), which also investigates the generalization capabilities of denoising neural networks?
- Could the authors provide more experimental evidence to support the claims about the generalization capabilities of denoising neural networks, perhaps by including experiments on larger datasets or using different network architectures?
