 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Subnetwork Projection (SNP) and SNP++, novel techniques for meta-learning in the context of continual learning, specifically designed to maintain performance on both seen and unseen tasks. The method involves projecting subnetworks into the same parameter space as the meta-learners, allowing for the manipulation of these subnetworks to retain performance. The paper explores how these techniques can be applied to large models, such as CLIP, and demonstrates their effectiveness through experiments on various datasets. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues in its presentation and lacks detailed experimental results and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to continual learning by projecting subnetworks into the same parameter space as meta-learners, which allows for the manipulation of these subnetworks to retain performance.
- The method is well-motivated and provides a clear explanation of its application to large models like CLIP, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its implementation.
- The experiments conducted are extensive, covering a wide range of datasets and tasks, which helps in validating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of continual learning, particularly in the context of large models like CLIP, which is a significant contribution to the ongoing research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed experimental results and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, which makes it difficult to assess the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method against existing solutions.
- The presentation of the paper needs improvement, particularly in the clarity and organization of the content, especially in the methodology section.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method to larger datasets or models, which is a critical concern given the increasing size and complexity of models in current research.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational overhead or time complexity of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- There are several instances of unclear or ambiguous statements and figures in the paper, which could confuse readers and detract from the overall clarity and impact of the research.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specific datasets used, the number of tasks, and the metrics used to evaluate the performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can you clarify the role and impact of the memory buffer in the proposed method, particularly how it influences the manipulation of subnetworks?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, especially in the context of larger datasets or models?
- Could you provide more insights into the computational overhead and time complexity of the proposed method, and how it compares to other methods in terms of practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method ensure that the manipulation of subnetworks does not lead to overfitting or other negative effects on the model's performance?
