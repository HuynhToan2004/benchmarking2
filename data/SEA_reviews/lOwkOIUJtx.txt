 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework that integrates foveal-peripheral sampling with saccade control and continuous scene reconstruction to enhance image recognition performance while significantly reducing the number of input pixels. The framework employs a reinforcement learning-based saccade mechanism and a self-trained scene reconstruction model to restore original scenes from sparse inputs, improving classification accuracy by up to 35%. The approach is evaluated on the GTSRB and ImageNet datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to state-of-the-art foveal-peripheral vision systems. The framework's innovative use of foveal-peripheral sampling and saccade control, along with its potential for energy efficiency and data efficiency, is highlighted.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework that merges multiple foveal-peripheral views controlled by saccades into a reconstructed image, which is a significant advancement in the field of foveal-peripheral vision systems.
- The use of reinforcement learning to train the saccade controller is highlighted as a promising approach, potentially reducing the need for extensive training and fine-tuning of classifiers.
- The framework demonstrates high data efficiency during the training phase, requiring only a portion of the training data for effective training, which is a significant advantage over traditional methods.
- The proposed method achieves similar image recognition performance with at least 70% fewer pixels compared to systems with conventional imaging front-ends, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
- The self-trained scene reconstruction model restores the original scene from highly sparse input and achieves an average of 0.88 structure similarity on the ImageNet dataset, which is a notable achievement.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach that is both innovative and practical.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing foveal-peripheral vision systems, particularly in terms of the number of glimpses and the classification accuracy across different datasets.
- The use of the term "foveal-peripheral view" is misleading as it suggests a continuous view of the scene, which is not the case. The term "glance" might be more appropriate.
- The experimental results presented in the paper are not sufficiently robust or comprehensive, with some results showing significant variations or inconsistencies that need further explanation.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations or drawbacks of the proposed framework, such as the impact of the number of glimpses on accuracy or the generalizability of the results across different datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the technical aspects of the framework, including the role of the predictive reconstruction model and the evaluation module in the training process.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to specific datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability or applicability of the proposed framework in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancy in the results shown in Figure 3 and provide a more detailed explanation for the significant variations observed?
- How does the proposed framework compare with existing foveal-peripheral vision systems in terms of the number of glimpses required to achieve comparable accuracy levels?
- What are the specific advantages of using the proposed framework over other existing methods in terms of energy efficiency and data efficiency?
- How does the predictive reconstruction model and the evaluation module interact within the framework, and what role do they play in the overall performance of the system?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other foveal-peripheral vision systems, including a direct comparison of the number of glimpses and the classification accuracy across different datasets?
- How does the proposed framework perform when applied to other datasets, such as those used in other studies, and what are the implications of these results for the generalizability and applicability of the framework?
