 **Summary:**
The paper explores the feasibility of inverting language model (LM) outputs to recover the input prompt, focusing on the next-token probability distribution. The authors propose a method to reconstruct prompts using a trained encoder-decoder model, which is evaluated against various baselines and settings. The paper highlights the vulnerabilities of LMs to prompt inversion and discusses the potential for malicious use. It also introduces a dataset, Instructions-2M, for evaluating prompt inversion and discusses the implications for model security. The methodology is comprehensive, including experiments across different model sizes and access conditions, and the paper provides a detailed analysis of the attack's effectiveness and potential countermeasures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and experiments.
- The topic of language model inversion is timely and relevant, with potential applications in understanding the inner workings of large language models (LLMs).
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the threat model and the dataset construction.
- The paper is comprehensive, including a wide range of experiments that explore the effectiveness of the attack across different settings and model sizes.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the attack's effectiveness and potential countermeasures, highlighting the vulnerabilities of LLMs to prompt inversion.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the results and methodology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the privacy implications of the attack, which could be significant if the method is used to recover personal or sensitive information.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing works on model inversion, particularly those that focus on prompt inversion.
- The methodology for constructing the Instructions-2M dataset is not sufficiently detailed, raising concerns about the dataset's quality and the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the limitations of the attack, including the conditions under which the attack is most effective and the factors that influence its success.
- The paper's claims about the attack's effectiveness and the model's vulnerability to inversion are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which show limited success in recovering prompts.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the ethical implications of the attack, particularly in the context of LLMs being used as services.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the Instructions-2M dataset was constructed, including the sources of prompts and the criteria for selection?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods for prompt inversion, particularly in terms of effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- What are the specific privacy concerns raised by the attack, and how can these be addressed?
- How does the attack perform under different conditions, such as varying temperatures or different model sizes?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies that illustrate the attack's effectiveness and the conditions under which it is most successful?
- How does the attack perform on different types of prompts, such as those containing personal or sensitive information?
- Can the authors discuss the potential countermeasures that could be used to defend against the attack, and how effective these measures might be?
