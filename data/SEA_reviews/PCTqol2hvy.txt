 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the approximation capabilities of ResNet architectures, specifically focusing on the expressive power of ResNet with bottleneck blocks (b-ResNet). It presents a detailed theoretical analysis, demonstrating that b-ResNet can approximate various function classes such as monomials, polynomials, and smooth functions using fewer parameters than traditional FNNs. The paper also addresses the curse of dimensionality by showing that b-ResNet can approximate functions from the Kolmogorov superposition theorem class with a polynomial dependence on the input dimension. The theoretical results are supported by numerical experiments that showcase the practical implications of these findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, with clear presentation and a thorough literature review, making it accessible to readers.
- The results are original, providing a novel approach to understanding the approximation capabilities of ResNet-type MLPs.
- The paper establishes a connection between ResNet and feedforward networks, showing that ResNet can be viewed as an FNN, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical results are well-supported by numerical experiments, demonstrating the practical applicability of the theoretical findings.
- The paper is well-written, with clear proof ideas and a clear presentation of the results, which makes it easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claims about the expressive power of ResNet with bottleneck blocks (b-ResNet) are not fully supported by the experiments, which only test a limited set of functions.
- The theoretical results are not significantly novel, as they largely follow from existing works with minor modifications.
- The paper's focus on ResNet with bottleneck blocks (b-ResNet) might limit its applicability to other types of ResNet architectures.
- The proofs and theoretical results are somewhat standard and do not offer significant new insights or techniques beyond what is already known in the field.
- The paper's results are not surprising, as they are consistent with previous findings on the expressive power of ReLU networks, which are already well-established.

**Questions:**
- How does the paper's approach to using ResNet with bottleneck blocks (b-ResNet) differ from or improve upon the results obtained using standard ReLU networks?
- Can the authors clarify the role and significance of the "bottleneck block" in the context of the results presented?
- How does the paper's approach to using ResNet with bottleneck blocks (b-ResNet) address or improve upon the known limitations of ResNet, such as the curse of dimensionality?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other types of ResNet architectures, particularly those without bottleneck blocks, to demonstrate the practical advantages of their approach?
- In light of the standard nature of the proofs and theoretical results, how do the authors justify the novelty and contribution of their work to the field?
