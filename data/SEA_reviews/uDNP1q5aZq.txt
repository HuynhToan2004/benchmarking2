 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel data-poisoning based backdoor attack method, focusing on selecting the most influential samples from the benign dataset to poison, rather than randomly choosing them. The authors propose a learnable poisoning sample selection strategy (LPS) that identifies hard poisoned samples through a min-max optimization process. This strategy aims to improve the performance of backdoor attacks by selecting samples that contribute significantly to the learning process. The proposed method was tested on various benchmark datasets and demonstrated to outperform existing methods, including random selection and filtering-and-updating strategies. Despite its effectiveness, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing works, and the practicality of the method is limited by its reliance on a surrogate model which may not accurately reflect the behavior of the target model.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and under-explored issue in the field of backdoor attacks by focusing on the selection of samples to be poisoned, which is crucial for the success of the attack.
- The proposed method is well-explained and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the optimization process and the constraints used.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method significantly outperforms existing methods, demonstrating a clear improvement in the performance of backdoor attacks.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and a well-structured methodology that enhances understanding of the proposed approach.
- The methodology is simple yet effective, utilizing a learnable poisoning sample selection strategy that enhances the performance of various data-poisoning based backdoor attacks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is limited as it shares similarities with existing works, particularly in the use of a surrogate model and the selection of samples based on their importance.
- The practicality of the proposed method is limited by its reliance on a surrogate model, which may not accurately reflect the behavior of the target model, especially when the target model has different hyperparameters or training settings.
- The paper's contribution is questioned as it mainly focuses on the selection of samples for poisoning, which is not a new concept in the field of backdoor attacks.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a small number of datasets, which may not adequately represent the method's effectiveness across different scenarios.
- The computational complexity of the method is not adequately discussed, raising concerns about its scalability and efficiency in larger datasets.
- The method's effectiveness in scenarios where the target model has different training settings or hyperparameters is not addressed, which could limit its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the proposed method's performance would be affected when the target model has different training settings or hyperparameters?
- How does the proposed method perform when the poisoning ratio is higher, and what are the implications for the clean accuracy of the model?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations on how the optimization process in Equation (3) is solved, and what are the computational complexities involved?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to include training controllable backdoor attacks, and if so, how might this be achieved?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and scalability to larger datasets?
