 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the role of message passing in graph neural networks (GNNs) for collaborative filtering (CF) tasks. It presents a novel test-time aggregation framework, TAG-CF, which conducts message passing only at inference time, enhancing performance with minimal computational overhead. The authors demonstrate that message passing improves low-degree nodes more than high-degree nodes, contradicting common assumptions in graph learning tasks. They also show that the performance gains from neighbor information dominate those from additional gradients. The paper includes extensive ablation studies and experiments to support these findings, which could potentially lead to more efficient and effective CF methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a clear and insightful analysis of the role of message passing in CF tasks.
- The proposed TAG-CF method is simple, effective, and efficient, with minimal computational overhead.
- The paper provides a comprehensive empirical study, including ablation studies and experiments, which support the claims and findings.
- Theoretical insights are provided to explain the observed phenomena, which are supported by empirical results.
- The methodology is innovative, using test-time aggregation to improve performance without requiring message passing during training, which is a novel approach in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in CF, such as those that leverage graph-based regularization during training.
- The theoretical analysis, particularly Theorem 1, is seen as overly simplistic and may not fully capture the complexity of the problem.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and a deeper analysis of the results, especially the discrepancies in performance improvements across different datasets and user groups.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the presentation of equations and the explanation of certain methodological choices.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of TAG-CF, which could be crucial for practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and existing methods that leverage graph-based regularization during training?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and performance with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in scenarios where the number of training epochs is limited?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations for the observed discrepancies in performance improvements across different datasets and user groups?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential issues of variance in learned representations, especially in scenarios where the variance is high?
- Can the authors discuss the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of TAG-CF in more detail?
- Would it be possible to include a more detailed analysis of the theoretical underpinnings of the method, particularly in relation to the assumptions made in Theorem 1?
