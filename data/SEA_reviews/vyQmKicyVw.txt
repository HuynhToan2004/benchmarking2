 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the problem of learning latent causal variables and their relationships from observational data, focusing on the identifiability of latent variables and their causal relations. It extends the identifiability results for latent causal variables to non-linear settings, using a variational autoencoder (VAE) to model the latent variables and their dependencies. The authors introduce a novel method called the Change Encoding Network to handle changes in latent factors across different domains. The paper provides theoretical proofs and experimental validations, demonstrating the identifiability of latent variables and their relationships under certain conditions. However, the paper's assumptions and the clarity of its presentation have been critiqued, with some reviewers expressing concerns about the practical applicability and the robustness of the identifiability results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for learning latent causal variables from observational data, which is a significant contribution to the field of causal representation learning.
- The theoretical proofs provided are solid, and the paper is well-written, making the content accessible and understandable.
- The identifiability results for latent variables are extended to non-linear settings, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-motivated, addressing the challenge of learning latent causal variables from observational data, which is a significant problem in the field of causal representation learning.
- The paper provides a comprehensive set of identifiability results, which are a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the smoothness and positivity of the density function of latent variables and the sparsity of the latent Markov network, are not clearly justified or explained.
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the presentation of the identifiability results and the assumptions required for these results.
- The identifiability results rely on strong assumptions, such as the sparsity of the latent Markov network and sufficient changes in causal influences, which may not hold in real-world scenarios.
- The paper's methodology and assumptions are not well-explained, making it difficult for readers to fully understand the implications and limitations of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its methodology, which could affect the practical applicability of the results.
- The experimental validations are limited, and the paper could benefit from more comprehensive evaluations to demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the paper, particularly the assumptions of sparsity and sufficient changes, and explain how these assumptions affect the identifiability results?
- How does the paper's methodology handle scenarios where the assumptions of sparsity and sufficient changes do not hold?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the assumptions and methodologies used in the proofs, particularly in the appendices where these details are currently lacking?
- How does the paper's methodology compare to other existing methods for learning latent causal variables from observational data?
- Can the authors provide more concrete examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applicability of their methodology?
- How does the paper address the issue of identifiability when the latent variables are not independent, as discussed in the related work section?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the experimental setups and results to better understand the robustness and generalizability of the results?
