 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for learning causal representations in a continual setting using nonlinear Independent Component Analysis (ICA). It explores the identifiability of latent variables under varying domain conditions, showing that with additional domains, the identifiability of changing variables can escalate to subspace or component-wise levels. The method employs a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) and a flow model to simulate the mixing process, alongside a Gradient Episodic Memory (GEM) technique to preserve reconstruction capabilities across domains. The paper includes theoretical and experimental validations, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in maintaining reconstruction capabilities while learning new domains.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem of learning causal representations in a continual setting, which is crucial for understanding complex systems and processes.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis on the identifiability of changing variables with respect to the number of domains, which is a novel contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a logical structure that facilitates understanding.
- The experiments are conducted on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in maintaining reconstruction capabilities across domains.
- The paper includes a thorough discussion on the theoretical aspects, including identifiability results and the use of GEM for constraining the gradient updates, which are well-explained and supported by the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly how it handles scenarios where the number of changing variables is not known in advance.
- The experimental setup is limited to synthetic data, which might not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss how the proposed method compares to other existing methods in terms of computational complexity or scalability, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other methods for continual learning, which could help in understanding the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples in the method section to enhance clarity and understanding.

**Questions:**

- How does the method handle scenarios where the number of changing variables is not known in advance?
- Can the authors discuss how the method performs in real-world scenarios, particularly in terms of scalability and computational complexity?
- What are the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, and how do they affect the generalizability of the results?
