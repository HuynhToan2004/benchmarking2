 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedGT, a novel federated learning framework for graph-structured data, focusing on node classification tasks. FedGT employs a hybrid attention mechanism to reduce computational complexity, enabling the model to attend to both local and global nodes, thereby addressing the challenge of missing links. It also incorporates a personalized aggregation scheme based on the similarity of global nodes, which helps manage data heterogeneity. The model is further enhanced with local differential privacy to protect client data. The paper presents extensive experiments across six datasets and two subgraph settings, demonstrating the effectiveness of FedGT over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem in federated learning for graph-structured data, focusing on the challenges of missing links and data heterogeneity.
- The proposed hybrid attention mechanism is innovative, allowing the model to attend to both local and global nodes, thereby reducing computational complexity.
- The use of personalized aggregation based on the similarity of global nodes is a novel approach that effectively manages data heterogeneity.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear writing and comprehensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The inclusion of theoretical analysis and experimental results, along with a detailed discussion on the limitations of the approach, adds depth to the paper.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The experimental results, while comprehensive, could be enhanced by including a broader range of datasets and more detailed ablation studies.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and potential negative societal impacts.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for training and the scalability of the model with increasing numbers of clients.
- There are some inconsistencies and potential errors in the presentation of the paper, such as incorrect citations and unclear descriptions in figures and equations.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed hybrid attention mechanism compare with other methods in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the model?
- How does the model handle the privacy concerns, especially in terms of the distribution of global nodes across clients?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of datasets and the rationale behind the experimental settings used in the study?
- How does the model handle the scalability issues, particularly in terms of the computational resources required for training and the scalability of the model with increasing numbers of clients?
- In light of the limitations discussed, what are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed methods, and how can these be mitigated?
