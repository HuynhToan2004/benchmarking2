 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel neural operator called the Orthogonal Neural Operator (ONO), which utilizes orthogonal attention mechanisms to prevent overfitting and improve generalization. The ONO employs a hierarchical structure with an encoder, a neural network block, an orthogonal attention module, and a decoder, which are designed to handle various PDEs. The authors claim that ONO outperforms existing methods on six standard neural operator benchmarks, demonstrating a reduction in prediction errors and test errors. However, the paper lacks detailed experimental results and comparisons, particularly with state-of-the-art methods like LSM and GNOT, and does not provide sufficient theoretical analysis to support its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method is novel, combining a neural operator with orthogonal attention, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is supported by comprehensive experiments, including ablation studies and generalization experiments, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper includes a detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed method, which enhances the credibility of the approach.
- The authors have provided a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods like LSM and GNOT, which limits the understanding of the method's relative performance.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical analysis to support the claims made about the method's effectiveness, particularly in terms of its ability to prevent overfitting.
- The experimental results presented are not comprehensive enough, and the paper does not include results for some benchmarks, such as the Darcy benchmark.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of the number of parameters and the computational cost.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its applicability in different scenarios.
- There are several minor issues with the presentation of the paper, including typographical errors and unclear figures.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods like LSM and GNOT to better understand the relative performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when trained on different data sizes, and what are the implications for its performance in terms of overfitting?
- Can the authors clarify the theoretical underpinnings of the method, particularly in terms of how the orthogonal attention mechanism contributes to preventing overfitting?
- How does the computational cost of the proposed method compare to other methods, and what are the implications for practical applications?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the results for the Darcy benchmark and a broader range of scenarios?
