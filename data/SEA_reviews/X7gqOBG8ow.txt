 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method termed Denoising Non-Equilibrium Structures (DeNS) for improving the performance of energy and force prediction tasks in molecular dynamics simulations. DeNS involves adding noise to non-equilibrium structures and predicting the noise using forces of the original structure, which helps in training equivariant networks more effectively. The method is evaluated across multiple datasets including OC20, OC22, and MD17, showing consistent improvements in performance. The authors also discuss the method's ability to generalize to non-equilibrium structures, which is a significant advancement over previous methods that primarily focused on equilibrium structures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, DeNS, is a straightforward yet effective approach that can be easily integrated into existing models without significant modifications.
- The method is generalizable to non-equilibrium structures, which is a significant improvement over previous methods that primarily focused on equilibrium structures.
- The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements across various datasets and tasks, including S2EF, IS2RE, and energy/force prediction.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's impact on different architectures and hyperparameters, which helps in understanding the method's effectiveness and applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other recent denoising methods for non-equilibrium structures, which could help in understanding the method's relative performance.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method, such as its applicability to other types of atomistic data beyond molecular dynamics.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a critical consideration for many applications.
- The method's performance on larger datasets like OC20 S2EF-All+MD is not as impressive as on smaller datasets, which might limit its practical utility.
- The method's computational efficiency is not discussed, which could be a concern for large-scale applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent denoising methods for non-equilibrium structures in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the method be adapted to other types of atomistic data beyond molecular dynamics, such as quantum chemistry data?
- How does the method perform on larger datasets like OC20 S2EF-All+MD, and what are the potential reasons for the observed performance drop?
- Could the method be adapted to other types of tasks beyond energy and force prediction, such as property prediction or molecular design?
- How does the method handle the trade-off between energy and force predictions, especially when the coefficients are adjusted?
- Is there a possibility to use the method for pre-training models on larger datasets like PCQM4Mv2, and how does this affect the model's performance on smaller datasets?
