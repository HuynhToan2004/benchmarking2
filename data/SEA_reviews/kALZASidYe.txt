 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for enhancing the controllability of diffusion models by utilizing a reference image to guide image generation. It employs a content encoder and a style encoder to learn spatial layout masks and semantic codes, respectively, which are then integrated into the diffusion model through an adaptive group normalization layer. The paper also proposes a timestep-dependent weight scheduling to control the influence of content and style codes during sampling. The method is evaluated on various datasets and shown to outperform existing methods in terms of both qualitative and quantitative results. The paper also discusses the limitations of the method and provides suggestions for future research.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive analysis of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and easy to implement, with the use of an adaptive group normalization layer to inject content and style codes into the diffusion model.
- The method demonstrates good performance in both qualitative and quantitative results, with improvements over existing methods such as DiffuseIT, SAE, and CDM.
- The paper includes a thorough ablation study and analysis of the proposed method, which helps in understanding the impact of different components.
- The method's ability to disentangle the content and style of an image is a significant contribution to the field of image generation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods such as ControlNet and T2ladapter, which also address the issue of controllable image generation.
- The method's reliance on a pre-trained LDM for generating low-dimensional latent spaces might limit its applicability to other types of image generation tasks.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect the understanding of its practical applications and potential drawbacks.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by including more detailed descriptions of the training process and the role of different components in the method.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definition and role of certain components such as the content and style codes, which could confuse the reader.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods such as ControlNet and T2ladapter?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of image generation tasks, such as those not related to LDM?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process, including the specifics of the training dataset and the role of different components in the method?
- How do the content and style codes interact with each other during the training process, and what are the implications of this interaction for the final output of the method?
- Can the authors provide more examples or visualizations to illustrate the disentanglement of content and style in the generated images?
