 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Causal-StoNet, a novel method for causal inference in high-dimensional complex data scenarios. It leverages a stochastic neural network (StoNet) architecture, which is shown to approximate deep neural networks, to address the challenges of high-dimensional covariates, unknown functional forms, and missing data. The method is validated through extensive numerical studies, demonstrating its effectiveness in estimating the Average Treatment Effect (ATE) and handling missing data. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of the method, including its consistency and the universal approximation property. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in some sections, insufficient discussion on the limitations of the method, and a need for more detailed comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the problem, methodology, and results.
- The use of StoNet to approximate deep neural networks is innovative and addresses the challenges of high-dimensional covariates, unknown functional forms, and missing data.
- The paper provides extensive numerical studies that validate the method's effectiveness in estimating the Average Treatment Effect (ATE) and handling missing data.
- The method is theoretically grounded, with a focus on consistency and universal approximation properties.
- The paper is generally easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and scope.
- There is a need for more clarity in some sections, particularly in the introduction and the presentation of certain theoretical results.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing ones, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- The paper does not address how the method handles data with missing values, which is a significant issue in many real-world applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made and their implications, especially in the context of causal inference.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the method handles data with missing values?
- How does the method perform in terms of computational efficiency and scalability compared to other methods?
- Can the authors provide more details on the assumptions made and their implications, particularly in the context of causal inference?
- In the context of the Causal-StoNet's performance, how does it compare to other methods in terms of handling high-dimensional data and unknown functional forms?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their method and how these might affect its practical application?
