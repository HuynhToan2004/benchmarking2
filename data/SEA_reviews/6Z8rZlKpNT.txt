 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to out-of-distribution (OOD) detection using normalizing flows in the latent space of pre-trained image classification models. It employs a single epoch of training for the normalizing flow model, which performs density estimation on normalized latent variables. The methodology is evaluated on various datasets, including CIFAR-10 and ImageNet-1k, and shows competitive results compared to existing methods. The paper also explores the impact of dimensionality reduction, flow architecture, and latent scaling on OOD detection performance. Despite the innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its limited evaluation on only two datasets and a lack of comparison with other generative models like VAEs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the methodology, including the use of normalizing flows for OOD detection and the evaluation of various parameters such as dimensionality reduction and flow architecture.
- The methodology is novel, particularly the use of a single epoch of training for the normalizing flow model, which is a departure from traditional methods that require multiple epochs.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the method, showing that it outperforms existing methods on benchmarks such as CIFAR-10 and ImageNet-1k.
- The use of normalizing flows for OOD detection is innovative and provides a new perspective on the problem, potentially leading to more effective detection methods.
- The paper includes a detailed discussion on the importance of the latent space for OOD detection, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader evaluation across multiple datasets, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's effectiveness.
- The comparison with other generative models like Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) is missing, which could provide insights into the relative advantages and disadvantages of using normalizing flows over other models.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which is a critical concern in the application of normalizing flows for OOD detection.
- The method's dependence on the distribution of latent representations of the backbone model raises concerns about the robustness of the method, particularly in scenarios where the backbone model's latent space might not be well-suited for the task.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method, which could include potential biases in the training data or the generalizability of the method across different types of datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and the scalability of the method, especially in terms of training time and resource requirements.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when applied to other datasets, such as those in the iWILDS dataset, which could provide a more varied and challenging evaluation?
- Could the authors provide more insights into the choice of using a single epoch for training the normalizing flow model? How does this approach compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- How does the method handle different types of OOD data, particularly those that are not visually distinct from the in-distribution data?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the normalizing flows in OOD detection and how they compare to other methods that use different types of generative models?
- Could the paper benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and potential future directions for research?
