 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the unreasonable translation capabilities of large language models (LLMs) by focusing on the impact of unintentional bilingualism at various levels of granularity, specifically word alignment. The authors argue that word alignment data can provide translation signals comparable to sentence alignment data, suggesting that LLMs can learn to translate even without explicit bilingual data. The study also examines the effects of monolingual data and parameter-sharing architecture on LLMs' translation abilities. The paper presents a comprehensive analysis using the BLOOM-family models as a case study, comparing the effects of different types of unintentional bilingualism on translation performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and under-explored question regarding the unreasonable translation capabilities of large language models (LLMs), particularly focusing on the impact of unintentional bilingualism at the word alignment level.
- The research is well-executed, with a clear motivation and well-structured presentation that makes the findings accessible and understandable.
- The paper includes a comprehensive experimental setup, which is necessary to support the claims made about the effects of unintentional bilingualism on LLM's translation capabilities.
- The findings are significant, suggesting that word alignment data can provide translation signals comparable to sentence alignment data, challenging the common assumption that sentence-level bilingualism is crucial for LLMs' translation abilities.
- The paper contributes to the understanding of how LLMs acquire translation abilities, which is a crucial aspect of the broader puzzle of understanding the success of LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's reliance on the BLOOM-family models as a case study might limit the generalizability of the findings to other LLMs trained differently or with different data.
- The experimental setup and methodology are not sufficiently detailed, which could hinder reproducibility and the ability for others to build upon the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the study, such as the potential impact of different data contamination rates across different corpora and the potential effects of fine-tuning on the results.
- The paper's claims about the impact of unintentional bilingualism on LLM's translation capabilities are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, particularly in the pre-training experiments where the model's translation ability seems to be influenced by the amount of data rather than the presence of bilingual data.
- The paper's use of metrics like COMET and BLEURT for evaluating translation performance might not be the most appropriate, as these metrics are not designed to evaluate the quality of translations produced by LLMs.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the data mining pipeline used to collect unintentional bilingualism from existing multilingual corpora ensures that the results are not biased towards specific types of bilingualism?
- How do the authors justify the use of COMET and BLEURT for evaluating the translation performance of LLMs, given that these metrics might not be the most appropriate for assessing the quality of translations produced by LLMs?
- In the pre-training experiments, why was the model trained for a fixed number of updates instead of reaching convergence? How does this affect the reliability of the results?
- Can the authors provide more detailed information on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the data contamination rate and the methodology used to evaluate the impact of unintentional bilingualism on LLM's translation capabilities?
- How do the authors explain the discrepancy in results between the post-training experiments and the pre-training experiments, particularly in terms of the effectiveness of unintentional bilingualism in enhancing translation capabilities?
