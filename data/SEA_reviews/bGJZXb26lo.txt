 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for offline imitation learning using a world model, named DITTO. This approach optimizes a divergence measure between expert and learner policies in the latent space of the world model, which is then used to train the agent in the model's latent space. The method aims to address challenges such as high-dimensional observations, offline learning, and covariate shift. The paper presents a theoretical framework that bounds the divergence between the learner and expert policies in the latent space, and empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness in Atari environments. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited scope in terms of the number of environments tested and the lack of comparison with other recent methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, DITTO, for offline imitation learning using a world model, which addresses challenges such as high-dimensional observations, offline learning, and covariate shift.
- The method is theoretically sound, with a clear explanation of the theoretical framework and a detailed description of the methodology.
- The paper includes empirical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it outperforms baseline methods in Atari environments.
- The use of a world model to train an agent in the latent space of the world model is innovative and provides a novel approach to imitation learning.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily evaluates the method in a limited set of environments (only five Atari environments), which does not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the method across different environments.
- The method's reliance on a world model may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where such models are not readily available or are not sufficiently accurate.
- The theoretical framework is based on a deterministic belief distribution, which may not hold in practical scenarios where the belief distribution is stochastic.
- The method's performance is not compared with other recent methods, which could provide a better understanding of its relative effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of covariate shift, which is a significant challenge in imitation learning.
- The experimental results are not comprehensive, with insufficient details provided on the experimental setup and the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of distribution shift, which is a critical challenge in imitation learning.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform in environments where the world model is not sufficiently accurate?
- Can the method be adapted to handle stochastic belief distributions?
- How does the method compare with other recent methods in terms of performance and generalizability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and the results, including the specifics of the environments used and the performance metrics reported?
- How does the method address the issue of distribution shift, and what steps are taken to ensure that the learned policy remains effective in different environments?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the world model in mitigating covariate shift and provide more details on the experimental conditions that demonstrate this effect?
