 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AbeT, a novel approach to Out-of-Distribution (OOD) detection that integrates learned temperature and energy scores. AbeT aims to improve OOD detection by adjusting the temperature in the energy score calculation, which is a departure from traditional methods. The authors claim that this modification enhances the detection of OOD samples, particularly in scenarios where misclassified ID examples are used to train the model. The paper presents empirical evidence across various datasets and tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and object detection, demonstrating the effectiveness of AbeT in detecting OOD samples. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned as it largely builds on existing methods, and the paper lacks a broader comparison with state-of-the-art methods and a comprehensive analysis of computational costs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the methodology and results.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, as demonstrated by the experimental results.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including a detailed analysis of the energy score and its components.
- The use of misclassified ID examples as surrogates for OOD samples is a novel approach, which is well-explained and supported by empirical evidence.
- The method's performance is evaluated across various tasks and datasets, showing consistent improvements over existing methods.
- The paper includes a thorough analysis of the method's limitations and failure cases, which adds credibility to the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it primarily combines existing methods (Learned Temperature and Energy Score) without significant modification.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational costs and efficiency.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where there are few misclassified ID examples during training.
- The method's effectiveness is not clearly distinguished from other methods that use misclassified ID examples for OOD detection.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the method's performance on different types of OOD data, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper lacks a broader comparison with existing OOD detection methods, which could help contextualize the contributions of the proposed method.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly in the semantic segmentation and object detection sections.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational costs and efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the method performs on different types of OOD data, particularly those that are significantly different from the training data?
- How does the method handle scenarios where there are few misclassified ID examples during training?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the Forefront Temperature Constant in the method's performance?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the ID and OOD datasets are significantly different, such as in the case of ImageNet-1K and ImageNet-V2?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results and analysis for the semantic segmentation and object detection experiments, particularly in terms of how the method handles different types of OOD data in these domains?
