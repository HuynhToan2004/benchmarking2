 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SAFHE, a novel approach to secure aggregation in federated learning (FL) that addresses both gradient inversion attacks and backdoor attacks. SAFHE utilizes a weighting function H, which can be evaluated in the encrypted domain using Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE), allowing the server to reject large updates without revealing their content. The method is evaluated against noise and switch classes attacks on a CIFAR-10 dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness in maintaining model accuracy while mitigating these threats. However, concerns are raised about the limited scope of the experiments, the lack of comprehensive comparisons with other defenses, and the practicality of the method in real-world FL scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and the results.
- The proposed method is novel, combining gradient norm clipping defense with Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) to address both gradient inversion attacks and backdoor attacks, which are significant challenges in federated learning.
- The paper is the first to propose using Chebyshev polynomials to approximate the weighting function for secure aggregation, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in defending against noise and switch classes attacks, maintaining model accuracy, and being computationally efficient.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the method, including the use of Chebyshev polynomials for polynomial approximation and the design of the weighting function H.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks comprehensive comparisons with other defense methods, particularly those that use zero-knowledge proofs or other cryptographic primitives, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The experiments are limited in scope, focusing on a single dataset (CIFAR-10) and a specific model (10-layer CNN), which may not generalize well to other datasets or model architectures.
- The paper does not adequately address the practicality of the method in real-world federated learning scenarios, particularly concerning the communication overhead and the scalability of the method to larger datasets or more complex models.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its applicability to other attacks or defense strategies and the potential impact on the training rate and accuracy.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the implementation of the Chebyshev polynomial approximation and its impact on the training process, which could affect the reliability and efficiency of the method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the implementation of the Chebyshev polynomial approximation and how it affects the training process, particularly in terms of the communication overhead and the impact on the training rate and accuracy?
- How does the method perform against other types of attacks, such as adaptive attacks or untargeted attacks?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and how it might be extended or adapted to address these limitations?
- How does the method compare to other defense methods that use zero-knowledge proofs or other cryptographic primitives, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practicality in federated learning scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons with other defense methods and a broader range of datasets and model architectures?
