 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called LSC for test-agnostic long-tail learning, aimed at estimating the test label distribution and adjusting predictions from a pre-trained model to align with this distribution. This approach leverages a shallow neural network within a generalized black box shift estimation framework to estimate the test label distribution, which is then used to adjust model outputs. Theoretical analysis and extensive experiments on three benchmark datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in reducing generalization error and improving the performance of existing long-tail learning methods. The paper also discusses the limitations of the method and provides a comprehensive review of related work, though it lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its implementation details.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and adaptable, able to be integrated with existing long-tail learning methods to improve their performance.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to support the effectiveness of the proposed method, which helps in understanding its theoretical underpinnings.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, including comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and ablation studies.
- The paper addresses the significant challenge of test-agnostic long-tail learning, which is crucial for real-world applications where test data distributions can significantly differ from training data distributions.
- The proposed method is versatile and can be seamlessly integrated with numerous existing long-tail learning models to enhance their generalization capabilities in scenarios with unknown test label distributions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of existing methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive view of the current state of the field.
- The novelty of the proposed method is somewhat limited as it builds upon existing methods like BBSE and GBBSE, and the main contribution appears to be the adaptive logit clipping module.
- The experimental results are not very convincing, with significant improvements only observed in certain scenarios, and the method's effectiveness in real-world applications remains unclear.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- The method's performance is not robust to different test label distributions, and the selection of the number of test samples used to estimate the test label distribution is critical but not well-explained.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its practical applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of the proposed method compared to existing methods like BBSE and GBBSE?
- How does the proposed method perform in real-world applications, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in practical deployment?
- What are the potential applications of the proposed method in real-world scenarios, and how might it be adapted to address specific challenges in these applications?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed method compare to existing methods, and what are the implications for practical deployment?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including comparisons with different test label distributions and a broader range of datasets?
- How does the proposed method handle the selection of the number of test samples used to estimate the test label distribution, and what are the implications of this selection on the method's performance?
