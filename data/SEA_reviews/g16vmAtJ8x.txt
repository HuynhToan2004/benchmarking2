 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the privacy implications of synthetic data generation, focusing on the use of similarity-based privacy metrics. It critiques the reliance on these metrics, arguing that they do not provide robust privacy guarantees and can be exploited by adversaries to reconstruct original data. The authors present a novel attack, ReconSyn, which demonstrates the vulnerabilities of these metrics by successfully reconstructing a significant percentage of outliers in the train data. The paper also discusses the potential for differential privacy (DP) to enhance privacy protection, although it acknowledges that the use of DP alone may not be sufficient. The experimental results show that ReconSyn is effective in reconstructing outliers across various datasets and models, highlighting the need for more robust privacy metrics in synthetic data generation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- It provides a comprehensive analysis of the limitations of similarity-based privacy metrics (SBPMs) and their implications for privacy in synthetic data generation.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed attack, ReconSyn, in reconstructing a significant percentage of outliers in the train data.
- The paper addresses a timely and relevant issue in the field of synthetic data, which is increasingly important for data privacy and security.
- The findings and recommendations are likely to be of interest to practitioners and researchers in the area of synthetic data, highlighting the practical implications of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the proposed attack, ReconSyn, could be adapted to other types of privacy attacks, such as membership or attribute inference attacks.
- There is an overemphasis on the use of differential privacy (DP) as a solution to the privacy issues highlighted by the paper, which may not be the most effective or practical approach in all cases.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the ethical implications of the research, particularly in terms of the potential negative impacts on the privacy of individuals or organizations.
- The evaluation of the proposed attack is limited to a few datasets, which might not adequately represent the performance of the attack across a broader range of datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed attack and how these limitations might affect its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the proposed attack, ReconSyn, could be adapted to other types of privacy attacks, such as membership or attribute inference attacks?
- How does the paper address the ethical implications of the research, particularly in terms of the potential negative impacts on privacy?
- Could the authors elaborate on the specific advantages of differential privacy (DP) in addressing the privacy issues highlighted by the paper?
- How do the authors envision the use of their findings in practical scenarios, such as in the development of more robust privacy metrics for synthetic data generation?
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed attack and how these limitations might affect its practical applicability?
