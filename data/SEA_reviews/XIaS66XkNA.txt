 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Idempotent Generative Network (IGN), a novel approach to generative modeling that leverages idempotent operators to map between source and target distributions. The IGN model is trained to map a source distribution to a target distribution, ensuring that instances from the target distribution remain unchanged and instances from the source distribution are projected onto the target distribution. This is achieved through the use of three loss terms: reconstruction, idempotence, and tightness. The model is demonstrated to generate images from noise and perform out-of-distribution projection, although it struggles with blurriness and artifacts. The theoretical underpinnings of the model are explored, and the paper provides a comprehensive review of related work, including discussions on the limitations and potential improvements.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to generative modeling using idempotent operators, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed model is theoretically sound, with a well-structured theoretical analysis that supports the claims.
- The manuscript is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing promising qualitative results and potential for further improvements.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of related work, situating the research within the existing literature and highlighting its contributions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from a lack of quantitative metrics, such as FID scores, which could provide a more objective evaluation of the model's performance.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with blurriness and artifacts in the generated images, which could limit the practical applicability of the model.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential improvements of the proposed method, which could enhance its impact and relevance.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing models, such as GANs and VAEs, which could help in understanding the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the training process and the role of the tightness loss, which is crucial for the model's performance.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the training process and the role of the tightness loss in achieving the desired results?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing models like GANs and VAEs in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their approach and suggest potential improvements to address these challenges?
- In the context of the experimental results, could the authors provide more quantitative metrics to support their qualitative findings?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of blurriness and artifacts in the generated images, and what steps are being taken to address these issues?
