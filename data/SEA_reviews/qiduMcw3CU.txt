 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to skill learning in reinforcement learning (RL) through the concept of Skill Machines (SMs), which are finite state machines that encode solutions to tasks specified using regular languages. SMs are composed of skill primitives, each associated with a WVF, and are capable of zero-shot generalization to new tasks. The authors demonstrate that SMs can be obtained directly from an LTL task specification, and show that these SMs are satisficing, ensuring adherence to task specifications. The paper also explores the application of SMs in various environments, including a tabular setting, a high-dimensional video game, and a continuous control environment, and compares their performance against state-of-the-art baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to skill learning in reinforcement learning (RL) through the concept of Skill Machines (SMs), which are finite state machines that encode the solution to tasks specified using regular languages.
- SMs are composed of skill primitives, each associated with a WVF, and are capable of zero-shot generalization to new tasks, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper provides a theoretical guarantee that the SMs adhere to the logical task specification, ensuring safety and verifiability in agent decision-making.
- The experimental results demonstrate that SMs perform near-optimally and show significant improvements in sample efficiency compared to baseline methods.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the concepts and methods used, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is shown to be effective in both tabular and high-dimensional video game environments, demonstrating its applicability across different settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the differences and advantages of the proposed Skill Machines (SMs) compared to other existing methods, such as those by Barreto et al. (2019).
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the improvements in performance are not consistently demonstrated across all tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of SMs in more complex environments with a larger number of propositions, which could limit their practical applicability.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of certain concepts and mathematical notations, which could confuse readers and hinder understanding.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to continuous action spaces and the potential for over-composition of skills.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the computational complexity of the method and the scalability of the SMs in more complex environments.

- How does the method perform when applied to more complex environments, such as those with a larger number of propositions?
