 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LatentCBF, a novel approach to control barrier functions (CBFs) in latent space, leveraging a Lipschitz AutoEncoder for system dynamics learning and a neural policy for safe control. This methodology enables the synthesis of safe controllers from observations without requiring full state access, which is a significant departure from traditional methods. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of LatentCBF through simulations involving quadrotors and cars, showcasing its ability to handle complex environments and achieve robust control. Despite its innovative approach, concerns were raised regarding the clarity of the paper's writing, the theoretical robustness of the Lipschitz network, and the need for more comprehensive experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to synthesize safe controllers from observations without requiring full state access, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The methodology is well-described, and the paper is easy to follow, with a clear structure that facilitates understanding.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of LatentCBF through simulations involving quadrotors and cars, showcasing its ability to handle complex environments and achieve robust control.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear description of the methodology and results, making it accessible and understandable.
- The proposed framework can be applied to a wide range of robotic systems due to its flexibility in representation learning using AutoEncoder.
- The paper includes a detailed appendix, which provides additional insights and information that can enhance the understanding of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the Lipschitz network is designed, which is crucial for the theoretical robustness of the method.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods like rCLBF-QP and BarrierNet, particularly in terms of novelty and effectiveness.
- The paper's writing could be improved for clarity, with some sections being overly technical and difficult to follow.
- The experimental validation is limited, with only a few environments tested, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the method.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other methods, such as rCLBF-QP and BarrierNet, which could help in understanding the relative performance and advantages of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for a complete evaluation of its applicability and effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the Lipschitz network is designed and ensure that it meets the theoretical robustness requirements?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods like rCLBF-QP and BarrierNet, particularly in terms of novelty and effectiveness?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the methodology ensures the robustness and reliability of the learned barrier function?
- How does the proposed method handle the complexity of real-world scenarios, such as noisy sensor data or dynamic environments?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specific environments used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the proposed method handle the generalization of the learned dynamics and barrier function to unseen states or scenarios?
