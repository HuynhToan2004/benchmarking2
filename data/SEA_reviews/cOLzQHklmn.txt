 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for model selection, focusing on independently-prepared query-efficient model selection. This approach aims to address scalability issues in model selection by allowing for the preparation of embedding vectors for different models independently, which can be compared to task embeddings for efficient selection. The method, termed Standardized Embedder, uses a baseline model to learn embeddings for different models and tasks, which are then used to select the most suitable models for a given task. The approach is evaluated against various models and tasks, showing promising results in terms of efficiency and effectiveness. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the method's technical details, the limited evaluation scope, and the potential for overfitting due to the use of a single dataset for learning embeddings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem by proposing a novel method for model selection, which is a crucial aspect in the era of increasing model diversity.
- The method is well-motivated, with clear explanations of its properties, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, and the experiments conducted demonstrate its efficiency and effectiveness.
- The paper introduces a new paradigm for model selection, which is query-efficient and independently-prepared, offering updatability, decentralizability, flexibility, and preservation of both candidate and query privacy.
- The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of the method, including a proof-of-concept solution called Standardized Embedder, which has been tested against 100 pre-trained models across various tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing model selection methods, such as transferability metrics, which could provide a better understanding of the proposed method's advantages and limitations.
- The technical details of the method, particularly the optimization process and the role of the baseline model, are not clearly explained, making it difficult for readers to fully grasp the proposed solution.
- The evaluation scope is limited, focusing only on a single dataset and a small number of models, which may not adequately demonstrate the method's effectiveness across different scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the potential for overfitting due to the use of a single dataset for learning embeddings, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the technical details of the method, particularly the optimization process and the role of the baseline model?
- How does the method compare to other model selection methods, especially in terms of efficiency and effectiveness?
- What are the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how might these affect its applicability in different scenarios?
- Can the authors provide more details on the evaluation setup, such as the selection of models and the choice of datasets?
- How does the method perform when using different datasets for learning embeddings, and what are the implications of this choice on the results?
