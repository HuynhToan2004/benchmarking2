 **Summary:**
The paper introduces L-Eval, a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating long context language models (LCLMs). It includes 20 sub-tasks, 508 long documents, and over 2,000 human-labeled query-response pairs, covering diverse question styles, domains, and input lengths. The authors propose a new evaluation metric, Length-Instruction-Enhanced (LIE) evaluation, to enhance the effectiveness of automatic evaluation metrics. The benchmark is designed to bridge the gap between open-source and proprietary models, offering a standardized approach to evaluating LCLMs. The paper also discusses the limitations of n-gram matching metrics and advocates for using LLM judges for open-ended tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating long context language models (LCLMs), which is crucial for the development of this technology.
- The benchmark covers a wide range of tasks, including closed-ended and open-ended tasks, and uses both human and automatic evaluations, providing a robust assessment of LCLMs.
- The paper addresses the limitations of n-gram matching metrics and proposes a new evaluation metric, Length-Instruction-Enhanced (LIE) evaluation, which is expected to enhance the effectiveness of automatic evaluation metrics.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments and analysis, providing valuable insights into the current state and limitations of LCLMs.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are thorough, including a wide range of open-source and closed-source models.
- The proposed benchmark covers a diverse set of tasks, which will be beneficial for future research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed description of the data annotation process, which is crucial for understanding the quality and reliability of the benchmark.
- There is a lack of clarity on the specifics of the data filtering and correction process, which is essential for ensuring the quality and reliability of the benchmark.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the data statistics, such as the average and maximum lengths of the documents, which are crucial for understanding the benchmark's scope and applicability.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the benchmark, particularly whether the results can be applied to different models or domains.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the benchmark and the potential biases that might affect the results.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the evaluation metrics used, such as the specifics of the human evaluation process and the criteria for selecting the models for evaluation.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the benchmark.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the data annotation process, including the qualifications and training of the annotators, the inter-annotator agreement, and the quality control measures in place?
- How were the specific examples chosen for each task, and what criteria were used to ensure their relevance and applicability to the benchmark?
- What are the specifics of the data filtering and correction process, including the number of samples filtered or corrected and the criteria used for these decisions?
- How were the models selected for evaluation, and what were the specific criteria used to ensure their representativeness and relevance to the benchmark?
- Can the authors clarify the evaluation metrics used, particularly the human evaluation process and the criteria for selecting the models for evaluation?
- What are the specifics of the Length-Instruction-Enhanced (LIE) evaluation, and how does it compare to other evaluation metrics in terms of effectiveness and reliability?
- How generalizable are the results of the benchmark to different models or domains, and what are the potential biases that might affect the results?
