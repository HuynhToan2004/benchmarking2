 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the integration of gradient compression, random reshuffling, and local steps in federated learning (FL) to enhance communication efficiency. It introduces various algorithms such as Q-RR, DIANA-RR, Q-NASTYA, and DIANA-NASTYA, each aiming to address specific challenges like variance due to compression and bias from local steps. The paper provides theoretical analysis and experimental validations, comparing these methods against existing algorithms like FedRR and FedNASTYA. Despite the theoretical and empirical contributions, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive empirical validation, and unclear presentation in some sections.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the theoretical contributions and experimental results, making it accessible and understandable.
- The combination of random reshuffling, local steps, and gradient compression is a novel approach that addresses the challenges of communication efficiency in federated learning.
- The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms, which is supported by empirical results that show the practical benefits of the proposed methods.
- The paper effectively compares the proposed algorithms with existing methods like FedRR, FedNASTYA, and FedPAQ, demonstrating the advantages of the proposed methods.
- The paper addresses a significant issue in federated learning by combining multiple techniques to improve communication efficiency, which is crucial for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation between the theoretical contributions and the practical implications of the proposed algorithms.
- There is a notable absence of empirical validation for some of the claims made in the paper, particularly regarding the performance of Q-RR and DIANA-RR compared to QSGD and DIANA.
- The experimental results are not comprehensive enough, with some figures not showing significant improvements over baseline methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of heterogeneity in client datasets, which is a critical aspect of federated learning.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned as it seems to be a combination of existing techniques without significant new insights or contributions.
- The paper suffers from poor writing in some sections, with unclear explanations and a lack of clarity in the presentation of the algorithms and their theoretical underpinnings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the differences between the theoretical contributions and the practical implications of the proposed algorithms?
- How do the authors justify the use of DIANA-style shift sequences in DIANA-RR and DIANA-NASTYA, and what are the specific advantages of these methods over other variance reduction techniques?
- Why is the variance reduction step necessary in DIANA-RR and DIANA-NASTYA, and how does it impact the overall performance of the algorithms?
- How does the paper address the issue of heterogeneity in client datasets, and what are the implications of this for the performance of the proposed algorithms?
- Could the authors provide more detailed empirical evidence to support the claims made about the performance of Q-RR and DIANA-RR compared to QSGD and DIANA?
- Can the authors elaborate on the differences between the proposed algorithms and existing methods like FedRR and FedNASTYA, and how these differences translate into practical benefits?
