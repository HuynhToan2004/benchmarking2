 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for evaluating open-domain text generation, specifically focusing on dialogue and summarization tasks. The method, termed Contrastive Distribution Methods (CDM), utilizes a contrastive momentum approach to model the relationship between model sizes and performance, with two primary paradigms: Generative CDM and Discriminative CDM. Generative CDM generates synthetic examples for training discriminator-based metrics, while Discriminative CDM directly uses distribution disparities between models for evaluation. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on multi-turn dialogue and factuality in abstractive summarization, showing improvements over existing metrics. However, concerns about the novelty and practicality of the method, especially its reliance on specific model architectures and its generalizability across different domains, were raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem in the field of evaluating open-domain text generation, which is crucial for assessing the quality of text generated by large language models (LLMs).
- The proposed method, Contrastive Distribution Methods (CDM), is well-articulated and innovative, offering a novel approach to evaluating text generation by leveraging the contrastive momentum between different model sizes.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, which is a significant advantage for readers looking to grasp the methodology and results.
- The methodology is supported by strong experimental results, demonstrating its effectiveness in evaluating text generation, particularly in dialogue and summarization tasks.
- The paper provides a comprehensive ablation study, which helps in understanding the impact of different components of the proposed method on its performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques such as contrastive decoding and discriminator-based evaluation metrics, without sufficient differentiation or improvement over these existing methods.
- The method's dependence on specific model architectures, such as T5, limits its applicability to other models like GPT-3.5/3.0, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodology, particularly in the definitions and roles of the amateur and expert models, which could confuse readers about the method's operation and effectiveness.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that the proposed method correlates better with human judgments than existing metrics, which is crucial for establishing the method's utility and validity.
- The method's reliance on the availability of a larger model to serve as an expert model could limit its practicality in real-world applications where such models may not be accessible or feasible.
- There are concerns about the method's scalability and applicability to other domains beyond dialogue and summarization, as the experiments are limited to these specific tasks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of the method and how it differs from existing techniques like contrastive decoding and discriminator-based evaluation metrics?
- How does the method perform when applied to other domains or tasks beyond dialogue and summarization?
- Could the authors provide more evidence or data to support the claim that the proposed method correlates better with human judgments than existing metrics?
- How does the method handle the selection of the amateur and expert models, and what are the implications of these choices on the method's effectiveness?
- Can the authors explain the rationale behind the choice of T5 as the model architecture for the experiments, and how the method would perform with other model architectures like GPT-3.5/3.0?
- How does the method address the potential biases or limitations in the datasets used for training and evaluation, and what steps are taken to ensure the method's robustness and generalizability?
