 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel second-order algorithm for sparse phase retrieval, leveraging Newton projection to achieve a quadratic convergence rate. This algorithm maintains the same per-iteration computational complexity as popular first-order methods, providing theoretical guarantees for convergence to the ground truth. The method is compared against existing state-of-the-art algorithms, demonstrating superior performance in terms of convergence speed and success rate in noisy scenarios. Despite its theoretical and experimental strengths, the paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in the explanation of certain algorithmic steps and the presentation of theoretical results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel second-order algorithm for sparse phase retrieval, which achieves a quadratic convergence rate while maintaining the same per-iteration computational complexity as popular first-order methods.
- The theoretical analysis provided is rigorous, and the experimental results are strong, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed algorithm over existing methods in terms of convergence speed and success rate in noisy scenarios.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings, making it accessible to readers.
- The use of Newton projection in the algorithm is highlighted as a significant contribution, and the paper provides a clear comparison with existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in the explanation of certain algorithmic steps and the presentation of theoretical results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity of the algorithm, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art algorithms, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and theoretical guarantees.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to fully evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
- There are several typographical and notation errors throughout the paper, which could confuse readers and detract from the overall quality of the work.
