 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for automatically generating a hallucination dataset using existing fact-checking datasets and large language models (LLMs). This approach involves prompting LLMs to generate references to claims and then using these references to determine if the LLM has generated hallucinations. The methodology is tested on various LLMs, including ChatGPT and Llama-2, and the results show that the proposed method can detect hallucinations effectively, outperforming existing baselines. The paper also explores the prevalence of hallucination in LLMs and provides insights into the types of content that tend to be hallucinatory. However, concerns are raised about the reliability of the generated dataset, the novelty of the approach, and the comparability of the results due to differences in model sizes and temperatures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to automatically generate a hallucination dataset using existing fact-checking datasets and large language models (LLMs), which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method is straightforward and effective, demonstrating superior performance over existing baselines.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the generated dataset, revealing insights into the prevalence of hallucination in LLMs and the types of content that tend to be hallucinatory.
- The methodology is simple and can be applied to any LLM, making it accessible and potentially useful for a wide range of applications.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experimental results and ablation studies, which support the claims made.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it mainly involves using LLMs to generate references and then using these references to detect hallucinations, which is a standard method in the field.
- The paper does not adequately address the reliability of the generated dataset, which is critical for the validity of the results.
- The methodology is not clearly described, particularly the process of collecting hallucination samples and the comparison with ground truth labels.
- The paper does not discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method, such as the impact of different model sizes and temperatures on the results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the generated dataset compares with existing benchmarks and whether it is suitable for different types of tasks.
- There is a lack of human evaluation, which is crucial for validating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with existing benchmarks, such as those used in the SelfCheckGPT paper?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the generated dataset is validated, particularly in terms of its reliability and the methodology used to collect and compare hallucination samples?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of tasks, such as question answering or summarization?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method, such as the impact of different model sizes and temperatures on the results?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of hallucination in LLMs, particularly in scenarios where the LLM might not have a clear understanding of the claim?
