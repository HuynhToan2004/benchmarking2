 **Summary:**
The paper introduces RF-Policy, a novel imitation learning algorithm that leverages rectified flow models to address the computational inefficiencies of diffusion-based methods. RF-Policy uses a conditional velocity field to optimize the ODE loss and predicts the conditional variance, which is used to adaptively determine the step size for the ODE solver. This approach aims to balance between deterministic and stochastic behaviors, allowing the model to perform efficiently in both uni-modal and multi-modal scenarios. The paper includes empirical evaluations across multiple robotics tasks, comparing the performance of RF-Policy with existing methods like Behavioral Cloning (BC) and diffusion-based policies. The experiments demonstrate that RF-Policy achieves a balance between success rate and diversity score, which is superior to both BC and diffusion-based policies. However, the paper's theoretical analysis and empirical results have been critiqued for their clarity and depth, respectively.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel algorithm, RF-Policy, which leverages rectified flow models to address the computational inefficiencies of diffusion-based methods in imitation learning.
- The authors provide a comprehensive empirical evaluation of RF-Policy across multiple robotics tasks, comparing its performance with existing methods like Behavioral Cloning (BC) and diffusion-based policies.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology, including the use of variance estimation to adaptively determine the step size for the ODE solver.
- The theoretical analysis provided is solid, and the paper introduces new metrics for comprehensive evaluation of an imitation learning algorithm.
- The experimental results show that RF-Policy achieves a balance between success rate and diversity score, which is superior to both BC and diffusion-based policies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear theoretical justification for the superiority of RF-Policy over diffusion-based policies, particularly in terms of its ability to handle both deterministic and stochastic behaviors effectively.
- The empirical results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with marginal improvements over BC and diffusion-based policies in some tasks.
- The paper's experimental setup and evaluation metrics are not clearly defined or justified, making it difficult to assess the robustness and generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the assumption of uni-modality in demonstration data and the potential for overfitting due to the use of variance estimation.
- The paper's theoretical analysis and empirical results are not sufficiently rigorous, with some sections lacking clarity and detail.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the theoretical justification for why RF-Policy is superior to diffusion-based policies in handling both deterministic and stochastic behaviors?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the demonstration data is multi-modal?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the dataset used and the evaluation metrics employed?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance across different tasks?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their approach, such as the assumption of uni-modality in demonstration data and the potential for overfitting due to the use of variance estimation?
