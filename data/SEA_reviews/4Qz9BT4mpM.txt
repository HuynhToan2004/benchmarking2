 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of the "agreement on the line" (AGL) phenomenon to foundation models, specifically focusing on how randomness in model initialization, data subsetting, and data shuffling affects the correlation between in-distribution (ID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) accuracies. The study extends previous work by Baek et al. (2022) by demonstrating that AGL can be observed even in models fine-tuned from pretrained foundational models, and that this correlation is robust across different datasets and tasks. The paper also investigates how different sources of randomness affect the AGL correlation and proposes methods to estimate OOD performance without labeled data. The findings suggest that AGL can be a reliable indicator of OOD performance, but only under certain conditions such as random initialization and data shuffling.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- The study of the AGL phenomenon is relevant and timely, particularly in the context of foundation models.
- The paper extends the AGL phenomenon to foundation models, which is an interesting and important direction for future research.
- The experiments are comprehensive and cover various datasets and tasks, providing a solid foundation for the conclusions drawn.
- The paper provides a novel approach to estimating OOD performance without labeled data, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The findings of the paper are surprising and could lead to new insights and applications in the field of out-of-distribution (OOD) performance estimation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not clearly define or explain the AGL phenomenon, which could confuse readers unfamiliar with the concept.
- The paper lacks a clear theoretical explanation for why AGL holds for foundation models and why different sources of randomness affect it differently.
- The experimental results are not always consistent across different datasets and tasks, which raises questions about the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not compare its results with a sufficient number of baselines, which limits the ability to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed methods against existing approaches.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its findings, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the findings for practical applications, such as in real-world scenarios where OOD performance is critical.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify why AGL holds for foundation models and why different sources of randomness affect it differently?
- How does the paper's method compare to other existing methods for estimating OOD performance, such as those mentioned in the paper?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations for the experimental results that are not consistent across different datasets and tasks?
- How does the paper's method perform in scenarios where the AGL phenomenon does not hold, such as in datasets where it is not observed?
- What are the implications of the findings for practical applications, particularly in real-world scenarios where OOD performance is critical?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their findings and suggest areas for future research to address these limitations?
