 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for jointly pre-training a visual tokenizer and a multi-modal large language model (LLM), aiming to represent both vision and language in a unified discrete token format. This approach involves a dynamic visual tokenizer that selects and merges informative image patches, reducing redundancy and improving computational efficiency. The model, named LaVIT, is evaluated on various vision-language tasks and demonstrates superior performance compared to existing methods. The methodology includes a two-stage pre-training process, with the first stage focusing on tokenizer training and the second stage on vision-language pre-training. Despite the innovative approach and strong experimental results, the paper is criticized for its complexity, lack of clarity in some sections, and insufficient discussion on related work.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for pre-training a visual tokenizer and a multi-modal LLM, which is a significant contribution to the field of multi-modal large language models (MLLMs).
- The dynamic visual tokenizer is a novel approach that effectively reduces redundancy and improves computational efficiency, enhancing the representation compatibility of image and text in LLMs.
- The methodology is supported by extensive experiments, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various vision-language tasks, such as image captioning and visual question answering.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing MLLMs.
- The authors provide a comprehensive ablation study, which helps in understanding the impact of different components of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is complex and difficult to follow, especially in sections where the methodology is detailed, such as the token merger and quantization processes.
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on related work, particularly in the area of vector quantization and other recent MLLMs that also use vector quantization.
- The novelty of the dynamic token length is questioned, as similar approaches have been previously explored.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for catastrophic forgetting in the LLM during pre-training, which could affect the performance on text-only tasks.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different datasets and the potential for overfitting due to the complexity of the model.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training data and the process of vector quantization, which could affect the reproducibility and understanding of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role of the U-Net in the pre-training process and whether it is trained on the same dataset as the tokenizer?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of catastrophic forgetting in the LLM during pre-training?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training data, such as the number of samples used for each task and the specific datasets involved?
- How is the vector quantization process performed, and what are the specifics of the codebook size and the number of visual tokens used?
- Can the authors explain the rationale behind the specific design choices in the token merger and quantization processes, particularly how these choices impact the computational efficiency and the quality of the generated visual tokens?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent MLLMs that also use vector quantization, such as BLIP-2 and Emu?
