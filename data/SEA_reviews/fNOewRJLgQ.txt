 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Ophiuchus, a novel SO(3)-equivariant autoencoder designed for protein structure modeling. Ophiuchus leverages a hierarchical coarse-graining approach to efficiently model protein structures, utilizing a combination of self-interaction, sequence convolution, and spatial convolution modules. The model's performance is evaluated through extensive ablation studies, demonstrating its effectiveness in recovering protein structures from coarse representations. Additionally, Ophiuchus employs a denoising diffusion probabilistic model to sample protein structures, showcasing its utility in generating new protein conformations. Despite its strengths, the paper is criticized for its lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art models and insufficient discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of the model.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel SO(3)-equivariant autoencoder that effectively models protein structures through a combination of self-interaction, sequence convolution, and spatial convolution modules.
- Extensive ablation studies are conducted to evaluate the effectiveness of the model, providing insights into the performance of different components.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the model's design and its application to protein modeling.
- The use of a diffusion model in the latent space for protein generation is innovative and could potentially enhance the model's ability to generate diverse and realistic protein structures.
- The paper demonstrates the utility of the learned latent space through experiments on conformational interpolation, showing that the model can effectively capture protein structures.
- The model's performance in terms of computational efficiency and scalability is highlighted, which is crucial for practical applications in protein modeling.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art models in terms of performance metrics and computational efficiency.
- The model's performance on larger proteins or more complex structures is not adequately evaluated, raising concerns about its scalability and applicability to real-world protein modeling challenges.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the model in detail, which could help in understanding its potential drawbacks and areas for improvement.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the computational costs and training times of the model, which are crucial for assessing its practicality and efficiency.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on how the model handles the complexity of protein structures, especially in terms of sequence-structure motifs and modularity.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation of the model's performance on a wider range of protein structures and a more rigorous validation of its results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the model's performance with other state-of-the-art models, including metrics such as computational efficiency, scalability, and structural accuracy?
- How does the model perform on larger proteins or more complex structures, and what are the implications of these results for its practical application in protein modeling?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the model and suggest potential areas for future research or improvement?
- How does the model handle the complexity of protein structures, especially in terms of sequence-structure motifs and modularity?
- Could the authors discuss the computational costs and training times of the model in more detail, and how these compare to other models in the field?
- How does the model ensure that the generated protein structures are chemically valid and physically plausible, and what steps are taken to validate the results?
