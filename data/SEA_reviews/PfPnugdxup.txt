 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel pre-training strategy for molecular property prediction, utilizing a multi-task learning approach across diverse datasets such as QM9, MatBench, and rMD17. This strategy leverages the GemNet-OC model to pretrain on a large corpus of molecular structures, demonstrating significant improvements in fine-tuning performance across various downstream tasks. The approach addresses the challenges of data imbalance and system size imbalance through innovative methods like temperature sampling and structure-wise loss reduction. Despite the significant advancements, the paper is critiqued for its lack of novelty in model design and the limited scope of its experiments, focusing primarily on energy and force predictions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to multi-task learning for molecular property prediction, showcasing a significant improvement in fine-tuning performance across various datasets.
- The methodology effectively addresses data imbalance and system size imbalance, which are critical challenges in molecular modeling.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with comprehensive ablation studies that provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of different components of the proposed method.
- The authors have provided a detailed analysis of the computational requirements of their method, demonstrating its efficiency in terms of training time and resource usage.
- The paper includes a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, comparing it against various baselines and demonstrating its effectiveness in a variety of downstream tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty in its model design, as it primarily utilizes the GemNet-OC model without significant modifications or enhancements.
- The scope of the experiments is limited, focusing primarily on energy and force predictions and not exploring other types of molecular properties.
- There is a lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, such as those using self-supervised learning or other types of neural networks, which could provide a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for bias in the pre-training datasets, which could affect the generalizability and reliability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including its applicability to different types of molecular properties and its scalability to larger, more complex datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly those using self-supervised learning or other types of neural networks?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of molecular properties, such as those related to quantum chemistry or materials science?
- Can the authors discuss the potential for bias in the pre-training datasets and how this might affect the generalizability and reliability of the results?
- How does the proposed method scale to larger, more complex datasets, and what are the implications of this for its applicability in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training times and resource usage for the pre-training phase, as well as the computational requirements for fine-tuning on different datasets?
