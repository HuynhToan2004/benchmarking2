 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DIPS (Data-centric Insights for Pseudo-labeling with Selection), a novel method aimed at enhancing the performance of pseudo-labeling in semi-supervised learning by focusing on the quality of labeled data. DIPS utilizes metrics like confidence and aleatoric uncertainty to select and discard potentially mislabeled or ambiguous samples, thereby improving the overall accuracy and efficiency of pseudo-labeling algorithms. The method is validated across various datasets, including both tabular and image data, demonstrating its effectiveness in real-world scenarios. Despite its strengths in empirical validation and methodological innovation, the paper is critiqued for its lack of novelty in the concept of learning dynamics and the absence of a broader comparative analysis with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in semi-supervised learning, focusing on the quality of labeled data which is often overlooked in existing pseudo-labeling methods.
- The proposed method, DIPS, is model-agnostic and can be applied to various types of data, including tabular and image data, making it versatile and applicable to different domains.
- The empirical results are strong, showing consistent improvements across different datasets and baseline methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-structured presentation that aids in understanding the proposed methodology.
- The use of learning dynamics to characterize samples and the novel selection of samples based on confidence and aleatoric uncertainty is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in the area of learning with noisy labels, which could have provided a better context for the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the method, particularly in the definitions and the mathematical formulations, which could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of computational overhead, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it builds on existing concepts of learning dynamics and uncertainty quantification, which have been previously explored in similar contexts.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and the potential negative societal impacts, which could be critical for understanding the broader implications of the work.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and definition of the selector r in the context of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance on different types of data?
- Can you provide more details on the selection criteria for samples based on confidence and aleatoric uncertainty, and how these criteria are applied in practice?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of noisy labels, particularly in scenarios where the label noise is not uniformly distributed across different classes?
- Could you elaborate on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, especially in applications where data quality and labeling accuracy are critical?
