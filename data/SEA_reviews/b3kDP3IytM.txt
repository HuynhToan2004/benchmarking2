 **Summary:**
The paper introduces KITAB, a new dataset designed to evaluate the constraint satisfaction capabilities of large language models (LLMs) in information retrieval tasks. It presents a dataset of 13,000 queries with constraints related to book metadata, aiming to measure the models' performance in adhering to these constraints. The dataset is constructed using data from WikiData and Open Library, and the experiments conducted on GPT4 and GPT3.5 demonstrate that while models can improve performance with context, they still struggle with constraint satisfaction. The paper also discusses the limitations of current models and provides insights into how these models handle constraints, suggesting potential areas for future research and development.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely research question regarding the capabilities of LLMs in constraint satisfaction tasks, which is highly relevant given the increasing use of LLMs in real-world applications.
- The dataset introduced, KITAB, is well-structured and comprehensive, providing a valuable resource for evaluating LLMs' performance in constraint satisfaction.
- The experimental setup is robust and well-designed, with clear and detailed explanations of the methodology and results.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The findings on the limitations of LLMs in constraint satisfaction tasks are significant and could guide future research and development in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The dataset, while comprehensive, is somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on book metadata and author information. It would be beneficial to see how LLMs perform with different types of constraints, such as those related to images or other forms of data.
- The evaluation of LLMs is limited to GPT4 and GPT3.5, which may not fully represent the capabilities of other LLMs. Including a broader range of models could provide a more comprehensive understanding of LLM performance.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the dataset and the methodology used, which could help readers better understand the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential biases in the dataset, which could impact the reliability and generalizability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the implications of the findings, particularly in terms of how they could be applied to improve LLM performance in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the performance of LLMs on KITAB compare to their performance on other benchmarks, such as factual question answering datasets?
- Could the authors provide more details on the data cleaning process used to ensure the accuracy and reliability of the dataset?
- How do the authors address the potential biases in the dataset, and what steps have been taken to ensure that the findings are not influenced by these biases?
- Given the limitations of the current dataset, what steps are being taken to expand the scope of KITAB to include a broader range of constraints and data types?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential implications of the findings for the development of more effective LLMs in constraint satisfaction tasks?
