 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel hierarchical policy framework for problem-solving using Large Language Models (LLMs), aiming to enhance their exploration capabilities by integrating a visionary high-level leader policy with a low-level follower policy. The high-level policy generates hints and tactics, while the low-level policy executes detailed problem-solving processes. A tournament-based approach is used to select the best reasoning chains from the pool, thereby improving the final answer accuracy on challenging problems. The framework is tested on the MATH dataset, showing promising results. However, concerns about the novelty of the approach, the limited scope of the experiments, and the need for a more rigorous evaluation persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a good balance of intuition and technical details.
- The proposed hierarchical policy framework is novel and effective, enhancing the exploration capabilities of LLMs in problem-solving.
- The tournament-based approach for selecting the best reasoning chains is efficient and effective, improving the final answer accuracy on challenging problems.
- The paper introduces a new metric, "Grouped-Majority Recall," which is a useful tool for evaluating the visibility of correct answers in the solution space.
- The use of a high-level policy to generate hints and tactics for a low-level policy to execute detailed problem-solving processes is a promising approach to improving LLM performance on challenging problems.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through extensive experiments on the MATH dataset, showing that the proposed method can improve the final answer accuracy on challenging problems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with marginal improvements over baselines and limited evaluation on a diverse set of tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the novelty of the approach, as similar hierarchical policies have been previously explored in other domains.
- The paper's focus on mathematical reasoning limits the generalizability of the findings to other types of problems.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential improvements.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to a single dataset and a single task, which does not adequately demonstrate the method's effectiveness across a broader range of problems.

**Questions:**
- How do the authors ensure that the high-level hints generated by the leader policy are both inspiring and meaningful, and how do they measure the quality of these hints?
- Can the authors provide more details on the retrieval process used in the high-level policy, including the dataset used and the metrics for selecting relevant problems?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the low-level policy fails to execute detailed problem-solving processes effectively?
- Could the authors clarify the distinction between the high-level and low-level policies, and provide examples of how these policies are applied in practice?
- How does the proposed method compare to other hierarchical policies in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of the method's limitations, such as the reliance on a specific dataset and the potential for overfitting to that dataset?
