 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Generalized Knowledge Distillation (GKD), a novel approach to knowledge distillation for auto-regressive language models. GKD leverages feedback from the teacher model to train the student model on its self-generated output sequences, thereby addressing the distribution mismatch between training and inference. The methodology involves a combination of supervised and on-policy training, allowing for the use of various divergence measures to optimize the distillation process. GKD has been tested across several tasks including summarization, translation, and reasoning, demonstrating its effectiveness and flexibility. The paper also explores the integration of GKD with reinforcement learning fine-tuning, showcasing its versatility and potential for further research.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, Generalized Knowledge Distillation (GKD), is innovative and addresses the issue of distribution mismatch between training and inference in knowledge distillation, which is crucial for real-world applications.
- GKD introduces a flexible framework that allows for the use of various divergence measures, enhancing its applicability across different tasks and scenarios.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of GKD in various scenarios, including summarization, translation, and reasoning, as well as its integration with reinforcement learning fine-tuning.
- The methodology is well-motivated, with clear explanations of the rationale behind the choices made, such as the use of different divergence measures and the combination of supervised and on-policy training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced perspective and guide future research.
- There is a potential issue with the computational cost of GKD, particularly when using on-policy data, which could be a significant drawback in resource-constrained environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the impact of different hyperparameters on performance.
- The paper could be improved by providing more detailed experimental setups and results, including standard deviations and a more thorough discussion of the experimental design and results.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of GKD over other methods, such as the use of forward KL in summarization, are not convincingly supported by the data presented.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational cost and efficiency with other existing methods, particularly in scenarios where computational resources are limited?
- Can you provide a more detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of GKD?
- How does the performance of GKD vary when using different divergence measures, and what are the practical implications of these choices?
- Could you elaborate on the experimental setup and results, particularly in terms of the choice of hyperparameters and the impact of different divergence measures on performance?
- In the context of the XSum dataset, how does the use of forward KL compare with other divergence measures, and what are the practical implications of these choices?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of train-inference distribution mismatch, and what are the implications of this mismatch on the performance of the distilled models?
