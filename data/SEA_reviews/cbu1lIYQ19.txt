 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the hybrid kernel Stein variational gradient descent (h-SVGD) algorithm, which extends the traditional SVGD method by utilizing different kernels for gradient and repulsive forces. This modification allows for the avoidance of variance collapse in SVGD, a significant issue in high-dimensional settings. The theoretical underpinnings of h-SVGD are analyzed, including its convergence properties and optimization within a specific RKHS. The paper also provides numerical experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of h-SVGD in mitigating variance collapse and maintaining competitive performance in inference tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and understandable, with a clear structure that facilitates comprehension.
- The motivation behind the research is strong, addressing the significant issue of variance collapse in SVGD, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The theoretical analysis provided is comprehensive, including proofs and lemmas that substantiate the claims made about the algorithm's behavior and convergence properties.
- The paper includes detailed numerical experiments that demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness in real-world scenarios, providing empirical evidence to support the theoretical findings.
- The paper contributes to the field by extending the SVGD algorithm to a hybrid kernel setting, which could be beneficial for a variety of applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the theoretical results, particularly in the large particle limit and the continuous time limit, which are currently under-explained.
- The assumption of the target distribution being a log-concave potential is strong and not well-justified, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the computational complexity of the h-SVGD algorithm, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- There is a need for a more comprehensive comparison with other SVGD variants, such as S-SVGD and GSVGD, to establish the superiority or equivalence of h-SVGD.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed exploration of the choice of kernels and their impact on the performance of the algorithm.
- Some minor issues such as typos and inconsistencies in the presentation of the paper need to be addressed for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical results in the large particle limit and the continuous time limit?
- How does the choice of kernels affect the performance of the h-SVGD algorithm, and can the authors provide recommendations for optimal kernel selection?
- Is there a way to compare the computational complexity of h-SVGD with other SVGD variants, such as S-SVGD and GSVGD?
- How does the h-SVGD algorithm perform in terms of convergence rate, and what are the implications of this for practical applications?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of the choice of potential function on the performance of the algorithm, and how this choice affects the theoretical guarantees?
- Is there a possibility to extend the theoretical analysis to more general potential functions, and how might this affect the algorithm's behavior and convergence properties?
