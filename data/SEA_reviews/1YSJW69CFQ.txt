 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for estimating uncertainty in medical image segmentation using a combination of test-time augmentation and Monte Carlo dropout. The proposed method, Uncertainty-Receptive Fusion (URF), adjusts the weight of the loss function during training based on predicted uncertainty estimates. The authors also employ an image acquisition model to consider noise and spatial transformations, which aids in predicting with higher accuracy. The paper's experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in terms of uncertainty estimation and image segmentation accuracy. However, the clarity of the writing and the novelty of the contributions are questioned, with concerns about the method's applicability to other datasets and its overall impact on the field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of medical imaging, focusing on the estimation of uncertainty in segmentation, which is crucial for improving the reliability of machine learning systems in healthcare.
- The proposed method, Uncertainty-Receptive Fusion (URF), is novel and well-explained, providing a clear mathematical foundation for its operation.
- The paper includes comprehensive experimental results, demonstrating the effectiveness of URF in improving segmentation accuracy.
- The use of Monte Carlo dropout for estimating uncertainty is highlighted as a significant contribution, particularly in the context of medical imaging where uncertainty estimation is critical.
- The methodology is supported by detailed mathematical formulations and theoretical explanations, which are well-integrated into the paper.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in the writing and presentation of the methodology. Key concepts are either not adequately explained or are presented in a confusing manner, which could hinder understanding and replication of the results.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation or improvement.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed or robust, particularly in terms of the datasets used and the comparisons made with existing methods.
- The paper's structure and organization are inconsistent, with some sections lacking clarity and detail, while others are overly technical or repetitive.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and operation of the "test-time augmentation" mentioned in the paper?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of noise and spatial transformations in medical images?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the "impromptu" uncertainty and its impact on the segmentation results?
- What are the specific advantages of using URF over other existing methods, particularly in terms of accuracy and reliability?
- How does the paper address the issue of overconfidence in model predictions, and what steps are taken to ensure that the model's uncertainty estimates are reliable?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a discussion on the computational efficiency and scalability of their proposed method?
