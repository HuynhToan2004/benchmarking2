 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SHARC, a novel framework that integrates associative memory with replay-based strategies to address catastrophic forgetting in continual learning. SHARC selectively stores salient data segments using sparse memory encoding, enhancing memory efficiency and recall accuracy. The framework is evaluated across various continual learning tasks and demonstrates improved performance over existing methods. However, the paper's novelty and the necessity of using a frozen backbone for the associative memory module are questioned. Additionally, the method's reliance on a pre-trained backbone and its performance on larger datasets are not adequately addressed.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to continual learning by combining associative memory with replay-based strategies, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The use of saliency-guided sparse memory encoding is innovative and provides a more efficient memory management mechanism.
- The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and includes comprehensive evaluations that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in continual learning tasks.
- The integration of associative memory with replay-based strategies offers a promising approach for improving memory efficiency and recall accuracy in continual learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential directions for future work.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as similar approaches using sparse memory and associative memory have been previously explored.
- The method's performance is heavily dependent on the pre-trained backbone, and it may not perform well on larger datasets such as ImageNet.
- The paper does not adequately address the necessity of using a frozen backbone for the associative memory module and the implications of this choice.
- The experimental setup and comparisons are limited, and the paper could benefit from a broader range of comparisons and benchmarks.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational overhead introduced by the associative memory module, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other associative memory-based methods to clarify the advantages of their approach?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets like ImageNet, and what are the implications of using a frozen backbone for the associative memory module in such scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of using different types of saliency methods on the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, especially in scenarios where the memory buffer is limited?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead introduced by the associative memory module and discuss potential strategies for reducing this overhead?
