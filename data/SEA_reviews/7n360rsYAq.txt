 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to trend filtering using a dynamic trend filtering network (DTF-net) and a reinforcement learning (RL) algorithm. The authors formulate the trend point detection problem as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) and employ a discrete action space to identify dynamic trend points (DTPs) in time series data. These DTPs are used to extract trends by interpolating between them. The DTF-net is trained using a forecasting MSE cost function as a reward, which allows for the consideration of temporal dependencies when capturing DTPs. The paper demonstrates that DTF-net outperforms other trend filtering methods in capturing abrupt changes and enhances performance in forecasting tasks. However, the experimental results are limited to small-scale datasets, and the method's scalability and applicability to larger, more complex datasets are not thoroughly explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the proposed method.
- The problem of trend filtering is well-motivated, and the proposed method is novel, particularly the use of reinforcement learning for trend filtering.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in capturing abrupt changes and enhancing forecasting performance.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the proposed method, including a comprehensive description of the DTF-net and the training process.
- The use of reinforcement learning to identify dynamic trend points (DTPs) is a novel approach that effectively addresses the issue of constant smoothness in traditional trend filtering methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of scalability and applicability to larger, more complex datasets.
- The experimental results are limited to small-scale datasets, which may not adequately demonstrate the method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art methods for trend filtering and anomaly detection.
- There is a lack of clarity in the reward function and its relationship to the loss function, which could be better explained.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training process, particularly how the DTF-net is trained and the impact of hyperparameters on the results.
- The paper could be improved by including more real-world datasets to validate the method's effectiveness in practical scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the scalability and applicability of the proposed method to larger, more complex datasets?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art methods for trend filtering and anomaly detection?
- Can you clarify the role of the reward function in the training process and how it relates to the loss function?
- How sensitive is the method to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the forecasting horizon and the action space?
- Could you provide more details on the training process, including the specifics of how the DTF-net is trained and the impact of different training strategies on the results?
- How does the method perform on larger, more complex datasets, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in applying the method to such datasets?
