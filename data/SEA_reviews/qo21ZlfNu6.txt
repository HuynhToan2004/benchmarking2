 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel attack method termed "neural phishing," which aims to extract sensitive information from large language models (LLMs) by inserting benign-appearing poisoned sentences into the training dataset. The attack is divided into three phases: pretraining, fine-tuning, and inference. The pretraining phase involves crafting poisoned data based on vague priors about the structure of user data. The fine-tuning phase uses the poisoned data to train the model, and the inference phase extracts sensitive information by prompting the model with text similar to the poisoned prefixes. The paper explores the effectiveness of this attack across various model sizes and datasets, demonstrating significant success rates. However, it also highlights the limitations of existing defenses against such attacks and suggests potential mitigation strategies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a timely and relevant issue of privacy risks associated with large language models (LLMs) trained on private data.
- The proposed attack method is novel and practical, requiring only vague priors about the structure of user data and the ability to insert benign-appearing sentences into the training dataset.
- The paper is well-organized, easy to follow, and provides a clear description of the proposed attack, including its implementation and evaluation.
- The experimental setup is comprehensive, covering different model sizes, datasets, and attack parameters, which demonstrates the robustness of the proposed method.
- The paper provides insights into the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can extract sensitive information from LLMs with high success rates.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, such as the assumptions about the adversary's knowledge and capabilities.
- There is a need for more concrete examples and case studies to illustrate the practical implications of the attack, especially in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the ethical implications of the research, particularly concerning the potential misuse of the findings.
- The paper's focus on specific types of sensitive information (e.g., credit card numbers) may not generalize well to other types of sensitive data, which could limit the applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential mitigation strategies against the proposed attack, such as data cleaning or model hardening techniques.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the specifics of the adversary's capabilities, particularly in terms of data access and knowledge of the training dataset?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing defenses against data extraction attacks, and what are the implications for the effectiveness of these defenses?
- Can you provide more details on the ethical considerations of the research, particularly in terms of the potential misuse of the findings?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of sensitive information beyond credit card numbers?
- Could you elaborate on the potential mitigation strategies against the proposed attack, and discuss the feasibility and effectiveness of these strategies?
