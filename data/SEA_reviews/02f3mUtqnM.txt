 **Summary:**
The paper explores a hybrid inference approach aimed at reducing inference costs by assigning queries to smaller models when the quality gap between the smaller and larger models is minimal. It introduces a router that predicts the quality gap between the outputs of smaller and larger models, enabling dynamic adjustment of the quality-cost tradeoff. The router is trained using a novel approach that integrates data transformation to address the imbalance in the dataset. The paper evaluates the router's performance across various scenarios, comparing it to existing baselines and demonstrating potential cost savings. However, concerns about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the experimental setup, and the generalizability of the findings persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem by proposing a novel hybrid inference approach that assigns queries to smaller models when the quality gap between smaller and larger models is minimal, potentially reducing inference costs.
- The introduction of a router that predicts the quality gap between the outputs of smaller and larger models is a significant contribution, providing a dynamic adjustment of the quality-cost tradeoff.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method can effectively route queries to smaller models, reducing inference costs by up to 40% without significant loss in response quality.
- The paper includes a detailed evaluation of the proposed method against several baselines, showing that it outperforms random assignment in all scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the description of the router's operation and the experimental setup. More detailed explanations and examples would enhance understanding.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as similar methods have been previously explored, such as in hybrid inference and query routing.
- The evaluation of the router's performance is limited to a single dataset, which may not fully demonstrate its generalizability across different datasets and models.
- The paper does not adequately address how the router handles out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios, which could significantly impact its practical applicability.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of the work.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential future directions for research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the router's output is used in practice and provide more detailed examples of its operation?
- How does the router handle out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios, and what are the implications for its performance in such cases?
- In the experimental setup, how were the hyperparameters for the router chosen, and what impact does this have on the results?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing approaches, such as those that use multiple LLMs in a cascade or ensemble setup?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the quality gap is estimated and how this affects the router's performance?
- What are the implications of using different evaluation metrics, such as GPT-4 score, and how might this affect the router's performance?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the quality gap between models is significant, and what are the potential impacts on the user experience?
