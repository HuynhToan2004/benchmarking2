 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the LVLM Hallucination Revisor (LURE), a novel post-hoc algorithm designed to rectify object hallucination in Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs). LURE leverages a dataset constructed using GPT-3.5 to generate hallucinatory descriptions, which are then used to fine-tune LVLMs, reducing the occurrence of hallucinations. The algorithm is evaluated against various baselines, demonstrating significant improvements in reducing object hallucination as measured by metrics such as CHAIR. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of object hallucination and provides empirical evidence of its effectiveness across multiple models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations that make it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is straightforward, easy to implement, and does not require additional training data, making it practical for real-world applications.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the factors contributing to object hallucination in LVLMs, including co-occurrence, uncertainty, and object position.
- The proposed method is effective in reducing hallucination in LVLMs, as demonstrated by significant improvements in hallucination metrics.
- The paper is structured well, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings, which are supported by both automated and human evaluations.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in reducing object hallucination in LVLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its generalizability to different types of images and its impact on the performance of the reviser.
- The use of GPT-3.5 for generating hallucinatory descriptions raises concerns about the quality and accuracy of the generated data, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the impact of different prompts on the performance of the reviser.
- The paper's reliance on a single dataset (LLaVA-150k) for constructing the hallucinatory dataset may limit the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the uncertainty score is calculated for the entire generated sequence, especially in terms of its sensitivity to the position of the object in the sequence?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of images, such as those from the COCO dataset, which may have different characteristics from the images used in the LLaVA-150k dataset?
- Could the authors provide more details on the process of generating the hallucinatory dataset using GPT-3.5, particularly how the prompts were chosen and how they might influence the results?
- How does the performance of the reviser vary with different uncertainty thresholds, and what are the implications of these variations for the overall effectiveness of the method?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in terms of how it might affect the accuracy and reliability of the generated descriptions?
