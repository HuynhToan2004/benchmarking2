 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training algorithm called "iterative linearization" for deep neural networks, which interpolates between standard training and linearized training. This method, which involves periodically re-linearizing the network during training, is shown to perform comparably to standard training in some settings, suggesting that it could be a useful tool for understanding feature learning in neural networks. The authors demonstrate that this method can achieve comparable generalization performance to standard SGD with fewer feature updates, thus reducing the computational burden. However, the paper's empirical evaluations are limited to small-scale datasets and simple architectures, which might not generalize well to more complex or larger-scale models. Theoretical analysis is also lacking, which could strengthen the claims made about the algorithm's effectiveness and its connection to second-order optimization methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of the iterative linearization method is innovative and provides a new perspective on understanding feature learning in neural networks.
- The empirical results demonstrate that the iterative linearization method can achieve comparable generalization performance to standard SGD with fewer feature updates, which is both surprising and intriguing.
- The paper provides a comprehensive explanation of the iterative linearization method, including its connection to second-order optimization methods, which is both theoretically sound and practically applicable.
- The empirical evidence presented supports the claims made about the iterative linearization method, showing that it performs well in certain settings and offers insights into the role of feature learning in neural network training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The empirical evaluation of the iterative linearization method is limited to small-scale datasets and simple architectures, which might not generalize well to more complex or larger-scale models.
- The paper lacks a detailed theoretical analysis of the iterative linearization method, which could strengthen the claims made about its effectiveness and its connection to second-order optimization methods.
- The paper's focus on CNNs might limit its applicability to other types of neural networks, such as transformers, which are increasingly popular in current research.
- The paper's claims about the iterative linearization method being a valid learning algorithm are not convincingly supported by empirical evidence, particularly in terms of its ability to converge to a local optimum.
- The paper's discussion on the relationship between the iterative linearization method and the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) is unclear and potentially misleading, particularly in its assertion that the NTK kernel remains constant during training, which is not supported by empirical evidence.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between the iterative linearization method and the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK)? Specifically, how does the NTK kernel evolve during training, and what implications does this have for the generalization performance of the model?
- How does the iterative linearization method perform in more complex or larger-scale models, and what are the implications of its performance in these settings?
- What is the rationale behind choosing a fixed period for the iterative linearization method, and how does this affect the learning process?
- Can the authors provide more detailed theoretical analysis to support the claims made about the iterative linearization method's effectiveness and its connection to second-order optimization methods?
- How does the iterative linearization method compare to other regularization techniques, such as dropout, in terms of its impact on generalization performance?
- Can the authors elaborate on the role of the learning rate in the iterative linearization method and how it affects the learning process?
