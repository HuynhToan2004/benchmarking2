 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an active learning framework for 3D molecular graphs, aimed at reducing annotation costs in molecular learning by querying the most informative samples. The framework employs a diversity sampling method based on 3D graph isometries to eliminate mutual redundancy and a Bayesian geometric graph neural network to compute uncertainties. The methodology is evaluated on the QM9 dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed diversity and uncertainty methods. The paper also explores the use of quadratic programming to select samples with maximal uncertainties and mutual diversity, enhancing the model's performance. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticisms for its clarity in defining key concepts, the complexity of its methodology, and the limited evaluation on only one dataset.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and detailed explanation of the methodology.
- The introduction of 3D graph isometrics for 3D graph isomorphism analysis is novel and provides a new perspective on molecular graph analysis.
- The diversity sampling method based on 3D graph isometrics is innovative and effectively eliminates mutual redundancy, enhancing the diversity of selected samples.
- The Bayesian geometric graph neural network for computing uncertainties is a novel approach that considers the uncertainty of 3D molecular graphs, which is crucial for active learning.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the computational time and complexity of the proposed method, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed diversity and uncertainty methods through experiments on the QM9 dataset, showing promising results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining key concepts such as "mutual redundancy" and "geometric graph isomorphism," which could confuse readers and hinder understanding.
- The methodology is complex, with multiple components (diversity sampling, uncertainty computation, and active sampling) which might be challenging for replication and understanding.
- The paper's reliance on the SchNet model for uncertainty computation might limit its applicability to other 3D GNNs.
- The evaluation is limited to the QM9 dataset, which is relatively small and might not adequately represent the performance of the proposed method on larger, more diverse datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the computational time and the size of the dataset.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its applicability to other types of datasets or tasks beyond molecular property prediction.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition of "mutual redundancy" and how it is calculated in the context of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational complexity and scalability, particularly in terms of the computational time and the size of the dataset?
- Can you provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method and its applicability to other types of datasets or tasks?
- In the context of the diversity sampling method based on 3D graph isometrics, how does the method ensure that the selected samples are truly diverse and not just different in terms of planar graph structure?
- How does the proposed method compare to other active learning methods for molecular graphs, and what are the advantages or disadvantages of using this method over others?
- Could you provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the computational time and the size of the dataset?
- How does the proposed method handle the uncertainty computation in the context of other 3D GNNs, and what are the implications of using different GNNs for uncertainty computation?
