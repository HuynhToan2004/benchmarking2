 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a simple open-loop baseline for reinforcement learning (RL) in locomotion tasks, leveraging biologically inspired oscillators to generate periodic joint motions. This baseline, which optimizes a minimal set of parameters, is compared against several RL algorithms and demonstrates robustness to sensor noise and real-world applications. The methodology involves optimizing the parameters of the oscillators using black-box optimization, aiming to reduce the complexity and computational demands compared to RL methods. The paper also discusses the transferability of the learned policies to real-world robots, showing that the open-loop oscillator can achieve reasonable performance in simulated and real-world environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The use of oscillators to generate periodic joint motions is an innovative approach that is both biologically inspired and computationally efficient.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the open-loop oscillator in simple locomotion tasks, showing comparable performance to more complex RL algorithms.
- The transferability of the learned policy to a real robot is highlighted, showing that the open-loop oscillator can be effective in real-world applications.
- The paper includes a thorough evaluation of the proposed method against various RL baselines, demonstrating the robustness of the open-loop oscillator to sensor noise and other perturbations.
- The method's simplicity and efficiency in terms of computational resources and parameter requirements are highlighted, making it a promising alternative to more complex RL methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison of the computational efficiency of the proposed method with RL baselines, which could be misleading regarding the actual computational demands.
- The method's robustness to sensor noise and other perturbations is not thoroughly explored, and the sensitivity of the learned policy to these factors is not adequately addressed.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the open-loop oscillator, such as its inability to handle complex tasks or adapt to changing environments.
- The transferability of the learned policy to real-world robots is limited by the assumptions made in the simulations, particularly the lack of motor dynamics and the use of a position control interface.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the method might be adapted or extended to handle more complex tasks or environments.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the computational efficiency of the proposed method compared to RL baselines? How does the proposed method perform in terms of computational resources required?
- How does the open-loop oscillator perform when trained in the presence of sensor noise or other perturbations? Is there a way to mitigate the sensitivity of the learned policy to these factors?
- How does the method handle complex tasks or environments where the oscillator approach might not be sufficient? Are there plans to extend the method to handle more complex tasks?
- Can you provide more details on the transferability of the learned policy to real-world robots? How do the assumptions made in the simulations affect the applicability of the method in real-world scenarios?
- Is there a possibility to combine the open-loop oscillator with RL methods to create a hybrid controller that leverages the strengths of both approaches?
