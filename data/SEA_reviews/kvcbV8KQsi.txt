 **Summary:**
The paper explores the existence and interpretation of "successor heads" in large language models (LLMs), focusing on how these heads handle incremental tasks like predicting the next number in a sequence. The authors introduce a methodology using sparse autoencoders to decompose token representations, identifying a set of "mod-10 features" that are common across different architectures and scales of LLMs. These features are used to understand the behavior of successor heads, which are shown to be present in both small and large models. The paper also investigates the polysemantic behavior of these heads, suggesting that they can predict not just the next number but also acronyms or previous numbers in the context. The findings contribute to the mechanistic interpretability of LLMs and provide insights into the internal workings of these models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant topic, the mechanistic interpretability of large language models (LLMs), which is crucial for understanding the inner workings of these models.
- The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their methodology, including the use of sparse autoencoders to decompose token representations and identify "mod-10 features."
- The paper is well-written, with a clear structure and logical flow, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The findings on the existence and behavior of successor heads in LLMs are novel and interesting, providing new insights into the mechanisms of these models.
- The paper demonstrates the importance of successor heads in incrementation-based tasks, showing that they can predict the next number in a numbered list of items.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more experiments to demonstrate the generalizability of the findings beyond the specific successor head studied.
- The term "successor heads" is misleading as it implies that the heads in question are responsible for the successor behavior of the model, but the paper does not convincingly argue this point.
- The paper's focus on a specific type of attention head (L12H0) may limit the generalizability of the findings, as other attention heads might behave differently.
- The paper's claims about the universality of successor heads across different model architectures and scales are not sufficiently supported by the data presented.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the study, particularly the generalizability of the findings to other models and tasks.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the experimental setups and results, especially in the appendix where more detailed discussions are expected.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify whether the mod-10 features are only present in the MLP0 layer or if they exist in other layers as well?
- How do the authors justify the claim that successor heads are responsible for the successor behavior of the model, given the findings from other studies?
- What are the implications of the successor heads' behavior on the interpretability of LLMs, and how do these findings contribute to a deeper understanding of the models' inner workings?
- Can the authors provide more examples or evidence to support the claim that successor heads are not only present in small models but also in larger models?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of their study, such as the generalizability of the findings to other models and tasks?
