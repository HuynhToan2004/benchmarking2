 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Quantile-Free Regression (QFR), a novel approach to constructing prediction intervals for regression tasks. QFR aims to address the limitations of traditional quantile regression by not requiring the a priori specification of quantiles. Instead, it optimizes the loss function to directly output the interval bounds, improving flexibility and potentially achieving better coverage probabilities. The method is evaluated against various baselines on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in terms of coverage and interval width. The paper also discusses the theoretical aspects of QFR, including its asymptotic coverage guarantees and the convexity of the loss function.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the problem and the proposed solution, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- The proposed method, Quantile-Free Regression (QFR), is simple, effective, and provides a novel approach to constructing prediction intervals without the need for a priori specification of quantiles.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of QFR in achieving good coverage and interval width, showing that it outperforms other methods in terms of coverage and interval sharpness.
- The paper provides a thorough review of existing methods and compares QFR with these, highlighting its advantages and potential contributions to the field.
- The method's theoretical analysis is robust, including proofs of asymptotic coverage guarantees and the convexity of the loss function, which supports the method's theoretical soundness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the QFR method compared to other methods like quantile regression.
- There is a need for more clarity in the presentation of the method's advantages over existing methods, particularly in terms of how QFR achieves better coverage and interval width simultaneously.
- The experimental setup and results could be improved, particularly in the presentation of results and the clarity of the experimental design.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation of the method's performance on datasets with different noise distributions and a more rigorous comparison with other methods.
- There is a lack of discussion on the practical implications of the method, such as its applicability in real-world scenarios and the potential impact of hyperparameter choices on the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison of the computational complexity of QFR with other methods like quantile regression?
- How does the method perform in terms of coverage and interval width when applied to datasets with different noise distributions?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of hyperparameters in the method's performance?
- How does the method handle the trade-off between coverage and interval width, and what are the practical implications of this trade-off in different scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the method's advantages in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability compared to existing methods?
