 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach called Physics Informed Distillation (PID), which leverages the connection between diffusion models and ODEs to distill a single-step diffusion model from a pre-trained teacher model. The method employs a PINN-inspired approach to learn the trajectory of the ODE system, which is then used to generate images in a single step. The paper presents results on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet 64x64, showing that PID can achieve competitive FID scores with fewer NFEs compared to some training-free methods. However, the method's novelty and performance relative to existing methods like DSNO and CD are questioned, as well as its practicality due to the high computational cost of training.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a good introduction to the problem.
- The proposed method is simple and intuitive, with a clear theoretical justification for the Physics Informed Distillation (PID) loss.
- The paper presents competitive results on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet 64x64, showing that the proposed method can achieve comparable or better performance than some existing methods.
- The use of the LPIPS metric for loss calculation is innovative and could potentially improve the model's performance.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the method's performance with respect to different hyperparameters, such as discretization number and distance metrics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited as it primarily applies the PINN framework to distill a single-step diffusion model, which has been explored in previous works.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance metrics like FID and IS scores.
- The computational cost of training the model is high, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its practical deployment.
- The theoretical justification for the PID loss and its connection to the Euler solver is not convincingly explained, and the theoretical underpinnings of the method are not thoroughly discussed.
- The paper does not include qualitative results, which could help in better understanding the generated images and their quality.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the training cost, such as the training time per iteration and the total training time?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of FID and IS scores with the state-of-the-art methods, particularly on larger datasets like ImageNet 256x256?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of the LPIPS metric for loss calculation and discuss its impact on the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets like ImageNet 256x256, and what are the implications of using a higher resolution for the teacher model?
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison with other methods, including DSNO and CD, to better understand the relative performance of their method?
- What are the potential limitations of the proposed method, and how do these affect its practical deployment?
