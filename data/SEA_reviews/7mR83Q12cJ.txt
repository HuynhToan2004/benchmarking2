 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel data augmentation method, COCOA, for estimating Conditional Average Treatment Effects (CATE) using contrastive learning. This method involves training a classifier to identify similar individuals based on outcome proximity and then imputing counterfactual outcomes for these individuals using a local regression model. Theoretical analysis and empirical studies on various datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of COCOA in improving CATE estimation models' performance. The approach is model-agnostic, and the methodology is supported by both theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence. However, the paper is critiqued for its lack of clarity in certain sections, insufficient discussion on related works, and potential limitations in the practical applicability of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method of using contrastive learning to identify similar individuals, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- Theoretical analysis and empirical studies are well-presented, providing a robust foundation for the proposed method.
- The methodology is model-agnostic, making it applicable across various CATE estimation models.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and its implications.
- The proposed method demonstrates effectiveness in improving the performance of CATE estimation models, as shown through both theoretical analysis and empirical results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the explanation of the contrastive learning module and the rationale behind the choice of similarity measures.
- There is a notable absence of discussion on related works, which could have enriched the context and comparison of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the method, such as the need for sufficient similar individuals in the dataset and the impact of the choice of hyperparameters on performance.
- The empirical results are somewhat limited, with only a few datasets used for evaluation, which could raise questions about the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's theoretical analysis does not include the use of contrastive learning, which is a critical component of the method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the choice of similarity measure and its rationale, especially in comparison to other possible measures like Euclidean distance in the feature space?
- How does the choice of the number of neighbors (K) and the size of the epsilon-ball (R) affect the performance of the method? Could more detailed ablation studies be provided to better understand these trade-offs?
- Could the authors discuss the implications of using different kernels in Gaussian processes and how this might affect the performance of the method?
- How does the method perform when the dataset does not have sufficient similar individuals, and what are the potential impacts on the accuracy and reliability of the imputed counterfactual outcomes?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the method's theoretical underpinnings, particularly the role of contrastive learning in the method's effectiveness?
