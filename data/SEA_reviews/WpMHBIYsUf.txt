 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Homeomorphic Model Transformation (HMT) aimed at adapting initial weights based on pre-trained weights, preserving performance when layers are modified. This approach significantly reduces training time and enhances network performance. The methodology involves a model tree search algorithm to optimize model structures automatically, which has been tested across various object detection tasks. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation scope, focusing primarily on object detection and not generalizing to other tasks like segmentation or classification. The method's scalability and applicability to larger, more complex models are also questioned, as well as its theoretical underpinnings and the depth of its experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental results are clearly presented.
- The idea of Homeomorphic Model Transformation (HMT) is innovative and potentially useful, allowing for the adaptation of initial weights based on pre-trained weights, which preserves the performance of the basic model.
- The methodology of HMT is simple and can be applied to various object detection models, enhancing the performance of these models.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the training time and precision (AP50) comparison between the conventional solution and the proposed HMT solution, demonstrating the effectiveness of HMT in reducing training time and enhancing performance.
- The methodology is simple and can be applied to various object detection models, which is a significant advantage in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's evaluation is limited to object detection tasks and does not generalize to other tasks such as segmentation or classification, which could limit the practical applicability of the proposed method.
- The method's scalability and applicability to larger, more complex models are not thoroughly explored, which could limit its real-world impact.
- The paper lacks a theoretical analysis and detailed explanation of the HMT method, which could enhance the understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The experimental validation is not comprehensive, with limited datasets and models used, which could affect the reliability and generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not discuss related works in depth, particularly those that address similar issues, which could provide valuable context and comparative analysis.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is essential for ethical considerations.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the mathematical definition of "Homeomorphism" in the context of their method and provide a clearer explanation of how it contributes to the overall methodology?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of training time and performance when applied to larger, more complex models?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed theoretical analysis and explanation of the HMT method to enhance the understanding of its workings?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of training time, performance, and scalability?
- Could the authors consider expanding the experimental validation to include more diverse datasets and models to enhance the reliability and generalizability of the findings?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of weight mismatch that arises from modifying some layers in object detection networks?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their method and propose mitigation strategies to address these concerns?
