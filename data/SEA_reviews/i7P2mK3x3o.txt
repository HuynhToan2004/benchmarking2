 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for computing optimal transport (OT) maps using flow-based models, specifically a neural ODE model termed Q-flow. This model aims to minimize the KL divergence between the terminal distribution and a target distribution, while also enforcing a Wasserstein-2 regularization to ensure the smoothness of the flow. The model is trained on pairs of distributions, with one set used to initialize the flow and the other to learn the OT map. The paper presents experiments on both synthetic and real data, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various applications such as image-to-image translation and density ratio estimation. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of theoretical analysis, unclear presentation, and insufficient comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in optimal transport (OT) by introducing a novel flow-based model, Q-flow, which is both innovative and potentially impactful.
- The method is clearly presented, making it accessible to readers, and the experiments are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed model.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear structure that makes it enjoyable to read.
- The authors provide a detailed discussion of the algorithm's computational complexity, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the model.
- The paper includes a variety of experiments on both synthetic and real data, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed model in various applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough theoretical analysis, particularly in the areas of convergence and complexity analysis, which are crucial for understanding the robustness and efficiency of the proposed model.
- There is a notable absence of a comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which makes it difficult to assess the model's relative performance.
- The presentation of the paper needs improvement, particularly in the clarity and organization of the content, especially in the preliminaries section.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the proposed method, which is a significant concern given the complexity of the model.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the regularization techniques used, particularly the Wasserstein-2 regularization, and their impact on the model's performance.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the term "continuous time" as used in the context of your model? How does it differ from a regular ODE, and what are the implications for the model's performance and computational complexity?
- In the context of the experiments, particularly those involving image-to-image translation, how does the proposed model compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance metrics like FID?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of invertibility in the flow, especially in high-dimensional spaces where the model's performance might be limited?
- Can you provide more details on the regularization techniques used, particularly the Wasserstein-2 regularization, and how it affects the model's performance and computational complexity?
- How does the proposed method perform when the initial flow is not optimal, and what are the implications for the model's convergence and accuracy?
- Could you discuss the limitations of the proposed method and provide examples or scenarios where it might not be applicable or effective?
