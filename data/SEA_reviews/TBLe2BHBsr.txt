 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach, the Dilated Convolution Neural Operator (DCNO), for solving multiscale partial differential equations (PDEs) using a combination of dilated convolutions and Fourier layers. DCNO is designed to capture both high-frequency and low-frequency features while maintaining computational efficiency. The method is tested on various PDEs, including elliptic, Navier-Stokes, and Helmholtz equations, demonstrating its effectiveness in preserving high-frequency information and achieving good accuracy-computation trade-offs. The paper also discusses the challenges of high-frequency component preservation in neural operators and presents an ablation study to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in neural operators, specifically the challenge of preserving high-frequency components, which is a crucial aspect of multiscale PDEs.
- The proposed method, DCNO, is novel and effectively captures both high-frequency and low-frequency features, offering a promising solution for multiscale operator learning.
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation and detailed experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of DCNO in various PDE problems.
- The methodology is well-explained, and the paper includes a comprehensive ablation study and detailed experimental results, which support the claims made.
- The paper contributes to the understanding of how dilated convolution can be used to solve multiscale PDEs, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and challenges of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of PDEs and its scalability to higher dimensions.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the specific challenges of preserving high-frequency information in neural operators and how the proposed method addresses these challenges.
- There is a need for more clarity in the presentation of the methodology, particularly in the description of the dilated convolution layers and the role of different components in the network.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical justification for the choice of hyperparameters, such as the dilation rates, which could affect the performance of the model.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including a broader range of PDE problems and varying the noise levels to better understand the robustness of the model.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations and challenges of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of PDEs and its scalability to higher dimensions?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods, such as the Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) and Hierarchical Attention Neural Operator (HANO), in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of different components in the network, such as the dilated convolution layers and the Fourier layers, on the overall performance of the model?
- How were the hyperparameters, such as the dilation rates, chosen, and what is the theoretical justification for these choices?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of PDE problems and the noise levels used in the experiments?
