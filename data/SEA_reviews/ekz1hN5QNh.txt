 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel fully hyperbolic convolutional neural network (HCNN) designed to operate entirely in the hyperbolic space, leveraging the Lorentz model to replace traditional Euclidean layers like convolutional and batch normalization layers. This approach is claimed to enhance the robustness of models against adversarial attacks and potentially improve classification accuracy on benchmark datasets such as CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. However, the paper's claims are met with skepticism due to the lack of detailed experimental evidence and a clear demonstration of the advantages of the proposed methods over existing approaches. The paper also suffers from issues in presentation clarity and the need for more comprehensive ablation studies to substantiate its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by proposing a fully hyperbolic convolutional neural network (HCNN), which is a significant advancement in the field of hyperbolic neural networks (HNNs).
- The authors have provided detailed mathematical formulations for various components of the HCNN, including the Lorentz convolutional layer, Lorentz batch normalization, and Lorentz MLR classifier, which are crucial for understanding the theoretical underpinnings of the model.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and includes a thorough discussion on the challenges of extending Euclidean architectures to the hyperbolic domain.
- The authors have conducted experiments on various benchmarks such as CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and TinyImageNet, demonstrating the potential of the proposed method in improving classification accuracy.
- The paper also explores the use of the Lorentz model, which is known for its numerical stability, as an alternative to the Poincaré model, potentially offering advantages in terms of robustness and performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed experimental evidence to support its claims, particularly in terms of the robustness of the proposed methods against adversarial attacks and the superiority of the HCNN over existing approaches.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in the clarity of figures and the organization of content. Some sections, such as the introduction and the discussion on adversarial robustness, are not well-explained or presented in a clear manner.
- The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, especially in terms of how the Lorentz model contributes to the performance improvements observed.
- There are several typographical errors and unclear formulations throughout the paper, which detract from its professional quality and clarity.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of the Lorentz model over the Poincaré model are not well-supported, and the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage of the proposed methods over existing approaches.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental evidence to support the claims made about the robustness of the proposed methods against adversarial attacks?
- How does the use of the Lorentz model specifically contribute to the performance improvements observed in the experiments?
- Could the authors clarify the typographical errors and unclear formulations noted in the paper to improve its professional quality?
- In terms of the experimental setup, can the authors provide more details on the training procedures and hyperparameters used, especially in comparison to existing approaches?
- How do the authors address the concerns regarding the clarity and organization of the paper, particularly in the introduction and discussion sections?
