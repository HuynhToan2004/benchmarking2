 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CHATS, a novel approach to generating spoken dialogues from written dialogues. CHATS leverages a dual-tower Transformer architecture to handle both content and pitch streams, enabling the generation of backchannels and laughter without requiring transcriptions. The model is evaluated using a variety of metrics, including automatic phoneme error rate (PER), backchannel frequency, and duration, and turn-taking event distribution. The results show that CHATS outperforms a baseline TTS system in terms of dialogue naturalness and meaningfulness, although it struggles with sound quality. The paper also discusses the challenges in handling overlapping speech and the impact of data augmentation on performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel dual-tower Transformer architecture that effectively handles both content and pitch streams, enhancing the generation of spoken dialogues.
- The approach is original, utilizing a turn-taking mechanism to predict the timing of spoken dialogues, which is crucial for generating natural-sounding conversations.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the characteristics of generated spoken dialogues, comparing them to human dialogues.
- The proposed method outperforms the baseline TTS system in terms of dialogue naturalness and meaningfulness, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear presentation of the proposed method, its implementation, and the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the methodology used for handling overlapping speech and the choice of datasets.
- The performance of the proposed method, especially in terms of sound quality, is not convincingly superior to existing methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of data scarcity, which is crucial for training a generative model like CHATS.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and generalizability to different languages and domains.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough evaluation, including a broader range of metrics and a larger test set to better assess the performance of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the methodology used for handling overlapping speech and how it compares to existing methods?
- How does the choice of dataset impact the performance of the proposed method, and what steps were taken to ensure the diversity and quality of the data used?
- In terms of data scarcity, how does the proposed method perform when trained on smaller datasets, and what are the implications for its scalability and generalizability?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and inference procedures for the IPU classifier, particularly how it handles ambiguous cases?
- How does the proposed method handle the generation of laughter and other non-verbal cues, and what impact does this have on the overall quality of the generated dialogues?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their work and how they plan to address these concerns?
