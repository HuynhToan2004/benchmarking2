 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel sampling method for training neural implicit surfaces, aiming to improve training efficiency by reducing the number of samples required. The method involves a double-sampling strategy to detect thin intervals of surface edges, followed by fitting the density functions of these intervals using cumulative distribution functions (CDFs) with bounded errors. This approach is claimed to reduce sample counts by up to 10 times while maintaining theoretical error bounds. The method is evaluated across various datasets and shows improvements in training time and geometry reconstruction details. However, the paper suffers from presentation issues, including inconsistent notation, unclear figures, and a lack of comprehensive evaluation metrics. The method's applicability is also limited to specific types of implicit SDFs, which might restrict its broader adoption.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel sampling method for training neural implicit surfaces, which reduces the number of samples required while maintaining theoretical error bounds.
- The method is evaluated on multiple datasets and shows improvements in training time and geometry reconstruction details.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, providing a clear motivation and detailed methodology.
- The use of a double-sampling strategy to detect the thin intervals of surface edges containing all valid samples is a significant contribution.
- The method is applicable to all neural implicit surfaces with equations mapping SDF values to density values under the volume rendering framework.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation, particularly in terms of evaluation metrics and the inclusion of more datasets like ShapeNet and ModelNet.
- The presentation of the paper needs improvement, with inconsistent notation and unclear figures, especially in Figure 1.
- The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies or comparisons with other sampling methods, which could better demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The method's applicability is limited to specific types of implicit SDFs, which might restrict its broader adoption.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities and challenges.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the specific mapping function used in the experiments, particularly in Figure 1?
- How does the proposed method compare to other sampling methods in terms of efficiency and accuracy?
- Could the authors include more datasets in the evaluation, such as ShapeNet and ModelNet, to demonstrate the generalizability of the method?
- What is the effect of the double-sampling strategy on the training time and geometry reconstruction details, particularly in scenarios where the beta value is small?
- Could the authors clarify the notation and presentation in Figure 1 to better align with the textual descriptions?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential issues of over-smoothing and under-smoothing in the interpolation process?
