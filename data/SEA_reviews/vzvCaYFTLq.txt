 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Sapling, a novel method for model compression in large language models (LLMs) that utilizes layer dropping to adapt models to specific knowledge domains, enhancing inference speed and reducing memory consumption. This method, inspired by knowledge localization, is designed to be hardware-agnostic and does not require specialized kernels for inference. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of Sapling through experiments on domain-specific datasets, showcasing its potential to achieve significant inference speedup and memory savings. The methodology is validated through empirical studies, including ablation studies and comparisons with other compression techniques.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow content, and includes a comprehensive review of related work.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments to validate their claims, including ablation studies and comparisons with various baseline methods.
- The proposed method is innovative and practical, requiring no specialized hardware or computational kernels, making it accessible for deployment on consumer-level hardware.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing quantization and pruning methods, highlighting its advantages in terms of inference speed and memory consumption.
- The method is supported by empirical studies that show significant improvements in inference speed and memory savings compared to other compression methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational overhead of the proposed method, particularly the time complexity of fine-tuning.
- The methodology section could benefit from more clarity and intuitive explanations, especially for non-experts in the field.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for reduced generalization ability and the impact of layer dropping on model performance.
- There is a lack of detailed information on the hardware and software configurations used in the experiments, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation on a wider range of datasets and hardware platforms to validate the method's effectiveness across different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational overhead of the proposed method, including the time complexity of fine-tuning?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency with other compression techniques, especially when considering the overhead of fine-tuning?
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancy in performance reported between the baseline models and those after layer dropping, as shown in Table 2?
- Is there a possibility to apply the proposed method to pre-trained models, and how might this affect the model's generalization ability?
- How does the method perform when applied to different hardware platforms, and what are the implications for the model's performance and efficiency?
