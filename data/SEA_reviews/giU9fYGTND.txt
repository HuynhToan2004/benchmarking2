 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedImpro, a novel approach to addressing client drift in federated learning (FL) by decoupling the model into high-level and low-level parts. This decoupling allows for the training of high-level parts on reconstructed feature distributions, promoting generalization and reducing gradient dissimilarity. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that the generalization contribution of local training is bounded by the conditional Wasserstein distance between clients' data distributions. The paper also proposes a method for estimating feature distributions with privacy protection, which is tested across various datasets. Despite the innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its experimental setup, specifically the use of a synthetic dataset and a limited range of non-IID degrees, which raises concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with a clear structure and logical flow, making it easy to follow.
- The proposed method is novel and well-motivated, with a theoretical analysis that supports the approach.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear introduction and a detailed explanation of the methodology.
- The method is technically sound and offers a novel perspective on improving generalization in federated learning.
- The experiments are well-designed, providing a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the theoretical results, particularly in the appendix.
- The experimental setup is somewhat limited, focusing on a synthetic dataset and a narrow range of non-IID degrees, which may not adequately reflect real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a comparison with some existing methods, such as FedDyn, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- There is a need for more extensive experiments to validate the method's effectiveness across different datasets and non-IID degrees.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of data and network architectures.
- The privacy implications of the method are not thoroughly discussed, which is crucial given the sensitive nature of federated learning.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the hyperparameter 'a' in the experiments?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to datasets with more classes, such as CIFAR-100?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other existing methods, such as FedDyn, to better understand the relative performance of the proposed method?
- What are the specific privacy risks associated with the method, and how does the paper address these concerns?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with a higher degree of non-IID data, and what are the implications for generalization performance?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the method in more detail, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of data and network architectures?
