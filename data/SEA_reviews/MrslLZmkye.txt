 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for Out-of-Distribution (OoD) detection using a Wasserstein-score-based generative adversarial training scheme. This approach involves a generator that explores OoD spaces and generates synthetic OoD samples, while a discriminator utilizes these samples to separate InD and OoD in the Wasserstein score space. The method is supported by theoretical guarantees and demonstrates superior performance in various computer vision datasets. The paper also explores the use of real OoD samples for supervised generation of OoD samples, aiming to improve the accuracy of OoD detection and enhance generalizability. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its limited novelty, as it heavily relies on existing methods and lacks comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art techniques.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue in the field of Out-of-Distribution (OoD) detection, which is crucial for ensuring the reliability of machine learning models in real-world applications.
- The method proposed is well-motivated, technically sound, and supported by theoretical guarantees, which enhance its credibility and applicability.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to understand, with clear and concise explanations of the proposed methodologies and results.
- The experimental results are robust and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over state-of-the-art techniques in various computer vision datasets.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear and detailed descriptions of the proposed methodologies, theoretical results, and experimental setups, which facilitate reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is somewhat limited as it heavily relies on existing works, such as the WOOD detector and GANs, without sufficiently distinguishing its contributions from these prior works.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art techniques, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, which could affect the practical applicability of the proposed method.
- The experimental setup and results are somewhat limited, focusing primarily on small-scale datasets and not sufficiently addressing the challenges posed by larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting to out-of-distribution (OoD) data, which is a critical concern in real-world applications where the OoD data distribution may not be well-known or representative.
- The theoretical results, while useful, do not provide a clear intuitive understanding of the method's operation or its advantages over existing methods.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential challenges in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method differs from existing methods, particularly in terms of its theoretical contributions and practical implications?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of overfitting to OoD data, especially in scenarios where the OoD data distribution is not well-known or representative?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art techniques, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle more complex datasets or to incorporate additional data modalities, such as text or audio?
- How does the method perform when the OoD data distribution is significantly different from the training data distribution, and what are the implications of this in practical applications?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
