 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, Mixture of Low-rank Experts (MoLE), for enhancing human-centric text-to-image generation, focusing on the refinement of facial and hand details. The authors propose a three-stage training process, utilizing a custom-collected dataset of over one million high-quality human-centric images and close-up images of faces and hands. The MoLE method leverages low-rank modules trained on these datasets to refine the generation process, demonstrating superior performance over existing methods in benchmarks such as COCO Human Prompts and DiffusionDB Human Prompts. The paper also discusses the limitations of the approach and provides a comprehensive evaluation, including user studies, to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in human-centric text-to-image generation, focusing on the refinement of facial and hand details, which are often underrepresented in current models.
- The methodology is well-motivated and clearly articulated, with a focus on the use of low-rank modules trained on specialized datasets to enhance the generation process.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation, both quantitatively and qualitatively, using metrics such as Human Preference Score (HPS) and ImageReward (IR), as well as user studies to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The dataset collection and curation are detailed, providing a valuable resource for the research community.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and figures that aid in understanding the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on facial and hand details, which may not fully address the broader issue of human-centric image generation, as it does not consider other body parts or scenarios involving multiple individuals.
- The novelty of the MoLE method is questionable, as similar approaches using low-rank modules for image refinement have been previously explored.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, such as VQ-Diffusion and Versatile Diffusion, which could provide a clearer picture of its advantages.
- There are some inconsistencies and potential errors in the figures and text, such as the alignment of figures and the clarity of the text, which could affect the reader's understanding.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability and generalization of the MoLE method to other types of images or scenarios beyond human-centric generation.

**Questions:**
- How does the MoLE method perform in scenarios involving multiple individuals, and what are the implications for the refinement of details in such cases?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the human-centric dataset in the MoLE method, particularly in terms of its contribution to the refinement of details in generated images?
- Is there a possibility to adapt the MoLE method to other types of images or scenarios, such as medical images or other specific use cases?
- How does the MoLE method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements?
- Could the authors provide more details on the user studies conducted, including the sample size, demographics, and statistical analysis methods used?
