 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Continuous Length EXtrapolation (CLEX), a novel method for enhancing the context window in large language models (LLMs) by generalizing Position Embedding (PE) scaling approaches using Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs). CLEX enables fine-grained extension to long contexts and facilitates length extrapolation, maintaining performance even when extended beyond the training sequence length. This approach is validated through experiments on the LLaMA-2-7B model, showing improvements in performance on the LongBench benchmark. However, concerns are raised regarding the clarity of the paper, the need for more comprehensive experiments, and the potential impact of the method on different model sizes and architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive discussion of related work.
- The proposed CLEX method is simple, efficient, and effective, with promising experimental results on the LLaMA-2-7B model.
- The idea of using a neural ODE to model the continuous dynamics of position embedding is innovative and effectively extends the context window of LLMs.
- CLEX is a drop-in component for RoPE-based LLMs, requiring minimal training and inference latency.
- The paper includes thorough experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of CLEX on the LongBench benchmark.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the need to extend the context window beyond the training sequence length, especially when the model is already large.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of CLEX, particularly in scenarios where it might not be as effective.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experiments to validate the claims made about the method's effectiveness and generalizability.
- The paper could be clearer in its presentation, particularly in the explanation of the ODE used and the methodology for sampling t.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of CLEX on different model sizes and architectures, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper could provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the training data used and the hyperparameters employed.

**Questions:**
- How does CLEX perform on different model sizes, especially larger models like LLaMA-2-13B?
- Can CLEX be applied to other model architectures, such as those using different position embedding methods?
- How does the performance of CLEX vary with different sampling strategies for t?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the training data used and the hyperparameters employed?
- How does CLEX compare to other methods in terms of the length of the context window it can effectively handle?
- Is there a possibility to combine CLEX with other methods to further enhance its performance?
- Could you clarify the methodology for sampling t and the impact of different sampling strategies on the results?
- How does CLEX handle the inconsistency between the frequency basis and position indices during training?
- Can you discuss the potential impact of CLEX on different types of tasks, such as generation and reasoning?
