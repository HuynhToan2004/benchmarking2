 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to investigating factual errors in Large Language Models (LLMs) by modeling factual queries as Constraint Satisfaction Problems (CSPs). This methodology allows for a systematic analysis of how LLMs handle factual constraints, focusing on attention patterns to predict factual errors. The authors propose a method called SAT Probe, which predicts constraint satisfaction using attention patterns, and demonstrate its effectiveness through extensive experiments across multiple datasets and LLM scales. The paper also discusses the implications of model size and attention patterns on factual error prediction, showing that larger models and more attention to constraints can improve accuracy.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear and concise structure that effectively communicates the authors' ideas.
- The introduction of the SAT Probe method is innovative and provides a novel approach to predicting factual errors in LLMs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the SAT Probe method, showing its ability to predict factual errors and provide insights into the internal mechanisms of LLMs.
- The use of attention patterns to predict factual errors is a novel and interesting approach, offering a new perspective on how LLMs process and generate factually incorrect text.
- The paper provides a systematic analysis of factual errors in LLMs, which is an important and relevant topic in the current landscape of AI research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of queries and its scalability to larger models or more complex constraints.
- The reliance on exact match as a verification method could introduce noise and bias into the evaluation, potentially affecting the accuracy of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the SAT Probe method to other types of constraints or factual errors, which could limit its practical utility.
- There is a lack of discussion on the societal impact of the findings, particularly in terms of how the insights into LLM's internal mechanisms could be used to mitigate factual errors in real-world applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing methods and a deeper exploration of the relationship between attention and factual errors, especially in terms of how this relates to the training data and model size.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition of "popularity" in the context of the paper and how it relates to the frequency of entities in training data?
- How does the SAT Probe method perform when applied to different types of constraints, such as disjunctive or negated constraints?
- Can you provide more details on how the SAT Probe method could be adapted to handle more complex or varied types of factual errors?
- How does the paper address the issue of potential noise introduced by the use of exact match as a verification method?
- Could you discuss the potential societal implications of the findings, particularly in terms of how the insights into LLM's internal mechanisms could be used to improve factual accuracy in real-world applications?
- How does the SAT Probe method compare to other existing methods for detecting and mitigating factual errors in LLMs?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the datasets used and the methodology for training the SAT Probe model?
