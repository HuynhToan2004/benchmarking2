 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the effectiveness of pre-training versus meta-learning (MAML) in few-shot learning, employing a rigorous statistical tool, Cohen's d, to measure effect sizes. It compares these methods across diverse datasets, demonstrating that pre-training is more effective in low diversity settings, while MAML excels in high diversity settings. The authors also introduce a novel metric, the diversity coefficient, to assess dataset diversity, which is critical for understanding the performance of these methods. Despite the comprehensive empirical analysis, the paper's contribution is seen as limited by its focus on a single meta-learning method (MAML) and its lack of novelty in statistical analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a rigorous statistical analysis using Cohen's d to determine the practical significance of the difference between a model trained with pre-training versus a MAML model, which is a significant improvement over previous work.
- The introduction of a novel metric, the diversity coefficient, to compute the average formal diversity of a dataset, adds depth to the analysis and provides a new perspective on the evaluation of few-shot learning methods.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex statistical concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The empirical study is extensive, covering a wide range of datasets and models, which strengthens the conclusions drawn from the analysis.
- The paper offers a comprehensive and fair comparison between pre-trained models and MAML models by using the same architecture and training all models to convergence.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is limited by its focus on a single meta-learning method (MAML) and its lack of novelty in statistical analysis, which may not sufficiently advance the understanding or application of meta-learning in practice.
- The paper's experimental results show that the performance of pre-training and MAML is comparable, which contradicts the claim that pre-training is always better than MAML.
- The paper's conclusion that the formal diversity of a dataset is a driving factor and that when considering all datasets diversities there is no difference is not surprising and lacks significant novelty.
- The use of Cohen's d as a measure of effect size is not new and has been previously discussed in literature, which questions the novelty of the statistical analysis presented.
- The paper's reliance on a single statistical tool, Cohen's d, and a single decision rule based on this tool, may not provide a comprehensive understanding of the performance differences between pre-training and MAML across different datasets and scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can you explain why the performance of pre-training and MAML is comparable as shown in the experimental results?
- What is the rationale behind the decision to use Cohen's d as the primary measure of effect size, and how does it contribute to a deeper understanding of the performance differences between pre-training and MAML?
- How do the authors address the potential issues of overfitting and the generalizability of the results across different datasets and scenarios?
- Could you provide more detailed explanations for the discrepancies observed in the performance of pre-training and MAML across different datasets, especially in terms of the effect size and the statistical significance of the results?
- The paper claims that the formal diversity of a dataset is a driving factor. Could you elaborate on this claim and provide more evidence or theoretical support for it?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the 1% threshold in their decision rule, and what are the implications of this choice for the interpretation of the results?
