 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HFDream, a novel method for text-to-3D generation that leverages human feedback to fine-tune a text-to-image model, enhancing the model's ability to generate multi-view images that align with textual prompts. This approach involves training a reward model to assess the alignment of generated images with specified viewpoints, which is then used to fine-tune the text-to-image model. The methodology is evaluated using a human study, demonstrating improvements in viewpoint consistency and geometric fidelity compared to existing methods like DreamFusion. Despite these advancements, concerns are raised about the method's scalability, generalization to unseen objects, and the potential for overfitting due to the limited diversity in the training dataset.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology.
- The method of training a reward model using human feedback is innovative and addresses the issue of generating multi-view images that align with textual prompts.
- The proposed method significantly outperforms existing methods like DreamFusion, showing improvements in generating diverse viewpoints and enhancing the quality of text-to-3D generation.
- The paper is structured clearly, with detailed explanations of the methodology and results, which are supported by comprehensive experiments and ablation studies.
- The introduction of a novel reward function that predicts human assessments of view alignment more accurately than existing methods like ImageReward adds value to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the method performs with unseen objects and the potential for overfitting due to the limited diversity in the training dataset.
- The method's scalability is questioned, especially in terms of its ability to handle a large number of objects and scenes, and its applicability to DreamBooth-style personalized generation.
- The use of a reward-weighted loss in fine-tuning the text-to-image model might not be the most effective approach, as it could lead to a loss of sample diversity and fidelity.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the training process for the reward model and the selection of human annotators, including their qualifications and training.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, such as its potential for generating fake images or copying content without permission.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform with unseen objects, and what measures are in place to prevent overfitting due to the limited diversity in the training dataset?
- Can the method be scaled up to handle a larger number of objects and scenes, and how does it apply to DreamBooth-style personalized generation?
- How were the human annotators selected and trained, and what qualifications did they have?
- How does the reward model handle the potential for bias in the human feedback data, and what steps were taken to mitigate this bias?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process for the reward model, particularly how it was fine-tuned from ImageReward and how it was trained on the ImageReward-2k dataset?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of sample diversity and fidelity, especially when fine-tuned with human feedback?
