 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the empirical data capacity (EDC) of neural networks, focusing on how different architectures, optimizers, and training conditions affect the number of training samples a network can fit. The authors propose the EDC as a metric to quantify the flexibility of neural networks, comparing it across various models and datasets. They find that convolutional networks can fit more data than multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) and vision transformers (ViTs), and that stochastic gradient descent (SGD) can fit more data than full-batch gradient descent. The study also explores how different optimizers and regularizers influence the EDC, and how the data distribution affects the capacity of neural networks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive discussion of related work.
- The experiments are extensive and well-designed, providing valuable insights into the empirical data capacity (EDC) of neural networks across various architectures, datasets, and training conditions.
- The paper includes a variety of experiments that systematically explore the effects of different model architectures, optimizers, and training conditions on the EDC.
- The authors have proposed a novel metric, the EDC, which can be used to compare the flexibility of different neural network models.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of machine learning, focusing on the practical implications of neural network flexibility and generalization.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and justification for the EDC metric, which is crucial for understanding its relevance and utility.
- The experiments are not always well-designed or explained, with some figures and results being confusing or unclear.
- The paper's claims about the capacity of neural networks to fit more data than they have parameters are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- There is a lack of theoretical analysis or discussion on the implications of the empirical findings, which could enhance the paper's contribution to the field.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of its experimental setup, such as the choice of datasets and model architectures, which may not generalize well to other settings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and utility of the EDC metric, and provide a more detailed explanation of how it is calculated and interpreted?
- How does the EDC vary with different training conditions, such as learning rates and batch sizes, and what are the implications of these variations?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the EDC across different model architectures, particularly in terms of the impact of depth and width on capacity?
- What are the implications of the EDC for generalization performance, and how does it relate to other metrics such as Rademacher complexity or VC dimension?
- How does the EDC compare to other notions of capacity or complexity in neural networks, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the practical application of neural networks?
