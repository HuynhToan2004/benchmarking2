 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for detecting distribution shifts in neural networks by comparing activation graphs of samples from different distributions. The authors propose a new family of representations, termed MAGDiff, which leverages the mean activation graph of each class in the training set to detect shifts. This method is evaluated against the state-of-the-art BBSD method, showing superior performance in some scenarios. The methodology is based on the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test applied to the mean activation graph of each class, which allows for the detection of shifts in the data distribution without retraining the model. The paper also provides empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness across various datasets and shift intensities.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is simple, easy to implement, and does not require retraining the model, which makes it practical and accessible.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method across various datasets and shift intensities, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The method's focus on comparing the mean activation graph of each class in the training set is innovative and allows for the detection of shifts without retraining the model.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The empirical results show that the proposed method outperforms the state-of-the-art BBSD method in some scenarios, indicating its potential utility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its method from existing approaches, such as BBSD, which also use activation graphs for shift detection.
- The method's reliance on the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test, which is not the most powerful test for multivariate data, might limit its effectiveness in certain scenarios.
- The method's focus on covariate shifts might limit its applicability to other types of shifts, such as label shifts.
- The paper lacks a theoretical analysis or explanation for why the proposed method works better than existing methods, which could enhance its credibility.
- The method's sensitivity to the choice of layer and its performance in scenarios with a large shift intensity or a low sample size is not adequately explored.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of their method with existing approaches, such as BBSD, to highlight the unique advantages of their method?
- How does the choice of layer affect the performance of the proposed method, and can the authors provide more insights into this?
- In scenarios with a large shift intensity or a low sample size, how does the proposed method perform compared to existing methods?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of their method, particularly why it might be more effective than existing methods?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of shifts, such as label shifts, which are not explicitly addressed in the paper?
