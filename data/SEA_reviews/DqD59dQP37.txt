 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the causal fairness in machine learning models under the presence of unobserved confounding. It introduces a novel neural prediction framework that provides worst-case guarantees for causal fairness under unobserved confounding. The framework is evaluated using both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in ensuring fairness in high-stakes applications. The paper also derives bounds for causal fairness metrics under different types of unobserved confounding and provides a theoretical analysis of these bounds. Despite its novel approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in some sections and the need for more comprehensive experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the concepts presented.
- The topic of causal fairness is both interesting and relevant, addressing a significant gap in the literature.
- The authors have developed a novel neural prediction framework for causal fairness, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper includes a theoretical analysis of the bounds for causal fairness metrics, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework in ensuring fairness in high-stakes applications.
- The paper is the first to study causal fairness under unobserved confounding, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the introduction and related work sections, which could benefit from more detailed explanations.
- The novelty of the framework is questionable as it appears to be a combination of existing methods without sufficient differentiation.
- The experimental results are limited, and the paper could benefit from more comprehensive experiments to validate the claims made.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework and how these limitations affect the practical applicability of the framework.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the binary sensitive attribute and the compatibility of the SCM with the sensitivity model, are not adequately justified or explained.
- The paper's notation and definitions are sometimes confusing, which could make it difficult for readers to follow the arguments and proofs presented.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the differences between the three types of unobserved confounding (UDE, UIE, USE) and how they impact the fairness metrics?
- How do the authors justify the compatibility of the SCM with the sensitivity model, and what are the implications if this compatibility is not maintained?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of binary sensitive attributes, and how does this choice affect the applicability of the framework in real-world scenarios?
- How do the authors determine the sensitivity parameter in practice, and what are the implications if this parameter is not known or not accurately estimated?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the fairness metrics are measured and how the bounds derived from the theory are validated in practice?
- How do the authors address the potential biases that could arise from the use of pre-trained estimators in the experimental setup?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of sensitivity model and how it impacts the results and the applicability of the framework?
