 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an Automated Continual Learning (ACL) method that formulates continual learning as sequence learning across long time lags, using self-referential neural networks to learn in-context continual learning algorithms. ACL encodes desiderata for continual learning into its learning objectives, aiming to achieve good performance on both old and new tasks. The method is evaluated on various few-shot and standard image classification tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness. However, concerns are raised about the practical applicability of the method, particularly in scenarios with a large number of classes, and the fairness of comparisons with other methods. The paper also discusses the limitations of the approach and its potential impact on the field of continual learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, providing a clear and comprehensive introduction to the problem of continual learning.
- The proposed method, Automated Continual Learning (ACL), is innovative and well-motivated, addressing the issue of catastrophic forgetting by formulating continual learning as sequence learning across long time lags.
- The method is evaluated on various few-shot and standard image classification tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and potential impact on the field of continual learning.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the methodology and the experimental setup, which enhances the understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The method's ability to encode desiderata for continual learning into its learning objectives is a significant contribution, potentially improving the efficiency and effectiveness of continual learning algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The experiments are primarily conducted on relatively simple datasets, which might not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness on more complex, real-world tasks.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, particularly in scenarios where the method is compared to other methods that do not suffer from this issue.
- The paper's focus on few-shot and standard image classification tasks might limit its applicability to other types of tasks, such as text-based tasks, which could benefit from the method's capabilities.
- The paper's evaluation might be biased due to the use of different datasets and tasks for training and testing, which could affect the fairness and generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that do not suffer from catastrophic forgetting, particularly in scenarios where the method is not optimally suited?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of datasets and tasks for training and testing, and discuss the potential implications of this choice on the fairness and generalizability of the results?
- How does the method perform on more complex, real-world tasks, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in applying the method to such tasks?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific datasets used and the parameters of the model, to enhance the reproducibility and transparency of the results?
