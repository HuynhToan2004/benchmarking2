 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Deep Independent Vector Analysis (DeepIVA), a novel method that integrates multidataset independent subspace analysis (MISA) and identifiable variational autoencoders (iVAE) to learn linked and identifiable latent sources across multiple data modalities. DeepIVA leverages the identifiability properties of iVAE to ensure proper unimodal disentanglement and uses the MISA loss to achieve latent cross-modal alignment. The method is evaluated on both synthetic and real data, demonstrating its effectiveness in recovering sources and aligning them across modalities. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty and the lack of a comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and well-defined problem.
- The proposed method, DeepIVA, is well-motivated and shows a good improvement over existing methods, particularly in terms of its ability to handle nonlinear mixtures and to identify linked sources across multiple data modalities.
- The method is tested on both synthetic and real data, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of evaluation metrics, which facilitate a thorough assessment of the model's performance.
- The application of the method to a large multimodal neuroimaging dataset showcases its potential in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as it primarily combines existing methods (MISA and iVAE) without significant modifications or innovations.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the identifiability of the sources and the handling of nonlinear mixtures.
- The method's reliance on auxiliary variables for identifiability may limit its applicability in scenarios where such variables are not available or not informative.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to existing methods.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently detailed, particularly in terms of the evaluation metrics used and the statistical significance of the results.
- The method's assumptions about the data-generating process and the identifiability of the sources are not thoroughly discussed or justified.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the identifiability of the sources is guaranteed in the absence of auxiliary variables?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational complexity and scalability?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the statistical significance of the results and the evaluation metrics used?
- How does the method handle the nonlinear mixing of sources, especially in scenarios where the mixing functions are unknown?
- Can you discuss the limitations of the method and potential future directions for improvement?
