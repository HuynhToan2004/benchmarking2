 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel generative retrieval method named LLM2GR, which leverages large language models (LLMs) to generate relevant document titles and passages in a zero-shot setting. The method involves a two-stage approach where the LLM generates document titles and then retrieves passages from these documents. This approach aims to enhance retrieval performance without the need for additional retrieval models or training. The method is evaluated on six knowledge-sensitive NLP tasks from the KILT benchmark, demonstrating improvements over traditional methods like BM25 and DPR. Despite its innovative approach, concerns were raised about the novelty of the method, the clarity of presentation, and the need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to retrieval using large language models (LLMs), which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The method is evaluated on six knowledge-sensitive NLP tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in retrieval performance.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology.
- The method does not require additional retrieval models or training, which simplifies its implementation.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's performance, including an ablation study that shows the impact of different components of the method.
- The use of a trie and FM-index for constraining the generation process is a novel approach that adds a layer of control to the retrieval process.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that also use LLMs for retrieval, such as LLM-URL and LLM-Retriever.
- The method's novelty is questioned as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques, such as using a trie for constrained decoding and an FM-index for constrained decoding.
- The paper does not discuss the potential impact of the method on the hallucination problem, which is a significant issue with LLMs.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, with some figures and descriptions being unclear or misleading.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the method, which is a significant limitation given the computational demands of LLMs.
- The method's effectiveness in scenarios where the generated prefix does not uniquely identify a document is not addressed, which could lead to ambiguities in the retrieval process.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other LLM-based retrieval methods, such as LLM-URL and LLM-Retriever?
- Can the authors clarify how the method's novelty is distinct from existing techniques, particularly in the use of the trie and FM-index for constraining decoding?
- How does the method address the hallucination problem, which is a known issue with LLMs?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the method and how it scales with different document sizes and LLM sizes?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the generated prefix does not uniquely identify a document?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of the method on the diversity of retrieved passages and the overall quality of the retrieval process?
