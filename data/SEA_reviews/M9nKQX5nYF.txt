 **Summary:**
The paper explores the issue of defection in federated learning (FL) settings, where agents may permanently disengage from the learning process, affecting the model's performance and generalization capabilities. The authors propose a novel algorithm, ADA-GD, to mitigate defections by updating gradients orthogonal to the gradients of defecting agents. This approach theoretically ensures convergence to an effective solution for all participating agents, preventing defections. The paper also provides numerical experiments to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method. Despite the innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its assumptions, such as the realizability of a single model that works well for all agents, which may not be practical in many FL scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and realistic problem in federated learning (FL), where agents may defect, potentially leading to suboptimal generalization, inconsistent final performance, and disproportionate workload.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear motivation for the problem addressed.
- The proposed algorithm, ADA-GD, is innovative and theoretically sound, providing a convergence guarantee.
- The paper includes numerical experiments to support the theoretical findings, which are well-presented and easy to understand.
- The authors consider the important problem of rational agents in FL and propose an algorithm to prevent defections, which is a novel approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes a strong realizability condition that may not hold in practical FL scenarios, where agents may have distinct loss functions.
- The proposed algorithm, ADA-GD, is complex and difficult to analyze, which might limit its practical applicability.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implications of the assumptions made, particularly the realizability assumption.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing methods, such as MW-FED, which could potentially address similar issues.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in the figures and the clarity of the explanations.
- There are concerns about the fairness of the comparisons made in the experiments, as the proposed method may have an unfair advantage due to its ability to prevent defections.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the practical implications of the realizability assumption and discuss how it might affect the results in more complex or diverse FL scenarios?
- How does the proposed algorithm, ADA-GD, compare to existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons between ADA-GD and other methods, such as MW-FED, to better understand the advantages and disadvantages of each approach?
- How does the algorithm handle the case where agents have different loss functions, and what are the implications of this for the convergence and effectiveness of the algorithm?
- In the context of the experiments, how do the authors ensure a fair comparison between ADA-GD and other methods, particularly in terms of the number of iterations and the model's performance?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the assumptions made in the paper, particularly in the context of the experimental results and the theoretical guarantees?
