 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for prompt optimization in large language models (LLMs) by employing a Mixture of Experts (MoE) approach, where each expert is assigned a set of demos and an instruction. The method involves a two-phase process: first, demos are clustered based on semantic similarity, and then instructions are optimized for each cluster. This approach allows for the generation of task-specific prompts that are tailored to the characteristics of the input data, thereby improving performance on various benchmarks. The paper also explores the use of different clustering algorithms and embedding models to optimize the performance of the LLM.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-structured presentation.
- The proposed method is novel and interesting, combining ideas from prompt optimization and Mixture of Experts, which is a unique approach.
- The paper includes extensive experiments and ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing significant improvements over baseline methods.
- The method's simplicity and the use of existing tools like K-means clustering make it accessible and potentially applicable in various settings.
- The paper provides a good evaluation, including comparisons with other methods and visualizations that help understand the method's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's approach might not be scalable or practical for larger datasets or more complex tasks, as it requires a significant amount of data and computational resources.
- The method's effectiveness is limited by the quality and diversity of the training data, as it relies heavily on the demos provided.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including its applicability to different types of tasks and the potential for overfitting to the training data.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with more recent prompt optimization methods or other clustering algorithms, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper's reliance on a single model (GPT-3.5-turbo) and a single dataset (APE benchmark) limits the generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the amount of data and computational resources required?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other LLMs or different datasets?
- What are the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments, and how do they affect the performance of the method?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their method, such as its applicability to different types of tasks or its sensitivity to the quality and diversity of the training data?
- How does the proposed method compare with other clustering algorithms, and what are the implications of using different embedding models for clustering?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other prompt optimization methods, especially those that do not rely on in-context learning?
