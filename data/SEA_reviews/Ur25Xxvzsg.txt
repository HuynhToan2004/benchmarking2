 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel transferable deep clustering model that adapts cluster centroids according to the distribution of data samples, addressing the challenge of transferring trained clustering models from source to target domains. It utilizes an attention-based module to update centroids, ensuring generalizability to new domains. The model is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing promising results in terms of performance and computational efficiency. The paper also provides a theoretical analysis that supports the model's superiority over traditional clustering algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of deep learning, focusing on the transferability of clustering models across different domains.
- The proposed model is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of its components and their roles in the overall framework.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis that supports the superiority of the proposed model over traditional clustering algorithms.
- The model is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency in real-world scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their theoretical underpinnings.
- The model's ability to adapt to new domains and its generalizability are significant strengths, particularly in scenarios where labeled data is not available.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the model may not perform optimally.
- There is a need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, especially in terms of computational cost and scalability.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the impact of the number of clusters (K) on the model's performance, which is a critical hyperparameter in clustering.
- The experimental results, while promising, are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art deep clustering models.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the model, particularly in terms of the number of samples and clusters, which could limit its applicability to large-scale datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and training time of the proposed model compared to existing methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison of the computational cost and scalability of their proposed model with existing methods, particularly in terms of training time and memory usage?
- How does the proposed model handle scenarios where the number of clusters (K) is not known in advance?
- Can the authors provide more detailed visualizations or examples to illustrate how the model adapts to new domains and how this adaptation improves clustering performance?
- How does the model perform when the number of samples in the source domain is significantly different from that in the target domain?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of the number of layers (L) on the model's performance and provide a more detailed analysis of the model's scalability with increasing numbers of samples and clusters?
- How does the model handle scenarios where the data distribution between the source and target domains is highly different, and what are the implications for the model's performance in such cases?
