 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of CLIP for long-tailed recognition, focusing on the imbalance between textual and visual features. It proposes a method that utilizes a linear adapter to refine biased visual features and a learnable classifier initialized by text embeddings to address the mismatch between refined visual features and textual features. The method introduces an unsupervised prototype-guided feature learning loss to help separate vision distribution by optimal transport, aiming to improve the performance of long-tailed recognition. The paper presents an extensive set of experiments on benchmark datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the methodology, and the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach to leverage textual features as prototypes to guide visual feature learning, which is a novel and promising direction for long-tailed recognition.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments on benchmark datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of their proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a detailed description of the proposed method, making it accessible to readers.
- The use of optimal transport to address the imbalance between textual and visual features is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is the first to address the long-tailed recognition problem using CLIP, a powerful vision-language model, which is a significant advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as similar methods have been previously explored, such as the use of textual features as prototypes and the alignment of visual and textual features.
- The methodology is not clearly described, and the paper does not provide a detailed explanation of how the optimal transport is used to address the imbalance between textual and visual features.
- The paper does not compare its results with the state-of-the-art (SOTA) on the benchmark datasets, which is essential for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The generalizability of the findings is limited as the experiments were conducted only on CLIP, and it is unclear how the results would apply to other vision-language models.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the computational cost of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the optimal transport is used to address the imbalance between textual and visual features?
- How does the proposed method compare with the state-of-the-art on the benchmark datasets?
- Is there a possibility to extend the proposed method to other vision-language models beyond CLIP?
- How does the proposed method handle the generalization to unseen classes or domains?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its potential drawbacks?
- What are the computational costs of the proposed method, and how do they compare with other methods in terms of efficiency?
