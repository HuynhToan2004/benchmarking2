 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for plausible deniability in encryption using large language models (LLMs) and standard encryption algorithms like AES. The approach involves encoding plaintext into a sequence of numbers that appear random when decrypted with incorrect keys, thereby maintaining the deniability of the encryption. This method leverages the statistical properties of LLMs to compress messages into a stream of numbers that are indistinguishable from random noise when decrypted with incorrect keys. The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation against statistical tests and demonstrates the method's effectiveness in maintaining plausible deniability. However, concerns were raised regarding the method's reliance on the security of the LLM and the potential for biased results in statistical tests.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to plausible deniability in encryption by leveraging the statistical properties of large language models (LLMs) and standard encryption algorithms.
- The method is theoretically sound, with a clear and detailed explanation of the proposed algorithm and its implementation.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method against various statistical tests, demonstrating its effectiveness in maintaining plausible deniability.
- The approach is generalizable to other generative models and domains, including images and audio, which could expand the applicability of the method.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear structure and detailed explanations that make it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and effective, providing a practical solution for achieving plausible deniability in encryption.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method relies heavily on the security of the LLM, which could be a significant vulnerability if the model is compromised.
- The paper's evaluation primarily relies on statistical tests, which may not be sufficient to prove the method's effectiveness against all possible attacks.
- The method's effectiveness is dependent on the quality of the LLM's statistical properties, which could vary depending on the model's training data and architecture.
- There is a potential for biased results in the statistical tests due to the method's dependence on the LLM's statistical properties, which could affect the reliability of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the potential impact of the method on the privacy of the sender, particularly in scenarios where the sender's intent or the original message's meaning is revealed.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational overhead of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the method would perform if the LLM's statistical properties are biased or not representative of natural language?
- How does the method ensure that the encoded plaintext is indistinguishable from white noise, especially when using a fixed quantization granularity?
- Is there a possibility to use the method with open-source LLMs to ensure transparency and reproducibility?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the LLM's statistical properties are not optimal, such as when the input probability distribution diverges significantly from dyadic points?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational overhead of the proposed method, particularly in terms of time and space complexity?
- How does the method ensure that the encryption process does not introduce biases or errors that could compromise the plausible deniability of the encrypted messages?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of the method on the privacy of the sender, particularly in scenarios where the sender's intent or the original message's meaning is revealed?
