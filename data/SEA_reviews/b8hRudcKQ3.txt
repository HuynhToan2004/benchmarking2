 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Alpha-Tuning, a framework designed to adjust client incentives in federated learning (FL) by dynamically adjusting model training parameters based on client preferences. This framework uses a novel approach to compute client weights, combining performance metrics and monetary quotations to influence model training. The authors propose a payment schema that compensates clients based on their data contribution, aiming to balance client preferences between performance gain and monetary reward. The paper also discusses the implementation of Alpha-Tuning and its potential to improve client satisfaction and model performance. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the presentation, the empirical evaluation, and the practical implications of the framework in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in federated learning (FL) by focusing on client incentives and their impact on model performance.
- The proposed framework, Alpha-Tuning, introduces a novel approach to adjusting client incentives by incorporating both performance metrics and monetary quotations, which could potentially improve client satisfaction and model performance.
- The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it accessible and understandable to readers.
- The concept of using client quotations to influence model training is innovative and could be beneficial in scenarios where clients have varying preferences.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the Alpha-Tuning framework, which could be a valuable reference for future research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive empirical evaluation, which limits the ability to convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved to make it more accessible to readers, especially in terms of clarity and organization.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the proposed framework, such as the scalability and efficiency of the proposed methods in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed framework, which could help readers understand the scope and applicability of the research.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and communication costs associated with the proposed methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods, which could limit its perceived novelty and contribution to the field.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the distinction between the "alpha" value and the "monetary quotation" in the context of the proposed framework?
- How do the authors justify the choice of using a single validation set for both local and global model training, given the potential for data leakage?
- Could the authors provide more details on the scalability and efficiency of the proposed methods in larger-scale federated learning environments?
- How does the proposed framework address the issue of data leakage, especially in scenarios where clients have access to validation sets?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and communication costs associated with the proposed methods?
- How does the proposed framework handle scenarios where clients have varying levels of computational power or network bandwidth?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the novelty and effectiveness of their approach?
