 **Summary:**
The paper explores the robustness of Energy-Based Models (EBMs) trained with Equilibrium Propagation (EP) against adversarial and natural perturbations. The authors compare EBMs with transformers and adversarially trained DNNs, showing that EBMs can achieve comparable robustness to adversarially trained DNNs without sacrificing clean accuracy. The paper also introduces a novel approach to adversarial robustness, focusing on the feedback connections in EBMs which are inspired by biological systems. The robustness of EBMs is demonstrated through experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, showing that EBMs are more robust to natural corruptions and adversarial attacks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to adversarial robustness using feedback connections in EBMs, which are inspired by biological systems.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors have performed a thorough analysis of the robustness of EBMs trained with EP, demonstrating that EBMs are more robust to natural corruptions and adversarial attacks than standard DNNs.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the robustness of EBMs against various attacks, including both white-box and black-box attacks, and natural perturbations.
- The results are supported by robust empirical evidence, including comparisons with transformers and adversarially trained DNNs, and the paper includes a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other models trained with EP, such as deep equilibrium models (DEQs), which could help clarify the specific contributions of feedback connections to robustness.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the computational cost of training EP-CNNs compared to other models, which could be a significant limitation for practical applications.
- The experiments are limited to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, which may not adequately represent the robustness of EBMs on larger, more complex datasets such as ImageNet.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the types of adversarial examples that EBMs are most robust against, which could be crucial for understanding the practical implications of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the biological plausibility of the feedback connections in EBMs, particularly in relation to the biological mechanisms they are modeled after.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the specific types of adversarial examples that EBMs are most robust against, and how this relates to the robustness of biological systems?
- How does the computational cost of training EP-CNNs compare to other models, and what are the implications for practical applications?
- Could the authors clarify the role of feedback connections in EBMs, particularly in relation to other models trained with EP such as DEQs?
- How do the findings of this study relate to other recent works on adversarial robustness in EBMs, such as those using spiking neural networks?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the robustness of EBMs on larger, more complex datasets such as ImageNet, and discuss the potential limitations of the current experiments in terms of dataset size and complexity?
