 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LOQA, a decentralized reinforcement learning algorithm designed for general-sum games, focusing on optimizing individual utility while promoting cooperation among agents. LOQA is built on the assumption that the opponent's actions are guided by an internal action-value function, which allows for the modeling of opponent policies and the shaping of their returns. The algorithm is compared to POLA, showing superior performance in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness in cooperative environments like the Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma and the Coin Game. However, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental validation, lack of comparison with other baselines, and the strong assumption about the opponent's policy.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel, decentralized reinforcement learning algorithm, LOQA, which effectively optimizes an agent's individual utility while promoting cooperation in partially competitive environments.
- The assumption that the opponent's actions are guided by an internal action-value function is innovative and allows for the modeling of opponent policies, influencing their returns for different actions.
- The paper is well-written, making the content accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for understanding complex algorithms and methodologies.
- The method is computationally efficient, which is a significant advantage in practical applications, especially when compared to other methods like POLA.
- The paper includes a detailed description of the method, including the algorithm, which is helpful for reproducibility and further research.
- The experiments are well-designed, showing that LOQA can outperform POLA in certain scenarios, which is a notable achievement.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other baselines, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness in cooperative environments.
- The assumption that the opponent's policy is guided by an internal action-value function is strong and may not hold in all scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
- The experimental validation is limited to a few environments, which may not fully demonstrate the algorithm's capabilities across different scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method, particularly the assumption about the opponent's policy and the lack of a broader range of experimental environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method and a broader range of experiments to validate the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly the strong assumption about the opponent's policy?
- How does the method perform in continuous action spaces, and could it be adapted to handle such scenarios?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational efficiency of LOQA with other methods, particularly in terms of the number of function evaluations required?
- Could the authors include a broader range of experimental environments to validate the effectiveness of the method in different scenarios?
- Is there a plan to compare the method with other state-of-the-art methods in multi-agent reinforcement learning to establish its relative performance?
