 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SPACoder, a novel semi-supervised code translation method that generates synthetic parallel code using a two-stage approach. The method leverages static analysis and compilation to filter and select high-quality translations, improving code translation performance across multiple languages. SPACoder employs a curriculum learning approach to manage the noise and alignment levels in the training data, enhancing the model's ability to translate code effectively. The method is evaluated on a variety of datasets and shows significant improvements over existing methods, particularly in low-resource scenarios. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticisms regarding the lack of novelty in its approach, the clarity of its presentation, and the depth of its experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel semi-supervised code translation method, SPACoder, which effectively generates synthetic parallel code with enhanced alignment, improving code translation performance across multiple languages.
- The methodology of SPACoder is robust and scalable, leveraging snippet training, static analysis, and compilation to generate synthetic parallel code with high alignment.
- SPACoder is generalizable to various models and languages, with little overhead, making it a versatile tool for code translation.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with extensive experiments demonstrating significant improvements over state-of-the-art baselines.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, with a focus on generating synthetic parallel data, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There is a lack of novelty in the methodology, as the approach of using static analysis and compilation to filter translations is not new.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance under different conditions.
- The presentation of the paper is not clear in some sections, particularly in the explanation of the hypothesis generation and selection processes.
- The paper does not include code, which could limit the reproducibility of the results.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a single dataset, which may not fully demonstrate its effectiveness across different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method and how they plan to address these in future work?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and performance with existing methods, particularly under different conditions?
- Could the authors clarify the process of hypothesis generation and selection, particularly in sections where the explanation is unclear or incomplete?
- How does the paper ensure the quality of the generated parallel data, and what steps are taken to validate the effectiveness of the generated data?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the performance of the method under different conditions, such as varying the amount of labeled data or the complexity of the code being translated?
