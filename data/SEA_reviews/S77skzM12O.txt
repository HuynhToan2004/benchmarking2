 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PROTO, a novel offline-to-online reinforcement learning (RL) framework that addresses the challenges of suboptimal performance, limited adaptability, and computational inefficiency in existing methods. PROTO employs a trust-region-style update to iteratively evolve a regularization term, enabling it to bridge diverse offline RL/IL and online off-policy RL methods with minimal computational overhead. The framework is evaluated on the D4RL benchmark, showing superior performance and efficiency compared to state-of-the-art methods. The paper also includes theoretical analysis and extensive experimental results to support its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with minimal assumptions on pretraining and finetuning methods, making it easily applicable to a wide range of RL algorithms.
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of the method and results, and the experiments are extensive, demonstrating the method's effectiveness across different RL methods.
- The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the proposed method, supporting its claims of superior performance and efficiency.
- The method's adaptability is highlighted, as it can be integrated into various RL algorithms with minimal modifications, making it a versatile tool for offline-to-online RL.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with recent offline-to-online RL methods, such as those by Agarwal et al. and Zhang et al., which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- There is a potential issue with the trust-region-style update, which might not be as effective as claimed, especially in scenarios where the pretrained policy is suboptimal.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The theoretical analysis, while present, could be more rigorous and detailed, particularly in explaining the convergence properties of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational costs associated with the proposed method, which is claimed to be efficient but may still require significant computational resources.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the proposed method and the method by Agarwal et al., particularly in terms of the regularization term used?
- How does the method handle the distributional shift during online fine-tuning, and what mechanisms are in place to mitigate this issue?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with recent offline-to-online RL methods, including those by Zhang et al. and Agarwal et al., to better contextualize the proposed method's contributions?
- How does the method perform when compared to other methods that do not use a regularization term, such as those that use conservative learning or pessimistic value initialization?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational costs and efficiency of the proposed method, perhaps including a more detailed analysis or experimental results?
