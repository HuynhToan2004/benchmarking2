 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for measuring fairness in machine learning models by utilizing empirical likelihood to construct a confidence region for the covariance between sensitive attributes and the classifier's decision boundary. This approach allows for a more robust and reliable assessment of fairness, particularly in scenarios where traditional methods may fail to account for uncertainty. The method is evaluated through both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in balancing fairness and accuracy. However, the paper's contributions are seen as incremental, and the theoretical underpinnings of the method, particularly the novelty and practical implications, are not convincingly argued.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method effectively balances fairness and accuracy by considering the uncertainty of the sample fairness criterion.
- The use of empirical likelihood to construct a confidence region for the covariance between sensitive attributes and the decision boundary is a novel and interesting approach.
- The method is robust to distribution shifts, as demonstrated through experiments on real-world datasets.
- The paper provides a clear and comprehensive overview of related work, highlighting the method's contributions and limitations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and convincing argument for why the proposed method is novel and superior to existing methods, particularly those that use covariance as a fairness criterion.
- The theoretical underpinnings of the method, particularly the empirical likelihood approach, are not adequately explained, and the proofs and assumptions are not convincingly justified.
- The paper's claims about the method's ability to handle uncertainty and its practical implications are not convincingly demonstrated, particularly in real-world scenarios.
- The experimental evaluations are limited in scope, focusing primarily on binary classification tasks and not adequately demonstrating the method's effectiveness across different types of classifiers or fairness metrics.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical challenges of setting fairness thresholds and the computational complexity of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty and practical advantages of their method over existing methods that also use covariance as a fairness criterion?
- How does the proposed method handle uncertainty in real-world scenarios, particularly in the context of fairness constraints?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical underpinnings of their method, including the assumptions and proofs used?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of classifiers or fairness metrics, and what are the implications for the trade-off between fairness and accuracy?
- Can the authors discuss the computational complexity and practical feasibility of their method, particularly in terms of its scalability and applicability to large datasets or complex models?
