 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel diffusion-based method for anomaly detection, leveraging the posterior distribution of diffusion time to identify anomalies. This approach, named Diffusion Time Estimation (DTE), is compared against various baselines on the ADBench benchmark, demonstrating competitive performance and significant computational efficiency. The authors propose a method that estimates the posterior distribution of diffusion time using a neural network, which can be parametric or non-parametric. This method is shown to outperform traditional diffusion models like DDPM and other anomaly detection methods in terms of inference time and accuracy. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method and its interpretability, as it closely resembles k-nearest neighbors (kNN) and lacks a clear advantage over existing methods in terms of computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodologies and results.
- The proposed method, DTE, is computationally efficient and shows good performance on the ADBench benchmark, outperforming other diffusion-based methods.
- The paper includes a thorough experimental evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of DTE across various datasets and settings.
- The authors have provided a detailed analysis of the method's limitations and discussed potential future work, showing a commitment to the ongoing development of the method.
- The paper is reproducible, with the code and supplementary materials provided, which is crucial for further research and validation of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The methodology of DTE is not entirely novel, as it closely resembles kNN, and the paper does not sufficiently address how DTE improves upon or differs from existing methods like kNN.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the computational efficiency of DTE, particularly in terms of training time and memory requirements, which are critical for practical deployment.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with other diffusion-based anomaly detection methods, such as those using in-painting techniques, to fully establish the advantages of DTE.
- The interpretability of the method is questionable, as the method's reliance on kNN for anomaly detection raises concerns about its ability to provide new insights or advantages over existing methods.
- The paper's contribution to the field is not clearly articulated, making it difficult to assess the significance and novelty of the proposed method compared to existing approaches.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific advantages of DTE over kNN in terms of computational efficiency and anomaly detection performance?
- How does DTE perform in terms of training time and memory requirements compared to other methods, especially in large-scale deployments?
- What are the specific advantages of using DTE over other diffusion-based anomaly detection methods, such as those using in-painting techniques?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process and the choice of hyperparameters for the neural network used in DTE?
- How does the choice of diffusion model (DDPM) affect the performance of DTE, and could the authors explore using other diffusion models to validate the method's robustness?
