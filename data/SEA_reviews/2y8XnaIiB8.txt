 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel vision-language dataset distillation method, which leverages trajectory matching to create a distilled dataset from a large-scale vision-language dataset. This method is applied to image-text retrieval tasks, demonstrating significant improvements over baseline methods. The approach uses a bidirectional contrastive loss for training and a bi-trajectory matching loss for updating the distilled dataset, which is evaluated on the Flickr30K and COCO datasets. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited evaluation scope and lack of comprehensive comparisons with other distillation methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel vision-language dataset distillation method, which is the first of its kind and addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of vision-language datasets.
- The method is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the problem formulation, methodology, and experimental setup.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including comparisons with several baseline methods and an ablation study that shows the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing coreset selection methods, achieving significant improvements in image-text retrieval performance.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed method and its implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough evaluation of the distilled dataset, particularly in terms of its generalization ability and its impact on model performance when used with different backbone models.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- There are some typographical and citation errors that need correction.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the implications of the distilled dataset, such as its potential biases and how they might affect the performance of the models trained on it.
- The evaluation is limited to only two datasets, which might not be sufficient to fully demonstrate the effectiveness and generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other distillation methods, including those that use different loss functions or different distillation strategies.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the typographical errors and citation issues pointed out in the review?
- How does the proposed method compare to other distillation methods that use different loss functions or different distillation strategies?
- Can the authors provide more details on the generalization ability of the distilled dataset and its impact on model performance when used with different backbone models?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential biases in the original datasets, and what measures are taken to mitigate these biases in the distilled dataset?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of the proposed method and its implications for future research in this area?
