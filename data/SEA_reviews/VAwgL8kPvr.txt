 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of Neural Architecture Search (NAS) for pruning pre-trained language models (PLMs) to reduce model size and latency while maintaining performance. It proposes a two-stage method where the first stage fine-tunes a super-network, and the second stage uses NAS to identify sub-networks with optimal trade-offs between model size and performance. The method is evaluated on the GLUE benchmark using BERT-base, achieving up to 50% compression with less than 5% performance loss. The paper also introduces a multi-objective approach to identify Pareto-optimal sub-networks, which allows for flexible and automated compression. Despite its novelty in applying NAS to PLMs, the paper is criticized for its lack of novelty in its NAS method and for not including larger models or more recent language models in its experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The use of NAS for pruning pre-trained language models (PLMs) is a novel approach, providing a new perspective on model compression.
- The paper is well-motivated and the experiments are thorough, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper includes a comprehensive ablation study, which helps in understanding the impact of different components of the proposed method.
- The methodology is simple and effective, with a straightforward approach to sub-network selection.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger models and different types of language models.
- The paper does not include experiments with larger models, which could provide insights into the scalability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of the search space on the results, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the NAS method used is questionable, as it appears to be a rehash of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The paper does not include experiments with more recent language models, such as LLaMA, which could provide a more current evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not clearly presented, making it difficult to interpret the results and understand the method's effectiveness.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform with larger models, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in applying it to such models?
- Can the method be adapted to other types of language models beyond BERT, and if so, what are the expected outcomes?
- How does the search space affect the results, and could you provide more details on the impact of different search spaces on the performance of the pruned models?
- Could you clarify the experimental setup, particularly the training and validation data used, and the performance metrics reported?
- How does the proposed method compare to other pruning methods that do not use NAS, and what are the advantages of using NAS in this context?
- Could you discuss the potential impact of using different NAS methods, such as NAS with reinforcement learning or Bayesian optimization, on the results?

**Paper Decision:**
- Decision: Reject
- Reasons: The paper, while introducing an interesting approach to pruning PLMs using NAS, lacks significant novelty in its methodology and experimental setup. The methodology closely resembles existing NAS methods, and the experiments are limited to a small model and do not include larger or more recent models, which could have provided a more robust evaluation. The paper also fails to address the limitations of its approach, such as its applicability to larger models and different types of language models. The reviewers have also expressed concerns about the paper's presentation and the clarity of its experimental results, which further detract from its strength. These issues, combined with the lack of novelty and a comprehensive evaluation, lead to the decision to reject the paper.
