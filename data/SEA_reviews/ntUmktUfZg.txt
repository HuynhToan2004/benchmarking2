 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach, G2D, to address catastrophic forgetting in continual learning by leveraging synthetic data to train domain discriminators. This method, which involves fine-tuning classifiers and generative models for each domain and using a domain discriminator to route samples to the appropriate classifier, shows promising results across various benchmarks in both vision and language domains. The proposed method outperforms existing methods by up to 7.6 and 6.2 points, and introduces a new benchmark, DermCL, to evaluate the performance of the method in real-world scenarios. The paper is well-structured, clearly presented, and supported by comprehensive experimental results, although it lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, clearly presented, and supported by comprehensive experimental results.
- The proposed method, G2D, outperforms existing methods by up to 7.6 and 6.2 points, demonstrating significant improvements in performance.
- The introduction of a new benchmark, DermCL, provides a valuable tool for evaluating the performance of continual learning methods in real-world scenarios.
- The use of synthetic data to train domain discriminators is an innovative approach that effectively addresses the challenge of catastrophic forgetting in continual learning.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including its effectiveness in domain discrimination and its ability to enhance performance even in scenarios with stringent data-sharing constraints.
- The method's ability to leverage the capabilities of generative models and synthetic data for continual learning is well-explored, offering insights into how these technologies can be used to improve performance in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and generalizability to other domains.
- The method's reliance on the quality of synthetic data generated by the generative model raises concerns about the potential for hallucinations and the impact on downstream tasks.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in healthcare applications where patient privacy and data security are critical concerns.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and inference times for the domain discriminator, which could affect the practical deployment of the method in real-world scenarios.
