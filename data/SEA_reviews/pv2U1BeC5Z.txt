 **Summary:**
The paper explores the spectral bias in Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) by establishing a theoretical framework for generalization in overparametrized PINNs. The authors derive a Neurally-Informed Equation (NIE) that approximates PINN predictions on test points, providing a link between the DNN's kernel, the original differential equation, and PINN's prediction. The NIE is shown to capture the difference between the exact solution and the PINN prediction in the limit of an infinite dataset. The paper also introduces a figure of merit for measuring spectral bias, which is crucial for assessing the performance of PINNs in solving partial differential equations. The theoretical insights are supported by experiments on a 1D heat equation, demonstrating the utility of the proposed measures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for generalization in overparametrized PINNs, providing a solid theoretical foundation for understanding the behavior of PINNs in solving partial differential equations.
- The derivation of the Neurally-Informed Equation (NIE) offers a comprehensive theoretical understanding of PINNs, which is crucial for assessing the performance of PINNs in solving PDEs.
- The paper establishes a link between the DNN's kernel, the original differential equation, and PINN's prediction, providing a new perspective on the spectral bias in PINNs.
- The proposed figure of merit for measuring spectral bias is a valuable tool for evaluating the performance of PINNs, which is crucial for assessing the generalization ability of PINNs.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience, including those not specialized in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on linear operators, which might limit its applicability to more complex, non-linear problems.
- The experiments are limited to a 1D heat equation, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed framework to more complex PDEs.
- The paper does not provide a clear discussion on how the results apply to non-linear operators, which could be a significant limitation for practical applications.
- The practical utility of the proposed framework is not convincingly demonstrated, especially in terms of its ability to guide the design of better PINNs.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed framework perform for non-linear operators, and can you provide examples or theoretical insights into its applicability?
- Can you discuss the practical implications of the proposed framework in guiding the design of better PINNs?
- How does the proposed framework compare to other existing methods for evaluating the performance of PINNs, such as those based on energy functions or other metrics?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the Gaussian process prior and its implications for the generalizability of the results?
- How does the proposed framework handle the spectral bias in PINNs, and what are the implications of this bias for the accuracy and reliability of PINN predictions?
