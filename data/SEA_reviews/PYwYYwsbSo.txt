 **Summary:**
The paper introduces InstructZero, a method that optimizes soft prompts for black-box large language models (LLMs) by reducing the problem to a low-dimensional continuous optimization problem. This method uses Bayesian optimization to iteratively optimize these soft prompts, with the goal of generating instructions that maximize the output performance of the black-box LLM. The approach is demonstrated to improve performance over existing methods on various tasks, including some from the BIG-Bench dataset. The method involves using an open-source LLM to generate instructions from optimized soft prompts, which are then evaluated on a black-box LLM to guide the optimization process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to optimizing soft prompts for black-box LLMs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, making it accessible and understandable, which is crucial for replication and further research.
- The paper demonstrates a high level of originality, particularly in its approach to reducing the combinatorial optimization problem to a low-dimensional continuous optimization problem.
- The empirical results show that the proposed method outperforms existing methods, indicating the effectiveness of the approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive ablation study and case study, which provide a deeper understanding of the method's components and their impact.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There is a need for a more thorough discussion on the hyperparameter selection and sensitivity analysis, which could help in optimizing the method for different scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the method across different models and tasks, which is a significant concern given the diversity of LLMs and tasks.
- The computational cost of the method, particularly in terms of the number of API calls required, is not adequately addressed, which could be a major concern for practical implementation.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the societal impact of the method, which is an important consideration for any technology that interacts with human users.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the choice of baselines and the metrics used for evaluation.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical application?
- How does the method perform when optimizing prompts for different models, and what are the implications of using different models for optimization?
- What are the implications of using different open-source LLMs for instruction generation, and how does this affect the performance and generalizability of the method?
- How sensitive is the method to different hyperparameters, and what are the recommended settings for different scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the method, including the number of API calls required and the time taken for each iteration?
- How does the method perform on more complex tasks, such as those involving refinement, multi-step planning, or human interactions?
- Could the authors discuss the potential societal impact of the method and how it might be mitigated or addressed?
