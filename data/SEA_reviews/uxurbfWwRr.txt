 **Summary:**
The paper explores the problem of learning time-varying convexifications of multiple fairness measures in a sequential decision-making process, where fairness measures and their weights can change over time. The authors introduce a framework that integrates feedback from multiple fairness measures, some of which may conflict, and develop algorithms to handle this complexity. The paper provides theoretical guarantees and empirical validation, demonstrating that the proposed algorithms can achieve near-optimal regret bounds. Despite its theoretical contributions, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in presentation, insufficient discussion on related work, and limited empirical validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem of fairness in machine learning, focusing on the learning of time-varying convexifications of multiple fairness measures with limited graph-structured feedback.
- The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including a detailed discussion on the trade-offs among multiple fairness measures and their potential incompatibilities.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and concise presentation of the algorithms and theoretical results.
- The paper includes a detailed discussion on the challenges of integrating feedback from multiple fairness measures, some of which may conflict, and how to handle these conflicts in a principled and effective manner.
- The paper provides empirical validation of the proposed algorithms, demonstrating their effectiveness in achieving near-optimal regret bounds.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in its presentation, particularly in the introduction and related work sections, which makes it difficult for readers to understand the contributions and the context of the study.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed framework, which could lead to misunderstandings or misapplications of the results.
- The empirical validation is limited, with only one example used to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, which may not be sufficient to convincingly demonstrate their general applicability or effectiveness.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing literature, particularly from the work of Awasthi et al. (2020), which could lead to confusion about the novelty and significance of the contributions.
- The paper does not adequately address the societal impact of the proposed framework, which could be a significant concern given the potential applications in areas such as political advertising.

- How does the proposed framework handle the potential conflicts among fairness measures, especially when they are incompatible?
- Can you provide more details on the empirical validation, including the selection of the example and the results of additional experiments that might support the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms?
