 **Summary:**
The paper explores the concept of self-predictive abstractions in reinforcement learning (RL), specifically focusing on state and history representations in both Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) and Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes (POMDPs). It proposes a minimalist RL algorithm that learns self-predictive representations, aiming to address the challenges of representation learning in complex environments. The theoretical framework is supported by extensive experiments across various benchmarks, including MuJoCo and MiniGrid tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The paper also provides practical guidelines for RL practitioners, emphasizing the importance of analyzing tasks and choosing appropriate representation learning methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to understanding self-predictive abstractions in reinforcement learning, offering a new perspective on the role of representation learning in RL.
- The theoretical insights provided are both interesting and valuable, contributing to a better understanding of how representations are learned in RL.
- The experimental results are extensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, providing a solid foundation for the theoretical claims.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide a unified view of state and history representations, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed minimalist RL algorithm is a simple yet effective method for learning self-predictive representations in RL.
- The paper is well-organized and provides a clear presentation of the theoretical insights and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical contributions, while interesting, are somewhat limited by the lack of novelty in the main results, which are largely derived from existing works.
- The experimental validation could be improved by including more complex environments and comparing the proposed methods with a broader range of baselines.
- There is a need for more detailed discussion and justification of the experimental setups, particularly in terms of how the results generalize to different environments and settings.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of self-predictive representations over other types of representations (e.g., observation-predictive) are not convincingly supported by the experimental evidence.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough literature review, especially in the area of self-predictive learning in RL.
- The paper's scope is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on MDPs and POMDPs, which might not fully capture the complexity and diversity of real-world RL applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the stop-gradient technique in learning self-predictive representations, particularly in the context of the minimalist RL algorithm?
- How does the proposed minimalist RL algorithm compare with other methods that learn self-predictive representations, such as those mentioned in the literature review?
- In the experimental results, how do the authors justify the choice of environments and the settings used, especially in relation to the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, such as those mentioned in the literature review, to better establish the effectiveness and novelty of their approach?
- How does the paper address the issue of representation collapse in learning self-predictive representations, especially in the context of stochastic environments?
- Could the authors discuss the implications of their findings for more complex RL environments, such as those involving high-dimensional or noisy observations?
