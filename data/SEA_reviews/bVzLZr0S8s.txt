 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Action Shapley, a metric designed to aid in the selection of training data for reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms, particularly in data-constrained environments. The method leverages Shapley values to quantify the marginal contribution of each data point, which is used to determine the indispensability of data points and guide the selection process. The paper proposes a randomized algorithm to compute Action Shapley values, aiming to reduce computational complexity. The methodology is tested across four real-world case studies, demonstrating the practical applicability and potential for improved performance in RL algorithms. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its limited scope, lack of comprehensive comparisons with other methods, and potential over-reliance on specific assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel application of Shapley values to the selection of training data in reinforcement learning (RL), which is both innovative and relevant to current research.
- The use of Shapley values for data selection is a significant contribution, as it addresses a critical issue in RL training where data availability is a challenge.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, Action Shapley, shows promise in reducing computational complexity, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The method is evaluated on real-world case studies, demonstrating its applicability and potential impact in various domains.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other methods for data selection in RL, which limits the understanding of its relative effectiveness.
- The scope of the method is limited to data-constrained environments, which may not generalize well to other types of RL problems.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical justification for the use of Shapley values in data selection, which could strengthen the argument for their effectiveness.
- The method relies on specific assumptions about the RL environment, which may not hold in all scenarios, limiting its applicability.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant drawback in terms of practical implementation.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a few case studies, which may not be sufficient to establish its robustness and effectiveness across a broader range of scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly how it scales with the size of the dataset?
- How does the method perform when compared to other methods for data selection in RL, and what are the key differences in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made about the RL environment and how these assumptions affect the applicability of the method?
- In the context of the method's reliance on Shapley values, can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical justification for their use in data selection?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the RL agent fails to achieve its goal, and what are the implications of this failure for the data selection process?
- Can the authors provide more examples or case studies to demonstrate the practical applicability and effectiveness of the method in different types of RL environments?
