 **Summary:**
The paper introduces UniINR, a novel method designed to recover sharp global shutter (GS) frames from rolling shutter (RS) blur images by integrating event data. The approach leverages a spatial-temporal implicit neural representation (INR) to map pixel positions and time coordinates to RGB values, thereby addressing the challenges of RS correction, deblurring, and frame interpolation. The method is evaluated on both simulated and real datasets, showing significant performance improvements over existing methods. The paper is commended for its innovative approach, clarity, and the thoroughness of its experiments, although concerns are raised about the novelty of certain components and the clarity of some experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method effectively addresses the challenges of RS correction, deblurring, and frame interpolation, which are crucial for enhancing the quality of images captured by rolling shutter cameras.
- The methodology is innovative, utilizing an event camera to guide the process, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is significant as it is the first to tackle the problem of rolling shutter (RS) correction, deblurring, and frame interpolation using event data, which is a promising direction for future research.
- The methodology is supported by a robust theoretical framework and a detailed analysis, which adds credibility to the proposed solutions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the Spatial-Temporal Implicit Encoding (STE) and the Exposure Time Embedding (ETE) modules, which are crucial components of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the paper is questionable as some components, such as the use of deformable convolution and the pixel-by-pixel decoder, are not entirely novel.
- The experimental results are not convincing in some cases, with noticeable color distortions in the output images, which raises concerns about the practical applicability of the method.
- The paper does not adequately address the real-world application of the method, particularly in scenarios where ground truth data is not available.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation and training of the method, which could hinder reproducibility.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's performance relative to existing solutions.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the Spatial-Temporal Implicit Encoding (STE) and the Exposure Time Embedding (ETE) modules, including their specific architectures and training procedures?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the ground truth data is not available, and what are the implications of this for the practical application of the method?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental results, particularly the color distortions observed in the output images, and explain how these distortions affect the overall performance of the method?
- How does the method perform in real-world scenarios, and what are the practical implications of using the method in such settings?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including a discussion of the method's advantages and disadvantages compared to these existing solutions?
