 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FLIRT, a framework designed for automatic red teaming of black-box models, specifically focusing on text-to-image models. FLIRT leverages in-context learning in a feedback loop to evaluate models and uncover vulnerabilities, utilizing different feedback mechanisms to optimize adversarial prompts. The framework is tested against various models, including Stable Diffusion, and demonstrates the ability to trigger unsafe content generation. The paper also introduces a benchmark dataset of over 76k prompts that successfully trigger unsafe outcomes in text-to-image models, which could aid in future research on enhancing model safety.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed framework is efficient and effective, providing a comprehensive evaluation of model safety.
- The introduction of a benchmark dataset of over 76k prompts that trigger unsafe outcomes in text-to-image models is a significant contribution to the field.
- The framework is flexible and can be applied to any black-box model, making it a versatile tool for model evaluation.
- The paper includes a diverse set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework in red teaming text-to-image models.
- The ablation studies conducted provide valuable insights into the robustness of the findings and the trade-offs between different attack strategies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and ethical implications of the framework, particularly concerning the generation of unsafe content.
- The definition of "safety" and "unsafe" content is not clearly defined, which could lead to confusion about the actual safety of the generated content.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the generalizability of the findings to other types of models or datasets, limiting the broader applicability of the research.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the framework, particularly concerning the generation of unsafe content.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing state-of-the-art models, which could help in understanding the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed framework.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential improvements of the framework, particularly in terms of handling different types of unsafe content and improving the diversity of adversarial prompts.

**Questions:**
- How does the framework handle the potential for the red LM to generate prompts that could be used to trigger unsafe content generation in other models or applications?
- Could the authors provide more details on the process of generating the benchmark dataset, including the selection criteria for the prompts and the methodology used?
- How does the framework ensure that the generated prompts are diverse enough to effectively test the model's safety across different scenarios?
- Can the authors provide examples or case studies of how the framework has been used in real-world applications to improve model safety?
- How does the framework handle the potential for adversarial prompts to be transferred to other models or applications, especially those not intended for red teaming purposes?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the framework, particularly concerning the generation of unsafe content, and propose measures to mitigate these risks?
