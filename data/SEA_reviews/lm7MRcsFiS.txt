 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Ring-A-Bell, a method designed to evaluate the effectiveness of safety mechanisms in text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models by generating adversarial prompts that bypass these mechanisms. Ring-A-Bell employs a model-agnostic approach using a CLIP-based text encoder to identify problematic prompts, which are then used to test various safety mechanisms. The methodology involves generating adversarial prompts that are optimized to evade safety checks, and these prompts are tested against a range of online services and concept removal methods. The paper demonstrates that Ring-A-Bell can successfully bypass safety mechanisms, highlighting the vulnerabilities in current models. However, concerns are raised about the novelty and practical applicability of the method, as similar approaches have been previously explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue of ensuring the safety of AI systems, specifically focusing on text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models.
- The methodology is clearly described, making it easy to follow and understand, and the experiments are well-designed to test the effectiveness of safety mechanisms.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of Ring-A-Bell, demonstrating its effectiveness across various online services and concept removal methods.
- The use of a model-agnostic approach allows for the application of Ring-A-Bell across different models without requiring specific knowledge of the target model.
- The paper includes a detailed description of the experimental setup, including the number of prompt pairs used, which aids in reproducibility and transparency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of Ring-A-Bell.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as similar methods have been previously explored, such as using prompt engineering to create adversarial prompts.
- The practical applicability of the method is limited due to the assumption that the adversary has full access to the text encoder, which may not be feasible in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the implications of the findings, particularly how they could be used to enhance safety mechanisms or mitigate the risks associated with T2I models.
- There are issues with the clarity and presentation of the paper, including inconsistent terminology and formatting errors, which could affect the reader's understanding and engagement.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to previous works that have also used prompt engineering for adversarial prompt generation?
- How does Ring-A-Bell handle scenarios where the model-specific evaluation is not feasible due to the lack of access to certain models?
- Could the authors provide more details on the practical implications of the findings, such as how they could be used to improve safety mechanisms or mitigate risks associated with T2I models?
- How does the paper ensure the robustness of the findings against potential biases in the dataset used for adversarial prompt generation?
- Can the authors address the formatting and clarity issues noted in the paper to improve readability and professional presentation?
