 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach, Split-N-Denoise (SnD), aimed at enhancing the privacy of Large Language Models (LLMs) during inference. SnD employs a split-inference mechanism to execute the token embedding layer on the client side, allowing for the addition of noise to the embeddings before transmission to the server. This is followed by a denoising process on the client side using a pre-trained denoising model, which helps maintain the utility of the embeddings while ensuring privacy. The paper claims that SnD achieves a better privacy-utility trade-off compared to existing methods, as demonstrated through extensive experiments across various LLM architectures and downstream tasks. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the experimental results, and the potential for overfitting due to the limited size of the training dataset.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations that make it easy to understand.
- The proposed method, Split-N-Denoise (SnD), is innovative, combining split inference and denoising techniques to enhance privacy protection in LLMs.
- Extensive experiments are conducted to evaluate the performance of SnD, demonstrating its effectiveness in optimizing the privacy-utility trade-off across various LLM architectures and diverse downstream tasks.
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the communication and computation complexity, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed method.
- The method is shown to improve utility even in extremely low privacy budget settings, which is a significant advantage over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is questionable, as similar approaches have been previously explored in the literature.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the scalability and generalizability of the approach.
- The experimental results are not clearly presented, making it difficult to interpret the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for overfitting due to the limited size of the training dataset, which could affect the robustness of the denoising model.
- There is a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which is crucial for evaluating the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential for information leakage from the denoising model, which could undermine the privacy benefits of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to existing methods, particularly in terms of the technical contributions and practical implications?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of privacy-utility trade-off and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process of the denoising model, including the size and diversity of the training dataset?
- How does the denoising model ensure that it does not leak information about the user's data, especially given that it is trained on a public dataset?
- Can the authors explain the discrepancies in the experimental results, particularly the low AUC values observed in some scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform under different privacy budgets, and what are the implications for the utility and privacy of the embeddings?
