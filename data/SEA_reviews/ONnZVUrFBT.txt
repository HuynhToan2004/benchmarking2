 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the asynchronous multi-agent multi-armed bandit (AMA2B) problem, where agents pull arms asynchronously and communicate to share information, aiming for near-optimal regret with constant communication. The proposed algorithm, SE-AAC-ODC, includes an Accuracy Adaptive Communication (AAC) protocol to determine when to communicate and an On-Demand Communication (ODC) protocol to decide whom to communicate with. The algorithm is analyzed theoretically and empirically, showing near-optimal regret and constant communication costs. However, the paper's assumptions about the availability of agents and the constant communication requirement have been questioned, as they may not hold in practical scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, with clear presentation of ideas and results.
- The proposed algorithm achieves near-optimal regret with constant communication costs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis and empirical results that validate the proposed algorithm's effectiveness.
- The introduction of local differential privacy (LDP) into the asynchronous multi-agent multi-armed bandits (AMA2B) model is a novel approach that adds depth to the research.
- The paper addresses a practical problem of asynchronous communication in multi-agent multi-armed bandits, which is relevant in many real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions, such as the availability of agents at each time step and the constant communication requirement, may not hold in practical scenarios, which could limit the applicability of the proposed algorithm.
- The novelty of the algorithm is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant new insights or contributions.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its results with existing works, particularly in terms of communication complexity and regret bounds.
- The paper's experimental setup is limited, and the results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithm over existing methods.
- The paper's claims about the algorithm's performance and communication efficiency are not adequately supported by the experimental results, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made about the availability of agents at each time step and the implications of these assumptions on the practical applicability of the proposed algorithm?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare in terms of communication complexity and regret bounds with existing methods, particularly those that do not require constant communication?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the algorithm's performance varies with different parameter settings, such as the number of agents and arms?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed algorithm over existing methods, particularly in terms of communication efficiency and privacy protection?
- Can the authors provide more comprehensive experimental results to better demonstrate the superiority of their algorithm over existing methods?
