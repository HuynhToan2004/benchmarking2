 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FireAct, a method for fine-tuning language models (LMs) to enhance their performance in question-answering (QA) tasks. It leverages trajectories from multiple tasks and prompting methods to fine-tune LMs, demonstrating improved performance over few-shot prompting. The approach is validated through a series of experiments, showcasing the benefits of fine-tuning over prompting, and the potential of using smaller, open-source models for QA tasks. The paper also explores the impact of data diversity and the scalability of fine-tuning across different model sizes and prompting methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive overview of the current state of language agents and their fine-tuning, highlighting the need for more systematic studies in this area.
- The authors conduct extensive experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of fine-tuning over prompting for QA tasks.
- The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides detailed analysis of various factors affecting the performance of fine-tuned models.
- The findings of the paper are significant and could lead to more efficient and effective language agents, particularly in QA tasks.
- The paper introduces a novel method for fine-tuning language models, which could be beneficial for researchers and practitioners in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on QA tasks, which may not fully represent the capabilities of language agents. It would be beneficial to include a broader range of tasks to validate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its approach, such as the potential for overfitting or the generalizability of the findings to other types of tasks or models.
- The experimental design could be improved by including more diverse datasets and model sizes, and by exploring the impact of different fine-tuning methods on the performance of the models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the methodology and results, particularly in the areas of data selection and the choice of models for fine-tuning.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other fine-tuning methods, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- How do the authors ensure that the fine-tuned models do not overfit to the training data?
- Can the findings of this paper be generalized to other types of tasks beyond QA?
- How does the choice of base LM affect the performance of the fine-tuned model?
- What are the implications of using different prompting methods, such as CoT and Reflexion, in the fine-tuning process?
- Could the authors provide more details on the data selection process, particularly how they ensure that the data used for fine-tuning is representative and diverse?
- How do the authors address the potential for negative societal impacts of their work, particularly in the context of language agents?
