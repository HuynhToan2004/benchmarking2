 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the alignment and evaluation of large language models (LLMs) using sparse feedback, specifically focusing on the inconsistency between ratings and rankings. The authors collect data from human and AI annotators, demonstrating a significant disagreement between these two methods, with 60% of comparisons showing inconsistencies. The paper also explores the impact of these inconsistencies on the performance of LLMs when trained with different feedback types. The findings suggest that the choice of feedback protocol can significantly influence the evaluation of LLMs, with rankings generally preferred over ratings. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the design of feedback acquisition protocols and the alignment of LLMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of large language models (LLMs) by investigating the inconsistency between ratings and rankings in feedback acquisition, which is crucial for improving LLMs.
- The collection of data from both human and AI annotators, along with a detailed analysis of the inconsistencies, provides valuable insights into the reliability and effectiveness of different feedback methods.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the experimental setup and results, which aids in understanding the complex interactions between different feedback methods and their impact on model performance.
- The findings are substantial and significant, highlighting the need for a more rigorous and systematic approach to feedback acquisition and alignment in LLMs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation for the observed inconsistencies between ratings and rankings, and the potential causes, such as the inherent nature of the feedback methods or biases in the data, are not adequately explored.
- The analysis of the data and the results is somewhat limited, with some findings, such as the agreement between human and AI annotators, being expected and not particularly insightful.
- The paper's focus on sparse feedback methods might limit its applicability to more densely annotated datasets, which could provide a more nuanced understanding of the feedback process.
- There is a lack of discussion on the implications of the findings for the design of feedback acquisition protocols and the alignment of LLMs, which could be a missed opportunity to contribute to practical solutions in the field.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential biases in the data, such as the influence of annotator preferences on the feedback, which could affect the reliability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations for the observed inconsistencies between ratings and rankings, and explore potential causes such as biases in the data or the inherent nature of the feedback methods?
- How does the quality of the data used in the study affect the results, and could the authors discuss potential biases in the data and their impact on the findings?
- Could the authors elaborate on the implications of their findings for the design of feedback acquisition protocols and the alignment of LLMs, and suggest practical solutions for addressing the inconsistencies observed?
- In the evaluation section, could the authors provide more detailed results on the performance of the Best-of-n policy when trained with different feedback types, and discuss the implications of these findings for the practical application of the findings?
