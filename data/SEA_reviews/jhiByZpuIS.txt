 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MSfusion, a collaborative learning framework designed to train large models on resource-constrained devices by employing a double shifting model splitting scheme. This approach allows each participant to train on a subset of model parameters, thereby reducing computational and communication costs. The framework includes novel designs such as adaptive model overlapping and contrastive loss functions to maintain training effectiveness despite model shifts across participants. Extensive experiments on image and NLP datasets demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of MSfusion, showcasing its strong scalability. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues in the presentation and lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, which could limit its impact.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant challenge of training large models on resource-constrained devices, which is a critical issue in the field of machine learning.
- The proposed method, MSfusion, is innovative and effective, utilizing a double shifting model splitting scheme that significantly reduces computation and communication costs for individual participants.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components, such as the adaptive model overlapping and contrastive loss functions.
- Extensive experiments on image and NLP datasets demonstrate the advantages of MSfusion in performance and efficiency for training large models, and its strong scalability.
- The methodology is designed to maintain training effectiveness against model shift across participants, which is crucial for collaborative learning scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from clarity issues in the presentation, with some sections being difficult to understand and certain figures and equations being unclear or not well-explained.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with the paper not adequately comparing MSfusion with existing methods, and the choice of baselines being questionable.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other methods, particularly those that use different splitting strategies, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of MSfusion's effectiveness.
- There are issues with the scalability and applicability of the method, particularly in scenarios where the model size is large or the data distribution is highly imbalanced.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of model drift, which is a critical challenge in collaborative learning scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations and clarifications for the unclear sections of the paper, particularly those related to the double shifting model splitting scheme and the adaptive model overlapping?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing methods in terms of performance, scalability, and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results and comparisons for the ablation studies, particularly for different model sizes and data distributions?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the data distribution is highly imbalanced or the model size is large?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other methods, particularly those that use different splitting strategies, to better understand the advantages and disadvantages of MSfusion?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of model drift, and what steps are taken to ensure that the models trained by different participants do not diverge significantly?
