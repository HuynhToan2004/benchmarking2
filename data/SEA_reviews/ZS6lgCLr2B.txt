 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the robustness of federated learning (FL) against Byzantine clients by analyzing a variant of the FedAvg algorithm named FedRo. FedRo is designed to handle client subsampling and multiple local steps, which are crucial aspects of FL that have been overlooked in previous studies. The paper presents theoretical analysis and empirical results, demonstrating the convergence of FedRo and its robustness against Byzantine clients. It introduces conditions for near-optimal convergence and discusses the impact of client subsampling and local steps on the learning process. The theoretical results are supported by experiments on the FEMNIST dataset, although the paper's novelty and practical applicability are questioned due to its similarity to existing works and the lack of diverse experimental settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations, making it easy to follow.
- It introduces a novel approach to robust federated learning by considering client subsampling and multiple local steps, which are crucial for the practical implementation of FL.
- The theoretical analysis is solid, providing a comprehensive understanding of the convergence properties of FedRo under different conditions.
- The paper includes empirical results that support the theoretical findings, enhancing the credibility of the results.
- The discussion on the diminishing returns of FedRo with respect to the number of sampled clients is insightful and provides practical guidance for tuning parameters.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it primarily combines existing techniques without introducing significant new ideas or insights.
- The experimental settings are limited, focusing only on the FEMNIST dataset and a single CNN model, which may not generalize well to other datasets or models.
- The convergence results are not clearly distinguished from existing works, and the paper does not adequately discuss the similarities and differences with prior research.
- The paper's scope is limited to a specific FL setting, which may not be applicable to more general scenarios.
- The paper does not address the practical implications of the theoretical results, such as how to choose the parameters for different datasets or the impact of varying parameters on the performance.
- The paper's assumptions, particularly regarding the bounded heterogeneity and the convergence guarantees, are not well-explained or justified, which could limit the understanding and applicability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their work and how it differs from existing studies, particularly in terms of the theoretical contributions and experimental settings?
- How does the paper's theoretical analysis compare to existing works, such as those by Karimireddy et al. (2020) and Yang et al. (2021)?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples of how the theoretical results can be applied in practical settings, especially in terms of choosing parameters for different datasets?
- How does the paper's approach handle the potential for convergence guarantees when the number of Byzantine clients exceeds the threshold, and what are the implications of this on the practical applicability of the results?
- Could the authors address the concerns regarding the assumptions and the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly in terms of their generalizability and applicability to different FL settings?
