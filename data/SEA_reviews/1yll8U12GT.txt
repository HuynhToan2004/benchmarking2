 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to decision tree learning by integrating a deep neural network architecture, termed Deep Linearly Gated Network (DLGN), which utilizes a hyperplane discontinuity score (HDS) to guide the splitting of data. The DLGN is designed to mimic the behavior of oblique decision trees (ODTs), which can represent decision trees that are not axis-parallel. The methodology involves training a DLGN on the entire dataset and then clustering the learned hyperplanes to select the most discontinuous one, which is used as the root node for the tree. This process is repeated recursively for each sub-dataset, leading to a decision tree that is more expressive than traditional axis-parallel trees. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing that it can outperform other decision tree learning algorithms in certain scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to decision tree learning by integrating a deep neural network architecture, which is both innovative and theoretically sound.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the underlying principles and their implications for feature learning in deep networks.
- The experiments conducted on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing promising results compared to other decision tree learning algorithms.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and includes a detailed description of the proposed method and its implementation.
- The use of a hyperplane discontinuity score (HDS) to guide the splitting of data is a novel and effective approach, which has the potential to significantly improve the performance of decision tree learning algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other decision tree learning algorithms, particularly those that use different splitting criteria or different network architectures.
- The methodology, while innovative, is somewhat limited in its scope, as it only applies to binary classification problems and does not extend to multi-class or multi-label classification scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant limitation for large-scale datasets or complex models.
- There are some issues with the clarity and precision of the mathematical notation and definitions used in the paper, which could confuse readers or lead to misunderstandings.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
