 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Model-based Offline Reinforcement Learning with Reward Consistency (MOREC), a novel method for enhancing model-based offline reinforcement learning (MBRL) by integrating a dynamics reward function. This function, derived from an inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) perspective, is used to filter and select transitions that maximize the dynamics reward. The methodology involves training a dynamics model using a dataset, learning a dynamics reward function from the data, and utilizing this function to filter transitions during rollouts. The paper claims that MOREC improves generalization and outperforms existing methods on benchmarks like D4RL and NeoRL. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the method's novelty, the generalization of the dynamics reward function, and the empirical results' robustness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise descriptions of the proposed method and its experimental results.
- The idea of learning a dynamics reward function and filtering transitions based on it is innovative and potentially beneficial for improving model-based offline reinforcement learning (MBRL).
- The empirical results are strong, showing significant performance improvements over previous state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods in various tasks.
- The paper is original in its approach to learning a dynamics reward function, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides theoretical justification for the dynamics reward learning algorithm, enhancing the credibility of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of how the dynamics reward function compares to other methods like CQL, TT, and RAHD.
- There is a lack of clarity on the generalization of the dynamics reward function and how it performs in out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the method, especially concerning the computational demands and the size of the offline dataset required.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for the dynamics reward function to be influenced by artifacts in the dataset.
- There are several presentation issues, including unclear definitions and figures that could be improved for better understanding.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the implications of using a discriminator for dynamics reward learning, particularly in terms of scalability and computational efficiency.

**Questions:**
- How does the dynamics reward function compare to other methods like CQL, TT, and RAHD? What are the advantages and disadvantages of using a dynamics reward function versus these other methods?
- Can the dynamics reward function be generalized to handle different types of environments or tasks? How does it perform in OOD scenarios?
- How does the method handle the scalability issues, particularly in terms of computational demands and the size of the offline dataset required?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process for the dynamics reward function, including the computational resources and time required?
- In light of the potential limitations discussed, how does the proposed method address the challenges of learning a dynamics reward function from offline data, particularly concerning the potential for artifacts in the dataset?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the discriminator in the dynamics reward learning process, especially in terms of scalability and computational efficiency?
