 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for computing the Sliced Wasserstein (SW) distance using Quasi-Monte Carlo (QMC) methods, specifically focusing on the 3D case. It proposes various methods to construct QMC point sets on the unit sphere and introduces Quasi-Sliced Wasserstein (QSW) and Randomized Quasi-Sliced Wasserstein (RQSW) approximations. Theoretical analysis and experimental results are provided to demonstrate the convergence and effectiveness of these methods. The paper also discusses the computational complexity and space complexity of the proposed methods, comparing them with traditional Monte Carlo methods. Despite its technical strength and novelty, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty in the theoretical contributions and the need for a clearer motivation and broader experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear organization and a comprehensive literature review.
- The proposed methods are well-motivated and clearly explained, with a focus on improving the estimation of the Sliced Wasserstein (SW) distance, particularly in the 3D setting.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing the effectiveness of the proposed methods in various applications such as point-cloud interpolation, image style transfer, and training deep point-cloud autoencoders.
- The paper introduces the first QMC point set on the unit-hypersphere, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to show the convergence of QSW to the SW distance and the unbiasedness of RQSW.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications for the proposed methods, particularly in the context of the SW distance and its applications.
- The theoretical contributions are seen as limited, with some results being straightforward or already known.
- The experimental validation is limited to a few examples, and it would be beneficial to see more comprehensive experiments across different scenarios.
- There are some presentation issues, such as typos and unclear descriptions in figures and algorithms.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the computational complexity and space complexity of the proposed methods, which are crucial for practical applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed methods, which could include the need for larger sample sizes for certain methods and the potential computational overhead of some approaches.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the differences between the proposed methods and those discussed in [1]?
- In the context of the SW distance, why is the use of QMC methods beneficial?
- How does the proposed method perform in higher-dimensional cases, and what are the implications for the computational complexity and space complexity?
- Can you provide more details on the convergence rate of the proposed methods, especially in comparison to other methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods for computing the SW distance, such as those based on low-discrepancy sequences on the unit-cube?
- Can you elaborate on the potential applications of the proposed methods beyond the examples provided in the paper?
