 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PLASMA, a novel approach that utilizes smaller language models for procedural planning and language-based planning. PLASMA employs a two-phase methodology: first, it uses a large language model to generate plans from goals, and then it distills these plans into a smaller model using a symbolic procedural knowledge distillation method. The second phase involves an inference-time algorithm that enhances the model's reasoning capabilities. The paper also introduces a new task, replanning, and demonstrates that smaller models can outperform larger ones in various planning scenarios. The approach is supported by extensive experiments and human evaluations, showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- It introduces a novel approach to procedural planning using smaller language models, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods, including both automatic and human evaluations, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The introduction of a new task, replanning, and the creation of a new dataset, CoPlan, contribute valuable resources to the research community.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear figures and detailed descriptions that aid in understanding the complex processes involved.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which could provide a more balanced view of the research.
- There is a noticeable absence of comparisons with other planning methods, such as those using planning algorithms or other large language models, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of reproducibility, particularly in terms of the availability of the datasets and code used in the experiments.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the societal impacts of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of bias and fairness in the generated plans.
- The use of only one teacher model raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings to other large language models.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on the limitations of the proposed methods and how these might affect the practical application of the models?
- How does the proposed method compare to other planning methods, particularly those using planning algorithms or other large language models?
- Can you provide more details on the dataset curation process, including the number of examples used and the criteria for selecting examples?
- How does the paper address the issue of reproducibility, especially in terms of making the datasets and code publicly available?
- Could you discuss the potential societal impacts of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of bias and fairness in the generated plans?
- How does the proposed method perform in more complex or realistic scenarios, such as those involving multiple agents or more complex environments?
