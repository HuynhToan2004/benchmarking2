 **Summary:**
The paper presents a comparative analysis of three offline reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms: Conservative Q-Learning (CQL), Behavior Cloning (BC), and Decision Transformer (DT). The authors evaluate these methods across various benchmarks, including D4RL and Robomimic, to assess their performance under different conditions such as data quality, task complexity, and stochasticity. Key findings include DT's robustness and its superior performance in scenarios with sparse rewards and low-quality data. The paper also discusses the scalability of DT and provides recommendations for its practical implementation. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope, focusing only on these three algorithms, and for not providing a clear theoretical justification for the observed trends.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of three prominent offline RL algorithms, comparing them under various conditions, which is useful for practitioners seeking to select the best algorithm for their specific use case.
- The experimental design is well-structured, with clear takeaways presented in a logical order, making it easy for readers to understand the implications of the findings.
- The authors have conducted a large number of experiments, which support their claims and provide valuable insights into the performance of different algorithms in different scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation and good organization, which enhances readability and understanding.
- The results are robust, with the conclusions supported by a significant number of runs and seeds, which adds credibility to the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear theoretical justification for the observed trends and does not provide a detailed explanation of why DT performs better in certain scenarios.
- The evaluation is limited to only three algorithms, which may not be representative of the broader offline RL landscape.
- The experimental setup could be improved by including more diverse benchmarks, such as those from the embodied AI community, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of DT, particularly in terms of parameter size, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the study, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous discussion on the limitations and assumptions of the Decision Transformer, which is a significant contribution of the study.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of why DT performs better in scenarios with sparse rewards and low-quality data?
- How does the performance of DT compare to other algorithms when the data is of higher quality, such as in expert datasets?
- Could the authors clarify the role of stochasticity in DT's performance and whether it is a significant factor in its effectiveness?
- How does the parameter size of DT affect its performance, and what are the practical implications of scaling the model size?
- In terms of practical implementation, what are the key considerations for using DT in real-world applications, particularly in scenarios where data quality and stochasticity are high?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their study, particularly in terms of the assumptions and constraints of the Decision Transformer, and how these might affect the generalizability of the findings?
