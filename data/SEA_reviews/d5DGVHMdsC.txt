 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CLIN, a language-based agent that utilizes a dynamic, textual memory to continually improve and generalize across various tasks and environments. This agent, which is evaluated on the ScienceWorld benchmark, demonstrates significant improvements in performance compared to state-of-the-art reflective language agents like Reflexion. The memory system is designed to store causal abstractions, which are updated after each trial to aid in future decision-making. The agent's performance is validated through extensive experiments, showcasing its ability to adapt to new tasks and environments. However, concerns were raised regarding the clarity of the experimental setup, the novelty of the approach, and the scalability of the memory system.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel approach to continual learning using a dynamic, textual memory, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology and experimental setup.
- The use of a memory generator to update memory after each trial is a novel and effective approach, enhancing the agent's ability to adapt and learn from past experiences.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including ablation studies that support the claims made.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the experimental setup, particularly the number of trials and the specifics of the environment resets, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as similar ideas have been explored in prior works, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing methods.
- The scalability of the memory system is a concern, as it may not be practical for larger or more complex environments.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the potential for memory overloading, the impact of memory size on performance, and the generalizability of the method across different types of environments.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that do not use LLMs, to establish a clearer advantage of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the number of trials and the specifics of the environment resets?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance and scalability?
- What are the specific advantages of using a memory generator over other methods for updating memory after each trial?
- How does the size of the memory impact the performance of the agent, and what strategies are used to manage the growth of the memory over time?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate how the memory generator updates memory after each trial and how this affects the agent's decision-making process?
