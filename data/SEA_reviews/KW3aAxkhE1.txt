 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, TextBind, for enhancing the multimodal instruction-following capabilities of large language models (LLMs) using an almost annotation-free framework. This method involves generating multi-turn multimodal instruction-response conversations from LLMs using image-caption pairs. The authors propose a new dataset, TextBindEval, for evaluating the performance of LLMs in handling multi-turn conversations with interleaved images. The methodology includes a new architecture, MIM, which integrates image encoder and decoder models seamlessly. Extensive experiments demonstrate that MIM trained on TextBind achieves remarkable generation capabilities in multi-modal conversations compared to recent baselines. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty, unclear presentation, and limited experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel dataset, TextBindEval, which is designed to evaluate the performance of LLMs in handling multi-turn conversations with interleaved images, providing a new benchmark for evaluating multimodal instruction-following capabilities.
- The methodology is innovative, using image-caption pairs to generate multi-turn multimodal instruction-response conversations, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the inclusion of detailed prompts and templates in the appendix is commendable.
- The dataset and the methodology are expected to be released, which will benefit the community by providing a practical and scalable solution for various real-world tasks.
- The paper includes a variety of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showcasing its potential for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty in its approach, as similar methods have been previously proposed, such as using image-text pairs to generate multimodal instruction-response conversations.
- The presentation of the paper is unclear, making it difficult to understand the methodology and the results.
- The experimental validation is limited, with only one model used for evaluation, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalization ability of the model to unseen images, which is a significant limitation in practical applications.
- The dataset construction process is not sufficiently detailed, which raises concerns about the quality and diversity of the data.
- The paper does not provide sufficient qualitative results, which are crucial for understanding the effectiveness and limitations of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the dataset construction process, particularly how the quality and diversity of the data are ensured?
- How does the model perform on unseen images, and what measures are in place to ensure its generalization ability?
- Could the authors clarify the presentation of the paper, particularly in sections where the methodology and results are described, to improve understanding and clarity?
- In the experimental results, how does the proposed method perform in terms of generalization ability, especially when compared to other models that have been trained on different datasets?
- How do the authors address the issue of potential hallucination in the generated responses, and what measures are in place to prevent this?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the diversity and quality of the generated responses, particularly in terms of how they compare to existing datasets and models?
