 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel model, the Mixture Stochastic Block Model (MSBM), for community detection in multiplex graphs. The MSBM groups similar layers based on shared community structures and assigns vertices to blocks within each group. The model uses an iterative algorithm with Expectation-Maximization (EM) and Variational EM (VEM) techniques for inference. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing it with other methods like K-means, SBM, GLouvain, and GFSC. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the model's identifiability and consistency, although it lacks a detailed discussion on computational complexity and scalability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel model, the MSBM, which groups similar layers based on shared community structures and assigns vertices to blocks within each group, offering a new approach to community detection in multiplex graphs.
- The MSBM is supported by theoretical guarantees for identifiability and consistency, providing a robust theoretical foundation for the model.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear presentation and detailed mathematical derivations, which aids in understanding the model and its applications.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the MSBM in identifying consistent community structures across diverse multiplex graphs.
- The paper is significant in its contribution to the field of community detection in multiplex graphs, addressing a gap in the literature by proposing a model that groups similar layers based on shared community structures.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the MSBM, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in large-scale real-world problems.
- The experimental setup is limited, with only a few synthetic datasets used to evaluate the model's performance. More diverse and real-world datasets are needed to validate the model's effectiveness across different scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, which could help in understanding the model's advantages and disadvantages more clearly.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, especially in the introduction and related work sections.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the MSBM, which could provide a more balanced view of the model's capabilities and potential improvements.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the MSBM, particularly in terms of the number of layers and vertices in the multiplex graph?
- How does the MSBM compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the MSBM be extended to handle directed graphs or weighted graphs? How would this affect the model's performance and applicability?
- How does the MSBM handle the initialization of parameters, particularly in the presence of noisy or incomplete data?
- Could the authors provide more insights into the model's assumptions, such as the distribution of layers across different groups and the distribution of vertices within each group?
- How does the MSBM handle the identification of different community structures within the same layer of a multiplex graph?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other methods, including a discussion on the similarities and differences between the MSBM and other existing models?
