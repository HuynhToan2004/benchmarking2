 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Contrastive Grouping-based Invariant Learning (CGIL) for graph invariant learning, which aims to address the out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization problem in graph neural networks (GNNs). The method involves a node grouper that clusters graph nodes into invariant and contrast groups, and an invariant subgraph generator that selects nodes with high weights in the invariant group to generate potential invariant subgraphs. These subgraphs are then used to learn invariant features. The approach is evaluated on various graph classification tasks and demonstrates better performance than existing methods. However, concerns are raised regarding the clarity of the methodology, the lack of detailed experimental setup, and the need for a more rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to graph invariant learning by focusing on the connectivity information of graph nodes, which is a departure from traditional methods that rely solely on predictive performance.
- The methodology is well-explained, and the paper is easy to follow, with a clear structure and good presentation of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses an important problem in graph invariant learning, which is crucial for improving the performance of graph neural networks (GNNs) in real-world scenarios.
- The proposed method, CGIL, shows better performance than several state-of-the-art methods, indicating its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the methodology and the definitions used. For instance, the definition of "node grouping" and the role of "supernodes" are not well-explained.
- The experimental setup and the details of the baseline methods are not adequately described, making it difficult to replicate the results and compare the proposed method fairly.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with the latest state-of-the-art methods, such as GIL and CIGA, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's advantages or disadvantages.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis and explanation of the experimental results, particularly the performance of CGIL in different scenarios and the impact of hyperparameters.
- The paper suffers from several grammatical and typographical errors that need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and roles of "node grouping" and "supernodes" in the methodology?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of spurious correlation in graph invariant learning?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the performance of CGIL in different scenarios and the impact of hyperparameters?
- Why were certain baseline methods not included in the comparisons, and how does the proposed method compare with the latest state-of-the-art methods?
- Can the authors address the concerns regarding the clarity and completeness of the methodology and the experimental setup?
