 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a hierarchical planning approach using diffusion models to address the limitations of the Diffuser model in handling long-horizon tasks. The proposed method, termed the Hierarchical Diffuser, employs a two-level diffusion model to generate subgoals and detailed trajectories between them. The approach aims to improve planning efficiency by reducing the computational burden of dense trajectory modeling and enhancing generalization capabilities. The paper presents empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the Hierarchical Diffuser in various tasks, showing improvements over the Diffuser and other hierarchical planning methods. However, the paper faces criticism for its clarity in explaining the subgoal generation process, the lack of theoretical justification for certain design choices, and the limited scope of environments tested.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and efficient, with good empirical results demonstrating improvements over the Diffuser and other hierarchical planning methods.
- The introduction of hierarchical planning into the Diffuser framework is a novel and interesting approach, which is supported by comprehensive experimental evaluations.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the generalization performance, which helps in understanding the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method.
- The use of diffusion models to model the dataset distribution and the two-level diffusion model for subgoal generation and detailed trajectory planning is a novel and effective approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the subgoals are generated, particularly the role of the guidance function in this process.
- The design of the low-level planner is not well-justified, and there is a lack of comparison with other hierarchical planning methods such as IRIS and HiGoC.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the training process and the role of the guidance function in the high-level planner.
- The experimental setup is limited to simple environments, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalization capabilities of the proposed method.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the computational costs of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including its dependency on the quality of the dataset and the choice of fixed sub-goal intervals.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the subgoals are generated and the role of the guidance function in this process?
- How does the proposed method compare to other hierarchical planning methods such as IRIS and HiGoC, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and generalization capabilities?
- Can you provide more details on the training process, including the number of training steps and the computational resources required?
- How does the proposed method handle the credit assignment problem, especially in long-horizon tasks?
- Could you elaborate on the design choices for the low-level planner and discuss the potential benefits of using different guidance functions for different sub-goals?
- How does the proposed method handle the generalization to unseen states or goals, and what are the implications of this for the practical applicability of the method?
