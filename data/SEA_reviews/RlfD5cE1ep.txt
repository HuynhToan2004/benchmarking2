 **Summary:**
The paper explores the dynamics of non-contrastive learning using the cosine loss, focusing on the role of feature normalization in preventing collapse. It extends previous theoretical analyses by considering the cosine loss, which is commonly used in practical non-contrastive methods like BYOL and SimSiam. The authors demonstrate that the cosine loss induces sixth-order dynamics, contrasting the third-order dynamics of L2 loss, and argue that feature normalization plays a crucial role in maintaining stability. The paper provides a theoretical analysis and numerical simulations to support these claims, although it lacks empirical results on real datasets and comparisons with other non-contrastive methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel theoretical analysis on the dynamics of non-contrastive learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- It introduces a new understanding of the role of feature normalization in preventing collapse in non-contrastive learning, which is a significant extension of previous work.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors provide a clear and detailed analysis of the dynamics of non-contrastive learning, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of neural networks in practical scenarios.
- The paper is well-positioned within the existing literature, effectively distinguishing its contributions from previous works.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical results on real datasets, which could have strengthened the claims made about the practical implications of the theoretical findings.
- The paper does not include a comparison with other non-contrastive methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive understanding of the role of feature normalization in non-contrastive learning.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the high-dimensional limit and the symmetry of the projection head, are not well justified or explained, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's focus on feature normalization as the primary factor preventing collapse may oversimplify the issue, as other factors such as the architecture of the network could also play a role.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are somewhat limited by the simplicity of the model used, which may not fully capture the complexity of real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the paper's analysis compare with other non-contrastive methods, particularly those that do not use feature normalization?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis affect the practical applicability of the findings?
- How do the authors justify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the high-dimensional limit and the symmetry of the projection head?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of the theoretical findings, especially in terms of how they might be applied to improve non-contrastive learning methods in practical scenarios?
- The paper discusses the role of feature normalization in preventing collapse. How do the authors address the potential impact of other factors, such as the architecture of the network, on the stability of non-contrastive learning?
