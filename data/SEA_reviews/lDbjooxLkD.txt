 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new evaluation strategy called PASSUNTIL to assess the scaling properties of large language models (LLMs) by estimating the probability of a model passing a task. This strategy uses a massive sampling approach during the decoding phase to improve the evaluation resolution, which is crucial for detecting subtle performance improvements in smaller models. The authors demonstrate that this method can predict the performance of larger models with high accuracy, even though some performance gains may not be linearly scalable with model size. The paper also explores the emergence of new abilities in LLMs and proposes a mathematical framework to describe these phenomena. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticisms regarding its experimental scope, the clarity of its experimental results, and the generalizability of its findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel evaluation strategy, PASSUNTIL, which enables the quantitative exploration of scaling properties of task performance.
- The proposed evaluation strategy can predict the performance of larger models with high accuracy, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and well-designed figures that aid in understanding the content.
- The mathematical formulation of the task scaling law and the identification of an accelerated emergence phenomenon are original and valuable contributions to the field.
- The paper provides a systematic attempt to verify predictable scaling, which is a significant issue in the field of large language models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed evaluation strategy, particularly in terms of its ability to predict the performance of smaller models and its generalizability across different tasks and models.
- The experimental results are not convincingly presented, with some figures and data not adequately explained or interpreted, which affects the clarity and reliability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed evaluation strategy with larger models, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion and comparison with existing works, particularly those that address similar issues in the field.
- The paper's claims about the predictability of emergent abilities and the generalizability of the findings are not convincingly supported by the presented evidence.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed evaluation strategy perform when applied to smaller models, and what are the implications for its generalizability across different model sizes?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the sampling process is controlled and how it affects the evaluation results?
- How does the proposed evaluation strategy compare to other existing methods for evaluating the performance of large language models?
- Could the authors clarify the claims about the predictability of emergent abilities and the generalizability of the findings, perhaps by providing additional empirical evidence or theoretical justifications?
- How does the paper's approach to evaluating the emergence of new abilities in LLMs compare to other recent works in the field, and what are the implications of these differences for our understanding of LLM scaling?
