 **Summary:**
The paper investigates stochastic contextual bandits with graph feedback, where the learner receives rewards from multiple arms, each associated with a graph. The authors propose a novel algorithm that adapts to time-varying graph structures, improving regret bounds in stochastic settings compared to existing methods. The algorithm is designed to be computationally efficient and does not require prior knowledge of graph parameters, making it practical for real-world applications. The paper also presents a gap-dependent upper bound on regrets and validates the findings through extensive numerical experiments. However, the paper's contributions are questioned due to its similarity to existing works and the limited novelty of the algorithm, which primarily extends prior work by incorporating graph feedback.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a novel problem of contextual bandits with graph feedback, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed algorithm is simple, computationally efficient, and does not require prior knowledge of graph parameters, making it practical for real-world applications.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow presentation, and includes extensive numerical experiments that validate the findings.
- The authors compare their algorithm with existing works and provide a comprehensive comparison, highlighting the strengths and limitations of their approach.
- The paper introduces a gap-dependent upper bound on regrets, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is limited as it primarily extends prior work by incorporating graph feedback, and the novelty of the algorithm is questioned.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing works, such as those by Zhang et al. (2023), which also address similar issues.
- The paper's assumptions and theoretical results are not well-explained, particularly the assumptions about the graph and the function class, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithm and the conditions under which it might not perform optimally.
- The numerical experiments are limited in scope, focusing only on randomly generated graphs, and do not include experiments on real-world datasets, which could have provided more robust validation of the algorithm's performance.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how their work differs from and improves upon the results of Zhang et al. (2023)?
- How does the proposed algorithm handle time-varying graph structures, and what are the implications of this on the performance of the algorithm?
- What are the conditions under which the algorithm might not perform optimally, and how does this affect the practical applicability of the algorithm?
- Could the authors provide more details on the construction of the exploration set and how it impacts the performance of the algorithm?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical implementation?
- Can the authors provide more detailed numerical results, such as regret vs. time plots, to better illustrate the performance of the algorithm over time?
