 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel continual reinforcement learning (CRL) method named Learning with Language Inference and Tips (LLIT), which integrates large language models (LLMs) to generate task descriptions and tips. These descriptions are used to train an auxiliary reward model and a prompt pool, which facilitate task-agnostic learning. The LLIT method leverages these elements to manage the trade-off between plasticity and stability, allowing for efficient learning and adaptation across tasks. Experiments conducted on the Continual World benchmark demonstrate that LLIT outperforms existing CRL methods in terms of task performance and forgetting reduction. However, concerns were raised regarding the clarity of the method's integration with LLMs, the fairness of the comparison with other methods, and the generalizability of the results across different benchmarks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to continual reinforcement learning (CRL) by integrating large language models (LLMs) to generate task descriptions and tips, which are then used to train an auxiliary reward model and a prompt pool.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the components and their interactions, making the paper easy to follow.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, comparing the proposed method against a variety of baselines and demonstrating its effectiveness through strong empirical results.
- The integration of LLMs into CRL is a significant advancement, showcasing the potential of this technology in enhancing the efficiency and generalizability of RL policies.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the experimental results and methodologies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining how the LLM is integrated into the CRL framework, particularly in how it provides task descriptions and tips.
- The paper's claims about the superiority of LLMs over other methods like experience replay are not convincingly supported by empirical evidence.
- The comparison with other methods, such as those that do not use LLMs, may not be entirely fair due to the different information available to the LLM-based method.
- The generalization of the LLIT method across different benchmarks and environments is not thoroughly explored, raising concerns about its applicability in more varied or complex settings.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be significant in real-world applications.
- There are some minor issues with the presentation of the paper, such as typos and unclear figures, which could be improved for better clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify how the LLM is integrated into the CRL framework and how it provides task descriptions and tips?
- How does the LLIT method ensure the generalizability of the learned policies across different environments and tasks?
- What are the specific advantages of using LLMs over other methods like experience replay in CRL?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the hyperparameters used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the LLIT method compare to other methods that do not use LLMs, especially in terms of computational efficiency and memory requirements?
- What are the potential societal impacts of the LLIT method, and how are these addressed in the paper?
- Could you address the minor presentation issues, such as typos and unclear figures, to enhance the clarity and professionalism of the paper?
