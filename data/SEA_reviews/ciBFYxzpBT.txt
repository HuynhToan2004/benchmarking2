 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the application of pre-trained transformer models in reinforcement learning (RL), focusing on self-supervised learning techniques. It introduces a novel methodology for tokenization at the action and state component level, using first-principles pre-training objectives like next token prediction. The study compares this approach with existing methods like MTM and SMART, showing that the proposed method can enhance performance in various downstream tasks. The experiments were conducted across multiple environments, including MuJoCo and Brax, and the results demonstrate improvements in zero-shot transfer learning. The paper also discusses the potential of this approach in real-world applications, suggesting its applicability in scenarios where sensor information is critical.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel methodology for tokenization at the action and state component level, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The use of first-principles pre-training objectives like next token prediction is innovative and has shown promising results.
- The study is comprehensive, covering a wide range of general downstream tasks and providing valuable insights for practitioners in building robust models.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results, which makes it accessible and easy to follow.
- The experiments conducted are thorough, covering various aspects such as different tokenization strategies, masking patterns, and pre-training objectives, which provide a robust evaluation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods like MTM and SMART, particularly in terms of computational resources and parameter efficiency.
- There is a need for more extensive experiments across different environments and tasks to validate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability in real-world scenarios with noisy or incomplete sensor data.
- The results presented in the paper are sometimes difficult to interpret due to the aggregation of results across different environments, which may obscure the individual performance of the model in specific tasks.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational resources and parameter efficiency of your method compared to existing methods like MTM and SMART?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to more complex tasks or environments beyond those tested in the study?
- Can you clarify the differences between the C-GPT and C-BERT models, particularly in terms of their training objectives and the impact of these differences on performance?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where sensor information is noisy or incomplete, which are common in real-world applications?
- Could you elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential negative societal impacts, if any?
