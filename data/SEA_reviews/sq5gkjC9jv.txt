 **Summary:**
The paper explores the topological expressivity of ReLU neural networks, focusing on the Betti numbers of the sublevel sets of their activation functions. It provides both upper and lower bounds for these numbers, demonstrating that the growth of Betti numbers depends on the depth of the network. The authors use a combination of theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to show that deep networks can represent more complex topological spaces than shallow ones. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the classification of data sets with nontrivial topologies, suggesting that deep networks are necessary for accurately modeling such data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a fundamental question about the expressivity of neural networks, focusing on the topological complexity of their sublevel sets, which is an interesting and novel approach.
- It provides both upper and lower bounds on the topological expressivity of ReLU neural networks, which are supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence.
- The paper is well-written and clear, making it accessible to readers not specialized in topology. It also includes a detailed description of the proofs in the appendix, which enhances the understanding of the theoretical underpinnings.
- The authors demonstrate the relevance of their findings to the classification of data sets with nontrivial topologies, showing that deep networks are necessary for accurately modeling such data.
- The paper includes a construction that is robust to small perturbations in the parameter space, indicating that the results are not overly sensitive to small variations in the weights of the neural network.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contributions are somewhat limited by prior work in the field, particularly in terms of the construction of networks with large Betti numbers, which is similar to existing methods.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in the introduction and related work sections, to better motivate the research and clearly articulate the contributions.
- The paper primarily focuses on the Betti numbers of sublevel sets, which may not fully capture the topological complexity of the data sets. It would be beneficial to consider other topological invariants like cohomology rings or homotopy groups.
- The paper's results are mainly asymptotic, which might not be as relevant in practical scenarios where constant factors can significantly affect the performance of neural networks.
- The paper's reliance on specific architectural constraints, such as the width of hidden layers being a multiple of the input dimension, could limit its applicability to more general network architectures.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the relationship between the Betti numbers of the sublevel sets and the topological complexity of the data sets? How do these numbers relate to the overall topological features of the data?
- Is there a possibility to extend the analysis to other activation functions beyond ReLU, such as sigmoidal functions?
- Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of their findings, particularly in terms of how deep networks can be used to model data with nontrivial topologies?
- How do the results of this paper compare to other measures of expressivity, such as the number of linear regions?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or simulations to illustrate the theoretical results, particularly in terms of how the Betti numbers of sublevel sets vary with different architectural parameters?
