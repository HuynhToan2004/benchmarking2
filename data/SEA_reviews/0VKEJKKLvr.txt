 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of graph neural networks (GNNs) for breast cancer risk prediction using genotype data. The authors propose a feature selection method using an ensemble neural network (ENN) to identify the most informative single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) and construct a graph based on these SNPs. The graph is then utilized to predict breast cancer risk using GCN, GAT, and GraphSAGE models. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, although the novelty is questioned due to the straightforward application of existing methods. The paper's contribution is seen as limited by the lack of significant innovation and the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The application of graph neural networks (GNNs) to predict breast cancer risk using genotype data is an innovative approach, which is both interesting and relevant to the field.
- The use of graph neural networks for breast cancer risk prediction is a novel application, demonstrating the potential of these models in healthcare applications.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the methodology, including the feature selection method and the construction of the graph, which enhances the reproducibility and transparency of the research.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method achieves a high area under the ROC curve (AUC), indicating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily applies existing methods without significant innovation. The use of GNNs for breast cancer risk prediction is not new, and the feature selection method is also standard.
- The paper lacks a comparison with state-of-the-art models for breast cancer risk prediction, which could have provided a better context for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The performance of the proposed method is not significantly better than existing methods, such as XGBoost, which raises questions about its practical utility.
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the specific GNN model used, nor does it discuss the potential limitations of the model or the dataset, which could affect the generalizability and applicability of the findings.
- The feature selection method, while innovative in some aspects, is not sufficiently differentiated from existing methods, and the paper does not provide a detailed comparison or analysis of the selected features.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the graph is constructed from the selected SNPs and how the graph is used in the prediction process?
- What are the specific advantages of using GNNs over other machine learning models for breast cancer risk prediction, and how do these advantages translate into practical benefits?
- How do the authors address the potential limitations of the dataset, such as sample size and diversity, and how do these limitations affect the generalizability of the findings?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the selected features with those from existing methods, and discuss any novel insights or findings from this analysis?
- What are the potential implications of using different feature selection methods, such as chi-square, ANOVA, decision trees, and Lasso regression, and how do these methods compare in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- How does the proposed method compare with other state-of-the-art models for breast cancer risk prediction, and what are the specific advantages of the proposed method over these models?
