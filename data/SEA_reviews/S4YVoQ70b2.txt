 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the identifiability of reward functions in Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) when agents employ non-exponential discounting, specifically hyperbolic discounting. It introduces three new behavioral models based on resolute, naive, and sophisticated policies to address the ambiguity in reward functions. The paper argues that these models, while theoretically sound, do not provide a clear path towards identifying the true reward function, even under strong assumptions. Theoretical results suggest that the behavioral model is not identifiable under certain conditions, and the paper provides several examples and discussions to support its claims. However, the paper lacks empirical results and detailed algorithmic discussions, which are crucial for practical application.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-structured presentation of ideas.
- The authors introduce a novel behavioral model for hyperbolic discounting in IRL, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the identifiability of reward functions in IRL, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper addresses a relevant and timely problem, focusing on the identifiability of reward functions in IRL, which is a significant challenge in the field.
- The authors provide a clear and detailed discussion of the theoretical results, including the proofs of propositions and theorems, which are well-explained and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical results, which is a significant limitation as it makes it difficult to assess the practical applicability of the proposed models.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion of algorithms, which is necessary for understanding how the theoretical results can be applied in practice.
- The paper's assumptions about the transition function and the discount function are not clearly justified, which raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.
- The paper's focus on hyperbolic discounting may limit its applicability to other types of discounting, which could be more broadly applicable.
- The paper does not provide a clear path towards identifying the true reward function, even under strong assumptions, which raises questions about the practical utility of the proposed models.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions about the transition function and the discount function, and discuss how these assumptions affect the results?
- How do the results of this paper compare to those of Skalse et al. (2022), particularly in terms of the identifiability of reward functions under different discounting schemes?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion of the algorithmic implications of their theoretical results, and discuss how these results could be practically applied?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion of the empirical implications of their theoretical results, and discuss how these results could be tested in real-world scenarios?
- How do the results of this paper relate to the identifiability of reward functions in other types of discounting, such as bounded planning or geometric discounting?
