 **Summary:**
The paper explores the influence of calibration on human decision-making, specifically focusing on the application of Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory. It presents a novel approach by integrating prospect theory with calibration methods to enhance the alignment of human perceptions with model predictions. The study is supported by a survey involving 150 participants, who were asked to evaluate the reliability and trustworthiness of various calibration methods. The results suggest that while calibration improves the correlation between human decisions and model predictions, it does not significantly alter trust ratings. The paper also discusses the limitations of the study, including the generalizability of the findings due to the specific calibration method used and the limited scope of the survey.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue in machine learning, focusing on the calibration of machine learning models and its impact on human decision-making.
- The integration of Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory with calibration methods is a novel approach that could potentially enhance the alignment of human perceptions with model predictions.
- The paper is well-structured, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed literature review that covers both related work and the theory behind the proposed method.
- The experimental design is robust, with clear explanations of the calibration methods used and the results of the survey, which demonstrates a high degree of transparency and reproducibility.
- The paper raises important questions about the role of calibration in machine learning and the impact of model predictions on human decision-making, which are crucial for understanding the practical implications of machine learning in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on a specific calibration method (isotonic regression) limits the generalizability of the findings to other calibration methods.
- The survey results are based on a relatively small sample size (n=150), which might not be sufficient to draw robust conclusions, especially considering the variability in responses across different groups.
- The paper does not adequately address the ethical implications of using machine learning models in decision-making processes, which could be a significant concern in various applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the study, including the specifics of the survey design, the selection of participants, and the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's claims about the impact of calibration on trust in machine learning models are not convincingly supported by the data presented, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the survey results would differ if a different calibration method were used?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the specific calibration method used in the study, and what are the implications of this choice for the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors provide more details on the survey design, such as the selection criteria for participants and the specific questions asked, to help understand the context and implications of the results?
- How does the paper address the ethical implications of using machine learning models in decision-making processes, and what steps are being taken to ensure that these models are used responsibly and transparently?
- In light of the concerns about the impact of calibration on trust, can the authors provide additional evidence or arguments to support their claims, perhaps through additional experiments or data analysis?
