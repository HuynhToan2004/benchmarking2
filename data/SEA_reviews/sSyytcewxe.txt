 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SEED, a novel ensemble-based approach for class-incremental learning (CIL) that employs a limited number of experts, each with a unique set of Gaussian distributions for class representation. SEED selects an expert based on the least overlap of distributions and updates this expert during task training. The method aims to address the challenge of catastrophic forgetting by diversifying class representations and limiting representation drift. Extensive experiments demonstrate that SEED achieves state-of-the-art performance across various CIL scenarios, including equal split, large first task, and task-incremental settings. However, the method's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing ensemble-based methods, and the experimental results do not convincingly outperform state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with comprehensive ablation studies and experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of each component.
- The method is simple, intuitive, and effective, as shown by empirical results that outperform previous ensemble-based methods.
- The use of Gaussian distributions for class representation is a novel approach that is both interesting and potentially impactful.
- The paper provides a detailed explanation of the method, including the selection of experts based on multivariate Gaussians and the use of a fixed number of experts to reduce computational overhead.
- The experiments are well-designed and comprehensive, including various ablation studies and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the method addresses the challenges of exemplar-free CIL, especially in scenarios where exemplars are not available.
- The method's novelty is limited as it closely resembles existing ensemble-based methods, and the use of Gaussian distributions for class representation is not a new concept.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, which is a significant challenge in CIL.
- The method's performance is not convincingly superior to state-of-the-art methods, as shown in some experiments where the improvement is marginal.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the method, such as the requirement for a fixed number of experts and the potential for singular covariance matrices, which could affect the method's applicability.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical analysis to support the method's effectiveness, which could strengthen the argument for its utility.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a clearer explanation of how the method addresses the challenges of exemplar-free CIL?
- How does the method compare to state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements?
- Can the authors provide a theoretical analysis to support the effectiveness of the method?
- What are the specific advantages of using Gaussian distributions for class representation over other methods?
- How does the method handle the issue of catastrophic forgetting, especially in scenarios where the method does not perform as well as expected?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the standard deviation of multiple runs, to better understand the variability in performance?
- What are the potential applications of the method beyond CIL, and how might it be adapted to other domains?
