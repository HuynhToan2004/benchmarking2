 **Summary:**
The paper explores the expressive power of LoRA, a popular method for fine-tuning pre-trained models by introducing low-rank adapters. It theoretically analyzes the conditions under which LoRA can adapt one model to another, specifically focusing on the expressive power of LoRA in fully connected neural networks (FNN) and transformer networks (TFN). The authors prove that LoRA can adapt any model to another model of the same size, under certain conditions, and discuss the approximation errors when the LoRA-rank is lower than expected. The paper also includes numerical experiments to validate the theoretical insights. Despite its theoretical contributions, the paper has been criticized for its lack of experimental validation on real-world datasets and its limited scope to linear models.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is the first to provide theoretical results on the expressive power of LoRA, a significant contribution to the field of fine-tuning pretrained models.
- The results are clearly stated and easy to follow, with a clear structure that makes the content accessible.
- The paper includes numerical experiments to validate the theoretical insights, providing empirical evidence to support the theoretical claims.
- The theoretical results are significant and interesting, providing a new perspective on the expressive power of LoRA.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to understand, and includes a comprehensive literature review that positions the work well within the current research landscape.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on linear models, which may not fully represent the capabilities of LoRA in more complex models like transformers or larger models like large language models (LLMs).
- The proofs and assumptions rely heavily on the linearity of the models, which may not generalize well to non-linear models.
- The paper lacks experiments on real-world datasets, which could have validated the theoretical insights more robustly.
- There are some minor issues with the proofs and assumptions, such as the assumptions about the non-singularity of certain matrices and the use of the Frobenius norm in the approximation error.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly how they apply to real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the practical implications of the theoretical results, especially how they apply to real-world scenarios where the pretrained model is not exactly the same size as the target model?
- How do the results change when the pretrained model is not exactly the same size as the target model?
- In the proofs, particularly in Lemma 1, how is the assumption of non-singularity of certain matrices justified, especially when the matrices are not necessarily full rank?
- Can you elaborate on the choice of the Frobenius norm in the approximation error and how it affects the interpretation of the results?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the models were initialized and how the results were validated against real-world datasets?
