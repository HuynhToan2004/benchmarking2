 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for training large language models (LLMs) using a Socratic-style user simulator, named Socratic, which generates more human-like questions compared to traditional LLMs. The Socratic model is trained on the ShareGPT dataset and used to generate synthetic data for training a response model, PlatoLM. This approach is evaluated against several baselines and shows promising results, particularly in the MT-Bench benchmark, where it outperforms other models. The paper also discusses the method's ability to generate more diverse and complex questions, which could potentially improve the performance of LLMs. However, the novelty of the method is questioned, as similar approaches have been explored in previous works.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is novel and effective, demonstrated by strong results in the MT-Bench benchmark.
- The use of Socratic questioning to generate more diverse and complex questions for training LLMs is an interesting and innovative approach.
- The methodology is supported by a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies, which provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper includes a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is commendable for its transparency and honesty.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's main contribution, the Socratic model, is not sufficiently novel or differentiated from existing methods, particularly those discussed in previous works.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and scope.
- There is a significant reliance on the quality and diversity of the training data, which might limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which could be a significant concern given the small size of the training dataset.
- The paper's claims about the novelty and effectiveness of the Socratic model are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which show only marginal improvements over existing methods.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant omission in the ethical considerations section.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the Socratic model was fine-tuned and how it differs from existing models like ChatGPT?
- How does the Socratic model ensure that the generated questions are not repetitive or biased towards certain topics or domains?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the automatic termination mechanism in the Socratic model and how it affects the quality and diversity of the generated questions?
- How does the proposed method compare to other approaches that use human-generated data, such as those discussed in previous works?
- What are the specific advantages of the Socratic model over other user simulators, and how does it address the limitations of existing models?
- How does the paper address the potential for overfitting given the small size of the training dataset?
- Can the authors provide more details on the ethical considerations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method?
