 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework, $\partial$-CROWN, designed to certify the correctness of solutions obtained by Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) for partial differential equations (PDEs). This framework extends the CROWN method by providing a post-training verification of PINNs, which is crucial for ensuring the accuracy and reliability of PINN predictions across the entire input domain. It offers a theoretical framework for global correctness conditions and a practical verification methodology, which are demonstrated through experiments on various PDEs. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its limited scope in terms of the types of PDEs considered and the potential over-reliance on computational resources, which could limit its practical applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to certifying the correctness of PINN solutions, which is a significant advancement in the field of PINN validation.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear and detailed descriptions of the proposed framework and its components, making it accessible and understandable.
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and concise structure that effectively communicates the research findings and the methodology used.
- The theoretical contributions, such as the derivation of linear upper and lower bounds for nonlinear terms, are well-supported by rigorous proofs and detailed derivations.
- The paper addresses a critical issue in the field of PINNs by focusing on the certification of PINN solutions, which is a crucial aspect for their practical application.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can be applied to a variety of PDEs and provide accurate results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on the Burgers and Schrödinger equations, which might not be representative of the full spectrum of PDEs that PINNs can solve.
- The computational cost of the proposed method, especially in higher-dimensional spaces, is a significant limitation, making it impractical for real-world applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, which could limit its applicability to more complex PDEs.
- The method's effectiveness in high-dimensional spaces is not thoroughly evaluated, which could affect its practical utility.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods for PINN certification, which could help in understanding the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The paper could be improved by providing more detailed explanations and examples of the method's application, especially in higher-dimensional spaces.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method and how it scales with the dimensionality of the input space?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods for PINN certification in terms of accuracy, computational efficiency, and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more examples or case studies to demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method in real-world applications?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issue, especially in higher-dimensional spaces?
- Can the authors clarify the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and discuss how these could be mitigated?
- Could the authors provide more details on the method's application to high-dimensional PDEs and discuss any challenges or limitations encountered in these applications?
