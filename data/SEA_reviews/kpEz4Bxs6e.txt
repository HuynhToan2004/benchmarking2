 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel curriculum data augmentation (CDA) method for dataset distillation, focusing on large-scale datasets like ImageNet-1K and ImageNet-21K. CDA leverages a curriculum learning strategy to optimize the difficulty of the data samples during training, aiming to enhance the synthetic dataset's quality and reduce the training cost. The methodology involves a three-stage process: knowledge compression, data synthesis, and post-training, which are optimized to achieve state-of-the-art results on various datasets. The paper also explores the application of CDA in continual learning scenarios, showing potential improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the incremental nature of the contribution, the lack of comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, and the potential for overfitting due to the focus on specific model architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive description of the proposed method.
- The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on ImageNet-1K and ImageNet-21K, showing the effectiveness of the proposed curriculum data augmentation (CDA) method.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to dataset distillation by leveraging curriculum learning to optimize the difficulty of the data samples, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is straightforward and practical, with a focus on reducing training costs and improving generalization across different models.
- The paper includes detailed experimental results and ablation studies, which provide insights into the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in dataset distillation, which limits the understanding of the proposed method's effectiveness and novelty.
- The application of CDA is limited to specific model architectures, such as ResNet, which may not generalize well to other architectures like ViT.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical analysis or intuition behind the curriculum learning strategy used in CDA, which could enhance the understanding of the method's effectiveness.
- The experimental setup and results are not comprehensive enough, with missing details on the training costs and the impact of different hyperparameters on the results.
- The paper lacks a broader evaluation on different datasets and models, which could strengthen the claims of the method's effectiveness and applicability.
- There is a noticeable absence of discussion on the potential for overfitting due to the focus on specific model architectures, which could limit the practical applicability of the method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods in dataset distillation, including results from other datasets and different model architectures?
- How does the proposed CDA method compare in terms of training costs and computational efficiency with other methods, especially in large-scale datasets like ImageNet-21K?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical analysis or intuition behind the curriculum learning strategy used in CDA?
- How does the proposed method perform in continual learning scenarios beyond the 5-step and 10-step class-incremental learning described in the paper?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the training costs and the impact of different hyperparameters on the results, especially in large-scale datasets like ImageNet-21K?
