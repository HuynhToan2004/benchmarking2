 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ProteinAdapter, a novel method for integrating pre-trained Large Protein Models (LPMs) to facilitate transfer learning for various protein tasks. ProteinAdapter leverages a multi-level adapter to fuse embeddings from different structure levels, enhancing the model's ability to capture both sequence and geometric structure information. The methodology is supported by extensive experiments on over 20 tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of ProteinAdapter in surpassing state-of-the-art methods in both single-task and multi-task settings. The paper is commended for its clarity, thoroughness, and potential impact on the field, although concerns are raised regarding the novelty of the approach and the need for more detailed comparisons with other methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, ProteinAdapter, is simple yet effective, integrating sequence and structure information from pre-trained models, which is crucial for understanding protein-protein interactions.
- The experimental results are extensive, covering a wide range of tasks and demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in enhancing protein representation learning.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the method's performance across various tasks, showing significant improvements over existing methods.
- The multi-level adapter design allows for the integration of multiple pre-trained models, which can be beneficial for downstream tasks.
- The paper is timely, addressing the current challenges in protein representation learning and providing a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its scope and applicability.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as similar methods have been previously used in other domains, such as natural language processing.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other methods, particularly those that also leverage pre-trained models for protein tasks.
- The use of ESM-IF1 for structure encoding is criticized, as it may not fully capture the structural information required for protein-protein interaction prediction.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications.
- There is a need for more detailed experimental setups, including the specifics of the datasets used and the training parameters, to ensure reproducibility and transparency.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, particularly in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the ProteinAdapter handle the integration of multiple pre-trained models, and what are the implications for the model's performance and generalizability?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the structural branch in the ProteinAdapter, particularly in terms of its contribution to the overall performance?
- In the context of protein-protein interaction prediction, how does the use of ESM-IF1 for structure encoding compare to other methods that might better capture the required structural information?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setups, including the specifics of the datasets used and the training parameters, to enhance the reproducibility and transparency of the results?
