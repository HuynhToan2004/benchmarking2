 **Summary:**
The paper explores the theoretical aspects of neural network pruning by introducing new concepts such as Analogous Projected Patterns (AP2) and Analogous-in-Probability Projected Patterns (AP3) to analyze the optimal sparsity in neural networks. The authors propose a probabilistic latent space to explain the behavior of pruned networks and demonstrate the relationship between these patterns and the performance of the pruned networks. The theoretical framework is supported by empirical experiments on standard benchmarks, aiming to provide a deeper understanding of the pruning process. However, the paper faces criticism for its unclear writing, limited scope, and lack of novelty in its theoretical contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel theoretical framework that leverages a probabilistic latent space of DNN weights to explain the optimal network sparsity, providing a deeper understanding of the pruning process.
- The authors propose new notions of Analogous Projected Patterns (AP2) and Analogous-in-Probability Projected Patterns (AP3) to explain the behavior of pruned networks, which could be beneficial for future research in neural network pruning.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions and proofs that are supported by empirical experiments, demonstrating the relationship between AP2 and AP3 properties with fine-tuning pruned DNNs and sparsity levels.
- The theoretical results are empirically validated through experiments conducted on standard pre-trained benchmarks, which showcase the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is unclear and difficult to read, with many undefined symbols and unclear definitions, making it challenging for readers to follow the arguments and understand the theoretical contributions.
- The paper lacks novelty in its theoretical contributions, as the concepts of AP2 and AP3 are not sufficiently distinct or innovative compared to existing literature.
- The paper's scope is limited, focusing primarily on weight magnitude pruning techniques and not addressing other types of pruning methods, which could limit the applicability of the findings.
- The empirical results are not convincing, with some figures (e.g., Figure 2) showing inconsistencies between the theoretical predictions and empirical outcomes, which undermines the credibility of the theoretical claims.
- The paper's assumptions and methodologies are not sufficiently justified or explained, which could lead to confusion about the validity and applicability of the theoretical results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definitions and notations used in the paper, especially those that are undefined or ambiguous?
- How do the authors justify the assumptions made in their theoretical analysis, and how do these assumptions hold in practical scenarios?
- What are the implications of the findings for other types of pruning methods beyond weight magnitude pruning?
- How do the theoretical results compare to existing methods, and what are the advantages or disadvantages of the proposed approach?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to illustrate the practical applications of the proposed concepts, such as AP2 and AP3?
- How do the authors address the discrepancies between the theoretical predictions and empirical outcomes, especially in cases where the empirical results do not align with the theoretical expectations?
