 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the issue of catastrophic forgetting in class-incremental learning (CIL) by proposing a method called BaCE. BaCE leverages knowledge distillation and causal effects to balance the adaptation of new and old classes, aiming to mitigate forgetting. The authors conduct extensive experiments on various datasets and models, comparing BaCE with several baselines. The methodology includes a pilot study to understand the behavior of pre-trained models in CIL, which led to the development of BaCE. The paper is well-written and the experiments are comprehensive, showcasing the effectiveness of BaCE in most cases. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of some figures, and the potential for further improvements in methodology.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and concise figures that effectively illustrate the main ideas.
- The pilot study conducted by the authors is novel and provides valuable insights into the behavior of pre-trained models in class-incremental learning (CIL).
- The proposed method, BaCE, is simple yet effective, and the causal analysis is thorough and interesting.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the causal effects in CIL, which is a crucial aspect often overlooked in existing literature.
- The experiments are extensive and cover various tasks such as image classification, text classification, and named entity recognition, using different backbones like ResNet-18, ViT, and BERT.
- The paper introduces a novel methodology that balances the causal effects of new and old data, which is a significant contribution to the field of CIL.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it primarily combines existing techniques such as knowledge distillation and causal inference.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods in CIL, particularly those that address similar issues like forgetting and class imbalance.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of BaCE compared to other methods, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The clarity of some figures, such as Figure 1 and Figure 3, needs improvement to better illustrate the results and the methodology.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, especially in terms of its applicability and scalability in different scenarios.
- There is a need for more detailed explanations and justifications for certain methodological choices, such as the use of K-Nearest-Neighbors in the collider effect and the choice of hyperparameters.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with state-of-the-art methods in CIL that address similar issues like forgetting and class imbalance?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of BaCE compared to other methods?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the class distribution is imbalanced?
- Why was K-Nearest-Neighbors chosen for the collider effect, and how does it affect the performance of the method?
- Could the authors clarify the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the number of neighbors and the weight of the collider effect?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of forgetting in the context of CIL, and what are the implications for the practical application of the method?
