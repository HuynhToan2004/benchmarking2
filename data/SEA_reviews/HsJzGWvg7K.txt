 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Sparse Cocktail, a novel co-training framework designed to generate multiple sparse subnetworks with varying sparsity patterns and ratios. This framework allows for the simultaneous training of different sparsity patterns, which is a significant advancement over traditional methods that focus on a single sparsity pattern. Sparse Cocktail employs a unified mask generation process and dense pivot co-training to ensure the subnetworks do not cancel each other's performance. The paper provides extensive experiments across various datasets and architectures, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, concerns were raised about the fairness of the comparison with existing methods and the complexity of the training process.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for co-training multiple sparse subnetworks with different sparsity patterns and ratios, which is a significant advancement over existing methods.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear figures and detailed explanations of the components involved.
- The paper provides extensive experiments on various datasets and architectures, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The authors have conducted an ablation study to evaluate the effectiveness of each component of the proposed method, which adds credibility to the results.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive related work section that provides a good overview of the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper claims that Sparse Cocktail achieves a Pareto-optimal trade-off, but this is not supported by experimental evidence.
- The comparison with other methods is not fair, as the number of subnetworks trained by Sparse Cocktail is significantly higher than in other methods, which could skew the results.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the computational complexity or training time of Sparse Cocktail with other methods, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different network architectures and its scalability to larger, more complex models.
- The paper does not provide a clear motivation for the need to train multiple sparse subnetworks with different sparsity patterns, especially considering the potential for reduced performance and increased training complexity.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the subnetworks are chosen for interpolation in the Sparse Cocktail method?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other co-training methods, especially in terms of computational complexity and training time?
- Could the authors provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different network architectures and its scalability to larger, more complex models?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of conflicting gradient directions mentioned in the paper, especially in the context of training multiple subnetworks with different sparsity patterns?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and training time of Sparse Cocktail compared to other methods, especially in the context of training on larger, more complex models?
