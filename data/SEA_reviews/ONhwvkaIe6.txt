 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two novel metrics, In-Subtree Probability (ISP) and Subtree Coverage Score (SCS), for evaluating the hypernymy understanding capabilities of text-to-image (T2I) models. These metrics utilize WordNet and ImageNet to quantify the models' ability to generate images consistent with the hypernymy relation. The metrics are evaluated across various T2I models, including GLIDE, Latent Diffusion, and Stable Diffusion, and are shown to correlate well with human evaluations. The paper also explores the impact of different hyperparameters and training data on these metrics, providing insights into the models' performance and limitations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces two novel metrics, In-Subtree Probability (ISP) and Subtree Coverage Score (SCS), to evaluate the hypernymy understanding of text-to-image (T2I) models, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed metrics are technically sound and easy to understand, making them accessible and usable for the community.
- The paper provides extensive experiments and evaluations, including a human study, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the metrics in measuring the language understanding capabilities of T2I models.
- The analysis of the proposed metrics in relation to training data frequency and hyperparameters like the number of diffusion steps and classifier-free guidance is thorough and insightful.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, which enhances its readability and accessibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on evaluating T2I models based on the hypernymy relation, which may not fully capture the models' overall language understanding capabilities.
- The metrics' sensitivity to different datasets and their ability to generalize across various datasets and models need further exploration.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed metrics and the potential biases in the evaluation process, such as the use of WordNet and ImageNet which might not fully represent the complexity of the models' language understanding.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential biases introduced by the use of human annotations and the lack of diversity in the human annotators.
- The paper's scope could be broadened to include a more comprehensive evaluation of the models' language understanding capabilities beyond the hypernymy relation.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the hypernymy relation and how it relates to the overall language understanding capabilities of T2I models?
- How do the proposed metrics perform when evaluated against a broader set of datasets and models, particularly those that might not be as closely aligned with the ImageNet dataset?
- Can the authors discuss the potential biases introduced by the use of WordNet and ImageNet in the evaluation process and how these biases might affect the results?
- How do the proposed metrics compare to other metrics that evaluate the language understanding capabilities of T2I models, such as those based on semantic object accuracy or other qualitative measures?
- Could the authors provide more details on the human annotation process, including the demographics of the annotators and the training and quality control measures used to ensure the reliability of the annotations?
