 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedSRC, a federated learning (FL) method designed to handle data corruption from the client side. This approach allows clients to determine their participation in FL rounds based on a local inference loss check, thereby potentially improving the global model's performance while reducing communication and computation costs. Theoretical analysis and extensive experiments on various datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of FedSRC in handling non-iid data and malicious clients. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the method, particularly in real-world scenarios with more complex data distributions and the potential for malicious clients to manipulate the system.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed FedSRC method is innovative and addresses a significant problem in federated learning, particularly the issue of malicious clients.
- The method is theoretically sound and supported by extensive experiments that demonstrate its effectiveness in improving the global model's performance and reducing communication and computation costs.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear motivation and detailed theoretical analysis that supports the proposed method.
- The method's simplicity and efficiency make it practical for real-world applications, as it does not require additional communication rounds or server-side computation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes a simplistic data distribution model, which may not accurately reflect real-world scenarios where data distributions can be more complex and varied.
- The method's reliance on a single inference loss check for client participation may not be sufficient to detect all types of malicious clients, particularly those that can manipulate the system by reporting low loss values.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational and communication overheads introduced by the FedSRC method, which could affect its practical implementation.
- The theoretical analysis and proofs in the paper are not thoroughly explained, which could limit the understanding of the method's theoretical foundations.
- The paper does not discuss potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a critical aspect of ethical considerations in AI research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the method performs in more complex data distribution scenarios, such as those found in real-world federated learning environments?
- How does the FedSRC method handle cases where clients report low loss values but are actually malicious or providing misleading information?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational and communication overheads introduced by the FedSRC method, and discuss potential ways to mitigate these costs?
- How does the FedSRC method compare to other existing methods for handling malicious clients in federated learning?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
