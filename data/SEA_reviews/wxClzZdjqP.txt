 **Summary:**
The paper explores the integration of large language models (LLMs) into graph contrastive learning (GCL) for text-attributed graphs (TAGs). It introduces two main pipelines: LLM-as-GraphAugmentor and LLM-as-TextEncoder, designed to enhance GCL through LLM-generated augmentations and text encodings. The study evaluates these approaches across six benchmark datasets, demonstrating the potential of LLMs in improving GCL performance. Despite the innovative approach, concerns are raised about the clarity and depth of the experimental results, the novelty of the methodology, and the fairness of comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely problem by integrating large language models (LLMs) into graph contrastive learning (GCL) for text-attributed graphs (TAGs), a topic that has been under-explored.
- It introduces a novel approach by leveraging LLMs for graph augmentation, which is a novel and interesting idea that has not been extensively explored in the literature.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with comprehensive and systematic experiments conducted on six benchmark datasets, which demonstrate the potential of LLMs in enhancing GCL performance.
- The use of LLMs for graph augmentation is a promising direction for future research in the field of graph representation learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly those that use LLMs for graph augmentation. This makes it difficult to assess the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently detailed or clear, with some figures and tables being hard to read or interpret. More detailed visualizations and explanations of the results would be beneficial.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational overhead and efficiency of using LLMs for graph augmentation, which is a significant concern given the resource-intensive nature of LLMs.
- There are concerns about the fairness of comparisons made in the paper, particularly the comparison of LLM-generated features with those of shallow embedding models like BoW, which may not be a fair comparison.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its methods, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods that use LLMs for graph augmentation?
- How does the computational overhead of using LLMs for graph augmentation compare to other methods, and what are the implications for scalability and efficiency?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental results, particularly in figures and tables where the data is hard to read or interpret?
- What are the specific advantages of using LLMs for graph augmentation over other methods, and how do these advantages translate into practical improvements in GCL performance?
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other self-supervised learning methods for graphs, such as GraphMAE and S2GAE?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their methods and provide recommendations for future research in this area?
