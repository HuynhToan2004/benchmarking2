 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, UMMAN, which integrates graph neural networks (GNNs) with the prediction of diseases related to the abundance of intestinal flora. UMMAN leverages a multiplex relationship between gut microbes and hosts by constructing a multi-graph and utilizing a novel Node Feature Global Integration (NFGI) module. The method aims to learn the complex relationships between gut microbes and diseases, which are often overlooked by traditional machine learning methods. The paper presents extensive experiments on five datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of UMMAN in disease prediction. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the methodology, the novelty of the approach, and the lack of detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by combining graph neural networks (GNNs) with the prediction of diseases related to the abundance of intestinal flora, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed NFGI module effectively represents the global embedding of a graph, enhancing the understanding and prediction of diseases based on gut microbiota.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the model architecture and the experimental setup, which facilitates reproducibility and transparency.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for scientific communication.
- The authors have demonstrated the effectiveness of their approach through extensive experiments on five datasets, showing improvements in disease prediction.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining the "multiplex relationship" between gut microbes and hosts, which is crucial for understanding the proposed method.
- There is a significant gap in the literature review, particularly in not citing recent and relevant works in the field of gut microbiota and disease prediction.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as it appears to be a straightforward combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The methodology, particularly the construction of the Original-Graph and Shuffled-Graph, lacks detail and clarity, which could confuse readers and hinder reproducibility.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with only marginal improvements reported.
- The paper suffers from several typographical and grammatical errors, which detract from its professionalism and credibility.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and significance of the "multiplex relationship" between gut microbes and hosts?
- Why was the decision made to use a GNN-based method for gut microbiota disease prediction, and how does it compare to other methods like random forest or support vector machine (SVM)?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of "dirty data" in gut microbiome datasets, and what steps are taken to ensure data quality and reduce noise?
- Can the authors provide more details on the construction of the Original-Graph and Shuffled-Graph, including the specific metrics used and the rationale behind their choice?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the model?
- Is there a possibility to release the code for reproducibility and further research in the field?
