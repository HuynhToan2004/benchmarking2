 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Scaff-PD, a novel federated learning algorithm designed to optimize distributionally robust objectives, enhancing fairness and robustness in federated learning scenarios. The algorithm leverages a min-max objective function, incorporating a dual variable to address heterogeneous client data, and is supported by a theoretical analysis demonstrating linear convergence rates. The paper also includes experimental results on various datasets, showing that Scaff-PD outperforms existing methods in terms of convergence speed and fairness metrics. The algorithm is particularly effective in resource-constrained and heterogeneous settings, and its practical evaluation demonstrates its potential for real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel algorithm, Scaff-PD, which addresses the challenges of fairness and robustness in federated learning, particularly in resource-constrained and heterogeneous settings.
- The algorithm's design is innovative, combining the Scaffold technique with a min-max objective function to optimize distributionally robust objectives, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including strong convergence guarantees and linear convergence rates, which are backed by experimental results demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness.
- The practical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating the algorithm's ability to achieve faster convergence rates compared to existing methods and showing its effectiveness in real-world datasets.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which enhances its readability and comprehensibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other existing methods for solving the min-max problem in federated learning, which could have provided a clearer understanding of Scaff-PD's advantages and limitations.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithm, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough exploration of the trade-offs between different fairness metrics, particularly in relation to the choice of regularization parameters.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are somewhat limited, focusing on a small number of clients and datasets, which might not adequately demonstrate the algorithm's performance in more diverse or large-scale settings.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the privacy implications of the algorithm, which is a significant concern in federated learning applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other existing methods for solving the min-max problem in federated learning, particularly in terms of convergence rates and practical performance?
- How does the algorithm perform in terms of privacy, and what measures have been taken to address this concern?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of regularization parameters and discuss the trade-offs between different fairness metrics?
- How does the algorithm handle different levels of data heterogeneity across clients, and what are the implications for convergence rates and fairness metrics?
- Could the authors discuss the potential of integrating the Scaff-PD algorithm with other privacy-preserving techniques to enhance its applicability in real-world scenarios?
