 **Summary:**
The paper explores the condensation phenomenon in convolutional neural networks (CNNs) during training, specifically focusing on two-layer CNNs. It theoretically demonstrates that under certain conditions, such as small initialization and gradient descent, the convolutional kernels of a two-layer CNN tend to condense towards a specific direction determined by the training samples. This directionality is supported by empirical evidence across various datasets and architectures, including three-layer CNNs. The paper also discusses how this phenomenon might be influenced by the structure of CNNs, particularly the convolution layer, and its implications for the generalization ability of CNNs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a theoretical framework for the condensation phenomenon in convolutional neural networks (CNNs), which is a significant contribution to the understanding of CNN training dynamics.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments to support their theoretical claims, demonstrating the condensation phenomenon in various settings and architectures, including MNIST and CIFAR10 datasets.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are comprehensive, covering various aspects such as different datasets, architectures, and optimization methods.
- The findings of the paper are novel and interesting, providing insights into the behavior of CNNs during training and potentially contributing to the development of more effective training strategies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the implications of the condensation phenomenon for the generalization ability of CNNs. This is a critical aspect that should be addressed in future work.
- The experiments primarily focus on small-scale datasets, which may not fully capture the dynamics of CNNs on larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its theoretical contributions from existing literature, particularly in terms of the novelty of the assumptions and the methodological approach.
- There are several typographical and notation errors throughout the paper, which could confuse readers and detract from the overall quality of the manuscript.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its theoretical and experimental approaches, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the distinction between the condensation phenomenon and the lazy training regime mentioned in related works?
- How does the condensation phenomenon affect the generalization ability of CNNs, particularly in terms of overfitting and underfitting?
- Can you provide more details on the specific values of the parameters used in your experiments, such as the learning rate and batch size, to ensure reproducibility?
- How do the findings of this paper relate to the neural tangent kernel theory, and what are the implications of the condensation phenomenon for the neural tangent kernel of CNNs?
- Could you discuss the potential implications of the condensation phenomenon for the training of deeper CNNs, and whether similar dynamics might be observed in more complex architectures?
