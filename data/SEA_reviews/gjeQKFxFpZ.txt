 **Summary:**
The paper investigates methods for eliciting confidence in Large Language Models (LLMs) by integrating prompting strategies, sampling methods, and aggregation techniques. The authors employ these components to evaluate different confidence elicitation algorithms across multiple datasets and models, focusing on improving the calibration and failure prediction of LLMs. The study reveals that LLMs tend to be overconfident, which can be mitigated by human-inspired prompting strategies and sampling methods. The paper also highlights the potential of using ensemble methods to enhance the reliability of LLMs' responses. Despite the paper's comprehensive evaluation and empirical insights, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach and the practical applicability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, with clear presentation and detailed empirical results, making it easy to follow.
- The topic of confidence elicitation is relevant and timely, addressing the need for more reliable and trustworthy decision-making in LLMs.
- The experiments are extensive, covering a wide range of models and datasets, which provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The paper provides a novel framework for evaluating confidence elicitation in LLMs, which can serve as a baseline for future research.
- The findings, such as the overconfidence of LLMs and the effectiveness of human-inspired prompting strategies, are insightful and contribute to the understanding of LLMs' behavior.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the novelty of the approach, particularly in relation to existing works such as those by Tian et al. (2023) and Kojima et al. (2022).
- The practical applicability of the findings is limited due to the focus on fixed-form question-answering tasks, which may not generalize well to other types of tasks like summarization or open-ended QA.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed analysis and discussion of the results, especially the inconsistencies and discrepancies observed across different datasets and models.
- The methodology for evaluating confidence elicitation is limited to black-box methods, which may not provide a complete understanding of the LLMs' capabilities in this area.
- The paper could be improved by addressing the readability and clarity of some sections, particularly in the methodology and results sections.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the differences between the prompting strategies proposed in this paper and those used in concurrent works like Tian et al. (2023)?
- How does the proposed framework compare to other methods for eliciting confidence in LLMs, especially in terms of practical applicability and generalizability?
- In the experimental results, why does the performance of Top-K + Self-Random + Avg-Conf vary significantly across different datasets and models?
- Could you elaborate on the discrepancies observed in the performance of different aggregation methods, particularly the significant differences noted in the results?
- How does the proposed framework handle the issue of overconfidence in LLMs, and what are the implications of this overconfidence for the reliability and trustworthiness of LLMs' responses?
