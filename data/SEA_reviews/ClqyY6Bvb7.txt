 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ChEF, a framework designed to evaluate multimodal large language models (MLLMs) by decomposing the evaluation process into four modular components: Scenario, Instruction, Inference, and Metric. This framework aims to provide a standardized and comprehensive evaluation of MLLMs across various scenarios, such as image classification, object detection, and question answering. It also includes six desiderata to assess the capabilities of MLLMs in areas like in-context learning and instruction following. The evaluation involves nine MLLMs on nine scenarios, revealing insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different models. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the framework, the clarity of the evaluation process, and the potential for bias in the evaluation metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive evaluation of nine MLLMs across nine scenarios and six desiderata.
- The introduction of in-context learning (ICL) into the evaluation framework is a significant contribution, providing a more comprehensive assessment of MLLMs.
- The framework is modular, scalable, and flexible, which facilitates the integration of new scenarios and models.
- The evaluation results are insightful, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of different models in various scenarios.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of different MLLMs, including LLaVA, LAMM, MiniGPT4, mPLUG, Otter, InstructBLIP, Shikra, and Kosmos-2, which are crucial for understanding the current state of MLLM research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the framework is limited as it mainly combines existing methods without significant innovation.
- The evaluation process and the selection of evaluation metrics lack clarity and detail, which could lead to confusion and misinterpretation.
- The framework's evaluation of MLLMs is limited to visual tasks, which might not fully represent their capabilities in other areas like text-based reasoning.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential biases in the evaluation metrics, which could affect the fairness and reliability of the results.
- The framework's scalability and flexibility are questioned due to the limited number of models and scenarios evaluated.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework and the potential for bias in the evaluation process.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the selection and design of the evaluation metrics, particularly how they address potential biases?
- How does the framework handle the potential biases in the evaluation process, and what steps are taken to ensure fairness and reliability in the results?
- Could the authors elaborate on the novelty of the framework and how it advances the evaluation of MLLMs beyond existing methods?
- In the evaluation of MLLMs, how do the authors ensure that the models are evaluated comprehensively across different tasks and domains?
- What are the potential implications of the findings on the future development of MLLMs, and how might these insights guide further research in this area?
