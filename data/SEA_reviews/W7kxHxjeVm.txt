 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for unsupervised anomaly detection in datasets with missing values, named ImAD. It integrates data imputation and anomaly detection into a unified optimization objective, addressing the challenge of imputation bias that can lead to misclassification of abnormal data as normal. The method uses a neural network-based architecture to perform data imputation and anomaly detection, aiming to improve the detection of anomalies in incomplete data. The paper presents extensive experiments across various datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to existing two-stage approaches. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the theoretical analysis, and the generalizability of the results to real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem of anomaly detection in datasets with missing values, which is a significant issue in real-world applications.
- The proposed method is novel, integrating data imputation and anomaly detection into a unified optimization objective, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and the results.
- Extensive experiments are conducted on various datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to existing two-stage approaches.
- The use of pseudo-abnormal samples generated in the latent space to mitigate imputation bias is a creative and potentially impactful contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters, which could significantly affect the performance of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as it appears to be a straightforward combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The theoretical analysis, particularly the proof of Theorem 3.1, is not convincing and may contain errors.
- The generalization of the results to real-world scenarios is unclear, as the paper primarily uses synthetic datasets for evaluation.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of imputation bias, which is a critical concern in anomaly detection with missing data.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, including those that use GAN-based imputation models.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on how the hyperparameters were chosen and their impact on the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other approaches that use GAN-based imputation models for anomaly detection?
- In the context of the theoretical analysis, could you clarify the proof of Theorem 3.1, particularly the assumptions and the implications of the theorem?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of missing data mechanisms (MCAR, MAR, MNAR)?
- Can you elaborate on the choice of the neural network architecture for the imputation and anomaly detection modules, and how this choice affects the performance of the method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data is not normally distributed, and what are the implications of this for the detection of anomalies?
