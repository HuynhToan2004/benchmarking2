 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for detecting unauthorized data usage in text-to-image diffusion models by injecting memorization into protected datasets. This is achieved by modifying images with a stealthy image warping function and conditioning the model to generate specific elements based on text prompts. The method involves training a signal classifier to distinguish between images generated with and without the injected memorization, which can be used to detect unauthorized data usage in models trained on these datasets. The effectiveness of the method is demonstrated through experiments on various datasets and models, showing high detection accuracy and minimal impact on model performance. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation scope, lack of discussion on related works, and potential ethical concerns regarding the use of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the idea of detecting unauthorized data usage in text-to-image diffusion models is novel and interesting.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and has a small impact on the generation quality of the model.
- The experiments are conducted on multiple datasets and models, demonstrating the effectiveness of the method in detecting unauthorized data usage.
- The paper introduces a novel method for detecting unauthorized data usage in text-to-image diffusion models, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed method is evaluated on multiple datasets and models, showing its effectiveness in detecting unauthorized data usage.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on related works, particularly those that discuss data memorization and unlearnable examples.
- The evaluation is limited to only one dataset (CUB-200), which might not be sufficient to validate the effectiveness of the method across different datasets.
- The method's effectiveness in detecting unauthorized data usage is not convincingly demonstrated, especially in scenarios where the data used for training the signal classifier is the same as the data used to generate the signal function.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations and potential negative social impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the signal function and its impact on the detection of unauthorized data usage.
- The method's applicability in real-world scenarios where the data used for training the signal classifier is not available is not discussed.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the signal function used in the method and how it affects the detection of unauthorized data usage?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the data used for training the signal classifier is not available?
- Could the authors conduct experiments on more datasets to validate the effectiveness of the method across different data distributions?
- What are the potential negative social impacts of the proposed method, and how can these be mitigated?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the data used for training the signal classifier is different from the data used to generate the signal function?
- Can the authors discuss the limitations of the method and potential future directions for research in this area?
