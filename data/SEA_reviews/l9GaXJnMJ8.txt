 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for approximating kernels in Markov systems using a dual subgradient method based on a kernel distance metric derived from the Wasserstein distance. This approach allows for the efficient computation of optimal stopping problems and other dynamic programming problems. The method is designed to handle non-linear dependencies on probability measures and provides a faster alternative to traditional mixed-integer programming methods. The paper includes a detailed theoretical analysis and empirical results that demonstrate the method's effectiveness in numerical examples. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper's presentation and the need for more extensive empirical evaluations to validate the method's practical utility.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to solving optimal stopping problems and other dynamic programming problems.
- The use of a dual subgradient method to solve the problem is innovative and potentially faster than traditional methods like mixed-integer programming.
- Theoretical results are provided, including a convergence analysis, which adds credibility to the proposed method.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of Markov systems and provides a method that could potentially be applied in various practical scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some parts, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key terms and concepts such as the Wasserstein distance and the kernel distance.
- The empirical evaluation is limited to a few examples, which may not adequately demonstrate the method's effectiveness or generalizability.
- There is a lack of comparison with existing methods, such as the binomial tree method, which could have provided a better context for evaluating the proposed method's performance.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the mathematical notations and assumptions used, as well as a more comprehensive discussion of the limitations and potential applications of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a clearer definition and explanation of the Wasserstein distance and the kernel distance, and how they relate to the problem being solved?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods like the binomial tree method, especially in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy?
- Can the authors elaborate on the choice of parameters and the sensitivity of the method to these choices?
- How does the method perform when applied to larger, more complex problems, and what are the practical implications of the computational efficiency gains?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or applications of the method to demonstrate its utility in real-world scenarios?
