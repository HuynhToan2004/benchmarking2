 **Summary:**
The paper introduces EGALA, a novel method for conducting large-scale graph adversarial attacks by approximating the gradient of loss with respect to the adjacency matrix through an efficient matrix product and clustering-based Approximate Nearest Neighbor Search (ANNS). This approach allows for the identification of edges with the most significant gradients in sublinear time, enhancing the scalability and efficiency of the attack. The method is validated on various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and scalability. However, the paper is criticized for its limited novelty, as it heavily relies on existing techniques and lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach for conducting large-scale graph adversarial attacks, focusing on the efficient approximation of gradients through a matrix product and ANNS, which significantly reduces computational complexity.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the theoretical underpinnings and practical implications of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and GNN architectures.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the limitations, which is appreciated for its transparency and honesty.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as it heavily relies on existing techniques, particularly the use of ANNS for approximating gradients, which has been previously explored.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including those that have been shown to outperform the proposed method in terms of efficiency and effectiveness.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical justification for the approximation of gradients, which is critical for understanding the method's effectiveness and reliability.
- The scalability of the method is limited by the size of the graph and the number of nodes, which may not be practical for very large graphs.
- The paper does not address the impact of the approximation on the performance of the attack, which is a significant concern given the potential for inaccuracies in gradient estimation.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that have been shown to outperform your method in terms of efficiency and effectiveness?
- How does the proposed method perform when the graph is very large, and the number of nodes exceeds the capabilities of existing ANNS methods?
- Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the approximation error introduced by the gradient approximation, and how it affects the performance of the attack?
- How does the proposed method perform under different attack settings, such as local attacks where the attacker can only modify a small number of edges?
- Can you clarify the computational cost of the ANNS algorithm and how it impacts the overall efficiency of the method?
- How does the method perform when the number of edges in the graph is close to the attack budget, and how does this affect the performance of the attack?
