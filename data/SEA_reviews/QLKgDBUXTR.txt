 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Minimal Sufficient Views (MSVs) as a method to understand the generalization capabilities of deep neural networks (DNNs) by identifying minimal and distinct features in input data that preserve the model's prediction for that input. MSVs are defined as a set of minimal and distinct features that maintain the model's prediction for a given input, and are empirically shown to correlate with prediction accuracy across various models. The paper proposes a greedy algorithm to compute approximate MSVs and presents experimental results demonstrating the relationship between the number of MSVs and prediction accuracy. Despite its novel approach, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty, lack of a clear definition of "view," and potential issues with the experimental setup and results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel concept of Minimal Sufficient Views (MSVs), which provides a new perspective on understanding the generalization capabilities of deep neural networks.
- The proposed method is simple and easy to implement, with a clear definition of MSVs and a greedy algorithm for their computation.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with comprehensive experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the MSVs in explaining the generalization performance of DNNs.
- The methodology is well-motivated and the paper is well-presented, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and its application to various models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and justification for the term "view," which is central to the proposed method. The concept of "view" is not adequately explained, leading to potential confusion and misunderstanding.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with significant discrepancies and inconsistencies noted, such as the lack of correlation between the number of MSVs and prediction accuracy in certain cases.
- The paper's theoretical underpinnings are not well-explained, particularly the relationship between MSVs and the generalization capabilities of DNNs, which is not adequately supported by empirical evidence.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited, as it appears to be a minor modification of existing methods without a clear distinction from previous works.
- The paper's organization and presentation need improvement, particularly in the clarity of the methodology and the explanation of experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of "view" in the context of MSVs? How does this concept contribute to the understanding of the generalization capabilities of DNNs?
- How does the proposed method differ from existing methods, particularly in terms of its theoretical contributions and empirical validations?
- The paper claims that the number of MSVs corresponds to the prediction accuracy. Could the authors explain this correlation and provide more detailed evidence to support this claim?
- The experimental results show that the number of MSVs does not always correlate with prediction accuracy. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of these results and explain the discrepancies observed?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of potential biases in the model's predictions, particularly in cases where the MSVs may not accurately represent the model's decision-making process?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup and results, particularly in sections where the results are inconsistent or unclear?
