 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to polynomial basis design for spectral graph neural networks, specifically addressing the challenge of heterophily in graph learning. The authors propose a universal polynomial basis, UniBasis, which adapts to the heterophily degree of graphs, aiming to improve the expressiveness of spectral graph filters. This basis is constructed by combining homophily and heterophily basis, allowing for the selection of the weight of homophily basis to balance the contributions of homophily and heterophily. The method is evaluated on several real-world datasets, showing improvements over existing methods. Despite its theoretical and empirical contributions, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty and the lack of a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and the empirical validation of the homophily ratio estimation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in graph learning, specifically the heterophily problem, which is crucial for the field.
- The proposed method is theoretically sound, with clear and well-explained theoretical results that provide a solid foundation for the proposed solution.
- The empirical results are strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method over a wide range of baselines.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear and well-explained mathematical derivations and experimental setups.
- The proposed method is simple and intuitive, yet it achieves strong performance across various datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability, especially in large-scale graph learning scenarios.
- The novelty of the proposed method is somewhat limited as it combines existing ideas of homophily and heterophily basis, with the main contribution being the integration of these two aspects.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its strengths and weaknesses.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the homophily ratio estimation, particularly how it is empirically validated and its impact on the overall performance of the method.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that use learnable bases or adaptive polynomial filters, which could undermine the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
