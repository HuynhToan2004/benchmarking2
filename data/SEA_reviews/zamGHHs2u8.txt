 **Summary:**
The paper explores the cold posterior effect (CPE) in Bayesian neural networks (BNNs) by focusing on the underfitting phenomenon that occurs when the CPE is present. The authors propose that the CPE is a manifestation of underfitting, which is influenced by both prior and likelihood misspecification, as well as data augmentation. They provide theoretical insights and empirical evidence to support this claim, suggesting that the CPE is not merely an artifact of inaccurate approximate inference methods but a more fundamental issue related to model misspecification and underfitting. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for the design of better BNNs and the role of data augmentation in mitigating the CPE.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel perspective on the cold posterior effect (CPE) by focusing on underfitting rather than the prior or likelihood, which is a significant shift in the understanding of this phenomenon.
- The theoretical analysis is sound, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, even for readers not deeply familiar with the topic.
- The paper is original in its approach, particularly in its focus on underfitting as a root cause of the CPE, which is a departure from previous explanations that often attributed the CPE to inaccurate approximate inference methods.
- The empirical evidence provided supports the theoretical claims, and the paper includes a comprehensive review of related literature, which is beneficial for understanding the current state of research on the CPE.
- The paper's insights into the relationship between the CPE and underfitting are valuable, as they could lead to the development of more effective Bayesian neural networks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of overfitting, which is a significant concern in the context of the CPE. The absence of a discussion on overfitting limits the paper's applicability to scenarios where overfitting is a more pressing concern.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is based on the generalized Bayes posterior, which is not widely used in practice, making the theoretical insights less practical.
- There is a lack of experimental results, particularly in large-scale settings, which could strengthen the paper's claims.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its approach, which could include the applicability of the findings to different types of neural networks or datasets.
- The paper's reliance on the CPE as a measure of model performance could be seen as controversial, as it may not accurately reflect the true performance of the model, especially in scenarios where the CPE is not indicative of good generalization.
- The paper's focus on underfitting as the root cause of the CPE is not entirely novel, as similar ideas have been proposed in previous works, which could limit the paper's contribution to the field.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between the CPE and overfitting? How does the CPE manifest in scenarios where overfitting is a concern?
- How does the paper's theoretical analysis apply to more practical forms of the Bayes posterior, such as those based on the evidence lower bound (ELBO)?
- In light of the paper's focus on underfitting, how does the CPE affect the performance of models that are already underfitting? Could the authors provide examples or scenarios where the CPE exacerbates underfitting?
- Could the authors discuss the practical implications of their findings for the design of better Bayesian neural networks? What specific steps could be taken to address the underfitting issue in BNNs?
- How does the paper's approach to the CPE differ from previous works that have also explored the underfitting hypothesis? What new insights or contributions does this paper offer to the ongoing discussion on the CPE?
