 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GRAFT, a novel method for training vision-language models (VLMs) for remote sensing images without textual annotations. It leverages co-located internet imagery taken on the ground as an intermediary, enabling the training of a large-scale VLM for remote sensing images. The approach, which includes both image-level and pixel-level VLMs, demonstrates zero-shot, open-vocabulary image classification, retrieval, segmentation, and visual question answering for satellite images. The paper claims that these VLMs outperform existing VLMs trained with supervision, with gains of up to 20% for classification and 80% for segmentation. The methodology involves collecting ground-satellite image pairs and training an image encoder for remote sensing images to align with the image encoder of CLIP using a large amount of paired internet and satellite images.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for training vision-language models (VLMs) for remote sensing images without textual annotations, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is clearly explained, and the paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow.
- The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art accuracy for zero-shot image classification, retrieval, segmentation, and visual question answering, showing that the proposed VLM can enable scientists of all stripes to analyze vast troves of satellite data.
- The paper is well-written, and the experiments are convincing, showcasing a significant improvement in performance compared to existing models.
- The authors have collected a large-scale dataset of ground-satellite image pairs and developed a foundational vision-language model for satellite images, which can understand open-world concepts at two different resolutions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the generalizability of the approach to other remote sensing modalities or different types of satellite images.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, such as privacy concerns or potential misuse of the technology.
- The paper does not include a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art models, which could have strengthened the claims of the paper.
- The paper does not discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of remote sensing data or its scalability to other domains.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity or the scalability of the proposed method, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- The paper does not address the potential limitations of the proposed method in terms of its ability to handle dynamic objects or changes in the environment.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for privacy violations, especially given the nature of remote sensing data?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the textual annotations in the training process, especially in the context of the claims about training without textual annotations?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the image-text pairs are not available, and how does it handle the potential for misalignment between the textual descriptions and the visual content?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of its applicability to other domains or remote sensing modalities?
- How does the method handle the potential for misuse of the technology, and what steps are being taken to mitigate these risks?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art models to strengthen the claims of the paper?
