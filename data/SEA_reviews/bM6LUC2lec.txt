 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MSA-Generator, a self-supervised generative protein language model designed to enhance multiple sequence alignment (MSA) quality, a critical component in protein structure prediction. The model is trained using a seq2seqs task, leveraging a large-scale protein database to generate informative MSAs that can enrich subpar alignments and improve prediction accuracy. The proposed methodology involves the use of protein-specific attention mechanisms to generate virtual MSAs, which are evaluated against the CASP14 and CASP15 benchmarks, showing improvements in LDDT for challenging sequences. Despite these advancements, the paper is criticized for its limited evaluation scope, focusing primarily on a single dataset and not adequately comparing its method with existing baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of protein structure prediction by focusing on the generation of high-quality multiple sequence alignments (MSAs), which are crucial for improving prediction accuracy.
- The introduction of the seq2seqs task for self-supervised pre-training is innovative and well-explained, providing a clear motivation and detailed methodology for its implementation.
- The MSA-Generator model is well-designed, incorporating protein-specific attention mechanisms and leveraging large-scale protein databases to generate informative MSAs.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and detailed descriptions of the dataset construction, training, and evaluation processes.
- The proposed method shows promising results in improving the LDDT scores of AlphaFold2 and RoseTTAFold on the CASP14 and CASP15 benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation scope is limited, focusing primarily on the CASP14 and CASP15 benchmarks, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method across different datasets or real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing baselines, such as the MSA Transformer and other relevant models, which could provide a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The dataset used for training and testing the MSA-Generator is not clearly defined, raising concerns about the model's performance on real-world datasets and its ability to generalize to different types of protein sequences.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including the potential impact of data quality and model performance on the generated MSAs.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation, including additional datasets and metrics to better assess the effectiveness and applicability of the proposed method in various protein structure prediction tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the evaluation process, particularly how the baseline method was selected and how it was trained on the CASP14 and CASP15 datasets?
- How does the MSA-Generator perform on real-world datasets, and what steps are being taken to ensure its generalizability to different types of protein sequences?
- Could you provide more details on the training and testing datasets, including the specific datasets used and their characteristics?
- How does the model ensure the quality of the generated MSAs, particularly in terms of their accuracy and relevance to real-world protein sequences?
- Are there any plans to release the code and pre-trained models for the MSA-Generator, which could facilitate further research and practical applications in the field?
