 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Pi-DUAL, a novel method for handling label noise in machine learning models using privileged information (PI). This approach involves a dual-network architecture where one network processes the input data and PI to predict clean labels, while another network processes PI to predict noise labels. The method leverages a gating mechanism to adaptively balance the contributions of these networks based on the PI. The paper claims that Pi-DUAL can outperform existing methods on noisy label benchmarks and can serve as a strong method for detecting wrong labels. Extensive experiments and ablation studies are conducted to validate the effectiveness of Pi-DUAL, particularly in scenarios with high-quality PI. However, the paper has been critiqued for its reliance on PI, which may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where PI is not readily available.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and can be easily integrated into existing learning pipelines.
- The paper includes extensive experiments and ablation studies, which validate the effectiveness of Pi-DUAL in various scenarios.
- Pi-DUAL sets a new state-of-the-art in datasets with high-quality PI, demonstrating significant performance improvements over existing methods.
- The paper is original in its approach to leveraging privileged information (PI) to distinguish clean from noisy labels, which is a novel and potentially impactful application of PI in machine learning.
- The methodology is scalable and practical, making it suitable for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies heavily on the availability of privileged information (PI), which may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where PI is not readily available.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of using PI, which could be a significant drawback in practical applications.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the method performs without PI, which is crucial for understanding its generalizability.
- The methodology's reliance on PI might limit its applicability in scenarios where PI is not available or where the PI is not reliable.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method when dealing with large-scale datasets, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.
- The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the proposed method, which could be a concern for large-scale applications.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods that do not rely on PI, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the method's effectiveness.
