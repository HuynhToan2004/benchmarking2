 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel generative model for Riemannian manifolds, leveraging a mixture of diffusion processes to model the data distribution without requiring the time-reversal approach used in previous denoising diffusion models. This method, termed Riemannian Diffusion Mixture, is designed to efficiently handle data on various manifolds and offers a scalable solution for high-dimensional manifolds. The model is validated through experiments on diverse datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency compared to existing methods. However, the paper's presentation and clarity need improvement, particularly in explaining the technical details and the implications of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to modeling data on Riemannian manifolds, which is a significant advancement in the field of generative modeling.
- The proposed method is shown to outperform existing models in terms of performance and efficiency, particularly in handling high-dimensional manifolds.
- The paper is well-structured and provides a thorough review of related work, effectively positioning the proposed method within the existing literature.
- The experimental results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets, including both synthetic and real-world data.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from clearer presentation and explanation of the technical details, particularly the intuition behind the proposed method and the training procedure.
- There is a lack of clarity in the description of the experimental setup, including the datasets used and the specifics of the experimental conditions.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper's claims regarding the scalability and efficiency of the proposed method are not adequately supported by empirical evidence, especially in high-dimensional settings.
- The paper's dependence on the availability of the logarithm map for certain manifolds might limit its applicability to more complex or less well-studied manifolds.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the training procedure, particularly how the method is trained and what the training objective is?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent works such as [1] and [2] in terms of performance and efficiency?
- In the experimental results, how does the proposed method perform when the number of training steps is increased?
- Can you clarify the definition and usage of the 'diffusion distance' in the context of your experiments?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational challenges associated with the logarithm map, especially in high-dimensional settings?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the time-scaled distribution in your experiments and its impact on the model's performance?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to non-compact manifolds, and if so, how might this affect the model's performance or applicability?
