 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PromptAgent, a method for optimizing prompts for large language models (LLMs) by treating prompt optimization as a strategic planning problem. It utilizes Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to explore the prompt space, integrating error feedback and domain-specific knowledge to enhance prompt quality. The method is evaluated across various tasks, demonstrating improvements over baseline methods. However, concerns about the novelty of the approach, the generalizability of the findings, and the clarity of the experimental setup and results have been raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodology.
- The method of using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to optimize prompts is novel and innovative, providing a new approach to prompt optimization.
- The method is evaluated on a variety of tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and applicability across different domains.
- The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important problem in prompt engineering, which is crucial for improving the performance of LLMs.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with a clear presentation of the algorithm and its implementation details.
- The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it appears to be a simple application of MCTS to prompt optimization, which has been explored in similar contexts.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that the optimized prompts can be effectively transferred to different base LLMs, which is a critical aspect of the proposed method.
- There are inconsistencies in the experimental results and a lack of clarity in the presentation of these results, which makes it difficult to draw definitive conclusions.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational costs and the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of the number of tasks and the size of the prompt space.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the generalizability of the findings, particularly the applicability of the method to different LLMs and the transferability of optimized prompts across models.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the optimized prompts are transferred to different base LLMs, and provide evidence to support the effectiveness of this transfer?
- How does the method handle the computational costs associated with the Monte Carlo Tree Search, and what are the scalability limits of the method in terms of the number of tasks and the size of the prompt space?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the generalization of the optimized prompts across different LLMs, including both stronger and weaker models?
- How does the method ensure that the optimized prompts are effective in real-world applications, especially in scenarios where the LLM's output needs to be trusted?
- Could the authors elaborate on the decision-making process for selecting the number of iterations for the Monte Carlo Tree Search, and how this affects the performance of the method?
- Can the authors provide more details on the methodology used for collecting error feedback, and how this feedback is incorporated into the optimization process?
