 **Summary:**
The paper introduces COREX, a multi-model collaboration strategy aimed at enhancing the reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) by leveraging diverse collaboration paradigms. These paradigms include Debate, Review, and Retrieve modes, which enable LLMs to function as autonomous agents, collaborating to solve complex reasoning tasks. The approach is evaluated across 18 tasks, demonstrating significant improvements in performance compared to existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, as similar approaches have been previously explored, and the clarity of the experimental setup and results. The paper also lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the method and does not adequately address the computational costs and scalability of the proposed framework.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed COREX method and its components.
- The use of human-inspired strategies to enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs is a significant and innovative approach.
- Extensive experiments are conducted across 18 tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the COREX method in enhancing the reasoning capabilities of LLMs.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the COREX method, including an analysis of the interaction rounds in the Debate mode, performance variability with different LLMs as judges, and cost-effectiveness of multi-model collaborations.
- The method is shown to be cost-effective and efficient in terms of annotation efficiency, making it a practical solution for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed COREX method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and scope.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as similar methods have been previously explored, such as using LLMs for debate and review.
- The experimental setup and results are not clearly explained, making it difficult to assess the validity and reliability of the findings.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational costs associated with the COREX method, which is a significant concern given the potential for high resource consumption.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the COREX method, particularly in terms of the number of LLMs involved and the complexity of the reasoning tasks.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer distinction from existing methods, such as those discussed in related works.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly how the COREX method was evaluated and what specific metrics were used?
- How does the COREX method handle scenarios where LLMs disagree on the reasoning process or the final answer?
- What are the computational costs associated with the COREX method, and how do these costs compare to other reasoning methods?
- How does the COREX method perform when scaled up to include a larger number of LLMs or more complex reasoning tasks?
- Can you provide more details on the novelty of the COREX method and how it differs from existing methods, particularly those discussed in related works?
- How do the authors address the potential for hallucinations and misinformation in the reasoning process, and what steps are taken to ensure the reliability of the generated answers?
