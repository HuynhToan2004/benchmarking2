 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SuSIE, a method that leverages a pre-trained image editing diffusion model to generate subgoals for robotic manipulation tasks, which are then used to guide a low-level goal-conditioned policy. This approach is tested in both real and simulated environments, demonstrating the method's ability to generalize to novel objects and scenarios. The paper claims that the method significantly improves precision and low-level skill execution compared to existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the method's reliance on a pre-trained image editing model and the potential for the subgoals generated to be overly specific or unrealistic, affecting the robot's ability to grasp or manipulate objects effectively.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to robotic manipulation by using a pre-trained image editing diffusion model to generate subgoals, which are then used to guide a low-level goal-conditioned policy. This approach is innovative and could potentially be applied in real-world scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the methodology and the results, with good visualization and a thorough experimental evaluation.
- The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in success rates compared to other methods, particularly in real-world settings.
- The paper addresses an important problem in robotic manipulation, specifically the ability to generalize to novel objects and scenarios, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity on how the subgoals are generated and whether they are realistic or overly specific, which could affect the robot's ability to grasp or manipulate objects effectively.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in scenarios where the method might fail or not perform optimally.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other existing methods, such as those that use pre-trained image editing models for generating subgoals.
- The method's reliance on a pre-trained image editing model raises concerns about the generalizability of the method to different robotic systems and environments.
- The paper does not sufficiently address how the subgoals are generated and how they are used to guide the robot's actions, which could be improved with more detailed explanations or visual aids.
- The experimental setup and the evaluation of the method could be more robust, particularly in terms of the diversity of objects and scenarios used, and the method's performance in real-world settings.

**Questions:**
- How are the subgoals generated, and what criteria are used to determine their realism and relevance to the task at hand?
- Can the method be adapted to different robotic systems or environments without requiring extensive retraining or fine-tuning?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the subgoals might not be achievable or where the robot's actions are not sufficient to reach the subgoals?
- Can the method be extended to handle more complex tasks or scenarios, such as those involving multiple objects or more complex language instructions?
- How does the method compare with other existing methods that use pre-trained image editing models for generating subgoals?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or visual aids to clarify how the subgoals are used to guide the robot's actions and how the method is evaluated in real-world settings?
