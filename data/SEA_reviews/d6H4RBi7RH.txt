 **Summary:**
The paper explores the out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization capabilities of ensemble-based models, specifically focusing on the WiSE-FT method which interpolates between a pre-trained and fine-tuned model. It introduces a new method called BANG (Balanced averaging) to address the overconfidence issue in fine-tuned models, which is a significant factor affecting OOD performance. Theoretical and empirical analyses are provided to support the findings, including the observation that ensembling models with different sets of spurious features can lead to better OOD performance. The paper also introduces a new dataset, MultiColorMNIST, to demonstrate these effects.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a clear and concise explanation of the "FalseFalseTrue" phenomenon and its implications for out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization.
- The theoretical analysis is well-structured and provides a solid foundation for understanding the behavior of ensemble-based models in OOD scenarios.
- The paper introduces a novel dataset, MultiColorMNIST, which is designed to demonstrate the effectiveness of different sets of spurious features in OOD generalization.
- The proposed BANG method is innovative and offers a practical solution to the overconfidence issue in fine-tuned models, enhancing their OOD performance.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and visualizations that aid in understanding the theoretical concepts and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions that may not hold in practical scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
- The experimental results and their interpretation are not convincing, particularly concerning the effectiveness of BANG and the significance of the observed improvements.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other ensemble-based methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed approach.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, especially in sections with complex theoretical derivations.
- The paper's focus on specific datasets and models (e.g., WiSE-FT) might limit the applicability of the findings to other ensemble-based models or datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the BANG method specifically addresses the overconfidence issue in fine-tuned models?
- How does the proposed BANG method compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical implementation to other ensemble-based methods for OOD generalization?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of parameters and their impact on the performance of the BANG method, particularly in scenarios where it might not perform optimally?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other ensemble-based models or datasets, and what are the implications for its broader applicability?
- The paper mentions the use of different sets of spurious features in OOD generalization. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of how these features contribute to the overall performance of the models?
- Given the focus on OOD generalization, how does the proposed method perform when tested on more challenging datasets or in scenarios with more complex spurious correlations?
