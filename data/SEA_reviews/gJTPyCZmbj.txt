 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PAGFormer, a transformer-based medical image segmentation method that utilizes a novel Polar Accumulator Grid (PAGrid) to process elliptical or oval objects more effectively. PAGFormer employs a polar accumulator and slicer architecture, which allows for the processing of polar-grid features in parallel, enhancing the model's ability to handle geometric shapes. The methodology is evaluated on three medical image segmentation datasets, demonstrating improvements over existing methods. However, the paper's scope is limited to these specific datasets, and the novelty of the PAGrid is questioned due to its similarity to existing techniques like the bilateral grid. The paper also lacks a broader evaluation across different medical image types and a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its integration into the PagFormer model.
- The use of polar accumulator and slicer architecture for processing elliptical or oval objects in medical images is innovative and potentially beneficial for improving segmentation performance.
- The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method on three medical image segmentation datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and potential for enhancing segmentation accuracy.
- The integration of PAGrid into the transformer network, PagFormer, is described as a significant contribution to the field of medical image segmentation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to specific medical image segmentation datasets, which may not generalize well to other types of medical images or diseases.
- The novelty of the PAGrid is questioned due to its similarity to existing techniques like the bilateral grid, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from these prior methods.
- The evaluation metrics used are limited to the Dice score, which may not fully capture the segmentation performance, especially in terms of boundary sharpness and shape fidelity.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a clearer picture of PagFormer's relative performance and advantages.
- The complexity introduced by PAGrid and its impact on training and inference times is not adequately addressed, which could be crucial for practical deployment in clinical settings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific advantages of PAGrid over the bilateral grid, especially in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of medical images, such as CT or MRI scans of different organs?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including not only quantitative metrics but also qualitative results such as visual examples of segmentation outputs?
- How does the proposed method handle different shapes beyond elliptical or oval, and what modifications or adjustments might be necessary for other shapes?
- Can the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its generalizability or applicability in different clinical contexts?
