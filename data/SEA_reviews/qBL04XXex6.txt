 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Boosting of Thoughts (BoT), a novel framework aimed at enhancing the reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) through an iterative process of generating and evaluating "thoughts" or reasoning steps. The BoT method involves constructing a tree of thoughts, aggregating these into a single thought chain, and using LLMs to evaluate the thought chain, providing feedback that is then incorporated into the prompt for the next iteration. This process continues until a satisfactory solution is reached. The method was tested on various datasets, including the Game of 24 and mathematical reasoning tasks, and showed promising results in improving the solve rate of LLMs. However, the method's novelty and the depth of its technical contributions were questioned, with concerns about its scalability and the clarity of its explanations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed explanations of the methodology and results, making it easy to follow.
- The BoT method is innovative, utilizing LLMs to analyze their own thought processes and provide feedback, which is then incorporated into the prompt for the next iteration.
- The method shows promising results in improving the solve rate of LLMs on various mathematical reasoning tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in enhancing the reasoning capabilities of LLMs.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the BoT method across different datasets, including the Game of 24 and mathematical reasoning tasks, showing its applicability and effectiveness.
- The BoT method is flexible and can be integrated with other methods like CoT, demonstrating its versatility and potential for further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the BoT method is questioned, as it appears to be a straightforward combination of existing methods like CoT and ToT, without significant technical innovation.
- The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the BoT method's limitations and failure cases, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The scalability of the BoT method is a concern, as it relies on the accuracy and reliability of LLMs' self-evaluation, which may not be robust enough for complex or ambiguous problems.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other existing methods, such as Progressive-Hint Prompting, which also use LLMs for feedback and prompt refinement.
- The presentation of the method and results is not always clear, with some sections lacking detailed explanations or intuitive examples that could help in understanding the method's workings and implications.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of error propagation in the BoT method, which could lead to inaccurate or misleading results if the LLM's self-evaluation is incorrect.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the BoT method differs from other existing methods like CoT and ToT, and what specific technical innovations does it bring to the table?
- How does the BoT method handle complex or ambiguous problems where the LLM's self-evaluation may not be accurate or reliable?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the BoT method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of problems and the computational resources required?
- How does the BoT method compare to other methods that use LLMs for feedback and prompt refinement, such as Progressive-Hint Prompting?
- Could the authors provide more examples or case studies to illustrate the method's workings and implications, particularly in scenarios where the method may not perform optimally?
- What are the specific challenges or limitations of the BoT method that were not addressed in the paper, and how might these be addressed in future research?
