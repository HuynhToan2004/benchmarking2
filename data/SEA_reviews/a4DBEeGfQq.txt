 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Structural Compression (StructComp), a novel training framework for graph contrastive learning (GCL) that employs a low-rank approximation of the adjacency matrix to reduce the complexity of message passing. StructComp replaces the message-passing step with node compression, significantly reducing memory consumption and computational time. The framework is supported by theoretical analysis and extensive empirical results, demonstrating its effectiveness across various datasets. However, the paper's scope is limited to GCL, and it lacks a broader evaluation on other tasks like node classification and regression, which could enhance its applicability. The theoretical analysis is also criticized for its assumptions and the clarity of its presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework for graph contrastive learning, StructComp, which effectively reduces memory consumption and computational time by compressing nodes.
- Theoretical analysis is provided to support the proposed method, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, with potential for broad applications in various fields.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to graph contrastive learning, which might limit its applicability to other tasks such as node classification and regression.
- The theoretical analysis is not clearly presented, and the assumptions made in the analysis are not well-explained, which could limit the understanding and applicability of the results.
- The paper lacks a broader evaluation of the proposed method on other tasks, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its effectiveness.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The methodology section is not clearly explained, particularly the process of generating positive and negative samples, which could lead to confusion about the practical implementation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method could be applied to other tasks beyond graph contrastive learning, such as node classification and regression?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage with other scalable training methods for graph neural networks?
- Could the authors provide more details on the theoretical analysis, particularly the assumptions made and their implications for the results?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger graphs, and what are the scalability limitations that might be encountered?
- Can the authors provide more details on the process of generating positive and negative samples, and how this affects the performance of the model?
- How does the compression rate affect the performance of the model, and what are the optimal conditions for achieving the best results?
