 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of model scaling on the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) to recall facts from pre-training and perform in-context learning (ICL). The authors conduct experiments using a suite of tasks to evaluate these capabilities across various model sizes, employing pruning and dense scaling techniques. They find that fact recall deteriorates significantly when model sizes are reduced, while ICL remains robust. The paper also explores the effects of pruning attention and feed-forward layers separately. The findings suggest that fact recall is more sensitive to model size reduction, while ICL is less affected. The paper is well-written and presents clear, insightful results, although it could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the implications of these findings and a broader range of models and tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and timely question regarding the impact of model size on LLM capabilities, specifically focusing on fact recall and in-context learning (ICL).
- The authors have curated a comprehensive set of tasks to evaluate these capabilities, which are well-defined and help in understanding the behavior of LLMs under different conditions.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a focus on factual and interpretable results that are relevant to the community.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are insightful, showing that fact recall is more sensitive to model size reduction than ICL.
- The paper provides a detailed discussion on the implications of these findings, which could inform future research and practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the implications of the findings, particularly how they might influence the development of LLMs.
- The evaluation is limited to a few models and tasks, which might not fully capture the range of capabilities across different models and applications.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis on why fact recall and ICL behave differently under model size reduction, and the paper could benefit from a deeper exploration of these phenomena.
- The paper does not consider the effects of different pre-training corpus sizes or the impact of different pruning methods, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- Some figures and descriptions in the paper are confusing or unclear, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on how the findings of this paper might influence the development of LLMs, particularly in terms of designing models that are more robust to size reduction?
- Why does fact recall seem to be more sensitive to model size reduction than ICL? Is there a theoretical explanation for this behavior?
- How might the results change if different pruning methods were used, or if the models were trained on different pre-training corpora?
- Can you provide more detailed insights into the specific capabilities that are affected by model size reduction, and how these capabilities might be improved through different training or pruning strategies?
- How do the findings relate to other capabilities of LLMs, such as generalization or out-of-distribution performance?
- Could you clarify the definitions and uses of terms like "fact recall" and "ICL" throughout the paper, and provide examples or more detailed descriptions to aid understanding?
