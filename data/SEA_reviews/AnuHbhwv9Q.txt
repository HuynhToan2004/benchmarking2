 **Summary:**
The paper explores the problem of out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization in regression models, focusing on the Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) solution's sensitivity to covariate shift. It proposes a method, termed Spectral Adapted Regressor (SpAR), which adapts the weights of a pre-trained neural regression model using unlabeled test data. The method is designed to identify and project out the subspaces with the most variance difference between training and test data, thereby improving OOD performance. The paper provides theoretical analysis and empirical evidence to support its claims, using both synthetic and real-world datasets. However, the method's assumptions, such as the availability of unlabeled test data and the assumption of covariate shift, limit its general applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel theoretical analysis of the Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) solution's sensitivity to covariate shift, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of regression models under distribution shifts.
- The proposed method, Spectral Adapted Regressor (SpAR), is innovative and well-motivated, offering a practical solution for adapting the weights of a pre-trained neural regression model to improve OOD performance.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical flow and clear presentation of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
- Empirical evidence is strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method and its application in practical scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's assumptions, such as the availability of unlabeled test data and the assumption of covariate shift, limit the general applicability of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on unlabeled test data raises concerns about its scalability and applicability in real-world scenarios where such data may not be readily available.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant drawback in practical applications.
- The method's performance is not consistently superior across all datasets, and the results show some variability, which could affect the reliability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods for OOD generalization in regression, which could help position the proposed method more effectively within the current research landscape.
- The method's assumptions about the data generating process and the noise distribution are not clearly defined, which could limit its applicability in more complex or varied real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions regarding the data generating process and the noise distribution, particularly how these assumptions affect the applicability of the proposed method in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the data generating process is more complex or varied, such as in subpopulation shift or concept shift?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods for OOD generalization in regression, highlighting the unique contributions and advantages of their proposed method?
- What are the computational complexities of the proposed method, and how do these compare to other existing methods in terms of scalability and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the selection of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of neural network architectures, and what are the implications for its general applicability?
