 **Summary:**
The paper introduces InstructRetro 48B, a retrieval-augmented large language model (LLM) that leverages retrieval-augmented pretraining and instruction tuning to improve question answering (QA) tasks. The model is trained on a 1.2 trillion token corpus and demonstrates significant performance enhancements over existing models like GPT-RAG-Instruct 43B and Retro 7.5B. The authors also explore the use of the decoder-only architecture, showing comparable performance to models with both encoder and decoder. The paper presents a detailed experimental setup, including a range of QA datasets, and provides insights into the effectiveness of retrieval-augmented pretraining and instruction tuning. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned, as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors conduct a thorough evaluation, including ablation studies, which demonstrate the effectiveness of their methods.
- The paper introduces a novel approach by scaling up Retro to 48B parameters, which is a significant advancement in the field of retrieval-augmented language models.
- The results show that the proposed model, InstructRetro 48B, outperforms other models like GPT-RAG-Instruct 43B and Retro 7.5B, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a detailed experimental setup, including a wide range of QA datasets, which enhances the credibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not compare the proposed model with other state-of-the-art models like Llama 2, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the model's performance.
- The ablation study on the Retro encoder suggests that it may not be necessary, which questions the utility of this component.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the retrieval process, which is a critical aspect of the proposed method.
- The paper's focus on QA tasks may limit its applicability to other tasks, which could be addressed by a more general evaluation across various downstream tasks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify why the Retro encoder, which is a significant component of the model, does not seem to be necessary according to the ablation study?
- How does the paper address the potential limitations of the proposed methods, especially in terms of scalability and generalizability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the retrieval process, particularly how the retrieved information is integrated into the model's training and inference phases?
- Given the focus on QA tasks, how do the authors justify the choice of these tasks over other types of downstream tasks, such as summarization or translation?
- What are the specific benefits of using a retrieval-augmented pretraining approach over other methods, such as those that use direct pretraining on large datasets?
- Could the authors discuss the potential implications of their findings for the broader field of LLMs, particularly in terms of the role of retrieval-augmented pretraining in improving model performance?
