 **Summary:**
The paper explores online reinforcement learning in adversarial linear Markov decision processes (MDPs) with bandit feedback, focusing on the computational efficiency and regret bounds. It introduces two algorithms: one achieving optimal $\sqrt{K}$ regret and another with a computationally efficient $K^{3/4}$ regret. The first algorithm employs a novel approach by estimating the occupancy measure and feature vector in the policy space, while the second algorithm utilizes a Follow-the-Regularized-Leader (FTRL) framework. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings of these algorithms, including the use of linear bandit techniques and policy optimization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the algorithms and their theoretical underpinnings.
- It introduces new algorithms that significantly improve upon previous works in terms of computational efficiency and regret bounds, offering a new perspective on the application of linear bandit techniques in adversarial linear MDPs.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the algorithms, including proofs and theoretical justifications, which are well-presented and accessible.
- The algorithms are novel, particularly the first algorithm which utilizes a different approach from previous works, and the second algorithm which incorporates a computationally efficient policy optimization framework.
- The paper contributes to the field by addressing a significant gap in the literature, specifically the lack of statistically optimal and computationally efficient algorithms for adversarial linear MDPs with bandit feedback.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with existing works, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the practical applicability of the algorithms.
- There are some minor typographical and formatting errors that need correction.
- The paper's novelty is questioned due to the similarity of some techniques to previous works, such as the use of FTRL and the logdet barrier.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the algorithms, which could be crucial for understanding their practical implementation.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the finite-horizon and the known state space, may limit the generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the algorithms, particularly the first algorithm which is computationally inefficient?
- How does the paper address the practical applicability of the algorithms in real-world scenarios, especially considering the assumptions made about the known state space?
- Could the authors elaborate on the novelty of their approach, particularly in relation to previous works that have also used similar techniques?
- In the context of the first algorithm, how does the discretization of the state space affect the performance, and could this be discussed in more detail?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the policy space discretization in the algorithm's performance, and how this affects the practical implementation of the algorithm?
