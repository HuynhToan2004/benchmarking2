 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for stereo matching by framing the problem as a risk minimization problem, leveraging an L1 loss function to improve robustness. This approach, which is differentiable and end-to-end trainable, is claimed to enhance the accuracy and efficiency of stereo matching, particularly in scenarios with multi-modal disparity distributions. The method is evaluated on several datasets, including SceneFlow, KITTI, and Middlebury, showing superior performance over state-of-the-art methods in terms of accuracy and efficiency. The paper also discusses the potential for broader applications in computer vision, robotics, and control engineering.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is simple, elegant, and achieves good performance, with significant improvements in accuracy and efficiency over state-of-the-art methods.
- The use of L1 loss in the risk minimization formulation is novel and well-justified, providing a theoretical framework for the proposed method.
- The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in accuracy and efficiency.
- The method's ability to handle multi-modal disparity distributions is highlighted, which is a significant advantage over traditional methods that use expectation as the final prediction.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not comprehensive, particularly in terms of the number of datasets used for evaluation. More diverse datasets, such as those from the Middlebury dataset, could provide a more robust validation of the method.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the claimed superiority of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational efficiency of the method, especially in terms of memory usage and the number of parameters, which could affect its practical applicability.
- The paper's reliance on binary search for finding the optimal solution in the risk minimization process could limit its scalability and applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform on more diverse datasets, such as those from the Middlebury dataset?
- Can the authors compare their method with more recent state-of-the-art methods, such as those mentioned in the review, to validate its superiority?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the ground truth disparity is not continuous, and how does it compare to other methods that also handle such scenarios?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational efficiency of their method, including memory usage and parameter count, to better understand its practical applicability?
