 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FedDA, a novel adaptive gradient framework designed for federated learning (FL) with constraints, specifically for biomarker identification tasks. FedDA utilizes a restarted dual averaging technique and a momentum-based variance reduction gradient estimation to achieve faster convergence rates. Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate the efficiency of FedDA in terms of sample and communication complexity, showing improvements over existing methods like FedAvg and FedDualAvg. The paper also discusses the practical implications of FedDA in healthcare applications, where it can potentially enhance the accuracy and efficiency of medical diagnoses.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a good literature review.
- The proposed method, FedDA, is a general framework that can be applied to various gradient estimation methods and adaptive learning rate schedules, making it flexible and adaptable.
- FedDA achieves significant improvements in sample and communication complexity, which is crucial for federated learning applications.
- The paper includes a theoretical analysis of the convergence property of the FedDA-MVR variant, which provides a solid foundation for the practical application of the method.
- The empirical results show that FedDA can outperform existing methods like FedAvg and FedDualAvg, demonstrating its effectiveness in practical scenarios.
- The paper discusses the application of FedDA in healthcare applications, particularly in biomarker identification tasks, which is a significant and relevant area of research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of FedDA, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in different settings.
- There is a need for more detailed comparisons with other adaptive gradient methods in the context of federated learning, including both constrained and unconstrained settings.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to the FedDA-MVR variant, and it would be beneficial to extend this analysis to other variants of FedDA to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the practical implications of FedDA in healthcare applications, including potential limitations and challenges.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently detailed, and it would be helpful to see more comprehensive comparisons with other methods in terms of convergence speed and practical performance metrics.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of FedDA, particularly how it scales with the number of clients and the size of the data?
- How does FedDA perform in comparison to other adaptive gradient methods in terms of sample and communication complexity?
- Could you elaborate on the practical implications of FedDA in healthcare applications, particularly in terms of its scalability and applicability in real-world scenarios?
- What are the specific advantages of FedDA over other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical performance?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specifics of the datasets used and the parameters of the model and the algorithm?
- How does FedDA handle the issue of heterogeneity in federated learning, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in this regard?
