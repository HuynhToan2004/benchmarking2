 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the fairness in the online knapsack problem, focusing on the trade-off between fairness and competitiveness. It introduces a new notion of fairness called α-conditional time-independent fairness (α-CTIF) and proposes algorithms that balance fairness and competitiveness. The paper presents a deterministic algorithm that achieves a Pareto-optimal trade-off between fairness and competitiveness, and also explores the use of randomization and learning-augmented algorithms to improve fairness. Theoretical results are supported by numerical experiments, although there are concerns regarding the practicality and the theoretical contributions of the proposed algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the problem of fairness in the online knapsack problem, a well-studied problem in the field of online algorithms, and proposes a new notion of fairness that is both natural and well-defined.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the trade-off between fairness and competitiveness, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The results are theoretically sound, with proofs that are well-explained and easy to follow.
- The paper includes numerical experiments that validate the theoretical results, providing empirical evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the problem of fairness in the online knapsack problem, which is crucial for understanding the relevance and applicability of the proposed solutions.
- The proposed algorithms, while theoretically sound, are not practical and may not be feasible in real-world scenarios due to their reliance on perfect predictions which are often unrealistic.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of computational complexity, which is a significant concern for the practical implementation of the proposed algorithms.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed algorithms with existing methods, which limits the understanding of the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed solutions.
- The theoretical contributions of the paper, particularly the results on the impossibility of achieving fairness with deterministic algorithms, are not sufficiently novel and may not provide significant new insights.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a clearer motivation for the problem of fairness in the online knapsack problem and explain how the proposed solutions address real-world challenges?
- How do the proposed algorithms perform in practical scenarios where predictions are not perfect?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their algorithms and discuss potential strategies for reducing this complexity?
- How does the proposed notion of fairness compare to other notions of fairness in related problems, such as online matching and online scheduling?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations of their work and suggest directions for future research?
