 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for learning reusable dense rewards in multi-stage tasks using a two-stage approach: first, a discriminator is trained to distinguish between success and failure trajectories, and then, these discriminators are used to learn dense rewards for each stage. The method is evaluated on the ManiSkill benchmark, demonstrating improvements over baseline methods and human-designed rewards in some cases. The approach leverages stage structures of tasks to learn high-quality dense rewards from sparse rewards and demonstrations, which can be reused in unseen tasks, reducing human effort for reward engineering.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and well-explained methodology.
- The proposed method is innovative and effective, particularly in its application to multi-stage tasks, and demonstrates strong performance on the ManiSkill benchmark.
- The approach is agnostic to the specific RL algorithm used, which enhances its versatility and applicability.
- The paper includes a detailed ablation study that investigates the impact of various design choices, providing a comprehensive understanding of the method's performance.
- The method's ability to learn reusable dense rewards for multi-stage tasks is significant, as it reduces the need for extensive human effort in reward engineering.
- The paper provides a novel approach to reward learning, which is crucial for real-world applications of reinforcement learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for a comprehensive understanding of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The evaluation of the method is limited to a single benchmark (ManiSkill), which might not fully demonstrate its generalizability across different task families or environments.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with a broader range of baseline methods, which could provide a more robust validation of its effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the stage indicators and their impact on the performance of the method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method to more complex or varied environments, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the method and potential areas for future research.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when the number of stages is reduced, and what are the implications for its effectiveness?
- Can the method be extended to tasks with more complex or varied environments, such as those involving multiple objects or more complex interactions?
- How does the method handle tasks where the optimal policy might not be monotonically increasing with the number of stages?
- What are the limitations of the method, and how do these impact its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios?
- Could the method be adapted to tasks where the stage indicators are not as easily defined or where the task structure is less clear?
- How does the method compare to other recent approaches in the field of reward learning, such as those mentioned in the paper by Xie et al. (2023)?
