 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LEVER, a novel method aimed at enhancing tail performance in extreme classification tasks by addressing label variance. It utilizes a Siamese network to estimate relevance probabilities, which are then used to reduce variance in label estimates, thus improving tail performance. The method is evaluated on a variety of datasets, showing consistent improvements over existing methods. The paper also introduces two new datasets, which are valuable contributions to the field. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper's presentation, particularly in explaining the methodology and the theoretical underpinnings. Additionally, the paper's novelty is questioned as similar approaches have been previously explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant problem of tail performance in extreme classification, which is crucial for practical applications.
- It introduces a novel method, LEVER, which effectively utilizes a Siamese network to estimate relevance probabilities, enhancing tail performance.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a detailed experimental setup and extensive ablation studies.
- The methodology is simple, intuitive, and effective, with a clear theoretical foundation and practical application.
- The paper provides code and datasets, which are valuable resources for further research and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in explaining the methodology, particularly how the Siamese network is trained and how it impacts tail performance.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are not clearly distinguished from existing literature, and the novelty of the method is questionable.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational costs of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in the clarity of figures and the organization of the content.
- The paper's claims regarding the novelty and effectiveness of the method are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which are sometimes inconsistent or underwhelming.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the methodology, particularly how the Siamese network is trained and how it affects tail performance?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the experimental results, especially the inconsistencies and underwhelming performance in some cases?
- How does the paper address the issue of label variance, and what are the specific advantages of using a Siamese network over other methods?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical contributions of the paper and how they differ from existing literature?
- How does the paper ensure the reliability and robustness of the results, especially in light of the potential for overfitting or other biases in the data?
