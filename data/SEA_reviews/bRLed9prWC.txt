 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the task of future language modeling, where the goal is to predict the text of future documents based on a history of documents from previous years. The authors propose a series of models that incorporate temporal information into pre-trained language models, with a focus on generating abstracts for ACL conference papers. The models are evaluated against strong non-temporal language model baselines using both automatic metrics and human evaluations, showing improvements over baseline models. The paper also explores different methods for incorporating temporal information into language models, including a word frequency model, a contextual temporal model, and a doubly contextualized temporal model. However, the novelty of the task and the methodological contributions are questioned, as similar approaches have been explored in other contexts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel task of future language modeling, which is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their applications.
- The task of predicting future language is relevant and interesting, and the paper presents a well-designed evaluation method that includes both automatic metrics and human evaluations.
- The proposed methods for incorporating temporal information into language models are novel and interesting, with the use of LSTM for predicting temporal word bias being a notable approach.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the methods used, including the training and evaluation processes, which are crucial for reproducibility and transparency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion of related work, which could help to contextualize the contributions of the study within the existing literature.
- The novelty of the task and the methodological contributions are questioned, as similar approaches have been explored in other contexts, such as temporal language models.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed methods, which could include the scalability of the models to larger datasets or the generalizability of the findings to other domains.
- The evaluation metrics used in the study are limited and do not provide a comprehensive assessment of the model's performance. The use of only perplexity and METEOR scores is criticized for not capturing all aspects of textual quality.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed methods with state-of-the-art models, which could help to establish the effectiveness and novelty of the proposed approaches.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of their work to the field of future language modeling, particularly in terms of novel methodologies or insights?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art models in terms of performance and scalability?
- What are the limitations of the proposed methods, and how might these impact the practical application of the models in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion of the related work in the field of temporal language models, and how their work builds upon or differs from existing approaches?
- How were the hyperparameters for the models selected, and what impact do these choices have on the model's performance?
- Could the authors consider expanding the evaluation metrics to include additional measures of textual quality, such as readability or coherence, to provide a more comprehensive assessment of the model's outputs?
