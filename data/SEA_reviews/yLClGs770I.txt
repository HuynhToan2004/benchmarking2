 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MAMmOTH, a series of open-source large language models (LLMs) tailored for general math problem-solving. These models are trained on a new dataset, MathInstruct, which includes both chain-of-thought (CoT) and program-of-thought (PoT) rationales. The models demonstrate superior performance on various math reasoning datasets, outperforming existing open-source models. The paper's strengths include its comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and its methodology of combining CoT and PoT to enhance model performance. However, the paper's contribution is seen as limited by the straightforward nature of the dataset creation and the similarity of the hybrid CoT/PoT approach to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and concise explanations of the proposed methods.
- The dataset, MathInstruct, is comprehensive and diverse, covering various math fields and complexity levels, which is beneficial for training models in math reasoning.
- The proposed hybrid CoT and PoT approach is effective, as demonstrated by the experimental results which showcase the model's ability to outperform existing open-source models on multiple math reasoning datasets.
- The paper provides a detailed evaluation of the model's performance across different datasets, both in-domain and out-of-domain, which helps in understanding the model's generalizability.
- The inclusion of both CoT and PoT rationales in the dataset enhances the model's ability to handle both structured and unstructured reasoning, which is crucial for mathematical problem-solving.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on mathematical reasoning, which might limit its applicability to other domains.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as the combination of CoT and PoT is a common method in the field.
- The dataset creation process is seen as straightforward and not particularly innovative, which could limit the perceived impact of the research.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, such as potential biases introduced by the dataset or the generalizability of the models to other types of problems.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the training and inference costs associated with the models, which could be a significant concern for practical deployment.

**Questions:**
- How does the model perform on other types of problems beyond mathematical reasoning?
- Could you provide more details on the training and inference costs associated with the models?
- What are the specific advantages of the hybrid CoT/PoT approach over other methods, and how does it compare to state-of-the-art models in terms of performance and efficiency?
- How does the model handle complex mathematical reasoning tasks that might not be covered by the training dataset?
- Could you discuss any potential biases introduced by the dataset, and how these were addressed during the model training?
