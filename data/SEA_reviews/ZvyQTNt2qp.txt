 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel clipped NGM method for adversarial attacks, which modifies the MI-FGSM by clipping the normalized gradient rather than using the sign operation. This method aims to address the instability and inefficiency issues in adversarial optimization by retaining the magnitude and direction information of gradients. Theoretical analysis and empirical results are provided to support the convergence and effectiveness of the proposed method. The paper also discusses the application of gradient clipping in adversarial attacks, a topic not extensively explored in the literature. However, the paper's theoretical contributions are seen as limited and the empirical results, while showing some improvements, do not consistently outperform existing methods across all settings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to adversarial attacks by clipping the normalized gradient, which is a significant departure from traditional methods like sign-based gradient attacks.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis on the convergence of the proposed method, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
- The proposed method is well-motivated and well-explained, with clear writing that makes the content accessible and easy to understand.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in improving the transferability of adversarial examples.
- The use of gradient clipping in adversarial attacks is a promising direction that has not been extensively explored in the literature, and this paper provides a valuable contribution to this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, such as NI-FGSM, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to the convex case, and it is unclear how the proposed method performs in more complex, non-convex settings.
- The empirical results are not consistently superior to existing methods, with some experiments showing similar or even worse performance compared to baseline methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the computational complexity or time efficiency of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the convergence properties of the proposed method, especially in relation to the specific constraints and conditions used in the experiments.
