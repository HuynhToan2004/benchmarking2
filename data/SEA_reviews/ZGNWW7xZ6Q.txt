 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Reasoning on Graphs (RoG) that integrates large language models (LLMs) with knowledge graphs (KGs) to enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs. RoG operates through a planning-retrieval-reasoning framework, where LLMs generate relation paths based on KGs, retrieve valid reasoning paths, and conduct faithful reasoning. This approach is designed to address the challenges of hallucinations and lack of knowledge in LLMs, which are significant issues in complex reasoning tasks. The method is evaluated on two benchmark KGQA datasets and shows state-of-the-art performance, demonstrating the effectiveness of the RoG framework.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely research problem by integrating large language models (LLMs) with knowledge graphs (KGs) to enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs.
- The proposed method, RoG, is well-motivated and provides a novel approach by using relation paths as plans to guide LLMs' reasoning, which is both innovative and effective.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The methodology is supported by extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the RoG framework, particularly in improving the reasoning capabilities of LLMs.
- The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies and case studies, which provide insights into the performance of the RoG framework and its components.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's dependency on the quality of the knowledge graph (KG) is a significant limitation. If the KG contains incorrect or incomplete information, the reasoning process will also be affected, raising concerns about the reliability of the results.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, such as UniKGQA and DECAF, which could have strengthened the argument for the superiority of the RoG framework.
- The paper's approach to handling multiple paths from the retrieval stage is not well-explained, which could lead to confusion about the methodology's effectiveness and robustness.
- The paper's reliance on the availability of knowledge graphs and the need for significant computational resources (e.g., 48GB RAM) may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios where such resources are not readily available.
- The paper's methodology could be improved by incorporating a more robust evaluation metric, such as a factuality metric, to ensure the accuracy and reliability of the reasoning process.

**Questions:**
- How does the RoG framework handle the case where the knowledge graph contains incorrect or incomplete information?
- Could you provide more details on how the RoG framework integrates with other LLMs, such as GPT-3.5-Turbo and GPT-4, to enhance their reasoning capabilities?
- How does the RoG framework ensure that the reasoning process remains faithful to the knowledge graph, especially when dealing with complex or ambiguous questions?
- In the ablation study, what specific aspects of the RoG framework were tested, and how do these results support the overall effectiveness of the framework?
- How does the RoG framework compare to other state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Could you provide more examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications of the RoG framework in real-world scenarios?
