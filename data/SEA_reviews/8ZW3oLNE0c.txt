 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel self-evolving framework, SEArch, for optimizing network architectures by iteratively refining a student model under a resource budget. The framework combines elements of network pruning, knowledge distillation, and neural architecture search to enhance the performance of student models. It employs an attention mechanism to transfer knowledge from a teacher network and an edge-splitting scheme to construct new networks. The framework's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet datasets, showing improved performance over baseline models. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the paper's structure, the novelty of the approach, and the fairness of the comparisons made.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the motivation behind the research is clearly articulated.
- The proposed self-evolving framework is innovative, integrating elements of network pruning, knowledge distillation, and neural architecture search, which is a novel approach.
- The framework demonstrates improved performance over baseline models, as shown through extensive experiments and ablation studies.
- The use of an attention mechanism for transferring knowledge from a teacher network and an edge-splitting scheme for constructing new networks are highlighted as effective components of the framework.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the paper, particularly in the introduction and the explanation of key concepts such as the "modification value score."
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned as it seems to resemble existing methods such as dynamic network search, and the integration of pruning and knowledge distillation is not novel.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and resource utilization, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, and the comparisons made are not always fair or relevant.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specifics of the "modification value score" and its role in the framework?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and resource utilization?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how are these addressed or mitigated?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other methods, especially in terms of performance and efficiency, to better establish the effectiveness of their approach?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issues, particularly in large-scale models like transformers, which are not addressed in the current study?
