 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SHRED, a novel zero-shot image restoration method that leverages pre-trained diffusion models to solve inverse problems without modifying the reverse sampling, thereby ensuring that the generated images remain on the data manifold. This approach optimizes the initial noise to achieve high-quality reconstructions, demonstrating effectiveness across various tasks such as super-resolution, inpainting, blind-deconvolution, and compressed sensing. The method's computational efficiency is highlighted, as it avoids the need for fine-tuning or retraining of diffusion models. The paper is well-received for its clarity and methodological innovation, although concerns are raised about the novelty and the depth of experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for zero-shot image restoration that is both effective and efficient, demonstrating its utility across various image restoration tasks.
- The methodology is clearly presented, making the paper easy to follow, and the experimental results are robust, showing superior performance compared to existing methods.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the proposed method and its results.
- The optimization of the initial noise is a novel approach that avoids the need for fine-tuning or retraining of diffusion models, making it a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method on multiple tasks, demonstrating its versatility and effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different types of degradation and its sensitivity to initialization.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as similar approaches using DDIM for image restoration have been previously explored.
- The experimental validation is considered insufficient, with concerns about the fairness of comparisons and the lack of comprehensive results, particularly in terms of quantitative metrics and the inclusion of more diverse datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational efficiency of the method, particularly in terms of the trade-off between computational cost and visual quality.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other zero-shot diffusion methods in terms of computational efficiency and visual quality?
- Could the authors provide more details on the optimization process, particularly how the initial noise is optimized and the impact of different initializations on the results?
- How does the method handle different types of degradation, and what are the implications of using different types of degradation operators?
- What is the computational cost of the proposed method, and how does it compare to other methods in terms of efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the hyperparameters introduced in the method, and provide more detailed ablation studies to better understand their influence on the results?
- How does the proposed method perform on more diverse datasets, such as those with different image styles or resolutions?
- Can the authors provide more visual results to better illustrate the method's capabilities and limitations?
