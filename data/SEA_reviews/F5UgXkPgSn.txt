 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for high-rank matrix completion (HRMC) using a Grassmannian optimization model, which involves minimizing a chordal distance and a geodesic distance to facilitate the clustering of incomplete data points. The approach is supported by theoretical convergence guarantees and validated through numerical experiments on synthetic and real datasets. The method's main contributions include the use of Grassmannian optimization to handle missing data and the introduction of a new paradigm that avoids the need for distance calculations between incomplete vectors. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited theoretical analysis, particularly in terms of the fundamental information-theoretic limit and the choice of baselines in numerical experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to high-rank matrix completion (HRMC) by utilizing Grassmannian optimization, which is both theoretically sound and practically applicable.
- The proposed method is well-explained and supported by extensive numerical experiments on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.
- The paper provides a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method, making it accessible to readers not familiar with the specifics of Grassmannian optimization.
- The theoretical analysis of the proposed method is robust, including the derivation of gradient directions on the manifold of subspaces and the use of Riemannian gradient descent steps.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology, its advantages over existing methods, and its theoretical guarantees.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the fundamental information-theoretic limit, which is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the proposed method.
- The choice of baselines in the numerical experiments is not adequately justified, and it would be beneficial to include a broader range of methods to establish the robustness of the proposed approach.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper's theoretical analysis could be strengthened by including a more detailed discussion on the assumptions made and the conditions under which the results hold.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by including more illustrative examples and figures to aid in understanding the proposed method and its practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the intuition behind the chordal distance term and its contribution to the overall performance of the method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational complexity and scalability, especially when dealing with large datasets?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the choice of baselines and the rationale behind their selection?
- How does the proposed method handle different levels of missing data, and what are the implications for the accuracy and completeness of the recovered subspaces?
- In terms of practical implementation, how does the method perform when applied to real-world datasets, and what are the observed limitations or challenges?
