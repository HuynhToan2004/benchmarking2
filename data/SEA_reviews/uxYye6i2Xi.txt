 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to designing large recurrent spiking neural networks (RSNNs) through a scalable architecture and automated architectural optimization. The proposed method, Hybrid Risk-Mitigating Architectural Search (HRMAS), optimizes the topology of recurrent motifs and the SC-ML layer architecture using a two-step alternating optimization process. This approach incorporates a self-repairing mechanism inspired by intrinsic plasticity to mitigate the risks of network instability and performance degradation. The paper demonstrates that the optimized RSNNs outperform existing manually designed RSNNs and ANNs on four datasets, including speech and neuromorphic datasets. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited evaluation scope, lack of comparison with recent NAS-based methods, and insufficient experimental validation of the proposed methods' effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for the automated design of large recurrent spiking neural networks (RSNNs), which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The proposed method, Hybrid Risk-Mitigating Architectural Search (HRMAS), is a systematic approach for optimizing the topology of RSNNs, which is a challenging and open problem.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in optimizing the topology of RSNNs, showing improvements over existing manually designed RSNNs and ANNs on four datasets.
- The paper provides a good background on the current state of the art, and the proposed method is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the methodology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comparison with recent NAS-based methods, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The experimental validation is insufficient, particularly in terms of the datasets used. The paper could benefit from evaluating the proposed method on a broader range of datasets, including more complex and varied tasks.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is a significant concern given the computational demands of NAS-based methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the biological plausibility of the proposed method, particularly in relation to the biological constraints and mechanisms used in the design.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, including the time and memory requirements?
- How does the proposed method compare to other NAS-based methods, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors elaborate on the biological plausibility of the proposed method, particularly in relation to the biological constraints and mechanisms used in the design?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability issue, especially in terms of the number of motifs and their size?
- Can the authors provide more details on the optimization process, including the specifics of the optimization algorithm and the criteria used for optimization?
- Could the authors clarify the role of the SpiKL-IP rule in the optimization process and its impact on the performance of the RSNNs?
