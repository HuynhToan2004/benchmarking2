 **Summary:**
The paper explores the convexity of decision regions in machine-learned latent spaces, drawing inspiration from Gardenfors' conceptual spaces. It proposes two measures, Euclidean and graph-based convexity, to evaluate the convexity of class decision regions in deep neural networks, which are robust to certain re-parameterization transformations. The study is conducted across various modalities including image, audio, text, and medical imaging, demonstrating the pervasiveness of convexity in neural representations. The paper also investigates the relationship between convexity and fine-tuning performance, suggesting that higher convexity predicts better subsequent fine-tuning. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the findings, the lack of a clear definition of "convexity," and the limited theoretical insights provided.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an interesting question about the convexity of decision regions in machine-learned latent spaces, which is relevant for understanding the generalization and alignment of machine and human representations.
- The study is conducted across multiple modalities, including image, audio, text, and medical imaging, demonstrating the pervasiveness of convexity in neural representations.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and detailed descriptions of the experimental setup and results, which are supported by a variety of models and datasets.
- The concept of convexity in machine-learned latent spaces is a novel and important dimension for understanding human-machine alignment, particularly in the context of few-shot learning and interpersonal alignment.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method across different domains and models, including self-supervised models for images, audio, movement, text, and brain images, and pretrained models like data2vec-base, human activity model, wav2vec2.0, and RoBERTa.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and formalization of the concept of "convexity" in the context of machine-learned latent spaces, which is crucial for understanding the theoretical underpinnings and practical implications of the findings.
- The relationship between the proposed convexity measures and the performance of fine-tuning is not well-explained, and the theoretical insights provided are limited.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate between Euclidean convexity and geodesic convexity, which are introduced as equivalent concepts without sufficient discussion on their implications.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently novel or surprising, as the findings on the pervasiveness of convexity in neural representations are not unexpected.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed convexity measures and their applicability in different scenarios, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential biases introduced by normalization schemes, which could affect the interpretation of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition and formalization of "convexity" in the context of machine-learned latent spaces, and how it relates to the practical implications of the findings?
- How does the proposed convexity measure relate to the performance of fine-tuning, and what theoretical insights can be provided to support this relationship?
- Can you elaborate on the differences and implications of using Euclidean convexity versus geodesic convexity in your analysis?
- How do the proposed convexity measures compare to other existing measures of representational geometry, and what are the advantages or disadvantages of using these measures?
- Can you provide more details on the computational complexity and practical implementation of the convexity measures, especially in high-dimensional latent spaces?
- How do the convexity measures perform in scenarios where the data distribution is not uniform or where the network architecture is varied?
- Could you discuss the potential biases introduced by normalization schemes and how these might affect the interpretation of the results?
