 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new variational autoencoder (VAE) framework named t3VAE, which employs Student's t-distributions for the prior, encoder, and decoder, and replaces the KL divergence with γ-power divergence in the evidence lower bound (ELBO). This modification allows the model to better fit heavy-tailed data, which is a significant issue in traditional VAEs that often struggle with encoding points in low-density regions. The paper demonstrates that t3VAE outperforms other VAE models on heavy-tailed synthetic and real datasets such as CelebA and imbalanced CIFAR-100. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the approach, as similar ideas have been previously explored in other works, and the paper's experimental results and theoretical contributions were seen as somewhat limited.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach by replacing the KL divergence with γ-power divergence in the ELBO, which is a significant modification that allows for the handling of heavy-tailed data.
- The use of Student's t-distribution for the prior, encoder, and decoder is a novel and interesting approach that is well-explained and justified.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the modifications made to the VAE framework.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed t3VAE, showing superior performance on heavy-tailed datasets compared to other VAE models.
- The paper extends the VAE framework to a hierarchical architecture, which is a notable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other heavy-tailed VAE models, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed model's performance.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as similar ideas have been previously explored in other works, such as the use of heavy-tailed distributions and the γ-power divergence.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational challenges associated with the γ-loss, particularly in terms of the computational complexity and stability of the training process.
- The paper's experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing models, and the performance gains are not consistently demonstrated across different datasets and conditions.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are seen as limited, and the proofs and derivations provided are either inadequate or not convincing.
- There are several typographical and grammatical errors that need correction to enhance the paper's professionalism and readability.

**Questions:**
- How does the t3VAE perform on other heavy-tailed datasets, such as those with different types of heavy-tailedness or different degrees of heavy-tailedness?
- Could you clarify the computational complexity and stability of the training process for the γ-loss? How does this compare to the training of other VAE models?
- How does the t3VAE handle the generation of samples from the t-distribution, especially in cases where the degrees of freedom are high?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the hyperparameter tuning and the computational resources used?
- In the context of the paper's theoretical contributions, can you elaborate on the proofs and derivations, particularly those related to the γ-power divergence and its use in the VAE framework?
- How does the t3VAE compare to other VAE models in terms of computational efficiency and the quality of generated samples?
