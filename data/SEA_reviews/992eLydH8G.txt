 **Summary:**
The paper examines the relationship between In-Context Learning (ICL) and Gradient Descent (GD) in large language models (LLMs), challenging the notion that ICL is equivalent to GD. It argues that ICL does not behave like GD in terms of order sensitivity, sparsity, and other factors. The paper presents several experiments to support these claims, including the observation that ICL does not maintain a constant set of weights, which contradicts the assumptions made in previous works. The authors also critique the assumptions made in prior studies and propose that ICL is not a specific instance of GD but rather an emergent behavior of LLMs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear and concise explanation of the experiments and results, which are supported by thorough and well-documented empirical evidence.
- The authors effectively challenge the assumption that ICL is equivalent to GD, highlighting significant differences in order sensitivity, sparsity, and other factors.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear organization that facilitates understanding.
- The research addresses a significant and timely question in the field of large language models, contributing to the ongoing discussion about the nature of ICL and its relationship to GD.
- The paper provides a comprehensive review of related work, situating the research within the broader context of existing studies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the implications of the findings, particularly how they might affect the practical applications of ICL in LLMs.
- There is a lack of novelty in the experimental design, as the experiments primarily involve fine-tuning LLMs with ICL demonstrations and comparing the results to GD.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential limitations of its experimental setup, such as the use of a single model size (7B) and the lack of exploration of other model sizes or different LLM architectures.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous theoretical analysis, particularly in terms of the mathematical formulation of ICL and GD, which could enhance the clarity and depth of the arguments presented.
- The paper's scope is somewhat limited by its focus on LLMs, which might not fully capture the broader implications of the findings for other types of models or applications.

**Questions:**
- How do the findings of this paper affect the practical application of ICL in LLMs? Are there any specific implications for the design or deployment of LLMs that should be considered in light of these results?
- Could you elaborate on the mathematical formulation of ICL and GD, particularly how these concepts are defined and operationalized within the framework of the paper?
- In the context of the experiments, how does the choice of model size (7B) influence the results, and what would be the implications of using different model sizes or architectures?
- How does the paper's argument regarding the equivalence of ICL and GD hold up under different training conditions, such as different learning rates or optimization algorithms?
- Could you discuss the potential implications of the findings for the broader field of machine learning, particularly in terms of how they might inform the development of other types of models or applications?
