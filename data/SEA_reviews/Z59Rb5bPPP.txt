 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel autoregressive model, "trajeglish," designed to simulate driving scenarios using a tokenization scheme that discretizes trajectories into a vocabulary of 384 tokens. This model is trained on the Waymo Open Motion Dataset (WOMD) and demonstrates competitive performance on the WOMD "sim agents" benchmark, achieving state-of-the-art realism. The model's architecture includes an encoder that processes initial scene information and a decoder that generates a distribution of actions for agents. The paper also discusses the limitations of the model, including its reliance on the WOMD dataset and the potential for overfitting.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined problem.
- The proposed tokenization scheme is novel, using k-disks to discretize trajectories into a vocabulary of 384 tokens, which is both efficient and effective.
- The paper presents a competitive model that achieves state-of-the-art realism on the WOMD "sim agents" benchmark, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the model's performance, including the impact of different model components and the transferability of the model to other datasets like nuScenes.
- The method is innovative in using autoregressive language modeling to predict next state, which is a novel approach in the context of autonomous driving.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly the reliance on the Waymo Open Motion Dataset (WOMD) and the potential for overfitting due to the small size of the dataset.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the model handles the complexity of driving scenarios, especially in terms of the number of agents involved and the realism of the simulated scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address how the model handles the complexity of driving scenarios with more than 24 agents, which is a significant limitation given the potential for overfitting and the need for scalability.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the model's performance on more complex driving scenarios, such as those involving pedestrians or cyclists, which could be critical for real-world applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other models and baselines, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the model handles scenarios with more than 24 agents, especially in terms of scalability and generalization?
- How does the model ensure the realism of the simulated scenarios, particularly in terms of agent interactions and the complexity of driving environments?
- Could the authors provide more details on the model's performance on nuScenes, especially in terms of how the model generalizes to different datasets and driving scenarios?
- How does the model handle the complexity of driving scenarios involving pedestrians or cyclists, and what are the implications for the model's performance in such scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the model, especially in terms of training and inference times?
- How does the model compare with other models in terms of performance, especially in scenarios with more complex driving conditions?
