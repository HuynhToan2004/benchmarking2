 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for instance segmentation of Deformable Linear Objects (DLOs) using a combination of CLIPSeg and Segment Anything Model (SAM). The method involves a prompt encoder network that converts semantic information from text prompts into point prompts, which are then processed by SAM to generate instance segmentation masks. The paper presents a new dataset specifically tailored for DLO segmentation, which is claimed to be the largest of its kind. The method demonstrates improved performance over existing methods, showcasing a significant advancement in the field. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as the method primarily combines existing models without significant innovation, and the dataset's quality and diversity are questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem of instance segmentation of Deformable Linear Objects (DLOs), which is crucial for various applications such as autonomous driving and industrial automation.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and easy to understand, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The paper introduces a new dataset specifically designed for DLO segmentation, which is claimed to be the largest of its kind, providing a valuable resource for the research community.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing significant improvements over existing methods.
- The methodology is well-motivated and well-explained, with a clear discussion of the challenges and potential solutions in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it primarily combines existing models (CLIPSeg and SAM) without significant innovation.
- The dataset used in the study is not sufficiently diverse or realistic, with concerns about the quality and realism of the images, which may not accurately reflect real-world conditions.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The method's performance is highly dependent on the quality of the input text prompts, which may limit its applicability in scenarios where such prompts are not available or accurate.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and resource requirements of the proposed method, which could be a significant limitation for practical deployment.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and relevant baseline methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific contributions of the proposed method beyond the combination of existing models?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the input text prompts are not accurate or not available?
- Can the authors provide more details on the dataset used, particularly regarding its diversity and realism, and how it was generated or collected?
- What are the specific computational requirements and resource needs of the proposed method, and how do these compare to other state-of-the-art methods?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the text prompts are ambiguous or not specific enough to the DLOs of interest?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and evaluation protocols used, particularly regarding the use of augmentations and the impact of these on the results?
- What are the potential applications and real-world scenarios where the proposed method could be most effective, and how does it compare to existing methods in terms of practical utility?
