 **Summary:**
The paper introduces two new availability attacks, AUE and AAP, designed to undermine the effectiveness of supervised and contrastive learning algorithms. These attacks leverage stronger data augmentations to create contrastive shortcuts, enhancing their ability to poison models. The proposed methods aim to improve upon existing attacks by incorporating stronger data augmentations and theoretical insights into the relationship between supervised and contrastive losses. The paper provides experimental evidence on various datasets, showing that the proposed attacks can outperform existing methods in terms of unlearnability. However, concerns were raised regarding the clarity of the threat model, the theoretical underpinnings of the attacks, and the need for more rigorous empirical validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the motivation is clearly presented.
- The proposed AUE and AAP attacks are novel and enhance the understanding of availability attacks in the context of supervised and contrastive learning.
- The paper provides a comprehensive overview of existing availability attacks and evaluates their unlearnability across different algorithms and datasets.
- The proposed attacks are evaluated on various datasets, demonstrating their effectiveness and robustness against different types of attacks.
- The paper introduces a novel approach by combining strong data augmentations with supervised learning to create shortcuts for contrastive learning, which is a significant advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and detailed threat model, which is crucial for understanding the security implications of the proposed attacks.
- The theoretical analysis, particularly the relationship between supervised and contrastive losses, is not convincingly argued and lacks rigorous mathematical proof.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of poisoned data generalization to clean data, which is a critical aspect of availability attacks.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to specific datasets and does not include a broader range of datasets or models, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed attacks, which is a significant oversight.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the experimental setups and results, particularly in the appendix, to enhance reproducibility and transparency.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the threat model and provide a more detailed explanation of how the proposed attacks could be used in real-world scenarios?
- How do the authors justify the use of stronger data augmentations in their attacks, and what are the potential risks associated with this approach?
- Can the authors provide more rigorous theoretical analysis or empirical evidence to support the claims made about the relationship between supervised and contrastive losses?
- How do the proposed attacks perform against different types of defenses, particularly those designed to mitigate the effects of availability attacks?
- Could the authors expand the experimental evaluation to include a broader range of datasets and models to assess the generalizability of their findings?
- How do the authors address the issue of poisoned data generalization to clean data, and what steps are being taken to ensure that the proposed attacks do not inadvertently harm legitimate users?
