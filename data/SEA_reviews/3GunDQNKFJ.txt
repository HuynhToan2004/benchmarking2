 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for domain adaptation of large language models (LLMs) in the Chinese legal domain, employing a three-step process: adapt, retrieve, and revise. This approach involves training a 7B Chinese LLM on specific domain data, retrieving relevant evidence using a sentence embedding model, and utilizing GPT-4 to revise the answers based on the retrieved evidence. The method is evaluated on various Chinese legal datasets, demonstrating significant improvements over direct generation by GPT-4 and retrieval-based baselines. However, the paper's scope is limited to the Chinese legal domain, and the generalizability of the method to other domains is not addressed.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and presents a clear and concise methodology.
- The proposed method is straightforward and achieves good performance on four Chinese legal datasets.
- The experiments are comprehensive, demonstrating significant improvements over direct generation by GPT-4 and retrieval-based baselines.
- The paper introduces a novel framework for domain adaptation of LLMs using an adapt-retrieve-revise process, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is simple and effective, combining the advantages of efficient adaptation of smaller LLMs with the evidence-assessing capability of GPT-4.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus is limited to the Chinese legal domain, and it does not explore the generalizability of the proposed method to other domains or languages.
- The use of GPT-4 for revision may not be the most effective approach, as it requires access to the knowledge base, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) methods, which could provide a better understanding of its relative performance.
- The evaluation is limited to a subset of test data due to the high cost of GPT-4 API, which may not fully validate the method's effectiveness.
- The method's dependency on the quality of retrieved evidence and the accuracy of the LLM's initial draft answers could limit its applicability in scenarios with poor-quality or biased evidence.
- The paper does not discuss the potential biases in the retrieved evidence, which could affect the accuracy and fairness of the final answers.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the specific domain data used for training the 7B Chinese LLM?
- How does the proposed method compare with other retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) methods, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors elaborate on the choice of GPT-4 for revision and discuss potential alternatives that might be more suitable for real-world applications?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the retrieved evidence is of poor quality or biased?
- Could the authors provide more details on the evaluation setup, including the number of test examples used and the specifics of the GPT-4 API cost?
- Would it be possible to include a broader set of legal domains or languages in future evaluations to assess the generalizability of the proposed method?
