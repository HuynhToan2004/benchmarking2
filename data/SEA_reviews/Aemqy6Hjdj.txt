 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Compositional Feature Alignment (CFA), a two-stage fine-tuning method designed to address the compositional generalization (CG) problem in machine learning. The CFA method involves learning two orthogonal linear heads for class and domain labels on a pretrained encoder, followed by freezing these heads during a second stage of fine-tuning. The authors also introduce CG-Bench, a suite of CG benchmarks derived from existing real-world image datasets, to evaluate the performance of the proposed method. The paper provides both theoretical and empirical evidence to support the efficacy of CFA in compositional feature learning, showing improvements over traditional fine-tuning techniques. However, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental scope, focusing on a single model architecture and a small set of datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel benchmark for compositional generalization (CG), which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments on the CG-Bench, demonstrating the effectiveness of their proposed method, CFA, in compositional feature learning.
- The paper is well-written and provides clear explanations of the proposed method, making it accessible and understandable.
- Theoretical justifications are provided for the proposed method, enhancing the credibility of the results.
- The method is computationally lightweight, which is advantageous in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on the CG problem, which might not be a significant concern in practical scenarios where data distribution shifts are more common.
- The methodology is limited to a single model architecture (CLIP), which might not generalize well to other models.
- The experimental setup is limited to a few datasets, which might not adequately demonstrate the robustness of the proposed method across different data distributions.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the theoretical justifications provided, particularly the assumptions made and their implications.
- There is a need for more comprehensive experiments, including comparisons with other baseline methods and a broader range of datasets to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other model architectures, such as ResNet or EfficientNet?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the data split and the choice of hyperparameters?
- How does the proposed method compare with other methods that also use orthogonal feature learning, such as those mentioned in related works?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of datasets and the data split, particularly why certain datasets were chosen and how they were split?
- What are the specific assumptions made in the theoretical justifications, and how do these assumptions affect the applicability of the method in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different domains or data distributions, and what are the implications of these results for the generalizability of the method?
