 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to estimating counterfactual outcomes in time series data using a Self-supervised Counterfactual Transformer (SCOT). This model employs a transformer-based architecture with both temporal and feature-wise attention mechanisms, alongside a component-wise contrastive loss tailored for temporal treatment outcome observations. The methodology aims to address the challenges of modeling complex temporal dependencies and feature interactions in observations, enhancing the generalization of estimates to out-of-distribution data. The paper is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating improved estimation accuracy and generalization capabilities compared to existing models. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited evaluation on only two datasets and the lack of clarity in its presentation, particularly in explaining the technical details and the rationale behind certain design choices.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to estimating counterfactual outcomes using a Self-supervised Counterfactual Transformer (SCOT), which integrates self-supervised learning with a transformer-based architecture.
- The methodology addresses the challenges of modeling complex temporal dependencies and feature interactions in observations, enhancing the generalization of estimates to out-of-distribution data.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental results, which are supported by a thorough evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The use of self-supervised learning in the context of time series data for counterfactual outcome estimation is innovative and could potentially lead to significant advancements in the field.
- The paper includes an ablation study that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed components, such as the encoder architecture and the use of self-supervised learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The evaluation is limited to only two datasets, which may not be sufficient to generalize the results to other datasets or real-world scenarios.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in explaining the technical details and the rationale behind certain design choices, such as the use of a tree-based feature positional encoding.
- The paper does not adequately compare its methodology with existing approaches, such as the Causal Transformer, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper's claims of generalization to out-of-distribution data and its ability to handle cold-start prediction are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the rationale behind the use of a tree-based feature positional encoding and how it contributes to the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method handle the cold-start prediction scenario, and what are the implications of using a pre-trained encoder for this task?
- Could the authors provide more details on the feature distribution shifts introduced in the datasets and how these affect the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method compare with other existing approaches, such as the Causal Transformer, in terms of performance and generalization capabilities?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the choice of datasets and the selection criteria for evaluating the model's performance?
- What are the specific advantages of using self-supervised learning over supervised learning in the context of time series data for counterfactual outcome estimation?
