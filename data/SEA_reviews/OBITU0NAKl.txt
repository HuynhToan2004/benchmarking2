 **Summary:**
The paper explores the learning dynamics of graph neural networks (GNNs) through the lens of the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) in function space. It proposes a novel method, Residual Propagation (RP), which maps the NTK to a graph adjacency matrix, thereby simplifying the training process and aligning it with label propagation. The authors demonstrate that the GNN's training dynamics can be interpreted as a label propagation process, providing insights into the generalization capabilities of GNNs, particularly in homophilic and heterophilic graphs. Theoretical analyses and empirical validations are provided to support these claims, showing that the RP algorithm can achieve state-of-the-art performance on certain datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex theoretical concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- It introduces a novel method, Residual Propagation (RP), which offers a fresh perspective on the optimization process of GNNs, potentially enhancing their training efficiency and interpretability.
- The alignment between the NTK and graph adjacency matrix, as demonstrated in the paper, provides a compelling explanation for the observed generalization capabilities of GNNs, particularly in homophilic graphs.
- The paper includes a comprehensive theoretical analysis and empirical validation, which support the proposed RP algorithm's effectiveness in achieving state-of-the-art performance on certain datasets.
- The paper's findings could have significant implications for the field of GNNs, potentially influencing future research and applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical contributions, particularly the alignment between the NTK and graph adjacency matrix, are not sufficiently novel, as similar ideas have been previously explored in the literature.
- The practical implications of the RP algorithm are limited, as it only achieves state-of-the-art performance on a subset of datasets and does not outperform GNNs consistently across all benchmarks.
- The paper's empirical validation is somewhat limited, focusing primarily on node classification tasks and not exploring other potential applications or settings, such as graph classification or regression.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods, which could affect the generalizability and applicability of the findings.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is somewhat restrictive, focusing on specific model architectures and training conditions, which may not be representative of more complex or diverse GNN scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of the alignment between the NTK and graph adjacency matrix, particularly in relation to existing literature?
- What are the specific conditions under which the RP algorithm is expected to outperform GNNs, and why does it not consistently do so across all datasets?
- How do the findings of this study relate to or differ from other recent works on the generalization of GNNs, such as those by Jegelka et al. (2022)?
- Could the authors provide more detailed empirical results, including the performance of the RP algorithm on additional datasets and tasks, such as graph classification or regression?
- What are the potential limitations or challenges in extending the proposed methods to more complex or diverse GNN scenarios, such as those involving different model architectures or training conditions?
