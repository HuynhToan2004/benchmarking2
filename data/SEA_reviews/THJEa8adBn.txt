 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of density ratio modeling in online reinforcement learning (RL), extending the work of Xie et al. (2023) to the online setting. The authors introduce two algorithms, GLOW and HyGlow, to address the challenges of online exploration and computational efficiency. GLOW uses a truncated density ratio to manage the distribution shift, while HyGlow leverages offline data to enhance computational efficiency. The paper also introduces a novel meta-algorithm, H2O, which facilitates the adaptation of offline RL algorithms to the online setting. Despite the theoretical advancements, the paper is critiqued for its limited experimental validation and the incremental nature of its contributions, which are seen as extensions rather than significant advancements.

**Strengths:**
- The paper extends the work of Xie et al. (2023) to the online setting, which is a significant step in advancing the understanding of density ratio modeling in RL.
- The introduction of GLOW and HyGlow algorithms, alongside the H2O meta-algorithm, provides a comprehensive framework for online RL, addressing both exploration and computational efficiency.
- The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the algorithms and theoretical results, which are supported by intuitive explanations and detailed proofs.
- The paper addresses the challenges of density ratio modeling in online RL, a topic that has been under-explored compared to offline RL, and provides a novel approach to handling these challenges.
- The paper is significant as it provides the first theoretical framework for density ratio modeling in online RL, which is a crucial area for further research and practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks experimental validation, which is crucial for assessing the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
- The contributions of the paper are seen as incremental, with the main algorithm, GLOW, being a straightforward extension of existing methods, and the theoretical results not offering significant new insights.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its theoretical contributions from existing works, particularly in the area of density ratio modeling.
- The computational efficiency of the proposed algorithms is not convincingly demonstrated, and the practical applicability of the theoretical results is not clearly established.
- The paper does not adequately address the challenges of online RL, such as distribution shift and computational efficiency, which are already well-known in the field.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- How do the authors address the challenges of distribution shift and computational efficiency in their proposed algorithms?
- Can the authors clarify the differences in assumptions and results between their work and that of Xie et al. (2023)?
- How do the authors justify the incremental nature of their contributions, particularly in light of the significant theoretical advancements made in the field of density ratio modeling?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the practical implications of their theoretical results, especially in terms of sample complexity and regret bounds?
- How do the authors plan to address the lack of experimental validation in future work?
