 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called R-EDL (Relaxed Evidential Deep Learning), which aims to address overconfidence issues in Evidential Deep Learning (EDL) by treating the prior weight as an adjustable hyperparameter and removing a variance-minimized regularization term. This approach is supported by a thorough theoretical analysis and extensive experimental evaluations across multiple benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. The authors argue that these modifications help in balancing the trade-off between leveraging the proportion and magnitude of evidence, thereby improving uncertainty estimation. Despite the paper's strengths in clarity, theoretical grounding, and experimental results, there are concerns about the novelty and the depth of the experimental validation, particularly in terms of the breadth of datasets and comparisons with more recent methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a strong theoretical grounding.
- The proposed R-EDL method is well-motivated and addresses the issue of overconfidence in EDL, which is a significant problem in deep learning.
- The paper includes extensive experimental evaluations across multiple benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The analysis of the significance of the commonly ignored prior weight and the removal of the variance-minimized regularization term are well-articulated and provide a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
- The paper includes a comprehensive ablation study that shows the impact of the proposed modifications on the performance of EDL.
- The method is simple, effective, and requires minimal modifications to the original EDL method, making it easy to implement.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples in Section 2 to enhance understanding of subjective logic theory.
- The experimental results are not comprehensive enough, with a limited number of datasets used, and the results are not compared with more recent methods like I-EDL.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which is a significant concern in deep learning.
- The paper could benefit from a broader discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper could include more detailed discussions on the hyperparameter selection and its impact on the performance of the method.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive experimental validations, particularly in terms of the number of datasets used and the breadth of comparisons with other methods.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of overfitting, especially in scenarios where the training data is limited?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations and examples in Section 2 to enhance understanding of subjective logic theory?
- How does the method perform on more complex datasets, such as ImageNet, and how does it compare with other recent methods like I-EDL?
- How is the prior weight set in practice, and how does this affect the performance of the method?
- Can the authors provide more detailed discussions on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method?
