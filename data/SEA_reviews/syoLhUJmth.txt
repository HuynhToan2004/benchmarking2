 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the effectiveness of different visual encoders in Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs), specifically focusing on CLIP and DINOv2. It proposes a novel feature merging strategy named COMM to combine these models, which reportedly outperforms existing methods on various benchmarks such as image captioning, visual question answering, visual grounding, and object hallucination. The authors conducted extensive experiments to analyze the performance of different visual encoders and proposed a multi-level feature merging strategy to enhance the visual capabilities of MLLMs. The paper's main contributions include the exploration of shallow layer features for fine-grained tasks and the integration of DINOv2 with CLIP, showing significant improvements in MLLM performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and presents a comprehensive analysis of different visual encoders in MLLMs, which is an important and under-explored area.
- The proposed COMM method integrates both shallow and deep features effectively, showing promising results across multiple benchmarks.
- The paper is the first to explore the integration of DINOv2 with CLIP, demonstrating a novel approach to enhancing the visual capabilities of MLLMs.
- The analysis of shallow layer features and their utility in fine-grained tasks provides valuable insights into the capabilities of different visual encoders.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear and concise figures that aid in understanding the proposed methods and their results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison of the computational costs associated with different visual encoders, which could be crucial for practical applications.
- The integration of DINOv2 with CLIP seems straightforward and may not represent a significant technical innovation.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough ablation study on the MLP module, particularly in terms of its impact on performance and computational efficiency.
- The experimental results presented are not sufficiently robust, with some results (e.g., in Table 1) showing significant discrepancies between the proposed method and existing models like Shikra.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations or societal impacts of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational costs associated with different visual encoders, including the training and inference times?
- How does the proposed COMM method perform when integrated with other visual encoders, such as MAE or DeiT?
- What are the specific contributions of the MLP module in the COMM method, and how does it impact the overall performance and efficiency of the model?
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies in the results presented in Table 1, particularly the significant performance differences between the proposed method and Shikra?
- How does the proposed method handle the alignment of visual and textual features, especially in the context of different visual encoders?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential limitations or societal impacts of their proposed methods, particularly in terms of fairness, bias, and privacy concerns?
