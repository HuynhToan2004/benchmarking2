 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel algorithm for transferring learning trajectories between different neural network initializations, focusing on the permutation symmetry between training trajectories. This method, which can be applied to both randomly initialized and pre-trained models, aims to accelerate training by utilizing the similarity in learning trajectories. The paper presents a theoretical framework and empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can achieve significant improvements in training efficiency and model accuracy. However, the paper also faces criticism for its limited applicability to more complex models, such as transformers, and the lack of detailed theoretical justification for the effectiveness of the method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel algorithm for transferring learning trajectories between different initializations, which is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated.
- The proposed method is well-motivated by the observation that learning trajectories of neural networks are similar under certain conditions, which can be leveraged to improve training efficiency and model performance.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the theoretical concepts and empirical results.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method can significantly reduce the number of training iterations required to achieve a certain level of accuracy, showing promising results across various datasets and network architectures.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the method, including comparisons with baseline methods and detailed analyses of the impact of different network architectures and initialization methods on the transferability of learning trajectories.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed theoretical justification for why the method works, particularly in terms of how the similarity between learning trajectories translates into improved training efficiency and model performance.
- The method's applicability is limited to simpler models, such as MLPs and CNNs, and does not extend to more complex models like transformers, which could limit its practical utility in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for some applications.
- The experimental results are somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on image classification tasks and not exploring other types of tasks or datasets that could demonstrate the method's versatility.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work, particularly in the area of mode connectivity, which is closely related to the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed theoretical justification for why the method works, particularly in terms of how the similarity between learning trajectories translates into improved training efficiency and model performance?
- How does the method perform when applied to more complex models, such as transformers, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in extending the method to these models?
- Can the authors discuss potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
- How does the method perform on other types of tasks or datasets beyond image classification, and what insights can be gained from these experiments about the general applicability and effectiveness of the method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational costs and efficiency of the proposed method, particularly in comparison to standard training methods?
