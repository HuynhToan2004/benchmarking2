 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to instruction tuning, termed JsonTuning, which represents tasks using JSON structures. This approach aims to improve the generalization, robustness, and controllability of large language models (LLMs) by structuring inputs and outputs in a JSON format. The methodology involves converting textual prompts and outputs into JSON, which is then used for instruction tuning. The paper presents a comparative analysis between JsonTuning and TextTuning using various models and benchmarks, showing that JsonTuning generally outperforms TextTuning in terms of generalization and robustness. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the method's advantages, the necessity of using JSON, and the fairness of the comparisons made.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to instruction tuning using JSON structures.
- The experiments are well-designed, including a thorough analysis of generalization and robustness, which are critical for instruction tuning.
- The proposed method, JsonTuning, shows promising results in improving the generalization and robustness of large language models (LLMs) compared to traditional TextTuning.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation on various benchmarks, including MMLU, BBH, and InstructUE, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how JSON structures specifically contribute to the improvement in generalization and robustness compared to other structured data formats like XML.
- The methodology of JsonTuning is not well-explained, particularly how it is applied in practice and how it differs from existing methods like LoRA tuning.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of controllability, which is a critical aspect of instruction tuning.
- The experiments are limited to a subset of the Flan 2022 collection and do not include a broader range of tasks and datasets, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare JsonTuning with other state-of-the-art methods, such as those using different data structures or training strategies.
- There are concerns about the fairness of the comparisons made between JsonTuning and TextTuning, particularly regarding the use of different data formats and the potential for data leakage.

**Questions:**
- How does the use of JSON structures specifically contribute to the improvements in generalization and robustness compared to other structured data formats like XML?
- Could you provide more details on how JsonTuning is applied in practice, including the specific steps involved in converting textual prompts and outputs into JSON format?
- How does JsonTuning compare with other methods that use different data structures or training strategies, such as those using XML or other structured data formats?
- How do the results of JsonTuning compare when using different data formats like XML, and how does this affect the generalization and robustness of the models?
- Could you clarify the comparisons made between JsonTuning and TextTuning, particularly regarding the use of different data formats and the potential for data leakage?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different models and datasets, and what are the implications for its applicability and effectiveness in various scenarios?
