 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of varying the number of inner iterations during inference on the performance of implicit deep learning methods, particularly in the context of Deep Equilibrium Networks (DEQs) and meta-learning. The authors theoretically demonstrate that overparametrization can lead to a phenomenon termed "inner iterations overfitting," which suggests that increasing the number of iterations at inference does not improve performance for overparametrized networks. The paper validates this theory through extensive experiments, showing that DEQs suffer from this phenomenon while meta-learning benefits from it. The theoretical analysis is supported by empirical evidence, providing a comprehensive understanding of the dynamics of implicit deep learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of implicit deep learning, particularly focusing on the phenomenon of inner iterations overfitting (I2O).
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments to validate their theoretical findings, supporting the claims with empirical evidence.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the theoretical results are supported by extensive experiments.
- The authors have demonstrated the impact of overparametrization on the performance of implicit deep learning methods, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is structured well, with clear explanations of the theoretical results and their implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper makes several strong assumptions, such as the affinity and quadratic nature of the loss function, which may not hold in practical scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the results.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to affine inner problems, which may not accurately reflect the behavior of more complex networks like DEQs.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the findings, particularly how the results might be applied to improve the performance of implicit deep learning methods.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with some figures (e.g., Figure 2) showing large error bars, which raises concerns about the reliability of the results.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the assumptions made and the potential impact of these assumptions on the validity of the results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify whether the results hold for non-affine inner problems, especially in light of the assumption that the inner problem is affine?
- How does the paper's analysis apply to more complex networks like DEQs, which are not strictly affine?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of parameters and the impact of these choices on the results?
- How does the paper's analysis relate to the work by Anil et al. (2022), which also investigates the effects of test-time inner iterations on DEQ performance?
- Could the authors discuss the practical implications of their findings, especially how they might be used to improve the performance of implicit deep learning methods?
