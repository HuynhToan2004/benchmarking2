 **Summary:**
The paper addresses the limitations of existing uniformity metrics in self-supervised learning by introducing a new metric, the Wasserstein-2 distance, which aims to be more sensitive to dimensional collapse. This metric is designed to fulfill five desiderata, including invariance to instance permutations, scalings, and cloning, and a decrease in uniformity when features are cloned or constants are added. The proposed metric is evaluated against the original uniformity metric and shows improvements in performance across various datasets and self-supervised learning methods. The paper also discusses the theoretical underpinnings and empirical evidence supporting the new metric's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides clear motivations for the proposed metric.
- The proposed metric is well-motivated and addresses the limitations of existing uniformity metrics, particularly in its sensitivity to dimensional collapse.
- The paper includes a comprehensive theoretical analysis and empirical evidence supporting the proposed metric, which is demonstrated to be effective in various self-supervised learning methods.
- The new metric is shown to improve the performance of existing methods, indicating its practical utility.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear sections and figures that aid in understanding the proposed method and its implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed metric, particularly in terms of its computational complexity and scalability.
- The experiments are limited to smaller datasets like CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, which may not adequately demonstrate the metric's effectiveness on larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are not sufficiently robust, with some claims (e.g., Theorem 2) being questionable or not adequately supported.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other metrics, especially in terms of their sensitivity to different types of collapse and their computational efficiency.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method, including the time complexity and memory requirements?
- How does the proposed metric perform on larger, more complex datasets such as ImageNet?
- What are the specific limitations of the proposed metric, and how do these compare to the limitations of existing metrics?
- How does the proposed metric compare in terms of sensitivity to different types of collapse (e.g., constant, dimensional) across various self-supervised learning methods?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed metric with other metrics, possibly including a visualization of the metric's effectiveness on different types of data?
