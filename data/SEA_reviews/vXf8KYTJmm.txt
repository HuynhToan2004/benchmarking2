 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the problem of degenerate modes in language models, particularly focusing on the issue of empty or truncated outputs. It proposes a method called Attribute-Conditional Beam Search (ACBS) to address this issue by conditioning the search on specific attributes such as length. The authors conducted experiments on various models including LLaMA-7B and LLaMA-13B, demonstrating that ACBS can improve the quality of outputs by avoiding degenerate modes. The study also explores the impact of different model sizes and training data on the occurrence of degenerate modes. The paper is well-received for its thorough empirical analysis and the novel approach of ACBS, although concerns are raised about the generalizability of the findings and the clarity of the presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of natural language generation, specifically the issue of degenerate modes in language models.
- The proposed solution, Attribute-Conditional Beam Search (ACBS), is a novel and effective approach that could significantly improve the quality of outputs from language models.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and logical structure that makes the content accessible and understandable.
- The experiments conducted are thorough and well-designed, providing a robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the behavior of degenerate modes across different model sizes and training data, offering valuable insights into the factors influencing these modes.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of how ACBS compares to other approaches like length-truncated beam search.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the findings, as the experiments were limited to a specific set of models and training data.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in terms of clarity and organization, especially in sections where the content is dense or complex.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the training of the classifier used in ACBS, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness and reliability of the method.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive evaluation of the classifier's performance, which is essential for assessing the robustness and reliability of the ACBS method.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational resources required for the proposed method, especially in terms of memory and runtime.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of the "quality" attribute in the context of ACBS, particularly how it is defined and how it influences the output quality?
- How does ACBS compare to other methods like length-truncated beam search in terms of effectiveness and computational efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training of the classifier used in ACBS, including the training data, hyperparameters, and evaluation metrics used?
- How does the classifier's performance vary with different training datasets, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors provide more examples or detailed analysis of the classifier's performance, particularly in scenarios where the classifier may misclassify outputs?
- How does the proposed method perform with different model sizes and training data, and what are the implications for the scalability and applicability of the method?
