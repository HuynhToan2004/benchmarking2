 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel training method for Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) named Spike Accumulation Forwarding (SAF), which is designed to reduce computational and memory overhead by accumulating spikes rather than retaining membrane potentials. This approach is theoretically shown to be equivalent to existing methods like OTTT and Spike Representation, with the potential to reduce training time and memory usage. The methodology is tested on the CIFAR-10 dataset, demonstrating improvements in training efficiency. However, the paper lacks empirical results on real neuromorphic hardware, which is critical for validating the practical benefits of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The introduction of the spike accumulation forwarding (SAF) method is innovative and could significantly reduce memory requirements and training time compared to existing methods.
- The theoretical analysis provided in the paper is robust and supports the claims made about the efficiency of the SAF method.
- The paper effectively compares the SAF method with existing methods like OTTT and Spike Representation, demonstrating its advantages in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage.
- The authors have conducted experiments on the CIFAR-10 dataset, showing that the SAF method can achieve comparable accuracy to existing methods while reducing computational costs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical results on real neuromorphic hardware, which is crucial for validating the practical benefits of the SAF method.
- The experimental results presented in the paper are not sufficiently robust or comprehensive, particularly concerning the comparison with other methods like OTTT and Spike Representation.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the SAF method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not include a detailed discussion on the implementation of the SAF method on real neuromorphic hardware, which is essential for practical application.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other training methods for SNNs, such as those based on Hebbian learning or spike timing-dependent plasticity.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the performance of the SAF method on real neuromorphic hardware?
- How does the SAF method compare with other training methods for SNNs, particularly those based on Hebbian learning or spike timing-dependent plasticity?
- Could the authors clarify the computational efficiency of the SAF method, especially in terms of the number of operations and memory usage compared to other methods?
- How does the SAF method perform in terms of training accuracy and convergence speed, particularly in scenarios where the number of time steps is limited?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the SAF method and how these might affect its practical application?
