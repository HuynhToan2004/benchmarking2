 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MACCA, a novel method for credit assignment in offline multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) settings. It models the generation of individual rewards and team rewards using a Dynamic Bayesian Network (DBN), which allows for the identification of causal structures and individual reward functions. The method involves learning the causal structure and reward function from offline data, which is then used to assign individual rewards to each agent. The paper demonstrates that MACCA outperforms existing methods in several benchmarks, including MuJoCo and SMAC, and provides theoretical evidence of identifiability. However, the paper's experimental setup and the clarity of its presentation have been criticized for potential biases and lack of clarity, respectively.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in offline MARL, specifically the credit assignment problem, which is crucial for understanding the impact of individual agents on team performance.
- The use of a Dynamic Bayesian Network (DBN) to model the causal relationship between states, actions, individual rewards, and team rewards is innovative and provides a novel approach to credit assignment.
- Theoretical evidence is provided to support the identifiability of the causal structure and individual reward functions, which is crucial for the credibility of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive literature review, which positions the research within the current state of the art.
- Experiments conducted on various benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, MACCA, in outperforming existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental setup might introduce biases due to the use of ground truth individual rewards in some experiments, which could unfairly favor the proposed method.
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the definition and explanation of key concepts such as the mask and the causal structure.
- There is a potential for overfitting due to the use of L1 regularization, which could lead to spurious correlations.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of computational resources and memory requirements.
- The comparison with other methods is not comprehensive, and the paper does not include experiments on more complex benchmarks like SMAC, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the role and definition of the mask in the causal model, especially in the context of the mask c^i,s->r and its relationship with the causal structure?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where individual rewards are not observable, and what are the implications for policy learning?
- Can you provide more details on the computational resources and memory requirements of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios with a large number of agents?
- How does the method ensure that the learned causal structure is not biased towards the specific data used in the experiments?
- Can you elaborate on the choice of benchmarks and the rationale behind not including more complex benchmarks like SMAC?
- Could you discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where the assumptions of the DBN model might not hold?
