 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel pre-training framework called SNIP, which aims to bridge the gap between symbolic and numeric domains in mathematical functions. SNIP utilizes a dual-encoder system to learn symbolic and numeric representations concurrently, leveraging a contrastive loss to enhance mutual understanding between these domains. The methodology is evaluated on various mathematical property prediction tasks and symbolic regression, demonstrating its effectiveness in low-data regimes. The paper also discusses the integration of SNIP with existing models like E2E transformer SR and ClipCap, enhancing its utility in diverse mathematical applications. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the experimental results, and the scalability of the method to more complex mathematical functions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, SNIP, which integrates symbolic and numeric domains, enhancing mutual understanding between these domains through contrastive learning.
- The methodology is well-presented, making the paper easy to follow, and the experiments are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of SNIP in various mathematical property prediction tasks and symbolic regression.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology, experiments, and results, and includes a thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed method.
- SNIP is shown to be effective in low-data regimes, outperforming fully-supervised models, and demonstrates robustness and versatility in integrating symbolic and numeric learning.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear sections and a good flow of ideas, and includes a detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the work.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some experimental results, particularly in the low-data regime, and could benefit from more detailed explanations or additional experiments to support the claims.
- The novelty of the approach is limited as similar methods have been previously explored, such as using contrastive learning for symbolic-numeric data and using ClipCap for mapping numeric embeddings to symbolic expressions.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the method to more complex mathematical functions, which could limit its applicability in more complex mathematical domains.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to more complex mathematical functions and its reliance on a pre-defined data generation protocol.
- The paper does not provide sufficient justification for the choice of pre-training data and the methodology used, which could affect the reliability and generalizability of the results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the experimental results, particularly in the low-data regime, and provide more detailed explanations or additional experiments to support the claims?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods that use contrastive learning for symbolic-numeric data and ClipCap for mapping numeric embeddings to symbolic expressions?
- Could the authors discuss the scalability of the method to more complex mathematical functions and explore potential updates to the data generation protocol to address this limitation?
- How does the method handle the limitations in the pre-defined data generation protocol, and are there plans to explore more flexible data generation methods?
- Could the authors provide more details on the choice of pre-training data and the methodology used, particularly in terms of its impact on the reliability and generalizability of the results?
