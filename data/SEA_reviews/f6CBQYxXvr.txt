 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Project and Probe (Pro2), a novel method for adapting pre-trained models to distribution shifts using a limited amount of target data. The method involves two stages: first, a projection of pre-trained embeddings onto orthogonal directions, and second, the learning of a linear classifier on top of these projected features. Theoretical analysis and empirical evaluations across various datasets demonstrate that Pro2 outperforms existing methods in adapting to distribution shifts. The paper also explores the bias-variance trade-off and the impact of different projection dimensions on sample efficiency, providing a comprehensive analysis of the method's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method, Project and Probe (Pro2), is novel and theoretically grounded, offering a unique approach to adapting pre-trained models to distribution shifts.
- The experiments are comprehensive, covering a variety of datasets and scenarios, demonstrating the effectiveness of Pro2 in adapting to distribution shifts with limited target data.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, providing insights into the bias-variance trade-off and the impact of different projection dimensions on sample efficiency.
- The method is simple, practical, and computationally efficient, making it suitable for real-world applications where computational resources are limited.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other methods, particularly those that use unlabeled data or do not require target data. This could provide a more comprehensive understanding of Pro2's advantages and limitations.
- There is a potential issue with the orthogonalization process, which might not be fully optimized due to the use of QR decomposition after each gradient step.
- The paper's experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness in scenarios with more severe distribution shifts, as shown in Figure 5.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The method's reliance on a pre-trained model might limit its applicability in scenarios where a pre-trained model is not available or not suitable.
- The paper could provide more details on the computational costs and training time associated with the Pro2 method, especially in comparison to other methods.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the differences between Pro2 and the method proposed by Morwani et al. (2023)?
- How does the Pro2 method handle more complex distribution shifts, especially those that cannot be easily represented by the shifted homoscedastic Gaussian (SHOG) model?
- Is it possible to extend the Pro2 method to scenarios where no pre-trained model is available, or where the pre-trained model is not suitable?
- How does the Pro2 method perform in terms of computational efficiency and training time, especially when compared to other methods?
- Could you provide more details on the orthogonalization process and its impact on the method's effectiveness?
- How does the Pro2 method handle the issue of overfitting, especially in scenarios with limited target data?
- Could you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the Pro2 method and how these might be mitigated?
