 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ProxCoRL, a novel curriculum learning approach for reinforcement learning (RL) agents in contextual multi-task settings. It leverages the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) concept to balance task difficulty and progression towards the target distribution, utilizing task correlations. Theoretical analysis and empirical evaluations across various domains demonstrate the effectiveness of ProxCoRL in accelerating the learning process of deep RL agents, outperforming state-of-the-art baselines. Despite its strengths, the paper faces criticism for its incremental contribution to the field, the need for a more detailed discussion on related works, and potential issues with the clarity and presentation of certain sections.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear structure that makes it accessible to readers.
- The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work, highlighting the differences between their approach and existing methods.
- The paper introduces a novel curriculum strategy, ProxCoRL, which effectively balances task difficulty and progression towards the target distribution, using task correlations.
- Theoretical analysis and empirical evaluations are provided, demonstrating the effectiveness of ProxCoRL in accelerating the training process of deep RL agents.
- The paper addresses the challenging problem of curriculum design in contextual multi-task settings, which is crucial for the success of RL agents in complex tasks.
- The proposed curriculum strategy is easy to integrate with deep RL frameworks, making it applicable in various scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the differences between ProxCoRL and other existing methods, particularly in terms of the ZPD concept and task selection strategies.
- The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of ProxCoRL over existing methods, with some results showing comparable or worse performance.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential issues with the assumptions made and the scalability of the approach.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational and sample efficiency of ProxCoRL, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- The paper lacks a clear discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential challenges.
- There are issues with the clarity and presentation of certain sections, particularly in the mathematical formulations and the definitions used.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed discussion on the differences between ProxCoRL and other existing methods, particularly in terms of the ZPD concept and task selection strategies?
- How does ProxCoRL compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational and sample efficiency?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental results, particularly where ProxCoRL underperforms compared to other methods?
- How does ProxCoRL handle the balance between task difficulty and progression towards the target distribution, especially in scenarios where the target distribution is not uniform?
- Could the authors clarify the mathematical formulations and definitions used in the paper, particularly in sections where the notation and definitions are unclear?
- What are the specific challenges and limitations of the proposed method that were not addressed in the paper?
