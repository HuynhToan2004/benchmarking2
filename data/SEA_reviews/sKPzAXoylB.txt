 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Utility-based Perturbed Gradient Descent (UPGD) for continual learning, aiming to address both catastrophic forgetting and loss of plasticity in neural networks. UPGD leverages an approximation of weight utility to prioritize weight updates, focusing on maintaining the utility of existing weights and promoting plasticity in less useful weights. The method is evaluated across various datasets and tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in mitigating forgetting and maintaining plasticity. The paper also proposes new metrics for evaluating forgetting and plasticity in the context of streaming learning. However, concerns were raised regarding the practicality of the method due to its reliance on a computationally intensive utility metric and the lack of a direct comparison with other methods in terms of computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed method and its experimental setup.
- The introduction of a new utility-based metric for weight importance and the use of a second-order Taylor approximation to estimate utility are novel and contribute to the field.
- The method is evaluated across various datasets and tasks, showing that it effectively mitigates catastrophic forgetting and loss of plasticity.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method against other existing methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The inclusion of a new metric for evaluating forgetting and plasticity in the context of streaming learning is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a direct comparison of the computational efficiency of the proposed method with other methods, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The practicality of the method is questioned due to the high computational cost associated with the utility metric, which might limit its scalability to larger or more complex models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and a broader comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- There is a need for more comprehensive experimental results, including the inclusion of more datasets and a comparison with a broader range of methods to validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper could be improved by addressing minor issues such as typographical errors and inconsistencies in the presentation of results.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational efficiency of the proposed method with other methods, including the time taken for each update step?
- How does the proposed method perform when scaled up to larger or more complex models?
- What are the specific computational requirements of the proposed method, and how do these compare to other methods in terms of efficiency and scalability?
- How does the method handle the trade-off between forgetting and plasticity, particularly in scenarios where these two factors might conflict?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancies in the results presented in the paper, such as the performance of S-RWalk in different scenarios?
- What are the implications of using different activation functions on the effectiveness of the proposed method?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of datasets, such as those with varying levels of non-stationarity or different types of tasks?
