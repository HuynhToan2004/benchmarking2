 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach, SMEAR, for training models with conditional computation by utilizing a weighted average of experts' parameters, avoiding the need for discrete routing decisions which typically rely on heuristic methods. This method, which is computationally efficient and enables standard gradient-based training, is validated through experiments on the GLUE and DomainNet datasets. The results show that SMEAR outperforms existing methods, including those that use gradient estimation and heuristic routing, without significantly increasing computational costs. The paper also discusses the potential limitations and societal impacts of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise language that effectively conveys the key points and methodology.
- The proposed method, SMEAR, is novel and provides a new perspective on training models with conditional computation, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experimental results demonstrate that SMEAR outperforms existing methods, particularly in scenarios where it is compared to models that use gradient estimation and heuristic routing.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of SMEAR, including a comparison to multiple baselines and a detailed analysis of the results.
- The authors have conducted a thorough literature review, which is beneficial for readers in understanding the context and relevance of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a significant drawback for readers seeking a comprehensive understanding of the technology.
- The computational cost analysis in the paper is not convincing, particularly in scenarios where the computational cost of SMEAR is compared to other methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of the number of experts and the complexity of the tasks.
- The methodology used in the paper, such as the use of the REINFORCE algorithm and the handling of gradient estimators, is not sufficiently explained or justified, which could confuse readers unfamiliar with these concepts.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant omission in the context of ethical considerations in AI research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost of SMEAR compared to other methods, particularly in scenarios where the computational cost is a significant concern?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with a large number of experts, and how does it scale with the complexity of the tasks?
- Can the authors clarify the methodology used in the paper, particularly in sections where the handling of gradient estimators and the REINFORCE algorithm is not adequately explained?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use gradient estimation, and what are the specific advantages of SMEAR in these comparisons?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training process, such as the number of training epochs and the duration of each epoch, to help readers better understand the experimental setup?
- How does the proposed method address the potential negative societal impacts, and what measures have been taken to mitigate these risks?
