 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for estimating aleatoric uncertainty in neural networks by leveraging class-contextual information to construct phantom representations. This approach is designed to mitigate the underestimation of uncertainty in neural networks, a common issue in probabilistic neural networks. The method involves defining class-contextual samples and phantom representations, which are then used to estimate uncertainty through variational inference. The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness through experiments on noisy label learning and cross-domain generalization, showing improvements over existing methods. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in explaining the method and its experimental setups, and lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of uncertainty modeling, specifically focusing on the underestimation of uncertainty in probabilistic neural networks.
- The proposed method is novel, introducing the concept of "phantom" representations, which are distinct from traditional methods and offer a new approach to uncertainty estimation.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental results.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in noisy label learning and cross-domain generalization tasks.
- The paper provides a theoretical foundation for the method, which is supported by rigorous mathematical proofs and empirical results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of experimental setup and results. This makes it difficult to assess the true effectiveness of the proposed method against current state-of-the-art techniques.
- The clarity of the paper needs improvement, particularly in explaining the methodology and experimental setups. The definitions and roles of class-contextual samples and phantom representations are not clearly defined, which could lead to confusion.
- The paper suffers from several presentation issues, such as unclear figures and inconsistencies in notation and terminology.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant limitation for its practical implementation.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with marginal improvements reported in some cases.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and roles of class-contextual samples and phantom representations in the methodology?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational complexity and practical implementation?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations of the experimental setups and results, particularly in comparison to existing methods?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the class-contextual samples are not readily available or where their selection is not straightforward?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of the proposed method on the estimation of epistemic uncertainty, which is also a significant area of interest in uncertainty modeling?
