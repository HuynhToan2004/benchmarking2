 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel numerical encoding scheme named xVal, which represents real numbers using a single token derived from an embedding vector scaled by the number value. This approach aims to enhance the token efficiency and generalization of Large Language Models (LLMs) in handling numerical data, particularly in scientific domains. The methodology involves a modified number-inference paradigm that renders transformer models continuous, enabling them to interpolate effectively between numerical values. The paper presents empirical evaluations on synthetic and real-world datasets, showing that xVal outperforms existing number encoding schemes in terms of token efficiency and generalization. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, as similar methods have been explored in prior works, and the empirical evaluations are limited to a few datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of Large Language Models (LLMs) by focusing on the encoding of numerical values, which is crucial for handling scientific data effectively.
- The proposed method, xVal, is novel and well-motivated, providing a straightforward yet effective solution to the challenges of encoding numbers in LLMs.
- The paper is well-written, making it clear and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and the results.
- The empirical evaluations are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of xVal in various scenarios, including both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The paper provides a comprehensive discussion on the limitations and potential improvements of the xVal method, which enhances the credibility of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as similar methods have been explored in prior works, such as those using Fourier features on the logarithm of numbers, which could potentially address the limitations of xVal.
- The empirical evaluations are limited to a few datasets, which may not adequately represent the generalizability of the xVal method across different types of datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential limitations or failure modes of the xVal method, which could affect its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and memory requirements of the xVal method compared to other number encoding schemes, which could be crucial for practical implementation.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive comparison with the latest LLMs and number encoding schemes, which could affect the relevance and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the novelty of their approach compared to similar methods in prior works, and discuss how their method addresses the limitations of these prior works?
- How does the xVal method perform when applied to a broader range of datasets, including those with different characteristics and scales?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and memory requirements of the xVal method compared to other number encoding schemes, and discuss any potential trade-offs in terms of computational efficiency and model performance?
- What are the specific advantages of the xVal method over other number encoding schemes, especially in terms of practical applicability and generalizability?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential failure modes of the xVal method and discuss how these might affect the performance and applicability of the method in real-world scenarios?
