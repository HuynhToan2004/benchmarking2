 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for foundation reinforcement learning (FRL) aimed at enhancing the learning efficiency of embodied agents by leveraging prior knowledge from foundation models. The FRL framework incorporates three types of priors: policy, value, and success reward, which are used to guide the learning process in goal-conditioned Markov decision processes (MDPs). The paper proposes a new actor-critic algorithm, FAC, which utilizes these priors to improve sample efficiency and robustness to noisy priors. The effectiveness of the proposed method is evaluated on the MetaWorld benchmark, showing significant improvements over baseline methods. However, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental scope, lack of comparisons with relevant works, and the need for more comprehensive ablation studies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-designed framework.
- The proposed FRL framework is intuitive and effective, and the FAC algorithm is simple yet powerful, demonstrating improved sample efficiency and robustness to noisy priors.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the FRL framework and its components, including the policy, value, and success-reward priors, which are well-motivated and well-explained.
- The empirical results are strong, showing significant improvements in sample efficiency and robustness to noisy priors compared to baseline methods.
- The ablation study is well-designed and helps in understanding the importance of each component of the FRL framework.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with relevant works, particularly those that explore similar ideas such as pre-training for foundation models and reward modeling.
- The experimental setup is limited, focusing only on the MetaWorld benchmark, which might not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and a failure mode analysis, which could help in understanding the practical applicability of the FRL framework.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method to more complex environments or real-world applications.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the potential negative societal impacts and ethical considerations of the FRL framework.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the proposed FRL framework can be scaled to more complex environments or real-world applications?
- How does the FRL framework handle different types of prior knowledge, especially those that might not be as well-defined or reliable as those used in the experiments?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the FRL framework can be adapted to handle non-goal-based tasks, such as those found in simulated environments like AI2-Thor?
- How does the FRL framework ensure the generalizability of the learned policies across different tasks or environments?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential negative societal impacts and ethical considerations of the FRL framework, particularly in terms of fairness, accountability, and transparency?
- How does the FRL framework compare to other methods that utilize foundation models in reinforcement learning, such as those that use pre-trained language models for policy learning or reward modeling?
