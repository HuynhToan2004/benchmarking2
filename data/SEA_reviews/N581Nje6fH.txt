 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel memory mechanism for Transformer-based agents aimed at enhancing memory efficiency and performance in long-horizon decision-making tasks. The proposed memory mechanism, termed Automatic Chunking, ForgetSpan, and SimilarityWeight, is designed to selectively attend to relevant memories, forget irrelevant ones, and weight new memories based on similarity to existing memories. The methodology is tested across various tasks, including Minigrid, Audio-Visual Instructions, and Visual Corridor, demonstrating improvements in memory efficiency and task performance compared to baseline TransformerXL. However, the paper is critiqued for its lack of novelty, inadequate empirical validation, and insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in Transformer-based agents, focusing on improving memory efficiency and task performance in long-horizon decision-making tasks.
- The proposed memory mechanism, including Automatic Chunking, ForgetSpan, and SimilarityWeight, is well-explained and easy to understand, making it accessible to readers.
- The paper presents clear and concise experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in memory efficiency and task performance compared to the baseline TransformerXL.
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive set of experiments that validate the proposed method across various tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as the proposed methods, such as Automatic Chunking and ForgetSpan, are similar to existing methods like Memory Augmented Transformers and Gated Transformer-XL.
- The experimental evaluation is insufficient, with only three tasks used to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, and the results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational costs associated with the proposed mechanisms.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods and a broader range of tasks to validate the generalizability of the proposed methods.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in the clarity and explanation of the proposed methods and the experimental setup.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the Automatic Chunking mechanism works, including how the memory is split into chunks and how the top-k chunks are selected?
- How does the paper address the potential issues of information loss due to forgetting in the ForgetSpan mechanism?
- What are the specific computational costs associated with the proposed mechanisms, and how do these compare to the computational costs of existing methods?
- How does the paper ensure the generalizability of the proposed methods across different tasks and domains?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a discussion on the advantages and disadvantages of their approach compared to these methods?
- How does the paper address the potential limitations of the proposed methods, such as the selective attention mechanism and the potential for information loss?
