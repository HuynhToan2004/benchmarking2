 **Summary:**
The paper introduces UnitigBin, a novel binning tool designed to address the limitations of existing methods in metagenomic contig binning. It leverages unitig-level assembly graphs and incorporates heterophilous constraints to enhance the clustering of contigs based on species information. The paper presents a three-stage binning process that includes graph disentangling, contig sampling, and graph neural network (GNN) modeling. Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of UnitigBin, showing improvements over existing methods in terms of completeness and purity. However, the paper suffers from several issues including unclear writing, inadequate explanation of certain methodologies, and a lack of comprehensive experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to metagenomic contig binning by utilizing unitig-level assembly graphs, which is a departure from traditional contig-level graphs.
- The use of GNNs for metagenomic binning is innovative, particularly in the context of unitig-level graphs, which have been underutilized in previous studies.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of UnitigBin, comparing it against a wide range of existing methods and demonstrating its effectiveness through extensive experiments.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear pseudocode and algorithmic descriptions, making it easier for readers to understand the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and logical flow, which aids in understanding the complex methodologies and results presented.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from unclear writing and lacks detailed explanations in certain sections, making it difficult for readers to fully grasp the proposed methods and their implications.
- There is a notable absence of detailed experimental validation, particularly concerning the effectiveness of the graph disentangling and contig sampling steps.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach, such as the potential for overfitting due to the use of GNNs and the lack of validation on larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not provide sufficient justification for the use of GNNs over other methods like k-nearest neighbors (KNN) or other clustering algorithms, which could have been more straightforward and potentially faster.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors throughout the paper, which detract from its professional presentation and readability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the rationale behind using GNNs for metagenomic binning, especially considering the potential for overfitting and the simplicity of other clustering methods?
- How does the paper ensure that the GNNs do not overfit the training data, particularly in light of the complex and varied nature of metagenomic data?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental validation, especially concerning the effectiveness of the graph disentangling and contig sampling steps?
- How does the paper address the scalability issues associated with the GNNs, particularly in terms of computational complexity and training time?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their approach, including potential biases and assumptions that could affect the accuracy of the binning results?
- How does the paper ensure the reproducibility of its results, particularly in terms of data availability and the replicability of the experimental setup?
