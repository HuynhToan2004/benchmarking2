 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Variational Information-based Interaction-Grounded Learning (VI-IGL) method aimed at optimizing the reward decoder in reinforcement learning (RL) scenarios where the learner infers latent binary rewards from interaction with the environment. The method leverages an information-theoretic approach to enforce the conditional independence assumption, which is critical for learning the optimal policy. The authors propose a variational formulation of the objective function, which is shown to be equivalent to a min-max optimization problem, and provide empirical results demonstrating the method's effectiveness in noisy feedback environments. The paper also discusses the limitations of the approach, including the computational complexity and the need for further theoretical analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel information-theoretic approach to the Interaction-Grounded Learning (IGL) problem, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The proposed VI-IGL method effectively enforces the conditional independence assumption, which is crucial for learning the optimal policy in RL settings.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and a good flow, making it accessible to readers.
- The use of variational representation of mutual information for estimating the objective function is a notable strength, providing a tractable solution to the optimization problem.
- The paper provides empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the VI-IGL method in noisy feedback environments, showing improved performance compared to previous IGL-based RL algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the VI-IGL algorithm, which is a significant concern given the need for sampling from the joint distribution of X, A, Y, and R, which can be intractable for continuous random variables.
- There is an absence of theoretical analysis on the sample complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods in the field, which limits the evaluation of its effectiveness and efficiency.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential pitfalls.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to a single task, which does not sufficiently demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overfitting, which is a significant challenge in the context of IGL.
