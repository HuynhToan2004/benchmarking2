 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for offline preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL) by proposing an algorithm that utilizes in-dataset exploration. This approach employs a pessimistic value iteration algorithm to manage the uncertainty in offline datasets and optimistically estimates the reward function to encourage exploration. The theoretical underpinnings of this method are supported by a theorem that provides guarantees on the performance of the algorithm. Empirical results demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness in reducing the number of queries needed, as shown in experiments on Meta-World and Antmaze environments. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope to linear MDPs and its reliance on heuristic approaches in certain parts, which may affect the robustness and scalability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research.
- The proposed method is intuitive, easy to understand, and the theoretical analysis is sound.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it outperforms existing baselines.
- The methodology for query selection is simple and easy to understand, with a clear explanation of the algorithm's components.
- The paper provides theoretical guarantees, which are supported by proofs and empirical results.
- The idea of in-dataset exploration is novel and interesting, potentially reducing the number of queries needed in offline PbRL.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies on heuristic approaches for some components, such as the use of randomized weighted trajectories and optimistic bonus, which may not be optimal.
- The algorithm's reliance on the linear MDP assumption limits its applicability to more complex environments.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to linear MDPs, and it's unclear how the results would generalize to more complex settings.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the number of queries used and the specifics of the hyperparameters.
- The empirical results are not convincing due to the limited scope of the experiments and the potential for cherry-picking results.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including its assumptions and potential negative societal impacts.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the method would perform if the offline dataset contains trajectories with the same state-action pairs?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the offline dataset is not sufficient, and what are the implications for the performance of the algorithm?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the number of queries used and the specifics of the hyperparameters?
- How does the method perform in more complex environments beyond linear MDPs?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a broader range of baselines and a more thorough ablation study?
- How does the method ensure that the queries are representative of the entire dataset, and what measures are in place to prevent biased or incomplete datasets from affecting the results?
