 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for estimating the performance of a model on unlabeled target data by utilizing the entropy of the network's predictions and the difference between the model's weights before and after fine-tuning on a self-supervised loss. This method aims to address the domain shift between source and target datasets, which is crucial in real-world scenarios where labeled data in the target domain is unavailable. The authors argue that their approach can provide a more reliable performance estimate compared to existing activation-based and performance-based baselines. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope in terms of the diversity of datasets and models used, and the lack of rigorous theoretical backing for its claims.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the authors have provided clear explanations for the proposed method and its components.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and effective, as demonstrated through extensive experiments on standard object recognition benchmarks using diverse network architectures.
- The paper addresses a significant and practical problem of predicting the performance of a model on unlabeled target data, which is crucial for real-world applications where labeled data in the target domain is unavailable.
- The methodology is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the intuition behind the proposed method.
- The paper includes a thorough analysis of the results, which helps in understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed method, particularly in terms of why the weight-based metric is effective and how it relates to the domain shift.
- The experimental setup is limited, focusing on a small set of datasets and models, which may not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a clearer picture of the method's performance relative to current best practices.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation, including a broader range of datasets and models to better demonstrate the method's effectiveness across different domains.
- The paper's claims about the method's effectiveness are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, particularly in scenarios where the method's performance is compared to other methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the selection of datasets and models, to clarify the scope and generalizability of the results?
- How does the proposed method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide a theoretical analysis or additional experimental evidence to support the claims made about the method's effectiveness and its relationship to the domain shift?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios where the domain shift is significant, and what are the practical implications of using this method in such scenarios?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and models, and the rationale behind the experimental design?
