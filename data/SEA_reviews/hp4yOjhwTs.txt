 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of causal inference to enhance curriculum learning in reinforcement learning (RL). It introduces the concept of causally aligned source tasks, which are critical for maintaining the invariance of optimal decision rules across different tasks. The authors propose a method for generating such tasks, leveraging a causal graph to ensure alignment. The paper also provides theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of this approach in high-dimensional confounded environments. The methodology involves constructing a causal graph from the original task and generating source tasks by modifying variables in the graph. This approach is evaluated in two toy environments and is shown to outperform non-causal baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear definitions and examples that aid in understanding complex concepts.
- The integration of causal inference into curriculum learning is a novel approach, providing a new perspective on how to design tasks that align with the target task.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and solid, with clear definitions and theorems that support the proposed method.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and logical flow, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, with a straightforward algorithm that is easy to implement.
- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the theoretical results, which are supported by empirical results in two toy environments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader empirical evaluation, focusing only on two simple environments which might not adequately demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on related works, particularly in the field of curriculum learning.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections, particularly in the definitions and explanations of certain variables and concepts.
- The paper's reliance on causal inference might limit its applicability to more complex environments where causal graphs are not readily available or are difficult to construct.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential extensions or improvements.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors elaborate on how the proposed method could be applied to more complex environments where causal graphs are not readily available or are difficult to construct?
- How does the proposed method compare to other approaches that use causal inference in curriculum learning, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to demonstrate the practical applications of the method in real-world scenarios?
- In the context of the experiments, how does the performance of the causal curriculum learning compare to that of the non-causal curriculum learning in environments where the causal graph is not known or difficult to construct?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their approach and suggest possible directions for future research to address these limitations?
