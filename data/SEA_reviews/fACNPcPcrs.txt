 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of large language models (LLMs) in text-based collaborative filtering (TCF) for recommendation tasks, focusing on the scalability of item encoders from 100M to 175B parameters. The authors examine the impact of LLMs on TCF performance and assess their potential for universal item representation. The study includes experiments on various datasets and models, including SASRec and DSSM, to investigate the effectiveness of LLMs in recommendation tasks. The paper also discusses the challenges and potential of LLMs in achieving universal representation and transferability in TCF.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The topic of using large language models (LLMs) for text-based collaborative filtering (TCF) is timely and relevant, as LLMs are increasingly used in recommendation systems.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies, which provide valuable insights into the performance of LLMs in TCF.
- The authors conduct a thorough analysis of the results, which helps in understanding the limitations and potential of LLMs in recommendation tasks.
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem, investigating the use of LLMs for recommendation, which is a significant area of interest in the current research landscape.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear and concise definition of TCF, which could confuse readers about the specifics of the methodology.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its work, such as the potential impact of LLMs on cold-start recommendations and the scalability of LLMs in real-world applications.
- The experimental results and their interpretations are not always convincing, particularly in the claims about the superiority of LLMs over traditional methods like IDCF.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its results with existing literature, which could have provided a more robust validation of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of LLMs in recommendation tasks and the potential strategies for addressing these limitations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of TCF in their study, especially in relation to the use of LLMs for recommendation?
- How does the paper address the scalability of LLMs in real-world applications, particularly in terms of computational resources and training time?
- Can the authors provide more detailed insights into the performance of LLMs in cold-start recommendations, and discuss potential strategies for improving their effectiveness in this area?
- How does the paper justify the use of LLMs as a replacement for IDCF in recommendation tasks, particularly in terms of the effectiveness and efficiency of these approaches?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of LLMs on the transferability of recommendations across different domains and platforms, and suggest strategies for enhancing this transferability?
