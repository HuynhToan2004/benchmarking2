 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Entropy-MCMC (EMCMC), a method aimed at sampling from flat regions of the posterior distribution in deep learning models, which are typically associated with better generalization. EMCMC utilizes a guiding variable, the stationary distribution of which resembles a smoothed posterior, to bias sampling towards these flat regions. The method is shown to converge faster than existing flatness-aware methods in the strongly convex setting and is supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence. The paper demonstrates that EMCMC outperforms various baselines on benchmarks such as classification, calibration, and out-of-distribution detection. However, concerns were raised regarding the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the strong convexity of the loss function, and the practical applicability of these assumptions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and intuitive explanations that make the content accessible.
- The proposed method, Entropy-MCMC, is novel and provides a simple yet effective approach to sampling from flat basins in the posterior distribution, which is crucial for generalization in deep learning models.
- The theoretical analysis is sound and robust, demonstrating the convergence of EMCMC and its superiority over existing flatness-aware methods in the strongly convex setting.
- The empirical results are strong, showing that EMCMC outperforms various baselines on benchmarks such as classification, calibration, and out-of-distribution detection.
- The paper is significant in its contribution to the field of Bayesian deep learning, addressing the importance of flat modes in the energy landscape for generalization and robustness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper makes strong assumptions, such as the strong convexity of the loss function, which may not hold in practical scenarios, and the theoretical analysis might not be applicable in these cases.
- The computational overhead of EMCMC, while similar to SGLD, might still be a concern for larger models, especially considering the additional computational demands of sampling from the joint distribution.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with related works, particularly in the context of MCMC methods for Bayesian neural networks.
- The practical applicability of the theoretical results, which are based on strong convexity assumptions, is questionable, as these assumptions might not hold in practical scenarios.
- There are some minor typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly regarding the strong convexity of the loss function?
- How does EMCMC compare in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability with other MCMC methods for Bayesian neural networks, especially those that do not rely on strong convexity assumptions?
- Is there a possibility to extend the theoretical analysis to more general cases where the strong convexity assumption might not hold?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational overhead of EMCMC compared to other methods, especially in practical scenarios?
- How does the paper address the potential issues of overfitting or generalization when sampling from flat basins, which might not always correspond to optimal solutions?
