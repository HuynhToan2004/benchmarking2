 **Summary:**
The paper investigates uncertainty quantification in black-box large language models (LLMs) using a novel approach that does not require access to the model's internal parameters. It proposes several methods for estimating uncertainty and confidence in LLM outputs, particularly focusing on natural language generation tasks like question answering. The authors evaluate these methods using a variety of datasets and models, comparing their performance against existing methods. The paper argues that its approach, which involves using metrics such as Jaccard similarity and natural language inference, can effectively quantify uncertainty and confidence in LLM outputs. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of novelty and its limited applicability to open-ended generation tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem of uncertainty quantification in black-box large language models (LLMs), which is crucial for practical applications in natural language generation (NLG).
- The proposed methods are straightforward, easy to implement, and do not require training or access to the model's internal parameters, making them practical for real-world scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and examples that aid in understanding the proposed methods and their implications.
- The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing the proposed methods with existing ones and demonstrating their effectiveness in practical scenarios.
- The paper addresses an important and under-explored topic of uncertainty quantification in LLMs, which is a significant challenge in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it primarily adapts existing methods from classification with rejection to NLG, which may not be a significant advancement in the field.
- The applicability of the proposed methods is limited to question answering tasks, which may not generalize well to open-ended generation tasks, such as those involving creative or novel responses.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of overconfidence in LLMs, which is a significant concern in the field.
- The paper does not provide a clear definition or distinction between different types of uncertainty (epistemic vs. aleatoric), which is crucial for understanding the nature and implications of the uncertainty quantified.
- The paper's evaluation metrics, such as AUROC and AUARC, may not adequately capture the nuances of uncertainty and confidence in LLM outputs, particularly in scenarios where multiple correct answers are possible.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its methods, such as their applicability to different types of LLMs or their effectiveness in scenarios where the model's confidence is misleading.

**Questions:**
- Can the proposed methods be adapted to open-ended generation tasks, such as those involving creative or novel responses? If so, how would the evaluation metrics need to be adjusted to account for the inherent uncertainty in these tasks?
- How does the paper address the issue of overconfidence in LLMs, particularly in scenarios where the model's confidence is misleading?
- Could the paper benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of its methods, including their applicability to different types of LLMs and their effectiveness in scenarios where the model's confidence is not a reliable indicator of accuracy?
- How does the paper distinguish between different types of uncertainty (epistemic vs. aleatoric), and what are the implications of this distinction for the practical application of the proposed methods?
- In light of the paper's focus on question answering tasks, how does it address the potential for multiple correct answers in these tasks, and what are the implications of this for the evaluation metrics used?
