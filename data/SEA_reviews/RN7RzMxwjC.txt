 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Harmony World Models (HarmonyWM), a method aimed at enhancing sample efficiency in model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) by balancing observation and reward modeling. It argues that existing methods either overemphasize observation modeling or rely solely on reward modeling, leading to suboptimal results. HarmonyWM addresses this by dynamically adjusting the weighting of these losses to maintain a balance, thereby improving performance. The paper is supported by empirical results across various domains, showing that HarmonyWM outperforms existing methods in some cases. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned due to similarities with existing methods, and the experimental results are seen as incremental rather than groundbreaking.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a good balance between technical details and intuitive explanations.
- The authors provide a comprehensive overview of the literature and clearly articulate the motivation behind their work, highlighting the importance of balancing observation and reward modeling in model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL).
- The experimental results are convincing, showing that HarmonyWM outperforms existing methods in some domains, particularly in visual control tasks.
- The paper introduces a novel approach to balancing observation and reward modeling in MBRL, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the importance of balancing these modeling tasks, which is a novel contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as similar methods have been proposed in the past, such as using the same scale for different losses.
- The paper's experimental results are seen as incremental, with marginal improvements over existing methods, and the performance of HarmonyWM is not consistently superior across all domains.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its strengths and weaknesses.
- There is a lack of comparative analysis with other methods that aim to balance observation and reward modeling, such as those mentioned in related works.
- The paper does not adequately address how the method performs in more complex or varied environments, which could affect its generalizability.
- The paper's claims about the novelty of the method are overstated, as similar approaches have been previously used in other contexts.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and how it might perform in more complex or varied environments?
- How does the performance of HarmonyWM compare to other methods that aim to balance observation and reward modeling, particularly those mentioned in related works?
- Could the authors elaborate on the specific advantages of the HarmonyWM approach over other methods, particularly in terms of its generalizability and scalability?
- Is there a possibility of incorporating a more dynamic weighting system that adapts to the specific needs of different tasks or environments?
- How does the method perform when applied to more complex or varied environments, and what are the implications of these findings for the broader applicability of the method?
