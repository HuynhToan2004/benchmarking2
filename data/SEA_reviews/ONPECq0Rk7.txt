 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method of pre-training language models by replacing the traditional cross-entropy objective with a contrastive objective, specifically Contrastive Weight Tying (CWT). This approach, termed "headless language modeling," aims to improve training efficiency and computational requirements, reducing the need for predicting probability distributions over token vocabularies. The methodology is evaluated across various tasks, including monolingual and multilingual settings, and demonstrates significant improvements in efficiency and downstream performance. However, the paper is critiqued for its lack of comprehensive empirical evaluation, particularly in terms of the scope of experiments and the depth of analysis, which limits the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and interesting approach to pre-training language models by replacing the cross-entropy objective with a contrastive objective, which is a significant shift in methodology.
- The experiments conducted are comprehensive, covering various tasks and settings, including monolingual and multilingual models, and demonstrating substantial improvements in training efficiency and computational requirements.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making the complex concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The methodology shows promise in reducing training costs and improving data efficiency, which is crucial for the scalability and accessibility of language model pre-training.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of how the proposed method compares in terms of training efficiency and computational requirements.
- The experiments are limited in scope, particularly in the size and diversity of the models and datasets used, which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
- There is a notable absence of error bars in the reported results, which could undermine the reliability of the claimed improvements.
- The paper does not provide sufficient analysis on the impact of vocabulary size and batch size on the model's performance, which are critical factors in language model pre-training.
- The paper does not include a comprehensive analysis of the model's performance on different types of tasks, such as language generation, which could provide a more complete picture of the model's capabilities.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of training efficiency and computational requirements?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger models, such as those used in the pre-training of models like BERT?
- Could the authors clarify the discrepancy in the reported results, particularly the significant differences in performance between the proposed method and the baseline models?
- How does the model perform on different types of tasks, such as language generation, and what are the implications of the observed differences in performance?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the impact of vocabulary size and batch size on the model's performance, and how does this compare to the performance of models trained with traditional methods?
