 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of persistent homology (PH) in high-dimensional spaces, specifically focusing on the robustness of PH against noise and the use of spectral distances for topological feature detection. The authors demonstrate that traditional Euclidean distances fail in high-dimensional spaces, and propose using spectral distances such as effective resistance and diffusion distance to enhance PH's ability to detect topological features. The paper includes a theoretical analysis and experimental results on synthetic and real-world datasets, showing that these spectral distances can effectively detect topological features even in noisy conditions. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of novelty, as the use of spectral distances in PH is not new, and the theoretical contributions are not sufficiently robust or generalizable.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a focus on a significant and timely problem: the application of persistent homology (PH) in high-dimensional spaces.
- The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the limitations of traditional Euclidean distances in high-dimensional spaces and propose using spectral distances to address these limitations.
- The paper includes a theoretical analysis of the effective resistance and its relation to diffusion distances, which is a novel contribution.
- Experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed spectral distances in detecting topological features in high-dimensional data.
- The application of PH to real-world datasets, such as single-cell RNA-sequencing, provides practical insights into the utility of PH in complex biological systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as the use of spectral distances in PH is not new, and the theoretical contributions do not significantly advance the field.
- The experimental setup and results are limited to specific datasets and do not adequately compare the proposed methods with state-of-the-art approaches.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which is crucial for understanding their practical applicability.
- The theoretical analysis is not robust or generalizable, particularly in high-dimensional spaces, and the assumptions made in the analysis may not hold in practical scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed methods, such as the computational complexity and the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis.
- There is a lack of discussion on the practical implications of using PH in high-dimensional spaces, particularly in terms of the choice of parameters and the robustness of the results to noise.

**Questions:**

- How do the authors justify the choice of parameters in their experimental setup, particularly in relation to the noise levels in the data?
- Could the authors provide more detailed theoretical analysis or proofs to support their claims regarding the effectiveness of the proposed spectral distances in high-dimensional spaces?
- What are the main limitations of the proposed methods, and how do these limitations impact the practical applicability of PH in high-dimensional spaces?
- How do the authors address the computational complexity of their methods, particularly in relation to the size and complexity of the datasets used?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical utility of PH in real-world applications, such as single-cell RNA-sequencing?
