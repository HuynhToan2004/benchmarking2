 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for variational inference in state-space models (SSMs) by employing a backward factorization of the variational approximation, which allows for the derivation of a new Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO). This approach is used to develop two new algorithms: dBFS, which is a decoupled second-order algorithm, and aBFS, an auto-encoding backward factorized smoother, which leverages deep learning tools. The paper also proposes a Lagrange multiplier argument to optimize the ELBO and develops an amortized version of the algorithm. Theoretical insights are supported by numerical experiments, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods. However, the paper is criticized for its presentation clarity, with some sections being difficult to follow, and the novelty of the approach is questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to variational inference in state-space models (SSMs) using a backward factorization of the variational approximation, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors propose a new Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) and develop two new algorithms, dBFS and aBFS, which are supported by theoretical insights and numerical experiments.
- The paper is well-written, with clear mathematical formulations and detailed derivations, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed algorithms, particularly aBFS, are noted for their ability to use modern deep learning tools, enhancing their applicability and potential impact.
- The paper provides a comprehensive discussion on the theoretical aspects of the algorithms, including the ELBO and the use of natural gradients, which are crucial for understanding the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper suffers from poor writing and lacks clarity in some sections, making it difficult for readers to follow the arguments and derivations.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the novelty of the proposed methods compared to existing approaches, particularly in the context of variational inference and smoothing algorithms.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by better organizing the content and providing clearer explanations for the mathematical formulations and derivations.
- Some sections of the paper, such as the discussion on the ELBO and the relationship between different algorithms, are overly complex and could benefit from simplification or reorganization.
- There is a need for more detailed experimental results and comparisons with existing methods to fully demonstrate the effectiveness and advantages of the proposed algorithms.
