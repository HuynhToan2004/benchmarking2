 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel concept of relative fairness in decision-making, focusing on the comparison of biases between different human decision-makers rather than absolute fairness. The authors propose two algorithms, Biased Bridge and Unbiased Bridge, to assess the relative fairness of decisions made on different datasets. These algorithms aim to identify biases by comparing the predictions of a trained model against the decisions of human raters. The paper presents a theoretical framework and empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of these methods, though it is critiqued for its clarity and depth in discussing the practical implications and limitations of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel concept of relative fairness, which is distinct from traditional notions of fairness and could provide a useful framework for evaluating decision-making processes in various contexts.
- The authors provide a clear theoretical framework and empirical evidence to support their claims, which is helpful in understanding the proposed methods and their practical applications.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The problem of testing fairness in human decisions is relevant and timely, with potential to impact real-world applications in areas such as hiring and loan approvals.
- The use of machine learning models to evaluate relative fairness is a novel approach that could be valuable for practitioners and researchers in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the definition and explanation of key terms and concepts, which could make it difficult for readers to fully understand the proposed methods and their implications.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach, particularly the assumption that the decision-makers are i.i.d. and the potential for biases to be introduced through the use of a single model for all comparisons.
- The empirical evidence provided is limited, with only two datasets used and no discussion on the generalizability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the proposed methods, such as how they might be applied in different contexts and the potential ethical considerations involved.
- The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing literature on fairness in human decision-making, which could lead to confusion about the novelty and contribution of the work.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and role of "absolute fairness" in the context of their work, and how it relates to the proposed concept of relative fairness?
- How does the paper address the potential for biases to be introduced through the use of a single model for all comparisons?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications of their proposed methods in real-world scenarios?
- How does the paper ensure that the relative fairness metrics are not misused or misinterpreted to justify biased decision-making?
- Could the authors discuss the potential ethical implications of their work and how these might be addressed in future research or applications?
- How generalizable are the findings of this study, and what steps were taken to ensure the validity and reliability of the results?
