 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HashOrder, a novel probabilistic graph reordering algorithm that leverages randomized hashing to optimize cache efficiency in graph processing. By utilizing a hashing-based approach, HashOrder aims to improve the locality of graph memory layouts, thereby enhancing cache utilization and reducing memory access overhead. Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate that HashOrder outperforms existing methods in terms of speed and efficiency, showing significant improvements in graph processing workloads and GNN data loaders. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and provides a comprehensive evaluation of HashOrder against several baseline methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed algorithm, HashOrder, is novel and innovative, leveraging hashing techniques to improve graph reordering.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, and the empirical results are convincing, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods.
- HashOrder is parallelizable, which is a significant advantage for handling large-scale graph processing tasks.
- The paper provides a clear definition of the problem and proposes a novel solution using LSH, which is well-justified and supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and show that HashOrder outperforms other baselines, including in terms of cache efficiency and parallelizability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods in terms of computational overhead and the time taken to perform graph reordering.
- The scalability of HashOrder is not thoroughly discussed, especially in terms of how it performs on larger graphs.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications for the choices made in the algorithm design, particularly in the use of specific hash functions and the impact of multi-hop neighborhoods.
- There are some inconsistencies and unclear definitions in the text, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect the reader's understanding of its applicability and effectiveness.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, especially in the context of LSH and its application to graph processing.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational overhead and time taken for graph reordering with HashOrder compared to other methods?
- How does HashOrder perform on larger graphs, and what are the scalability limitations?
- Can the authors clarify the choice of hash functions and the impact of multi-hop neighborhoods on the performance of HashOrder?
- How does HashOrder compare to other LSH-based methods for graph reordering, and what are the specific advantages of using HashOrder?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of their proposed method and discuss potential scenarios where HashOrder might not be the most effective solution?
- How does the paper's definition of fitness (fit(P)) compare to other definitions used in graph reordering literature, and what are the implications of using different definitions?
