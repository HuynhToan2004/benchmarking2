 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the generalization problem in neural networks (NNs) with spurious correlations, focusing on the disparity in complexity between spurious and core features. It employs the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) to analyze the spectral properties of NNs, revealing that spurious correlations can lead to over-reliance on simplistic features, which negatively affects generalization. The authors propose a method to adjust the spectral properties of NNs to mitigate this bias, without requiring knowledge of the spurious attributes. The paper provides both theoretical insights and empirical evidence to support its claims, using datasets like MNIST and CIFAR to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue in machine learning, focusing on the generalization problem in neural networks (NNs) with spurious correlations.
- The authors provide both theoretical insights and empirical evidence to support their claims, which enhances the credibility of their findings.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for a broad audience.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, as demonstrated by the results presented, showing that it can improve the performance of NNs in certain scenarios.
- The paper is well-motivated and addresses a significant problem in machine learning, which is relevant and of interest to the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is limited as it primarily applies existing ideas from spectral bias to the context of spurious correlations, which might not be seen as a significant advancement.
- The paper lacks comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, which could have strengthened its claims and provided a clearer context for its contributions.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could limit its practical applicability.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are somewhat limited, and the empirical results do not convincingly support the claims made.
- The paper's focus on specific datasets (e.g., MNIST and CIFAR) might limit its generalizability to more complex or varied datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially how it scales with the size of the dataset?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the hyperparameters $\gamma$ and $\beta$ in the proposed method, and discuss their impact on the performance of the model?
- How do the authors address the issue of overfitting, especially in the context of the spectral bias adjustment?
- Could the authors provide more detailed theoretical insights into the spectral bias adjustment and its impact on the model's performance?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing methods, and how does it address the limitations of those methods?
