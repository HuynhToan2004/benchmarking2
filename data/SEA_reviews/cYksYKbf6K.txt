 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Conservative Rollout Length Adaptation (CRLA) aimed at improving sample efficiency in model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) by dynamically adjusting the rollout length. CRLA predicts the distribution of frequently taken actions in the current state and truncates the rollout when the output of the actor and the conservator diverge significantly, preventing the agent from selecting infrequently taken actions. The method is evaluated on the Atari 100k benchmark, demonstrating performance improvements over DreamerV3 in most Atari games. The paper also includes a theoretical analysis and empirical results, showing that CRLA can effectively balance data quality and efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear illustrations that aid in understanding the proposed method.
- The proposed method, CRLA, is novel and offers a practical approach to adaptively adjusting rollout lengths, which is crucial for balancing data quality and efficiency in model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL).
- The method is evaluated on the Atari 100k benchmark, showing significant performance improvements over the default setting of DreamerV3.
- The theoretical analysis provided supports the proposed method, and the empirical results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of CRLA in most Atari games.
- The paper includes a thorough discussion of the limitations of the method, which is appreciated for its transparency.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method is primarily evaluated in the Atari environment, which may not fully demonstrate its effectiveness in more complex or diverse settings.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the method, particularly concerning the training of the conservator network.
- The choice of hyperparameters, such as the threshold α and the rollout length range, is critical but not sufficiently justified or analyzed.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other rollout length adaptation methods, which could help in understanding the novelty and effectiveness of CRLA.
- There are some typographical and formatting errors that need correction for clarity and professionalism.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of CRLA with other rollout length adaptation methods to highlight its unique advantages?
- How does the computational complexity of training the conservator network compare to other components of the model?
- How sensitive is the performance of CRLA to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly α and the rollout length range?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential negative societal impacts of their work?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to continuous action spaces, and if so, what are the challenges and potential solutions?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the conservator network is trained and its impact on the overall model performance?
