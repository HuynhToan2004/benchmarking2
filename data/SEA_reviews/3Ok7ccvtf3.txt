 **Summary:**
The paper discusses a novel approach to unlearning in Matrix Factorization-based recommender systems, focusing on a method that dilutes the impact of unwanted data by introducing a rescue model. This model, trained using corrected data, is then used to refine the latent features of the original model, aiming to maintain personalized recommendations while minimizing the impact of unwanted data. The paper proposes a Convolution Fusion Function (CFF) to fuse the latent features of the rescue model with those of the original model, enhancing the model's ability to adapt to new, unwanted data. The approach is evaluated on two datasets, showing some improvements over baseline methods, but concerns are raised about the method's practicality, theoretical grounding, and the clarity of its presentation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to unlearning in matrix factorization-based recommender systems, focusing on diluting the impact of unwanted data rather than training from scratch.
- The proposed method is technically sound, with a clear and intuitive algorithm that is easy to understand.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined problem formulation.
- The paper is significant as it addresses the challenge of unlearning in recommender systems, which is a growing area of interest due to data protection laws and user privacy concerns.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the theoretical foundation of the proposed method, particularly the use of the convolution fusion function (CFF) and its contribution to the model's performance.
- The experimental setup is limited, with only two datasets used, and the results are not convincing, especially when compared to retraining-based methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in large-scale recommender systems.
- There are several grammatical and typographical errors that need correction, which could affect the paper's professionalism and readability.
- The paper's contribution to the field is not clearly articulated, and it lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in the context of recommender systems.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical underpinnings of the CFF and its contribution to the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method perform in large-scale recommender systems, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in such environments?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and the methodology used for training and evaluation?
- How does the proposed method compare to other unlearning methods in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness?
- Can the authors provide a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in the context of recommender systems, to better establish the novelty and effectiveness of their approach?
