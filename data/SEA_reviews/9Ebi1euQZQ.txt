 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the object existence hallucination issue in large vision-language models (LVLMs) through a novel evaluation method, CCEval, which is assisted by GPT-4. The method assesses the performance of LVLMs in generating captions that accurately reflect the objects present in images, distinguishing between hallucinated and real objects. The authors also propose a method to control hallucinations by introducing a parameter that can be adjusted to influence the model's output. The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the factors influencing hallucination, including image resolution, language decoder size, and instruction data quality. The proposed method, Halle-Switch, aims to mitigate hallucinations by controlling the extent of parametric knowledge in the model's outputs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel evaluation method, CCEval, which is GPT-4 assisted and provides a more comprehensive evaluation of object existence hallucination in detailed captions compared to existing methods.
- The authors conduct a thorough analysis of factors influencing hallucination, such as image resolution, language decoder size, and instruction data quality, which is valuable for understanding the root causes of hallucination in LVLMs.
- The proposed method, Halle-Switch, is innovative and effective in controlling hallucinations by conditioning the model to shift between depicting contextual knowledge and parametric knowledge.
- The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the motivation and challenges of the research, and provides a detailed analysis of the impact of various factors on hallucination.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in reducing hallucination, as shown by a significant reduction in hallucination scores.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on object existence hallucination and does not address other types of hallucinations, such as object attribute or relationship hallucinations, which could limit the comprehensiveness of the evaluation.
- The evaluation method relies on GPT-4, which might not be accessible to all researchers, making it difficult to reproduce the results without this resource.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the proposed method could be adapted for other types of hallucinations, such as object attribute or relationship hallucinations.
- The method's reliance on GPT-4 for evaluation could introduce bias, as the model might have been trained on similar data, which could affect the accuracy of the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential impact of the proposed method on the performance of downstream tasks that require detailed and accurate captions.
- The method's effectiveness is not compared to other existing methods, such as those that use different evaluation metrics or methodologies, which could provide a more robust validation of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the proposed method could be adapted to address other types of hallucinations, such as object attribute or relationship hallucinations?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of effectiveness and efficiency in reducing hallucination in LVLMs?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of the proposed method on the performance of downstream tasks that require detailed and accurate captions?
- Is there a possibility to make the evaluation method more accessible and less reliant on GPT-4, to make the results more reproducible?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where the model is required to generate detailed and accurate captions, but the ground truth is not available or is incomplete?
