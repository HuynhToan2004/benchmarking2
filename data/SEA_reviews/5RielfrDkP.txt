 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel Multiresolution Meta-Framelet-based Graph Convolutional Network (MM-FGCN) for adaptive multiresolution representation learning across diverse graph instances. This method employs a Multiresolution Meta-Framelet System (MMFS) to generate learnable multiresolution bases, enabling the network to adaptively capture graph features at different resolutions. The MM-FGCN is evaluated on various graph learning tasks, demonstrating superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods. The methodology involves a meta-framelet generator that learns adaptive multiresolution bases for each graph instance, which is then integrated into the graph convolution process. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its clarity, particularly in the presentation of key concepts and the organization of the content.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is novel, integrating multi-resolution graph representation learning with meta-learning, which is a new direction in graph neural network research.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experimental results that demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method.
- The methodology is clearly presented, and the paper includes detailed explanations of the proposed method and its experimental results.
- The idea of learning adaptive multiresolution bases for each graph instance is innovative and addresses the challenges of fixed multiresolution analysis strategies.
- The paper includes a detailed theoretical explanation of the proposed method, which aids in understanding its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from better organization and clarity, particularly in the introduction and related work sections.
- The mathematical notation and definitions are sometimes unclear or inconsistent, making it difficult to follow the methodology and results.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from existing literature, particularly in the area of graph neural networks and meta-learning.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to state-of-the-art methods, and the paper could benefit from more extensive comparisons and ablation studies.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the definition and role of the meta-framelet generator in the context of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle the balance between low-pass and high-pass filters in the graph convolution process?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training process, particularly how the meta-framelet generator is trained and how it affects the overall performance of the model?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of graph instance heterogeneity, and what are the implications for its generalizability across different types of graphs?
- Could the authors elaborate on the computational efficiency and memory usage of the proposed method, especially in comparison to other state-of-the-art methods?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of scalability, and what are the potential limitations in handling larger or more complex graphs?
