 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to generalizable NeRF by utilizing point-based representations, which is a departure from traditional image-based methods. This methodology involves the explicit modeling of visibilities using geometric priors and augmenting these with neural features. The authors propose a nonuniform log sampling strategy to enhance rendering speed and quality, and a learnable kernel spatially augmented with features for feature aggregations. The paper presents a three-stage finetuning scheme aimed at improving the generalizability of the model. The method is tested against several benchmarks and reportedly outperforms existing methods in terms of reconstruction quality and rendering speed. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues in its writing and lacks a detailed comparison with other point-based methods, which could undermine its claims of superiority.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel paradigm for generalizable NeRF based on point-based neural rendering, which is a departure from traditional image-based methods.
- The methodology includes a visibility-oriented feature fetching mechanism that explicitly models visibilities by geometric priors and augments them with neural features.
- A robust log sampling strategy based on point density prior and perturbations to sampling parameters is proposed to improve the reconstructed geometry and accelerate rendering speed.
- A spatially feature-augmented learnable kernel is presented as a feature aggregator, which is effective for generalizability and geometry reconstruction at shape-varying areas.
- The paper demonstrates significant improvements in rendering speed and quality compared to image-based methods, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and their implementation details, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear and concise presentation of the main ideas and methodologies, which facilitates understanding and replication of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other point-based methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The clarity of the paper needs improvement, particularly in the descriptions of the methodologies and the results. More visual aids and detailed explanations are needed to clarify the main contributions.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of initialization, which is crucial for the success of the proposed method. The reliance on PatchmatchMVS for initialization could limit the generalizability of the method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and inference times, which are crucial metrics for evaluating the practicality and efficiency of the proposed method.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the potential societal impacts of the proposed method, which is an essential aspect of responsible AI development.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison with other point-based methods to establish the superiority of the proposed method more convincingly?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of initialization, especially in scenarios where PatchmatchMVS may not be available or effective?
- What are the training and inference times for the proposed method, and how do these compare with other methods in terms of efficiency?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of occlusions, especially in scenarios where multiple objects or complex scenes are involved?
- Could the authors provide more details on the limitations of the proposed method and how these might affect its practical application?
- Is there a plan to release the code for the proposed method to facilitate replication and further research by the community?
