 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the concept of orbit-equivariance in graph neural networks (GNNs), which allows GNNs to differentiate between nodes in the same orbit, extending beyond the limitations of traditional equivariant functions. This relaxation enables GNNs to handle symmetry-breaking problems, such as those encountered in drug design, where similar nodes require different outputs. The paper presents a new dataset and four GNN architectures that demonstrate orbit-equivariance, along with a specialized loss function for training. Theoretical analysis and empirical evaluations on new datasets are provided, although the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation and the need for a broader range of datasets and comparisons.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces the concept of orbit-equivariance, a novel property of graph neural networks (GNNs) that allows for different outputs for similar nodes within the same orbit, which is a significant extension of traditional equivariant functions.
- It provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of orbit-equivariance, including its formal definition, positioning within the hierarchy of node-labeling functions, and the properties of orbit-equivariant functions.
- The paper includes empirical evaluations on two new datasets, demonstrating the practical application of orbit-equivariance in real-world scenarios.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the concepts and a detailed evaluation section that includes multiple GNN architectures and datasets.
- The introduction of a new dataset specifically designed for evaluating orbit-equivariance adds a valuable resource for further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a broader range of datasets and tasks to more comprehensively demonstrate the utility and applicability of orbit-equivariance.
- The novelty of the proposed orbit-equivariance property is questioned, as it seems to be a natural extension of existing equivariant functions.
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation from existing methods, such as those using unique IDs or random noise, and does not provide sufficient empirical evidence to support the claims made.
- The evaluation section is criticized for being limited to a few datasets and not including comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed models.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed models.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the new datasets were constructed and the criteria used for their selection?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods, such as those using unique IDs or random noise, in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Can the authors clarify the differences between orbit-equivariance and other related concepts, such as node embeddings and graph equivariance?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting, especially in scenarios where the model might be trained on a limited dataset?
- Could the authors provide more empirical evidence to support the claims made about the effectiveness of the orbit-sorting cross-entropy loss?
- In light of the criticisms regarding the novelty and the scope of the evaluation, how does the paper address these concerns and what steps could be taken to enhance the robustness of the evaluation in future work?
