 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the robustness of latent diffusion models (LDMs) by analyzing their white-box and black-box vulnerabilities to adversarial attacks. It introduces a dataset for evaluating LDMs' robustness and proposes a methodology for evaluating adversarial robustness across different modules. The study identifies the denoising process, particularly the Resnet module, as the most vulnerable component. It also explores the transferability of adversarial examples across different models and prompts, highlighting the vulnerability of newer models like SD-v2 compared to older versions. The paper also discusses potential defense mechanisms against these attacks, including image transformations and compression techniques.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear presentation of the problem statement and methodology.
- The introduction of a dataset for evaluating the robustness of LDMs is a significant contribution to the field, providing a valuable resource for future research.
- The paper conducts a comprehensive evaluation of the robustness of LDMs, including both white-box and black-box settings, and provides insights into the vulnerabilities of different components of LDMs.
- The study on the transferability of adversarial examples across different models and prompts is novel and adds to the understanding of the robustness of LDMs.
- The proposed dataset construction pipeline is detailed and clear, which is beneficial for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly the potential negative societal impacts and ethical considerations.
- The analysis of the robustness of LDMs is limited to a specific dataset (COCO), which may not generalize to other datasets.
- The evaluation of adversarial robustness is based on metrics such as CLIP score, PSNR, SSIM, and FID, which may not adequately reflect the human perception of image quality or the functionality of image editing.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the construction of the adversarial examples, particularly how they are generated and the specifics of the attack algorithms used.
- The experimental results and their interpretations are not consistently supported by visual examples, which could enhance the understanding of the results.
- The paper does not discuss potential defense mechanisms against the identified vulnerabilities, which could limit the practical applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you elaborate on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its generalizability to other datasets and the potential negative societal impacts?
- How do the adversarial examples affect the functionality of image editing, and could you provide visual examples to support the claims made in the paper?
- Could you provide more details on the construction of the adversarial examples, including the specific attack algorithms used and the parameters involved?
- How do the defense mechanisms proposed in the paper perform against the identified vulnerabilities, and could you provide experimental results to support the effectiveness of these defenses?
- The paper mentions the transferability of adversarial examples across different models and prompts. Could you discuss the implications of this transferability and whether it could be mitigated by training on more diverse datasets?
