 **Summary:**
The paper introduces GRepsNets, a novel equivariant architecture for arbitrary matrix groups, leveraging tensor representations in hidden layers and a mixing mechanism to achieve equivariance. This architecture, which includes T_i layers, is claimed to be efficient and scalable, and is evaluated across various tasks such as image classification, N-body dynamics prediction, and PDE solving. The authors demonstrate that GRepsNets can perform competitively with existing methods like EMLP and MLP-Mixer, showing potential for broader application in the field of group equivariant learning. However, the paper's experimental results and the clarity of its contributions are questioned, with concerns about the novelty and the depth of the theoretical underpinnings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and simple equivariant architecture, GRepsNet, which is claimed to be efficient and scalable, making it suitable for a wide range of applications.
- The GRepsNet architecture is shown to be effective across various tasks such as image classification, N-body dynamics prediction, and PDE solving, demonstrating its versatility and applicability.
- The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation of GRepsNet, comparing it to existing methods like EMLP and MLP-Mixer, and showing that it can achieve competitive performance.
- The authors have successfully integrated tensor representations into the hidden layers of a neural network, which is a significant advancement in the field of equivariant learning.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed experimental section that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear definition and explanation of the T_i layers, which are crucial to understanding the functionality and effectiveness of the GRepsNet architecture.
- There is a lack of novelty in the proposed method, as it appears to be a combination of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical analysis or proof of the equivariance of the GRepsNet architecture, which is a critical aspect of the research.
- The experimental results are not convincing, with some results showing only marginal improvements over existing methods, and the absence of results for certain datasets such as CIFAR100 and ImageNet raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the computational complexity and training time of the GRepsNet architecture, which are important factors for practical deployment.
- The paper does not adequately compare the GRepsNet architecture with other existing methods, such as those based on tensor dual and Kronecker sum, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a clearer definition and explanation of the T_i layers, including their input and output types, and how they contribute to the overall functionality of the GRepsNet architecture?
- How does the GRepsNet architecture ensure equivariance, especially in the context of the T_i layers, and can this be mathematically proven or theoretically justified?
- What are the specific advantages of using GRepsNet over existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including the training time and computational complexity of the GRepsNet architecture, and how these compare to other methods in practical scenarios?
- How does the GRepsNet architecture handle the computational complexity associated with higher-order tensor representations, and what strategies are used to manage this complexity in practical applications?
- Can the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the T_0 layer in the GRepsNet architecture, and how it contributes to the overall functionality of the model?
