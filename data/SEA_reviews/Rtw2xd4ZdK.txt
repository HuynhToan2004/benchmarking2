 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LeCo-NeRF, a novel method for estimating occupancy in large-scale NeRF scenes using a compact MLP-based occupancy representation. This method employs a heterogeneous mixture of experts (HMoE) structure, including a tiny empty-space expert and a common scene expert, to manage the imbalanced distribution of occupied and unoccupied regions. The HMoE is trained using an imbalanced gate loss and a density loss to guide the gating network, achieving significant speed-up and competitive performance compared to Switch-NeRF. The method is evaluated on large-scale datasets like Mega-NeRF and Block-NeRF, demonstrating improvements in efficiency and accuracy. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the approach, the clarity of presentation, and the lack of comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed method.
- The use of a compact MLP-based occupancy representation is innovative and effective in modeling large-scale scenes.
- The proposed HMoE structure, imbalanced gate loss, and density loss are novel and contribute to the field of NeRF modeling.
- The method is shown to be effective in large-scale scenes, with significant improvements in speed and performance over Switch-NeRF.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the methodology and includes thorough experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is limited as similar ideas have been previously explored in other Mixture of Experts (MoE) works.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with existing methods like Switch-NeRF and other relevant works, making it difficult to assess the true improvements and innovations of the proposed method.
- The presentation of the paper is unclear in some sections, particularly in the description of the HMoE structure and the loss functions.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of memory usage, which is a significant concern in large-scale NeRF modeling.
- The experimental setup and results lack depth, particularly in terms of the impact of different hyperparameters and the generalizability of the method across different datasets and scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential future research directions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with Switch-NeRF and other relevant works to highlight the true improvements and innovations of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in scenarios with varying levels of scene occupancy, and what are the implications for the accuracy and efficiency of the model?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over other occupancy estimation methods, especially in terms of memory usage and computational efficiency?
- How does the method handle the issue of over-smoothing, particularly in scenes with complex geometry and appearance distributions?
- Can the authors clarify the operation of the HMoE structure and the role of the occupancy network in the gating process?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of large-scale scenes, such as indoor and outdoor environments, and what are the implications for the model's performance and generalizability?
