 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework, Data-dependent Randomized Response Majority (DaRRM), for computing a private majority function from a set of K (ε, δ)-differentially private mechanisms. The framework optimizes the probability of outputting the majority vote based on the number of votes that agree with the majority, thereby improving privacy-utility trade-offs. The DaRRM framework is designed to optimize the noise function γ, which is data-dependent, and is shown to achieve a 2x improvement in utility over simple subsampling in the case of i.i.d. mechanisms and no failure probability. The paper also proposes a method for optimizing γ using linear programming, which reduces the number of constraints from infinitely many to a polynomial set. The framework is tested on real-world applications, showing improvements over existing methods like subsampling and randomized response.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a novel approach to optimizing the noise function γ for better privacy-utility trade-offs.
- The proposed framework, DaRRM, is a general privacy framework that can be applied to any private majority algorithm, offering a flexible and adaptable solution.
- The paper provides a clear motivation and a strong theoretical foundation, supported by rigorous proofs and a well-defined problem statement.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods like subsampling and randomized response.
- The paper includes a detailed discussion on the limitations and practical implications of the proposed method, which is appreciated for its transparency and realism.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of privacy-utility trade-offs and the generalizability of the proposed method across different types of datasets and applications.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets and do not include a broader range of datasets to validate the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is a significant limitation for practical implementation.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the implications of the results, particularly in terms of their broader impact and potential societal implications.
