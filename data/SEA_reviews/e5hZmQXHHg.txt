 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a variance-reduced adaptive algorithm (VRAda) designed for stochastic parameter-agnostic minimax optimization. VRAda aims to address the challenges of varying inherent variances in stochastic settings by adapting learning rates without relying on problem-dependent parameters. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that VRAda achieves an optimal sample complexity of O(1/\u03f5\u00b3) without large data batches, and experimental validations across diverse applications confirm its effectiveness. The algorithm is highlighted for its ability to automatically adjust learning rates and achieve convergence to an epsilon-stationary point, making it a significant contribution to the field of stochastic minimax optimization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel variance-reduced adaptive algorithm (VRAda) for stochastic parameter-agnostic minimax optimization, which effectively addresses the challenge of varying inherent variances in stochastic settings.
- The algorithm is designed to automatically adjust learning rates without relying on problem-dependent parameters, which is a significant advancement over existing methods.
- The theoretical analysis demonstrates that VRAda achieves an optimal sample complexity of O(1/\u03f5\u00b3) without large data batches, which is a notable improvement over previous methods.
- The paper provides extensive experimental validations across diverse applications, confirming the effectiveness and robustness of VRAda.
- VRAda is the first parameter-agnostic algorithm designed specifically for stochastic minimax optimizations, making it a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed algorithm, particularly in scenarios where it might not perform optimally.
- There is a lack of detailed comparison with existing variance-reduced adaptive algorithms, which could have provided a clearer picture of VRAda's advantages and disadvantages.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with the convergence curves of VRAda and TiAda being very close, which raises questions about the practical significance of VRAda's performance improvements.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of VRAda, particularly in terms of the computational overhead introduced by variance reduction techniques.
- The proofs in the paper are not thoroughly checked, with potential issues in the proofs of Lemma 1 and Lemma 2.

**Questions:**

- How does VRAda compare with existing variance-reduced adaptive algorithms in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can you provide more insights into the computational complexity of VRAda, particularly the overhead introduced by variance reduction techniques?
