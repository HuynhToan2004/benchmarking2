 **Summary:**
The paper introduces AutoLoRa, a novel approach to robust fine-tuning (RFT) that addresses the divergence in gradient directions between adversarial and natural objectives during RFT. This divergence is a significant issue that hampers the optimization process and hinders the achievement of adversarial robustness. AutoLoRa employs a low-rank branch to disentangle RFT into two distinct components, optimizing natural objectives via the LoRa branch and adversarial objectives via the feature extractor. The paper also introduces heuristic strategies for automating the scheduling of learning rates and loss terms. Extensive empirical evaluations demonstrate that AutoLoRa achieves new state-of-the-art results across a range of downstream tasks, showing significant practical utility in converting a pre-trained feature extractor into an adversarially robust model for downstream tasks without the need for hyperparameter tuning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and important issue in robust fine-tuning (RFT) by focusing on the divergence in gradient directions between adversarial and natural objectives, which is a crucial aspect of RFT that has been overlooked in previous studies.
- The proposed AutoLoRa framework is innovative and practical, offering a new approach to RFT that disentangles the optimization of natural and adversarial objectives, thereby improving the robustness of models in downstream tasks.
- The empirical evaluations provided by the authors are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of AutoLoRa in achieving new state-of-the-art results across a range of downstream tasks.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed heuristic strategies for automating the scheduling of learning rates and loss terms are simple yet effective, enhancing the practical utility of the AutoLoRa framework.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other robust fine-tuning methods, particularly those that also address the issue of divergent gradient directions. This limits the ability to assess the relative performance and novelty of AutoLoRa.
- The paper does not provide sufficient theoretical analysis or intuitive insights into why the divergence in gradient directions occurs and how AutoLoRa addresses this issue.
- The empirical evaluations are limited to specific datasets and do not include larger, more diverse datasets such as ImageNet, which could provide a more robust validation of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not discuss the potential impact of the LoRa branch on the inference latency, which is a critical factor in practical applications.
- There are some minor issues with the clarity and organization of the paper, particularly in the presentation of equations and the structure of certain sections.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the computational efficiency of AutoLoRa, particularly in terms of training time and memory usage, compared to other robust fine-tuning methods?
- How does AutoLoRa perform on larger, more diverse datasets such as ImageNet, and what are the potential implications for its performance and generalizability?
- Could you elaborate on the specific reasons why the divergence in gradient directions occurs during RFT and how AutoLoRa addresses this issue?
- How sensitive is AutoLoRa to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly those related to the scheduling of learning rates and loss terms?
- In the context of AutoLoRa's performance on different datasets, how does the rank of the LoRa branch affect its effectiveness, and what are the implications for the inference latency?
- Could you provide more details on the implementation of the LoRa branch, particularly how it is trained and how it affects the overall performance of the model?
