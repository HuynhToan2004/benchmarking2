 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for removing specific concepts from deep neural network representations, focusing on adversarial linear classifiers trained on a concept dataset. This approach, termed Deep Concept Removal, utilizes adversarial probing classifiers across various network layers to address concept entanglement and enhance out-of-distribution generalization. The methodology is evaluated on various datasets, including MNIST and CelebA, and demonstrates improved performance over existing methods. However, the paper's experimental scope is limited, focusing primarily on image datasets, and it does not sufficiently address the scalability and applicability to more complex or diverse datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of adversarial concept removal, focusing on removing specific concepts from deep neural network representations.
- The methodology is well-motivated, clearly explained, and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow.
- The paper introduces an implicit gradient-based technique for adversarial training using linear classifiers, which is a novel approach.
- The proposed method shows good performance on several benchmarks, including MNIST and CelebA, and is shown to be effective in removing concepts from neural network representations.
- The paper is structured around a clear problem statement and the methodology is well-motivated, which helps in understanding the approach and its application.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks sufficient experimental validation, particularly on larger and more complex datasets such as ImageNet, which limits the generalizability of the findings.
- The methodology is primarily evaluated on image datasets, and it is unclear how the method would perform on other types of datasets like text or tabular data.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially concerning the computational resources required for training on larger datasets.
- There is a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a better understanding of the method's relative performance.
- The paper's claims regarding the method's effectiveness in removing concepts are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, particularly in the case of the Waterbirds dataset.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, with some sections appearing rushed and lacking in detail, particularly in the experimental setup and results.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between the proposed method and existing methods, particularly in terms of how the concept is removed and the impact on the performance of the neural network?
- How does the proposed method compare to state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency, especially when applied to larger and more complex datasets?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational resources required for training on larger datasets and discuss potential strategies for improving scalability?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the experimental results, particularly the low accuracy on the Waterbirds dataset and the effectiveness of the method in removing concepts from neural network representations?
- How does the proposed method perform on other types of datasets, such as text or tabular data, and could the authors provide examples or additional experiments to demonstrate this?
- Could the authors address the concerns regarding the method's effectiveness in removing concepts and discuss potential improvements or modifications to the method to enhance its performance?
