 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an information-theoretic framework for evaluating text-based explanations, focusing on the mutual information between input, target, and explanations. The framework uses two metrics, relevance and informativeness, to assess the effectiveness of explanations in reducing uncertainty about the input and target. The authors employ ChatGPT to generate explanations and compare them with human-annotated explanations and those from other large language models. The study also explores the trade-off between relevance and informativeness in different types of explanations, such as rationale and natural language explanations (NLEs). The paper's primary contribution is the application of mutual information to evaluate the quality of explanations, although it has been critiqued for its limited evaluation scope and lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a logical structure that effectively communicates the main ideas.
- The application of mutual information to evaluate the quality of explanations is innovative and provides a novel approach to assessing the effectiveness of explanations.
- The paper presents interesting findings about the trade-off between relevance and informativeness in different types of explanations, such as rationale and NLEs.
- The use of ChatGPT to generate explanations is a significant contribution, as it demonstrates the potential of large language models (LLMs) in generating high-quality explanations.
- The study is comprehensive, covering a wide range of evaluations and providing a systematic approach to assessing the quality of explanations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the use of mutual information as a metric for evaluating explanations, and it does not sufficiently justify why this metric is superior to other existing methods.
- The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (e-SNLI) and does not include comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have provided a more robust validation of the proposed framework.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of hallucination in explanations, which is a critical aspect of explanation quality.
- The paper's approach to evaluating the quality of explanations is seen as overly simplistic, relying on automatic metrics which may not adequately capture the nuances of human judgment.
- The paper does not sufficiently explore the relationship between the proposed metrics and human judgment, which is crucial for validating the effectiveness of the proposed framework.
- The paper's conclusions are seen as limited by the scope of the evaluation, and the findings may not generalize well to other types of explanations or datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the motivation for using mutual information as a metric for evaluating explanations and how it addresses the specific challenges in this domain?
- How does the paper address the issue of hallucination in explanations, and what steps are taken to ensure that the generated explanations are faithful to the input data?
- Could the authors provide more details on how the relevance and informativeness scores are computed and how they relate to human judgment?
- How do the authors justify the use of a single dataset for evaluation, and what steps were taken to ensure the generalizability of the findings to other datasets and types of explanations?
- Could the authors elaborate on the relationship between the proposed metrics and human judgment, and how these metrics could be used to improve the quality of explanations in practical applications?
