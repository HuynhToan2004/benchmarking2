 **Summary:**
The paper introduces SpikeGCL, a novel graph contrastive learning framework that utilizes spiking neural networks (SNNs) to learn compact and efficient representations of graphs. The methodology involves converting full-precision node representations into binary spikes through a spiking neuron, which is then used to generate binary graph representations. This approach is supported by a blockwise training strategy to address the vanishing gradient problem in SNNs. The paper provides theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence that SpikeGCL is competitive with full-precision GCL methods, showing potential for significant reductions in model size, memory usage, and energy consumption. However, the paper has been criticized for its unclear presentation, insufficient empirical evidence, and lack of comparison with other quantization techniques.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to graph contrastive learning using spiking neural networks (SNNs), which is both innovative and timely.
- Theoretical guarantees are provided, which support the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methodology and experimental results.
- Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing significant reductions in model size, memory usage, and energy consumption compared to full-precision GCL methods.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the approximation properties of SpikeGCL with respect to its full-precision counterparts, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other quantization techniques such as bit-shift, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The presentation of the paper is unclear, with some sections and figures not being self-explanatory, which could hinder understanding.
- The empirical evidence provided in the paper is insufficient, with only a few datasets used for evaluation, which limits the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of handling larger graphs and more complex datasets.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for a complete understanding of its applicability and potential shortcomings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of training times, which is essential for assessing the practical efficiency of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the training times for SpikeGCL and other baselines to better understand the practical efficiency of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to larger and more complex datasets, such as those used in the Open Graph Benchmark?
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancies in the experimental results, particularly the accuracy reported in different tables and figures?
- How does the proposed method compare to other quantization techniques, such as bit-shift, in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential areas for future research?
