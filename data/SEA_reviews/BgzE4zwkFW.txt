 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Morphology-Environment Co-Evolution (MECE), a novel approach to optimize the morphology and policy of a reinforcement learning (RL) agent in a dynamic environment. MECE involves training two policies, one for modifying the agent's morphology and another for altering the environment, which creates a curriculum to enhance the adaptiveness and versatility of the agent. The method is evaluated against several baselines, demonstrating improved generalization and faster learning speeds. However, concerns are raised regarding the novelty and depth of the experimental validation, particularly the comparison with existing methods and the clarity in explaining the method's advantages and contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the problem addressed.
- The proposed method, MECE, is novel and innovative, introducing a new approach to optimize morphology and policy for RL agents in dynamic environments.
- The methodology is supported by thorough ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of MECE in improving generalization capabilities.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear introduction, methodology, and results sections, making it easy for readers to understand the contributions and implications of the research.
- The method's originality lies in its ability to optimize the morphology and policy of an RL agent through a co-evolutionary approach, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the complexity and diversity of the environments used.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with the performance gains not being clearly demonstrated.
- There is a lack of detailed explanation on how MECE improves generalization capabilities, and the paper could benefit from more explicit comparisons and visualizations to illustrate the method's advantages.
- The paper's reliance on the environment policy to modify the environment raises questions about the complexity and adaptability of the environment, which could be more effectively addressed by allowing the agent to directly modify the environment.
- The method's generalizability to more complex or varied environments is not adequately tested, which could limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the environment policy in modifying the environment, particularly in more complex or varied environments?
- How does MECE compare to other methods in terms of generalization capabilities, and can the authors provide more detailed comparisons or visualizations to support these claims?
- Could the authors elaborate on the method's ability to handle more complex environments or tasks, and discuss the potential challenges or limitations in scaling up the method?
- In terms of the method's implementation, how does the alternating training of policies affect the overall performance, and is there a potential for optimizing this process to improve results?
- The paper mentions several hyperparameters that could affect the results; could the authors provide a sensitivity analysis or ablation studies on these parameters to better understand their impact?
