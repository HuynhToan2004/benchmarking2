 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, IMO, for out-of-distribution (OOD) text classification using pre-trained models. This method involves learning sparse mask layers to identify and retain invariant features while discarding irrelevant ones. It employs a top-down greedy layer-wise sparse representation learning technique and incorporates an attention mechanism to focus on tokens that are useful for prediction. The method is evaluated on various datasets and reportedly outperforms strong baselines in terms of evaluation metrics. However, the paper suffers from significant issues in clarity, presentation, and the strength of its theoretical and empirical contributions. The reviewers have highlighted several concerns, including unclear explanations, potential errors in theoretical formulations, and insufficient comparisons with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in OOD text classification, which is highly relevant and challenging in the current landscape of pre-trained models.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and well-motivated, with a clear focus on identifying and retaining invariant features through sparse mask layers.
- The paper provides a detailed description of the proposed method, including the use of sparse mask layers and a top-down greedy layer-wise approach, which are innovative and practical.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms strong baselines in various evaluation metrics, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The paper includes a theoretical framework that helps to understand the relationship between domain-invariant features and causal features, providing a deeper insight into the method's operation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the theoretical analysis where assumptions and notations are not clearly explained, making it difficult to follow.
- The presentation of the paper is not clear and concise, with some parts being hard to read and understand. The use of abbreviations and undefined terms without adequate explanation can hinder comprehension.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing works, especially in terms of sparsity and the use of attention mechanisms in NLP models.
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the theoretical formulations, such as the use of incompatible assumptions and the lack of rigorous proof or justification for certain claims.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its method, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the proposed approach.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, particularly in scenarios where the proposed method underperforms compared to simpler baselines.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the theoretical assumptions and notations used in Section 3.3, particularly regarding the independence of noise and the definition of causal features?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use sparsity and attention mechanisms in NLP models?
- Can you provide more detailed explanations for the methodology used in the experiments, especially the selection of datasets and the choice of hyperparameters?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of text data, such as social media text versus formal text, and how does it perform under varying data volumes?
- Could you elaborate on the method's ability to handle different types of spurious correlations and how it manages to identify and discard them effectively?
- In the context of the theoretical analysis, how does the paper ensure that the assumptions made, such as the independence of noise and the separation of causal and non-causal features, are valid and not overly restrictive?
