 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method named Language-based Distributional Robust Optimization (L-DRO) to address sub-population shifts in vision-language models, specifically focusing on the CLIP model. L-DRO leverages natural language inputs to debias image feature representations, aiming to improve performance under domain shifts. The methodology involves training a feature adapter to transform image representations into debiased embeddings, which are then used to maintain consistency with original representations. Experiments on datasets like CelebA and Waterbirds demonstrate the effectiveness of L-DRO in mitigating the impact of sub-population shifts. However, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental scope, lack of theoretical rigor, and potential over-reliance on specific datasets and text prompts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to address sub-population shifts in vision-language models, specifically using natural language inputs to debias image feature representations.
- The proposed method, L-DRO, is simple yet effective, as demonstrated by its ability to improve performance under domain shifts.
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.
- The use of a two-layer MLP adapter for debiasing is highlighted as a simple yet effective solution.
- The experiments conducted on the CelebA and Waterbirds datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in mitigating the impact of sub-population shifts.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability across different datasets and its reliance on specific text prompts.
- There is a notable absence of theoretical analysis and a detailed discussion on the mathematical formulation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not include a comparison with other methods, such as those using different loss functions or architectures, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets and do not include a broader range of sub-populations or more diverse datasets, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of domain shift, which is crucial for the application of the proposed method in real-world scenarios.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to specific datasets and text prompts, and it may not generalize well to other datasets or tasks without modification.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other datasets or tasks that do not involve sub-population shifts?
- Could you provide a more detailed discussion on the mathematical formulation of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that address sub-population shifts, such as those using different loss functions or architectures?
- How sensitive is the performance of the proposed method to the selection of text prompts, and what are the implications of using different prompts for different datasets or tasks?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the entropy loss in the proposed method and its impact on the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method handle domain shifts that are not explicitly related to sub-population shifts, such as those caused by variations in lighting conditions or camera angles?
