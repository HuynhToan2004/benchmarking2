 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for model pruning, termed Sparse Model Soups (SMS), which averages multiple sparse models to improve generalization and out-of-distribution (OOD) performance. This approach is applied using Iterative Magnitude Pruning (IMP) with varied hyperparameter configurations, such as batch ordering or weight decay, to ensure identical sparse connectivity. The method is evaluated across various datasets including ImageNet, CIFAR, and WMT16, demonstrating significant improvements in performance and robustness. The paper also discusses the potential for extending this method to other pruning-during-training approaches, showcasing its versatility and applicability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel method for merging sparse models into a single classifier, leveraging the idea of starting each prune-retrain phase of IMP from an averaged model, which significantly enhances the performance of IMP.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear figures and pseudocode that aid in understanding the proposed approach.
- The paper includes comprehensive experimental results and comparisons with baseline methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in enhancing the performance of pruning-during-training approaches.
- The proposed method is straightforward, easy to implement, and does not require complex techniques or algorithms, making it accessible and practical for application.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise language that makes it easy to follow, and the results are well-presented, enhancing the readability and comprehensibility of the content.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the computational cost and memory requirements associated with the proposed method, which could be crucial for assessing its practical applicability in real-world scenarios.
- There is a need for more clarity on how the methodology relates to existing works, particularly in terms of differentiating the proposed approach from similar methods like weight rewinding and other pruning techniques.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, including potential drawbacks or challenges that may arise in its application.
- The experimental results presented in the paper are not sufficiently robust, with a lack of comprehensive testing across different datasets and architectures, which could affect the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger models or more complex architectures, which could limit its practical utility.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational cost and memory requirements to other pruning methods, especially when considering the need for multiple retraining phases?
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed methodology relates to existing works, particularly in terms of its novelty and distinctiveness from similar approaches?
- What are the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing pruning methods, especially in terms of performance and efficiency?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of architectures or datasets beyond those tested in the current study?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific hyperparameters used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential issue of reduced sparsity when averaging models with different sparse connectivities?
- Could the authors elaborate on the methodology and its implications, particularly in terms of its practical applicability and scalability?
