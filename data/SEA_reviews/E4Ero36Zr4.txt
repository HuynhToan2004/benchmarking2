 **Summary:**
The paper explores the Teacher-Student Curriculum Learning (TSCL) problem from a novel perspective, integrating cooperative game theory to analyze the interactions between the teacher and the learner. It proposes a data-centric approach, where each unit of experience is modeled as a player in a cooperative game, and the teacher's role is to optimize the allocation of these units to the learner. The paper introduces a cooperative game-based framework to understand the TSCL problem, employing cooperative game theory to formalize the interactions between the teacher and the learner. The paper presents experiments across various domains, including supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and classical games, to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in motivation, the limited scope of experiments, and the need for more rigorous theoretical analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise sections that effectively convey the authors' ideas.
- The integration of cooperative game theory with Teacher-Student Curriculum Learning (TSCL) is a novel and interesting approach, providing a fresh perspective on the TSCL problem.
- The experiments conducted across various domains, including supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and classical games, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper presents a data-centric perspective on TSCL, which is a promising direction for understanding and improving curriculum learning algorithms.
- The use of cooperative game theory to analyze TSCL is a novel and intriguing approach, which could potentially lead to new insights and solutions for TSCL.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for using cooperative game theory to analyze TSCL, and it is unclear how this approach provides new insights or solutions for the TSCL problem.
- The experiments conducted are limited in scope and depth, with the results not convincingly demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach over existing methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its approach, which could affect the credibility and applicability of the proposed framework.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed framework, which are critical for practical implementation.
- The paper does not adequately address the challenges of applying game theory to TSCL, such as the potential for negative interactions between units of experience and the computational complexity of solving cooperative games.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the motivation behind using cooperative game theory to analyze TSCL and how this approach provides new insights or solutions for the TSCL problem?
- How does the proposed framework handle negative interactions between units of experience, and what are the implications of these interactions for the learning process?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of their framework, especially in relation to the size and complexity of the cooperative games involved?
- How does the proposed framework compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of experiments and their relevance to the TSCL problem, particularly in relation to the supervised learning and reinforcement learning experiments?
- Is there a potential for extending the framework to continuous sets of units, and if so, how might this be achieved?
