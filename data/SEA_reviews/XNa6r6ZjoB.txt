 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel relational cross-attention mechanism, designed to disentangle relational information from object-level features within the Transformer architecture. This mechanism, termed the "abstractor," is aimed at improving the sample efficiency and generalization capabilities of Transformers in relational reasoning tasks. The proposed methodology is evaluated across various tasks, including discriminative relational tasks, sequence-to-sequence tasks, and math problem-solving tasks. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its lack of novelty in the use of symbolic representations and for not sufficiently differentiating its contributions from existing methods. Additionally, the paper's experimental setup and the clarity of its presentation have been noted as areas requiring improvement.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and intuitive descriptions of the proposed relational cross-attention mechanism and the abstractor module.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over standard Transformers and other relational architectures.
- The introduction of relational cross-attention as a novel module for explicit relational reasoning is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, covering both discriminative and sequence-to-sequence tasks, and includes an ablation study.
- The authors have made the code publicly available, which enhances the reproducibility and accessibility of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear differentiation from existing methods such as CoRelNet, which also uses symbolic representations for relational reasoning.
- The paper's reliance on symbolic representations may limit its applicability in more complex scenarios where relational information is not readily represented in this manner.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as it appears to be a straightforward extension of existing methods, such as CoRelNet, with minor modifications.
- The paper's experimental setup and comparisons are not robust, with potentially unfair comparisons due to differences in model sizes and architectural configurations.
- The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly in the clarity of figures and the explanation of certain methodological choices, such as the use of symbolic attention.
- The paper's focus on relational tasks might limit its broader applicability to other types of tasks, such as natural language understanding.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the main differences between their proposed method and CoRelNet, particularly in terms of the use of symbolic representations and the relational cross-attention mechanism?
- How does the proposed method perform when relational information is not readily represented in symbolic form?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specific model sizes and architectural configurations used in the comparisons?
- How does the proposed method scale with larger, more complex datasets or tasks?
- Can the authors provide more intuitive explanations or examples of the symbolic attention mechanism, particularly how it binds symbols to objects based on their relations to symbol binding vectors?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and training time to other methods, especially in larger or more complex tasks?
