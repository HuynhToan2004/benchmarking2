 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of sparse autoencoders to disentangle internal activations of large language models (LLMs) into semantically interpretable features. It argues that these features can be used to identify causally-important features for specific tasks, such as indirect object identification, and can provide insights into the model's behavior. The approach involves training an autoencoder with a sparsity penalty on the hidden activations to encourage sparse linear combinations of learned features. The paper evaluates the method against various baselines and demonstrates its effectiveness in improving interpretability and reducing polysemanticity. However, concerns were raised about the generalizability of the method to other tasks and the lack of rigorous testing against alternative methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined research question.
- The proposed method is simple, effective, and well-motivated, with a clear rationale for its application.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing the method's ability to improve interpretability and reduce polysemanticity in LLMs.
- The use of sparse autoencoders to disentangle internal activations of LLMs is a novel and interesting approach, providing a scalable and unsupervised method for resolving superposition in language models.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's effectiveness in identifying causally-important dictionary features for specific tasks, demonstrating the method's utility in understanding model behavior.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion of related works, particularly the recent studies on interpretability and causality in LLMs, which could have provided a richer context for the research.
- The method's generalizability to other tasks beyond the IOI task is not clearly demonstrated, raising concerns about its applicability in more complex or varied contexts.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training of the sparse autoencoder, including the hyperparameters used and the specifics of the training procedure, which could affect the reproducibility and robustness of the results.
- There is a lack of rigorous testing against alternative methods, such as other types of autoencoders or different training strategies, which could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation of the method's effectiveness.
- The paper's focus on a single task and model (Pythia-70M) limits the generalizability of the findings and raises questions about the method's applicability to other models and tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition of "monosemantic" and its implications for the interpretability of the learned features?
- How does the method perform when applied to other models or tasks, and what are the implications for its broader applicability?
- What are the specific hyperparameters used for training the sparse autoencoder, and how were these chosen?
- How does the method compare to other types of autoencoders or training strategies, and what are the advantages or disadvantages of the chosen approach?
- Could you provide more details on the training procedure for the sparse autoencoder, including the number of epochs used and the specifics of the training data?
- How do the learned features compare to other types of features, such as those learned by other types of models or methods?
- Can you discuss the limitations of the method and suggest areas for future research to address these limitations?
