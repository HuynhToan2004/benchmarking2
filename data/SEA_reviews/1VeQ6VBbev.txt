 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the convergence properties of policy gradient methods in finite-horizon Markov Decision Processes (MDPs), focusing on two variants: simultaneous and dynamic policy gradient. The simultaneous approach updates all parameters at once, while the dynamic approach updates parameters sequentially in reverse order. The authors provide theoretical analyses of these methods, including convergence rates and bounds for exact and sampled gradient scenarios. The paper also discusses the practical implications of these findings, suggesting that the dynamic policy gradient method may offer improved convergence rates. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of empirical validation and the potential irrelevance of its theoretical results to practical applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear and well-structured presentation of the results, with a focus on the convergence of policy gradient methods in finite-horizon MDPs.
- The introduction of the dynamic policy gradient method offers a novel approach to policy gradient training, potentially leading to improved convergence rates.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, with a focus on the convergence of policy gradient methods under different conditions, including exact and sampled gradients.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible to readers not specializing in the field, and includes a detailed comparison of the convergence rates of simultaneous and dynamic policy gradient methods.
- The authors effectively use dynamic programming to analyze the convergence of policy gradient methods, which is a novel approach in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical validation, which is crucial for demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The theoretical results are not directly applicable to practical scenarios, as they are based on the assumption of exact gradient computation, which is impractical in real-world applications.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the theoretical results could be translated into practical algorithms, which limits the practical utility of the findings.
- The convergence rates and bounds provided in the paper are loose and not comparable to existing literature, which could limit the paper's impact.
- The paper's assumptions, such as the constant state space across epochs, may not hold in practical scenarios, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and the assumptions made in the analysis, which could affect the paper's credibility.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a clearer explanation of how the theoretical results could be applied in practical scenarios, perhaps through the development of a simple algorithm based on the dynamic policy gradient method?
- How does the paper's analysis compare to existing literature in terms of convergence rates and bounds, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the practical utility of the findings?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in their analysis and discuss the limitations of these assumptions in practical scenarios?
- How does the paper's analysis address the challenges posed by the finite-horizon MDP setting, particularly in terms of the convergence rates and bounds provided?
- Could the authors discuss the potential for extending the analysis to more complex or practical policy gradient methods, such as those using neural networks?
