 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to handling multi-task, multi-source prompt tuning in large language models (LLMs) by utilizing a mixture of prompts (MoPs) with a smart gating functionality. The method involves assigning different prompts to each task and dynamically selecting the most relevant prompts based on the current input, thus addressing the challenge of task and data heterogeneity. The proposed method is tested on various datasets, demonstrating significant improvements over baseline methods in both centralized and federated learning scenarios. The paper also discusses the application of this method to compressed LLMs, showing that it can effectively manage prompt training interference and mitigate model drift issues in federated learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to handling multi-task, multi-source prompt tuning in large language models (LLMs) using a mixture of prompts (MoPs) with a smart gating functionality, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The method is demonstrated to be effective in both centralized and federated learning scenarios, showing significant improvements over baseline methods in terms of final perplexity reduction.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, and the experiments are extensive, covering various pruning ratios and both centralized and federated settings.
- The paper addresses the challenge of task and data heterogeneity in LLM instruction tuning, which is a significant and relevant problem in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of how the proposed method differs from and improves upon existing approaches.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper could be improved by including more experiments on larger datasets or more diverse tasks to further validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper assumes that each subtask is drastically different and represents one distinct skill, and each prompt embeds the corresponding skill, which might not always be the case in real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational and memory costs associated with the proposed method, especially in comparison to baseline methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing methods, highlighting the key differences and improvements?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different model sizes or types of LLMs, such as those based on encoder-decoder architectures?
- Is there a potential for the proposed method to be adapted for use in generative models like GPT-3, and if so, what would be the expected outcomes?
- How does the proposed method handle the selection of prompts when the number of tasks is unknown or when the relationship between tasks is not clearly defined?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and provide examples or scenarios where it might not be as effective?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of data distributions, and what are the implications for the selection and effectiveness of the gating function?
