 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel variational approach to solve inverse problems using diffusion models, specifically focusing on image restoration tasks. It proposes a new method called RED-diff, which leverages the variational distribution to approximate the true posterior distribution, providing a regularization mechanism that denoisers at different timesteps impose different structural constraints on the image. This approach is evaluated against state-of-the-art sampling-based diffusion models, demonstrating superior performance in terms of image quality and GPU efficiency. The paper is structured around a variational inference framework, which allows for the formulation of sampling as stochastic optimization, making the method adaptable and efficient. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of theoretical depth and comparative analysis with existing methods, particularly in the context of nonlinear inverse problems.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel variational approach to solving inverse problems using diffusion models, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear theoretical derivations and practical applications demonstrated through experiments.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are extensive, covering both linear and nonlinear inverse problems.
- The proposed RED-diff method is computationally efficient and memory-friendly, which is beneficial for practical applications.
- The paper provides a new perspective on solving inverse problems with diffusion models, offering a fresh approach to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparative analysis with existing methods, particularly in the context of nonlinear inverse problems.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the theoretical underpinnings of the variational approach, which could enhance the understanding and applicability of the proposed method.
- There is a noted absence of diversity in the method's solutions, which could limit its effectiveness in certain applications.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its generalizability and scalability.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational cost and memory requirements of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the discrepancy in the number of steps used in the ablation study versus the main paper?
- How does the proposed method compare with other variational approaches to solving inverse problems using diffusion models?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational cost and memory requirements of the proposed method, particularly in comparison to existing methods?
- How does the method handle the potential for mode collapse in the variational approach, and what strategies are in place to mitigate this risk?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the variational distribution and how it affects the performance of the method?
- How does the method perform in more complex or varied inverse problem scenarios, such as those involving different types of measurements or noisy observations?
