 **Summary:**
The paper explores the application of gradient subspaces in reinforcement learning (RL), specifically focusing on the Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) and Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) algorithms. It demonstrates that a significant fraction of the gradients in these algorithms lie within a low-dimensional subspace of high curvature, suggesting that these methods could benefit from second-order optimization techniques. The paper also highlights the stability of these subspaces over time, indicating potential for more efficient optimization. However, the paper's scope is limited to a few RL algorithms and environments, and the practical implications of the findings are not thoroughly explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear and comprehensive analysis of the gradient subspace in the context of RL, specifically focusing on PPO and SAC algorithms, which are widely used in the field.
- The authors have conducted a thorough empirical study, demonstrating the existence of low-dimensional gradient subspaces in RL, which is a novel finding in this context.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the experimental setup and the methodology used, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The findings are supported by empirical evidence, showing that the gradient subspace remains stable throughout the training process, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The potential applications of these findings, such as improving parameter-space exploration and enabling second-order optimization, are highlighted, suggesting significant implications for the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is somewhat limited, focusing on only two RL algorithms and a few environments, which may not generalize well to other RL methods or more complex scenarios.
- The practical implications of the findings are not thoroughly explored, particularly in terms of how they could be applied to enhance RL performance in real-world applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of implementing second-order optimization methods, which could be a significant barrier to practical adoption.
- The analysis is primarily based on offline data, which may not fully capture the dynamics of RL training, where the data distribution shifts over time.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and potential negative societal impacts, which are crucial for a comprehensive evaluation of the work.

**Questions:**
- Can the findings of this paper be generalized to other RL algorithms or more complex environments, such as those involving continuous control tasks?
- How can the insights from this study be practically applied to enhance RL performance in real-world applications?
- What are the specific computational challenges associated with implementing second-order optimization methods in RL, and how can these be addressed?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential negative societal impacts of their findings, and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
- How do the authors address the issue of the data distribution shift in RL training, which could affect the stability and effectiveness of the gradient subspace?
