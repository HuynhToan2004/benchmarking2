 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the correlation between sequence-level certainty and hallucination in Knowledge-Grounded Dialogue Generation (KGDG), proposing two methods: Probabilistic CRR (P-CRR) and Semantic CRR (S-CRR). These methods aim to mitigate hallucination by ranking model responses based on their probabilistic certainty and semantic alignment. The study demonstrates that higher levels of certainty are associated with lower hallucination rates. However, the paper's empirical scope is limited to KGDG, and the generalizability of the findings to other NLG tasks is unclear. Moreover, the method's reliance on a black-box LLM's output without direct access to its probability distribution raises concerns about the validity of the semantic certainty metric.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in natural language generation (NLG) by investigating the relationship between sequence-level certainty and hallucination, which is crucial for improving the quality of generated responses.
- The authors provide a theoretical proof and analysis to demonstrate the correlation between sequence-level certainty and hallucination, which is supported by extensive experiments across multiple datasets and NLG models.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, and the methodology is clear, which aids in understanding the proposed solutions.
- The study introduces a novel approach by proposing Certainty-based Response Ranking (CRR), which can be applied to different decoding methods, enhancing the robustness of the proposed solutions.
- The empirical results show that the proposed methods, P-CRR and S-CRR, can effectively reduce hallucination in model outputs, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on KGDG as the primary empirical setting limits the generalizability of the findings to other NLG tasks, which could be more broadly applicable.
- The methodology's reliance on a black-box LLM's output without direct access to its probability distribution raises concerns about the validity of the semantic certainty metric.
- The paper lacks a broader evaluation across various NLG tasks, which could have provided a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed methods' effectiveness.
- The paper's experimental setup and results do not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed methods significantly outperform existing methods, which could undermine the claimed improvements.
- There is a need for more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed methods, especially considering the computational demands of generating multiple candidate responses.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the proposed methods, such as the assumption that semantic certainty is proportional to probabilistic certainty, which might not hold true in all scenarios.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other NLG tasks beyond KGDG?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed methods?
- How does the proposed method handle scenarios where semantic certainty is not proportional to probabilistic certainty?
- Could the authors elaborate on the methodology used for training the KGDG models and the evaluation metrics used to assess the effectiveness of the proposed methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for mitigating hallucination in NLG, and what are the specific advantages of the proposed methods?
