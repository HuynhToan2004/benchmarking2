 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel extension of the Modern Hopfield Network (MHN) by integrating an encoder-decoder architecture to enhance the network's capacity for associative memory storage and recall. The authors propose that this extension, termed the Hopfield Encoding Network (HEN), can handle cross-stimulus associations by encoding input patterns into a latent space using a pre-trained auto-encoder. This approach aims to improve the network's performance by reducing spurious attractor states, which are a significant issue in traditional MHN models. The paper presents various experiments using the MS-COCO dataset, demonstrating the network's ability to recall images based on textual queries, thus bridging the gap between vision and language modalities.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to extend the Modern Hopfield Network (MHN) by integrating an encoder-decoder architecture, which is both innovative and relevant to current research in neural networks.
- The integration of a pre-trained auto-encoder for encoding input patterns is a significant contribution, enhancing the network's capacity for associative memory storage and recall.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the network's ability to recall images based on textual queries, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper addresses the issue of spurious attractor states in MHN, which is a significant limitation of the original model, and proposes a solution that is both practical and theoretically sound.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear motivation for the choice of the auto-encoder, particularly whether it should be a Variational Auto-Encoder (VAE) or another type, and how this choice affects the results.
- The theoretical underpinnings of the proposed solution are not thoroughly explained, which could limit the understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are not sufficiently robust, with insufficient details on the training and testing data sets, and the choice of parameters such as the number of images used in different experiments.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed model with existing methods, particularly in terms of memory capacity and the handling of spurious attractor states.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the results and the methodology, which could make it difficult for readers to replicate the experiments or understand the implications of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the choice of the auto-encoder and its impact on the results?
- How does the proposed model compare to other existing methods in terms of memory capacity and handling of spurious attractor states?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and testing data sets used in the experiments, and the choice of parameters such as the number of images used?
- How does the proposed model handle the issue of spurious attractor states, and what are the implications of this in practical applications?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed solution, and how it addresses the limitations of the original MHN model?
