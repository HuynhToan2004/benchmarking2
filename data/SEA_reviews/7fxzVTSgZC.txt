 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Behavioral Cloning with Dynamic Programming (BCDP), a novel method for offline imitation learning that leverages transition information from low-quality data to enhance policy performance. BCDP operates by maximizing the probability of transitions from suboptimal states to expert-observed states, thereby improving the likelihood of reaching expert-observed states. The method is evaluated on various D4RL tasks and demonstrates significant performance improvements, particularly when the expert data is limited. Despite its innovative approach, the paper is criticized for its lack of theoretical rigor, particularly in the absence of a formal definition of low-quality data and the lack of a clear theoretical foundation for the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, Behavioral Cloning with Dynamic Programming (BCDP), which is a significant contribution to the field of offline imitation learning.
- The proposed method is simple yet effective, as demonstrated by its ability to leverage low-quality data to improve performance in offline imitation learning tasks.
- The paper is well-written and provides a clear exposition of the proposed method, making it accessible to readers.
- The experiments are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing baselines.
- The paper addresses an important and understudied problem in offline imitation learning: the use of low-quality data, which is a significant challenge in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a formal definition of low-quality data, which is a critical concept for the study. The absence of such a definition makes it difficult to evaluate the method's performance across different types of data.
- The theoretical foundation of the method is weak, with no formal definition of the imitation gap and no theoretical guarantees for the performance of the learned policy.
- The method's performance is not robust to variations in the quality of data, as shown in some experiments where the method underperforms compared to baselines.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with existing methods in the field, which could provide a clearer picture of its advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the hyperparameters used and the size of the datasets, which could affect the reproducibility of the results.
- The paper does not address the issue of offline data collection, which is a critical aspect of offline imitation learning.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a formal definition of low-quality data and explain how the method performs under different types of data?
- How does the method compare with existing methods in the field, particularly in terms of performance and theoretical guarantees?
- What are the hyperparameters used in the experiments, and how do they affect the performance of the method?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the size of the datasets and the specifics of the data collection process?
- How does the method perform when the expert data is of low quality, and what are the implications of this for the performance of the learned policy?
- Could the authors clarify the theoretical underpinnings of the method, particularly the concept of the imitation gap and the performance guarantees of the learned policy?
