 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the convergence of Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) in non-convex settings, focusing on the existence of stationary points in the last iterations. It provides a theoretical analysis that shows under certain conditions, the last iterates of SGD can converge to a stationary point, thus challenging the common belief that the last iterate of SGD may not converge to the minimum in non-convex settings. The paper also explores the density of stationary points in the final iterates, which is shown to be close to 1 for large enough iteration counts. The theoretical results are supported by numerical experiments on both smooth and non-smooth loss functions. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, as the results are seen as extensions of existing works, and for not adequately addressing the practical implications of the theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the main results.
- The paper addresses the convergence of the last iterate of SGD in nonconvex settings, which is a significant and relevant problem in machine learning.
- The theoretical results are supported by numerical experiments, providing a practical validation of the theoretical findings.
- The paper includes interesting and relevant open questions that could guide future research in the field.
- The authors have provided a comprehensive literature review, which helps in understanding the context and contributions of the paper.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's novelty is questioned, as the results are seen as extensions of existing works, particularly those by Khaled and Richtarik (2022).
- The practical implications of the theoretical findings are not adequately discussed, which could limit the paper's impact on real-world applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of its assumptions, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The proof techniques and assumptions used in the paper are not clearly distinguished from those in previous works, which could lead to confusion about the originality of the contributions.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical challenges of finding stationary points in non-convex settings, which could be a significant barrier to applying the theoretical results in practice.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their results compared to those of Khaled and Richtarik (2022)?
- How do the authors address the practical challenges of finding stationary points in non-convex settings, especially given the theoretical results?
- Could the authors provide more details on the proof techniques used in the paper, particularly how they differ from or build upon those in previous works?
- What are the implications of the assumptions made in the paper, and how do they affect the generalizability of the results?
- How do the authors envision their results being applied in practice, and what are the potential barriers to implementing these findings in real-world scenarios?
