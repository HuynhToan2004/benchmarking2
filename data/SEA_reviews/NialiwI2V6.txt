 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MOTOR, a novel foundation model for time-to-event (TTE) predictions in medical settings, trained on up to 55M patient records and 9B clinical events. The model leverages a self-supervised pretraining objective to capture temporal structure from clinical events, which is then used to improve predictions on downstream tasks. MOTOR's architecture includes a Transformer-based model and a piecewise exponential TTE model, which are optimized for scalability and memory efficiency. The model is evaluated across various datasets and tasks, demonstrating improvements over existing methods. However, concerns are raised about the model's performance in out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios, the choice of baselines, and the fairness of the model's performance across different patient groups.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive discussion of related work and limitations.
- The introduction of the MOTOR model, which is pre-trained on a large dataset of medical records, is a significant contribution to the field of medical AI, particularly in time-to-event predictions.
- The paper presents a novel self-supervised pretraining objective that uses thousands of TTE tasks during pretraining to learn broadly useful features for TTE modeling, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The MOTOR model's architecture is optimized for scalability and memory efficiency, which is crucial for handling large datasets and complex tasks.
- The paper includes comprehensive experimental results that demonstrate the model's effectiveness and its ability to improve predictions on downstream tasks.
- The release of the MOTOR model, along with the code and pre-trained weights, will enable further research and development in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of how the pre-training task is designed and how it differs from other tasks, which could benefit from more detailed ablation studies.
- The choice of baselines and the comparison with existing methods like DeepSurv and DeepHit are not convincing, particularly in terms of the model's performance in out-of-distribution scenarios.
- There is a lack of discussion on the potential limitations of the model, such as its performance in real-world scenarios where data is not as clean or structured as in the pre-training dataset.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of censoring in the data, which could significantly affect the model's performance and the interpretation of the results.
- The paper's claims about the model's generalizability and robustness are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, particularly in the out-of-distribution settings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on how the pre-training task is designed and how it differs from other tasks?
- How does the MOTOR model handle the issue of censoring in the data, and what steps were taken to mitigate its impact on the model's performance?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the MOTOR model with other existing methods, particularly in terms of its performance in out-of-distribution scenarios?
- What are the potential limitations of the MOTOR model, and how do these affect its performance in real-world scenarios?
- How does the MOTOR model handle missing data in the input, and what impact does this have on the model's performance?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and the data used in the out-of-distribution experiments, particularly in terms of the data's quality and the model's performance in these settings?
