 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for quantitatively evaluating the expressiveness of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) using a concept called homomorphism expressivity. This framework quantifies the ability of GNNs to count graphs under homomorphism, providing a systematic and comprehensive tool for comparing the expressive power of different GNN architectures. The authors demonstrate how this framework can be applied to various GNN models, including MPNNs, Subgraph GNNs, Local GNNs, and Folklore-type GNNs, and show that it aligns well with practical performance. The paper also discusses the limitations and potential future directions of this framework.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework for quantitatively evaluating the expressiveness of GNNs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The framework is well-motivated and theoretically grounded, providing a clear and elegant description of the pattern family for a wide range of GNN architectures.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive literature review, enhancing its relevance and comprehensiveness.
- The results are validated through extensive experiments, demonstrating the practical applicability and effectiveness of the proposed framework.
- The paper addresses an important and timely topic in the field of GNNs, which is crucial for understanding and improving the expressive power of these models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on homomorphism expressivity, while novel, might limit its applicability to other types of expressivity, such as invariant or equivariant expressivity.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of its framework, which could be a significant drawback for readers looking for a comprehensive understanding of its scope and applicability.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the framework can be extended to higher-order GNNs and how it compares to other existing frameworks.
- The paper's title might be misleading as it primarily focuses on homomorphism expressivity, which might not accurately reflect the scope of the work.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational complexity of the proposed framework, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed framework can be extended to higher-order GNNs, especially in light of the potential limitations discussed in the review?
- How does the proposed framework compare to other existing frameworks for evaluating the expressive power of GNNs?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed framework, especially in relation to the models it is applied to?
- How does the proposed framework handle the issue of the incomparability of certain GNN models, such as Subgraph GNN and Local GNN, as mentioned in the review?
- Can the authors elaborate on the practical implications of the findings presented in the paper, particularly in terms of improving the expressive power of GNNs in practical applications?
