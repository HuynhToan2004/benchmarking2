 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to world modeling in autonomous driving using a discrete diffusion model and a VQVAE-like tokenizer. This methodology tokenizes sensor observations, particularly point cloud data, into discrete tokens, which are then used to predict future observations. The authors claim that this approach significantly reduces Chamfer distance compared to existing methods, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance. The paper also explores the use of discrete diffusion models to enhance world modeling capabilities, showing improvements over existing methods like MaskGIT. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, as it primarily adapts existing techniques without significant innovation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in world modeling for autonomous driving, focusing on the tokenization of point cloud data, which is a crucial aspect of the methodology.
- The proposed method demonstrates state-of-the-art performance in terms of Chamfer distance reduction, showcasing the effectiveness of the approach.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experimental results are convincing, with clear improvements over existing methods.
- The use of discrete diffusion models and VQVAE for world modeling is a novel approach that effectively reduces the Chamfer distance in point cloud forecasting.
- The methodology is broadly applicable to various domains, including autonomous driving, and could potentially scale with the advancement of technology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as it primarily adapts existing techniques like MaskGIT and VQVAE without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method to longer prediction horizons, which is a significant limitation given the nature of the task.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and inference of the method, which is necessary for reproducibility and understanding the computational requirements.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with more recent and relevant works, such as those that use different tokenization methods or focus on longer prediction horizons.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and inference of the method, including computational requirements and training time?
- How does the method handle the scalability issues related to longer prediction horizons?
- What are the specific advantages of using discrete diffusion models over other types of diffusion models in the context of world modeling?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential areas for future research?
- How does the method compare with other recent works in the field, particularly those that use different tokenization methods or focus on longer prediction horizons?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of the different objectives in the training process, and how they contribute to the overall performance of the method?
