 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Rob-FCP, a novel algorithm designed to enhance federated conformal prediction by addressing the challenges of Byzantine failures in the federated learning environment. Rob-FCP utilizes characterization vectors to identify and exclude malicious clients based on their conformity scores, thereby ensuring the integrity of the prediction sets. Theoretical analysis and empirical evaluations demonstrate the robustness of Rob-FCP against various Byzantine attacks and its effectiveness in both IID and non-IID settings. The paper also addresses the issue of unknown malicious client numbers by proposing a malicious client number estimator. The robustness of the algorithm is validated through extensive experiments, showcasing its potential in practical applications.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel algorithm, Rob-FCP, which effectively addresses the issue of Byzantine failures in federated conformal prediction, providing a robust framework for handling malicious clients in the federated learning setting.
- Theoretical analysis is provided for both IID and non-IID settings, including a malicious client number estimator, which enhances the understanding and applicability of the algorithm in diverse scenarios.
- Empirical evaluations demonstrate the effectiveness of Rob-FCP against various Byzantine attacks, showcasing its robustness and practical utility.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and understandable, with clear explanations of the problem setup, algorithm design, and theoretical analysis.
- The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of their approach with existing methods, highlighting the novelty and contribution of their work.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed approach, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The assumption of a fixed number of benign clients may not be practical in all scenarios, and the paper does not explore how the algorithm performs when the number of benign clients varies.
- There is a lack of comparison with other existing methods that address similar issues in federated learning, which could help in evaluating the relative advantages of the proposed approach.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of communication costs associated with the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, such as the bounded distribution disparity of benign clients, may not hold in all practical scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity or scalability of the proposed method, which could be crucial for understanding its practical applicability in large-scale or complex systems.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison of their approach with other existing methods that address similar issues in federated learning?
- How does the proposed method perform when the number of benign clients varies?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of communication costs associated with the proposed method in practical applications?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the malicious clients are not aware of their status, and how does this affect the overall performance of the algorithm?
- Could the authors elaborate on the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the assumption of bounded distribution disparity of benign clients, and discuss how these assumptions might limit the generalizability of the results?
- What are the implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis for the practical applicability of the proposed method?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity or scalability of the proposed method, and discuss how these factors might influence the practical implementation of the algorithm in large-scale or complex systems?
