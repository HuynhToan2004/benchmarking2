 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for defending against alignment-breaking attacks in Large Language Models (LLMs) by proposing a robust alignment checking function (RAC). This function is designed to reduce the success rate of adversarial prompts by randomly dropping tokens from user inputs. The RAC function is theoretically analyzed, and its effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on various LLMs, including GPT-3.5 and GPT-4. The method aims to improve the robustness of LLMs against adversarial attacks by introducing a simple yet effective mechanism. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the method, particularly in real-world scenarios, and the potential impact on the user experience and model performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to defending against alignment-breaking attacks by randomizing user inputs, which is an innovative concept.
- The method is theoretically sound and supported by practical experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing the success rate of adversarial prompts.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive analysis of the theoretical and practical aspects of the proposed method.
- The method does not require additional training or fine-tuning of the LLM, which is a significant advantage in terms of computational efficiency.
- The paper addresses a critical issue in the field of LLMs, which is the vulnerability to alignment-breaking attacks, and proposes a simple yet effective method to mitigate this risk.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's practicality is questionable, especially in real-world scenarios where users might be confused or frustrated by the randomized responses.
- The method's effectiveness is limited to specific types of adversarial attacks, and it may not be effective against more sophisticated or diverse attacks.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential negative impacts of the method on the user experience, such as increased latency or reduced model performance.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational costs of the method, which is crucial for understanding its scalability and applicability in various scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the method, such as its potential to reduce the model's performance on benign requests or its applicability to different types of LLMs.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on how the method performs in real-world scenarios, especially in terms of user experience and potential confusion?
- How does the method perform against more sophisticated or diverse types of adversarial attacks?
- Can the authors discuss the potential negative impacts of the method on the model's performance and user experience, and propose strategies to mitigate these impacts?
- How does the method scale with larger or more complex LLMs, and what are the computational costs associated with its implementation?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the method's effectiveness against different types of adversarial attacks, and discuss the potential trade-offs between model performance and adversarial robustness?
