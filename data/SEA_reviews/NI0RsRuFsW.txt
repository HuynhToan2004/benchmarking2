 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for designing evasive trojans that are difficult to detect and reverse-engineer. The authors employ a Wasserstein distance-based loss to ensure the distribution of trojaned models closely resembles that of clean models, which enhances the stealth of the trojans. The methodology is tested across various datasets and models, demonstrating that these evasive trojans can evade detection with high success rates. The paper also discusses the implications of these evasive trojans on the defense side, suggesting that current detection methods may need significant improvements. However, concerns are raised about the practicality of the threat model, the novelty of the approach, and the depth of the experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for designing evasive trojans, which are more difficult to detect and reverse-engineer compared to standard trojans.
- The methodology is well-explained, with clear descriptions of the evasion loss, distribution matching, and randomization techniques used to make trojans harder to detect.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experiments are thorough, demonstrating that the proposed evasive trojans can evade detection across various datasets and models, and can also make reverse-engineering harder.
- The paper is the first to systematically evaluate evasive trojans, providing a comprehensive analysis of the threat landscape.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practicality of the threat model, particularly the assumption that the defender has access to a dataset of clean and trojaned models.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable as it primarily combines existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of false positives, which could lead to the detection of clean models as trojans.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly robust, with some results showing marginal improvements or no improvement over existing methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation of the evasive trojans against a broader range of detection methods, including those that are not model-level.
- The paper's conclusions are not sufficiently supported by the experimental results, particularly the claims about the effectiveness of the evasive trojans against different types of detectors.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the evasive trojans can be inserted into models, especially in scenarios where the attacker does not have full control over the model's training process?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for making trojans harder to detect, such as those mentioned in the literature?
- What are the specific advantages of the evasive trojans over other types of trojans, such as those that are more difficult to reverse-engineer?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the performance of the evasive trojans against a broader range of detection methods, including those that are not model-level?
- How does the paper address the issue of false positives, particularly in scenarios where clean models are incorrectly identified as trojans?
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, especially the discrepancies between the claimed improvements and the actual performance metrics?
