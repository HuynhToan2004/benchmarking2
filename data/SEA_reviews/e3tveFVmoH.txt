 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel zeroth-order optimization method, Stochastic Two-Point (S2P), which is a variant of the Stochastic Three-Point (STP) method. S2P is designed to be more efficient by eliminating the need for evaluating the objective function at the current point, thus reducing the computational cost. The authors also propose an accelerated version, Accelerated Stochastic Two-Point search (AS2P), which incorporates dynamic step sizes and statistical information to potentially capture both first-order and second-order dynamics of the objective function. Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrate that AS2P outperforms existing methods in terms of convergence and computational efficiency. However, the paper lacks a detailed discussion on the experimental results, particularly the choice of hyperparameters and the comparison with other baseline methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, the Stochastic Two-Point (S2P) method, which is a significant contribution to the field of zeroth-order optimization.
- The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the S2P method, including the convergence properties under both general and relaxed smoothness assumptions.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making the complex theoretical concepts accessible.
- The proposed method shows empirical improvements over existing methods, demonstrating its practical effectiveness.
- The paper provides a detailed comparison with related works, highlighting the novelty and contribution of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters, particularly in the experimental section, which could benefit from more detailed explanations and ablation studies.
- The convergence analysis and theoretical results are not clearly explained or justified, particularly the choice of step sizes and their impact on convergence rates.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other baseline methods, such as those mentioned in the Malladi et al. paper, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the experimental results, particularly the choice of hyperparameters and the impact of different settings on the performance of the proposed method.
- The paper could be improved by providing more detailed experimental results, such as the training time and performance of the proposed method under different conditions.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the impact of different settings on the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare with other baseline methods, such as those mentioned in the Malladi et al. paper?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, such as the training time and performance of the proposed method under different conditions?
- How does the proposed method handle the choice of step sizes, particularly the impact of different settings on the convergence rate?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of different noise distributions on the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method handle the non-smoothness of the objective function, particularly in non-convex optimization scenarios?
