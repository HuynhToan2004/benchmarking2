 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called instruction backtranslation for training instruction-following language models, which leverages a seed dataset and a large unlabeled corpus to generate high-quality training examples. This method involves a two-stage process: first, the seed dataset is used to fine-tune a model that predicts instructions from outputs, which generates augmented training examples. These examples are then curated using a self-assessment process to select high-quality instances, which are used to further fine-tune the model. The approach demonstrates strong performance on the Alpaca leaderboard, outperforming many other non-distilled models. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, as it bears resemblance to existing techniques like self-instruct and backtranslation, and the method's reliance on a seed dataset of high-quality human-annotated examples.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the experimental results are strong, showing that the proposed method can outperform many other non-distilled models.
- The method of instruction backtranslation is novel and interesting, providing a scalable approach to building high-quality instruction-following language models.
- The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments, ablations, and analyses, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in terms of performance and data efficiency.
- The method's ability to scale up instruction-following models using unlabeled web data is highlighted, showing significant improvements over models trained solely on human-annotated data.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the quality of self-generated data and its impact on model performance, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques such as self-instruct and backtranslation, without significant new contributions.
- The method's reliance on a high-quality seed dataset of human-annotated examples is a significant limitation, as it may not be practical or scalable to obtain such data.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art models, which could provide a clearer understanding of the method's advantages and disadvantages.
- The method's performance is not consistently superior across all datasets and models, which raises concerns about its generalizability and effectiveness.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of data quality in the self-generated data, which could affect the reliability and validity of the results.
- The method's scalability is questioned due to the reliance on a seed dataset and the need for high-quality human-annotated examples, which may limit its applicability in practical scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their method and how it differs from existing techniques like self-instruct and backtranslation?
- How does the method perform when using different seed datasets, and what are the implications for the model's performance and generalizability?
- Could the authors provide more details on the quality assessment process and how it impacts the final performance of the model?
- Is there a possibility to compare the method with other state-of-the-art models to provide a more comprehensive evaluation?
- Can the authors discuss the potential impact of different hyperparameters on the model's performance and how these were chosen?
- How does the method handle the potential for biases in the seed dataset, and what measures were taken to mitigate these biases?
