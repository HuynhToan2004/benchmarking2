 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for online hyperparameter tuning in contextual bandits, termed Continuous Dynamic Tuning (CDT), which leverages a double-layer bandit framework to optimize hyperparameters in real-time. This framework, which includes the Zooming TS algorithm, enables the tuning of hyperparameters within a search space without requiring pre-specified candidates. The CDT framework is shown to achieve sublinear regret and performs better than existing methods across both synthetic and real datasets. The paper also introduces a new problem setting for Lipschitz bandits under a switching environment, which is the first of its kind. Despite its theoretical contributions and experimental validations, the paper has been critiqued for its incremental novelty, lack of comparison with related works, and potential issues in the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework for online hyperparameter tuning in contextual bandits, which is the first of its kind to utilize a double-layer bandit framework, named CDT, for hyperparameter optimization.
- The proposed Zooming TS algorithm with Restarts is a significant contribution, providing theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence of its effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes extensive experimental validations that demonstrate the efficiency of the proposed method.
- The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the algorithm, including a sublinear regret bound, which is a notable achievement.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the field of online contextual bandits, where the performance of the algorithm heavily depends on the values of hyperparameters, and theoretically derived parameter values may lead to unsatisfactory results in practice.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with related works, particularly those that address similar problems such as online model selection in bandits with a continuous candidate set.
- There is a significant gap between the theoretical regret bound and the empirical performance of the algorithm, which is not adequately explained.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincing, with issues such as the choice of hyperparameters and the comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the novelty and advantages of the proposed framework over existing methods, particularly in the context of continuous hyperparameter tuning.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the practical implications of the Lipschitz assumption and its applicability in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the hyperparameters are chosen in practice, which is crucial for the practical application of the proposed framework.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role of hyperparameters such as T1, T2, and H in the experimental results, and provide a detailed explanation of their impact on the performance of the algorithm?
- How does the proposed framework handle the assumption of a piecewise stationary environment, and what are the implications of this assumption in practical scenarios?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing works, particularly those that address similar problems in the field of online model selection in bandits?
- Is there a possibility to extend the theoretical analysis to include a lower bound on the regret, and if not, what are the challenges in achieving this?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the novelty and advantages of their proposed framework over existing methods, particularly in the context of continuous hyperparameter tuning?
- How does the proposed framework handle the practical challenges of hyperparameter tuning in real-world scenarios, and what are the implications of this for the performance of the algorithm?
