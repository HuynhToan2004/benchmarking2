 **Summary:**
The paper explores the expressive power of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) through a probabilistic lens, focusing on their ability to approximate joint distributions over Markov Random Fields (MRFs) and their impact on node classification and link prediction tasks. It introduces the concept of phantom nodes and phantom edges to enhance GNNs' expressive power, which are validated through both theoretical analysis and experimental results on synthetic and real-world datasets. The paper also discusses the limitations of GNNs in approximating joint distributions and proposes new methods to address these limitations. However, the paper is criticized for its clarity and the practicality of the proposed methods, particularly in terms of computational complexity and the need for large datasets to observe significant improvements.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with a clear and well-organized structure, making it easy to follow.
- The introduction of phantom nodes and phantom edges is a novel approach that enhances the expressive power of GNNs.
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, supported by rigorous proofs and experimental results that validate the theoretical claims.
- The paper offers new insights into the expressive power of GNNs and their ability to approximate complex joint distributions, which is crucial for understanding the limitations and potential of GNNs.
- The proposed methods are simple and can be easily integrated into existing GNN architectures, making them practical for real-world applications.
- The paper is significant in its contribution to the field of GNNs, addressing a critical gap in understanding the expressive power of these models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more clarity and conciseness, particularly in the introduction and some sections of the theoretical analysis.
- The practical implementation of the proposed methods, such as the use of phantom nodes and phantom edges, is not clearly explained, which could limit their adoption by other researchers.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed methods, such as the computational complexity and the need for large datasets to observe significant improvements.
- The theoretical contributions of the paper are seen as somewhat limited, as the results are largely expected based on existing literature.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of GNNs in approximating joint distributions and the practical implications of these limitations.
- There are some typographical and formatting errors that need to be corrected for clarity and professional presentation.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the practical implementation of the proposed methods, particularly how phantom nodes and phantom edges are added to the graph and how they interact with existing nodes and edges?
- How do the phantom nodes and phantom edges affect the computational complexity of GNNs, and what are the practical implications of these additional computational demands?
- In the experimental results, how do the proposed methods perform on larger datasets, and what are the scalability limitations of these methods?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific datasets used and the parameters of the GNN models employed?
- How does the paper address the limitations of GNNs in approximating joint distributions, and what are the potential solutions or workarounds for these limitations?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the clique size in the experiments and how it affects the performance of the GNNs?
