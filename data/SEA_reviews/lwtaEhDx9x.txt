 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the memorization of tabular data by Large Language Models (LLMs), specifically focusing on GPT-3.5 and GPT-4. It introduces various tests to assess the degree of memorization, distinguishing between knowledge, learning, and memorization. The authors demonstrate that LLMs can reproduce important statistics of the data, but may not memorize it verbatim. They also explore the implications of memorization on downstream prediction tasks, suggesting that LLMs can perform complex tasks with data they have seen during training without overfitting. The paper is structured around a set of experiments designed to test these concepts, and the results indicate that LLMs can learn tabular data distributions effectively, though there are concerns about the novelty and depth of the experimental insights.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and timely issue in the field of Large Language Models (LLMs), focusing on the potential for data contamination and memorization in LLMs.
- The authors provide a comprehensive overview of existing literature and effectively structure their work around a set of experiments designed to test the proposed concepts.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a strong focus on the importance of verifying data contamination before applying LLMs.
- The tests introduced in the paper are practical and allow practitioners to assess whether a given tabular dataset was likely part of the model's training corpus.
- The paper includes a variety of tests that demonstrate the ability of LLMs to learn tabular data distributions effectively, distinguishing between knowledge, learning, and memorization.
- The results are interesting, highlighting that LLMs can learn tabular data distributions effectively, which is crucial for understanding their capabilities and limitations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty in its experimental insights, as it primarily reiterates known issues in the field without providing significant new insights or methodologies.
- There is a lack of clarity and detail in the experimental setup and results, particularly in the description of the datasets used and the methodology for testing memorization.
- The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from existing works, such as those by Carlini et al., which have already addressed similar issues.
- The paper's reliance on LLMs for generating and classifying tabular data is seen as a potential limitation, as it does not sufficiently explore the use of LLMs in conjunction with other methods like data processing or augmentation.
- The paper's conclusions are somewhat limited by the scope of the experiments, which are primarily focused on GPT-3.5 and GPT-4, and do not extend to a broader range of LLMs or datasets.
- There is a need for more rigorous testing and a deeper exploration of the implications of LLM's memorization of tabular data, particularly in terms of its impact on the reliability and generalizability of LLM-based predictions.

**Questions:**
- Can you clarify the definition and implications of "knowledge" in the context of your experiments? How does this relate to the broader understanding of knowledge in LLMs?
- How do the authors ensure that the tests for memorization are robust and not influenced by other factors such as the model's ability to generalize from similar datasets?
- Could you provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly how the tests were conducted and what the results indicate about the memorization of tabular data by LLMs?
- How do the authors address the potential for data contamination in the LLMs, and what steps are being taken to mitigate this issue?
- Given the limitations of the current experiments, what future directions do the authors plan to explore in terms of expanding the scope of the research and addressing the identified weaknesses?
