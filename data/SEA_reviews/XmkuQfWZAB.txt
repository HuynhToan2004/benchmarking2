 **Summary:**
The paper explores the use of human feedback, specifically ratings and preferences, to learn rewards in contextual bandit problems. It introduces a novel model for human ratings that allows for a more realistic representation of human biases and uncertainties, which are modeled as a monotonic transformation of true rewards. The paper provides theoretical comparisons between rating-based and preference-based approaches, showing that the latter can be more efficient in certain scenarios. It also discusses the impact of human biases and uncertainties on the learning process and the suboptimality of policies. The paper is well-written and presents a clear theoretical framework, although it lacks empirical validation and could benefit from a broader range of experimental setups.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel model for human ratings that can capture both human bias and uncertainty, which is a significant advancement over previous models.
- It provides a theoretical comparison between rating-based and preference-based approaches, which is crucial for understanding the advantages and limitations of each method.
- The paper is well-written and accessible, making complex theoretical concepts understandable for a broad audience.
- It addresses a significant problem in the field of offline RL, where reward engineering is a critical challenge.
- The paper is original in its approach, providing a theoretical explanation for the empirical success of preference-based methods from a modeling perspective.
- The theoretical results are well-presented, and the paper is clear and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical validation, which is crucial for demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed models and methods.
- The comparison between rating-based and preference-based approaches is not comprehensive, as it does not consider the full range of possible feedback models.
- The paper could benefit from a broader range of experimental setups to validate the theoretical claims more thoroughly.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed models and methods, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a clear comparison of the sample complexity of the proposed methods with existing works, which is essential for evaluating the practical efficiency of the proposed methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the assumptions and implications of the theoretical results, particularly in the context of real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setups used to validate the theoretical claims, including the specific conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- How does the proposed model for human ratings compare to other existing models in terms of its ability to capture human biases and uncertainties?
- Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the monotonic function in the model for human ratings and how it affects the learning process?
- In the context of the theoretical results, how do the assumptions and conditions affect the applicability and relevance of the findings to real-world scenarios?
- How do the authors envision the practical implementation of the proposed methods in different contexts, and what are the potential challenges and limitations?
