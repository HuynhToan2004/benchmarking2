 **Summary:**
The paper investigates data-constrained backdoor attacks, where attackers cannot access the entire training data, instead relying on a subset of it. The authors propose a novel method that employs CLIP-based techniques to address the challenges of entanglement between benign and poisoning features. Specifically, the paper introduces two methods: Clean Feature Suppression (CLIP-CFE) and Poisoning Feature Augmentation (CLIP-UAP and CLIP-CFA). These methods aim to minimize the impact of benign features and enhance the expression of poisoning features, respectively. The paper presents extensive experiments on three datasets and three target models, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods. However, concerns were raised regarding the clarity of the experimental setup, the lack of comparison with more recent baselines, and the potential for overfitting due to the use of a pre-trained CLIP model.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a realistic scenario of data-constrained backdoor attacks, which is a significant and relevant problem in the field.
- The proposed methods are well-motivated, clearly presented, and supported by extensive experiments.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methods and their implications.
- The use of CLIP-based methods to tackle the entanglement between benign and poisoning features is innovative and contributes to the field.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the problem, the proposed methods, and the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with more recent baselines, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The experimental setup and the details of the CLIP-based methods are not clearly explained, which could lead to confusion and misunderstanding of the results.
- There is a potential for overfitting due to the use of a pre-trained CLIP model, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed methods, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with other backdoor attack methods, especially those that do not rely on a pre-trained CLIP model.
- The paper's assumptions about the availability of a pre-trained CLIP model might not be practical in all real-world scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the details of the CLIP-based methods and the role of the pre-trained CLIP model in the experiments?
- How do the authors address the potential for overfitting due to the use of a pre-trained CLIP model?
- Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other backdoor attack methods, especially those that do not rely on a pre-trained CLIP model?
- How do the proposed methods compare in terms of efficiency and effectiveness with other state-of-the-art backdoor attack methods?
- What are the limitations of the proposed methods, and how do these limitations affect the practical applicability of the findings?
