 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method called Newton Losses for optimizing non-convex and hard-to-optimize losses in machine learning, particularly in weakly-supervised learning. This method approximates the loss function with a quadratic term using second-order information, allowing for the use of gradient descent to optimize the neural network. The method is evaluated on benchmarks such as MNIST sorting and Warcraft shortest-path problems, showing significant improvements in performance. The approach is based on Newton's method and leverages both Hessian and empirical Fisher information to construct the Newton loss. Despite its potential, concerns are raised about the method's novelty and the practicality of its application, particularly in scenarios where the Hessian matrix is not available.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is simple and effective, with potential applications in various fields of machine learning.
- The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation, demonstrating the method's effectiveness across different benchmarks and tasks.
- The use of Newton's method to optimize the loss function is novel and interesting, especially in the context of weakly-supervised learning.
- The method's approach to approximating the loss function with a quadratic term using second-order information is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a clear explanation of the method's advantages over existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability.
- There is a lack of theoretical justification for the use of Newton Losses, which could enhance the paper's credibility and applicability.
- The paper's claims regarding the method's ability to improve performance on non-convex and hard-to-optimize losses are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical challenges associated with computing the Hessian matrix, which is crucial for the method's effectiveness.
- The paper's presentation could be improved by including more detailed descriptions of the experimental setup and results, as well as clearer figures and tables.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific advantages of using Newton Losses over other optimization methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods in terms of convergence speed and stability, especially when applied to different types of non-convex and hard-to-optimize losses?
- What are the practical implications of using the Hessian-based Newton Losses versus the empirical Fisher-based Newton Losses, and when should one be preferred over the other?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the hyperparameters used and the computational resources required for the experiments?
- How does the method perform when the Hessian matrix is not available, and what are the potential workarounds for this limitation?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed theoretical justification for the use of Newton Losses, especially in terms of its effectiveness in addressing the challenges posed by non-convex and hard-to-optimize losses?
