 **Summary:**
The paper introduces CODE, a novel outlier detection method for generative text summarization, focusing on cross-document and cross-domain outliers. It leverages the encoder-decoder cross-attention framework to detect infrequent words that exhibit domain-specific characteristics, which can identify outlier paragraphs. The method is evaluated on various datasets and shows promising results in reducing false positive rates while maintaining high true positive rates. The paper also discusses the impact of pre-training with cross-document outliers and the role of hyperparameters in the detection process. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, the need for more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, and the lack of a detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem of outlier paragraph detection in text summarization, which is crucial for improving the quality of summaries.
- The proposed method, CODE, is novel and well-explained, utilizing the encoder-decoder cross-attention framework to detect infrequent words, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and intuitive figures that aid in understanding the proposed method and its results.
- The methodology is supported by a thorough experimental evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of CODE in reducing false positive rates and maintaining high true positive rates.
- The paper provides a detailed discussion on the impact of hyperparameters such as cross-attention and word frequency, which is essential for understanding the method's performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the detection performance, which is crucial for understanding the robustness of the proposed method.
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it primarily leverages existing techniques such as cross-attention and word frequency, which have been previously explored in other works.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare its method with existing baselines, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's advantages or disadvantages compared to current approaches.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust, with some results showing only marginal improvements over existing methods, which raises concerns about the practical utility of the proposed method.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- How does the choice of hyperparameters, such as alpha and beta, affect the detection performance, and what are the optimal values for different datasets?
- Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing baselines, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and detection accuracy?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of text summarization tasks, such as abstractive summarization or extractive summarization?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical applications of the proposed method in real-world scenarios?
- How does the method handle different types of outliers, such as those that are semantically or grammatically incorrect, and what are the implications of these on the detection performance?
