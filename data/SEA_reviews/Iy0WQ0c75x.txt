 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel contrastive learning framework for hyperbolic graph embedding, aimed at addressing the dimensional collapse problem in hyperbolic spaces. This framework incorporates an isotropic Gaussian loss on the tangent space to enhance the effective rank of feature representations, thereby preventing the collapse of hierarchical data-invariant information. The methodology is validated through experiments on several benchmark datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in various downstream tasks. Despite its innovative approach, the paper faces criticism for its clarity in presentation and the need for more rigorous experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of hyperbolic graph embedding, focusing on the dimensional collapse issue, which is an essential aspect of hyperbolic contrastive learning.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, with clear explanations of the intuition behind the method, including the use of hyperbolic geometry and the concept of dimensional collapse.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The experiments conducted are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and tasks.
- The paper is original in its approach to using hyperbolic contrastive learning to enhance graph embedding, which is a promising direction for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the definitions and explanations of key terms and concepts such as "Effective Rank" and "isotropic Gaussian loss."
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the notation and definitions used throughout the paper, which could confuse readers.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, particularly in terms of the statistical significance of the improvements.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to other types of graphs beyond trees and its generalization to other graph learning tasks.
- There is a lack of detailed analysis and discussion on the theoretical implications of the proposed method, which could strengthen the paper's contribution to the field.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the definition and calculation of "Effective Rank" and how it is used in your method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- What are the specific advantages of using hyperbolic contrastive learning over other methods for graph embedding?
- How does the paper address the issue of dimensional collapse in hyperbolic spaces, and what are the implications of this for the effectiveness of the method?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the parameters used and the statistical significance of the results?
- In the context of hyperbolic geometry, what is the relationship between the "ambient space" and the "hyperbolic space," and how does this affect the interpretation of the results?
