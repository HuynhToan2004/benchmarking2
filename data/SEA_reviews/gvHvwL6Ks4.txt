 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel influence approximation method termed "lazy influence" to enhance data quality assessment in federated learning while preserving privacy. This method, which involves estimating the influence of a data point on the model's performance without retraining, is designed to be privacy-preserving and computationally efficient. The approach involves a distributed influence aggregation scheme that ensures data quality and privacy through the use of differentially private mechanisms. The paper evaluates the method on CIFAR10 and CIFAR100 datasets, showing that it can effectively filter out corrupted data while maintaining strong differential privacy guarantees. However, concerns about the method's applicability to more complex models and its scalability in larger federated learning systems remain.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel influence approximation method, "lazy influence," which is simple, efficient, and privacy-preserving.
- The method is evaluated on CIFAR10 and CIFAR100 datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in filtering out corrupted data while maintaining strong differential privacy guarantees.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the proposed method and its advantages over existing methods, making it accessible and understandable.
- The method is model-agnostic and can be applied to various models, making it versatile.
- The paper addresses the important problem of data quality assessment in federated learning, which is crucial for ensuring the reliability and effectiveness of models trained in this setting.
- The method is designed to be privacy-preserving, which is a significant concern in federated learning where data privacy is paramount.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not provide a formal definition or theoretical analysis of the influence function, which could limit the understanding and applicability of the method.
- The method's reliance on a small number of local epochs for estimating influence might not be sufficient for larger or more complex models, raising concerns about its effectiveness and scalability.
- The paper's evaluation is limited to two datasets and does not include experiments on more complex models like transformers, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods in federated learning, which could provide a better understanding of its relative performance.
- The method's application is limited to classification tasks, and it is unclear how it would perform in other types of tasks such as regression.
- The paper does not discuss the potential impact of data heterogeneity on the method's performance, which could be a significant factor in federated learning systems.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity or communication costs associated with the method, which could be crucial for practical implementation.

**Questions:**
- How does the method perform when applied to more complex models, such as transformers?
- Can the method be extended to other types of tasks beyond classification, such as regression?
- How does the method handle data heterogeneity, and what are the potential impacts on its performance?
- Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and communication costs associated with the method?
- How does the method handle the potential for malicious or uncooperative participants in the federated learning system?
- Could you provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in federated learning to better understand the relative performance of your method?
- How does the method handle the potential for data leakage, especially in a federated learning setting where data privacy is a significant concern?
