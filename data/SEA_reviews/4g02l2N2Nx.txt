 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel linear attention mechanism, termed "Hedgehog," designed to mimic the spiky and monotonic properties of softmax attention. This mechanism is aimed at addressing the performance gap between linear and softmax attention in various training scenarios, including training from scratch, finetuning, and pre-trained model conversion. The Hedgehog model is trained using a learnable linear attention feature map and a distillation loss to match the spikiness and monotonicity of softmax attention. The paper presents empirical evidence showing that Hedgehog can recover over 99% of the performance of softmax attention in these scenarios, outperforming prior linear attention mechanisms. However, concerns were raised about the generalizability of the results to larger models and the lack of detailed analysis on the computational and memory efficiency of the Hedgehog model.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the motivation, methodology, and results.
- The proposed method, Hedgehog, is simple yet effective, and the paper provides a clear explanation of its design and implementation.
- The paper includes comprehensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of Hedgehog across various settings, including training from scratch, finetuning, and pre-trained model conversion.
- The results show that Hedgehog can recover over 99% of the performance of softmax attention, outperforming prior linear attention mechanisms.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the properties of softmax and linear attention, which helps in understanding the design choices of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks detailed analysis on the computational and memory efficiency of the Hedgehog model, especially when compared to other linear attention models.
- The generalizability of the results to larger models is not clearly demonstrated, which is a significant limitation given the current trends in model scaling.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other linear attention models, such as those proposed in recent works, to establish the superiority of Hedgehog.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and inference times for the Hedgehog model, which is crucial for understanding its practical efficiency.
- There are concerns about the scalability of the method to larger models, as the paper primarily focuses on smaller models.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, which could affect the credibility of the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the Hedgehog model perform in terms of computational and memory efficiency compared to other linear attention models, especially in larger models?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and inference times for the Hedgehog model, and how these compare to other models?
- How does the proposed method perform when scaled to larger models, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in doing so?
- Could the authors clarify the training procedure for the Hedgehog model, particularly how the MLPs are trained and whether the distillation loss is used during this process?
- How does the proposed method compare to other linear attention models in terms of performance and efficiency, especially in different model sizes and training scenarios?
