 **Summary:**
The paper introduces MetaConceptContext (MetaCoCo), a new benchmark for few-shot classification that focuses on spurious correlations. It evaluates various state-of-the-art methods across different learning strategies and architectures, highlighting the challenges posed by spurious correlations in few-shot classification tasks. The dataset, which includes 100 classes with varying contexts, is designed to reflect real-world scenarios, providing a valuable resource for further research in this area. The paper also proposes a metric using CLIP to quantify spurious correlations, enhancing the evaluation of models' performance under these conditions. However, concerns were raised about the novelty of the dataset, the limited scope of the benchmark, and the lack of detailed analysis on the impact of spurious correlations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research.
- The proposed MetaCoCo benchmark is a significant contribution, focusing on the spurious-correlation shifts in few-shot classification, which is an understudied area.
- The dataset is diverse and realistic, consisting of 100 classes with varying contexts, and is manually annotated, ensuring high quality.
- The paper provides extensive experiments using a variety of learning strategies and architectures, which are well-documented and detailed.
- The use of CLIP to quantify spurious correlations is innovative and helps in evaluating the performance of models under these conditions.
- The paper includes a novel metric based on CLIP to quantify spurious correlations, which is a valuable addition to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis on the impact of spurious correlations, which is a critical aspect of the research.
- The dataset, while diverse, is limited in scope, with only 155 contexts, which might not fully capture the complexity of real-world scenarios.
- The novelty of the dataset is questionable as it appears to be a subset of existing datasets like DomainNet, and the inclusion of spurious correlations is not sufficiently justified or novel.
- The paper does not provide sufficient insights into how the dataset was collected or the methodology behind the CLIP-based metric, which could affect the reproducibility and reliability of the results.
- There are several typographical errors and inconsistencies in the paper, which could detract from its professionalism and credibility.

**Questions:**
- How does the CLIP-based metric compare to other methods for measuring spurious correlations, and what are the advantages of using CLIP specifically?
- Can the authors provide more details on how the dataset was collected and annotated, including the criteria used for filtering out outliers and the inter-annotator agreement rates?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the number of contexts (155) in the dataset, and what are the implications of this choice for the dataset's ability to capture real-world scenarios?
- In light of the concerns about the novelty and scope of the dataset, how does the paper differentiate its contributions from existing works, particularly in terms of the inclusion and handling of spurious correlations?
- Could the authors elaborate on the impact of spurious correlations on model performance and suggest potential strategies for mitigating these effects?
