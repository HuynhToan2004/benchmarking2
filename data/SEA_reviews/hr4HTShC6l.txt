 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method using the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) to detect shortcuts in training data by estimating mutual information between the input and learned representation. This approach is applied across various datasets including MNIST, CelebA, NICO, Waterbirds, and BenchMD, showing potential in identifying shortcuts that other out-of-distribution (OOD) detectors fail to notice. The methodology is based on the hypothesis that models tend to learn a compressed representation of the input space, which can be quantified by mutual information. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in defining shortcuts, insufficient theoretical justification, and the need for more comprehensive experiments to validate its effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of machine learning by focusing on the detection of shortcuts in training data, which is a crucial aspect of model generalization.
- The proposed method using mutual information (MI) as a domain-agnostic metric is novel and innovative, providing a new perspective on detecting shortcuts in training datasets.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a comprehensive experimental evaluation across various datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) to detect shortcuts in training datasets is a significant contribution to the field, providing a potentially useful tool for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining what constitutes a shortcut, which is crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the proposed method.
- The theoretical justification for the proposed method is insufficient, particularly in explaining why MI is an effective metric for detecting shortcuts.
- The experimental setup is not robust enough to convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, with concerns about the selection of baselines and the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to different types of shortcuts and its computational complexity.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the relationship between the proposed method and other existing methods for detecting shortcuts, such as those based on linear mode connectivity.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed definition of what constitutes a shortcut and how this definition guides the application of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods for detecting shortcuts, particularly in terms of effectiveness and computational complexity?
- Could the authors provide more detailed theoretical justification for why MI is an effective metric for detecting shortcuts?
- How does the proposed method perform in the presence of reverse spurious correlations, and what are the implications of this for the generalizability of the results?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup, particularly the selection of baselines and the criteria for selecting datasets for evaluation?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of shortcuts, and what are the implications of this for the generalizability of the results?
