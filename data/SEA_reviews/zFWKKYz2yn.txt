 **Summary:**
The paper explores the stability and effectiveness of two methods for extracting symbolic rules from trained recurrent neural networks (RNNs) using deterministic finite automata (DFA). Specifically, it compares the quantization and equivalence query methods, employing a large-scale experiment involving 3600 RNN models and 18000 DFAs. The study investigates the stability and performance of these methods across various RNN architectures and grammar types, focusing on the Tomita and Dyck grammars. The experiments reveal that the quantization method outperforms the equivalence query method in terms of stability and accuracy, especially when the RNNs are partially trained. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope, focusing only on regular grammars and not sufficiently discussing the theoretical underpinnings or broader implications of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a large-scale experiment involving 3600 RNN models and 18000 DFAs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper provides a thorough comparison of quantization-based rule extraction and equivalence query-based methods, demonstrating the superior performance of quantization-based methods in terms of stability and accuracy.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments across different RNN architectures with varied hidden state sizes and grammar types, showcasing the stability of the rule extraction method.
- The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers.
- The experiments are well-documented, and the results are clearly presented, which enhances the paper's credibility and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's scope is limited to regular grammars, which may not generalize well to other types of grammars such as context-free or context-sensitive grammars.
- The theoretical analysis in the paper is weak, particularly the proofs and definitions, which could undermine the paper's claims.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the stability of the quantization-based approach, especially concerning the number of states in the extracted DFAs.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the study, such as the potential impact of the number of hidden states on the performance of the RNNs.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of some sections, particularly in the definitions and proofs, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed methods to more complex grammar types or larger datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the stability of the quantization-based approach is achieved, especially concerning the number of states in the extracted DFAs?
- How does the paper address the limitations mentioned, such as the scalability and generalizability of the findings to other types of grammars?
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and proofs in the paper, particularly in sections where there is confusion or ambiguity?
- How does the paper justify the choice of the number of hidden states in the RNNs, and what impact does this have on the performance of the models?
- Could the authors discuss the implications of the findings for other types of grammars beyond regular grammars, and how the methods might be adapted or applied in these contexts?
