 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Clinical Prediction with Large Language Models (CPLLM), a method that leverages pre-trained large language models (LLMs) to predict clinical diagnoses by fine-tuning them with specific prompts. The approach involves using LLMs to understand the sequence of diagnoses and predict future diagnoses, outperforming traditional methods such as logistic regression and other state-of-the-art models like Med-BERT. The methodology involves fine-tuning LLMs using quantization techniques and prompts tailored to medical concepts, demonstrating superior performance on datasets like MIMIC-IV and eICU-CRD. The paper also discusses the potential of using LLMs for clinical prediction tasks and highlights the method's adaptability and computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the methodology used.
- The use of large language models (LLMs) for clinical prediction is a novel approach, demonstrating a new direction in clinical prediction tasks.
- The paper effectively utilizes quantization techniques and prompts to enhance the performance of LLMs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The experiments conducted show that the proposed method outperforms existing baselines, indicating the effectiveness of the approach.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the methodology, datasets, and experimental results.
- The authors provide code and data, enhancing the reproducibility and transparency of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art models, particularly those that use different approaches like retrieval-augmented generation.
- There is a lack of detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities and challenges.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in handling large volumes of data or complex clinical scenarios.
- The use of ROC-AUC as the primary metric for evaluation is questionable, as it may not be the most appropriate metric for imbalanced datasets.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the failure cases or the impact of different hyperparameters on the model's performance, which could enhance the understanding of the model's behavior.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the ethical implications of using LLMs in clinical settings, which is a critical aspect of healthcare AI research.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when compared to other state-of-the-art models, especially those that use different approaches like retrieval-augmented generation?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results on the impact of different hyperparameters on the model's performance?
- How does the proposed method handle large volumes of data or complex clinical scenarios, and what are the potential limitations in such settings?
- Could the authors discuss the ethical implications of using LLMs in clinical settings, particularly in terms of data privacy, bias, and accountability?
- How does the proposed method compare to other methods that use different LLMs, such as BioMedLM versus Llama2?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or case studies to illustrate the practical application of the method in clinical settings?
