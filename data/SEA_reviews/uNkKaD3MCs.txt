 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PALM, a novel distance-based method for Out-of-Distribution (OOD) detection, which models the embedding space using a mixture of von Mises-Fisher distributions to represent each class. This method employs a mixture of prototypes to capture the diversity within classes and dynamically updates these prototypes based on reciprocal neighbor soft assignment weights. The method is evaluated on multiple datasets and reportedly achieves state-of-the-art results, although concerns were raised about the novelty and clarity of the method's contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of the proposed method and extensive experiments.
- The proposed method achieves good performance, with impressive results reported in Table 1 and Figure 2.
- The paper provides a clear explanation of the proposed method and its contributions to the field of OOD detection.
- The use of a mixture of prototypes to capture the diversity within classes is a novel and reasonable approach.
- The paper includes ablation studies and discussions on the proposed method, which help in understanding its effectiveness and limitations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the paper is questioned, with concerns that the main contributions are not clearly distinguished from existing works.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive literature review, missing out on relevant studies such as those by Du et al. (2022a,b) and Tao et al. (2023).
- The method's reliance on a fixed number of prototypes per class may limit its adaptability to different datasets.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant drawback in practical applications.
- The experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, with some results showing marginal improvements or even inferior performance in certain scenarios.
- The paper suffers from inconsistencies and unclear descriptions, particularly in the methodology and experimental setups.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the main contributions of their work and how they differ from existing methods?
- How does the proposed method compare to other recent works, such as those by Du et al. (2022a,b) and Tao et al. (2023)?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity of their method against other state-of-the-art methods?
- What is the rationale behind the choice of the number of prototypes per class, and how does this affect the method's performance across different datasets?
- How does the method handle the assignment of samples to prototypes, particularly in scenarios where there are many prototypes?
- Can the authors explain the inconsistencies and unclear descriptions noted in the paper, especially in the methodology and experimental setups?
