 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FLNeRF, a novel method for estimating 3D facial landmarks directly from NeRF representations, which is a significant advancement over traditional methods that rely on 2D images or 3D models. FLNeRF utilizes a coarse-to-fine approach to predict facial landmarks, employing a bilinear model for accurate estimation. The method is evaluated against various state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating its effectiveness and utility in applications such as face swapping and editing. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its limited novelty, lack of comprehensive experimental validation, and potential issues with scalability and generalization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for predicting 3D facial landmarks directly from NeRF representations, which is a significant advancement over traditional methods that rely on 2D images or 3D models.
- The coarse-to-fine approach is well-designed and effectively utilized, improving the accuracy and efficiency of the landmark prediction process.
- The method is well-motivated, with a clear focus on utilizing NeRF representations for facial landmark estimation, which is a promising direction for future research.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand, and the supplementary material provides additional insights into the method's implementation and performance.
- The proposed method is evaluated against various state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating its effectiveness and utility in applications such as face swapping and editing.
- The paper includes an ablation study that helps in understanding the contribution of different components of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited as it primarily builds upon existing methods like MoFaNeRF and does not introduce significant new techniques or approaches.
- The paper lacks comprehensive experimental validation, with limited comparisons to other methods and insufficient quantitative results.
- The scalability and generalization of the method are questionable, as the method is trained on a dataset that is not widely available, which may limit its applicability to real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of scalability, as the method's reliance on a dense 3D face model for landmark estimation could limit its applicability to faces with different shapes or expressions.
- The method's performance is evaluated primarily on a small dataset, which may not accurately reflect its performance in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss potential limitations or societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be crucial for understanding its broader implications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the coarse model in the overall method, especially in terms of its contribution to the final results?
- How does the proposed method handle variations in facial expressions and identities, particularly in scenarios where the dataset may not include a wide range of expressions or identities?
- Could the authors provide more details on the dataset used for training and testing the method, including its size, diversity, and representativeness of real-world scenarios?
- How does the method perform when applied to faces with different shapes or expressions that are not included in the training dataset?
- Could the authors provide additional results or comparisons that demonstrate the method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios, such as face swapping or editing of faces with different expressions or identities?
- How does the method handle potential issues related to scalability and generalization, particularly in scenarios where the dataset may not include a wide range of expressions or identities?
