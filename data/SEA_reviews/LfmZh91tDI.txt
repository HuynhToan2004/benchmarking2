 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the layer-wise linear mode connectivity (LLMC) in neural networks, particularly focusing on the behavior of loss barriers between models during training. The authors propose a novel approach to understand the layer-wise structure of the loss surface, showing that deep linear networks exhibit convexity in individual layers, which may explain the absence of layer-wise barriers. This study is supported by empirical evidence across various model architectures and datasets, including ResNet18 on CIFAR10 and ViTs on CIFAR10. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for federated learning and personalization, suggesting that layer-wise aggregation might not be beneficial in certain scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a novel approach to understanding the layer-wise structure of the loss surface, which is crucial for understanding the dynamics of neural network training.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments across different model architectures and datasets, including ResNet18 on CIFAR10 and ViTs on CIFAR10, which supports the robustness of their findings.
- The paper introduces a new perspective on the robustness of models, which is not commonly discussed in the literature.
- The analysis of the robustness of the model and the layer-wise linear mode connectivity (LLMC) is interesting and provides new insights into the training dynamics of neural networks.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the experiments are well-designed and comprehensive.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a formal theoretical analysis, which could strengthen the claims made about the layer-wise structure of the loss surface.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and visualizations, especially in figures where the data is dense, to aid in understanding the results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the implications of the LLMC findings for federated learning and personalization, particularly how it could be applied to improve these processes.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the LLMC approach and how it might not apply to certain types of neural networks or training scenarios.
- The paper could be improved by including more recent and relevant literature in the related work section to provide a more comprehensive review of the current research in this area.
