 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for Neural Architecture Search (NAS) that leverages generative modeling to navigate large search spaces. It employs a two-level hierarchy of generative models: the first level focuses on micro-cell design using Conditional Continuous Normalizing Flow (CCNF) and the second level generates macro architectures using a transformer-based sequence generator. The method is evaluated on various datasets including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, ImageNet, and NAS-Bench-360, demonstrating competitive results against existing NAS methods. The paper also explores the use of zero-cost proxies to quantize the graph design space, enhancing the efficiency and scalability of the search process. However, concerns are raised about the novelty of the method, the complexity of the search space, and the practical implications of the results, particularly in terms of real-world performance and the scalability of the approach.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method.
- The use of generative modeling for Neural Architecture Search (NAS) is innovative and shows potential for reducing the cost and complexity of NAS.
- The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through empirical results that outperform existing NAS methods.
- The approach of using a hierarchical generative model for NAS is novel and provides a new perspective on the problem.
- The paper is well-motivated, addressing the challenges of limited search spaces in existing NAS methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited as it heavily relies on existing generative modeling techniques without significant innovation.
- The search space is complex and not well-explained, which could limit the understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The results are not compelling, with the performance of the proposed method not significantly better than existing methods like ZenNAS.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and generalization of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to different datasets and architectural styles.
- The method's reliance on zero-cost proxies for clustering micro designs may limit its effectiveness in real-world scenarios where these proxies may not accurately reflect performance.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of using GPT for conditioning the sequence generator, which could be a significant bottleneck in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the novelty of their method in relation to existing generative modeling approaches for NAS?
- How does the proposed method handle the complexity of the search space, particularly in terms of ensuring scalability and generalization to different datasets and architectural styles?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and evaluation of the models, including the specifics of the training data and the evaluation metrics used?
- How does the proposed method compare to other generative modeling approaches for NAS, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of their method, particularly in terms of its applicability and scalability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the method perform when applied to different datasets and architectural styles, and what are the implications of these differences for the effectiveness of the search process?
