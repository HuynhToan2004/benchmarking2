 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the inconsistencies in saliency metrics by analyzing the effect of perturbations on prediction probability and pixel importance ranks. It introduces two new metrics, ARPPD and APRC, to quantify these inconsistencies. The study uses Gaussian Blur as a perturbation mechanism to demonstrate that these metrics can be applied to any perturbation. The paper argues that existing saliency metrics that rely on pixel importance become unreliable for measuring the fidelity of saliency maps, emphasizing the need for careful selection of perturbation techniques. The experiments are conducted on several datasets and models, showing that the proposed metrics can measure the inconsistency of saliency metrics. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, insufficient empirical validation, and lack of comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and interesting problem related to saliency metrics, which is crucial for understanding the reliability of saliency maps.
- The introduction of new metrics, ARPPD and APRC, provides a novel approach to quantifying inconsistencies in saliency maps.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The experiments are well-designed, and the results are clearly presented, contributing to the paper's clarity and readability.
- The paper provides a theoretical framework that helps in understanding the unreliability of saliency metrics, enhancing the overall understanding of the topic.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks novelty as it primarily builds on existing work by Tomsett et al. (2020), with minimal additional contributions.
- The empirical validation is insufficient, particularly in terms of the number of images used in experiments, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed metrics with existing methods, which limits the understanding of their relative performance.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodology and results, particularly in the definition and explanation of the proposed metrics and the experimental setup.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential for adversarial attacks on saliency maps, which could be a significant practical concern.
- The paper's contribution is seen as limited due to its focus on a specific perturbation technique (Gaussian Blur) without a broader exploration of other perturbations.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definitions and calculations of ARPPD and APRC, especially in relation to the perturbations used and the expected outcomes?
- How do the authors justify the use of Gaussian Blur as the primary perturbation technique, and what are the implications of this choice for the generalizability of the findings?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, possibly including the results for each class separately to enhance the understanding of the data?
- How do the proposed metrics perform when compared to existing methods, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the practical utility of the new metrics?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical framework and its implications for the reliability of saliency metrics, particularly in light of the criticisms regarding the novelty and depth of the analysis?
