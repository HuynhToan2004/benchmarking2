 **Summary:**
The paper introduces new variants of the ACVI algorithm for solving constrained variational inequalities, focusing on the convergence rates of the last iterate. It extends the analysis of the ACVI algorithm, showing that it converges at a rate of O(1/\u03b8K) under certain conditions, including monotonicity and Lipschitz continuity of the operator F. The authors also propose an inexact ACVI variant and a specialized ACVI for inequality constraints, demonstrating their effectiveness through experiments on various games. The paper addresses the limitations of the original ACVI algorithm, particularly its inability to converge when the operator is not L-Lipschitz, and provides a theoretical framework for handling such scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear and concise introduction of the main results.
- The authors address a significant gap in the literature by providing convergence guarantees for the last iterate of ACVI without requiring the operator to be L-Lipschitz.
- The paper introduces a warm-start technique to solve subproblems approximately and initializes variables with the approximate solution found at the previous iteration, which can lead to faster convergence.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the convergence of the last iterate of the ACVI algorithm, which is a novel contribution to the field.
- The proposed algorithm is straightforward and easy to implement, making it accessible for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and assumptions of the proposed algorithms, particularly in scenarios where the operator F is not monotone or Lipschitz continuous.
- The convergence analysis seems to rely heavily on the smoothness of the barrier function, which may not be applicable in all scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a clear comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of convergence rates and practical applicability.
- The numerical experiments are limited, and the results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, such as GDA, which are also shown to be effective in similar scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and the practical implications of the proposed algorithms.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the conditions under which the ACVI algorithm is guaranteed to converge, especially when the operator F is not monotone or Lipschitz continuous?
- How does the proposed warm-start technique compare to other methods in terms of computational efficiency and convergence rates?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of convergence rates and practical applicability?
- How does the proposed algorithm perform when the operator F is not monotone or Lipschitz continuous, and what are the implications of these assumptions on the convergence guarantees?
- Can the authors provide more detailed numerical results, including a comparison with other methods on more complex problems, and discuss the practical implications of the proposed algorithms in real-world scenarios?
