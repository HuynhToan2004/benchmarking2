 **Summary:**
The paper introduces the Generalist Equivariant Transformer (GET), a novel method for representing and modeling molecular interactions across different domains, including proteins, small molecules, and RNA/DNA. The GET leverages a bilevel graph representation, where each molecule is represented as a set of blocks, and each block contains a set of atoms. This approach allows for the modeling of both domain-specific hierarchies and domain-agnostic interaction physics. The GET is composed of a bilevel attention module, a feed-forward module, and a layer normalization module, all designed to be E(3) equivariant and capable of handling sets of variable sizes. The paper presents extensive experiments demonstrating the effectiveness and generalization capability of the GET across different domains.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for representing molecular interactions, focusing on a unified representation that can be applied across different domains and granularities.
- The proposed GET model, consisting of a bilevel attention module, a feed-forward module, and a layer normalization module, is well-motivated and effectively captures both domain-specific hierarchies and domain-agnostic interaction physics.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components, including the bilevel attention module and the feed-forward module.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness and generalization capability of the proposed method across different domains, including proteins, small molecules, and RNA/DNAs.
- The paper is original in its approach to modeling molecular interactions, providing a novel perspective on how to represent and model these interactions in a unified manner.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of computational resources and training time, which could be a significant limitation for practical applications.
- There is a need for more clarity on the experimental setup, including the specific datasets used, the training and testing set splits, and the hyperparameter settings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, which could include the need for more complex datasets or the potential for overfitting on simpler datasets.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing methods without significant new contributions.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the potential societal impact of the proposed method, which could be a concern for some readers.

**Questions:**
- Can you provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method, including the computational resources required and the training time?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Could you clarify the experimental setup, including the specific datasets used, the training and testing set splits, and the hyperparameter settings?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for overfitting on simpler datasets, and what steps have been taken to mitigate this risk?
- Could you provide more details on the societal impact of the proposed method, and how it addresses any potential ethical concerns?
