 **Summary:**
The paper introduces LEMON, a method for lossless model expansion that enhances the training of large models by leveraging the weights of smaller, pre-trained models. This method is applicable to various network structures, including Vision Transformers and BERT, and employs an optimized learning rate scheduler to reduce computational costs by up to 56.7% for Vision Transformers and 33.2% for BERT compared to training from scratch. The authors demonstrate that LEMON maintains the performance of the larger models while significantly reducing training time. The method is supported by theoretical insights and extensive empirical evaluations, although concerns are raised about its novelty and the depth of theoretical analysis.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the content.
- The proposed method, LEMON, is simple, intuitive, and effective, showing significant performance improvements over existing methods.
- The paper includes comprehensive empirical evaluations that demonstrate the effectiveness of LEMON, especially in reducing computational costs and maintaining model performance.
- The authors provide detailed theoretical insights and ablation studies that support the claims made about the method's effectiveness.
- The methodology is versatile, applicable to various network structures, and can be used in both vision and language domains.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed method, particularly in terms of how the model's expressiveness is maintained and how the learning rate scheduler affects the model's performance.
- The novelty of the method is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without significant new insights or contributions.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent methods, such as LiGO, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method to larger models, which is a critical limitation given the current trend towards larger models.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of the method, which could provide a more balanced view of its capabilities and potential drawbacks.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the rationale behind using different learning rates for different modules in the model, and how this affects the model's performance and generalization capabilities?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and model performance?
- Could the authors provide more detailed theoretical analysis to support the claims made about the method's effectiveness and the impact of the learning rate scheduler on model performance?
- How does the method handle the scalability issues when applied to larger models, and what are the potential solutions for addressing these challenges?
- What are the limitations of the proposed method, and how do these impact its practical applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios?
