 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HELP, a hierarchical explainable latent pooling method designed to enhance the interpretability of graph neural networks (GNNs). HELP employs a novel pooling mechanism that clusters nodes into meaningful subgraphs, enhancing the model's ability to predict graph-level outcomes based on these subgraphs. This methodology allows for a more interpretable approach to GNNs by identifying and explaining the contribution of subgraphs to the final prediction. The paper also proposes a new metric, concept conformity, to assess the noise in discovered concepts. The method is evaluated on various datasets, showing comparable performance to existing methods while providing more interpretable results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and relevant problem in the field of explainable GNNs, focusing on interpretability and explainability, which is crucial for enhancing trust and understanding in AI systems.
- The proposed method, HELP, is a novel hierarchical pooling procedure that is the first non-spectral method to learn graph partitioning end-to-end, enhancing interpretability by design.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it can outperform existing methods in terms of interpretability and performance.
- The use of a new metric, concept conformity, to measure the noise in discovered concepts is a significant contribution, providing a more robust evaluation of the model's performance.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the method's performance and limitations, which aids in understanding the implications and potential applications of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and interpretability. More detailed comparisons, including ablation studies, are necessary to substantiate the claims of superiority.
- The method's computational complexity and scalability are not adequately addressed, raising concerns about its applicability to larger or more complex datasets.
- The paper's presentation and clarity could be improved, particularly in the explanation of key concepts and methodologies, such as the concept of "concept conformity" and the clustering process.
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the experimental results and methodological descriptions, which could affect the reliability and reproducibility of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to node-level predictions and its potential for generalization to other types of graphs.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including ablation studies to demonstrate the superiority of their proposed method?
- How does the computational complexity of the proposed method compare to existing methods, and what are the implications for scalability to larger or more complex datasets?
- Could the authors clarify the concept of "concept conformity" and provide a more intuitive explanation of how it relates to the interpretability of the model's predictions?
- How are the initial clusters defined in the k-means clustering process, and what impact does this have on the interpretability of the results?
- Can the authors provide more detailed examples or visualizations of the discovered concepts and their impact on the model's predictions?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to node-level predictions, and what are the implications for the interpretability of the results in this setting?
