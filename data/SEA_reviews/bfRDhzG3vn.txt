 **Summary:**
The paper introduces COCONUT, a method for continual learning in spoken language understanding (SLU) tasks, leveraging a combination of experience replay and contrastive learning. COCONUT employs two contrastive learning objectives: Negative-Student Positive-Teacher (NSPT) and Multi-Modal (MM), to mitigate catastrophic forgetting and enhance the alignment of audio and text representations. The methodology is evaluated on two SLU datasets, showing improvements over baseline methods. However, concerns are raised about the clarity of the experimental setup, the scalability of the method to larger datasets, and the lack of a comprehensive ablation study.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method, COCONUT, which effectively combines experience replay and contrastive learning for continual learning in spoken language understanding tasks.
- The methodology is well-articulated, with clear descriptions of the proposed method and its components, including the use of contrastive learning and knowledge distillation.
- Extensive experiments are conducted, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various datasets and settings, including a thorough ablation study.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the methodology and results.
- The integration of audio and text modalities in the multi-modal contrastive loss is highlighted as a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the experimental setup, particularly the specifics of the continual learning setup and the choice of datasets.
- The scalability of the method to larger datasets and more complex tasks is not adequately addressed, raising concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed method in real-world scenarios.
- There is a noticeable absence of comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a clearer picture of the method's relative performance.
- The paper suffers from some clarity issues, particularly in the presentation of tables and figures, which could be improved for better readability.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the issue of catastrophic forgetting, which is a critical aspect of continual learning.
- The choice of datasets and the specifics of the continual learning setup (e.g., number of classes per task, sequence of tasks) are not clearly described, making it difficult to assess the generalizability and robustness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the specifics of the continual learning setup, particularly the number of classes per task and the sequence of tasks?
- How does the method perform on larger datasets, and what are the potential challenges or limitations in scaling up the method?
- Can you provide more details on the choice of datasets and the rationale behind using these specific datasets for your experiments?
- How does the method compare with state-of-the-art methods, and what are the specific advantages or disadvantages of the proposed method compared to these methods?
- Could you elaborate on the choice of the temperature parameter and its impact on the performance of the model?
- How does the method handle the issue of catastrophic forgetting, and what steps are taken to mitigate this issue?
- In terms of the experimental results, could you provide more detailed insights into the performance of the model on different tasks and datasets, possibly including additional metrics or visualizations to support your claims?
