 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel technique called CORE (Common Random Reconstruction) for compressing information transmitted between machines in distributed optimization, aiming to reduce communication complexity without requiring strict conditions. CORE projects vector-valued information onto a low-dimensional space using common random vectors and reconstructs it with the same random noises after communication. The technique is applied to convex and non-convex optimization problems, demonstrating lower communication complexities compared to existing methods. The paper presents theoretical results and experimental validations, showing the effectiveness of CORE in reducing communication costs. However, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of empirical validation, particularly in real-world scenarios, and its dependence on strong assumptions about the Hessian matrix.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel technique named CORE, which projects vector-valued information to a low-dimensional space through common random vectors and reconstructs it with the same random noises after communication.
- The CORE technique is applied to two distributed tasks, namely convex optimization on linear models and generic non-convex optimization, demonstrating a significant reduction in communication complexity.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of CORE, including its convergence rate and communication complexity, which are lower than those of existing methods.
- The proposed CORE technique is a feasible compression method that achieves a limited bounded variance of the estimate and provably reduces communication complexity when eigenvalues drop fast.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes a detailed description of the CORE technique and its application in distributed optimization.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical validation of the CORE technique in real-world scenarios, which could demonstrate its practical effectiveness and robustness.
- The paper heavily relies on the Hessian domination assumption, which might not hold for many real-world optimization problems, limiting the applicability of the CORE technique.
- The paper's theoretical analysis assumes that the Hessian matrix is available at each machine, which is not practical in most distributed optimization scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the CORE technique with other compression methods, such as quantization and sparsification, which could help in understanding its advantages and disadvantages.
- The paper's theoretical analysis is limited to centralized optimization settings, which may not generalize well to decentralized optimization scenarios.
- The paper does not discuss the impact of the CORE technique on privacy, which is a significant concern in distributed optimization applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide empirical results to validate the effectiveness of the CORE technique in real-world scenarios?
- How does the CORE technique perform in comparison to other compression methods, such as quantization and sparsification, in terms of communication complexity and convergence rate?
- Can the CORE technique be applied to non-convex optimization problems, and if so, what are the specific conditions that need to be met?
- How does the CORE technique handle scenarios where the Hessian domination assumption does not hold?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of the CORE technique on privacy in distributed optimization applications?
- Is it possible to extend the CORE technique to decentralized optimization settings, and if so, what are the challenges and potential solutions?
