 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel metric, the Box Stability Score (BoS), to evaluate the stability of bounding boxes in object detection models. This metric is derived from the observation that stable bounding boxes under feature dropout indicate good detection accuracy. The authors demonstrate a strong correlation between the BoS and mean average precision (mAP) across various datasets and models, suggesting that the BoS can be used to predict mAP without ground truth data. The methodology involves using Monte Carlo dropout to perturb feature maps and computing the IoU between original and perturbed bounding boxes, which are then matched using the Hungarian algorithm. The paper also discusses the potential of using BoS as an unsupervised evaluation tool for object detection, although it is noted that the correlation between BoS and mAP may not hold in all scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel metric, the Box Stability Score (BoS), which is an interesting and potentially valuable addition to the field of object detection evaluation.
- The correlation between BoS and mean average precision (mAP) is strong and consistent across various datasets and models, suggesting that the BoS can be used as a reliable indicator of detection accuracy.
- The methodology is simple, straightforward, and well-explained, making it accessible and understandable to a broad audience.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a comprehensive literature review and a detailed experimental setup.
- The potential application of the BoS as an unsupervised evaluation tool for object detection is highlighted, which could be particularly useful in real-world scenarios where ground truth data is not available.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential failure cases of the BoS metric, which could undermine its practical applicability.
- The correlation between BoS and mAP is not absolute and may not hold in all scenarios, especially when the detection model's performance is poor.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the BoS metric, which could be a significant drawback in practical applications.
- The experimental setup and the selection of datasets are somewhat limited, focusing primarily on pedestrian and vehicle detection. More diverse and challenging datasets could have been included to strengthen the validity of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous analysis of the relationship between the BoS and mAP, including a discussion on the statistical significance of the correlation and its generalizability across different detection models and datasets.

**Questions:**
- Can the BoS metric be used as a loss function for object detection during training, and how might this affect the training process?
- How does the BoS metric perform when applied to other self-supervised signals, and what are the implications of using different pre-training methods?
- Is there a possibility to use the BoS metric to predict the mAP for multi-class detectors, and how might this be achieved?
- How does the BoS metric compare to other existing metrics in terms of its ability to predict mAP accurately, and what are the implications of using different metrics in practical applications?
- Could the authors provide more detailed insights into the failure cases of the BoS metric and discuss how these might be addressed or mitigated?
- Given the potential limitations of the BoS metric, what are the future directions for research in this area, and how might these advancements benefit the broader community of object detection researchers?
