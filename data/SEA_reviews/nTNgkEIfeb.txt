 **Summary:**
The paper discusses the privacy risks in federated learning (FL) through model inversion attacks (MI), where an attacker, posing as a benign participant, can reconstruct data from other clients. The authors propose FedInverse, a method that leverages the Hilbert-Schmidt independence criterion (HSIC) to enhance the diversity of generated images for MI attacks, thereby potentially improving attack success rates. The paper evaluates FedInverse using various MI attacks on datasets like MNIST and CelebA, demonstrating improved attack success rates. However, concerns are raised about the limited evaluation scope, the need for broader dataset and model diversity, and the potential impact of HSIC on model performance.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the proposed method, FedInverse, and its application in federated learning (FL) settings.
- The experimental results show that FedInverse can improve the attack success rate of model inversion attacks (MI) in FL, indicating the potential effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses an important and relevant issue in FL, focusing on the privacy risks associated with model inversion attacks, which is a significant concern in practical applications.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and examples that aid in understanding the proposed method and its application.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, with limited datasets and models used, which may not fully demonstrate the effectiveness or generalizability of FedInverse.
- There is a lack of discussion on the impact of HSIC on the performance of the model in FL settings, which could be crucial for understanding the trade-offs involved in using FedInverse.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed method to larger-scale FL systems, which is a significant limitation given the potential impact of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately compare the proposed method with other existing methods, particularly in terms of attack success rates and computational efficiency, which could help in understanding the relative performance of FedInverse.
- The paper does not include a discussion on potential defenses against MI attacks in FL, which is a significant omission given the practical implications of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of FedInverse with other existing methods, including a discussion on the impact of HSIC on model performance?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of attack success rates and computational efficiency compared to other MI attack methods?
- Can the authors discuss the scalability of the proposed method to larger-scale FL systems, and how it might be adapted or optimized for such environments?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between privacy and performance in FL settings, particularly in terms of the impact of HSIC on model performance?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the potential defenses against MI attacks in FL, and how these might be integrated or optimized to enhance the security of the system?
