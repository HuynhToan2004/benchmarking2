 **Summary:**
The paper explores the implicit biases of gradient descent (GD) and its variants, specifically focusing on diagonal linear networks. It examines the effects of momentum-based methods, such as Heavy Ball and Nesterov's Accelerated Gradient, on the implicit biases of GD. The study reveals that these methods introduce different implicit biases compared to GD, which can affect generalization performance in sparse regression. The paper also discusses the impact of hyperparameters like learning rate and momentum factor on the implicit biases. Numerical experiments are provided to support the theoretical findings, though concerns about the novelty and depth of the analysis persist.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and provides a clear presentation of the main ideas, making it accessible to readers.
- The study of the implicit bias of GD and its variants, particularly the use of momentum-based methods, is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper includes both theoretical analysis and numerical experiments to support the claims, enhancing the credibility of the findings.
- The paper addresses an interesting and relevant question concerning the implicit biases of GD and its variants, which is of broad interest in the community.
- The analysis of momentum-based methods in the context of diagonal linear networks is novel and provides a deeper understanding of these methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies heavily on the approximation of the continuous time dynamics, which may not accurately represent the behavior of GD in practical settings.
- The novelty of the analysis is questioned as it primarily applies existing results from other works to the specific problem of diagonal linear networks.
- The paper's contribution is seen as limited as it mainly applies existing techniques to a new setting without significant innovation.
- The paper's theoretical results are criticized for not being robust enough, particularly in the handling of the O(η) approximation error.
- The paper's focus on diagonal linear networks is questioned for its relevance to more complex deep neural networks, which are more commonly used in practical applications.
- The paper's numerical experiments are considered insufficient and do not convincingly demonstrate the advantages of momentum-based methods over GD.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the significance of the approximation error in Theorem 1 and discuss its impact on the theoretical results?
- How does the paper's analysis of momentum-based methods in the context of diagonal linear networks compare to similar works in the literature?
- Could the authors elaborate on the differences in the implicit biases of HB and NAG compared to GD, particularly in the context of diagonal linear networks?
- How do the authors justify the choice of focusing on diagonal linear networks, and what insights can be gained from this focus that are not already known from other settings?
- Could the authors provide more detailed numerical experiments to better demonstrate the advantages of momentum-based methods over GD in practical settings?
