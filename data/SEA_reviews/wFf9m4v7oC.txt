 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for estimating causal effects in the presence of unobserved confounding variables using a conditional front-door adjustment (CFD) framework. The authors propose a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) based method, CFDiVAE, to learn the representation of the latent CFD adjustment variable directly from data. The method is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing promising results compared to existing methods. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the identifiability of the learned representations and demonstrates the method's effectiveness in handling unobserved confounding variables. However, it faces criticism for its assumptions, particularly the requirement for at least three independent proxy variables for the latent CFD adjustment variable and the assumption that the distribution of the latent CFD adjustment variable is restricted to the exponential family distribution.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and practical problem of estimating causal effects in the presence of unobserved confounding variables, which is crucial for understanding causal relationships in various domains.
- The introduction of conditional front-door (CFD) adjustment and the CFDiVAE method provides a novel approach to learning the representation of the latent CFD adjustment variable directly from data, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers with varying levels of expertise in causal inference.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms existing methods in estimating causal effects, showing a clear advantage in handling unobserved confounding variables.
- The paper includes a comprehensive literature review and theoretical analysis, which strengthens the credibility and depth of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes that there are at least three independent proxy variables for the latent CFD adjustment variable, which may not be practical or realistic in all scenarios.
- The assumption that the distribution of the latent CFD adjustment variable is restricted to the exponential family distribution is strong and may limit the general applicability of the method.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the assumptions made and the conditions under which these assumptions hold, especially in relation to the identifiability of the CFD adjustment variable.
- The experiments are primarily conducted on synthetic datasets, which may not fully capture the complexities of real-world data, and the real-world dataset used is not extensively discussed or analyzed.
- There are several typographical and notation errors that need correction to enhance the clarity and professionalism of the paper.
- The paper could be more rigorous in its proofs and theoretical analyses, particularly in defining key terms and ensuring the consistency and clarity of the assumptions and proofs.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform when the assumptions of at least three independent proxy variables and the exponential family distribution do not hold?
- Can the authors clarify the necessity and practicality of the assumptions made in the paper, especially in relation to the identifiability of the CFD adjustment variable?
- What are the implications of using a specific distribution for the prior of the latent CFD adjustment variable, and how does this affect the identifiability of the variable?
- How does the method handle the presence of multiple confounders, and what are the implications for the identifiability and estimation of causal effects?
- Could the authors provide more details on the real-world dataset used in the experiments, including the causal graph, the number of samples, and the results of causal discovery?
- How does the method perform in scenarios where the causal graph is unknown or incomplete, and what are the potential biases or errors introduced by such incomplete information?
