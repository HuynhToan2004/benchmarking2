 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PolyGCL, a novel graph contrastive learning method aimed at enhancing the performance of heterophilic graphs. It leverages polynomial filters to capture both low-pass and high-pass spectral information, which is crucial for heterophilic graphs. The method is supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results on synthetic and real-world datasets. PolyGCL is shown to outperform existing methods in both homophilic and heterophilic settings, although its computational complexity and the novelty of its approach are questioned. The paper also discusses the practical implications of the method, including its potential to address the challenges posed by heterophilic graphs in self-supervised learning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive experimental setup that includes multiple real-world datasets.
- The proposed method, PolyGCL, is simple yet effective, and the use of polynomial filters to capture both low-pass and high-pass information is novel and innovative.
- The paper provides both theoretical analysis and empirical results, demonstrating the effectiveness of PolyGCL across different homophily levels.
- The method is shown to be effective in both homophilic and heterophilic settings, and it outperforms existing methods in most cases.
- The paper includes a complexity analysis, which is helpful in understanding the computational efficiency of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the approach is questioned as it appears to be a combination of existing methods, such as GPR-GNN and ChebNetII, with minor modifications.
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, such as those mentioned in related works, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
- The computational complexity of PolyGCL is higher than some existing methods, which could limit its practical applicability in large-scale or resource-constrained environments.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the choice of polynomial filters and the impact of different filter orders on the performance.
- There are some inconsistencies and unclear explanations in the paper, such as the definition of homophily and the rationale behind the choice of polynomial filters.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods to clarify the relative performance of PolyGCL?
- How does the proposed method handle the trade-off between low-pass and high-pass information in heterophilic graphs?
- Could the authors provide more details on the choice of polynomial filters and the impact of different filter orders on the performance?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational complexity and efficiency with other existing methods, especially in large-scale or resource-constrained environments?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the theoretical underpinnings of the method, particularly how the high-pass information enhances the performance in heterophilic graphs?
