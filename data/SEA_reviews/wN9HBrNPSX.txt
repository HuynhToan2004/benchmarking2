 **Summary:**
The paper introduces an incremental training framework for Temporal Knowledge Graphs (TKGs) aimed at handling entities not observed during training or with sparse connections. It proposes a model-agnostic enhancement layer that leverages a broader, global definition of entity similarity and a weighted sampling strategy to enhance the training process. The framework is evaluated on two benchmark datasets, showing improvements in overall link prediction, inductive link prediction, and addressing long-tail entities. However, the paper's novelty is questioned as similar methods have been previously used in other contexts, and the performance improvements are not substantial.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear structure that effectively communicates the research objectives and methodologies.
- The proposed method is innovative, particularly the use of a global definition of entity similarity, which is a novel approach in TKG completion.
- The method is model-agnostic, which enhances its applicability across various TKG completion methods.
- The experiments are comprehensive, including two benchmark datasets specifically designed for incremental learning, which allows for a thorough assessment of the model's capabilities.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in TKG completion, specifically focusing on entities that are either not observed during training or have sparse connections, which is a crucial aspect for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The novelty of the method is questioned as similar approaches have been previously used in other contexts, such as in the continual learning of semantic knowledge graphs.
- The performance improvements are not substantial, with only marginal improvements observed in some experiments.
- The paper does not adequately address how the proposed method handles entities with extremely sparse neighborhoods, which could limit its effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods in the field of TKG completion, which could have provided a clearer picture of the method's relative performance.
- The paper's focus on link prediction and not on entity representation learning might limit its applicability in broader knowledge graph tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific advantages of their proposed method over existing methods, particularly in terms of performance and applicability?
- How does the method handle entities with extremely sparse neighborhoods, and what strategies are employed to mitigate this challenge?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of their method and how these might impact its practical implementation?
- Why was the decision made to focus on link prediction rather than entity representation learning, and how does this choice affect the method's applicability in broader knowledge graph tasks?
- How does the proposed method compare to state-of-the-art methods in the field of TKG completion, and what are the implications of these comparisons for the method's effectiveness and novelty?
