 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Memoria, a novel memory network architecture that incorporates Hebbian theory to enhance the handling of long-term dependencies in Transformer-based models. Memoria is designed to mimic the human memory system, with distinct levels for working memory, short-term memory, and long-term memory, each governed by a Hebbian mechanism that adjusts connection weights based on long-term potentiation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of Memoria through experiments on various tasks, including sorting, language modeling, and text classification, showing improvements over existing methods. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its lack of clarity in methodological details, particularly the role of the memory encoder and the effectiveness of the Hebbian mechanism.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear motivation for the research.
- The concept of Memoria is innovative, integrating Hebbian learning with memory networks, which is a novel approach.
- The paper includes a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including the use of Hebbian theory, which is supported by a comprehensive set of experiments.
- The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various tasks.
- The paper provides a good overview of the literature and discusses the differences between the proposed method and existing models like RNNs and Transformers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in some methodological details, particularly the role and operation of the memory encoder and the connection between different memory levels.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational efficiency of the proposed method compared to existing models, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the scalability of the memory network and the potential for overfitting due to the increased number of parameters.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other models that use similar mechanisms, such as the Memorizing Transformers, to better establish the novelty and effectiveness of Memoria.
- The paper's experimental results, while promising, are not consistently superior across all tasks, and the improvements are sometimes marginal.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and operation of the memory encoder in the Memoria model?
- How does the Memoria model handle the connection between different memory levels, particularly the transfer of information from short-term memory to long-term memory?
- What is the computational complexity of the Memoria model compared to other models like Transformers, and how does this affect the scalability of the model?
- How does the Memoria model handle the potential for overfitting due to the increased number of parameters?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the datasets used and the hyperparameters tuned for the experiments?
- How does the Memoria model compare in terms of performance and efficiency with other models that use similar mechanisms, such as Memorizing Transformers?
