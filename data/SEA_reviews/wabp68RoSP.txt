 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for active learning in chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting for large language models (LLMs). It proposes an approach where the model is queried multiple times to generate answers, and the uncertainty of these answers is used to select the most uncertain questions for annotation by human experts. These questions are then used to enhance the LLM's performance on reasoning tasks. The methodology is evaluated on eight complex reasoning tasks, demonstrating improvements over baseline methods. The paper explores different uncertainty metrics and their impact on model performance, highlighting the importance of selecting the right questions for annotation. However, concerns are raised about the scalability of the method, the need for more diverse datasets, and the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach to active learning in chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The methodology is clearly presented, making it easy to follow and understand, and the experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the uncertainty metrics used, which is crucial for understanding the performance of the model.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and detailed results, making it accessible and valuable for the community.
- The methodology shows promising results, demonstrating the potential for significant improvements in model performance by focusing on the most uncertain questions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the generalizability of the findings and the scalability of the method to larger datasets or different LLM architectures.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodology, particularly in the selection process for the most uncertain questions and the role of human annotation in the process.
- The paper does not include a comparison with other active learning methods, which could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a more diverse set of datasets to test the robustness of the method across different domains and task complexities.
- The paper does not discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant omission in the ethical evaluation of the work.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the selection process for the most uncertain questions and the role of human annotation in this process?
- How does the method perform when using different uncertainty metrics, such as variance or entropy?
- Is there a potential for the method to be adapted to other LLM architectures, such as those based on open-source models like Llama2?
- How does the method handle the transferability of annotated examples to different models or datasets?
- Could you provide more details on the annotation process, particularly how the rational chains and answers are annotated?
- How does the method compare to other active learning methods, especially in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness?
- Can you discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method and how these might be mitigated or addressed?
