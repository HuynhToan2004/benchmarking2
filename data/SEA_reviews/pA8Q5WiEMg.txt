 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the online-within-online (OWO) meta-learning problem, focusing on the initialization and step size of online algorithms in a non-convex setting. It introduces a new algorithm that achieves a better regret bound than previous methods, and provides theoretical analysis on the generalization bounds and PAC-Bayes generalization error bound for statistical multi-task learning. The authors leverage the regret analysis to derive a transfer risk bound for batch meta learning and a PAC-Bayes generalization error bound for statistical multi-task learning. The paper is well-organized and provides a detailed comparison with previous works, although it lacks empirical validation and some technical details are missing.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and good background on online meta learning.
- It provides a new algorithm with a better regret bound than previous works, and introduces a new method to derive generalization bounds for meta learning.
- The paper is technically sound and the proofs are well-organized, making the content accessible and understandable.
- The authors provide a detailed comparison with previous works, highlighting the improvements and innovations of their approach.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in meta learning, which is of great theoretical interest and has practical implications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks empirical validation of the proposed algorithm, which could have strengthened the claims made about its performance.
- There are some technical details that are missing, particularly in the proofs and the definitions of key terms like "piecewise Lipschitzness."
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithms and the assumptions made in the proofs.
- The novelty of the proposed algorithm is not clearly established, and the paper does not adequately discuss how it differs from existing methods.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, which could be a significant limitation in practical applications.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms?
- How does the proposed algorithm perform in terms of runtime and memory usage compared to existing methods?
- What are the limitations of the proposed algorithms, and how do they compare to existing methods in terms of performance and practical applicability?
- Can you clarify the definitions and assumptions used in the proofs, particularly regarding the piecewise Lipschitzness and the assumptions made in the proofs of the main theorems?
- How does the proposed algorithm handle the non-convexity of the loss function, and what are the implications of this for the convergence and generalization properties of the model?
- Could you discuss the potential implications of the results for other settings, such as batch meta learning, and how the findings might be applied in practice?
