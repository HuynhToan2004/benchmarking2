 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel graph learning architecture, Per-Edge Weights (PEW), for predicting maximum link utilization in multi-commodity network flow problems. PEW uses distinct parametrized message functions along each link, which is different from traditional methods that use the same message function for all neighbors. The architecture is evaluated on real-world ISP topologies, showing substantial gains over existing methods. The paper also explores the relationship between graph structure and predictive performance, which is crucial for data-driven routing of flows. The methodology is validated through extensive experiments, demonstrating the effectiveness of PEW in handling large-scale network flows.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed PEW architecture is novel and innovative, particularly in its approach to parametrizing per-edge weights differently, which is not commonly seen in traditional message passing neural networks.
- The experiments are thorough and comprehensive, involving a large number of runs and a variety of network topologies, which strengthens the validity of the results.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the relationship between graph structure and predictive performance, which is crucial for understanding the impact of network topology on routing efficiency.
- The introduction of the PEW architecture represents a significant contribution to the field, potentially enhancing the performance of graph neural networks (GNNs) in routing problems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The novelty of the PEW architecture is questionable as it appears to be a minor modification of existing methods like RGAT.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, especially those that use different types of graph neural networks or other machine learning techniques.
- There is a lack of discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the PEW architecture, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability in large-scale networks.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the findings beyond the specific routing schemes and topologies used in the study.
- The paper's claims about the expressivity of the PEW architecture are not substantiated with sufficient theoretical or empirical evidence.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the PEW architecture, particularly in terms of its applicability to larger, more complex networks?
- How does the PEW architecture compare to other state-of-the-art methods, especially those that use different types of graph neural networks or other machine learning techniques?
- Could the authors elaborate on the novelty of the PEW architecture and how it differs significantly from existing methods like RGAT?
- What are the specific advantages of using the PEW architecture over other existing methods, and how does it address the limitations of traditional GNNs in routing problems?
- How does the PEW architecture handle the generalizability of its findings to other routing schemes and topologies beyond those used in the study?
- Can the authors provide more detailed theoretical or empirical evidence to support the claims made about the expressivity of the PEW architecture?
