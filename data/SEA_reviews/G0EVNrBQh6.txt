 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the presence of human-identifiable features in adversarial perturbations, specifically focusing on the masking and generation effects. It employs various attack algorithms and datasets to demonstrate that averaging adversarial perturbations across multiple neural networks enhances the visibility of these features, potentially leading to misclassification by humans. The study also explores the transferability of adversarial examples and their impact on model interpretability. Despite the novelty in focusing on human-identifiable features, the paper's methodology and the novelty of its findings are questioned due to similarities with existing works and the lack of rigorous experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the experimental setup and results.
- The focus on human-identifiable features in adversarial perturbations is a novel and interesting approach, potentially opening new avenues for understanding and mitigating adversarial attacks.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of adversarial perturbations, which is crucial for understanding the behavior of adversarial examples and their impact on model performance.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and a logical flow of ideas, making it accessible to readers.
- The results are presented in a clear and concise manner, with figures and tables effectively illustrating the findings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough literature review, missing out on significant related works that could provide a richer context for the study.
- The novelty of the findings is questioned, as similar observations about human-identifiable features in adversarial perturbations have been made in previous works.
- The experimental setup and methodology are not clearly described, which makes it difficult to replicate the results or understand the experimental conditions fully.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its methodology, such as the choice of datasets and the potential biases introduced by averaging adversarial perturbations.
- The paper's claims about the impact of averaging adversarial perturbations on human-identifiable features are not convincingly supported by the evidence presented.
- The paper's findings are not sufficiently novel or surprising, as the presence of human-identifiable features in adversarial perturbations has been previously reported.
