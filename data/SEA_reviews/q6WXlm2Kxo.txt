 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to self-supervised pre-training for semantic segmentation using a masked diffusion model (MDM). It replaces the traditional Gaussian noise in diffusion models with a masking mechanism, aiming to improve representation learning for segmentation tasks. The MDM is trained using a structural similarity index (SSIM) loss, which is compared to the standard mean squared error (MSE) loss in prior works. The model is evaluated on both medical and natural image datasets, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods in few-shot scenarios. The paper also discusses the limitations of the model, including its current application to U-Net architecture and the need for further validation on larger, more diverse datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to self-supervised pre-training for semantic segmentation using a masked diffusion model (MDM), which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
- The MDM shows promising results in few-shot scenarios, particularly in medical image segmentation, demonstrating its potential for label-efficient tasks.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- The use of SSIM loss instead of MSE loss in the reconstruction process is a notable contribution, as it better aligns with the structural information needed for segmentation tasks.
- The experimental results are robust, showing that MDM outperforms other self-supervised methods and traditional segmentation methods in various datasets.
- The paper addresses the issue of mismatch between generative pre-training and downstream tasks, which is a significant challenge in current models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claims about the superiority of the MDM over DDPM are not well-supported, especially in terms of the generation quality of the MDM.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets and do not include a broader range of datasets, which could affect the generalizability of the results.
- The use of the SSIM loss is not convincingly justified, and the paper does not sufficiently compare the MDM with other state-of-the-art models like Stacked VAEs and MAE.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the MDM, such as its application only to U-Net architecture and its performance on larger, more diverse datasets.
- There are inconsistencies and unclear descriptions in the paper, particularly in the methodology and experimental setups, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational efficiency of the MDM compared to other models, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Questions:**
- How does the MDM handle the computational efficiency compared to other models, especially in real-world applications where computational resources are limited?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art models like MAE and Stacked VAEs, particularly in terms of segmentation performance and computational efficiency?
- Why was the decision made to use the SSIM loss instead of the MSE loss, and how does this choice affect the model's performance and applicability?
- How does the MDM perform on larger, more diverse datasets, and what are the implications of its current application only to U-Net architecture?
- Could the authors clarify the inconsistencies and unclear descriptions in the paper, particularly in the methodology and experimental setups, to improve the clarity and understanding of the proposed method?
