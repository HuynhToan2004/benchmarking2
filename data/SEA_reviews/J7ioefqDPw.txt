 **Summary:**
The paper explores the vulnerability of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to label poisoning attacks, proposing a novel evaluation framework to assess the robustness of GNNs against such attacks. The authors identify several pitfalls in previous evaluations and introduce two new attacks: linear surrogate attacks and meta attacks. These attacks are evaluated against existing defenses and demonstrate significant improvements in attack effectiveness. The paper also discusses the limitations of previous evaluations and provides a theoretical analysis of the attacks' effectiveness. The proposed framework is designed to provide a more reliable and faithful evaluation of GNNs' robustness against label poisoning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in the field of GNNs, specifically focusing on the vulnerability of GNNs to label poisoning attacks.
- The proposed evaluation framework is comprehensive, systematic, and provides a novel approach to assessing the robustness of GNNs against label poisoning attacks.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed technical descriptions of the proposed attacks and theoretical analysis.
- The authors have identified several pitfalls in previous evaluations and provide a detailed discussion on these issues, contributing to a more reliable and faithful evaluation of GNNs' robustness against label poisoning attacks.
- The paper introduces two new attacks that are simple yet effective, and the experiments demonstrate significant improvements in attack effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed attacks, which could be crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the proposed attacks with other existing attacks, which could help in understanding the relative effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications and feasibility of the proposed attacks in real-world scenarios.
- The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that the proposed attacks are transferable to other models, which is a significant limitation for the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's reliance on small-scale datasets and the lack of discussion on the scalability of the proposed methods to larger datasets could limit the applicability of the findings.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed methods, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.

**Questions:**

- How do the proposed attacks compare in terms of effectiveness and practical applicability with other existing attacks, particularly in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more evidence to support the claim that the proposed attacks are transferable to other models?
- How do the proposed attacks perform on larger, more complex datasets, and what are the computational and scalability implications of the proposed methods?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the practical implications and feasibility of the proposed attacks in real-world scenarios?
