 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the alignment of brain activity across individuals using a shared response model (SRM) to enhance the prediction of neural responses to a spoken podcast using GPT-2-XL contextual embeddings. The authors propose using SRM to denoise individual neural responses and demonstrate improved performance in predicting shared information features. They also evaluate the generalization of the SRM across new subjects and its effectiveness in different brain areas, showing significant improvements in the superior temporal gyrus and inferior frontal gyrus. The study also explores the application of SRM in reducing noise in individual responses and its potential to generalize to new subjects, albeit with some limitations in the experimental setup and the clarity of the results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach using a shared response model (SRM) to analyze neural responses across multiple subjects, which is a significant advancement in the field of brain activity modeling.
- The methodology is well-presented, making it easy to follow and understand, which is crucial for reproducibility and further research.
- The application of SRM to denoise individual brain responses is highlighted as a novel and potentially impactful finding, with significant improvements observed in the superior temporal gyrus and inferior frontal gyrus.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the SRM's performance, including its ability to generalize to new subjects and its effectiveness in different brain areas.
- The authors demonstrate the potential of SRM to improve the alignment of brain activity across individuals, which is crucial for understanding the neural basis of language comprehension.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental setup is limited, with only eight subjects and a single stimulus (a podcast), which may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the SRM across different subjects and stimuli.
- The paper lacks a clear comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, making it difficult to assess the relative advantages of the proposed approach.
- The results are not consistently presented, with some figures and tables (e.g., Figure 1 and Table 2) being unclear or inadequately explained, which could hinder the understanding of the findings.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the study, such as the potential impact of individual differences on the effectiveness of SRM and the generalizability of the findings to other types of stimuli or subjects.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential applications and implications of the SRM in brain signal decoding and brain-computer interfaces.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the differences in the performance of SRM in denoising neural responses across different brain areas, particularly the superior temporal gyrus and inferior frontal gyrus?
- How does the SRM perform when applied to different types of stimuli, such as spoken sentences versus written text?
- What are the specific advantages of using SRM over other methods like principal component analysis (PCA) or other linear encoding models in terms of predicting brain activity?
- Could you provide more details on the behavioral experiment mentioned in Section 2.2 and how it relates to the main findings of the study?
- How does the SRM generalize to new subjects, and what are the implications of this generalization for the application of SRM in brain-computer interfaces or other clinical settings?
- Could you elaborate on the potential applications of the SRM in brain signal decoding and how it could be used to enhance the accuracy of decoding in clinical or research settings?
