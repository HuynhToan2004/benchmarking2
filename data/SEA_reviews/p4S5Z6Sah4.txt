 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel recurrent neural network (RNN) architecture, termed the wave-RNN, which exhibits traveling waves in its hidden state. This architecture is designed to mimic the wave-like dynamics observed in the brain, particularly in the context of short-term memory storage and sequence learning. The wave-RNN is tested against traditional RNNs and other state-of-the-art models across various tasks, demonstrating superior performance in memory tasks and comparable performance in sequence classification tasks. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been criticized for its limited experimental scope, particularly in terms of the complexity and diversity of the tasks tested.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel wave-RNN architecture that exhibits traveling waves in its hidden state, which is a significant advancement in understanding the computational implications of wave-like dynamics in the brain.
- The proposed wave-RNN is tested against traditional RNNs and other state-of-the-art models, demonstrating superior performance in memory tasks and comparable performance in sequence classification tasks.
- The paper provides a clear and comprehensive presentation of the wave-RNN architecture and its experimental results, which are supported by detailed ablation studies and comparisons with other models.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of the wave-RNN, demonstrating its ability to solve complex sequence tasks and its potential for further development in the field.
- The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the wave-RNN architecture and its implications for memory storage and sequence learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on memory tasks and does not adequately demonstrate the wave-RNN's capabilities in more complex or diverse tasks, such as language modeling or other sequence tasks.
- The experimental results are somewhat limited in scope, with the paper primarily testing the wave-RNN on small-scale tasks and not comparing it against a broader range of models or tasks.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the wave-RNN architecture, such as its potential scalability issues or its applicability to more complex or varied tasks.
- There is a lack of discussion on the relationship between the wave-RNN and other models that exhibit traveling waves, such as the Neural Wave Machine (NWM), which could have provided a more comprehensive understanding of the wave-RNN's capabilities and limitations.
- The paper's claims about the wave-RNN's ability to store short-term memory are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which primarily focus on sequence classification tasks rather than memory tasks.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations of the wave-RNN's performance on more complex or varied tasks, such as language modeling or other sequence tasks?
- How does the wave-RNN compare to other models that exhibit traveling waves, such as the Neural Wave Machine (NWM)? What are the key differences between these models, and how do they impact the wave-RNN's performance and capabilities?
- In terms of scalability, how does the wave-RNN perform on larger or more complex tasks, such as those involving longer sequences or more varied input data?
- How does the wave-RNN's performance compare to other models that use different approaches to short-term memory storage, such as those based on different neural network architectures or training methods?
- Could the authors provide more detailed insights into the wave-RNN's memory storage capabilities, perhaps through additional experiments or simulations that directly test its ability to store and retrieve information over time?
