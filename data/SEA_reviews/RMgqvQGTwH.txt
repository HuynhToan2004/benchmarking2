 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel hybrid reinforcement learning (RL) algorithm that integrates on-policy actor-critic methods with offline data, aiming to leverage the benefits of both on-policy and off-policy learning. This approach, termed Hybrid Actor-Critic (HAC) and Hybrid Natural Policy Gradient (HNPG), is designed to maintain theoretical guarantees of on-policy NPG while also utilizing offline data to enhance performance. Theoretical analysis and empirical results, including in challenging environments like the combination lock problem, demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness. The paper also discusses the challenges of integrating on-policy and off-policy learning, and provides a detailed theoretical framework for the proposed algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to hybrid RL by combining on-policy and off-policy learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including proofs of the proposed algorithms and a detailed discussion on the assumptions and guarantees.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and illustrations, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms in challenging environments, showing superior performance compared to baseline methods.
- The paper addresses the limitations of previous hybrid RL methods by focusing on on-policy learning, which is a promising direction for future research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implementation of the proposed algorithms, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and scalability.
- There is a lack of comparison with more recent and advanced off-policy algorithms, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed algorithms, such as their applicability in different MDP settings and the potential for overfitting in offline datasets.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently detailed, with some figures being hard to read and interpret, and there is a lack of discussion on the standard deviations and failure rates of the algorithms.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion on the related works, especially in the context of hybrid RL and the integration of on-policy and off-policy learning.

**Questions:**
- Could you provide more details on the practical implementation of the proposed algorithms, including computational efficiency and scalability?
- How do the proposed algorithms handle different MDP settings, especially in terms of their applicability and performance?
- Can you discuss the potential for overfitting in offline datasets and how the algorithms address this issue?
- How do the proposed algorithms compare to more recent and advanced off-policy algorithms, and what are the specific advantages of the proposed methods?
- Could you clarify the definitions and assumptions used in the theoretical analysis, particularly in relation to the Bellman completeness assumption and the transfer coefficient?
- How do the algorithms perform in different environments, and what are the implications of the theoretical guarantees in practical scenarios?
