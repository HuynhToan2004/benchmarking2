 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the use of Wasserstein distance for self-supervised learning, specifically focusing on simplicial representation learning using a SimCLR-like framework. It introduces a new methodology that utilizes a Jeffrey divergence regularization to address the non-differentiability of the 1-Wasserstein distance. The paper compares this approach with traditional methods such as cosine similarity and real-valued vector embedding, demonstrating its effectiveness through empirical results on various datasets. However, the paper is criticized for its limited scope, lack of novelty, and insufficient comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method is simple and easy to implement, with clear explanations of the challenges and solutions in the methodology.
- The paper introduces a novel approach using Jeffrey divergence regularization to address the non-differentiability of the 1-Wasserstein distance, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The empirical results are robust, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in simplicial representation learning.
- The paper provides a clear and comprehensive literature review, effectively situating the research within the existing body of knowledge.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of the tree structure and the regularization methods used.
- The novelty of the approach is questionable, as the use of Wasserstein distance for contrastive learning and simplicial representation learning has been explored in previous works.
- The empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing methods, particularly in terms of performance on larger datasets like CIFAR100.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as its applicability to larger datasets or its computational efficiency.
- The paper could benefit from more extensive experiments and comparisons with a broader range of datasets and models to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the proposed method differs from existing works, particularly in terms of the tree structure and regularization methods used?
- How does the proposed method perform on larger datasets, such as CIFAR100, and what are the implications of its performance on such datasets?
- Could the authors clarify the computational efficiency of the proposed method, particularly in comparison to existing methods?
- Are there any potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how might these be mitigated or addressed?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing methods, including a head-to-head comparison of the results?
