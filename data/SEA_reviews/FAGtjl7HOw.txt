 **Summary:**
The paper explores the interpretability of kernel k-means clustering by introducing an interpretable feature map that approximates the implicit feature map in the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). This approach allows for the construction of decision trees from the kernel k-means, preserving the interpretability of the clustering results. The paper proposes several algorithms, including Kernel IMM and greedy cost minimization algorithms like Kernel ExKMC and Kernel Expand, to enhance the interpretability of the clustering results. Theoretical and empirical evaluations are provided to support the proposed methods, although the paper has been critiqued for its clarity, the practicality of its assumptions, and the lack of comprehensive experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to interpretable kernel k-means by using a surrogate feature map, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided is robust, and the paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow.
- The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by focusing on the interpretability of kernel k-means, which is a crucial aspect for practical applications.
- The authors have provided a clear definition of interpretable decision trees and have discussed the limitations of kernel k-means, which are not often discussed in previous works.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear introduction and detailed explanation of the proposed methods, which are supported by theoretical guarantees.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly in the explanation of the surrogate feature map and the construction of the decision tree, which could be improved with more detailed explanations and examples.
- The assumptions made in the paper, such as the boundedness of the kernel and the differentiability of a specific function, may not hold for all types of kernels, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The experimental evaluation is limited, and the paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other interpretable clustering methods, especially those that do not rely on kernel methods.
- The paper could be more self-contained and less reliant on external references for understanding the main contributions and results.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the proposed methods, which is a significant concern for large-scale datasets.
- The paper's claims about the interpretability of the clustering results are not convincingly supported by empirical evidence, which could undermine the credibility of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition of the surrogate feature map and provide examples of its construction?
- How do the authors ensure that the surrogate feature map remains interpretable in practical scenarios, especially when the kernel k-means clustering is not optimal?
- Can the authors provide more details on the construction of the decision tree from the surrogate feature map and the reference clustering?
- How do the authors address the scalability issues of their proposed methods, particularly in the context of large-scale datasets?
- Could the authors provide more empirical evidence to support the claim that the proposed methods lead to interpretable clustering results?
- Is there a possibility to extend the proposed methods to other types of kernels, such as those that are not bounded or differentiable?
- How does the paper's approach compare to other interpretable clustering methods, especially those that do not rely on kernel methods?
