 **Summary:**
The paper introduces TransFace, a novel framework for direct talking head translation, which converts audio-visual speech from one language to another without relying on text or audio. It employs a speech-to-unit translation model and a unit-based audio-visual speech synthesizer to achieve this. The framework includes a Bounded Duration Predictor to ensure isometric talking head translation and prevent jarring video transitions. The authors claim that TransFace achieves a notable improvement in synchronization and inference speed compared to existing methods. However, the paper's experimental setup and the novelty of the approach have been questioned, with concerns about the lack of comparison with relevant baselines and the clarity of the experimental results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel framework for direct talking head translation, which is a significant advancement in the field of audio-visual speech synthesis.
- The use of a Bounded Duration Predictor is innovative and helps ensure isometric talking head translation, which is crucial for maintaining synchronization and avoiding jarring video transitions.
- The proposed unit-based audio-visual speech synthesizer, Unit2Lip, is a significant contribution to the field, offering a parallel synthesis method that enhances the speed of audio-visual speech synthesis.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental results, making it accessible and understandable.
- The experiments conducted demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements in synchronization and inference speed compared to existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with relevant baselines, particularly in terms of synchronization and inference speed, which limits the evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness.
- The novelty of the approach is questioned, as it appears to be a combination of existing methods without significant innovation.
- The experimental setup and results are not clearly explained, making it difficult to understand the methodology and the implications of the results.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the paper, with some sections being difficult to follow and understand.
- The paper does not adequately address the generalization ability of the proposed method across different languages and dialects.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the Bounded Duration Predictor in ensuring isometric talking head translation?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of synchronization and inference speed with other relevant baselines, such as those mentioned in the paper?
- What are the specific advantages of using a unit-based audio-visual speech synthesizer over other existing methods, and how does it enhance the speed of audio-visual speech synthesis?
- How does the proposed method handle the generalization ability across different languages and dialects, especially those with distinct phoneme sets?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and results, particularly how the synchronization and inference speed were measured and what the results indicate?
- What are the implications of the findings in the paper for practical applications, such as in real-world translation scenarios?
