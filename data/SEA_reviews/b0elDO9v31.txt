 **Summary:**
The paper explores the theoretical underpinnings of intrinsic convolutions on surfaces, focusing on the choice of priors in the construction of these convolutions. It builds on existing frameworks by Monti et al. (2017) and Bronstein et al. (2021) to develop a novel class of intrinsic mesh CNNs, emphasizing the Dirac prior for its ability to learn a broad range of features. The theoretical contributions are supported by empirical evaluations on the FAUST dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of the Dirac prior in learning surface features. The paper also discusses the implications of different priors on the learnability of features and proposes a framework for comparing various intrinsic convolutions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise sections that make it easy to follow.
- It introduces a novel class of intrinsic mesh CNNs, which are expected to be beneficial in various applications.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the Dirac prior, which is used to learn features on the surface, and presents a clear definition of the class of Intrinsic Mesh CNNs.
- The experiments conducted on the FAUST dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of the Dirac prior in learning surface features.
- The paper is significant in its exploration of the theoretical foundations of intrinsic convolutions, which is crucial for advancing research in geometric deep learning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive review of existing works, particularly those that have already explored the use of Dirac priors in similar contexts.
- The experiments conducted are limited to shape correspondence, and it is unclear how the proposed framework would perform in other applications such as semantic segmentation or classification.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost or the scalability of the proposed framework, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The theoretical analysis is not sufficiently rigorous, with some parts relying on intuitive arguments rather than rigorous mathematical proofs.
- The paper does not discuss the limitations of its methodology, which could help in understanding the scope and applicability of the proposed framework.
- There are several typographical errors and inconsistencies in the manuscript, which could affect the readability and the credibility of the research.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of their work with existing studies that have used Dirac priors in similar contexts?
- How does the proposed framework perform in other applications beyond shape correspondence, such as semantic segmentation or classification?
- Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost and scalability of their framework?
- Is there a specific reason why the experiments were limited to the Dirac prior, and could other priors be considered in future work?
- How does the proposed framework handle the computational challenges associated with the Dirac prior, especially in practical implementations?
- Could the authors clarify the theoretical underpinnings of the Dirac prior and its implications for the learnability of features on surfaces?
