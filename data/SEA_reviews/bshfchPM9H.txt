 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Rapper, a two-stage method designed to address the implausibility and hallucination issues in Natural Language Explanation (NLE) for Visual Question Answering (VQA) tasks. The first stage employs a large language model (LLM) to generate pseudo rationales, which are then filtered using a QA model. The second stage uses a visual-grounded rationale generator to refine these rationales, and a reasoning module to generate the final answer and explanation. Rapper is evaluated on two VL-NLE benchmarks, showing improvements over existing methods in plausibility and faithfulness metrics. However, the paper's experimental results are not convincing, and the novelty of the approach is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the significant issue of implausibility and hallucination in Natural Language Explanation (NLE) for Visual Question Answering (VQA), which is crucial for enhancing the performance and interpretability of VQA systems.
- The proposed method is well-motivated, with a clear and logical structure, and is supported by extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in generating plausible and faithful NLE.
- The use of reinforcement learning to refine the rationale generator is innovative and shows potential for improving the quality of NLE.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including both quantitative and qualitative results, which helps in understanding the performance of the proposed method across different scenarios.
- The methodology is well-presented, making it easy to follow, and the paper includes a detailed ablation study, which is beneficial for understanding the impact of different components of the model.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the pseudo rationales generated by the LLM, which is a critical component of the proposed method. More detailed analysis and comparison with ground truth rationales could strengthen the paper.
- The experimental results do not convincingly support the claims of the paper, particularly the improvements in implausibility and hallucination. The paper could benefit from a more rigorous evaluation, including human evaluations and comparisons with other metrics like MMLU.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper's methodology is not entirely novel, as similar approaches have been previously explored in other works, which raises questions about the true innovation of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from a clearer presentation of the experimental results, particularly in tables and figures, to improve readability and understanding.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more detailed analysis and comparison of the pseudo rationales generated by the LLM with ground truth rationales?
- How does the proposed method compare with other metrics like MMLU, particularly in terms of hallucination detection?
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the QA model in the filtering process of pseudo rationales?
- How does the proposed method handle the potential for hallucination in the LLM's generated rationales?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their method and how these might affect its applicability and effectiveness in different scenarios?
- In terms of experimental design, could the authors consider including human evaluations or other types of evaluations to better assess the practical impact of the proposed method?
