 **Summary:**
The paper introduces Differentiable Tree Search (DTS), a novel neural network architecture that integrates a best-first online search algorithm into a world model, aiming to enhance the performance of reinforcement learning in environments with limited training data. The architecture is designed to learn a robust world model by optimizing the search algorithm and the learnt world model concurrently, thereby mitigating the impact of model inaccuracies. The methodology includes a stochastic tree expansion policy and variance reduction techniques to address potential Q-function discontinuities and high variance in gradient estimates. The paper presents experiments in Procgen and a navigation task, demonstrating that DTS outperforms model-free and model-based baselines. However, concerns were raised about the clarity of the paper's presentation and the need for more comprehensive empirical validation across a broader range of environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel architecture, Differentiable Tree Search (DTS), which effectively combines the best-first search algorithm with a neural network to optimize the world model and the search algorithm concurrently.
- The proposed method addresses the challenges of suboptimal performance in decision-making problems with limited training data by learning a world model from the limited data and determining actions through online search.
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and includes comprehensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The use of a stochastic tree expansion policy and variance reduction techniques for gradient computation is innovative and addresses potential issues in the Q-function and gradient estimates.
- The method shows promise in improving sample efficiency and generalization capabilities, which is crucial for reinforcement learning in complex environments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and scope.
- There is a lack of clarity in the paper, particularly in the explanation of the architecture and the methodology used. The paper would benefit from clearer explanations and additional visual aids to aid comprehension.
- The empirical results are not convincing, with limited environments used for testing, and the results do not consistently outperform existing methods like TreeQN across all environments.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experiments, including comparisons with other model-based methods and a broader range of environments to validate the generalizability of the proposed method.
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the mathematical notation and equations used, which could affect the understanding and reproducibility of the results.
- The paper could be improved by addressing the computational demands of the method and providing a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and memory requirements.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the role and impact of the auxiliary losses in the training process?
- How does the proposed method compare to other model-based methods in terms of computational efficiency and generalization capabilities?
- Can the authors provide more detailed results and comparisons in the Procgen environment, particularly in terms of the Z-score and other performance metrics?
- How does the method handle stochastic environments, and what modifications are necessary for such scenarios?
- Can the authors discuss the potential of extending the method to continuous control tasks, and what modifications would be required for such applications?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational demands of the method, including the computational complexity and memory requirements?
- Can the authors clarify the mathematical notation and equations used in the paper to ensure their correctness and clarity?
