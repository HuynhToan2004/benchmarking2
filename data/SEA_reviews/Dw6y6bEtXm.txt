 **Summary:**
The paper introduces PICL, a novel framework designed to enhance the predictive capabilities of models operating on coarse-grained data in physical systems modeling. PICL integrates a learnable fine-grained state representation from coarse-grained data, which is crucial for predicting subsequent coarse-grained observations. The framework is divided into two stages: a base-training period and a two-stage fine-tuning period, employing both labeled and unlabeled data to optimize model performance. The authors claim that PICL outperforms existing methods in predicting coarse-grained observations, though concerns about the novelty and clarity of the methodology are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the methodology is clearly explained.
- The motivation behind the research is compelling, addressing the challenge of using coarse-grained data in physics-informed machine learning.
- The proposed method, PICL, shows promising results in predicting coarse-grained observations from coarse-grained data, which is a significant advancement in the field.
- The paper includes ablation studies and sensitivity analyses, providing insights into the impact of different parameters and data qualities on the model's performance.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods in certain scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational cost and performance under different data qualities and sizes.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodology, with some sections and figures (e.g., Figure 1) not being adequately explained, which could confuse readers.
- The paper does not adequately address the novelty of the work, as it appears to be a combination of existing techniques without sufficient differentiation from prior art.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly superior to existing methods, particularly in terms of predictive accuracy and computational efficiency.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and does not provide sufficient guidance on how to apply the method in different scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including computational cost and performance under different data qualities and sizes?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of predictive accuracy and computational efficiency to existing methods, especially under conditions of insufficient or low-quality data?
- Could the authors clarify the role and impact of different parameters (e.g., γ, β, α) in the model's performance, and provide a more detailed explanation of their roles and effects?
- How does the method perform when fine-tuning is not possible, and what are the implications of this limitation on the model's performance?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the methodology, particularly in sections where the methodology is not clearly explained, such as Figure 1 and the implementation of the FNO in the encoding module?
