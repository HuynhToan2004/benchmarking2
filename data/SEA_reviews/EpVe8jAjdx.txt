 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method, SCAFFOLDER, for leveraging privileged information in reinforcement learning (RL) settings, particularly in model-based RL (MBRL) contexts. SCAFFOLDER extends DreamerV3 by integrating privileged information into the training of the world model, the critic, and the policy, aiming to improve learning outcomes. The method is evaluated on a new benchmark, S3, which includes ten simulated robotics tasks, and the results demonstrate that SCAFFOLDER outperforms baseline methods. The paper also discusses the use of privileged information in various components of the RL training process, including the world model, critic, exploration policy, and representation learning, and provides a detailed analysis of the benefits of each component.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a well-defined problem setting.
- The proposed method is simple and intuitive, yet effective, as demonstrated by strong results across multiple tasks in the S3 benchmark.
- The use of privileged information in RL is a novel approach, and the paper provides a detailed analysis of how different components of the method utilize this information.
- The paper includes a new benchmark, S3, which is well-defined and provides a good testbed for evaluating the proposed method and other related approaches.
- The method is well-motivated and provides a comprehensive analysis of the method's components, which helps in understanding the impact of different parts of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to real-world robotic systems and its generalizability to other types of RL settings.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the description of the method and the experimental results, which could benefit from more detailed explanations and visual aids.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other related works, such as those that use similar privileged information in RL settings.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the method, particularly in terms of the architecture and training details, which could affect the reproducibility and understanding of the results.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, particularly in the context of real-world robotics applications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the privileged information is used in the training of the world model, particularly in terms of the input and output of the model?
- How does the method perform when using only a subset of the privileged information, and what are the implications of this for the learning outcomes?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and implementation of the method, particularly in terms of the hyperparameters and training settings used?
- How does the method perform in more complex or realistic settings, such as those with more varied or noisy privileged information?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with other related works, particularly those that use similar privileged information in RL settings?
- How does the method handle the issue of the imitation gap, particularly in tasks where the privileged information may not be directly applicable to the target observations?
