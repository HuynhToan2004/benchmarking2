 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the use of distilled datasets to guide Iterative Magnitude Pruning (IMP) for finding sparse architectures in neural networks. By using distilled data to initialize pruning, the authors aim to improve the stability and performance of the pruned models. The study explores the stability of these models through visualizations of loss landscapes and Hessian analysis, demonstrating that distilled pruning can lead to smoother landscapes and better generalization. However, the paper's novelty and the depth of its analysis are questioned, with concerns about the practical applicability and the scope of the experiments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the figures are well-designed to aid understanding.
- The authors provide a thorough analysis of the stability of subnetworks, which is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the pruning process.
- The use of distilled datasets to guide pruning is a novel approach that has been shown to lead to smoother loss landscapes and better generalization.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear sections that facilitate understanding of the complex concepts discussed.
- The authors provide code and distilled datasets, enhancing reproducibility and accessibility for further research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical applicability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its scalability and efficiency in real-world scenarios.
- The novelty of the work is limited as the use of distilled datasets for pruning is not a new concept.
- The experiments are limited to smaller models and datasets, which may not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in more complex scenarios.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other pruning methods, such as LQF, which could have strengthened the argument for the superiority of the proposed method.
- The analysis of the loss landscape and Hessian matrix is not sufficiently detailed, and the conclusions drawn are not convincingly supported by the data presented.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the study, which could have provided a more balanced perspective on the findings.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the practical implications of using distilled datasets for pruning in larger, more complex models? What are the potential challenges and limitations in such scenarios?
- How does the proposed method compare to other pruning methods, particularly in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specifics of the distilled datasets used and the conditions under which the experiments were conducted?
- In Figure 7, the performance of the proposed method seems to be lower than that of the baseline. Could you explain this discrepancy?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to different types of datasets, such as those with different levels of complexity or diversity?
- Could you elaborate on the conclusions drawn from the analysis of the loss landscape and Hessian matrix? Are these conclusions supported by a comprehensive analysis of the data?
