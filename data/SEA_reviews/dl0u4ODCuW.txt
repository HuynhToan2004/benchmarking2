 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to retrosynthesis planning by incorporating uncertainty into the reaction feasibility and buyability models. This approach allows for the estimation of the probability of a synthesis plan being successful, termed the "successful synthesis probability" (SSP). The authors propose a new algorithm, retro-fallback, which maximizes SSP by expanding nodes with the highest probability of leading to a successful synthesis plan. The algorithm is tested on a benchmark of 500 molecules, demonstrating its effectiveness in generating backup plans. However, the paper's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods, and the clarity of its presentation and the experimental setup are noted as issues.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear motivation and a clear presentation of the problem and the proposed solution.
- The introduction of a new evaluation metric, the successful synthesis probability (SSP), is a novel approach that considers the uncertainty of reaction feasibility and buyability.
- The proposed algorithm, retro-fallback, is a natural and effective solution for the SSP problem, with a clear description and explanation of its components.
- The paper includes detailed derivations and theoretical results, which are helpful in understanding the proposed method.
- The paper's approach is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of how the proposed algorithm addresses the challenges of retrosynthesis planning.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is questioned due to its similarity to existing methods, particularly the use of Monte Carlo simulations for uncertainty estimation.
- The experimental setup is not clearly explained, particularly the selection criteria for the molecules used in the experiments and the comparison with existing methods.
- The paper's novelty is questioned, as the proposed algorithm is seen as a modification of existing methods rather than a fundamentally new approach.
- The paper's clarity and organization could be improved, particularly in the presentation of the algorithm and the experimental results.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method, especially in terms of the computational complexity and the feasibility of the algorithm in practical settings.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Can the authors clarify the selection criteria for the molecules used in the experiments and explain why these molecules were chosen?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of multiple synthesis plans, especially in cases where the backup plans are not ideal?
- Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specific conditions under which the experiments were conducted and the results obtained?
- How does the proposed method handle the uncertainty in the reaction feasibility and buyability models, especially in practical settings where these models may not be perfect?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential ethical implications of their work and how they address these concerns?
