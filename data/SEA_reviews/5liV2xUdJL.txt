 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for training stateful policies in reinforcement learning (RL) environments, focusing on the use of a stochastic stateful policy gradient (S2PG) to replace traditional Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT) methods. The S2PG approach decomposes the stateful policy into a stochastic internal state kernel and a stateless policy, which are jointly optimized to avoid issues like vanishing or exploding gradients and biased policy updates. Theoretical analyses and empirical results are provided to support the effectiveness of S2PG, showing improvements in training efficiency and performance on complex tasks. The paper also discusses the potential of S2PG to scale with task complexity and its applicability to various RL algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a significant problem in reinforcement learning by proposing a novel approach to training stateful policies without the need for Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT), which is known for its computational inefficiencies and issues with gradient stability.
- The method is well-motivated and supported by a theoretical analysis that provides new insights into the variance of the gradient estimator, which is crucial for understanding the performance of the proposed approach.
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the theoretical underpinnings and practical applications of the proposed method, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over traditional BPTT methods in terms of training efficiency and performance.
- The paper is significant as it presents a new method for training stateful policies, which is a crucial area of research in reinforcement learning, and provides a new gradient estimator that is both unbiased and efficient.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the choice of using stochastic nodes in the computational graph, which is a critical aspect of the proposed method. The rationale behind this choice and its impact on the training process are not thoroughly explored.
- The experimental results, while showing improvements over BPTT, do not consistently outperform other state-of-the-art methods, which could limit the perceived effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive experimental evaluations, including a broader range of tasks and environments to better demonstrate the generalizability and robustness of the proposed method.
- The paper's reliance on stochastic nodes for gradient estimation could introduce additional variance, which may not be fully addressed by the current methodology.
- There is a lack of discussion on the practical implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, which could limit the applicability of the findings to real-world scenarios.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in terms of training time and computational efficiency, to better establish the advantages of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of why stochastic nodes were chosen in the computational graph and how they affect the training process?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of training time and computational efficiency with other state-of-the-art methods, especially in more complex environments?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the stochastic nodes in gradient estimation and discuss potential methods for reducing the variance introduced by these nodes?
- How does the proposed method perform in environments with different types of state-action value functions, such as those based on Markovian or non-Markovian dynamics?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of tasks and the parameters used in the experiments?
- In light of the theoretical analysis, can the authors discuss the practical implications of the assumptions made in the analysis and how they affect the applicability of the findings?
