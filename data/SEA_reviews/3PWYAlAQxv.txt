 **Summary:**
The paper explores the universal approximation property (UAP) of permutation-trained neural networks, focusing on the approximation of one-dimensional continuous functions using a two-layer permutation-trained ReLU network. The theoretical analysis demonstrates that such networks can approximate any continuous function on the unit interval, provided the weights are permuted appropriately. The paper also presents numerical experiments to validate the theoretical results, showing that the network can achieve the UAP even with random initialization. The proofs are based on a divide-and-conquer algorithm and a pseudo-copy technique, which are used to construct a piecewise constant function and eliminate unused parameters. The paper also discusses the implications of these findings for practical applications, such as in hardware implementations and other network architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and understandable proofs that effectively illustrate the theoretical concepts.
- The proofs are novel and interesting, particularly the use of the four-pair construction of step function approximators, which is a novel approach in the field.
- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the universal approximation property (UAP) of permutation-trained neural networks, which is a significant contribution to the understanding of these networks.
- The paper includes numerical experiments that support the theoretical results, enhancing the credibility of the findings.
- The paper addresses the interesting problem of the universal approximation property of permutation-trained networks, which is a relatively unexplored area in neural network research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on one-dimensional continuous functions, which may limit its applicability to higher-dimensional functions or more complex network architectures.
- The proofs rely on specific assumptions, such as the equidistant initialization of parameters, which may not generalize well to other types of initializations.
- The paper does not provide a detailed discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical results, such as how the findings could be applied in real-world scenarios.
- There is a lack of comparison with other training methods, which could help in understanding the advantages and disadvantages of permutation training.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the results and the practical feasibility of the proposed training methods.
- The paper does not address the computational complexity or the scalability of the proposed methods, which are important considerations for practical applications.
