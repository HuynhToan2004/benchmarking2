 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of catastrophic forgetting in large language models (LLMs) when finetuned on noisy datasets, focusing on the forgetting of biased and toxic content. It introduces a novel approach, the ForgetFilter algorithm, which filters out unsafe data based on the model's forgetting signal, thereby enhancing safety during finetuning. The paper also explores the impact of model size on forgetting behavior and evaluates the algorithm against various safety metrics and downstream tasks. The study reveals that larger models exhibit more pronounced forgetting of unsafe content, suggesting that this could be a significant concern in real-world applications where LLMs are finetuned on noisy data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a critical and timely issue of catastrophic forgetting in large language models (LLMs) finetuned on noisy datasets, focusing on the safety implications of this behavior.
- The experimental design is well-structured and the results are clearly presented, with a focus on the impact of model size on forgetting behavior.
- The introduction of the ForgetFilter algorithm, which filters out unsafe data based on the model's forgetting signal, is a novel and potentially impactful contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive review of related work that positions the study effectively within the existing literature.
- The experimental setup is well-designed, including a variety of safety metrics and a thorough evaluation of the ForgetFilter algorithm against alternative strategies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the practical implications of the findings, particularly in real-world scenarios where LLMs are finetuned on noisy data.
- The evaluation of the ForgetFilter algorithm is limited to a few datasets and safety metrics, which may not fully capture the algorithm's effectiveness in diverse scenarios.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational costs associated with the ForgetFilter algorithm, which could be a significant concern in practical applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the study, including the potential biases introduced by the use of specific datasets and the generalizability of the findings to other models or datasets.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods for mitigating catastrophic forgetting and a more thorough exploration of the underlying mechanisms that influence forgetting behavior.

**Questions:**
- How does the ForgetFilter algorithm perform when applied to different types of noisy data, such as data with varying levels of bias or toxicity?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational costs associated with the ForgetFilter algorithm, including the training time and the resources required?
- How does the performance of the ForgetFilter algorithm vary with different hyperparameters, such as the learning rate and the number of training epochs?
- Can the authors provide examples or case studies that demonstrate the practical application of the ForgetFilter algorithm in real-world scenarios?
- How does the ForgetFilter algorithm compare to other methods for mitigating catastrophic forgetting, particularly in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
- What are the underlying mechanisms that influence the forgetting behavior observed in LLMs, and how do these mechanisms vary across different models or datasets?
