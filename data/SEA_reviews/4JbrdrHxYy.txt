 **Summary:**
The paper explores the integration of CLIP and SAM to address the challenges in object boundary detection, a critical aspect often overlooked in existing models like DINO and SAM. By leveraging CLIP's semantic understanding and SAM's instance segmentation capabilities, the authors propose a novel method to identify object boundaries and enhance the performance of instance segmentation tasks. The approach is evaluated on the COCO dataset, showing improvements over existing methods in annotation-free object detection and instance segmentation. However, concerns about the novelty of the method and its generalizability to different datasets are raised.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible.
- The integration of CLIP and SAM is a novel approach that effectively utilizes the strengths of both models to enhance instance segmentation capabilities.
- The method is evaluated on the COCO dataset, demonstrating improvements over existing methods in annotation-free object detection and instance segmentation.
- The paper provides a clear and concise explanation of the methodology, including the use of semantic-aware initialization and the clustering of features from a specific middle layer of CLIP to outline object boundaries.
- The results show a significant improvement over previous methods, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the method is limited as it primarily combines existing models without significant innovation.
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the limitations and potential failure cases of the proposed method, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its performance.
- The method's generalizability is questionable, as it is evaluated only on the COCO dataset and not on other standard benchmarks like Pascal VOC or MS COCO.
- The paper does not adequately address how the method performs on different datasets, particularly those with varying densities and clutter levels, which could affect the segmentation results.
- The computational efficiency of the method is not discussed, which is crucial for practical applications where computational resources are limited.
- There is a lack of clarity in some sections of the paper, particularly in the explanation of how the method handles different scenarios and the impact of initialization on the results.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method perform on different datasets, particularly those with varying densities and clutter levels?
- Can the method be adapted to handle different object scales, and how does it perform with varying object sizes?
- What are the potential failure cases of the proposed method, and how do these impact the segmentation results?
- How does the method handle occlusions and overlapping objects, and what are the strategies for handling such scenarios?
- Is there a possibility to use other models besides SAM for instance segmentation, and how might this affect the performance?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the method and discuss any potential optimizations?
- How does the method perform when using different initialization strategies, and what are the implications of using different initialization methods?
- Can the authors clarify the method's performance on other datasets and provide more detailed experimental results to support the claims made in the paper?
