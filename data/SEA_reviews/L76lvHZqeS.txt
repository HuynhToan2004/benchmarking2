 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a unified theoretical framework for robust contrastive losses in the context of noisy labels, focusing on the InfoNCE loss and proposing a novel SymNCE loss by integrating a Reverse InfoNCE (RevNCE) term. The framework aims to enhance the robustness of contrastive learning under label noise, which is a significant challenge in machine learning applications. The theoretical analysis provides insights into the robustness of contrastive losses under different noise conditions, and the paper includes empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of SymNCE over existing methods. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited experimental scope, focusing primarily on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, and its assumptions about symmetric label noise which may not hold in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel Symmetric InfoNCE (SymNCE) loss by combining the InfoNCE loss with a Reverse InfoNCE (RevNCE) loss, which is a significant theoretical contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided in the paper is comprehensive, rigorous, and supported by a robust set of experiments.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making complex concepts accessible to a broader audience.
- The proposed SymNCE loss is shown to outperform other robust losses in experiments, demonstrating its practical utility.
- The paper addresses an important problem of learning with noisy labels, which is crucial for many real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's theoretical framework heavily relies on assumptions of symmetric label noise, which may not hold in practical scenarios, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, which may not adequately represent more complex or varied data sets.
- The paper does not include experiments with different backbone architectures or different data augmentation methods, which could affect the robustness and applicability of the proposed methods.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and memory requirements of the proposed methods, which are crucial for practical deployment.
- The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with the latest state-of-the-art methods, which could undermine the perceived novelty and effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors elaborate on how the theoretical framework would need to be modified to handle asymmetric label noise?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with the latest state-of-the-art methods, including both robust supervised classification losses and robust supervised contrastive losses?
- How does the proposed SymNCE loss perform when combined with different data augmentation methods or different backbone architectures?
- What are the computational complexity and memory requirements of the proposed methods, and how do they compare with existing methods?
- The paper mentions the use of a hyperparameter β to balance robustness and accuracy. Can the authors provide more insights into how this parameter should be set in practice, especially in real-world scenarios?
