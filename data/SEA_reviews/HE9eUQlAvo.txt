 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for selecting and trimming training data based on influence functions, aiming to enhance model performance in terms of utility, fairness, and robustness. It utilizes decision trees to estimate data influences and proposes a data trimming strategy to improve model performance by removing data points that negatively impact model performance. The approach is tested across various datasets and scenarios, including adversarial robustness, fairness, and active learning, showing potential improvements in model performance. However, the paper is criticized for its lack of clarity in defining "data influence," the absence of a clear algorithmic description, and insufficient comparisons with existing methods. The experimental results, while promising, are seen as limited by the complexity of the models used and the scope of the evaluations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to data selection using influence functions, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The methodology is clearly presented, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The paper is well-written and includes comprehensive experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper addresses the important problem of selecting the right data for machine learning models, which is crucial for improving model performance and reducing the negative impacts of misleading data.
- The proposed method is simple, interpretable, and effective, as shown by the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks clarity in defining "data influence," which is a critical concept for the study. A clearer definition or a more detailed explanation of how data influence is measured would be beneficial.
- The experimental results are limited in scope and depth, focusing primarily on simpler models like logistic regression and decision trees. More complex models like neural networks were not evaluated, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's effectiveness.
- The paper does not adequately compare its method with existing methods, particularly those that also use influence functions for data selection. This lack of comparison makes it difficult to assess the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the proposed method handles different types of data or how it scales with larger datasets, which are crucial for practical applications.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which is essential for understanding its practical applicability.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation or definition of "data influence" to clarify its meaning and measurement in the context of the study?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of data, such as structured data like images or text, which might require different data processing techniques?
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method with larger datasets? How does the computational complexity of the method scale with the size of the dataset?
- How does the proposed method compare with existing methods that use influence functions for data selection? What are the key differences and advantages of the proposed method over these existing methods?
- The paper mentions that the method is effective in improving model performance. Could the authors provide more detailed insights into how the method achieves this improvement? For example, what specific aspects of the data are being targeted by the method, and how does this impact the model's performance?
- The paper uses a surrogate model for non-convex models. How does this approach handle the complexity and non-linearity of these models, and what are the implications for the model's performance and interpretability?
