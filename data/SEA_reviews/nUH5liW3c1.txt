 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel supervised contrastive learning objective function, SCHaNe, which combines supervised contrastive learning with hard negative sampling to optimize the selection of positive and negative samples. This method prioritizes hard negative samples, enhancing the model's performance without requiring specialized architectures or additional resources. The SCHaNe approach is evaluated across various datasets, including few-shot and full-dataset fine-tuning scenarios, showing consistent improvements over baseline methods. However, the novelty of the approach is questioned due to similarities with existing methods like Supervised Contrastive Learning (SupCon) and the use of hard negative mining, which has been previously explored.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel supervised contrastive learning objective function, SCHaNe, which effectively utilizes hard negative sampling to enhance model performance.
- The methodology is clearly explained, and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.
- The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results in various benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
- The experimental results are convincing, showing improvements over baseline methods in both few-shot and full-dataset fine-tuning scenarios.
- The paper is well-organized, with a clear introduction of the proposed objective function and its components, making it easy to understand the methodology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not clearly distinguish its contributions from existing methods like SupCon, particularly in the use of hard negative mining, which has been previously explored.
- The novelty of the paper is limited, as the combination of supervised contrastive learning with hard negative sampling is not a new concept.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of its applicability and effectiveness.
- There is a lack of comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in few-shot learning, which could have strengthened the paper's claims.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational overhead introduced by the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which could be a concern for ethical considerations.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the proposed method differs from existing methods like SupCon, particularly in the use of hard negative mining?
- How does the proposed method perform when applied to other types of models, such as graph neural networks or convolutional neural networks?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and its applicability in different scenarios?
