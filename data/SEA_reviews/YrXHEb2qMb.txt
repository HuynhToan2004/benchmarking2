 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for posterior sampling in Bayesian inverse problems using conditional MMD flows with negative distance kernels. It leverages Wasserstein gradient flows to model the conditional distributions, providing theoretical insights into the error bounds and stability of the posterior distributions. The method is demonstrated through numerical experiments on various datasets, including MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR10, and real-world imaging tasks such as super-resolution and CT reconstruction. The approach aims to improve upon existing methods by offering a more stable and theoretically grounded approach to generative modeling.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel method for posterior sampling in Bayesian inverse problems using conditional MMD flows with negative distance kernels, which is a new approach in the field.
- It provides a theoretical analysis of the error bounds and stability of the posterior distributions, which is crucial for understanding the reliability and accuracy of the proposed method.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the theoretical results and their implications.
- The authors have demonstrated the effectiveness of their method through various experiments, including conditional image generation, super-resolution, and CT reconstruction, which showcase the practical applicability of the proposed approach.
- The paper provides a comprehensive literature review, highlighting the connections between the proposed method and existing works, which helps position the research within the current state of the art.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods that use Wasserstein gradient flows, which might lead to confusion about the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are not sufficiently robust or convincing, with some figures (e.g., Figure 2) showing poor quality or misleading comparisons.
- There is a lack of clarity in the presentation of the theoretical results, particularly in the proofs and the explanation of certain assumptions and results, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility of the work.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for practical applications in high-dimensional or complex inverse problems.
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous comparison with existing methods, including a broader range of datasets and metrics to better establish the effectiveness and generalizability of the proposed approach.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how their method differs from existing approaches that use Wasserstein gradient flows, particularly in terms of stability and error bounds?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational complexity and scalability, especially in high-dimensional or complex inverse problems?
- Could the authors provide more detailed explanations or examples to illustrate the theoretical results, particularly in the proofs and the assumptions used?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of computational efficiency and effectiveness with other existing methods for Bayesian inverse problems?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including metrics such as FID, PSNR, and SSIM, to better assess the performance of their method across different datasets and scenarios?
