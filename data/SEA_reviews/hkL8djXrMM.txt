 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel framework for diffusion models, termed Neural Diffusion Models (NDMs), which generalizes conventional diffusion models by incorporating a learnable transformation of the data distribution. This transformation is optimized using a variational bound in a simulation-free setting, enabling the derivation of time-continuous formulations for efficient inference. The paper demonstrates the utility of NDMs through experiments on various datasets, showing improvements in negative log-likelihood and FID scores compared to baseline models. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited novelty, incremental technical contributions, and lack of significant performance improvements over existing models like DDPM.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the proposed method and its implementation, with detailed derivations in the appendix.
- The proposed method is a generalization of conventional diffusion models, which is both novel and technically sound, enabling the derivation of time-continuous formulations for efficient inference.
- The experiments demonstrate the utility of the proposed method, showing improvements over baselines in terms of negative log-likelihood and FID scores.
- The method is flexible and can be applied to different types of data distributions, which is highlighted through the experiments on datasets such as MNIST, CIFAR10, and ImageNet.
- The paper is original in its approach to learning the forward process, which is a significant contribution to the field of diffusion models.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art diffusion models, which limits the evaluation of its performance against current advancements in the field.
- The novelty of the proposed method is questioned, as it appears to be a straightforward extension of existing methods without significant new insights or technical contributions.
- The paper does not provide sufficient experimental results to support the claims made, particularly in terms of the effectiveness of the learned transformations and the performance improvements over baseline models.
- The method's reliance on a learnable transformation of the data distribution increases the number of parameters, which could lead to slower training times and potentially overfitting, especially on smaller datasets.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- There are several typographical and formatting errors in the paper, which detract from its professional presentation.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare with recent state-of-the-art diffusion models in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including visualizations of the learned transformations and comparisons with baseline models on larger, more diverse datasets?
- How does the method perform when applied to different types of data distributions, such as text or audio?
- What are the specific advantages of using NDMs over other types of diffusion models, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and sample quality?
- How does the method handle the issue of overfitting, especially on smaller datasets where the number of parameters is high?
- Can the authors clarify the role and effectiveness of the learned transformations in improving the performance of the model?
