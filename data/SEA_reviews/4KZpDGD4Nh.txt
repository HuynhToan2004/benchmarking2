 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to compositional generalization in object-centric world modeling, integrating neurosymbolic grounding to enhance the model's ability to generalize across different compositions of objects. The framework, named Cosmos, employs a neurosymbolic attention mechanism that binds entities to learned rules of interaction, leveraging a vision-language foundation model to compute symbolic attributes of objects. This approach allows the model to generalize to novel compositions of known visual "atoms," which is crucial for handling complex environments. The methodology is evaluated on the 2D Block Pushing Domain, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods. However, the paper's scope is limited to this domain, and there are concerns about the scalability and applicability of the method to more complex, real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to compositional generalization in object-centric world modeling, integrating neurosymbolic grounding to enhance the model's ability to generalize to novel compositions of known visual "atoms."
- The framework, Cosmos, leverages a vision-language foundation model to compute symbolic attributes of objects, which is a novel and interesting approach.
- The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a comprehensive review of related work.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method achieves better performance than baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
- The paper is the first to leverage vision-language foundation models to compute symbolic attributes for objects, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily focuses on the 2D Block Pushing Domain and does not evaluate the method on more complex, real-world scenarios, which limits the understanding of its applicability and effectiveness in more complex environments.
- There is a lack of clarity regarding the specific contributions of the paper, particularly how the use of a vision-language foundation model enhances the model's ability to generalize to novel compositions of known visual "atoms."
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could provide a more balanced view of its strengths and weaknesses.
- The methodology and some of the experimental results (e.g., the AE-MSE in the 5-object case) are not well-explained or justified, which could lead to confusion or misinterpretation of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability and efficiency of the proposed method, especially in more complex or large-scale scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the specific contributions of the paper and how the use of a vision-language foundation model enhances the model's ability to generalize to novel compositions of known visual "atoms"?
- How does the proposed method handle different compositions of objects, especially in more complex or real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors provide more details on the training and evaluation of the model, particularly the number of seeds used and the metrics reported?
- What are the implications of using a vision-language foundation model to compute symbolic attributes for objects, and how does this approach compare to traditional methods of symbolic attribute computation?
- How does the proposed method handle the scalability and efficiency issues, especially in more complex or large-scale scenarios?
