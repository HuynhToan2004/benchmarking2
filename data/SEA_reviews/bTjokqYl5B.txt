 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the phenomenon of robust overfitting in adversarial training, focusing on the role of normal data in this process. It proposes two methods, OROAT_AS and OROAT_DA, to mitigate robust overfitting by either removing adversarial perturbations or augmenting data to balance robustness between training and test sets. The paper provides empirical evidence that robust overfitting is primarily caused by non-effective features derived from normal data, which can lead to a widening robustness gap between training and test sets. The proposed methods aim to address this by either eliminating non-effective features or aligning robustness across datasets, thus potentially improving adversarial robustness. However, the paper's approach is seen as somewhat heuristic and lacks a strong theoretical foundation, which could limit its applicability and generalizability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the robust overfitting phenomenon, which is crucial for understanding and improving the robustness of adversarial training.
- The authors have conducted extensive experiments across various datasets, network architectures, and adversarial training variants to validate their findings.
- The paper introduces two novel methods, OROAT_AS and OROAT_DA, which are orthogonal and aim to address robust overfitting from different angles.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed methods are simple and effective, with empirical results showing significant improvements in adversarial robustness across different baselines.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a strong theoretical foundation and instead relies heavily on empirical evidence, which may limit its generalizability and applicability to other scenarios.
- The methodology of OROAT_AS and OROAT_DA is somewhat heuristic and not well-explained, particularly in terms of how they address the root causes of robust overfitting.
- The paper's experimental setup and results are somewhat limited, focusing primarily on small-scale datasets and not adequately addressing the scalability of the proposed methods to larger, more complex datasets.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity and practical implications of the proposed methods, especially in terms of their scalability and real-world applicability.
- The paper's claims regarding the causes of robust overfitting and the effectiveness of the proposed methods are not convincingly supported by the experimental results, particularly in the context of larger perturbation budgets.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how OROAT_AS and OROAT_DA address the root causes of robust overfitting?
- How do the proposed methods perform in scenarios with larger perturbation budgets, and what are the implications for the robustness of the trained models?
- Are there any theoretical guarantees or insights that can be provided to support the claims made in the paper?
- How do the proposed methods compare in terms of computational complexity and practical applicability to existing methods for mitigating robust overfitting?
- Could the authors clarify the experimental setup and results, particularly in terms of the scalability and generalizability of the findings?
