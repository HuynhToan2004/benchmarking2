 **Summary:**
The paper introduces ToolEmu, a framework designed to emulate tool execution using language models (LMs) to assess the safety and helpfulness of large language models (LLMs) in various scenarios. The framework includes an automatic safety evaluator and an adversarial emulator, which are tested against a benchmark of 36 toolkits and 144 test cases. The evaluations show that the framework can identify failures that are both realistic and genuinely risky, with a precision of 72.5% and a true failure incidence of 39.6%. The paper also explores the limitations of the framework, including the potential for minor issues in emulation and the need for further validation with more LLMs and test cases.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach using language models (LMs) to emulate tool execution, which is a significant advancement in the field of LLM-based agents.
- The framework is well-designed, with clear presentation and detailed validation of its components, including an emulator, an adversarial emulator, a safety evaluator, and a helpfulness evaluator.
- The evaluation is robust, with a benchmark of 36 toolkits and 144 test cases, which provides a comprehensive assessment of the framework's capabilities.
- The paper addresses an important problem in the community by focusing on the safety and helpfulness of LLM-based agents, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The use of an LM-based emulator and automatic evaluators allows for scalable testing and risk assessment, which is a significant advantage over traditional manual testing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the framework, which is critical for understanding the scope and applicability of the proposed solutions.
- The evaluation is limited to a few LLMs, which may not fully represent the capabilities or limitations of different LLMs.
- There is a potential for minor issues in emulation, such as incorrectly emulating generic entries like phone numbers, which could affect the accuracy and reliability of the emulation.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the ethical implications of the work, particularly in the context of the potential risks associated with the deployment of LLM-based agents.
- The paper's reliance on human validation for critical issues and the agreement between human annotators is a significant limitation, and more detailed validation and discussion of these aspects would be beneficial.

**Questions:**
- How does the framework handle scenarios where the LLM-based agent's actions are not explicitly defined in the tool specifications?
- Can the framework be adapted to emulate tools that are not typically used in the context of LLM-based agents, such as physical tools like robots or medical equipment?
- How does the framework ensure the consistency of emulation across different LMs, and what are the implications of using different LMs for emulation?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the effectiveness of the adversarial emulator in identifying failures, and how these results compare to the standard emulator?
- How does the framework handle scenarios where the LLM-based agent's actions are ambiguous or open-ended, and what are the implications for the safety and helpfulness evaluations?
- Can the authors clarify the methodology used for the human validation and the agreement between human annotators, and discuss the potential sources of disagreement or inconsistency in the evaluations?
