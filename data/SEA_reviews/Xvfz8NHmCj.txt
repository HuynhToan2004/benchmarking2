 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel semi-supervised continual learning setting called "CL on Diet," which addresses the challenges of limited computational budget and sparse label rates in continual learning. It proposes a baseline method, DietCL, which effectively integrates unlabeled data with labeled data to enhance the efficiency of learning. The methodology is evaluated on several datasets, demonstrating that DietCL outperforms existing supervised and semi-supervised continual learning algorithms. The paper is praised for its clarity, thorough experiments, and the innovative approach to addressing computational and labeling constraints in continual learning. However, concerns are raised regarding the novelty of the method and the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the impact of different labeling rates.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and realistic setting of continual learning with a limited computational budget and sparse labeling, which is relevant to many real-world applications.
- The proposed method, DietCL, is simple yet effective, with a well-motivated approach to utilizing unlabeled data for continual learning.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with detailed explanations and comprehensive experimental results.
- The experimental results are promising, showing that DietCL outperforms existing methods across various datasets.
- The use of MAE for unlabeled data pre-training is innovative and helps in understanding the performance of the proposed method.
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of the impact of different label rates and computational budgets on the performance of DietCL.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and the impact of different labeling rates.
- The novelty of the method is questionable as it seems to be a combination of existing techniques without significant innovation.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of sparse labeling, which is a critical aspect of the proposed setting.
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the experimental results and methodology, such as the use of unlabeled data for training and the handling of labeled data in different stages of training.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of DietCL.
- The paper's focus on a specific dataset (ImageNet10k) and its limited variety in labeling rates (only 1% and 5%) may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the specific role and impact of the MAE pre-training on the performance of DietCL?
- How does the proposed method compare with state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and the handling of sparse labeling?
- Can the authors provide more detailed explanations and results on the impact of different labeling rates on the performance of DietCL?
- How does the proposed method address the issue of sparse labeling, and what are the implications for the generalizability of the findings?
- Could the authors discuss the potential impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of DietCL and suggest any tuning strategies?
- In the context of the proposed method, how does the computational budget affect the performance, and what are the implications for practical applications?
