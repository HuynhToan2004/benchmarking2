 **Summary:**
The paper introduces DRSM, a novel certified defense for malware detection using de-randomized smoothing. It addresses the challenges of adversarial attacks in malware detection by segmenting input samples into non-overlapping windows and training a base classifier for each window. The DRSM framework ensures robustness against attacks by requiring a large margin of votes for the correct class, thus providing a theoretical lower bound for attack success. The paper also introduces a new dataset, PACE, which includes 15.5K recent benign raw executables, aimed at enhancing research in malware detection. The DRSM methodology is evaluated against various attacks, demonstrating its effectiveness in maintaining high accuracy while providing robustness against adversarial perturbations.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel defense mechanism, DRSM, which is a certified defense for malware detection, providing robustness against adversarial perturbations.
- DRSM is a simple yet effective method that does not require retraining of models, making it accessible and practical for real-world applications.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The introduction of the PACE dataset, which contains 15.5K benign raw executables, is a significant contribution to the field, potentially benefiting future research in malware detection.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the DRSM methodology, comparing it with existing methods and demonstrating its effectiveness in maintaining high accuracy while providing robustness against adversarial attacks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations and potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method.
- The evaluation of the DRSM methodology is limited to a few models, and it would be beneficial to see how it performs against other models and different types of malware.
- The paper does not address the adaptive attacks, which could be a significant limitation in practical scenarios where attackers may adapt their strategies to bypass the defense.
- There is a lack of clarity on how the DRSM methodology handles different types of malware, particularly those that may not fit neatly into the window ablation scheme.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples of how the DRSM methodology handles different types of malware and adversarial attacks.
- The computational complexity of the DRSM methodology is not discussed, which could be a concern for real-world applications where computational resources are limited.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the DRSM methodology handles different types of malware, particularly those that may not fit neatly into the window ablation scheme?
- How does the DRSM methodology ensure robustness against adaptive attacks, where the attacker adapts their strategy to bypass the defense?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the DRSM methodology and discuss how it scales with the size of the dataset and the complexity of the malware samples?
- How does the DRSM methodology handle malware that is not present in the training set but is similar in structure to existing malware?
- Could the authors discuss the potential negative societal impacts of the DRSM methodology and how these could be mitigated?
- It would be beneficial to see how the DRSM methodology performs against a wider range of models and different types of malware. Could the authors consider expanding the evaluation to include more diverse scenarios?
