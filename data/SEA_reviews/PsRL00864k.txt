 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach to accent conversion by treating it as a sequence-to-sequence task at the semantic token level, using a combination of a conversion module and a speech neural codec-based generative model. The conversion module, trained on a large amount of target accent speech, converts source-accent semantic tokens into target-accent semantic tokens, which are then used to generate speech with target accent prosody. The model is trained on a pretext task using a large amount of target accent speech and a small amount of parallel accent data. The proposed method shows promising results in converting Indian-English to general American-English, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance in terms of accent similarity, speech quality, and speaker maintenance. However, the evaluation is limited to a single source and target accent pair, and the generalizability of the method to other accents is not thoroughly tested.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important and challenging problem of accent conversion, which is a significant research area.
- The proposed method is innovative, treating accent conversion as a sequence-to-sequence task on the semantic token level, which is a novel approach.
- The methodology is well-described, making it easy to follow, and the paper provides a clear explanation of the proposed method.
- The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance in terms of accent similarity, speech quality, and speaker maintenance, which is impressive.
- The use of a pretraining method to learn the probability space of the target accent domain with large amounts of target accent speech available is a promising approach.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear description of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the method's generalizability to other accent pairs beyond Indian-English to general American-English.
- The evaluation is limited to a single source and target accent pair, which may not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness across different accent pairs.
- The method's dependence on the availability of a large amount of target accent speech for pretraining may limit its applicability to less common accent pairs.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the training and inference of the conversion module, which is crucial for reproducibility.
- The paper's claims about the generalizability of the method are not adequately supported by experiments.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of speaker identity, which is a significant concern in accent conversion.
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, which could have strengthened the paper's argument.

**Questions:**
- How generalizable is the proposed method to other accent pairs beyond Indian-English to general American-English?
- Can the authors provide more details on the training and inference of the conversion module, including hyperparameters and training data details?
- How does the method perform when applied to other accent pairs, such as Chinese-English or Korean-English to general American-English?
- Could the authors clarify the method's approach to maintaining speaker identity in the accent conversion process?
- How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance and efficiency?
- Can the authors provide more details on the pretraining and finetuning stages, including the amount and quality of data used?
- How does the method handle the issue of speaker identity in the accent conversion process?
