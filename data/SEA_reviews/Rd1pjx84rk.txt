 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the size generalization capabilities of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) using a data-driven approach focused on real-world biological datasets. It identifies that the spectrum differences induced by graph size correlate with subgraph patterns, specifically average cycle lengths. The authors propose three model-agnostic strategies to enhance GNNs' size generalizability: self-supervision, augmentation, and size-insensitive attention. These strategies are evaluated against various GNN backbones and other model-agnostic strategies, demonstrating improvements in performance on large test graphs. The paper also provides a spectral analysis perspective, highlighting the importance of cycle-related information in reducing spectral differences between small and large graphs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a data-driven approach by focusing on real-world biological datasets, which is a significant strength in understanding the size-induced distribution shifts in graphs.
- The authors adopt a spectral perspective, which is a novel approach for investigating the size generalizability of GNNs.
- The proposed model-agnostic strategies, such as self-supervision, augmentation, and size-insensitive attention, are simple yet effective in enhancing the size generalizability of GNNs.
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise sections, and includes a comprehensive discussion of related work, making it accessible to readers.
- The experimental results are robust and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed strategies, with a detailed analysis of the impact of cycle-related information on GNNs.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough literature review, particularly in the area of size generalization in GNNs, which would provide a more comprehensive context for the research.
- The description of the dataset split and the methodology used in the experiments are not sufficiently detailed, which could hinder reproducibility and understanding of the experimental setup.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed strategies and potential negative societal impacts, which are crucial for a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods.
- The paper's focus on graph classification tasks may limit the generalizability of the findings to other types of graph learning tasks.
- The paper does not include a discussion on the limitations of the proposed strategies and potential negative societal impacts, which are crucial for a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods.
