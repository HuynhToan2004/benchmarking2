 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the capabilities of auto-regressive next-token predictors, specifically focusing on their ability to approximate any Turing machine-computable function. It introduces a new complexity measure, termed "length complexity," which quantifies the number of intermediate tokens required to approximate a target function. The authors demonstrate that even simple models like linear predictors can learn complex tasks effectively when trained on chain-of-thought (CoT) data. They also explore the relationship between length complexity and other complexity measures, showing how different architectures like linear and MLP models perform on text generation and arithmetic tasks. Despite the theoretical contributions, the paper's experimental results are seen as underwhelming, particularly in terms of the model's performance compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a new complexity measure, length complexity, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical framework for studying auto-regressive next-token predictors is well-articulated, providing a clear structure for understanding the capabilities of these models.
- The paper demonstrates that even simple models like linear predictors can approximate complex functions efficiently, which is a compelling result.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental setup is detailed, which aids in reproducibility and validation of the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's experimental results are underwhelming, particularly the performance of the linear model on the TinyStories dataset, which is much less impressive than claimed.
- The theoretical analysis of the paper is seen as straightforward and does not sufficiently differentiate the proposed framework from existing methods.
- The paper does not sufficiently explore the relationship between length complexity and other complexity measures, which could have provided a deeper understanding of the model's capabilities.
- The paper lacks a discussion on the limitations of the proposed framework, particularly in terms of its applicability to more complex tasks or different types of data.
- There is a lack of discussion on how the results of the paper could be applied in practical scenarios, such as improving the performance of existing models.

**Questions:**
- Could you clarify the relationship between the length complexity and other complexity measures, particularly how these measures interact and influence each other?
- How does the paper's framework compare to other existing methods, particularly in terms of its novelty and effectiveness?
- Can you provide more details on the training data used for the linear model, especially how it was generated and whether it was used to train the MLP model as well?
- How does the paper's framework apply to more complex tasks or different types of data, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in these scenarios?
- Could you discuss the implications of the results for the development of more effective or efficient models, and how the findings might be used to improve existing models?
