 **Summary:**
The paper introduces HOMER, a novel training-free method aimed at enhancing the context limit of large language models (LLMs) by merging context chunks hierarchically. This method employs token reduction to manage memory usage, and the computational order is optimized to scale logarithmically with the input length. The method was tested on various tasks such as passkey retrieval, question answering, and language modeling, showing promising results in terms of memory efficiency and context handling. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation, lack of comparisons with relevant baselines, and potential issues with the novelty and technical depth of its contributions.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and easy-to-follow content, making it accessible to readers.
- The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, with a focus on memory efficiency and the handling of long contexts in large language models (LLMs).
- The method demonstrates a significant reduction in memory usage, which is crucial for managing computational resources in LLM inference.
- The paper includes thorough ablation studies that support the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in its ability to maintain context and reduce computational complexity.
- The experimental results are robust, showing improvements in retrieval accuracy and perplexity compared to existing methods, indicating the method's potential for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with relevant baselines, such as YaRN and NTK, which also address the context limit in LLMs. This omission makes it difficult to assess the method's relative performance and novelty.
- The method's technical contributions are not clearly defined, with some aspects appearing to be straightforward extensions or adaptations of existing methods.
- The evaluation is limited to specific tasks, primarily retrieval, which may not fully demonstrate the method's effectiveness across different LLM applications.
- There is a lack of discussion on the method's limitations, particularly in terms of its scalability and generalizability to different LLM architectures and context lengths.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the impact of the method on the quality of context handling and the potential for generating hallucinations or other undesirable outputs.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with YaRN and NTK, including results on the same tasks and datasets to better understand the method's relative performance?
- How does the method handle the merging of chunks in the context of LLMs, particularly in terms of ensuring the integrity and relevance of the merged information?
- Can the authors clarify the technical contributions of the method, especially in terms of how it differs from or improves upon existing methods like YaRN and NTK?
- How does the method perform on other tasks beyond retrieval, such as question answering or text generation?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its scalability and generalizability to different LLM architectures and context lengths?
