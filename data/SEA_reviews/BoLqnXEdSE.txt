 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a new fairness metric, Probable Demographic Parity (PDP), which measures the maximum prediction disparity across probable segments of continuous sensitive attributes. PDP addresses the challenges of underestimation and overestimation in mean and maximum disparity-based fairness metrics. It segments sensitive attributes into probable and non-probable segments based on a predefined probability threshold, focusing on the former for fairness evaluation. Theoretical analysis and empirical studies are provided to validate the metric's effectiveness. The paper also discusses the estimation error and computational complexity of PDP, comparing it with other fairness metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed methodologies and theoretical contributions.
- The introduction of Probable Demographic Parity (PDP) as a new fairness metric for continuous sensitive attributes is innovative and addresses a significant gap in the literature.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including stability and independence properties, which are crucial for the robustness and validity of the proposed metric.
- Experimental evaluations are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed metric, showing improvements over existing metrics in certain scenarios.
- The paper is well-structured, with clear sections and a logical flow that facilitates understanding of the concepts and methodologies presented.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing fairness metrics, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency.
- The novelty of the PDP metric is questionable as it appears to be a simple combination of existing metrics, which might not offer significant new insights or benefits.
- The theoretical analysis, while comprehensive, is not sufficiently novel or groundbreaking, as it primarily builds on existing fairness metrics.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the proposed metric, such as its applicability in real-world scenarios and its impact on model performance.
- The experimental setup and results are not convincingly robust, with limited datasets and a lack of comprehensive statistical analysis, which could undermine the credibility of the findings.
- There are several minor typographical and formatting errors that need correction to improve the overall presentation of the paper.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison with existing fairness metrics, particularly in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
- How does the proposed PDP metric perform in scenarios where the sensitive attributes are quantized or discretized?
- Could the authors clarify the practical implications of the proposed metric, such as its impact on model performance and its applicability in real-world scenarios?
- How does the proposed metric handle scenarios where the sensitive attributes are not normally distributed?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, including statistical analysis and additional datasets, to support the claims made about the effectiveness of the proposed metric?
