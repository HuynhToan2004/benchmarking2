 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel zero-order federated learning (ZOFL) framework designed to address communication and computation bottlenecks in wireless federated learning environments. The framework leverages two gradient estimators, one-point and two-point, to estimate gradients without the need for channel state information, thereby reducing communication overhead. Theoretical analysis and experimental results demonstrate the convergence of the proposed algorithms, showcasing their effectiveness in both IID and non-IID settings. The paper also discusses the impact of the wireless channel on gradient estimation, offering a novel approach to federated learning in wireless networks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel zero-order federated learning (ZOFL) framework that addresses the challenges of communication and computation bottlenecks in wireless federated learning environments.
- The proposed ZOFL framework is theoretically analyzed, demonstrating convergence with rates of O(1/K) for both one-point and two-point gradient estimators.
- The paper provides a detailed analysis of the convergence of the proposed algorithm, supported by experimental results that show the effectiveness of the proposed method in both IID and non-IID settings.
- The paper is well-structured, with a clear presentation of the algorithms, theoretical analysis, and experimental results, making it accessible to readers.
- The paper addresses a significant problem in federated learning, namely, the communication and computation bottlenecks, and proposes a novel solution that is both practical and theoretically sound.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed comparison with other zero-order federated learning algorithms, which could provide a better understanding of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method.
- The experimental results are limited to specific datasets and models, and the paper does not include comparisons with other federated learning algorithms that use different communication strategies, such as quantization or sparsification.
- The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the communication complexity of the proposed method, which is a critical factor in federated learning over wireless channels.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the assumptions about the channel and the noise, which could limit the generalizability of the results.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern in federated learning applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed comparison with other zero-order federated learning algorithms, highlighting the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method perform in terms of communication complexity compared to other federated learning algorithms that use different communication strategies?
- Can the authors clarify the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis, particularly the assumptions about the channel and the noise, and discuss the impact of these assumptions on the generalizability of the results?
- How does the proposed method handle the computational complexity in federated learning applications, and what are the implications for practical deployment?
- The paper mentions the use of different datasets and models in the experiments. Could the authors provide a more detailed description of these datasets and models, including their characteristics and the specifics of the data and models used?
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the practical implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis. Could the authors elaborate on these assumptions and discuss their realism and impact on the results?
