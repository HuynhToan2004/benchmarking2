 **Summary:**
The paper introduces FINOLA, a novel autoregressive model for image representation, which represents each image as a first-order autoregressive process in the latent space. This model is designed to generate feature maps from a single vector, which can be used for image reconstruction and self-supervised learning. FINOLA leverages a first-order norm+linear autoregressive process to predict the next position based on the current position's features, and it is validated through experiments on image reconstruction and self-supervised learning tasks. Despite its innovative approach, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty, lack of theoretical analysis, and marginal performance improvements over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach, FINOLA, which represents images as feature maps and utilizes a simple autoregressive process, which is different from traditional methods that rely on complex models.
- The paper provides empirical evidence for the mathematical description of FINOLA in the form of partial differential equations (PDEs), offering a new insight into image representation.
- The experiments are comprehensive, including image reconstruction and self-supervised learning, which demonstrate the effectiveness of FINOLA in generating feature maps and its potential for further applications.
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The concept of using first-order norm+linear autoregression to generate feature maps is innovative and could potentially be applied in various downstream tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough theoretical analysis of the FINOLA model, which could provide deeper insights into its mathematical properties and the theoretical underpinnings of its behavior.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned as it primarily extends existing methods by using first-order norm+linear autoregression, which might not be considered a significant advancement.
- The performance of FINOLA is marginal, and it does not outperform existing methods significantly, especially in terms of reconstruction quality and classification performance.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare FINOLA with other self-supervised learning methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of its effectiveness.
- There are concerns about the computational efficiency and scalability of FINOLA, especially in larger image sizes and more complex scenarios.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of FINOLA and potential directions for future research.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the mathematical properties of FINOLA, particularly how it captures spatial relationships and how this impacts its performance in image reconstruction and self-supervised learning tasks?
- How does FINOLA compare to other self-supervised learning methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability, especially in larger image sizes and more complex scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of FINOLA and suggest directions for future research to address these limitations?
- In terms of performance, how does FINOLA compare to other methods in more challenging datasets or scenarios?
- Can the authors clarify the role of the "norm" in the "norm+linear" term and explain why this specific combination was chosen for FINOLA?
- How does FINOLA handle the invariance of the coefficient matrices across different images, and what implications does this have for the model's generalization to new images or datasets?
