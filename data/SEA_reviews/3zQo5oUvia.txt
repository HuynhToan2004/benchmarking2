 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for contrastive learning in time-series data, specifically focusing on the identification of positive and negative pairs for contrastive learning. The authors propose using a retrieval-based reconstruction method, termed REBAR, which leverages a convolutional cross-attention architecture to calculate the REBAR error between different time-series. This error is used to predict mutual class membership, thus guiding the selection of positive and negative pairs. The method is evaluated on three datasets and shown to outperform existing methods, although the evaluation is criticized for being insufficient and the method's novelty is questioned due to its similarity to existing techniques like MAE.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed method is novel and well-motivated, with a clear explanation of its intuition and methodology.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over existing methods.
- The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the method, including an ablation study that shows the importance of each component of the proposed method.
- The use of a retrieval-based reconstruction method is innovative and addresses the challenge of identifying positive pairs in time-series data, which is crucial for contrastive learning.
- The paper is well-organized and structured, with detailed explanations of the methodology and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to other modalities beyond time-series data.
- The evaluation of the method is insufficient and does not include a broad range of datasets or comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method, particularly in terms of its computational complexity and scalability with larger datasets.
- The novelty of the method is questioned due to its similarity to existing techniques like MAE, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from these prior works.
- The paper contains several typographical errors and could benefit from a more rigorous proofreading process.
- The paper's focus on time-series data limits its applicability to other domains, and the method's effectiveness in real-world scenarios, such as medical applications, is not adequately addressed.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the definition and usage of "motif" in the context of their method?
- How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in terms of computational complexity and scalability?
- Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, particularly those that address similar challenges in time-series data?
- How does the proposed method handle the issue of class imbalance in time-series data?
- Can the authors provide more insights into the selection of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the method?
- How does the proposed method perform in real-world applications, such as medical diagnosis, and what are the potential limitations or challenges in such scenarios?
