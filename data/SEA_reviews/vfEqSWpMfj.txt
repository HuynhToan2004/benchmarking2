 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel method for evaluating the importance of individual words in system prompts using large language models (LLMs). This method involves masking each word in the prompt and assessing the impact on model outputs based on various text scores. The approach is inspired by permutation importance in tabular data and is tested using GPT-3.5 Turbo and Llama2-13B models. The experiments demonstrate that the importance scores are positively correlated with the impact of suffixes on the model outputs. However, the paper's methodology and results are criticized for their simplicity, lack of comprehensive evaluation, and potential over-reliance on specific model outputs. The paper also faces criticism for not adequately addressing the complexity of natural language processing tasks and for not providing a robust method for measuring the impact of user prompts.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel approach to evaluating the importance of individual words in system prompts using large language models (LLMs), which is a significant advancement in understanding the behavior of these models.
- The methodology is simple, intuitive, and does not require direct access to the model, making it accessible and applicable to a wide range of models.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with helpful figures that aid in understanding the proposed method.
- The approach has the potential to be applied to proprietary models, which could be beneficial for practitioners and researchers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's methodology is considered overly simplistic and does not adequately address the complexity of natural language processing tasks, such as grammar and semantics, which are crucial for understanding the impact of words in prompts.
- The evaluation of the method is limited, with insufficient testing across different models and a lack of comparison with other methods like SHAP.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive analysis of the impact of user prompts, which is a critical aspect of prompt engineering.
- The paper's experimental setup is not robust enough, with limited testing across different models and a lack of rigorous statistical analysis.
- The method's reliance on specific model outputs (GPT-3.5 Turbo and Llama2-13B) may limit its applicability and generalizability to other models.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential for bias in the results due to the model's inherent biases.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify the rationale behind the choice of specific models (GPT-3.5 Turbo and Llama2-13B) for testing and discuss the implications of this choice on the method's applicability and generalizability?
- How does the method handle the impact of stopwords, and could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how these are handled?
- Could the authors provide more detailed statistics and analysis on the impact of user prompts, especially in scenarios where the system prompt is fixed?
- How does the method ensure that the results are not biased by the model's inherent biases, and what steps can be taken to mitigate these biases?
- Can the authors discuss the potential for extending the method to include the impact of user prompts and the integration of other models or datasets to enhance the robustness and applicability of the findings?
