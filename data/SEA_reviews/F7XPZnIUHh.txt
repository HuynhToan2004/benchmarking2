 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel adversarial deep representation learning (ADR) algorithm for estimating individual treatment effects (ITE) from observational data. The ADR algorithm decomposes the covariates into instrumental variables (I), confounders (C), and adjustment variables (A) based on a causal graph, which helps in estimating ITE more effectively. This method is theoretically grounded and empirically validated through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets. The authors aim to improve the estimation of ITE by learning decomposed representations, thereby reducing the variance of the conditional treatment effect estimator. However, the paper's contributions are questioned due to similarities with existing methods, and the novelty of the theoretical analysis is limited.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow, and the problem of estimating individual treatment effects (ITE) is clearly articulated.
- The proposed method, ADR, is theoretically grounded and empirically validated through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the paper provides a rigorous definition of variables decomposition via the causal graph, which is identifiable and can be used to guide the design of algorithms.
- The paper includes both synthetic and real datasets in the experiments, which enhances the credibility of the results.
- The authors demonstrate the advantage of their method compared to state-of-the-art methods through experiments.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how the decomposition is related to the causal graph, particularly the equivalence between the graphical definition and the probabilistic definition of the decomposition.
- The methodology appears to be a direct application of existing methods like DR-CFR and DeR-CFR, with similarities in loss functions and model architecture.
- The theoretical analysis, while rigorous, does not provide significant new insights or contributions to the field of causal inference.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed method, such as the potential for overfitting and the need for more extensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of the ADR algorithm.
- The experimental results are not convincing enough to demonstrate the effectiveness of the ADR algorithm, especially in scenarios where it underperforms compared to existing methods.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the ADR algorithm, particularly in large-scale datasets.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the relationship between the decomposition and the causal graph, specifically how the graphical definition implies the probabilistic definition?
- How does the ADR algorithm differ from existing methods like DR-CFR and DeR-CFR, particularly in terms of the theoretical contributions and practical applications?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the ADR algorithm, especially in large-scale datasets?
- Why is the ADR algorithm not as effective as other methods in certain scenarios, and how does it compare in terms of computational efficiency and resource requirements?
- How do the authors address the issue of overfitting in the ADR algorithm, and what measures have been taken to prevent this?
- Could the authors provide more detailed results on the experimental setups, including the specific experimental conditions and the rationale behind the choices made?
