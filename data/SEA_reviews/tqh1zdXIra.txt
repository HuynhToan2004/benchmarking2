 **Summary:**
The paper introduces QuickTune, a method designed to select the most appropriate pre-trained model and optimize its hyperparameters for a given dataset. QuickTune employs a meta-learning approach to predict performance and cost, allowing for efficient hyperparameter optimization across various datasets. The method is evaluated against several baselines and shows promising results in terms of performance and computational efficiency. However, the paper has been critiqued for its limited evaluation scope, focusing primarily on computer vision tasks and not sufficiently addressing the scalability and generalizability of the approach across different domains.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a clear motivation and a novel approach to solving the problem of selecting the most appropriate pre-trained model and optimizing its hyperparameters.
- The proposed method, QuickTune, is technically sound and innovative, leveraging meta-learning and cost-awareness to predict performance and cost, which is particularly useful in scenarios with limited computational resources.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation that demonstrates the superiority of QuickTune over various baselines, showing that it can significantly reduce computational costs and improve performance in various settings.
- The method's application to model hubs is well-explained, providing a clear rationale for its utility in practical scenarios.
- The paper is well-organized and includes detailed descriptions of the methodology, experimental setup, and results, making it accessible and reproducible.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to other domains beyond computer vision and its scalability with larger datasets.
- The evaluation is limited to a specific set of datasets, which might not generalize well to other scenarios. The paper could benefit from a more diverse set of experiments to validate the method's effectiveness across different domains and datasets.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not sufficiently compare the proposed method with other state-of-the-art methods, which could provide a better understanding of its relative performance.
- The paper does not adequately address the issue of model selection and hyperparameter tuning being two separate problems, which could be addressed by using a unified approach.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion on the computational complexity and the scalability of the proposed method with larger datasets and more complex models.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the scalability of the proposed method, particularly in terms of its applicability to other domains and its scalability with larger datasets?
- How does the method handle scenarios where the dataset size is very large, and what are the potential challenges and solutions in such cases?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of the proposed method and how these could be addressed in future work?
- How does the method compare with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method and how it scales with the size of the dataset and the complexity of the model?
- How does the method handle the issue of model selection and hyperparameter tuning being two separate problems, and could a unified approach be used to address this?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the choice of datasets and the methodology used for evaluating the proposed method?
