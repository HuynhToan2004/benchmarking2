 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel approach for neural network compression by integrating an end-to-end training method that optimizes for FLOPs through a latency surrogate based on L1/L2 regularization. This method aims to reduce computational costs without compromising performance, achieving a 50% reduction in FLOPs with minimal performance loss in BERT compression. The approach is tested on various models including BERT and MobileNetV3, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing model size and latency. However, the paper lacks detailed analysis of the latency regularizer and does not provide sufficient hardware-aware comparisons, which are critical for assessing the practical efficiency of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a novel method for neural network compression, which is both end-to-end and effective in reducing computational costs.
- The methodology is well-articulated, making it easy to follow and understand, with clear explanations of the proposed techniques.
- The paper provides extensive experimental results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across various models and datasets.
- The approach is versatile, applicable to a wide range of neural network models, including those used for both image and language tasks.
- The paper is well-written, with clear figures and tables that aid in understanding the results and methodologies.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the latency regularizer, which is crucial for understanding the performance of the proposed method.
- The paper does not provide sufficient hardware-aware comparisons, which are essential for assessing the practical efficiency of the proposed method.
- There is a lack of discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, which could help in understanding its applicability and potential drawbacks.
- The paper does not provide sufficient details on the experimental setup, such as the hardware used for training and inference, which could affect the reproducibility and comparability of the results.
- The paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods, including both hardware-aware and hardware-agnostic approaches.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide a detailed analysis of the latency regularizer, including how it affects the performance of the proposed method?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of hardware efficiency with existing methods, particularly those that are hardware-aware?
- Can the authors clarify the hardware setup used for training and inference, including the specific hardware models and their configurations?
- How does the proposed method compare in terms of training and inference times with other methods, especially in scenarios where the training time is critical?
- Could the authors discuss the limitations of their method and how these might affect its applicability in different scenarios?
- Is there a plan to extend the method to include other types of neural network models, such as those used in different domains or with different architectures?
