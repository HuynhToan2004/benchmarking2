 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a method for training agents to solve a set of 162 language-based tasks using a combination of pre-trained compositional value functions and a large language model (LLM). The approach involves a pre-training phase where value functions are trained to handle specific tasks, followed by a training phase where the LLM learns to map language instructions to these value functions. The method shows promising results in terms of generalization and sample efficiency, with the LLM able to learn the mapping from language to value functions through a process involving in-context learning and feedback from environment rollouts. However, the paper is critiqued for its lack of novelty, as it primarily builds upon existing methods like WVF and LLM-based semantic parsing, and for not sufficiently addressing the generalization of the method to more complex or varied environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in reducing sample complexity and improving generalization, as shown in Figure 3.
- The method is novel in its application of in-context learning with feedback from environment rollouts to enhance the semantic parsing capabilities of the LLM.
- The paper is significant in its contribution to the field by solving a large set of language-RL tasks and showing that the proposed method significantly outperforms previous baselines in terms of sample complexity.
- The authors have provided a comprehensive review of the literature, highlighting the paper's position within the current research landscape.

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's novelty is limited as it primarily builds upon existing methods like WVF and LLM-based semantic parsing, which have been previously explored.
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion on the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of its generalization to more complex or varied environments.
- There is a lack of clarity in the methodology, especially in the role of the LLM in mapping language to value functions and the process of pretraining the WVFs.
- The experimental setup and results are not sufficiently robust or varied, with insufficient discussion on the statistical significance of the results and the potential impact of different training conditions.
- The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of the method, such as the computational costs and the scalability of the approach to more complex or varied environments.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the role and contribution of the LLM in the mapping from language to value functions, particularly in terms of how it enhances the semantic parsing capabilities?
- How does the method perform when the LLM is not involved in the mapping process, and what are the implications for the generalization and sample efficiency of the approach?
- Could the authors provide more detailed examples or scenarios where the method might not generalize well, and discuss potential strategies for addressing these limitations?
- How does the method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational costs and scalability, especially when applied to more complex or varied environments?
- Can the authors provide more information on the training conditions, such as the number of steps used for pretraining the WVFs and the impact of these conditions on the performance of the method?
