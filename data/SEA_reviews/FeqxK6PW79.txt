 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the geometric properties of transformer models applied to time series forecasting, specifically focusing on the intrinsic dimension and mean absolute principal curvature (MAPG) of data representations across layers. The authors employ manifold learning tools to analyze these properties, revealing insights into the behavior of transformers in this context. They find that transformers exhibit unique geometric behaviors compared to those observed in other domains like image classification, suggesting that the forecasting task requires different geometric properties. The study also explores how these geometric features evolve during training and how they correlate with model performance. However, the paper's contributions are seen as somewhat limited, as it primarily applies existing manifold learning techniques to a new domain without significantly advancing the theoretical understanding or practical applications of these techniques.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an interesting and novel approach to the application of manifold learning techniques in the context of time series forecasting, which is a relatively unexplored area.
- The empirical results presented are both interesting and provide valuable insights into the behavior of transformers in this specific application, which could be beneficial for future research in this field.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The authors have conducted a thorough analysis of the geometric properties of transformers, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The paper is well-positioned within the literature, highlighting its relevance and contribution to the ongoing research in transformer models and time series forecasting.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper primarily applies existing manifold learning techniques to a new domain without significantly advancing the theoretical understanding or practical applications of these techniques, which may limit its impact and novelty.
- The paper's claims about the behavior of transformers in time series forecasting are not sufficiently supported by a comprehensive theoretical framework or additional empirical evidence.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practical implications of the findings, which could limit its applicability and relevance to practitioners.
- The paper's focus on specific transformer architectures (Autoformer and FEDformer) may limit its generalizability to other transformer-based models, which could be a significant drawback for broader applicability.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of its approach, which could be crucial for understanding the scope and applicability of the findings.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors clarify how the intrinsic dimension and mean absolute principal curvature are estimated and what the specific values mean in the context of the study?
- How do the authors justify the choice of the two transformer architectures (Autoformer and FEDformer) for their analysis, and what insights do these choices provide about the behavior of transformers in time series forecasting?
- In light of the findings, what are the practical implications for improving time series forecasting models, and how can these insights be applied in real-world scenarios?
- Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their approach and how these might affect the generalizability and applicability of the findings?
- How does the paper's focus on transformer-based models for time series forecasting compare to other models, such as recurrent neural networks or graph neural networks, in terms of geometric properties and model performance?
