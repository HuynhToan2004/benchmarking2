 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the impact of discrete tokenization on masked image modeling (MIM) and proposes a novel tokenizer, K-MIM, designed to improve the alignment between discrete tokens and true image classes. The authors argue that this alignment is crucial for enhancing the performance of MIM models, particularly in downstream tasks. Theoretical analysis is provided to support the claim that better tokenization leads to better performance, and empirical evidence is presented to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed tokenizer. However, the paper is criticized for its limited experimental scope, focusing primarily on ImageNet datasets, and for not adequately addressing the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel metric, TCAS, for evaluating the quality of discrete tokenization in MIM, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The theoretical analysis provided is comprehensive and well-articulated, offering insights into the role of discrete tokenization in MIM.
- The proposed K-MIM tokenizer shows promising results in improving the performance of MIM models, as evidenced by empirical results.
- The paper is well-written, making it accessible and easy to follow, which is crucial for advancing research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a broader experimental validation, focusing primarily on ImageNet datasets, which may not generalize well to other datasets or real-world applications.
- The computational efficiency of the proposed method is not adequately addressed, raising concerns about its scalability and practical applicability.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of the proposed method, particularly concerning the scalability of the K-MIM tokenizer and its generalization to other datasets or real-world applications.
- The paper does not adequately address the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, which is a significant oversight.
- The paper's theoretical contributions are somewhat limited, as the analysis is based on simplified models that may not fully capture the complexities of real-world applications.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, particularly in terms of scalability and practical applicability?
- How does the proposed method perform on other datasets beyond ImageNet, and what are the implications for its generalization to real-world applications?
- Can the authors clarify the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential avenues for future research to address these limitations?
- How does the proposed method compare to other existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?
- Could the authors provide more detailed experimental results, particularly on other datasets, to support the generalization of their findings?
