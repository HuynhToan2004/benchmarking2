 **Summary:**
The paper introduces a novel distributional successor measure (DSM) in reinforcement learning (RL), which extends the traditional successor representation (SR) by representing the distribution of future state occupancies. The DSM is used to evaluate the distributional return of a policy under a new reward function, facilitating zero-shot distributional policy evaluation. The approach uses a delta-model to approximate the DSM and minimizes a two-level maximum mean discrepancy to learn the distributional successor measure from samples. The paper also discusses the theoretical foundations of the DSM and provides empirical evidence of its effectiveness in various environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel distributional successor measure (DSM) which can be used to evaluate distributional return under a new reward function without requiring further learning, a significant advancement in distributional RL.
- The approach allows for zero-shot distributional policy evaluation, which is a unique feature not previously possible in standard distributional RL.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, with a comprehensive background section that effectively explains the necessary concepts for understanding the rest of the paper.
- The proposed method is sound and provides a clear explanation of the approach, including the use of delta-models and the MMD loss for training.
- The paper is technically sound, with good experimental results and a clear presentation of the methodology.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from more extensive empirical evaluations, especially in more complex environments like Atari or MuJoCo.
- The choice of the number of delta-models (m) and the kernel for MMD in the experiments is not well justified, which could affect the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
- The paper could be more self-contained, as it heavily relies on the references for some key concepts and notations, which might confuse readers unfamiliar with the field.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding its practical applicability.
- The novelty of the approach is somewhat limited, as the distributional successor measure is a natural extension of the successor representation, and the use of delta-models and MMD loss is not entirely novel.

**Questions:**
- How does the proposed method compare to other distributional RL methods, such as C51 or DQC, in terms of computational efficiency and performance?
- Can you provide more details on the choice of the number of delta-models (m) and the kernel for MMD in the experiments?
- How does the method perform in more complex environments like Atari or MuJoCo?
- Can the method be adapted to work with different types of state spaces, such as continuous or discrete?
- How does the method handle the computational complexity and scalability, especially in large-scale applications?
- Is there a possibility to extend the method to handle non-Markovian environments, such as those with stochastic transitions?
