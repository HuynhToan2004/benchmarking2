 **Summary:**
The paper explores conformal prediction in the context of inductive node classification on graph neural networks (GNNs), specifically addressing the challenges posed by the addition of new nodes or edges to the graph. It introduces two variants of conformal prediction: subgraph conformal prediction and weighted subgraph conformal prediction, which are designed to maintain coverage guarantees under node or edge exchangeability. Theoretical proofs and empirical results are provided to support these approaches. However, the paper is critiqued for its limited scope, focusing only on node classification and not extending to other types of graph learning tasks. The method's applicability to sparse graphs and its empirical performance are also questioned.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces an innovative approach to conformal prediction in inductive node classification, addressing the unique challenges posed by the dynamic nature of graph structures.
- The theoretical analysis is robust, providing a strong foundation for the proposed methods, and the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow.
- The paper is the first to tackle conformal prediction in inductive node classification, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The empirical evaluation is thorough, including multiple datasets and different conformity functions, which supports the robustness of the findings.
- The authors have provided a detailed discussion of the limitations and potential challenges associated with their proposed methods, which enhances the credibility and reliability of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's focus on node classification limits its applicability to other types of graph learning tasks, which could be a significant drawback for broader impact.
- The empirical results show that the proposed methods do not significantly outperform standard conformal prediction in terms of classification accuracy, raising questions about their practical utility.
- The paper's assumptions of node/edge exchangeability are strong and may not hold in real-world scenarios, which could limit the generalizability of the findings.
- The paper's presentation could be improved, particularly in the clarity of figures and the explanation of certain technical details, such as the definition of the conformity function and the methodology for calculating coverage probabilities.
- There are concerns about the scalability of the proposed methods, especially in large-scale graph learning scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify how the conformity function, s(x,y), is defined and how it is used in the proposed methods?
- How does the paper handle the issue of distribution shift when new nodes or edges are added to the graph?
- Could the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, particularly the selection of hyperparameters and the rationale behind the choice of datasets?
- What are the implications of the assumptions of node/edge exchangeability for the practical applicability of the proposed methods?
- How do the authors address the scalability issues in large-scale graph learning scenarios?
- Could the authors elaborate on the potential applications of their methods beyond node classification, such as in other types of graph learning tasks?
