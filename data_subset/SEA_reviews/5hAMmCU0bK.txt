 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the robustness of offline reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms under data corruption, focusing on the Implicit Q-Learning (IQL) algorithm and its robustness to various types of data corruption. The authors introduce the Robust IQL (RIQL) algorithm, which incorporates observation normalization, Huber loss, and quantile Q estimators to enhance RL's resilience against data corruption. The paper presents empirical and theoretical analyses, demonstrating that RIQL outperforms other offline RL algorithms in handling data corruption. Despite its strengths, the paper has been critiqued for its limited novelty and the scope of its experiments, which primarily focus on synthetic data.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear and concise explanations of the main ideas, algorithms, and experiments.
- The authors conduct extensive experiments to validate their claims, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
- Theoretical results are provided to support the claims, which adds depth to the empirical findings.
- The motivation behind the research is clear, focusing on the robustness of offline RL algorithms to data corruption, which is a significant and relevant problem.
- The paper presents a novel approach by integrating the Huber loss and quantile estimators, which is not commonly seen in offline RL algorithms.
- The experimental results show that the proposed method, RIQL, outperforms other baselines in handling data corruption, which is a notable achievement.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty of the proposed method is limited as it primarily modifies existing algorithms like IQL, and the theoretical analysis appears to be a direct application of existing results without significant new insights.
- The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with recent robust offline RL methods, which could have provided a more robust evaluation of the proposed method.
- The empirical evaluations are primarily conducted on synthetic datasets, which may not fully represent real-world scenarios where data corruption is more complex and varied.
- The paper does not adequately address the computational efficiency of the proposed method, which could be a significant concern for practical applications.
- There are inconsistencies and potential errors in the theoretical analysis, such as the handling of cumulative corruption levels and the assumptions made about the dataset's size.
- The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the limitations of the proposed method, particularly in handling complex and realistic data corruption scenarios.

**Questions:**
- Could the authors clarify the differences between the results presented in this paper and those in the IQL paper, especially regarding the robustness claims?
- How does the proposed method compare with other robust offline RL methods, particularly in terms of theoretical and empirical performance?
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of the proposed method, especially in comparison to other methods?
- How does the proposed method handle different types of data corruption, especially those that are not as straightforward as the synthetic corruption scenarios used in the experiments?
- Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical assumptions and their implications for the practical applicability of the method?
- What are the potential negative societal impacts of the proposed method, and how can these be mitigated?
