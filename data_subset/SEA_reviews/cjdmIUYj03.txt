 **Summary:**
The paper investigates the generalization ability of various temporal graph learning algorithms, focusing on the generalization error's relationship with the number of layers/steps in GNN-/RNN-based methods and the feature-label alignment (FLA) score. It introduces a new algorithm, SToNe, which is designed to minimize generalization error and improve overall performance. Theoretical insights are provided to explain the observed phenomena, and empirical results demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness on real-world datasets. The paper also discusses the limitations of existing methods and offers a new perspective on designing practical algorithms for future studies.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the generalization error of temporal graph learning algorithms, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The introduction of the Simplified-Temporal-Graph-Network (SToNe) algorithm, which is motivated by theoretical insights, is a novel approach that has been validated through empirical results.
- The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
- The proposed SToNe algorithm is simple, efficient, and effective, as demonstrated through empirical results.
- The paper discusses the limitations of existing methods and provides a new perspective on designing practical algorithms for future studies, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion on the limitations of the proposed algorithm, particularly in terms of its applicability and generalization to other datasets or scenarios.
- The empirical results are limited to link prediction tasks and do not include other tasks such as node classification, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the algorithm's performance.
- The paper does not sufficiently discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm compared to other state-of-the-art methods.
- The novelty of the paper is questioned as the proposed algorithm appears to be a simplified version of existing methods, which may not be sufficiently distinct to warrant a new algorithm.
- There is a lack of comparison with other methods in terms of the number of parameters, which is crucial for evaluating the efficiency of the proposed algorithm.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of the feature-label alignment (FLA) score and its practical implications.

**Questions:**
- Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm compared to other state-of-the-art methods?
- How does the proposed algorithm perform on other tasks beyond link prediction, such as node classification?
- Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of the proposed algorithm and discuss potential future research directions to address these limitations?
- How does the proposed algorithm compare to other methods in terms of the number of parameters, and what are the implications of this comparison for the practical applicability of the algorithm?
- In the discussion of the FLA score, could the authors provide more details on its theoretical underpinnings and practical implications, particularly in the context of temporal graph learning?
